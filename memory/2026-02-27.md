## Cross-Layer Behavioral Discordance (CLBD) Network Study - COMPLETED

### Theoretical Motivation and Research Context

This study emerged from the growing literature on computational approaches to coordinated inauthentic behavior (CIB) detection on social media platforms. Drawing on network science traditions in communication research (Himelboim et al., 2017; González-Bailón & Wang, 2016), we investigated whether behavioral inconsistency across different interaction modalities—what we termed "Cross-Layer Behavioral Discordance" (CLBD)—could serve as a reliable signal for identifying coordinated actors in political discourse networks.

The theoretical premise drew from two streams of research: (1) the multi-layer network paradigm, which conceptualizes social media platforms as overlapping interaction systems where users engage through distinct affordances (retweets, replies, quotes) with potentially different audiences and purposes (Magnani et al., 2021); and (2) coordination detection literature suggesting that inauthentic actors may exhibit behavioral signatures distinct from organic users (Pacheco et al., 2021; Nizzoli et al., 2021).

Our guiding hypothesis posited that authentic users would demonstrate coherent behavioral patterns across interaction layers—engaging similar accounts whether retweeting, replying, or quoting—while coordinated actors might exhibit discordant patterns, interacting with different account sets across modalities as a function of role specialization within coordinated campaigns.

### Research Design and Methodological Approach

**Dataset Construction**

We analyzed a corpus of 266,242 tweets from 102,706 unique users discussing the Ukraine crisis during a 72-hour window (June 7-9, 2023). This dataset was selected for several methodological reasons: (1) sufficient temporal density to construct meaningful interaction networks; (2) geopolitical salience likely to attract both organic discourse and potential influence operations; (3) multi-lingual character enabling examination of cross-national participation patterns.

**Multi-Layer Network Construction**

Following established protocols in network analysis of political communication (Barberá et al., 2015; Freelon et al., 2020), we constructed three distinct interaction networks representing different communicative affordances on Twitter/X:

| Network Layer | Nodes | Edges | Density | Interpretation |
|---------------|-------|-------|---------|----------------|
| Retweet (RT) | 87,412 | 151,847 | 1.99×10⁻⁵ | Amplification/endorsement |
| Reply | 11,203 | 12,456 | 9.92×10⁻⁵ | Conversational engagement |
| Quote Tweet | 9,847 | 10,234 | 1.06×10⁻⁴ | Commentary/reframing |

The substantial differences in network size across layers reflect known platform affordance usage patterns (Metaxas et al., 2015): retweeting requires minimal cognitive investment and serves amplification functions, while replies and quotes demand greater engagement and serve more conversational or commentary purposes.

**Operationalizing Cross-Layer Behavioral Discordance**

We operationalized CLBD through ego-network overlap analysis. For each user *u* appearing in multiple layers, we computed the Jaccard similarity coefficient between their interaction partners across layer pairs:

*J(A,B) = |A ∩ B| / |A ∪ B|*

Where *A* and *B* represent the sets of accounts user *u* interacted with in layers A and B respectively. Users with *J* = 0 across all layer pairs exhibited complete behavioral discordance—never interacting with the same account through different modalities.

### Principal Findings: A Null Result with Methodological Implications

**Finding 1: Discordance as the Norm, Not the Exception**

Contrary to our theoretical expectations, we found that cross-layer behavioral discordance is the dominant pattern among Twitter users discussing geopolitical events. Fully **80.3%** of users appearing in multiple interaction layers demonstrated zero overlap in their interaction partners—complete behavioral discordance.

This finding fundamentally challenged our initial premise. If the vast majority of users exhibit discordance, the metric cannot discriminate between authentic and inauthentic actors; it merely captures normative platform behavior.

**Finding 2: Account Age as Confound—The Inverse Relationship**

To salvage potential diagnostic value, we examined whether discordance patterns varied by account characteristics that might correlate with authenticity. We stratified accounts by age (using account creation date relative to data collection), hypothesizing that newer accounts—which often characterize bot or sockpuppet campaigns (Varol et al., 2017)—might exhibit higher discordance.

The results revealed the **opposite** pattern:

| Account Age Category | CLBD Score (mean) | Complete Discordance (%) |
|---------------------|-------------------|--------------------------|
| <30 days | 0.12 | 53.1% |
| 30-100 days | 0.08 | 67.4% |
| 100-365 days | 0.05 | 78.2% |
| >365 days | 0.03 | 83.5% |

Established accounts—those most likely to represent authentic, long-term platform participants—exhibited **more** discordance than new accounts. This inverse relationship effectively disqualifies CLBD as a coordination indicator: the behavioral pattern we hypothesized as suspicious is actually characteristic of authentic, established users.

**Interpreting the Null Finding**

We propose several explanations for this counterintuitive pattern, drawing on platform affordance theory (boyd, 2010) and user behavior research:

1. **Audience Differentiation**: Established users develop differentiated networks for different communicative purposes. They may retweet news sources and public figures, reply to personal acquaintances, and quote-tweet for commentary to followers—with minimal overlap between these audiences.

2. **Functional Specialization of Affordances**: Each interaction type serves distinct communicative functions (Meraz & Papacharissi, 2013). Sophisticated users leverage these distinctions, while newer users may interact more uniformly across modalities as they learn platform norms.

3. **Network Size Effects**: Established users accumulate larger, more diverse networks over time, mathematically reducing the probability of random overlap across sub-networks even absent intentional differentiation.

### Multi-Model Validation: Epistemic Contribution of Agentic Review

A methodologically significant aspect of this study was the multi-model peer review process conducted through the CommDAAF framework. After Claude generated the initial analysis and interpretation, GLM-4.7 conducted an independent critical review.

**GLM's Critical Contribution**: The GLM review identified the baseline validation gap as the study's central weakness—an issue Claude's initial analysis had underemphasized. GLM noted that claiming any metric indicates anomalous behavior requires first establishing expected distributions under null conditions. This observation precipitated the account-age stratification analysis that ultimately revealed the inverse relationship.

This finding has implications for agentic research workflows: multi-model review serves not merely as reliability checking (ensuring consistent outputs) but as a form of **epistemic diversification**—different models may weight methodological concerns differently, catching blind spots in initial analyses.

### Contributions and Limitations

**Theoretical Contribution**: This study contributes a null finding to the coordination detection literature—documenting what does *not* work. Such findings, while often undervalued in publication hierarchies, serve important scientific functions by preventing future researchers from pursuing unproductive approaches (Fanelli, 2012).

**Methodological Contribution**: We demonstrate the critical importance of baseline validation in computational social science. Metrics that appear intuitively reasonable as coordination indicators may, upon empirical examination, simply capture normative behavior. This echoes broader concerns in the field about the validity of computational proxies for latent constructs (Lazer et al., 2020).

**Limitations**: (1) Single-platform analysis limits generalizability to multi-platform coordination; (2) 72-hour temporal window may miss longer-term behavioral patterns; (3) We did not examine CLBD in conjunction with other metrics (temporal synchronization, content similarity) that might produce valid composite indicators.

### Methodological Lessons for CommDAAF Framework

This study yielded several insights applicable to future agentic content analysis projects:

1. **Baseline Validation Protocol**: Any metric proposed as an indicator of anomalous behavior must first be tested against expected distributions under null/organic conditions. This should be formalized as a mandatory phase in CommDAAF study protocols.

2. **Multi-Model Review as Epistemic Safeguard**: The GLM review caught a methodological weakness that single-model analysis missed. Multi-model review should be framed not merely as reliability checking but as epistemic diversification—leveraging the different "priors" and emphases of distinct model architectures.

3. **Value of Negative Findings**: Null results merit documentation and dissemination. The finding that CLBD does not indicate coordination saves future researchers from pursuing this approach, representing genuine scientific contribution even absent positive findings.

4. **Contextual Interpretation of Network Metrics**: Raw network statistics are meaningless absent behavioral and temporal context. Baseline comparisons, stratification by user characteristics, and temporal trend analysis are necessary interpretive scaffolding for any network metric proposed as diagnostic.
