## Research Questions

### RQ1: Rhetorical Construction of Enemy
*How do users linguistically construct "the enemy" in war discourse (Russia) vs protest discourse (Iranian regime)?*

**Method:** Critical Discourse Analysis (CDA)  
**Data:** INJUSTICE-coded posts from both datasets  
**Focus Areas:**
- Naming practices (Russia, Putin, regime, mullahs)
- Dehumanization strategies (orcs, criminals, terrorists)
- Moral framing (war crimes, oppression, evil)
- Us/them boundary construction

**Sample Exemplars (Ukraine - INJUSTICE):**
> "#RussianWarCrimes #RussianLooters #RussianRapists"  
> "#Lavrov is a notorious liar. He can't concede that #Russia made war crimes, mocks the victims"  
> "All is left from of a #Russian BMP is a pile of mangled scrap metal and what remains of a tankie's helmet"

**Analytical Questions:**
1. What naming conventions are used for the enemy?
2. How is collective guilt vs individual blame constructed?
3. What actions are attributed to the enemy?
4. How does visual content (images) complement textual framing?

---

### RQ2: Solidarity Performance in Diaspora Discourse
*What discursive strategies do diaspora users employ to perform solidarity with victims they cannot physically help?*

**Method:** Thematic Analysis  
**Data:** SOLIDARITY-coded posts, especially English-language  
**Focus Areas:**
- Witnessing ("We see you")
- Amplification ("Share this")
- Identity claims ("We stand with")
- Emotional labor (expressing grief, outrage on behalf of)

**Sample Exemplars (Ukraine - SOLIDARITY):**
> "Three cheers for #Ukraine & all those who supported the vote. Ukraine and Moldova have both been granted EU candidate status"  
> "Thank you for your support, Madam President! We are waiting for 'YES' from the #EU! #Ukraine is #Europe!"  
> "NATO exercise Ramstein Alloy... A demonstration of our interoperability"

**Analytical Questions:**
1. How do users position themselves vis-à-vis the conflict?
2. What symbolic actions substitute for physical presence?
3. How is international/institutional support framed as solidarity?
4. What pronouns are used (we/they/us)?

---

### RQ3: Frame Hybridization and Emotional Regulation
*How do users blend frames (e.g., INJUSTICE + HOPE) and what does this reveal about emotional regulation in crisis discourse?*

**Method:** Sequential/Structural Analysis  
**Data:** Posts where models disagreed on frame (frame boundary cases)  
**Focus Areas:**
- Emotional arcs within single posts
- Coping strategies (humor, defiance, hope after grief)
- Narrative structure (problem → response → outcome)

**Sample Boundary Cases:**
> "Today @ScaniaGroup opens a brand new facility near @Kyiv. Scania trucks will help #Ukraine rebuild what #Russia destroys. Nothing can break the resilience of Ukrainians."  
> - Claude: HOPE (resilience, rebuilding)  
> - Kimi: SOLIDARITY (international support)

**Analytical Questions:**
1. What patterns emerge in frame co-occurrence?
2. How do users move from negative to positive valence within posts?
3. What linguistic markers signal frame transitions?
4. Does frame hybridization differ by language (English vs native)?

---

### RQ4: Silence and Absence
*What is NOT said? What frames are conspicuously absent in each context?*

**Method:** Negative Case Analysis  
**Data:** Frame distribution comparison across contexts

**Preliminary Observations:**

| Frame | Ukraine | #MahsaAmini | Gap Analysis |
|-------|---------|-------------|--------------|
| HOPE | 2.4-6.8% | ~10%? | Less hope in war? Realistic pessimism? |
| CONFLICT | 2.7-11.8% | Low | Protest ≠ military conflict |
| CALL_TO_ACTION | 0.6-4.1% | Higher | Different mobilization strategies? |

**Analytical Questions:**
1. Why is HOPE less prevalent in war discourse?
2. What does low CONFLICT framing in protest reveal about movement strategy?
3. Are certain frames culturally inappropriate for certain contexts?
4. What topics are systematically avoided?

### RQ5: Irony as Resistance
*How does ironic/sarcastic framing function in political resistance discourse across cultural contexts?*

**Method:** Discourse Analysis of Irony  
**Data:** Posts where Claude coded INFORMATIONAL but Kimi detected evaluative frame

**Sample (Irony Detected):**
> "📸Photo of the Day📸 🇷🇺🇮🇷 #Russia worships its true master."  
> - Claude: INFORMATIONAL (photo post)  
> - Kimi: INJUSTICE (sarcastic criticism)

**Analytical Questions:**
1. What are the markers of political irony? (emojis, quotes, hyperbole)
2. Does irony function differently in war vs protest contexts?
3. What is the target of irony in each context?
4. How does irony create in-group solidarity?

## Methodological Notes

### Sampling Strategy
1. **Consensus cases** (all 3 models agree) = Clearest exemplars
2. **Claude-Kimi consensus** = Secondary exemplars (GLM INFORMATIONAL bias excluded)
3. **Disagreement cases** = Frame boundary cases for theoretical development

### Analytical Approach
1. Close reading of 20-30 posts per RQ
2. Memo writing during analysis
3. Pattern identification across posts
4. Negative case analysis (what doesn't fit?)
5. Cross-context comparison (Ukraine vs #MahsaAmini)

### Validity Strategies
1. Thick description (preserve context in analysis)
2. Member checking (would participants recognize interpretations?)
3. Negative case incorporation
4. Peer debriefing (adversarial review)
