## 1. Research Problem

Wikipedia is the *de facto* first draft of history for millions of readers. During active geopolitical conflicts, Wikipedia editors engage in intense battles over terminology, classification, sourcing, and narrative emphasis. These battles reveal **whose perspective becomes "neutral"**—a fundamentally political question disguised as an editorial one.

The 2023-present Gaza war and the 2025-2026 Iran war provide an unprecedented opportunity to study **epistemic contestation in real-time** across two interconnected but distinct conflicts involving overlapping actors (Israel, Iran, US) but different editorial dynamics.

### Why This Matters

1. **Wikipedia as epistemic infrastructure**: 50M+ views for "Russian invasion of Ukraine" in 2022; likely similar for Iran war
2. **Framing has real consequences**: "War" vs. "conflict" vs. "operation" shapes public understanding
3. **Collaborative knowledge production under stress**: How does consensus-based epistemology function when consensus is impossible?
4. **Global South perspectives**: ICA 2026 theme—whose knowledge counts in English Wikipedia?

## 2. Theoretical Framework

### 2.1 Framing Theory (Entman, 1993)
Framing involves **selection** and **salience**: highlighting certain aspects of reality to promote particular interpretations. In Wikipedia, framing operates through:
- **Naming**: "War" vs. "conflict" vs. "strikes"
- **Classification**: Who is a "belligerent"?
- **Attribution**: Sources cited as authoritative
- **Prominence**: What appears in the lead section?

### 2.2 Social Construction of Knowledge (Bruckman, 2022)
Wikipedia exemplifies how knowledge is **negotiated** through community processes. But:
- What happens when the community is polarized?
- How do Wikipedia policies (NPOV, RS, UNDUE) get weaponized?
- Is "consensus" possible on contested geopolitics?

### 2.3 Epistemic Injustice (Fricker, 2007)
**Testimonial injustice**: Whose testimony is discounted?
**Hermeneutical injustice**: Whose interpretive frameworks dominate?

Applied to Wikipedia:
- Which sources are deemed "reliable"?
- Whose framing becomes "neutral"?
- Which perspectives are marked as "POV-pushing"?

### 2.4 Discourse-Historical Approach (Wodak, 2001)
CDA that emphasizes:
- **Intertextuality**: How arguments reference Wikipedia policies
- **Interdiscursivity**: How geopolitical frames enter editorial discourse
- **Contextualization**: Historical trajectories of editorial disputes

## 3. Research Questions

### Primary RQ
**How do Wikipedia editors contest geopolitical framing during active conflict, and what patterns emerge across different but interconnected conflicts?**

### Sub-questions

**RQ1 (Computational)**: What are the structural patterns of editorial conflict?
- 1a: Do editors cluster into identifiable "camps" based on revert patterns?
- 1b: How does editing intensity correlate with real-world events?
- 1c: Are there differences in editor experience/tenure between camps?

**RQ2 (Discourse)**: How is framing contested through talk page deliberation?
- 2a: What framing devices (naming, classification, attribution) are most contested?
- 2b: How are Wikipedia policies (NPOV, RS, UNDUE, SYNTHESIS) invoked to justify competing frames?
- 2c: What discursive strategies do editors use to legitimize their perspective?

**RQ3 (Comparative)**: How do editorial dynamics differ between the Gaza war and Iran war?
- 3a: Do the same editors participate in both article clusters?
- 3b: Are certain framing disputes unique to one conflict or shared?
- 3c: How does the trilateral dynamic (US-Israel-Iran) differ from bilateral (Israel-Palestine)?

## 4. Mixed-Methods Design

### Phase 1: Computational Analysis (Quantitative)

#### Data Collection
| Corpus | Articles | Time Period |
|--------|----------|-------------|
| Gaza war cluster | ~50 articles | Oct 2023 – Mar 2026 |
| Iran war cluster | ~30 articles | Jan 2025 – Mar 2026 |

**Data sources:**
- Wikipedia API: Revision history, talk page archives
- XTools: Editor statistics, page statistics
- WikiWho: Attribution of text to editors

#### Analytical Methods

**A. Revert Network Analysis**
- Nodes = editors; edges = mutual reverts
- Detect communities using Louvain algorithm
- Identify "camps" and bridge editors
- Compare to Suh et al. (2007) "Us vs. them" methodology

**B. Temporal Event Analysis**
- Time-series of edit volume
- Correlate with real-world events (strikes, casualties, political statements)
- Identify "surge" periods

**C. Editor Profile Analysis**
- Account age, edit count, prior contentious topic involvement
- Compare profiles of "camp" members
- Detect potential coordinated accounts (following ADL 2025 methodology)

#### Expected Outputs
- Revert network visualizations
- Editor cluster classifications
- Temporal edit patterns

---

### Phase 2: Critical Discourse Analysis (Qualitative)

#### Sampling Strategy
Informed by Phase 1 findings:
1. **Turning points**: Talk page discussions around major editorial decisions
2. **Contested categories**: Discussions where camps clash
3. **Policy invocations**: Threads heavily citing Wikipedia policies

#### Analytical Framework (Fairclough, 1995)

**Level 1: Textual Analysis**
- Lexical choices: "War" vs. "conflict" vs. "strikes"
- Transitivity: Who acts? Who is acted upon?
- Modality: Certainty vs. hedging in claims

**Level 2: Discursive Practice**
- Intertextuality: References to Wikipedia policies, external sources
- Genre: How talk page conventions structure argument
- Presupposition: What is taken for granted?

**Level 3: Social Practice**
- Power relations: Who gets heard? Who gets blocked?
- Ideology: What geopolitical frames underlie editorial positions?
- Historical context: How do editing patterns connect to wider media discourse?

#### Coding Scheme (Preliminary)

| Category | Sub-codes |
|----------|-----------|
| **Framing devices** | Naming, classification, attribution, emphasis |
| **Policy invocations** | NPOV, RS, UNDUE, SYNTHESIS, WEIGHT, BLP |
| **Discursive strategies** | Legitimation, delegitimation, wiki-lawyering, appeals to expertise |
| **Epistemic claims** | Source reliability, factual accuracy, neutrality |
| **Power moves** | Block threats, admin appeals, arbitration references |

#### Expected Outputs
- Taxonomy of framing disputes
- Typology of discursive strategies
- Case studies of key editorial battles

---

### Phase 3: Integration

**Convergent Design** (Creswell & Plano Clark, 2018):
- Computational findings identify *patterns*
- CDA findings explain *mechanisms*
- Integration reveals *how* editorial camps form and contest frames

| Computational Finding | CDA Elaboration |
|----------------------|-----------------|
| Two-camp structure | What ideological positions define camps? |
| Surge during events | How do editors frame breaking news? |
| Policy invocations | How are policies weaponized? |

## 5. Case Selection Rationale

### Gaza War (2023-present)
- **Longest-running**: Established editorial dynamics
- **Well-documented**: ADL 2025 report, Haaretz coverage, arbitration cases
- **Bilateral**: Israel vs. Palestine framing
- **Arbitration**: Extended confirmed protection since 2008

### Iran War (2025-2026)
- **Emerging**: Editorial dynamics still forming
- **Trilateral**: US, Israel, Iran
- **Novel disputes**: "Regime change war," belligerent classification
- **Fresh protection**: EC applied February 2026

### Comparative Value
- Same arbitration regime (Arab-Israeli conflict remedies)
- Overlapping but distinct editor pools
- Different conflict structure (bilateral vs. trilateral)
- Different temporal stage (established vs. emerging)

## 6. Pilot Findings (March 5, 2026)

From preliminary Talk:2026 Iran war analysis:

### A. Naming Dispute
- Article renamed "conflict" → "war" on March 4, 2026
- **CDA potential**: How was "war" justified?

### B. Language Variety as Proxy
- American vs. British English debate
- MOS:TIES invoked: "American English given US is only English-speaking state involved"
- **Interpretation**: Language choice = claim to ownership of narrative

### C. Belligerent Classification
- UK, Turkey contested
- "WP:SYNTHESIS" invoked to exclude
- **Power dynamic**: Being listed as "belligerent" has diplomatic implications

### D. NPOV on Protests
- "Pro-Iran rallies underrepresented" vs. "celebrations more prevalent"
- **Framing**: Whose reactions count as "significant"?

### E. "Regime Change War" Terminology
- Editor requested inclusion in lead
- **Stakes**: Labels the US/Israel as aggressors with specific political intent

## 8. Limitations & Ethical Considerations

- English Wikipedia only (multi-language comparison for future work)
- Cannot definitively attribute coordination without Wikipedia checkuser data
- Real-time conflict means ongoing events may shift patterns

### Ethical Considerations

- No identification of editors beyond public usernames
- Avoid contributing to harassment of editors
- Acknowledge positionality: Researcher perspective inevitably shapes interpretation
