## Research Direction 1: Naming as Framing

### Core Question
**How do Wikipedia editors negotiate terminology for ongoing conflicts, and what does this reveal about competing geopolitical frames?**

### Specific RQs
- RQ1: What terminology disputes emerge on Iran-conflict articles? (war vs. conflict vs. operation vs. strikes)
- RQ2: How do editors justify competing terminology using Wikipedia's policies (NPOV, RS, BRD)?
- RQ3: Do terminology outcomes correlate with which "camp" has more active/experienced editors?

### Theoretical Grounding
- **Framing theory** (Entman, 1993): Naming shapes perception
- **Social construction of knowledge** (Bruckman, 2022): Wikipedia as negotiated reality
- **Agenda-setting** (McCombs & Shaw): What we call things matters

### Why This Matters
The rename from "2026 Iran conflict" to "2026 Iran war" on March 4, 2026 is a concrete framing shift. "War" implies scale, justification, sides. "Conflict" is more neutral. "Strikes" minimizes.

### Data Sources
- Wikipedia revision history (API)
- Talk page discussions
- Requested Moves archives
- Arbitration Committee decisions

### Novel Contribution
First systematic study of how geopolitical naming is negotiated in collaborative knowledge production during active conflict.

## Research Direction 4: Editor Network Analysis

### Core Question
**Do Wikipedia editors form coordinated "camps" on Iran-related articles, and what editing patterns reveal this?**

### Specific RQs
- RQ1: Do editors who edit Iran articles also edit Israel-Palestine articles?
- RQ2: Are there clusters of editors who consistently revert each other?
- RQ3: Do new accounts appear during conflict escalation?

### Methods
- **Co-revert networks**: Nodes = editors, edges = mutual reverts
- **Article co-editing**: Bipartite network of editors × articles
- **Temporal analysis**: New accounts during key events

### Theoretical Grounding
- **Coordinated Inauthentic Behavior** literature
- **Information operations** (Starbird et al., 2019)
- **Wikipedia sockpuppet detection** (Kumar et al., 2017)

### Ethical Consideration
Identifying "camps" ≠ proving coordination. Distinguish organic alignment from orchestration.

## Appendix: Live Examples from Talk:2026 Iran war

### A. The Name Dispute (Framing)
The article was renamed from "2026 Iran conflict" to "2026 Iran war" on **March 4, 2026**. This is a framing shift—"war" implies scale and justification; "conflict" is more neutral.

### B. English Variety Dispute (Whose War?)
Editors debating American vs. British vs. Oxford English:
- **MOS:TIES argument**: "This article should be written in American English given the United States is only English-speaking state directly involved" (Mb2437)
- **Precedent argument**: "The 2025–2026 Iranian protests page has already settled on British English" (ConflictFan)

**Research insight**: The *language variety* dispute is a proxy for **whose perspective dominates**—American involvement vs. British Commonwealth precedent on Iran coverage.

### C. Belligerent Classification (Who Is "Involved"?)
Intense debate over whether UK and Turkey should be listed as belligerents:
- Turkey: NATO surveillance, but denied involvement
- UK: Defensive runs protecting personnel, but "just caught up in it"
- **WP:SYNTHESIS** argument: "U.S. Air Force has been operating Incirlik Air Base since 1955. That doesn't mean Turkey is involved in every war"

**Research insight**: The **belligerent infobox** is a site of intense contestation. Being listed as a "belligerent" has political implications beyond Wikipedia.

### D. NPOV on Protests (Whose Reactions Count?)
Direct NPOV dispute:
> "The lead only includes the informations of opposition celebration rallies, but worldwide pro-Iran/anti-war protests are absent in the info, which is a violation of WP:NPOV and WP:UNDUE"

**Counter-argument**: "The prevalence of pro-Khamenei rallies appear to pale in comparison to those celebrating his death"

**Research insight**: The **comparative prominence** of reactions is contested—whose protests get lead coverage?

### E. "Regime Change War" Terminology
Editor explicitly requested:
> "The section of this being a part of a regime change war (with a reliable source) was removed from the first paragraph, are you able to bring that back?"

**Research insight**: Whether to label this a "regime change war" vs. "military operation" vs. "strikes" is actively contested.

### F. Source Disputes
- Turkish Minute source cited for Turkey's denial
- Sources dated *before* conflict began (Feb 24) challenged for post-conflict claims
- "WP:SYNTHESIS" invoked repeatedly
