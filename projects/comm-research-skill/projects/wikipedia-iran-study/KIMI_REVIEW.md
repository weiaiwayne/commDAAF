# Peer Review: "Whose History? Epistemic Contestation in Wikipedia's Coverage of Middle East Conflicts"

**Reviewer:** Communication Research Journal Reviewer  
**Date:** March 6, 2026  
**Manuscript Version:** Preprint v2.0  

---

## Overall Recommendation: **MINOR REVISION**

This is a methodologically innovative exploratory study that makes a genuine contribution to understanding platform epistemology. The authors demonstrate admirable intellectual honesty in acknowledging limitations and alternative explanations. The manuscript is well-structured and clearly written. With minor revisions to strengthen theoretical framing and clarify methodological choices, this paper should be publishable.

---

## Main Strengths

### 1. Methodological Innovation and Transparency

The multi-model LLM coding protocol represents a genuinely novel methodological contribution. Rather than treating inter-model disagreement as a failure to be corrected through human adjudication, the authors reframe it as a substantive finding about construct validity. This is a sophisticated approach that advances methodological debates in computational communication research. The transparency with which limitations are reported—including the honest admission that most constructs showed poor reliability—is exemplary and should be a model for the field.

### 2. Intellectual Rigor and Epistemic Humility

The authors consistently resist the temptation to overclaim. They present multiple interpretations of their findings (epistemic injustice vs. legitimate quality control), acknowledge when data cannot adjudicate between alternatives, and explicitly note what evidence they lack. This intellectual honesty is refreshing and strengthens rather than weakens the paper's credibility. The discussion of alternative explanations (Section 5.3) is particularly well-developed.

### 3. Timely and Relevant Phenomenon

The study addresses a genuinely important question: how collaborative knowledge platforms mediate understanding of active geopolitical conflicts. With Wikipedia remaining a dominant information source and Middle East conflicts continuing to generate contested narratives, this research has clear practical and scholarly relevance. The comparative design (Iran war vs. Gaza war) offers a useful, if imperfect, opportunity to examine whether patterns are topic-specific or reflect broader platform dynamics.

---

## Main Weaknesses

### 1. Theoretical Framework Underdeveloped

While the paper invokes Fricker's (2007) epistemic injustice framework, the theoretical development remains somewhat thin. Several issues need addressing:

- **Conceptual stretching**: The application of face-to-face interaction concepts (testimonial/hermeneutical injustice) to pseudonymous platform environments requires more theoretical justification. The authors acknowledge this challenge but don't fully resolve it.
- **Missing literature**: The paper would benefit from engagement with platform governance literature (e.g., Gillespie, 2018) and Wikipedia-specific scholarship on editor socialization (e.g., Bryant et al., 2005) to better theorize whether observed patterns represent injustice or legitimate community norms.
- **Construct validity concerns**: The operational definitions (e.g., "testimonial injustice" as explicit credibility challenges based on account characteristics) may not fully capture Fricker's original concept, which emphasizes identity prejudice rather than platform status.

### 2. Sampling Strategy Weakens Generalizability Claims

The purposive sampling of high-conflict articles and contentious talk page threads is defensible for an exploratory study, but the manuscript sometimes drifts toward implying broader patterns without sufficient evidence. For example, statements about "Wikipedia's coverage of Middle East conflicts" (Abstract) suggest generalizability that the sampling strategy cannot support. The authors need to be more consistent in limiting claims to "high-conflict articles" specifically.

### 3. Temporal Asymmetry Insufficiently Addressed

The Iran cluster (1 week) and Gaza cluster (2.5 years) are fundamentally different temporal units, yet the paper attempts comparative analysis. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, they don't fully grapple with its implications:

- The Iran data captures acute conflict dynamics (breaking news, initial framing contests)
- The Gaza data captures chronic conflict dynamics (established narratives, entrenched positions)

These are different phenomena that may not be comparable even with caution. The authors should either limit comparative claims more severely or develop a theoretical rationale for why these different temporal windows illuminate similar underlying processes.

---

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

### Theoretical Development

1. **Engage more deeply with platform governance literature**: Distinguish between "epistemic injustice" (unfair exclusion) and "epistemic governance" (legitimate community regulation). The paper gestures toward this distinction but doesn't develop it theoretically.

2. **Clarify the relationship between platform status and identity**: Fricker's testimonial injustice requires identity prejudice, but EC protection is based on edit count/tenure, not identity. The authors need to either argue that platform status proxies for identity (which requires evidence) or develop a distinct theoretical framework for platform-specific epistemic harms.

3. **Consider incorporating "epistemic bubbles" or "echo chambers" literature**: The finding that 10% of editors appear in both clusters suggests potential epistemic community formation that could be theorized more fully.

### Methodological Clarification

4. **Report model-specific coding patterns**: Table 2 shows aggregate counts, but readers would benefit from knowing which specific excerpts generated disagreement. Were there systematic patterns in what Claude vs. Kimi vs. GLM coded differently? This could illuminate cultural differences in interpreting epistemic injustice constructs.

5. **Justify the three-model selection**: Why these specific models (Claude, GLM-4.7, Kimi)? The rationale should include discussion of their training data differences and why this diversity matters for the research question.

6. **Expand the reliability discussion**: For constructs with negative κ (epistemic dispossession, policy weaponization), what kind of systematic disagreement occurred? Did models have opposing conceptual understandings, or were they applying the same definition differently?

### Presentation and Structure

7. **Strengthen the abstract**: The abstract currently buries the low reliability finding in a subordinate clause. Given that this is a key methodological contribution, it deserves more prominence.

8. **Reconsider the "Findings" organization**: The current structure (RQ1 → RQ2 → RQ3) is clear, but the integration section (3.5) promises connections between network position and discourse patterns that aren't fully delivered in the findings. Either develop these connections or remove the promise.

9. **Add a table comparing alternative explanations**: The discussion of alternative explanations (Section 5.3) is strong but could be summarized in a table showing: (a) what evidence would support each interpretation, (b) what evidence the study provides, and (c) what evidence remains needed.

### Minor Issues

10. **Citation completeness**: The references section is abbreviated. Full bibliographic information is needed for all citations (e.g., Ajmani et al., 2024; Kwok, 2025 appear to be preprints without full citation details).

11. **Define abbreviations on first use**: "EC" (Extended Confirmed) is used before being defined (appears in Abstract/Introduction but defined in Section 2.3).

12. **Clarify "reverter" vs. "reverted" classification**: The 0.3%/0.0% "Bidirectional" rates seem surprisingly low. Is this because most editors do one or the other, or is the classification threshold creating artificial separation? A sensitivity analysis or discussion of classification criteria would help.

---

## Questions for the Authors

1. **Construct validity**: Do you believe epistemic injustice concepts are ultimately applicable to Wikipedia, or do your findings suggest that platform-native constructs are needed? Your conclusion hints at the latter but doesn't commit.

2. **Practical implications**: Given your findings, what would you advise Wikipedia administrators? If the answer is "nothing yet" (which is defensible), what further research would be needed before recommendations could be made?

3. **Model bias**: Did you observe any systematic patterns in which model was more likely to code particular constructs? For example, did Chinese-trained models interpret "hermeneutical injustice" differently than Western-trained models in ways that illuminate cultural assumptions embedded in the construct?

---

## Summary

This is a thoughtful, methodologically innovative exploratory study that makes genuine contributions to understanding platform epistemology. The authors demonstrate exemplary intellectual honesty and transparency about limitations. The manuscript would benefit from deeper theoretical development, more consistent limiting of generalizability claims, and fuller exploration of what multi-model disagreement reveals about construct validity. With these revisions, this paper should be suitable for publication.

**Recommendation: Minor Revision**
