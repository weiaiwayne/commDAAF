## 2. Updated Theoretical Framework

### Anchor: Epistemic Injustice (Fricker, 2007)
Core concepts:
- **Testimonial injustice**: Speaker's credibility discounted due to identity prejudice
- **Hermeneutical injustice**: Systemic absence of interpretive resources for lived experience

### Recent Extensions (2023-2026)

#### A. Epistemic Injustice in CSCW (Ajmani et al., 2024)
*arXiv:2407.03477 — Directly relevant to Wikipedia*

Key contributions:
- **Technology-mediated epistemic harm**: Policies on knowledge (verifiability, reliable sources) carry normative repercussions
- **Wikipedia-specific example**: Trans individuals cannot be "knowers" of their own identity under verifiability rules
- **Intersectional lens**: Epistemic injustice unifies CSCW research across identity, power, and platform design

**Application to our study**: Wikipedia's NPOV, RS, and UNDUE policies are *epistemic policies* that determine whose framing counts as "neutral."

#### B. Epistemic Dispossession (Kwok, 2025)
*Expanding Iris Marion Young's five faces of oppression*

New concept:
- **Epistemic dispossession**: When socio-technical infrastructures systematically extract, appropriate, and redistribute epistemic resources in ways that benefit dominant groups
- Extends beyond individual-level injustice to **structural epistemic harm**

**Application to our study**: Whose geopolitical frames get "dispossessed" through Wikipedia's consensus mechanisms? How do experienced editors appropriate newcomers' epistemic contributions?

#### C. Epistemic Justice Framework (Cummings et al., 2023, 2025)
*Sustainable Development journal — 8 pillars*

Updated pillars:
1. Recognition
2. Participation
3. Distribution
4. Capabilities
5. Correction
6. Representation
7. Transformation
8. **Anti-epistemicide** (new in 2025 update)

**Epistemicide**: The destruction of knowledge systems, particularly non-Western/non-dominant ways of knowing

**Application to our study**: Do Wikipedia editing wars constitute a form of "epistemic violence" against certain geopolitical perspectives? Is there systematic epistemicide of Iranian, Palestinian, or Global South framings?

#### D. Onto-Epistemicide (Patin et al., 2024)
*Information science literature*

New term:
- Combines **ontological** (ways of being) and **epistemological** (ways of knowing) destruction
- Goes beyond content removal to challenge how existence itself is framed

**Application to our study**: Naming disputes ("war" vs. "conflict") are onto-epistemological—they don't just describe events, they constitute how those events exist in the historical record.

#### E. Global Epistemic Inequality (2026)
*Decolonial turn in platform studies*

Key insight from bibliometric analysis:
- 2024-2025 shows "clearer turn towards decolonial critique, community-based standpoints and vocabularies of epistemic justice"
- Emphasis on marginalized agency, culturally grounded identity-making

**Application to our study**: English Wikipedia as site of "epistemic colonialism"—whose language variety? whose sources? whose framing?

## 4. Research Questions (Refined)

### Primary RQ
**How do Wikipedia editors engage in epistemic contestation during geopolitical conflict, and what forms of epistemic injustice emerge?**

### Sub-questions

**RQ1 (Computational)**: What are the structural patterns of epistemic contestation?
- 1a: Do editors form identifiable "epistemic camps" based on revert/collaboration patterns?
- 1b: How does contestation intensity correlate with real-world conflict events?
- 1c: What role does editor tenure/experience play in whose edits survive?

**RQ2 (Discourse)**: How is epistemic injustice enacted through talk page deliberation?
- 2a: What forms of testimonial injustice appear? (source credibility, editor credibility)
- 2b: What forms of hermeneutical injustice appear? (available naming, categorization)
- 2c: How are Wikipedia policies weaponized as instruments of epistemic control?

**RQ3 (Comparative)**: How does epistemic contestation differ between Iran war and Gaza war articles?
- 3a: Do the same epistemic patterns appear in both corpora?
- 3b: Does the trilateral structure (US-Israel-Iran) produce different injustice patterns than bilateral (Israel-Palestine)?
- 3c: Is there evidence of systematic epistemicide of specific geopolitical perspectives?

## 5. Methods

### Phase 1: Data Collection (Week 1-2)

- Event articles: Strikes, assassinations, retaliations

**Gaza war cluster (50 articles)**:
- Core articles: Israel-Hamas war, 7 October attacks
- Event articles: Major operations, incidents
- Actor articles: Hamas, IDF, key figures
- Thematic: Humanitarian crisis, genocide debate, hostages

#### Data Sources
- Wikipedia API: Full revision history (past week for Iran, full for Gaza)
- Talk page archives
- WikiWho: Text attribution
- XTools: Editor statistics

---

### Phase 2: Computational Analysis (Week 3-5)

#### A. Revert Network Analysis
- **Method**: Build editor × editor network where edges = reverts
- **Analysis**: Community detection (Louvain), identify camps
- **Metrics**: Modularity, bridge editors, camp size asymmetry

#### B. Temporal Event Analysis
- **Method**: Time-series of edit volume
- **Analysis**: Correlate with real-world events (strikes, deaths, statements)
- **Visualization**: Event-aligned edit heatmaps

#### C. Edit Survival Analysis
- **Method**: Track which edits persist vs. get reverted
- **Analysis**: Model survival by editor tenure, camp membership, content type
- **Question**: Whose knowledge survives the editing process?

#### D. Policy Invocation Frequency
- **Method**: Regex extraction of policy citations (WP:NPOV, WP:RS, etc.)
- **Analysis**: Who invokes which policies? In what contexts?

---

### Phase 3: LLM-Assisted Critical Discourse Analysis (Week 5-8)

#### Coding Scheme

| Category | Sub-codes | Example |
|----------|-----------|---------|
| **Testimonial injustice** | Source discounting, editor discounting, expertise claims | "That source is Iranian state media" |
| **Hermeneutical injustice** | Naming disputes, categorization, missing frames | "This should be called a 'conflict' not 'war'" |
| **Epistemic dispossession** | Consensus weaponization, experience asymmetry | "You need 500 edits to edit this" |
| **Policy weaponization** | NPOV, RS, UNDUE, SYNTHESIS invocations | "This violates WP:UNDUE" |
| **Discursive strategies** | Legitimation, delegitimation, appeals to neutrality | "Wikipedia is not a soapbox" |

#### Multi-Model Validation (AgentAcademy Protocol)

**Step 1**: Claude codes 100 talk page excerpts
**Step 2**: GLM-4.7 independently codes same excerpts
**Step 3**: Kimi K2.5 independently codes same excerpts
**Step 4**: Calculate inter-model reliability (κ)
**Step 5**: Human adjudication for disagreements
**Step 6**: Apply validated scheme to full corpus

#### Sampling Strategy
Informed by Phase 2:
1. **High-conflict threads**: Discussions with most reverts
2. **Policy disputes**: Threads citing Wikipedia policies
3. **Naming battles**: Terminology negotiation
4. **Source debates**: Reliability discussions
