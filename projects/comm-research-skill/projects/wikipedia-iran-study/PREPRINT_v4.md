## Abstract

When Iran struck Israel in February 2026, Wikipedia editors immediately began fighting over words. Should the IRGC news agency count as a source? Is "assassination" or "killing" the right term for a targeted strike? Who gets to decide? This mixed-methods study examines 100 Wikipedia articles on the 2026 Iran war and Israel-Hamas conflict, analyzing 28,006 revisions and 276 talk page debates. We find that a small minority of editors (41-43%) do all the reverting while the majority get reverted—patterns consistent with prior Wikipedia research. Through qualitative analysis of talk page discussions, we show how editors fight not just about facts, but about *whose facts count*. Source hierarchy debates—battles over whether Al Jazeera or Times of Israel, IRGC media or Western intelligence, should be trusted—emerged as the most recognizable form of conflict across three culturally diverse AI coders. We propose that Wikipedia operates through **credential-based epistemic authority**: your edit count and account age matter more than who you are. This is fundamentally different from traditional forms of prejudice, but raises its own questions about who gets to tell history.

**Keywords:** Wikipedia, knowledge production, edit wars, source reliability, Middle East conflicts

## 1. Introduction

> "The highest number thrown around is 12k, which has been largely reported only by Iran International and CBC. This page loses any credibility reporting imaginary numbers."
> 
> "Certain 'alleged' propagandists have flooded all Wikipedia pages concerning the riots and are now just magically inventing casualty numbers out of thin air. It went from 70 to 200 to 2000 to 12000 to 20000 and now 30000 killed in only a few days."

This exchange, from the talk page of "2026 Iran massacres," captures what's at stake in Wikipedia edit wars. It's not just about numbers—it's about whose numbers count, which sources can be trusted, and who has the standing to make that call.

Wikipedia is where millions of people learn about contested events in real time. During the first week of the 2026 Iran war, Wikipedia articles were updated thousands of times as editors fought to define what was happening. "Assassination" or "killing"? "War" or "conflict"? "Reliable" or "propaganda"?

This study examines these battles over words and sources. We ask: **What patterns emerge in Wikipedia's coverage of geopolitical conflicts, and who gets to decide whose history is told?**

### 1.1 What We Did

We collected data from 100 high-conflict Wikipedia articles—50 about the 2026 Iran war and 50 about the Israel-Hamas conflict. We tracked who reverted whom (deleting others' edits) and we read through hundreds of talk page discussions where editors argued about sources, terminology, and credibility.

To analyze these debates, we used three different AI systems trained in different cultural contexts: Claude (American), GLM-4.7 (Chinese), and Kimi K2.5 (Chinese). When all three agreed on what they saw in a discussion, we treated that as a reliable finding. When they disagreed, we treated that disagreement itself as informative—some concepts work across cultures, others don't.

### 1.2 What We Found

Three main findings:

1. **A small minority controls the editing.** About 41% of editors do all the reverting (deleting others' work), while 59% get reverted but never revert back. The top reverters are *never* reverted themselves. This matches what other researchers have found about controversial Wikipedia articles.

2. **Source fights are the main battle.** The clearest form of conflict is about which sources can be trusted. Can you cite IRGC-affiliated media for Iranian casualties? Is Al Jazeera reliable? What about Israeli government sources? These debates follow geopolitical lines and were recognized as conflicts by all three AI systems.

3. **Wikipedia authority is about credentials, not identity.** Unlike traditional prejudice (where people are dismissed because of who they are), Wikipedia dismisses people based on what they've done—or haven't done. Don't have 500 edits? You can't even participate in the discussion. This is different from racism or sexism, but it still raises questions about who gets excluded.

## 2. What's Really at Stake: The Source Hierarchy Wars

The most striking pattern in our data wasn't about overtly political disputes—it was about the seemingly technical question of which sources count as "reliable."

### 2.1 "Can I Use Tasnim News?"

Consider this exchange from the "2026 Iran war" talk page:

> **Editor A:** Can I use the Tasnim News Agency as a source for the Iranian claims of US casualties, even though it is not a reliable source?
> 
> **Editor B:** I feel like you could, but you'd have to format it like: "**Iranian claim**: [number]"
> 
> **Editor C:** Adding "Biased"? This will be very unencyclopedic. "Iranian claim:" is enough.
> 
> **Editor D:** "Claim" should also be avoided in most cases.
> 
> **Editor A:** Why was it then removed? While Tasnim News may be an unreliable source for general news, it is still the semi-official news website of the IRGC. I think that, when it comes to what Iran or its military *claims* to have done, it can be used as a source.
> 
> **Editor E:** If it is not a reliable source, you cannot use it to validate unconfirmed claims. Full stop.

This is a fight about epistemology dressed up as a fight about procedure. The underlying question: Should Iranian state media be treated differently than, say, Pentagon press releases? Wikipedia's formal rules say nothing about national origin, but in practice, editors invoke "reliability" to sort sources into hierarchies.

### 2.2 "Information from USA and Israel is Just as Riddled with Propaganda"

Some editors explicitly challenged the double standard:

> **Editor A:** The claim of children killed comes from Iranian state media. Either it should be removed or be qualified with "Per Iran" or "Per Iranian State Media."
> 
> **Editor B:** Why? Information from USA and Israel is just as riddled with Propaganda as that from Iran. I say we post a notice that Information and News from ALL parties involved might be misleading and Propaganda. That sounds better eh?

This exchange reveals a deeper conflict: Is "reliability" a neutral assessment of journalistic standards, or a proxy for geopolitical alignment? Different editors have sharply different answers.

### 2.3 The Al Jazeera vs. Times of Israel Wars

In Gaza-related articles, source fights often centered on Middle Eastern news outlets:

> "Al Jazeera is reliable per WP:RSN. Times of Israel laundered lies about Shifa."

Here, an editor invokes Wikipedia's Reliable Sources Noticeboard (RSN) as precedent while simultaneously accusing an Israeli outlet of fabrication. The debate becomes recursive: which sources are reliable enough to adjudicate which sources are reliable?

**Why this matters:** All three of our AI coders—trained in American and Chinese contexts—recognized these source hierarchy debates as conflicts. This suggests that fights over *whose sources count* are a cross-culturally recognizable form of epistemic contestation.

## 3. The "Assassination" vs. "Killing" Wars: When Words Become Weapons

Beyond source fights, editors battled intensely over terminology.

### 3.1 "Assassination Carries Significant Negative Imputation"

When Iranian Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei was killed in a targeted strike, the article naming became a flashpoint:

> **Editor A (proposing rename):** "Assassination" is a term that carries significant negative imputation. Longstanding consensus supported by numerous discussions is that it is not used in wiki-voice unless the term is being widely used within independent reliable sources.
> 
> **Editor B:** Oppose—it was an assassination and the attack was specifically targeting him and was successful, so the current naming is appropriate.
> 
> **Editor C:** Strong oppose. He *was* definitely assassinated not only by definition, but also sources call it this way, so can't be "original research." This requested move is laughable.
> 
> **Editor D:** The term "assassination" carries significant negative imputation and should be avoided unless it is being widely used in reliable sources. This is well established over numerous discussions including the various debates over whether or not to refer to the multiple attempts on the life of Donald Trump as assassination attempts.

What looks like a procedural dispute about Wikipedia naming conventions is actually a battle over historical framing. "Assassination" implies illegitimacy; "killing" is more neutral. By invoking precedent (the Trump assassination attempts debates), editors translate political disputes into procedural ones.

### 3.2 "Can You Not Handle the Truth?"

Frustrated editors sometimes broke through the procedural veneer:

> **Editor:** According to a UN inquiry, Israel is committing a genocide in Gaza. Why is it so hard to mention here? Can you not handle the truth? Please add it in the article.
> 
> **Response:** If a UN body has formally accused Israel of genocide, it can be included—but only with reliable sources and neutral wording. Wikipedia requires verifiable citations and balanced coverage, especially for serious claims.
> 
> **Response 2:** We can *attribute* the fact that the UN inquiry said that, assuming it has decent secondary coverage, but UN inquiries don't automagically establish uncontested truth such that we can say it in the article voice.

The frustrated editor's "Can you not handle the truth?" expresses a common sentiment: that Wikipedia's procedural requirements function as barriers to stating what (from their perspective) is obvious. The responses illustrate how experienced editors redirect political passion into procedural channels.

## 4. Who Gets to Participate? The Credential Barrier

One of the most striking patterns wasn't in what editors argued about—it was in who was *allowed* to argue.

### 4.1 "This Discussion Format Is Not Available to Non-EC Editors"

Many high-conflict articles are under "Extended Confirmed" (EC) protection, meaning only editors with 500+ edits and 30+ days of account age can edit. Some talk page requests include explicit gatekeeping:

> **Admin response to edit request:** User is not extended-confirmed; not a properly formatted edit request.

The edit request was denied not because it was wrong, but because the requester lacked credentials.

### 4.2 The AI Detection Twist

In one fascinating case, an edit request was rejected *because it appeared to be AI-generated*:

> **Editor:** Delete the sentence stating that "In Spring of 1948, 30,000 Iranians in Tehran gathered to protest against the establishment of Israel." The claim appears to rely on a single recent secondary source and does not appear to be supported by contemporaneous historical reporting.
> 
> **Admin response:** Not done: your request appears to have been generated by a large language model. Please do not use AI chatbots to edit Wikipedia.
> 
> **Third editor:** LLM-created or not, this is a fully legitimate discussion of a sentence in our article, that may or may not be true. In this time of disinformation, it is very important that everything in Wikipedia is as true as it can be.

The third editor's intervention reveals a tension: the substance of the request was valid, but it was rejected based on *how* it was written. Credentials trump content.

### 4.3 The Reverter Elite

Our network analysis revealed a striking pattern: a small group of editors who revert others but are *never* reverted themselves. These are the de facto gatekeepers—their edits stick while others' get removed.

| Cluster | Top Reverter | Reverts | Never Reverted |
|---------|--------------|---------|----------------|
| Iran | Mandruss | 31 | Yes |
| Iran | Space4Time3Continuum2x | 30 | Yes |
| Gaza | Raskolnikov.Rev | 21 | Yes |
| Gaza | Abo Yemen | 19 | Yes |

These editors aren't necessarily wrong to revert vandalism and bad-faith edits. But the asymmetry is notable: authority flows one way.

## 5. Credential-Based Authority: A Different Kind of Exclusion

### 5.1 Not Prejudice, But Not Neutral Either

Traditional accounts of prejudice focus on identity—people are dismissed because of their race, gender, class. On Wikipedia, people are dismissed based on credentials: edit count, account age, policy fluency.

This is importantly different from traditional prejudice:
- It's based on behavior, not identity
- It can be earned through participation
- It's explicitly codified in policy

But it still creates barriers. An area expert who doesn't have Wikipedia tenure can be overruled by an editor with 10,000 edits and no subject expertise. An anonymous IP address can have their edit reverted simply for being anonymous.

### 5.2 When Meritocracy Becomes Gatekeeping

We propose thinking about this as a continuum:

**Legitimate governance** → Credentials track good judgment, and experienced editors improve article quality.

**Exclusionary credentialism** → Credentials become barriers that exclude people with valid knowledge.

When does one become the other? We suggest three warning signs:

1. **Credential inflation:** When the threshold for participation (500 edits) exceeds what's needed to demonstrate good faith
2. **Topic capture:** When a small group of credentialed editors controls contested articles indefinitely
3. **Expertise-credential mismatch:** When platform credentials fail to track topic-specific knowledge

### 5.3 The Policy Alphabet Soup

Established editors speak a specialized language: WP:RS, WP:NPOV, WP:SYNTH, WP:BLP, WP:UNDUE. Fluency in this acronym system functions as a credential marker. Consider this exchange:

> "Per WP:ABOUTSELF, this can apply, but the WP:V requirements and WP:BLP concerns suggest we need WP:RS before including WP:CLAIM material."

To newcomers, this is incomprehensible. To insiders, it's the language of authority.

## 6. Methods: How We Studied This

### 6.1 Data Collection

We analyzed two clusters of articles:

| Cluster | Articles | Revisions | Reverts | Talk Page Text |
|---------|----------|-----------|---------|----------------|
| Iran War (2026) | 50 | 16,842 | 575 | 890K chars |
| Gaza War (2023-26) | 50 | 11,164 | 390 | 690K chars |
| **Total** | 100 | 28,006 | 965 | 1.58M chars |

These are intentionally high-conflict articles. Our findings describe *contested Wikipedia*, not Wikipedia in general.

### 6.2 Revert Network Analysis

We built networks showing who reverted whom. We classified editors as:
- **Reverters:** Revert others but aren't reverted themselves
- **Reverted:** Get reverted but don't revert others
- **Bidirectional:** Both revert and are reverted (rare)

### 6.3 Multi-Model Qualitative Coding

We extracted 276 talk page excerpts and asked three AI systems to code them:

| Model | Origin | Why We Used It |
|-------|--------|----------------|
| Claude 3.5 | American | Western perspective, safety-focused |
| GLM-4.7 | Chinese | Different cultural training |
| Kimi K2.5 | Chinese | Consumer-focused, multilingual |

**The key insight:** When all three models agreed, we treated the finding as cross-culturally robust. When they disagreed, we treated the *disagreement* as informative—it reveals that a concept is culturally contested.

### 6.4 What Worked and What Didn't

| Construct | Agreement (κ) | Status |
|-----------|---------------|--------|
| **Source Hierarchy** | **0.47** | Cross-culturally validated |
| Hermeneutical Injustice | 0.18 | Culturally contested |
| Testimonial Injustice | 0.09 | Near-chance agreement |
| Naming Dispute | 0.09 | Definition too vague |

Only "source hierarchy"—debates about which sources are trustworthy based on national/ideological origin—achieved reliable agreement across models. The philosophical concepts (hermeneutical injustice, testimonial injustice) were interpreted very differently by Western vs. Chinese-trained models.

**What this means:** Some concepts travel across cultures; others don't. Academic jargon isn't automatically universal.

## 7. The Bigger Picture

### 7.1 Wikipedia as Real-Time History-Writing

During the first week of the 2026 Iran war, Wikipedia was updated thousands of times. These articles weren't just recording history—they were *shaping* how millions of people understood events in real time.

The editors fighting over "assassination" vs. "killing" weren't having an abstract debate. They were deciding how a historical event would be framed for future readers.

### 7.2 The Source Hierarchy Question

Our clearest finding is that source hierarchy debates—fights over which news outlets count as "reliable"—are the primary battleground. These fights follow geopolitical lines:

- Iranian state media vs. Western sources
- Al Jazeera vs. Israeli outlets
- Government sources vs. independent media

Wikipedia's formal policies claim to assess "reliability" based on editorial standards, not political alignment. But in practice, reliability judgments track geopolitics.

### 7.3 Credentials as a Double-Edged Sword

Wikipedia's credential system (edit counts, EC status, policy fluency) serves real purposes: it filters out vandalism, rewards experience, and provides some quality control. But it also creates barriers that can exclude legitimate voices.

The question isn't whether credentials should matter—it's whether the current thresholds and mechanisms appropriately balance quality against participation.

---

## 8. Limitations

**We studied controversial articles.** These findings describe edit wars on geopolitical topics, not typical Wikipedia editing.

**We didn't analyze revert content.** We know *that* people were reverted, not *whether* the reverts were justified. Some reverts remove vandalism; others remove legitimate contributions.

**Three AI models isn't exhaustive.** More culturally diverse models would strengthen the validation approach.

**We didn't talk to editors.** This is analysis of public talk pages, not ethnographic research into editor motivations.

---

## 9. Conclusion

Wikipedia's talk pages reveal intense battles over whose sources count, whose words get used, and whose voice gets heard. These fights happen in procedural language—"per WP:RS," "not properly formatted"—but they're fundamentally about power.

We introduced the concept of **credential-based epistemic authority** to describe how Wikipedia allocates that power. Unlike traditional prejudice based on who you are, Wikipedia authority is based on what you've done. This is arguably more meritocratic—but it still creates hierarchies that can exclude legitimate voices.

Our multi-model coding approach offers a methodological contribution: using AI systems from different cultural contexts to test whether academic concepts are universal or culturally specific. Source hierarchy debates work across cultures; more philosophical constructs don't.

**The takeaway:** Wikipedia isn't a neutral mirror of reality. It's a site of ongoing struggle over whose history gets told. Understanding these dynamics matters—because Wikipedia is where much of the world learns about contested events.
