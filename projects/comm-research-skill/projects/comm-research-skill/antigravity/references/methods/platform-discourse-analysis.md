# Platform Discourse Analysis

*Added from Wikipedia Epistemic Authority study (March 2026)*

## Overview

Thick description of platform interactions (talk pages, comment threads, moderation logs) to reveal power dynamics, gatekeeping, and epistemic authority structures. Goes beyond content analysis to examine *how* participants establish authority and exclude others.

## When to Use

- Analyzing Wikipedia talk pages, Reddit mod logs, forum debates
- Understanding how platforms mediate knowledge production
- Studying gatekeeping and exclusion dynamics
- Examining procedural discourse as power (policy invocation, credential displays)

## Theoretical Grounding

**Foucault (1972)**: Platform policies as "discursive regimes" that determine what can be said, who can speak, and what counts as legitimate knowledge.

**Bourdieu (1984)**: Platform credentials (edit count, karma, tenure) as "cultural capital" that can be converted into participation rights and influence.

**Geertz (1973)**: "Thick description" — not just what happened, but the webs of meaning actors construct.

## Key Patterns to Look For

### 1. Source Hierarchy Debates
Arguments over which sources are "reliable" that encode geopolitical/ideological positioning.

**Example pattern:**
> "Tasnim News is unreliable" vs "Why is Tasnim unreliable but NED-funded HRANA reliable?"

**What it reveals:** "Reliability" is not neutral—it sorts sources into hierarchies along political lines.

### 2. Policy Weaponization
Invoking platform rules to dismiss without substantive engagement.

**Example pattern:**
> "Per WP:RS, this doesn't meet reliability standards." (No engagement with content)

**What it reveals:** Policy fluency as gatekeeping credential; procedural dismissal as power move.

### 3. Credential-Based Exclusion
Participation denied based on platform status rather than argument quality.

**Example pattern:**
> "User is not extended-confirmed; not a properly formatted edit request."

**What it reveals:** Credential systems create barriers that may exclude valid contributions.

### 4. Naming Disputes as Proxy Wars
Fights over terminology that encode framing battles.

**Example pattern:**
> "Assassination" vs "Killing" — carries imputation of illegitimacy

**What it reveals:** Technical disputes often mask political struggles.

### 5. The "Alphabet Soup" as Insider Marker
Policy acronyms (WP:RS, WP:NPOV, WP:BLP) as signals of legitimate participation.

**Example pattern:**
> "Per WP:ABOUTSELF, the WP:V requirements and WP:BLP concerns suggest..."

**What it reveals:** Mastery of procedural code signals insider status.

## Sampling Strategy

### Purposive sampling targeting:
1. **High-conflict threads** — Multiple reversions or heated exchanges
2. **Policy invocation threads** — Explicit rule citation
3. **Gatekeeping moments** — Participation denied/challenged
4. **Naming/terminology disputes** — Article titles, terminology

### Volume:
- Minimum 50 excerpts for exploratory
- 200+ for systematic analysis
- Include full exchanges, not isolated comments

## Probing Questions

1. **What platform are you analyzing?** (policies differ)
2. **What credential systems exist?** (edit count, karma, admin status)
3. **What protection/moderation levels exist?** (who can participate?)
4. **What policies govern disputes?** (get the acronym dictionary)
5. **What's the geopolitical context?** (for source hierarchy analysis)

## Analytical Protocol

### 1. Immersion
Read extensively before coding. Understand the platform's culture and procedures.

### 2. Extract Full Exchanges
Don't cherry-pick single comments. Extract the full back-and-forth.

### 3. Identify Structural Positions
Who has credentials? Who's an outsider? Map to network positions if available.

### 4. Code for Discursive Moves
- Authority claims ("I've been editing this for 5 years")
- Credential displays (policy citations, experience)
- Dismissals (procedural, substantive)
- Challenges (to sources, to standing, to credentials)

### 5. Thick Description
Write interpretive memos explaining not just WHAT was said but WHAT IT MEANS in context.

## Writing Up: Thick Description Style

❌ Thin: "Editor A challenged Editor B's source."

✅ Thick: "Editor A's challenge invoked WP:RS—the sacred text of Wikipedia reliability doctrine—without engaging the source's content. This procedural dismissal allowed A to reject B's contribution without substantive argument, deploying policy fluency as a gatekeeping credential. B, an IP address lacking insider markers, had no procedural countermove available."

## Integration with Quantitative Methods

Platform discourse analysis is powerful when **integrated with network analysis**:

1. Build network from interactions (reverts, replies, mentions)
2. Classify structural positions (central vs peripheral, reverter vs reverted)
3. Sample discourse from different positions
4. Analyze whether structural position predicts discursive strategy

Example finding: "Editors in 'reverter' positions invoke policy more frequently than those in 'reverted' positions, suggesting policy fluency is both cause and effect of structural power."

## Pitfalls

- ❌ Analyzing isolated comments without exchange context
- ❌ Ignoring platform-specific rules and culture
- ❌ Treating all participants as equally positioned
- ❌ Describing without interpreting (thin description)
- ❌ Imposing external frameworks without attending to emic meanings
