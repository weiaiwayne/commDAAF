Meeting Summary
The Committee met in Parliament with the Department of Higher Education and Training, the Media Information and Communications Technology Sector Education and Training Authority (MICT SETA) and the Tshwane North Technical and Vocational Education and Training (TVET) College, to discuss matters related to the state of governance, administration, teaching and learning.
The MICT SETA reported several notable achievements, including a 94% performance rating, three consecutive clean audits, and one unqualified audit with no material misstatements or findings. However, Members expressed dissatisfaction regarding the payment of R1.6 million for subsistence and transport (S&T) and the board fees for the chairperson of the MICT SETA board. Many Members felt the amount was excessive, particularly because S&T accounted for a significant portion of this payment.
In response, the SETA and the DHET provided a detailed breakdown and justification for these high costs. They attributed the expenses to the wide geographical distribution of board members and the challenges associated with holding virtual meetings. The SETA board explained that virtual meetings had proved to be unmanageable and chaotic, with instances of confidential documents being leaked to the public during meetings.
The Tshwane North TVET College (TNC) presented its readiness for the 2025 academic year. Members expressed concern over three projects that were currently at a standstill, despite the fact that millions of rands had already been paid to service providers. One project had received R2.8 million, another R3 million, and a third project R416 000, but no work had been completed on any of them. They were dissatisfied with the College's decision to purchase land intended for skills development from the City of Tshwane for R3.2 million. The College had opposed this move, arguing that it should have been able to acquire the land at no cost since it was meant for public purposes.
Members questioned TNC about its efforts to implement consequence management and blacklist service providers who failed to fulfil their contractual obligations, resulting in monetary losses for the College. Several projects had faced delays due to the termination of contracts, leading Members to wonder whether risk assessments and feasibility studies had been conducted before these contracts were awarded. TNC assured Members that most projects were currently underway, and had recently been monitored and evaluated by officials from the Department and Treasury.
Meeting report
Chairperson's opening remarks
The Chairperson welcomed everyone present, acknowledged those who had sent their apologies, and allowed everyone to introduce themselves. Apologies were received from the Minister and the Director-General, who were attending to an urgent matter at the University of Fort Hare. The Chairperson also announced that Mr T Louw (ANC), a former Member of the Committee, had been deployed to the Northern Cape legislature, and was no longer part of the Committee.
He advised the Media and Information Communication Technology (MICT) Sector Education and Training Authority (Seta) and the Tshwane North TVET College to focus their presentations on their operations, issues related to governance, administration, and the academic projects. The Committee aimed to engage with entities, not to confront them, but to understand their work and the challenges they face to determine how the legislative body could support and enhance their efforts.
The rate of unemployment in South Africa was unacceptable, with a significant number of young people not engaged in employment, education, or training. There were Setas whose primary objective was to skill, upskill, and reskill South Africans for the workplace, and the Committee wanted to understand what actions had been taken in this regard. The MICT SETA was expected to present its annual performance plan soon, and Members were eager to see the targets it intended to achieve. If they claimed to have met 100% of their targets, the Committee expected convincing evidence of the impact of each target achieved. For instance, if SETA aimed to teach robotics, Members wanted to see how this would positively affect ordinary young people in rural areas and townships. They were looking for tangible impacts on young lives.
The same standards applied to the TVET colleges. When they report on enrolment achievements, Members wanted to know how many graduates had found employment to assess the relevance of the courses offered. There was no time to provide training that did not lead to job opportunities -- the goal was to ensure graduates were absorbed into the South African economy, despite its challenges.
Acting Director-General's remarks
Mr Sam Zungu, Acting Director General, Department of Higher Education and Training (DHET), expressed his gratitude to the Committee for their unwavering support in addressing the pressing challenges within the sector. The Committee’s commitment continued to drive meaningful transformation and equitable access to education.
The proceedings would begin with a briefing from the MICT Seta, which would provide insights into its governance framework, administrative systems, and related matters. This engagement would help identify and examine best practices and potential improvements.
The Committee would also hear from Tshwane North TVET College regarding governance, teaching and learning, as well as general administration within the institution. Input would also be gathered from the student body and organised labour unions.
The MICT SETA had received a clean audit opinion, with no significant issues reported. Its financial statements complied with the relevant standards and the provisions of the Public Finance Management Act (PFMA). No major weaknesses were identified during the review period. The DHET was dedicated to ensuring that its entities adhere to all governance matters, allowing a focus on the real issues, particularly in the TVET sector.
MICT SETA briefing
Board chairperson's remarks
Mr Simphiwe Thobela, Chairperson: MICT SETA board, provided an overview of the Seta, and highlighted that there were currently two vacancies on the board. Critical policies had been established to limit Seta's administrative commitments to no more than 10%. This policy was recommended for all Setas, as exceeding this limit negatively impacted their operations.
The board had developed and adopted a rural development strategy, which was supported by a video featuring testimonials about its impact. They had successfully reduced the dropout rate of beneficiaries to less than 2%, down from just above 4% when they took over the board.
All senior managers had been permanently appointed, achieving a perfect gender balance of 50% for both men and women. The MICT SETA had reported a positive upward performance trend, with current results showing a 94% performance compared to 70% previously. They had also achieved three clean audits, along with one unqualified audit that had no material findings.
The primary challenge to address was the cap on the 10% administrative fee, as the demand for services was significantly higher than available resources. Mr Thobela encouraged the National Student Financial Aid Scheme (NSFAS) and the Unemployment Insurance Fund (UIF) to contribute to their efforts in delivering these essential skills.
It was important to note that while 96% of small, medium and micro enterprises (SMMEs) operated in the sector, they only accounted for 10% of the industry's value, with larger companies holding the remaining 90%.
Mr Matome Madibana, Chief Executive Officer (CEO), MICT Seta, delivered the entity’s presentation, which covered matters related to governance, administration and others. On governance, the MICT SETA board had 13 members, with two vacancies that had been submitted to the Minister.
Allegations related to board chairperson's fees
Out of R1.6 million, R621 000 was related to actual board fees earned for the 2023/24 financial year, while R1.051 million was related to travel and accommodation. The Chairperson did not receive a subsistence and travel allowance during the 2023/24 financial year. The travel and accommodation expense of the Chairperson was attributed to the fact that he resides outside Gauteng and travels from Kokstad, which is southwest of Durban and Flagstaff in the Eastern Cape, to attend MICT SETA meetings and engagements.
Special Investigation Unit (SIU) report on MICT SETA
The SIU had completed its investigation, and the final report was submitted to the Presidency on 20 March 2024. On 28 November 2024, the MICT SETA received correspondence from the Presidency requesting a report on the steps it had taken to implement the recommendations emanating from the SIU's final report. A report on the steps taken by MICT SETA in implementing the recommendations was submitted to the Presidency and the office of the Minister on 27 January.
Finance overview
The SETA’s revenue base had grown significantly from the 2020/21 financial year, having reached the R1 billion mark in the 2021/22, 2022/23 and 2023/24 financial years.
Overview of the organisational performance 2020 – 2024
Over the past four years, the MICT SETA has made a substantial impact through its funded learning programmes, benefiting 44 792 unemployed learners. Notably, more than 60% of these learners were women, totalling over 26 875 individuals. The programmes covered fibre optics, 3D printing, the Internet of Things, artificial intelligence (AI), cyber security, software development, and design thinking, amongst others.
The learning programmes had enabled 40 488 unemployed youth to embark on their professional journeys, inclusive of 2 503 learners living with disabilities who had participated in transformative learning experiences.
In addition, 8,279 learners from rural areas had been involved, which assisted in bridging the digital divide and promoting inclusive economic growth.
The programmes had supported 1 577 SMMEs by providing dedicated skills and tools of trade for effective learning through partnerships.
Overall, more than R1.6 billion had been invested in bursaries, learnerships, internships, and skills programmes, with funding for learning programmes increasing by more than 76% from 2021/22 to date.
Achievements
Development of over 52 Fourth Industrial Revolution (4IR) qualifications, part-qualifications and skills programmes.
Achievement of an unqualified audit with no material findings (clean audit) for two consecutive financial years.
Organisational performance had been on an upward trajectory, with a slight drop of 1% in the 2023/24 financial year (FY).
539 beneficiaries were trained on pilot 4IR programmes. 40% of the cohort were female while 60% were male and 5% were people living with disabilities. These programmes had been rolled out in rural and peri-urban areas.

Responses
Tshwane North TVET College
Ms Tsibogo responded to the issue of enrolments, and said if NSFAS could open up allocations to part-time students, it would go a long way. Most people who needed access to college enrolments were people who did not have money. They could open the campuses in the evenings for those part-time students, but there was no money, so they approached the SETAs to assist. She would respond in detail on this matter in writing.
The Chairperson cautioned the college principal to refrain from mentioning matters that she could not substantiate right now. She must focus only on what she could respond to now, and any information that required detail should be submitted in writing.
Ms Tsibogo acknowledged the advice, and addressed the issue of land ownership. This was a widespread problem, as it often complicated audits. Many colleges did not own the land on which they were located. There were inconsistencies across provinces -- in some areas, the provincial government, through the Department of Public Works and Infrastructure (DPWI), grants land to colleges. For instance, they had to purchase land from the City of Tshwane because they were unable to develop further after receiving donor funding from the Swiss, who were investing in an entrepreneurship hub in Mamelodi. They had spent R3.5 million to acquire the land from the City of Tshwane, which posed a significant challenge.
Regarding the withdrawal of the Chinese project, it originally began in 2007 when the Chinese solicited proposals. The project was revived in 2021 when an agreement was reached and signed by the Director-General. The processes eventually started, with the project being shared between Tshwane South and Tshwane North. An allocation of R350 million was designated for infrastructure development, primarily focused on student support services, workshops, and classrooms. However, they encountered difficulties in securing the land, which belongs to the City of Tshwane. They had written to the Minister requesting intervention, because the situation required political assistance. Due to the delays, they eventually received a letter from the Director-General in November or December stating that it had taken too long for the two colleges to initiate construction, and as a result, the funding would be redirected to institutions that were prepared to move forward. They felt this decision was unfair, as they had not received any support from the Department to help them acquire the necessary land. They had made efforts to approach the Office of the Speaker in Tshwane, but unfortunately, due to their policies, they could not donate or give away land -- it had to be purchased.
Regarding the questions on Higher Health, they had weekly and bi-weekly programmes on campus that provide services to students.
She requested to respond in writing regarding the allegations against her. She assured Members that the allegations were not true, but she felt it would be best to respond in writing. She had become aware of the issues when she visited the campus, and realised various decisions had been taken unlawfully.
On the Ernst & Young matter, as soon as she knew the Department was instituting an investigation, she took all the files and placed them in her office because she had learnt that people were shredding key documents and evidence, including the minutes that were needed. Unfortunately, she was never requested by Ernst & Young for those documents or information. The other employees who could not be charged were not charged because they had been wrongfully accused. Ernst & Young had misunderstood the environment, and had somehow made findings based on incorrect documents. All the documents were available, and she could submit them whenever needed.
She asked the CFO to respond to the matters involving the College Infrastructure Efficiency Grant (CIEG).
The CFO explained that the low spending on the CIEG was primarily due to the contractor's inability to complete the work. He had not been at the institution when the contractor was appointed. However, he acknowledged that a risk assessment was not conducted at that time. One significant issue was that the appointed contractor had died during the COVID-19 pandemic, leaving the project stalled in its initial stages. The tendering process had taken a considerable amount of time, and two subsequent contractors were appointed, but they had also failed to deliver, leading to the termination of their contracts. The tender was readvertised, and work eventually resumed, but this led to further losses in CIEG funding.
Last week, officials from the Department and Treasury had visited the institution to monitor and evaluate the projects. Since the CIEG began, two projects on two campuses had been completed, while three others were nearing completion. One project, costing R30 million and related to hostel maintenance, was 59% complete, and was expected to be finished by the end of this year.
The Chairperson said the College had reported that it would finish the ongoing project in April, but now the CFO claimed it would be finished by the end of the year.

MICT SETA
Mr Thobela began by discussing partnerships, highlighting the collaborations established with various stakeholders. One significant partnership was with the UIF on a labour activation plan, which was a co-funded project that had already been executed. However, there were now challenges, as the UIF was seeking additional funding and wanted to operate on a 50/50 basis. This was complicated, given the other ongoing projects on which the SETA had partnered. Another project was with the NSF, focusing on university placements, which was nearing completion.
Regarding governance and allegations of purging, Mr Thobela commented that boardrooms often had dynamic interactions, and disagreements were common. In 2021, the board's remuneration committee (Remco) sought to operate outside its terms of reference and the constitution of the MICT SETA. It had convened ten meetings in eight months, prompting him to intervene as chairperson to understand why they were meeting so frequently -- more often than the EXCO, which met monthly. There was a push from Remco to become involved in operations and to overtake the CEO by making appointments as a sub-committee. This led to disagreements with the board, resulting in several resignations.
In another incident, a board member had questioned the validity of the constitution under which he was appointed, claiming it could not be relied upon. The board had invited the Department to clarify the constitution's status, but the member in question had avoided the session, presumably to evade exposure. This same member had also disrupted meetings by speaking over the chairperson and other members. It was essential to address the issue that board dynamics did not justify rudeness or disruptions. This behaviour persisted in various sub-committees, leading to conflicts with their chairpersons. To address these issues, the Institute for Dispute Resolution in Africa (IDRA) had been brought in to guide managing board dynamics, resulting in a report on the matter.
The same member had later met with the chief information officer (CIO) at a restaurant, alongside two other members, discussing how to undermine board decisions and create chaos. Fortunately, the CIO had recorded this conversation, and it was subsequently investigated by Gobodo & Associates. Their report was submitted to the office of the Minister. This individual had also claimed publicly that action should be taken against the board chairperson. The board had provided all the evidence to the Organisation Undoing Tax Abuse (OUTA) and several media outlets, which indicated an attempt to deflect attention away from his own wrongdoing in the entity. He further claimed that he would approach Parliament regarding the issue.
Regarding the high travel expenses incurred by the board chairperson, he explained that they had tried to hold meetings online. However, many board members resided in rural areas and were dispersed across various provinces, so virtual physical meetings were necessary. This travel was essential for his role. Additionally, the lack of infrastructure in rural areas posed significant connectivity challenges. Many of the troublesome meetings were held virtually, during which members tended to create chaos from their locations. Confidential information was leaked even before some meetings concluded. Nonetheless, the board would continue to explore the best methods to address these issues.
The SIU report had been sent in February 2025, and had been processed internally, leading to the implementation of consequence management. Cases were opened against individuals who were no longer employed with the organisation. The issues concerning Infocom, which were highlighted in the SIU report, did not suggest that the company had been exonerated from being blacklisted. In 2021, the former CEO had approached the Treasury to blacklist Infocom without following the necessary due process and obtaining approval from the accounting authority. As a result, the company had initiated litigation for being wrongfully blacklisted. The board had decided to withdraw that blacklist to initiate the process anew and ensure it was conducted properly this time.
Some of the beneficiaries of the MICT SETA were spread out through the communications sector. They wanted to share a video of testimonials with the Committee, but it could be submitted later.
DHET
Mr Zungu said the Department would provide operational budgets to institutions, and they procured their software licences. If it was possible to obtain licences that could be shared by institutions, the MICT SETA may advise accordingly, and that advice might be heeded.
On the accessibility of Higher Health, there was a limitation because the service was available physically. However, there were discussions about making these services to be available online and after hours.
The total applications received amounted to 1.5 million, but the available spaces would be around 10% due to limitations because they relied on the physical space to enrol. There were also funding limitations. They did not have a system that could track, and they do not know how many of that 1.5 million had been absorbed in universities or private colleges, because there was no tool to track them.
A lot of work had been ongoing regarding the names submitted to the Department for processing and appointments. They had to ensure all the names met the requirements, and some of them were sent back to ensure that colleges complied with the requirements. They would follow up with the Ministry on the status of the process.
He requested to make a written submission regarding the allegations that had been levelled against the principal of Tshwane North TVET college. The corporate services unit was not present today, and they were ones involved in the matter.
Chairperson’s closing remarks
The Chairperson said that last week, they had discussed the situation of the Tshwane North and South campuses, highlighting that the land they occupied belongs to the municipality, which was not generating any income from it. The land had been purchased from the municipality for R3.2 million. However, the Committee believed that the institution served as a public entity providing a public service. He said the decision to buy the land reflected weak leadership. He suggested that leadership should have assembled a team to conduct an impact assessment study and engaged with the municipality regarding plans to improve access to education for South Africans.
The institution had a responsibility to educate the residents of Tshwane and the surrounding areas. The municipality had owned the land for 15 years without using it. If the land were donated to the institution, the City could generate revenue through rates and other charges. The institution had funding available to develop infrastructure, but not to purchase land, and it could make this idle piece of land profitable for the municipality.
As the principal, one's role went beyond just hiring and firing staff -- it also involved maximising limited resources for the greatest possible outcomes. Effective leadership in this context required a focus on innovative approaches to fulfil the institution's mandate, rather than simply complaining.
Tshwane was home to an automobile industry, and the institution should evaluate the courses offered in other countries that were positioned near economic zones, particularly how their curriculums were structured. For instance, while Volkswagen operates in Tshwane, how many courses did the TVET college offer that were specifically aligned with the needs of the automobile industry in the province? Did the institution have any partnerships with the automobile industry to develop a curriculum that directly addresses the skills required by the sector? Were there any partnerships between the College and the industry for graduate absorption once they completed their studies?
According to the presentation from the TVET college, three projects were currently at a standstill, and no progress had been made even though millions of rands had already been paid to service providers. One project had been allotted R2.8 million, another R3 million, and the third R416 000, yet no work had been completed on any of them. It raised questions about what had led the project manager to authorise these payments when no work had been done. Additionally, one of the projects, which was assessed and expected to be completed next month, was currently under 60% complete. This situation left one wondering if the project would be finished within the designated timeframe.
The institution was expected to provide details in writing to all the significant issues raised above. Details related to consequence management implemented against transgressors must also be submitted, and the steps taken explained in detail. Members wanted to know the process followed to institute consequence management.
He also encouraged the stakeholders to pay attention to the work they send to Parliament and ensure that due diligence was done to avoid silly mistakes.
The Committee had met with Nelson Mandela University (NMU) two weeks ago, during which the labour unions presented their concerns. However, the unions were now facing threats of suspension, a practice that Members consistently warned against. Similarly, labour unions at the University of Limpopo were undergoing disciplinary hearings. When these unions came to Parliament, they were informed that they were protected under the Powers and Privileges Act. Yet, upon returning to their institutions, they continued to face intimidation and disciplinary actions.
When the Committee requested that workers and students appear before them, the Members did not want management to use state resources to oppose them. If management used state resources in court and lost cases, the costs should be deducted from their pension funds. They were consistently advised against using state resources for frivolous legal battles, which ultimately cost the state millions. Therefore, if they lose a court case based on flimsy charges, the financial repercussions should directly impact their pensions. This directive needed to be considered by the Ministry.
Additionally, the Committee needed to assess the number of cases involving SETAs, Community Education and Training (CET) colleges, TVET colleges and universities concerning their employees. Specifically, how many of these cases were escalated to court, how many were lost, and what was the total amount spent on legal battles? The overall figure was significantly high -- it may well amount to billions. At the University of Limpopo, there was a legal firm that had been allocated 60% of the cases, but had lost more cases than it had won. It had continued to receive tenders due to associations or friendships.
The meeting was adjourned.
