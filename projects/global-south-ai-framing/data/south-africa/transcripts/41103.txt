2025/26 Annual Performance Plans
The Department of Agriculture briefed the Committee on its Strategic Plan and Annual Performance Plan for 2025/26, following its decoupling from the Department of Rural Land Reform and Rural Development.
The Minister of Agriculture reported that one of its challenges was to find ways to integrate small-scale farmers into established value chains, which he described as a core focus area of the department. He indicated that viability, sustainability, and profitability were the three critical pillars for the success of any agricultural enterprise. Therefore, the department's emphasis was on connecting small-scale producers to markets at the right scale—markets that could offer the guaranteed uptake needed for these producers to become viable and sustainable businesses.
Discussions centred on the Department’s vision for a dynamic and sustainable agricultural sector, its financial allocation, and key strategic priorities. These included fostering partnerships, modernising legislation, improving market access, and ensuring biosecurity. Members of the committee raised various questions regarding the Department’s interaction with provincial agricultural departments, vaccine production capabilities, biosecurity measures, support for new-generation farmers, digitalisation, food prices, economic growth drivers, the use of consultants, and the Department’s climate change resilience strategies. In response, the Department provided updates on initiatives such as revitalising animal health facilities, promoting young farmers through agricultural colleges, reducing food costs, integrating small-scale farmers into commercial value chains, and addressing ongoing biosecurity threats such as Foot and Mouth Disease. Additional emphasis was placed on sustainable land management, the potential for hemp and cannabis cultivation, and strengthening collaboration with provincial governments.
The Committee also received a briefing from the Department of Energy and Electricity on its Strategic Plan and Annual Performance Plan for 2025/26.
Key issues discussed included the timeline for confirming a permanent appointment to the Acting Director-General position and the significant municipal debt owed to Eskom, which was obstructing departmental plans and delaying Eskom’s unbundling process. Members also discussed the progress and long-term goals of the Integrated Transmission Plan (ITP), intended to close infrastructure backlogs, and sought clarity on the stark electrification disparities among provinces such as the Western Cape, KwaZulu-Natal, and particularly Gauteng, attributed to high rates of urbanisation and the prevalence of informal settlements.
Further points of discussion included ensuring that the new department’s targets were ambitious yet realistically deliverable, particularly given inherited capacity limitations and the urgent need to stabilise the electricity sector. Members expressed concern about the timelines for procuring critical energy infrastructure, especially in light of the 2030 emissions compliance deadline and the necessity to replace 16,000 megawatts of coal-fired generation. The implementation of the just energy transition at provincial and local levels was raised, with calls for province-specific plans and clear job transition strategies for coal-dependent regions.
Additionally, mechanisms for transparency and public engagement were discussed regarding the cost-benefit analysis of procuring up to 10 gigawatts of nuclear energy and reactivating the Pebble Bed Modular Reactor (PBMR) programme, ensuring alignment with long-term energy affordability and emissions reduction goals. Members also raised concerns about delays in contracting for new renewable generation capacity—planned for 2027 or 2028—despite earlier targets for initiating procurement and finalising the Integrated Resource Plan (IRP) 2025. These delays pose a risk to the timely replacement of coal capacity by 2030. Other limitations discussed included restricted grid access in areas with high renewable energy potential.
Waste management issues were also explored, particularly the need for a central interim storage facility for low- and medium-level radioactive waste, along with the potential for recycling radioactive materials. In response to these concerns, the Department highlighted ongoing efforts, including new strategies to address municipal debt, operationalising the ITP model, and adopting a more holistic approach to municipal electricity distribution. They emphasised refining their performance targets to ensure measurable societal benefits, evaluating suitable nuclear technologies, and establishing a domestic value chain for renewable energy. A consistent theme throughout the discussion was the need to attract investment and maintain sustainability within the evolving energy sector.

Department of Agriculture Presentation
Doctor Phuthi, Acting Chief Director, and his colleague from the Department of Agriculture led the committee through the presentation.
A situational analysis indicates that agriculture contributes between 2.4% and 2.57% to South Africa's GDP, employs 9.6% of the workforce, and is a net exporter of agricultural products. The presentation also included a SWOT analysis, highlighting strengths such as sufficient knowledge and expertise, weaknesses like a lack of strong private sector partnerships and slow digitalisation, opportunities in digitalisation and donor funding, and threats from political instabilities, climate change, and budget cuts.
The Department's impact statements are geared towards improved food security and market access through a sustained agricultural sector, with specific outcomes focusing on improved governance, increased agricultural production, enhanced food and nutrition security, increased participation of Black producers, greater market access, and strengthened biosecurity and disaster risk reduction.
Financially, the Department of Agriculture has a budget allocation of R7.609 billion for 2025/26. Programme Three, Food Security, receives the largest allocation at R3.270 billion (43.0% of the total budget), with significant funding directed towards farmer support through conditional grants like the Comprehensive Agricultural Support Programme (CASP) and Ilima Letsema projects. Programme Two, Agricultural Production, Biosecurity, and Natural Resources Management, receives the second-highest allocation of R2.482 billion (32.6%) to address disease outbreaks and support research and development. Programme One, Administration, is allocated R973.928 million (12.8%), and Programme Four, Economic Development, Trade and Marketing, receives R883.071 million (11.6%). The budget is distributed across various economic classifications, including compensation of employees, goods and services, and transfers to provinces and departmental agencies.

Responses
Minister Steenhuisen expressed his gratitude to the members of the committee for what he described as some very considerate and well-considered questions.
He noted that one of the members had asked about the relationship with MECs and explained that they had already held a MinMEC, but he also wanted to emphasise that he had a very good relationship with the MECs. He specifically commended the MECs for being responsive when the department conducted visits to their provinces and also during times of crisis. He highlighted that they were fortunate to host the World Food Day in the Northern Cape, which had the highest incidence of food insecurity, and they were grateful for the support of the MEC there. He also thanked MEC Ramokgopa from Gauteng for her proactive stance in addressing foot and mouth disease, as well as the MEC in KwaZulu-Natal for being responsive to the department’s inquiries.
He stressed that the relationship between the three spheres of government was absolutely important and mentioned that, as he had expressed the previous day during the question session in the National Assembly, they had been able to achieve far more by using both the district development model and provincial cooperation than any of the departments would have been able to achieve on their own, whether operating at local, provincial or national level. He said he hoped those synergies would continue.
He noted that one of the members had also asked about OBP (Onderstepoort Biological Products). He explained that Onderstepoort had once been one of the world leaders in animal diseases but, sadly, due to decades of neglect, maladministration, and poor decision-making, it had lost its standing as a global leader in animal health. He assured members of the Select Committee that the department was actively engaged in getting OBP back on its feet and ensuring that it could regain its position as one of the premier animal health facilities in the world. He pointed out that the importance of this effort had been demonstrated by the recent outbreak of foot and mouth disease in South Africa. He explained that OBP used to produce vaccines, but because of issues such as the poor quality of water pipes, they had lost the ability to produce vaccines at the required level and quality to meet the international guidelines of the World Organisation of Animal Health. As a result, they had lost their status and now had to procure vaccines from the Botswana Vaccine Institute.
The Minister stated that they had recently allocated R42 million for the initial stage, with a total budget of R70 million, to purchase vaccines and begin a coordinated vaccination programme for foot and mouth disease in South Africa. However, he made it clear that this situation was not desirable, and they would prefer to produce their own vaccines. He reported that the Agricultural Research Council (ARC) was in advanced stages of producing a South African vaccine that would cover the SAT1, SAT2 and SAT3 strains of the disease. He believed this would be highly beneficial for South Africa as it would ensure an uninterrupted supply and would also create opportunities to supply vaccines to other African countries, generating premium income.
Regarding OBP, he explained that they had made several interventions at both the board and management levels. He said he was pleased to report that the last two reports from OBP to the Portfolio Committee and other oversight structures in the ministry had indicated signs of a turnaround and that he believed some recovery was beginning to take place. He added that he had managed to get Aspen to conduct a floor-to-ceiling assessment of the OBP facilities, which had resulted in valuable input on how to improve the facilities.
He also mentioned that the freeze dryers, which had contributed to the shortage in producing routine vaccines such as the African horse sickness vaccine, had arrived and were in the process of being installed, accredited and calibrated. He said they hoped that by October that year, they would be able to significantly ramp up vaccine production. He stated that he believed OBP was well on the path to recovery but emphasised that oversight from the Select Committee and the Portfolio Committee of the National Assembly would be required to ensure they remained on track. He encouraged the Select Committee to visit OBP to receive a firsthand update. He said he would leave it to his colleague to determine how the assessments would be performed and how they would be allocated.
The Minister said that the agricultural colleges were obviously going to be a very important point and mechanism for the department to start producing the farmers of the future. He pointed out that, unfortunately, the agriculture sector was plagued by an ageing population and, given its importance for food security and the future national security of the country, it was necessary to ensure that younger farmers entered the sector so that they would be able to compete, provide, and fill the gap left by retiring farmers. He explained that this was why agricultural colleges were fundamentally important, and noted that he had just inherited responsibility for them under the Department of Agriculture.
He added that just in the previous week, they had been able to convene a colloquium with the Association of Principals of Agricultural Colleges (APAC) as well as the overarching student body to discuss strategies and interventions required by the Department of Agriculture to ensure that those facilities were fit for purpose. He said that the aim was to ensure that graduates from those colleges would be able to get out into the field and fill the gap left by farmers exiting the sector, and therefore it was absolutely essential that the colleges were prioritised.
The Minister said they had also had to listen to some terrible stories of neglect and pointed out that, unfortunately, agricultural colleges had become a bit of a “Cinderella” — falling between provinces, higher education, and other mandates. However, they were now under the responsibility of the Department of Agriculture, and the department was determined to work together with the Department of Higher Education and Training to get them up to standard.
He indicated that he was in the process of seeking a meeting with the Minister of Higher Education to finalise the mandating process. He explained that, if the mandating could be finalised under the Department of Higher Education, it would mean that students at agricultural colleges would then be able to access NSFAS funding and other funding options available to students at other higher education institutions. He emphasised the need to recognise that agricultural colleges were fundamentally the cornerstone for the survival of the agriculture sector.
He believed the agricultural colleges would provide a steady source of young farmers who could then be considered for allocation of state-owned land and other properties acquired through the PLAS (Proactive Land Acquisition Strategy) system, with the benefit of practical experience from having studied farming, to ensure their future success.
He appreciated the kind words about the assignees and explained that the matter flowed directly from a directive President Ramaphosa had issued to ministers during the MDDP discussions on lowering food costs for ordinary consumers. He said the department had therefore undertaken a comprehensive, top‑to‑toe assessment of agricultural food chains to identify “friction costs” and to find ways to eliminate as many of them as possible, because those costs ultimately end up being paid by the consumer.
He noted that assignees—especially the leaf‑services assignee—would have imposed a heavy burden on the grain sector and driven up the prices of bread and other staple foods in South Africa, which was why the decision had been taken to halt that arrangement. He emphasised, however, that this was only one element of an ongoing internal discussion about cutting red tape and friction costs so that consumers receive products at the best possible value, particularly in a tight fiscal environment and amid a severe cost-of-living crisis.
By way of illustration, he said Shoprite Checkers had approached the department about problems with the proposed assignee service. The retailer currently offers a R10 “loss‑leader” meal that provides carbohydrates, protein and nutrients—something affordable to everyone from car guards and people who rely on begging to factory workers. He explained that if the assignee service were implemented, it would push the price of that meal above R10, making it a true loss for the supermarket instead of a break-even promotion, and Shoprite would have to discontinue it. He then said that the department’s actions form part of a broader, continuing effort to reduce red tape throughout the sector.
The Minister said it was a misconception that mega farmers were responsible for all the value added in the South African agricultural value chain. He had several reasons for holding this view and strongly recommended to the committee a new book by Wandile Sihlobo and John Kirsten, who had also authored
The Country of Two Agricultures
. He said the new book revealed where the real challenges in agriculture lay and that it was essential reading for anyone wishing to understand the sector more deeply.
He explained that the book showed clearly that the common belief in the dominance of mega farmers was flawed and that there was a significant underestimation of the role played by small-scale farmers in both food security and the broader economy. He noted that this was most clearly demonstrated during the COVID-19 pandemic, when, in his view, South Africa would have faced mass starvation were it not for the efforts of small-scale producers, the
bakkie
trade, and those operating in township markets. He credited these farmers, their value addition, and their access to informal markets with helping many South Africans access food during the crisis, thereby averting a large-scale starvation event.
The Minister said the challenge now was to find ways to integrate small-scale farmers into established value chains, which he described as a core focus area of the department. He reiterated that viability, sustainability, and profitability were the three critical pillars for the success of any agricultural enterprise. Therefore, the department's emphasis was on connecting small-scale producers to markets at the right scale—markets that could offer the guaranteed uptake needed for these producers to become viable and sustainable businesses.
He stressed that it was not enough to simply allocate land, equipment, and seeds to farmers without simultaneously ensuring they had access to markets. He posed the rhetorical question of how such farmers were expected to get their produce to market and pointed out that even if they grew the finest cabbages in the world, their efforts would be futile without a clear and reliable market linkage.
He linked this to the broader role that agriculture could play in job creation, pointing to first-quarter GDP figures that underscored the sector’s contribution to the economy. He noted that South Africa’s unemployment crisis was concentrated among unskilled or underskilled individuals and said there were only a few sectors capable of rapidly absorbing such labour—industrial processing, tourism, and, crucially, agriculture.
The Minister said that this was why it was exciting to see the planned work to grow the agricultural sector, as expansion would further increase the sector’s capacity to create jobs. He added that his colleague would be providing additional information on the legacy contracts.
He went on to address biosecurity, saying the committee had asked about the long-term plan. In his view, South Africa faced an existential reality: the country would never be entirely free of foot‑and‑mouth disease (FMD). He explained that FMD was endemic in buffalo populations, particularly in northern KwaZulu-Natal, along the Kruger National Park border in Limpopo, and in parts of Mpumalanga. Whenever game fences failed or buffalo and cattle mixed, outbreaks became unavoidable.
He said the department, therefore, had to take a decisive position in the coming months. He had studied Brazil, Argentina and other peer countries that had launched mass‑vaccination campaigns, achieved “FMD‑free with vaccination” status, and then used ongoing vaccination to push the virus back into endemic zones. Brazil, he noted, had succeeded after nearly nine years of intensive intervention. South Africa might adopt a similar strategy, but only after thorough consultation with the industry: departmental officials would soon convene a discussion with both small‑ and large-scale cattle producers because, without their buy-in, even the best plans would fail. Nonetheless, he believed progress was possible.
About the mood on the ground, he said farmers generally maintained hope and optimism—qualities essential for staying in the sector—but they also had serious concerns. Chief among these were the poor condition of municipal roads and infrastructure, rural insecurity linked to policing, and constraints on exports caused by underperforming ports and harbours. He welcomed Minister Creecy’s recent intervention, which was already easing port congestion and improving efficiency. That progress mattered, he explained, because the citrus and deciduous‑fruit industries had told the department they could at least quadruple production within four to five years, provided they could rely on ports to move their produce abroad. Global demand for South African agricultural goods was strong, yet exports were limited by livestock and fruit‑sector diseases and by inefficiencies “of our own making.” Improved rail and port functionality, he argued, would have a catalytic effect on agriculture.
Overall, he reported, the feeling among farmers was “cautiously optimistic,” and it was now the department’s responsibility to ensure that optimism translated into tangible progress.
He replied that the department would do its best, week by week, to allay members’ concerns, and he highlighted “resilience” as a guiding theme in two priority areas
.
He said agricultural technology and innovation advanced rapidly, so legislation had to be nimble enough to keep pace. If new, climate-resilient crop varieties became available, the rules should allow them to reach South African farmers quickly. This agility was vital both for climate adaptation and for competitiveness: despite operating without government subsidies—unlike many foreign peers—the country’s farmers, farm‑workers and agribusinesses still held their own in global markets, which he described as a tribute to their resilience. The department therefore intended to promote new crops, remedies and other innovations—those that used less water or tolerated drought, for example—to help everyone from subsistence producers to commercial farmers build long-term resilience.
He welcomed a question about digitisation and said artificial intelligence, machine learning and big‑data tools offered the department a “huge opportunity.” The first target was the Act 36 certification process, which governs all farm remedies and products. Backlogs had accumulated because the system was paper-based; in collaboration with the director, Jonathan Matsunga, the department was converting it to an online platform so that new technologies and remedies could be registered far more quickly. He added that many other departmental procedures were also ripe for digitisation—a necessity, he argued, because tight fiscal conditions meant they had to “do more with less.” Instead of hiring hundreds of extra staff, the department would have to work smarter through technology.
He responded and continued by saying that it would be important for the department to give a presentation to the Committee not only on hemp, but also on the Cannabis Master Plan, which was now under the stewardship of the Department of Trade, Industry and Competition. He emphasised the importance of updating the Committee on what the agricultural side of government had been doing to create opportunities in this space.
He fully agreed with the member who raised the issue, stating there were “huge opportunities” in both the hemp and cannabis sectors, and that the government needed to act much faster to open up these industries. He shared an anecdote, saying he had always assumed cannabis only grew in KwaZulu-Natal and the Eastern Cape, but was fascinated to learn that some of the best strains came from the North West and the Northern Cape. This, he said, demonstrated that every part of the country had potential when it came to cultivating hemp and cannabis.
The Minister agreed that sustainable land use was crucial for the future of agriculture in South Africa. Unfortunately, he said, the focus over the past 30 years had been almost exclusively on yield—“yield at all costs”—which had led to farming practices and the use of products that, while boosting output in the short term, had damaged soil in the long term. He stressed that soil itself, and the living organisms within it, were as important as the crops grown in it. Without healthy soils, there could be no sustainable agriculture.
He said it was vital to phase out highly hazardous pesticides and other inputs that damaged soil quality and to shift toward more sustainable land management practices. As a result, of the 71 municipalities involved, about 64 had already failed to meet the program's requirements or had fallen behind in their repayments. This had necessitated a reconsideration of the entire municipal debt relief program.
She highlighted a positive initiative by Eskom called the Distribution Agency Agreement, which allowed Eskom to take over the collection of funds from municipalities, pay itself first, and then return the remaining balance to the municipality. While this was still a voluntary arrangement at present, there was consideration of making it mandatory, especially for municipalities under the debt relief programme. She stressed that every municipality needed to be assessed individually, as each one faced different contextual challenges. For some, the problem was purely financial, while others faced deep systemic issues. She pointed out that municipalities owed Eskom over R90 billion, and this massive debt was seriously affecting Eskom’s ability to unbundle. The debt made the distribution component look unattractive as a going concern. She stated that the ministry was exploring different methodologies and new strategies to address this debt. Pilot projects were being run with Senedi and Eskom to test sustainable solutions for municipal debt. She emphasised that addressing the issue was a key focus of the ministry, but acknowledged that it was not something that could be resolved in weeks or months—it required an incremental, municipality-by-municipality approach.
She added that many foreign governments were also assisting municipalities in finding alternative solutions, and that numerous stakeholders were involved in trying to resolve the issue. She linked this to a question that had been asked about municipal infrastructure, specifically noting that distribution infrastructure at the municipal level was a major problem. Many municipalities, she said, did not even know the full layout of their networks. Therefore, mapping municipal networks to determine the location and condition of lines and infrastructure was essential for functionality and sustainability.
She reiterated that municipal distribution would become a major focus area. The challenges stemmed from financial unsustainability and years of neglected maintenance, and a great deal of work was needed to address them. However, she assured the committee that the matter was receiving attention.
Regarding the Integrated Transmission Plan (ITP), she noted that the Minister would address that matter as it fell within his portfolio. The current ITP was targeting 1,164 kilometres of transmission, but in order to meet the national target of 14,000 kilometres over 10 years, at least 1,400 kilometres would need to be built each year. She said this would be an important test of the program’s feasibility. In the meantime, Eskom—or more specifically, the NTCSA (National Transmission Company of South Africa)—had its own parallel build program and was expected to construct about 280 kilometres of transmission lines in the current year.
She said that the acting Director-General could respond to the matter concerning household electrification in the provinces and the significant gaps that remained.
She thanked Ms Boshoff for her condolences. She noted that some of the questions Ms Boshoff had asked were more technical in nature, particularly those relating to the APP and the steps taken to ensure that the targets were realistic and deliverable. She said that this issue had also been raised by the National Assembly Portfolio Committee and had been addressed by the department in a subsequent meeting. The same applied to procurement-related queries.
Regarding the Just Energy Transition (JET), she emphasised the importance of recognising that, although the Minister chairs the JET Implementation Program, the actual implementation does not necessarily fall within their department. Instead, the presidency is primarily responsible for overseeing the process, with a dedicated JET Project Management Unit located within the presidency. She explained that several departments were involved as role players—most notably, the Department of Environment and the Department of Energy, with the latter being the main player.
She pointed out that accessing funding for JET was proving to be challenging. A large portion of the funds allocated by the International Partners Group were in the form of loans, which the department was hesitant to access due to concerns about further encumbering the country. She stated that while the department was making use of available grant funding and in-kind donations—such as technical assistance—they were being cautious about taking on additional loan funding. This caution, she said, was making access to JET financing very difficult.
She noted that, as she had mentioned during a panel discussion the previous week, one of the core issues with JET was that transmission had not been a focal area during its development. At the time JET was designed, it was not yet clear that transmission infrastructure would pose a major challenge. As a result, JET had focused heavily on renewables, battery storage, and green hydrogen, but had neglected transmission, which was now revealed to be a critical component for unlocking the rest of the program.
She suggested that there might be a need to rethink and re-strategise the JET approach so that it aligns more closely with South Africa's actual needs rather than being shaped solely by the directives of the International Partners Group. She said this was a conversation that would need to happen within the department during the next strategic planning session.
On the issue of nuclear energy, she deferred to the nuclear branch to respond. She also suggested that the Acting Director-General could address the topic of generation capacity.
Turning to theft and vandalism, she stated that this was becoming a serious issue that the department needed to prioritise. She explained that theft and vandalism were among the reasons why load reduction was being implemented in certain areas. She cited a recent incident in which around 16 mini-substations were stolen within a single month, underlining the severity of the problem. She added that some private companies had resorted to hiring security guards to protect mini-substations from theft at night. She then said that a much better strategy would be needed to deal with the growing problem.
She continued by addressing the topic of green hydrogen, stating that it had been one of the focus areas, and added that the Minister could possibly contribute further on the matter. She explained that one of the key considerations in the department’s discussions had been the inherent difficulty in transporting and storing green hydrogen. This raised the question of whether transporting pure green hydrogen should remain the core focus of the country’s strategy.
Based on ongoing internal discussions and consultations with advisors, she said the department had come to the conclusion that South Africa's comparative advantage would more likely lie in value-added products derived from green hydrogen rather than in exporting the raw form itself. For example, at Coega, the Hive project would focus on producing green ammonia instead of transporting green hydrogen. Green ammonia, she explained, is far easier to export. Similarly, in Saldanha, the focus would be on producing green steel—another example of a value-added product made possible through the use of green hydrogen.
She said that the department was also exploring the production of sustainable aviation fuel using green hydrogen, in collaboration with Sasol, and possibly with support from the European Union. Other value-added opportunities being considered included fuel cell technology, among others.
She emphasised that the transportation of green hydrogen in its pure form posed significant logistical challenges for South Africa. Therefore, the department had determined that its strategic strength would lie in the development of products that incorporated green hydrogen—products that were in demand globally and could be produced and exported from South Africa. She said this would form the basis of the country’s green hydrogen strategy moving forward.
Regarding Koeberg and nuclear energy, she said the nuclear branch would respond to those matters, including small modular reactors (SMRs). However, she noted that NEXA would soon be issuing a request for information (RFI) to gauge the range of SMR technologies currently available, given the significant recent developments in the field. Based on the information received, an assessment would be conducted to determine which technologies were most suitable for adoption, whether a single option or multiple. From there, decisions would be made regarding the implementation of SMR technology in South Africa.
She continued by addressing the issue of scaling up renewable energy. She explained that renewable energy expansion was once again being limited by grid access, as the primary resource areas for renewables in South Africa were located in regions that were grid-constrained. This presented a significant challenge. To address it, the department had explored several alternative approaches, including curtailment strategies, microgrids, and direct feed into off-grid solutions. She noted that while there were many avenues being pursued to upscale renewable energy, the relationship between renewable generation and the availability of grid infrastructure remained a “difficult dance,” requiring simultaneous efforts on both fronts.
Turning to local manufacturing, she highlighted that the South African Renewable Energy Master Plan (SAREM or SARM) had recently been launched at the Green Hydrogen Conference and again showcased at the Africa Energy Forum the previous week. She described it as an industrialisation strategy aimed at developing a domestic value chain for the renewable energy sector. The plan went beyond just manufacturing—it also focused on building a strong service industry around renewables.
She acknowledged that manufacturing jobs could be limited due to mechanisation, so the department also intended to create job-rich service sector opportunities. These service industries would help build valuable skills, which were a key focus of the Renewable Energy Master Plan. She added that these skills, along with components, could be exported to the rest of Africa as part of the continent’s broader electrification efforts. While South Africa would not compete in producing solar panels or turbines at this stage—due to China's dominance—there were numerous components that could be manufactured locally, especially where local minerals could be beneficiated. She stressed that South Africa should focus on what it was good at and build multiple value-added mechanisms around those strengths.
She said the goal was for South Africa to become a renewable energy platform for the continent, able to export components, skills, and services. The Project Management Office for the Renewable Energy Master Plan was expected to be operational within the next month, after which work streams would be launched to focus on services and manufacturing development.
In response to a question about support for households in becoming more energy efficient, she explained that as of 7 December, the Energy Performance Certificate (EPC) requirement would become mandatory for commercial and public buildings. However, she acknowledged that there had been limited focus on households. To address this gap, the department had been engaging with banks to discuss ways to incentivise energy efficiency at the household level.
She mentioned that INAP (presumably an electrification programme) would provide energy efficiency support to historically disadvantaged individuals. Additionally, the department was exploring ways to encourage municipalities to use indigent grants to support energy-efficient solutions for low-income households.
She also noted that the department was collaborating with banks and the South African Property Owners Association to develop projects and incentives aimed at improving energy efficiency in both residential and commercial properties. These were the current strategies in place for addressing energy efficiency.
She then said that the Minister or the Acting Director-General would speak to the measures in place for addressing energy shortages and load shedding.
She continued by thanking Mr Mabilo for his kind words about the department’s efforts to keep the lights on. She said she believed a significant portion of the credit should go to the Minister for the work he had done during his time as Minister of Electricity, before the department had been formally established. She noted that his efforts had laid the foundation for the ongoing work being done.
She then moved to the final issue she would respond to, noting that the Deputy Director-General responsible for nuclear could also contribute to the matter of radioactive waste. She explained that there was a site at Vaalputs in the Northern Cape—Mr Mabilo’s home province—where low-level and medium-level radioactive waste was currently stored. She said that NEXA had held the license for the site, but that it was expected to be transferred to the National Radioactive Waste Disposal Institute (NRWDI) in August, as the institute had now qualified to take over that responsibility.
Once the license was transferred, NRWDI would assume responsibility for managing the low-level and medium-level radioactive waste. In the meantime, NEXA and Koeberg continued to store their own radioactive waste on site. These facilities were originally designed to hold their waste for the reactors’ operational lifespans. However, with the extension of Koeberg’s operational life, those storage capacities had reached their limits.
As a result, she said there was now a need to build a central interim storage facility. The department would be initiating the process of developing and designing such a facility, which would also involve all necessary public participation processes, including those required for the safe transportation of waste to the new site.
She noted that, at this stage, South Africa was not pursuing radioactive waste recycling, although it was being done in some other countries. It was something the department might explore in the future. For now, however, the priority was storage, to ensure the country did not find itself in a position, a few years down the line, of having delayed action and then being forced to scramble to find solutions.
She emphasised that this was a key focus for the nuclear branch and assured members that they would be kept informed of progress. She concluded by saying that she believed she had addressed all the questions directed to her and then handed back to the Acting Director-General.
Mr Pillay said he would deal with as many issues as he could and would ask the Deputy Director-Generals (DDGs) to respond to some of them. He noted that the Deputy Minister (DM) had already addressed the issue concerning municipal debt. He stated that the District Agency Agreements (DAAs) were a comprehensive part of the weakness within the municipal debt relief package, as they focused singularly on revenue collection as a criterion for determining whether municipalities qualified for the relief. However, he emphasised that the reality for municipalities was that the entire ecosystem within the energy space required substantial effort to function properly.
He explained that the DAAs were structured to address technical skills, asset management of distribution infrastructure, energy loss management, and pricing structures and tariffs. This was part of a holistic approach to addressing the problems in the municipal distribution sector. According to him, this layer of work was aimed at eventually stabilising the municipal distribution sector to a point where it could attract investment. Only then, he argued, could efforts to reinvest in the distribution industry begin, ultimately leading towards sustainability at the distribution level.
He added that there was also separate work being done around the restructuring of the distribution industry, referring to the DM’s earlier point about the unbundling of Eskom’s distribution business. However, he stressed that such efforts could not proceed with municipalities in their current condition, which was why the DAA work was so critical—to build a level of sustainability that would make those investments viable over time.
Regarding the Independent Transmission Project (ITP), he stated that it would not fund the full extent of the 14,000 kilometres, as the DM had indicated. He clarified that the ITP was currently being implemented as a model, with the first phase covering just over 1,000 kilometres. He mentioned the National Transmission Company of South Africa (NTCSA), which would fund a portion of the Transmission Development Plan (TDP) from its own balance sheet.
He said that as the ITP model was refined, the level of investment through it would be upscaled. For now, he noted, the funding model was hybrid: funding would continue both on-balance-sheet through the fiscus and through the NTCSA. He concluded by saying that as both the technical and financial methodology for the ITP matured, it would take up a significantly larger share of the 14,000 kilometres over the next ten years to deliver the required transmission infrastructure.
He continued by addressing the issue around the so-called "Gauteng Gap." He explained that, when examining urbanisation patterns, it became clear that the levels of non-electrification were closely linked to the levels of urbanisation in the province. He noted that as urbanisation in Gauteng increased, the levels of informality also rose in direct correlation. This, in turn, resulted in a growing number of unelectrified households.
He clarified that, when computing household connections to determine connection points, the figures used were those of formalised connections. However, when assessing the number of unelectrified households, the figures included informal settlements, which would eventually need to be converted into household connections. While there was a process of formalisation involved, he emphasised that the department’s overarching objective—under the banner of universal access—was to ensure the inclusion of these informal areas. This, he said, explained the high figures reported for Gauteng, which were driven by high urbanisation levels and the resulting growth of informal settlements, thereby increasing the number of households without formal electricity connections.
Turning to the question about the “smartness” of the targets, he stated that every effort had been made to ensure that the targets complied with the SMART principle. He added that, at a technical level, the department was working with the Office of the Auditor-General (AG), which was supporting efforts across government to refine targets. The aim, he said, was to ensure that the targets were not only smart and measurable but also reflected outputs rather than just inputs.
He stressed that, over the five-year period, the intention was for these targets to be designed in a way that they would inform real impacts in society, instead of serving merely as a number-counting exercise. He then stated that this work was ongoing, and that as previously noted, the department was refining the APP and would continue to do so until the strategic plan and the APP together constituted a credible and effective programme of action, capable of achieving the intended impacts in the five strategic areas.
He continued by saying that DDG Mbambo would address the cost-benefit analysis for nuclear energy as well as the other questions related to nuclear.
He then responded to a question about the targets for renewable energy, specifically referencing concerns that the renewable component provided for in the Integrated Resource Plan (IRP) would largely only come online in 2027/28. He explained that the timeline from issuing a bid window under the Independent Power Producer (IPP) programme to commercial operation was lengthy. Although the process could take upwards of 24 months in an optimistic scenario, in reality, it generally took more than 36 months.
He reiterated an earlier point that work was underway to establish a one-stop shop for energy infrastructure, with a focus on improving the regulatory environment to enable quicker advancement of projects to a bankable stage. He highlighted two specific areas of reform: firstly, addressing the regulatory requirements for bid compliance, and secondly, resolving challenges related to the grid connection process and grid capacity. He stated that by addressing these issues, the department hoped to significantly reduce the turnaround time required to move projects from inception to commercial operation, thereby accelerating the pace at which they begin feeding into the transmission system.
He also addressed a question regarding the totality of investments in energy. He stated that over a three-year period, a total investment of R219 billion would be made in energy infrastructure. He clarified that this amount did not sit solely within the department’s budget but was distributed across the department and its entities, with Eskom accounting for the bulk of the R219 billion investment. This included funding for generation, distribution, and transmission. He explained that a clear distinction had been made between the investments that would fall under the ITP and those to be delivered by the National Transmission Company of South Africa (NTCSA). He added that the figure also encompassed investments made at the Nuclear Energy Corporation of South Africa (Necsa), particularly in nuclear capabilities and the research reactor.
Regarding the mitigation of impacts on coal-dependent communities, he stated that this was a major focus under the JET Investment Plan. He stressed that investments aimed at offsetting the negative impacts on these communities, as coal power stations began to scale down, needed to be front-loaded. He cited the experience at Komati as an example, pointing out that the assessment of impacts had been too narrowly focused on the power station itself, without considering the broader ecosystem and socio-economic dependence of the surrounding community on the coal economy.
He emphasised that the department's approach going forward was to expand the scope of socio-economic impact assessments to include the entire local economy. Once those impacts had been fully understood, he said, interventions would need to be implemented early—before the scaling down of coal activities—in order to ensure a smooth transition and the continuity of economic activity in those areas. This approach, he explained, was central to the department’s strategy in managing the energy transition in coal-reliant communities.
He continued by acknowledging that theft and vandalism were major issues. He explained that Eskom was working specifically on decongesting the networks, with the distribution team focused on reducing congestion. He mentioned that the department’s work on microgrids would help address transformer congestion and overload problems. Regarding vandalism and theft, he said the main interventions involved deploying technology-based solutions to combat losses in the prepaid meter vending system and to monitor and protect physical infrastructure, particularly copper-related components.
He then addressed a question about scaling up renewables, stating that the department was doing a lot of work, especially in improving the regulatory environment. He reiterated that major bottlenecks in the Independent Power Producer (IPP) programme included delays in BID codes, grid access connections, and ultimately the contracting process once financial structures were in place. The department was actively working to reduce timelines across this entire value chain, which would enable faster commissioning of renewable energy projects.
Regarding energy efficiency incentives for households and businesses, he said that implementing energy efficiency measures inherently brought incentives because reducing energy consumption lowered electricity bills, which was itself an incentive to households.
He also responded to a question about the ability to resell energy, saying that this was generally accepted in principle and was a policy direction they were pursuing. The major challenge, he explained, was the need to install bi-directional smart meters capable of measuring energy flow both from and back to the grid, including the timing of usage and appropriate tariffs. He said the department, together with Eskom, was working—especially under the district distribution agency agreements—to deploy smart meters that would enable households to resell excess energy back into the system.
On the timeline for the universal access program, he agreed there were two key points: first, they were developing a methodology to fast-track the process, including creating a centralised Project Management Office (PMO) to reduce turnaround times. Second, this centralised model would allow them to efficiently use the Medium Term Framework (MTF) allocations, which included over R12 billion for the current financial year.
He explained that they were engaging with Development Finance Institutions (DFIs), such as the Development Bank of Southern Africa (DBSA), to explore using the MTF as collateral or a basis to secure upfront financing to accelerate household connections. As the government’s fiscal allocations came through over the next three years, they would service the front-loaded investments. This approach combined technical, financial, and execution solutions, and the scale of bulk delivery would generate economies of scale, thereby reducing infrastructure delivery costs.
He further noted that the scale of the program created opportunities to focus on industrialisation and localisation, referencing the Minister of Domestic Affairs and Trade’s point about how the South African Renewable Energy Masterplan (SARAM) framework could guide interventions to strengthen manufacturing value chains and inputs required for project delivery.
He concluded by noting that these addressed the questions, except for specific ones related to Koeberg and the cost-benefit analysis for nuclear energy.
Mr Zizamele Mbambo, DDG: Nuclear Energy, DEE, said that he wanted to begin with a question that had been raised regarding Koeberg, specifically about when Koeberg would be functional. He stated that it was important to highlight to the committee that Koeberg was one of the most efficient nuclear power stations within the Eskom fleet and that it was operational.
He further explained that, in July of the previous year, the National Nuclear Regulator had granted Koeberg Unit 1 the license to operate for an additional 20 years, extending its operation up to 2044. As a result, that unit was now operational under the life extension program. Regarding Unit 2, he stated that Eskom was actively working on extending its operational life and had already submitted the safety case for Unit 2 to the regulator. A decision from the regulator was expected by November of the current year. He emphasised that the station continued to supply clean baseload electricity to the grid and was operational, reaffirming that it remained one of the key power stations for stabilising the grid.
He mentioned that there had also been a question, which he believed had been addressed by the Deputy Minister (DM), about small modular reactors and their implementation to repurpose coal-fired power plants. He said he would not repeat that point. Similarly, he acknowledged that the DM had addressed a question regarding radioactive waste management, providing a comprehensive response about the country’s processes for handling such waste, and he chose not to dwell on that topic.
He went on to refer to another question concerning the socio-economic cost-benefit analysis report and the mechanisms that would be established to ensure transparency in the procurement process for up to 10 gigawatts of nuclear power, as well as matters related to the Pebble Bed Modular Reactor (PBMR). He emphasised the importance of noting that the socio-economic cost-benefit analysis report supporting the procurement of the 10 gigawatts was largely driven by the recognition that nuclear energy formed part of South Africa’s energy mix, especially for providing clean baseload electricity.
He stated that investigations had been conducted and they had justified the need to upscale nuclear capacity to ensure a viable nuclear industry that encompassed all aspects of the nuclear cycle. He added that achieving a full, sustainable nuclear industrialisation required the identified capacity as a minimum.
He further noted that, to ensure transparency in the process, the key mechanism would be effective communication. He stressed the importance of having a comprehensive communication plan and engaging stakeholders throughout the decision-making process related to the advancement of the nuclear program. He believed this would help ensure transparency and stakeholder involvement in every step taken. According to him, the department was committed to communicating openly with stakeholders and to demystifying nuclear energy.
He explained that the department had developed and was implementing a communication strategy, and that all the relevant state-owned entities involved in the nuclear sector had their own communication strategies as well. He stressed the need for coherence and collaboration across these efforts to maintain open and consistent communication about decisions concerning the nuclear procurement program.
He then addressed the issue of the Pebble Bed Modular Reactor (PBMR) Act, describing it as a very important matter. He reminded the committee that South Africa had been a leader in developing small modular reactor technology in the form of the PBMR. He recounted that, in 2010, the Cabinet had decided to scale down the PBMR project and place it under care and maintenance, to preserve its intellectual property and assets while the broader state policy framework was still being developed.
However, he observed that there had since been a global increase in the development of small modular reactors, some of which followed the PBMR model. He said that South Africa believed it was well-positioned to benefit from this trend and that it was time to revisit the 2010 decision. He indicated that the department intended to recommend to Cabinet that the state-owned company be removed from care and maintenance, to allow the country to develop the PBMR technology further.
He shared that the department had been working closely with NECSA and Eskom to transfer the PBMR from Eskom to NECSA so that NECSA could continue developing the technology. He expressed confidence that this represented the future of small modular reactors (SMRs) in South Africa. He concluded by saying that work in this regard was at an advanced stage and that the department would be making a formal recommendation to Cabinet to lift the PBMR from care and maintenance, in order to place South Africa at the forefront of developing cutting-edge technologies within the SMR space.
Minister Ramokgopa said that his colleagues had comprehensively addressed the questions, but he wanted to respond to a specific set of questions related to the filling of vacancies. He explained that the process of filling vacancies could only be initiated at the beginning of the current financial year. Before that, they were unable to proceed because they did not have a budget vote. He emphasised that they had to wait for the proper financial year to begin to be organised as a standalone department from a budgeting standpoint.
He added that, secondly, it was necessary to complete the national macro reorganisation process, a process led by the Department of Public Service and Administration (DPSA) and the Presidency. That process, he explained, was crucial in determining the distribution of resources as the Department of Mineral Resources and Energy (DMRE) was being untangled. It helped to clarify which resources would move with the new department and which would remain with the mineral and petroleum resources division.
He noted that it was only after the completion of that process that they could proceed with advertising vacancies. He said that they had already drafted the advertisement for the Director-General (DG) position and had planned to publish it as early as April or May. However, they were advised by the DPSA to hold off, as there was an ongoing process related to the regrading of positions across all Deputy Director-General (DDG) roles.
He mentioned that he had personally spoken with his counterpart at DPSA to stress the urgency of their situation and to request a special dispensation, as they were currently without permanent leadership. Echoing the Deputy Minister’s earlier point, he acknowledged the excellent work being done by the interim DG, but emphasised the need to stabilise the organisation. He pointed out that, as of that moment, they did not have a Chief Financial Officer (CFO) either, and had to have one seconded from Sanedi. He stated firmly that an organisation could not function in such a manner.
He added that he planned to continue engaging with Nkosi Buthelezi to ensure that the process was expedited. He clarified that while the department initiates the appointment, the DPSA technically makes the appointment and is responsible for the financing. He said he felt it was important to explain this point, particularly regarding the DG position.
Moving on to the rest of the organisation, he explained that, as a general rule in this reorganisation process—resulting from the creation of this and other departments—there was no new money being allocated. All departments were expected to draw from the same existing funding pool. He stated that those present on the call were already aware of the budget-related difficulties they were facing. As a result, there were significant demands on the budget, which meant that many of the positions they ideally wanted to create would not be funded, even if they were formally established.
He said the department had accepted that no new money would be made available, and so they had designed more innovative approaches to securing additional resources. These approaches did not involve adding more positions to their organisational structure or drawing from the national fiscus. Instead, they were working with partners to second resources and make funds available that could be channelled through some of the state-owned enterprises (SOEs). Through this method, they could recruit through SOEs to bring in the resources necessary to fulfil their responsibilities.
He explained that in other parts of the world, it was not necessarily the case that resources were housed within core departments, but rather that the resources were accessible and available through various channels. He stated that whether the resources were at Sanedi or Eskom, they were looking for the most effective way to work within current constraints to address the situation.
He concluded that he wanted to highlight these challenges—not as a complaint or grievance session—but rather to demonstrate their choice to find constructive and innovative solutions. The department was focused on plugging the gaps creatively to ensure it could carry out its mandate effectively despite the financial and structural limitations. He ended by saying he would leave it there, as the team had done an excellent job addressing the questions on his behalf.
Closing Remarks
The Chairperson expressed his gratitude to the Minister, Deputy Minister, and the team led by the Acting Director-General for their responses to the questions, concerns, and comments raised by honourable members. He stated that the Select Committee had been concerned that, despite more than a year having passed since the start of the seventh administration, the Minister had not yet attended any of their meetings, particularly the physical meetings. He said they were glad to have had the Minister attend that meeting, as it sent a positive message.
He acknowledged, in the Minister’s presence, that the Deputy Minister had been holding the fort and that her presence in their meetings had been very helpful. While he clarified that political leadership was not expected to be present at every meeting, he emphasised the importance of their availability when requested. He explained that certain decisions and discussions required political leadership for the committee to make meaningful progress.
He then referenced a report—specifically one of the slides, possibly slide 15—which addressed the gas master plan and its draft implementation. He said it prompted him to wonder about the alignment between departments, especially after attending a meeting the previous day or two prior, led by Minister Mantashe, where Shell gas and related matters were discussed. He questioned how the two departments—energy and mineral resources—communicated on matters of gas, and whether gas fell under energy or mineral resources. He admitted that these questions were still on his mind, but expected they would receive proper clarity on these issues in future meetings.
He thanked the Minister again for his presence and acknowledged that it had been a long day for everyone. He extended his appreciation to the Minister, Deputy Minister, the Acting DG, and the rest of the team for making themselves available. He noted that the people of South Africa were unaware that, at that late hour—23:44—they were still in session. He pointed out that the public likely assumed they had all finished work for the day and gone home, while in fact they were still working hard to find solutions that would improve the lives of South Africans and address the country's pressing challenges.
He stated that, since the Minister’s appointment, there had been a noticeable turnaround within the department. He mentioned that there was general acknowledgement of this shift by ordinary South Africans, particularly regarding the issue of load shedding. He reflected that, by that point, the meeting should have long been disrupted by blackouts, as had often been the case in the past. However, due to the steady supply of electricity, interruptions had become so rare that people now complained when the power went out, because it was no longer considered normal. He praised the Minister, the Deputy Minister, and their team for the substantial work they had done.
He then took the opportunity to propose to the members that they adjourn the meeting and defer the consideration of the minutes to the next meeting, so as not to prolong the session any further. He asked if there was agreement from the honourable members to defer the minutes; if not, he said he would release the Minister, Deputy Minister, and the rest of the team, while the remaining members continued with the meeting.
Ms Boshoff expressed her agreement with the proposal to defer the minutes, and Ms M Dhlamini (ANC, Free State) seconded the motion.
He then confirmed that the minutes would be dealt with in the next meeting and officially adjourned the session until that time. He thanked everyone again, joking that it was almost time to say "good morning" due to the late hour, and closed the meeting by saying, "Good afternoon, good evening."
The meeting was adjourned.
