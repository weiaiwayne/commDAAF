Meeting Summary
Tourism Sector Masterplan
The Portfolio Committee on Tourism convened to receive briefings from the Department of Tourism on the recently gazetted White Paper for the Development and Promotion of Tourism and the Tourism Sector Masterplan.
The Department outlined the objectives of the revised White Paper that had been gazetted on 4 October 2024 following Cabinet approval on 18 September 2024. The Committee welcomed the updated policy as essential to address the sector's current challenges, though it noted the near three-decade gap since the last revision in 1996. The new White Paper aimed to align South Africa’s tourism sector with modern technological advancements and better position it to capitalise on economic opportunities.
The Department highlighted key issues identified in recent reviews by National Treasury and the Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development, which pointed to an underperformance of the tourism sector relative to its potential. Additionally, the Tourism Business Council of South Africa’s 2022 Revised Growth Strategy emphasised that South Africa lags behind comparable countries in tourism growth despite the sector's vast potential.
The Department discussed the White Paper’s role in fostering sector growth and transformation, with goals to promote year-round tourism, economic contribution, and job creation, especially focusing on women and youth empowerment. The policy also emphasises resilience against crises, proposing measures for risk mitigation, better data gathering, and enhanced responsiveness to both domestic and regional markets. Other priorities include community engagement, sector inclusivity, skills development, and creating a regulatory environment that adapts to technological shifts and attracts investment to bolster tourism infrastructure.
The Department also outlined its next steps for implementation, including efforts to ensure policy alignment across government levels and within the industry. Planned amendments to the Tourism Act (2014) and National Tourism Sector Strategy (2016–2026) were cited as necessary to support the policy's objectives.
The Department briefed the Committee on the Tourism Sector Masterplan, explaining how the three-year Tourism Sector Recovery Plan, developed in response to COVID-19, is aligned with the national Economic Reconstruction and Recovery Plan (ERRP). Approved by Cabinet in April 2021, the TSRP aims to support public employment, green economy projects, infrastructure development, and skills development, particularly for women and youth. The Committee was informed that implementation, ongoing since 2021/22, has already yielded positive outcomes, with several sectors approaching pre-pandemic performance levels.
Committee Members praised the presentations, calling for regular updates on the progress of the White Paper and Masterplan. They underscored the urgency of implementing a policing strategy for tourist hotspots, especially as the tourism season has commenced. Members further urged the Department to address the bureaucratic challenges that hinder community access to tourism funding, particularly in rural areas.
Meeting report
Opening remarks
The Chairperson accepted apologies from the Minister of Tourism, Ms Patricia De Lille, Ms G Mchunu (MK) and Ms E Freeman (PA). She announced the Committee would receive a presentation from the Department of Tourism on the

Deputy Minister’s
introduction
Deputy Minister of Tourism, Ms Maggie Sotyu, said that the day’s presentations would discuss the White Paper, which Cabinet had approved for implementation on 4 October 2024, and the Tourism Sector Masterplan (TSMP), which Cabinet had approved at the end of 2023.
Department of Tourism
briefing on the
Policy Review Process
Mr Victor Tharage, Director-General (DG), Department of Tourism (DT), said that presentations would be given by Ms Aneme Malan, Deputy Director-General (DDG): Tourism Research, Policy and International Relations, DT (Masterplan discussion), and Mr Senzo Nkala, Director: Policy Planning and Strategy, DT (Policy Review Process), noting that as of the 1 November 2024, Mr Nkala would be receiving an internal promotion to Chief Director for Policy and Strategy.
The presentation focused on the White Paper on the Development and Promotion of Tourism in South Africa, which was gazetted on 4 October 2024.
Tourism Policy Review
- Reviewed 1996 White Paper to align with modern challenges and opportunities.
- Consultation with 26 national departments, provinces, and the private sector.
- White Paper gazetted on 4 October 2024.
Tourism Performance Issues
- Sector contributes 7.2% to South Africa's gross domestic product (GDP) but underperforms relative to potential.
- Challenges include crime, unregulated short-term rentals, transformation gaps, and regulatory bottlenecks.
Economic Impact and Recovery
- Tourism generated R121bn in domestic tourism in 2023 and R95bn from international tourism, creating 1.7m jobs.
- The Economic Reconstruction and Recovery Plan (ERRP) and National Development Plan (NDP) incorporate tourism to boost economic recovery post-COVID.
Policy Goals
- Boost year-round tourism, create jobs, promote inclusivity, and improve crisis resilience.
- Focus on transforming and de-risking the sector with innovative technology and improved governance.
Key Thematic Areas
- Includes tourism governance, responsible tourism, and sustainable development.
- Prioritises rural tourism, tourism safety, immigration issues, and easing visa restrictions for key markets like India and China.
Budget and Strategic Investments
- R1.2bn was allocated to the Tourism Equity Fund to boost transformation and development of small, medium and micro enterprises (SMMEs), particularly in rural and township tourism.
- Government committed R30m towards demand-led skills development in the sector, particularly for artificial intelligence (AI) and digital transformation.
Next Steps
- Ongoing consultations with municipalities, provinces, and the industry to implement the White Paper.
- Planned amendments to the 2014 Tourism Act to incorporate new policy directives.
Alignment of the National Tourism Sector Strategy (NTSS) with policy objectives.

Briefing on the
TSMP
The TSMP outlines a comprehensive approach to revitalising and transforming South Africa's tourism industry. It is built on three strategic pillars: demand generation, supply creation, and destination management. It aims to enhance job creation and economic recovery by targeting a 52% recovery rate in domestic tourism by 2024 and reaching 94% of pre-pandemic employment levels.
Economic Contributions
- Tourism contributed R234.7 billion to the GDP in 2022, equal to 3.5% of the national GDP.
- The domestic tourism market showed substantial recovery, significantly bolstered by the renewed focus on promoting travel within South Africa.
Support for SMMEs and Inclusivity
- Through partnerships with the Tourism Business Council of South Africa and provincial stakeholders, the TSMP encourages investment in SMMEs (Small, Medium, and Micro Enterprises) across all provinces, particularly in previously underserved communities.
- A goal of 50% SMME involvement in tourism projects was set to ensure broader inclusivity and sustainability.
Green Initiatives and Digital Transformation
- In line with global green tourism trends, TSMP incorporates environmental sustainability through eco-friendly practices and aims to reduce carbon footprint in tourism infrastructure.
- Emphasis on digital transformation, including the adoption of e-visas and AI-driven travel facilitation, aligns with efforts by peer nations and positions South Africa to stay competitive in a digitally evolving tourism landscape.
Governance Structure and Monitoring
- The TSMP establishes a public-private governance structure with the Department of Tourism and the private sector to jointly oversee project implementation.
- Enhanced monthly monitoring and quarterly reporting procedures are part of efforts to improve oversight, ensure accountability, and address underperformance promptly.
Event Bidding and Global Presence
- With the support of the Tourism Master Plan, the Department successfully bid for significant events in 2024, boosting South Africa’s profile as an attractive tourist destination.
- The tourism industry has set ambitious targets for international collaboration and increasing global awareness of South African destinations, which should positively impact foreign direct investment and tourism flow.
The TSMP emphasises equitable growth by integrating township tourism, promoting agritourism in partnership with local governments, and ensuring fair representation across provinces, aiming for a tourism sector that is both resilient and inclusive.
Way Forward: The Department will align its budget in 2025/26 to fund TSMP implementation fully, with additional monitoring to ensure that it supports job creation, SMME growth, and overall economic resilience.

Director-General’s input
Mr Tharage put forward the idea that the 1996 White Paper was ahead of its time as there were many things that were still relevant now. He clarified that the R1.2bn for the Tourism Equity Fund is not all the Tourism Department’s held budget, stating that the Departmental budget is almost half of that. The remainder of the money is made up of the loan component that comes from the Small Enterprise Finance Agency (SEFA) and what SEFA leverages from other banks, be that development banks or commercial banks.
Deputy Minister’s input
Deputy Minister Sotyu addressed the Tourism Equity Fund. She observed that one of the weaknesses of the Fund that she and Minister De Lille had observed was that it was very difficult to manage due to the requirements that people need to meet before they can receive funding. She informed the Committee that the DG and the team had held a strategic workshop recently where this concern had been raised.
Discussion
Ms L Ligaraba (ANC) drew attention to what seemed like a mistake regarding figures on slide 8 of the presentation on the TSMP. The number of adults (222) and the number of youth (202) employed in maintenance and beautification programmes in 2023 did not add up to the total given (426).
Ms E Linde (DA) thought that the Department was taking steps in the right direction but that there was a lot of work ahead for the tourism sector. She sought clarity regarding what the presentation meant when it stated that “the Department is creating and enabling an environment of regulation that addresses short-term rentals and a framework of reporting.” She asked what the Department sought to achieve in this regard, particularly concerning the White Paper. On the topic of the Masterplan, she asked what has been done regarding the “tourism tech” mentioned in the presentation. She recalled that tourism tech had been part of the Tourism Sector Recovery Plan and asked if it was also a part of the Masterplan.
Additionally, she asked about the Green Tourism Incentive Programme applications. Had they been implemented? What had been done and how had it been done? She asked when the 5-year medium term plan would be presented and when the programme of action would be brought before the Committee. Lastly, she questioned where the Portfolio Committee would ever receive reports from the Executive Oversight Committee and the Steering Committee.
Ms L Tito (EFF) raised concerns regarding people’s ability to get funding, especially for people in rural areas. She praised the Minister and Deputy Minister for their efforts to make funding more accessible. However, she noted that many people do not even have access to the internet, emphasising that the Department needs to go to the ground and ensure the funding is there and proper education is given to local communities on how to access funding.
Mr S Moodley (MK) took issue with the fact that the previous White Paper had been approved in 1994, and questioned why it took so long to realise tourism was not working. He asked what the Department had done to create a place where people could pay and go see things and why South Africa’s tourism sector was not making much money. He argued that corruption that took place through previous Ministers needs to be considered in relation to issues that the Department now faces. He said that he liked what he had read in the presentation, stating that the policies and the Masterplan are great, but there needs to be more than just words. He emphasised that the Department needs to build more places, rather than just focusing on promoting things like Table Mountain and Drakensberg which are God-given tourist attractions. He said there is a need to look at what the Department has built and what has been done, emphasising the overall need for discussions about how the Department and the Committee can make tourism work.
Ms H Ismail (DA) was also interested in seeing the Executive Oversight Committee report. She had sent multiple requests for reports on various programmes of the Department. She suggested that the Committee develop an action plan so that they do not need to ask for reports at every meeting. She noted that SANParks had been boosted with funds, putting forward the idea that the PC could initiate joint oversight visits with the Department of Forestry, Fisheries and the Environment (DFFE) to investigate the condition of some tourism facilities, as some facilities were in a bad state.
Ms T Mchunu (ANC) was grateful for the presentations. She acknowledged the significant efforts involved in the policy reviews and remarked on the extensive work that had gone into drafting and refining them. She addressed the Deputy Minister's earlier comments on reducing red tape, specifically in relation to funding access. Would the forthcoming action plan prioritise simplifying bureaucratic processes, especially for SMMEs from previously disadvantaged communities? Additionally, she sought clarification on whether the Memorandum of Understanding with SEFA could be structured to further streamline funding access for these groups. She then raised the potential establishment of a dedicated tourism police unit, noting the Minister’s previous statements on this opportunity. Had a feasibility study been conducted for this initiative? Had steps been taken to ensure the unit would work in collaboration with the South African Police Service (SAPS) rather than duplicating its efforts? On the White Paper and Master Plan, she wondered whether the Department’s expanded responsibilities and objectives might prompt National Treasury to increase its budget allocation. Referring to the Master Plan's strengths, weaknesses, opportunities and threats (SWOT) analysis, she highlighted threats such as climate change, service delivery challenges, and interdepartmental dependencies. She stressed the need to involve the DFFE and the Climate Change Commission led by the President in addressing climate-related threats impacting tourism. She also suggested considering a broader executive oversight structure to manage the identified threats, pointing out that her calculations showed room for additional members beyond the suggested cap of ten. Finally, she recommended including representatives from service delivery departments, such as the Department of Water and Sanitation, to ensure that identified threats are adequately managed.
Responses by the Department of Tourism
Mr Nkala provided a thorough response to Ms Linde’s question about the regulation of short-term rentals, emphasising the need to address the current regulatory gap in the sector. He acknowledged that the absence of clear regulations has posed challenges for smaller tourism enterprises, which adhere to general business requirements while competing with unregulated short-term rentals. This regulatory imbalance has led to growing concerns among formal accommodation providers who follow standard business laws but find themselves competing with informal short-term rentals operating under no formal oversight. To create a fair and competitive environment, the Department is exploring "proportionate regulation," meaning targeted, balanced guidelines that cater to both short-term rentals and existing tourism businesses.
He explained that the proposed regulatory framework includes a differentiated approach to short-term rentals, with an emphasis on developing threshold-based guidelines informed by international standards. The Department has reviewed global best practices to assess regulatory measures that could best suit South Africa’s tourism landscape. For instance, in certain countries, regulations stipulate that individuals cannot operate more than one property as a short-term rental unless they register as a formal business entity. This distinction is intended to support long-term rental markets and prevent large property owners from monopolising housing supply for short-term rental purposes, which can lead to rising rental costs and the displacement of long-term tenants.
During the Department's consultations, estate agents expressed concerns about issues arising from unregulated short-term rentals. They reported that property owners often purchase multiple properties for short-term rental use without involving agents, leading to neighbourhood disruptions such as noise and nuisance complaints. Stakeholders in the formal tourism sector, including estate agents, have asked the Department to include safeguards in the policy to help manage these issues, given that they directly impact residential areas and community welfare.
To address these challenges, he emphasised the Department's intent to bring policy certainty to this area. The proposed differentiated approach aims to ensure short-term rentals remain an accessible pathway for individuals entering the tourism sector while maintaining a level playing field for existing, formalised tourism businesses. The Department intends to initiate extensive consultations with key stakeholders, focusing on how threshold-based rules can be implemented to balance market access with regulation and public interest.
Responding to feedback on the delayed review of the Tourism White Paper, he acknowledged that stakeholders have been seeking clarity for some time. However, he noted that the tourism sector has made considerable strides since the White Paper's initial implementation. He highlighted that, as intended by the White Paper, tourism has been elevated as a priority sector, contributing significantly to South Africa's GDP and workforce. Recent data from 2022 indicates that tourism now supports one in twenty-one jobs nationwide, a significant achievement aligning with the White Paper’s goals of economic impact and employment creation.
Regarding the potential for increased budget allocations to the tourism sector, he stated that the revised White Paper aims to refine the tourism business case presented to National Treasury, thereby positioning the Department more favourably for budgetary support. The White Paper also underscores the importance of exploring alternative funding mechanisms. For example, the Industrial Development Corporation (IDC) could be leveraged to conduct feasibility studies on potential tourism projects, reducing direct costs for the tourism sector while ensuring informed investment decisions. This approach aligns with the White Paper’s emphasis on utilising existing financial institutions to support tourism’s growth, allowing the Department to maximise impact while safeguarding resources.
Ms Malan addressed the additional concerns of the Members. She said that the Tourism Masterplan requires comprehensive sector-wide involvement from both public and private entities, as well as from provincial government, due to the fact that tourism is a concurrent governmental function. Therefore, the Masterplan’s implementation progress is tracked across the sector, beyond the Department’s direct contributions.
Addressing Ms Linde’s questions, she highlighted the Department’s tech incubators, acknowledging valuable proposals received. She agreed that it would be valuable for the Committee to have a detailed report on their specific achievements. On the Executive Oversight Committee reports, she confirmed that the outcomes of the Executive Oversight Committee are incorporated into the Department’s quarterly reports.
She addressed the Deputy Minister’s remarks on reducing bureaucratic barriers to funding access. The Department had an ongoing collaboration with the Department of Small Business Development (DSBD) aimed at simplifying access, particularly for SMMEs from previously disadvantaged communities. In response to Ms Ismail’s report request, she assured the Committee that any pending reports would be provided.
In response to Ms Mchunu’s questions, she noted that the Memorandum of Understanding with SEFA is being reviewed for better support to SMMEs. She said that under the Tourism Monitors Programme and in collaboration with SAPS, the Department aims to bolster citizen and tourist safety. Finally, she expressed appreciation for Ms Mchunu's suggestion to expand interdepartmental collaboration and agreed to consider involving additional departments in relevant discussions.
Mr Nkala added that the White Paper on Safety proposes a specialised police unit focused on tourism safety. He explained that this proposal emerged from benchmarking against other countries, particularly those with heightened police visibility in tourism hotspots. These countries prioritise visibility, benefiting not only tourists but also the surrounding communities. He noted, however, that no local studies had been conducted on the matter. Initially, stakeholders strongly advocated for establishing a tourism police unit, but further discussions highlighted the need to investigate its feasibility before incorporating it into the policy. He said that emphasising the importance of feasibility studies was likely the Minister’s intent, as best practices show that countries with dedicated tourism safety measures tend to have specialised support for tourist protection.
The Chairperson noted the administrative and political aspects of the concerns raised.
Mr Tharage clarified that the primary policy challenges concerning short-term rentals relate to employment. He noted instances of industry players expressing interest in rezoning their hotels to operate like short-term rentals, allowing them to generate similar revenue without government taxes or obligations such as Unemployment Insurance Fund (UIF) contributions. Employment and decent work were significant concerns in this context. Another key issue was ensuring equitable participation in the sector by those not directly benefiting. He also highlighted the need for differentiation in policy. For example, in areas without a single tourism product, individuals might use their properties for accommodation, a scenario warranting different treatment from cases where investors purchase multiple properties near hotels, potentially causing hotels to decline and impacting jobs. Differentiation would thus be based on property usage. He said a 90-day threshold was proposed; beyond this, individuals would need to register as businesses. He noted that while other social dynamics were emerging, they did not fall under the current policy scope. He clarified that while there was previously a specific name associated with this policy, it was not about any particular company but aimed at addressing short-term rental facilitation broadly.
He assured the Committee that information on tourism tech would be provided, explaining that internal processes could take time. He cited examples, such as national parks and Robben Island, where measures like photo-voltaic panels had resulted in savings in electricity generation costs.
Regarding the Medium Term Development Plan, he explained that it would be finalised in collaboration with the whole of government, culminating in the State of the Nation Address (SONA). This address would guide the completion of departmental plans, after which the Committee would receive a briefing, enabling it to make budget recommendations. While unable to provide an exact date, he estimated that this would occur around February, aligning with the start of the financial year on 1 February.
He also referenced the Deputy Minister’s point on reviewing support mechanisms for process efficiency, mentioning cases where zoning applications had been pending for up to 14 years. Without confirmed classifications, SEFA funds could not be disbursed, as in cases where a bed-and-breakfast with over 12 rooms might be reclassified as a lodge or guest house. He emphasised that these issues, often arising at the local government level, required further examination to ensure operational businesses could receive the necessary support.
He addressed the topic of Executive Oversight Committee reports. He could not commit to sharing all details, but he noted that the report on the implementation of the Masterplan and challenges encountered would be presented to the Portfolio Committee. However, he expressed reservations about sharing nuanced discussions with stakeholders publicly, as doing so might inhibit stakeholders' openness in future engagements. He assured the Committee that the Department would be accountable for its performance commitments.
Responding to Ms Ismail’s query, he highlighted the Department’s collaborative work with SANParks, and said that joint oversight with SANParks would be beneficial in gathering insights from Committee Members. He referenced recent visits to project sites in the Free State that exemplified the productive partnership between the Department and SANParks. He noted that post-COVID, significant efforts had been made to preserve the properties within the parks. With additional resources, this work could continue and expand.
On the question of a specialised police unit, he confirmed that the Department had a close working relationship with SAPS. This included regular communication with the Police Commissioner, facilitated through the Deputy National Commissioner for Policing, who coordinates these matters. He recalled that the top SAPS leadership, led by the Commissioner, attended the signing of the implementation plan at his office. This demonstrated SAPS’s commitment to the collaboration. He clarified that while SAPS holds the policing mandate in the country and policing policy remains under the authority of the Minister of Police, the Department of Tourism’s role is to support and provide SAPS with relevant information and technical input. He also recalled a previous joint session of the Police and Tourism Portfolio Committees where the Police Commissioner suggested exploring avenues for collaboration.
He acknowledged the need for increased financial resources and mentioned the upcoming medium-term budget speech by the Minister of Finance. He said that, while uncertain of the budget's content, there were expectations for prudent resource management, as mandated by Cabinet. He confirmed that the Minister of Tourism had emphasised maximising impact with limited resources, and underscored the importance of forming partnerships and influencing other Departments to consider the Department of Tourism’s needs within their own projects.
Responses by the
Deputy Minister
Deputy Minister Sotyu responded to Ms Tito’s concerns by acknowledging and agreeing that many people in rural communities lack awareness of Department programmes and thus do not participate in them. She emphasised her plans to conduct a roadshow, targeting rural areas in particular, to improve access to information about tourism opportunities. She noted that she had already visited Gauteng and KwaZulu-Natal to engage with communities and share relevant programme details, observing that while many people are eager to participate in the sector, they often lack access to necessary information.
Addressing Ms Mchunu’s question about Executive Oversight committees, she drew from her experience overseeing the ocean economy and forestry sector Masterplans in her previous role. She explained that in those cases, she chaired the Executive Oversight committee, which finalised reports for the Portfolio Committee as needed. This committee was relatively small, comprising representatives from industry, organised labour, and various departments. Reports from each focus area chairperson were reviewed quarterly, or monthly if urgent, to monitor progress. She suggested that, once the implementation of the tourism TSMP commences, a similar process be considered, with periodic progress reports to the Portfolio Committee.
On the topic of policing in the tourism sector, she agreed with the DG’s points but emphasised that, given the constitutional limitations, the Department could not assign policing powers to any entity other than SAPS. She cautioned that tourism monitors are not equipped to counter armed threats and highlighted the urgency of working with SAPS on policing strategies specific to tourism. She noted that security concerns had already impacted tourism, with cancellations by visitors to the Wild Coast due to crime. She warned of potential financial losses over the festive season if security issues were not addressed, and stressed the importance of collaborating with SAPS to formulate a strategic response.
Reflecting on her previous Department’s approach, she described a model where reservists were deployed to guard government buildings, freeing police officers to focus on broader crime prevention. She explained that the Department of Public Service and Administration had supported the initiative, allowing the deployment of reservists on a salary scale. She suggested this approach could inform a similar strategy for tourism safety, as the current police capacity is insufficient to cover the tourism sector. She expressed concern over the lack of action on tourism safety, stressing that immediate attention to this issue was essential to prevent further harm to the sector.
Concluding remarks
The Chairperson expressed appreciation to the Department for its presentations and responses to the Committee’s questions. She emphasised the importance of joint oversight between departments and noted that early planning is essential. She suggested that engagement with other portfolio committees should occur not only at the departmental level but also at the portfolio committee level. She indicated that the Department would be expected to update the Committee on the progress of the Masterplan’s implementation.
She stressed the urgency of a strategy for policing tourism areas, describing it as both crucial and time-sensitive. She encouraged the Ministers and Deputy Ministers of both Tourism and Police to consider leveraging their experience with volunteers and reservists as a potential strategy. However, she noted that it would be up to the Minister and Deputy Minister of Tourism to coordinate with the Minister of Police on this proposal. She reminded the Committee that South Africa’s tourism season begins in September, when many international tourists arrive during their holiday period.
Addressing the Department, she offered the Committee’s support in facilitating collaboration with other departments if needed. Would a joint meeting with, for instance, the Department of Home Affairs, be beneficial, considering the impact of visa processes on tourism?
Ms Linde proposed that the Tourism Portfolio Committee hold a joint meeting with the Portfolio Committee on Police to receive an update on SAPS’s initiatives to ensure tourism safety. She noted that while the Minister had indicated ongoing engagements with SAPS, the Portfolio Committee lacked detailed information on these discussions. She emphasised the importance of being fully informed about SAPS’s measures to address tourism safety concerns.
The Chairperson noted that before approaching other Portfolio Committees to request oversight presentations, it was essential for the Committee to complete its internal review of relevant issues. She suggested that, in the near future, the Minister of Tourism could be asked to elaborate on collaboration efforts with the Ministry of Police before any further external requests. She then thanked the Department representatives, allowing them to take their leave.
Committee business
The Chairperson announced that she had received correspondence from the House Chair confirming that the week of 18–22 November 2024 would be Committee Week. Additionally, next week would be Constituency Week, during which no meetings would be held. She noted that the week of 18 November could be used for a full-day meeting if the Committee deemed it necessary. While some committees would be addressing legislation, this Committee’s current focus on policy, part of the legislative framework, has been discussed today. She suggested that if feasible, the Minister could be available to brief the Committee on issues raised, given that the country was in the peak of the tourism season. However, if this proved impractical, alternative arrangements would be considered.
Ms Ismail inquired about the meeting originally scheduled for 5 November, when the Department of Tourism was to give a briefing. She asked if the content would be covered in the Committee's next meeting in the week of 18 November or if an alternative approach to reports would be implemented.
The Chairperson replied that they would accommodate all outstanding items within the day allocated by the House Chair, potentially scheduling the missed briefing from the Department for the morning session on 19 November. She confirmed that the programme would be reviewed and members would be advised accordingly.
The meeting was adjourned.
