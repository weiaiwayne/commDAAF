SA Post Office
In an in-person meeting, the Committee met with the Department of Communication and Digital Technologies and its entities to receive briefings on their 2023/24 annual performance plans. The entities present were the Independent Communications Authority of South Africa; the South African Post Office; the Film and Publication Board; the State Information Technology Agency; the National Electronic Media Institute of SA; Sentech; the Postbank; Broadband Infraco and the .za Domain Name Authority.
Members asked the Department how its strategies and objectives aligned with job creation, curbing under-development, and eradicating inequality.
The promised virtual schools in rural areas, digital migration and turning the Post Office around, were noted as empty promises that should compel the Department to go back to the drawing board, particularly regarding the Post Office. With bailout after bailout, the South African Post Office continued to go down, with 3 000 to 6 000 jobs on the line, yet a R2.4 billion bailout had been granted.
Members did not mince their words when expressing dissatisfaction over SAPO, saying they believed that the Department was not doing enough to resuscitate the Post Office because none of the strategies implemented before had addressed the root causes of SAPO’s woes.
Members also felt that the Department had too many entities with overlapping mandates. They suggested that some of these entities should be integrated to work smartly, effectively and efficiently. However, with the BBI/Sentech merger not yet finalised, leaving staff members at the entities uncertain about their future, Members had little to no hope of this ever coming to fruition. They also considered that the Department's entities did not seem aligned with the 2023 national development plan (NDP). They lamented the lack of impact of the entities, because no examples of the work and projects completed were showcased to Members. The substantive impact of the work of the Department and some of its entities was questioned.
Members also asked questions about the appointment of the Independent Communications Authority of SA (ICASA) board chairperson, and its role in the upcoming 2024 national elections; support to small, medium and micro enterprises (SMMEs) for the acquisition of spectrum; and the reasons behind the Film and Publication Board's (FPB’s) high attrition rate. They also probed how the Postbank aimed to attract entry-level clients and informal traders; how it planned to assist these types of clients in growing their businesses and manage money better; and how the grey-listing of South Africa affected the entity.

Mr Mondli Gungubele, Minister of Communications and Digital Technologies, said that the Ministry had to deal with the issues of the Department that had huge responsibilities for the future of the country.

Discussion
Mr T Gumbu (ANC) was pleased to hear that vacancies would be filled soon at the Department. He asked about the time frames relating to the release of the spectrum.
Mr M Mdakane (ANC) said he was interested to hear what was new in the strategy of the Department. Some of the matters symbolised the challenge of unemployment, under-development, and inequality. From what had been presented, it was strategic for the fiscus to reduce the number of unemployed.
Ms N Mazzone (DA) said that the country had been promised virtual schools in rural areas, digital migration by 2013, and that the Post Office would be turned around. They were now sitting with a possible bailout of R2.4 billion for the Post Office, and a possible job loss of 3 000 to 6 000. The Department needed to go back to the drawing board to figure out what the root cause of the problem was. Load-shedding had not made things easier for the Post Office either. She did not understand why Starlink was not welcomed in South Africa, and wanted to know why.
Ms T Bodlani (DA) said the Department had too many entities with cross-cutting mandates, making it difficult to effectively deliver its mandate. There should be a way to integrate and work smarter.
The National Development Plan (NDP) was supposed to be the blueprint for the country, but one finds that entities seemed to have misaligned their objectives with those of the NDP. How far was the process of appointing the ICASA chairperson? How was ICASA planning to assist in the upcoming provincial and national elections?
Ms Z Majozi (IFP) asked about the spectrum, and if the Department had partnered with or invited any small, medium and micro enterprises (SMMEs) to come on board for the spectrum as well, and what programmes had been 40% set aside for youth and women's development businesses.
Mr L Molala (ANC) said the Minister had raised two important issues, connectivity and skills development, but he wanted to know which target would be prioritised -- the target for connectivity in rural communities; the filling of vacancies at the Department; the cost of communication in South Africa in reference to the Competition Commission's report on data costs; the release of the audited report of ICASA; the status of the Broadband Infraco (BBI) and Sentech merger; and the SABC’s new board appearing before the Committee.
Ms N Khubheka (ANC) commented that Members were now counting on their last hope for the Post Office. Hopefully, the new turnaround strategy would be implemented to turn it around.
Responses
Ms Jordan-Dyani said there were regulatory requirements for anyone or entity that wants to operate in the country or deploy any network. This was covered in the Act, and those criteria must be met. The Department had engaged Starlink to understand the process it must go through to operate in the country. No application has been received from Starlink yet. The Department was doing its own investigation concerning the media statements doing the rounds on this issue.
As for the spectrum auction and litigation, competition assessment was one of the major issues the Department was being litigated on.
Ms Maluleke-Disemelo replied to the cost of communication or the data inquiry, and said that the matter was still before the court and being litigated. A huge chunk of the budget went towards the compensation of employees.

South African Post Office
The financials had been prepared against a backdrop of increased financial pressures, difficult trading conditions and ongoing liquidity challenges. SAPO's financial difficulties continued for the 2022/2023 financial year, with a loss of R2.3 billion forecast.
Its cash flow position was extremely dire, as creditors, medical aid contributions, pension fund contributions and South African Revenue Service (SARS) obligations remained unpaid and would continue not to be paid unless urgent financial assistance was provided. The confirmation for the financial assistance had not yet been confirmed by National Treasury.
The outstanding liabilities at 31 December 2022 amounted to R5.3 billion, and a further amount of R3.2 billion was owed to Postbank, resulting in total liabilities of R8.5 billion.
The monthly liabilities continue to increase due to the monthly losses, as monthly expenses continue to exceed monthly revenue. The unpaid trade liabilities and property rentals continue to negatively impact SAPO's ability to recover lost revenue and move to financial sustainability.
Revenue of R2.6 billion for the 2022/2023 financial year was projected to decline by R969 million due to lower postal services and financial services revenue. The South African Social Security Agency (SASSA) contract was ceded to Postbank from 1 October 2022, and the payments of grants at cash payment points were also transferred to Postbank.
Broadband Infraco(BBI)
The entity touched on its organisational overview; performance overview; human capacity; audit findings and budget information.
Its revenue analysis was highlighted in a pie chart, indicating its main sources of revenue, most of which would come from SA Connect contracted sales and the West African Cable System (WACS).
[See the presentation for details]
Discussion
Ms Bodlani asked the FPB about reskilling its employees and the 28% attrition rate, the importance of high morale at the workplace, the bloatedness of the entity and the new legislative mandate.
She said Members learn half of the things happening at SAPO through the media. It did not take the Committee into confidence. When the news of the provisional liquidation broke, Members learnt about it through the media. She hoped an urgent meeting would be convened with SAPO. Even today, there was no clear indication of what was really happening. It was worrying the way SAPO was treating the Committee. If they were going to be protected because things were
sub judice
, it was not assisting the Committee. What could Members say about this when they did not know any more than the public? She did not believe there was a truthful reflection by the DCDT about the state of SAPO, and she did not believe there was a truthful will to turn things around at the entity. The sense of hope in SAPO needed to be revisited as to where this organisation was going. R2.4 billion would be given to SAPO, yet SAPO continued to sink.
Ms A Mthembu (ANC) asked the FPB about its funding requirements.
Ms Mazzone said she had sat in the Rules Committee of Parliament for seven and half months rewriting the Rules of Parliament. During that process,
sub judice
was taken out of the Rules of Parliament and invoked Section 94 of the Powers and Privileges Act, which gives a Member of Parliament the right to question anyone or entity who appears before them. Thus,
sub judice
had fallen away and Members’ rights and privileges had come into effect. The
sub judice
law did not exist for Members of Parliament anymore. She told Members that she had spent a very long time testifying at the Zondo Commission. The last report of the Zondo Commission had, verbatim, her interpretation of how the rules of Parliament should work and change. The existential crisis in which South Africa found itself went beyond the Post Office – it directly affected the poor, the old and the vulnerable who needed to access the Post Office to collect their grants.
There were all these entities under the Department, and she did not know why they existed. Almost all of them were at financial risk. The only thing that had changed over the years was that the money had changed from millions to billions that get unaccounted for, or bailout money. R2.4 billion should be used to build homes, not funnelled to the Post Office. Therefore, at what point does one say it was enough, and initiate an inquiry? Why were these entities all unable to keep themselves sustainable?
The provisional liquidation of the Post Office may result in 3 000 to 6 000 job losses. SAPO and the Post Bank were victims of State Capture. South Africa was going to have riots, because citizens were already fed up with load-shedding, and if the Post Office goes under, riots may erupt.
Ms Bodlani asked a follow-up question about the BBI and Sentech merger and the collaboration of work that the two entities are already involved in.
Responses
Minister Gungubele said the issue may not be that the provisional liquidation was
sub judice
, but the Ministry was concerned about SAPO going down. No fewer than 700 SMMEs and more than 100 creditors depended on SAPO to survive. They were worried about sending mixed signals before engaging the relevant stakeholders. It did not stop the Committee from having a closed meeting and asking the kind of questions they wanted to ask. The discussion of this matter had the potential to undermine the process.
No one was running away from responding to questions from Members, but the Ministry was concerned about compromising the process by putting critical information pertaining to the process out in public whilst the process was still unfolding. Members were more than welcome to have a closed meeting to engage on this matter.
Ms Nomkhita Mona, Chief Executive Officer (CEO), SAPO, felt that Members had more comments than questions pertaining to the entity.
The Chairperson also echoed the sentiments of Ms Mazonne. There was a need to think about those who do not have any other option but to use the services from institutions of government.
Broadband Infraco
Mr Gift Zowa, CEO, BBI, replied that the entity existed to provide connectivity to the most marginalised and rural areas. The private sector did not have the incentive to supply these services to those areas, even though they were being fined for not providing services to the under-developed areas. BBI existed because of the failure to provide connectivity in areas where the private sector refuses.
As for the merger, BBI collaborated with Sentech, conducted regular meetings, and utilised each other’s personnel. The two entities were now occupying the same premises. The unions had been engaged continuously about the merger, and they were on board.
Minister Gungubele said there was a general problem in South Africa, where departments treated themselves as independent entities.
Ms Majozi said the Department must decide when the merger would happen because it created uncertainty for the employees.
Mr Philly Mapulane, Deputy Minister of Communications and Digital Technologies, said that the Department was still deciding where best to locate the Universal Service and Access Agency of South Africa (USASSA). For now, the Department was considering taking the funds to the Postbank, but that was not yet finalised.
Since the stabilisation of the FPB with the appointment of the CEO and other critical positions, the environment was now different. One could see the direction the entity was going.

State Information Technology Agency (SITA)
SITA briefed Members on its 2023/24 annual performance plan, which covered its strategic overview; performance information; revised strategic plan outcomes, indicators and targets; annual performance plan targets per programme; updated key risks and mitigations; budget and medium term expenditure framework (MTEF) estimates.
Its revenue for the 2023/24 financial year was estimated at R6.616 billion. This would be an overall revenue increase of 11%, compared to the forecast for 2022/23. The increase in revenue would be achieved through continued efforts to build on SITA’s business development strategies for revenue growth, harnessing more value and achieving a larger share of the current approved information communication technology (ICT) spend.
Discussion
Mr Mdakane said entities needed to show Members and provide examples of where they were making an impact. This was not assisting Members, because they could not go back and report to their constituencies on the actual work done through the approval of the budgets and the impact it had on the lives of the people. Members want to hear about examples of the transformed lives of South Africans with the money allocated to the Department and its entities, instead of overloading Members of Parliament with too much information that was not assisting in identifying the impact on the people.
Ms Mazzone said 90% of what she had seen today was not satisfactory at all -- it was extremely concerning. There was an element of energy in the way that officials approached their work, and entities were advertising when they appeared before the Committee to get their budgets approved. The substance of the work was questionable, however. Where did the money go? At what point did a forensic audit ask questions, and at what point did entities inform the Minister about where the money went?
Postbank aimed to attract entry-level clientele and informal traders, but it should aim to assist its clients in achieving their aspirations. An informal trader aspires to be registered and formal. It could copy the approach that Capitec used to attract its clientele. She supported the idea of teaching children at school about money. Many South Africans were heavily indebted, and one should educate people to keep them away from debt. South Africa must be the only country where a person could not be granted debt without having any debt. People start off in debt because the system requires them to already have debt when they apply for essential things like home loans and vehicle financing.
Ms Bodlani said SAPO’s financial difficulties may affect Postbank’s distribution channels, and asked the entity to elaborate on this. How did the Postbank plan to protect the vulnerable bankers or clients that it was targeting?
Government departments did not support other government departments. Often government departments would make use of the private sector instead of circulating the money within the state and amongst entities that could offer the same services. How was the grey-listing going to affect the Postbank? She wanted to know how Postbank intended to protect its investments and its clientele.
Ms Majozi asked NEMISA about its training of unemployed youth. In the previous financial year, this had involved 500 youths, but the target for the year had increased to 1 000. What happened after the young people had completed the training?
She asked ZADNA why it was in court with the former CEO. Why were vehicles purchased, and why were the employee costs so high?
How was the Postbank ensuring its clients were safe and protected, especially the most vulnerable and the pensioners? Were the automatic teller machines (ATMs) fully functioning, and was the Postbank resourced to ensure its ATMs were functioning well?
Due to time constraints, entities were requested to provide outstanding responses in writing.
The meeting was adjourned.
