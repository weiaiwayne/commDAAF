Date: 25 Feb 2026

Meeting Summary
Video
The Portfolio Committee on Human Settlements (National Assembly) met for a briefing by the
- Department of Human Settlements (DHS) and the Housing Development Agency (HDA) on the acquired abandoned and hijacked buildings
- Department of Human Settlements and the Social Housing Regulatory Authority (SHRA) on plans to deal with blocked projects; and
- National Housing Finance Corporation (NHFC) on governance and oversight as well as reasons for suspension of the Chief Executive Officer (CEO).
Ms Thembi Simelane, Minister of Human Settlements, said the meeting was in response to the many questions posed by the Committee in a previous meeting. The issues were mostly around governance and administration within the entities under the DHS. In particular, these entities were to provide clear details on their programmes to enhance service delivery. She told the Members that the maladministration within entities was being investigated.
The HDA presented a progress report on the five buildings they have acquired. They had no blocked projects. Their objective was to use human settlements to regenerate inner cities. Funding remained a challenge.
The NHFC made a detailed presentation on serious governance failures and the attempt by the newly installed board to turn the entity around. They provided more details on the investigations into more than 30 counts of alleged corruption and financial mismanagement. The NHFC also provided detailed explanation of the reasons for the suspension of executives. They provided extensive details on their loan book and how they plan to increase their pipeline.
The SHRA were going to present on blocked projects and recovery plans to unblock them. However, due to reports being late or missing, the Committee deemed it necessary to excuse them from the meeting because it would have been unfair on the Minister or the Members to participate meaningfully.
The Members appreciated the detailed plans and wanted more details on actual progress. They also asked for clear timelines for the development of projects. Some Members were very vocal about the slow pace or lack of implementation. Delivery of affordable housing did not sit in feasibility studies, one Member retorted. Members were concerned that not enough attention was given to rural areas. Members also needed granular details of some of the mismanagement cases being investigated.
Meeting report
Opening remarks by the Minister
Ms Thembi Simelane, Minister of Human Settlements, reminded Members of their most recent discussion where the Committee made several inputs for the Department to consider. Among others, they included governance and administration issues at the various entities under the Department.
Of particular importance was the need for entities to demonstrate clear programmes on how they were planning to improve service delivery. These entities, like the Housing Development Agency (HDA), Social Housing Regulatory Authority (SHRA) and the National Housing Finance Corporation (NHFC), played an important role in service delivery.
She emphasised that this did not render the other entities, like the National Home Builders Registration Council (NHBRC), the Property Practitioners Regulatory Authority (PPRA) and the Community Schemes Ombud Service (CSOS), less important.
The Chairperson said the issue of blocked projects was also raised under the previous Minister. The current Minister agrees with this priority and even brought the matter before the cabinet.
The Committee need to remain focused on their strategic plan and ensure that by the end of their term, these projects are unblocked.
Addressing the SHRA, the Chairperson said SHRA would realise the importance of unblocking projects if they visited those projects.
Opening remarks by the Department
Dr Alec Moemi, Director-General, Department of Human Settlements (DHS), told the Committee that the Department took their directives on board and met with the ACEO of the SHRA on three occasions. On the third occasion an agreement was reached on what plans the SHRA were to submit to the Department to address these blocked projects.
It was important for the SHRA to submit those plans because it needed approvals from the Minister as well as National Treasury.
Unfortunately, six months later those plans were still not forthcoming.
The Chairperson reminded the SHRA that it was an entity of the DHS and its mandate was to assist the DHS in service delivery. He had met with the Chairperson of the SHRA on a few occasions and always cautioned that they could not be slower than the Department. If they are slower, the Department might as well work without them.
He said commitment was needed, notwithstanding the fact that they were volunteers. The plans were promised by the end of January 2026, yet it was already the end of February and the plans were still not ready.
The Chairperson suggested that the Minister meet with the SHRA after this meeting to address this concern. The delay also meant the Committee could not submit a report to parliament.
It was now the SHRA’s obligation to demonstrate they still needed to exist.
Without the plans submitted timeously it was not fair for the Minister to be subjected to the planned presentation by the SHRA. In light thereof, the Chairperson directed that the SHRA delegates be excused from this meeting.
Presentation by HDA
Mr Hosi Ngove, Chairperson, HDA, said he understood that they were in attendance not to report on activities in general but to focus on issues highlighted by the Committee earlier on.
He made the following remarks
- They would report on the properties they have acquired
- They cannot respond to the issue of abandoned, hijacked or blocked projects because as the HDA they do not have such issues
- All the properties under their control were secured
He confirmed that they would report on the progress made, timelines going forward as well as the bankability of some properties.
During the previous year, they had a meeting with the Special Investigating Unit (SIU) in which the planned implementation of the relevant proclamation was shared with the HDA. In the same meeting, the HDA committed itself to assisting the SIU with any investigations needing its input.
In this regard, it was important to note that some properties listed in the proclamation belonged to other entities. The costs of the services provided by the SIU were exorbitant, and it was not fair to expect the HDA to cover such costs. Other entities should assist in this regard.
Mr Bheki Khenisa, CEO, HDA, confirmed that they do not have any blocked projects but have other projects like
-
The Informal Settlement Upgrading Programme (ISUP) in Knysna
- Drip lands in Breede Valley
- The Title Deeds Restoration Programme (TRP) for the Western Cape
He also wished to provide context for the presentation to follow. Some of the buildings were acquired for the HDA itself and for other departments. They are considering public private partnerships to fund some of the building projects.
The HDA also approached the SHRA to assist with these buildings, but they were told that the SHRA could not accommodate them at that point.
The Committee Chairperson replied that their researchers were reporting that even the HDA had blocked and hijacked projects. This was obviously a matter for dispute and would be attended to another time. Perhaps the Committee should visit these projects to ascertain for themselves whether there are blocked or hijacked projects.
Mr Lucien Rakgoale, Head of Land, HDA, said the presentation would focus on progress made with regard to buildings acquired and the planned development of such.
He shared the details of the various buildings which included
- The SABC building in Sea Point
- The Eskom Building in Braamfontein
- The Transnet building in eThekwini Municipality
- The Eskom building in Sol Plaatjie, the tallest building in Kimberly
- Building in Mangaung acquired from a private owner.
The first four buildings were acquired in terms of the Department of Public Enterprise policy that provides for government to have first right of refusal where state-owned land must be disposed of.
The HDA have active conversations with other state enterprises on their non-core properties. For instance, in Limpopo Lephalale Municipality, they have acquired 200 fully serviced stands which will be allocated to beneficiaries to build their own houses. In the same municipality they were talking to Eskom. They own a land parcel where there is an informal settlement. Eskom is willing to donate this land to the municipality.
Of course, from time to time there were re also difficult conversations. For instance, they offered us the old Carlton Hotel but it was too expensive.
The HDA’s overall objective is to utilise human settlements as an instrument to regenerate inner cities.

Response by the Department
The Minister said that the HDA had a strategy session which she attended. During this session they brainstormed various plans including
- The notion of creating precincts
- The opportunity to assist in providing student accommodation
- Providing more affordable housing and tenure
Some of the identified land may not be owned by the HDA at present.
The Minister also made the following remarks
- The Department could not assist the HDA with its funding requirements
- The pace at which buildings were renovated could happen faster if the Department offered help
- The presentation did not touch on the overall plan and how government could save money based on certain projects
- The cost of the Temporary Accommodation (TA) would be saved if these buildings are acquired and developed. They need to close all the TAs eventually.
- It was disappointing when money had to be returned to the fiscus when implementing agencies did not work faster
- She hoped that the efforts by the HAD, with the assistance of the DHS, these projects would bear fruit sooner rather than later.
The Minister also remarked that she agreed with the HDA that any costs for the SIU services should be borne by the relevant municipality or province.
The Chairperson asked whether it was possible to redeploy some of the monies returned to the fiscus. These monies would go a long way to assist the HDA in their funding needs.
The Chairperson also requested the Minister to engage the Minister of Justice and the Committee on Justice to help address the issues with regard to the SIU challenges. She could then provide this Committee with a report on the way forward.
Presentation by the NHFC
Mr Desmond Golding, Chairperson, NHFC, provided the following introduction and background:
- When the board was appointed on 1 August 2025, there were engagements with the portfolio committee
- On 4 June 2025 the Minister made a commitment that the incoming board would be tasked to devise a turnaround strategy focusing on the breakdown in corporate governance
- The Minister provided the new board with the framework against which the turnaround should be handled.
- This Committee also provided the board with some directives. The board was to devise concrete actions within 90 days.
- Simultaneously, other entities like the Treasury and the Public Protector were also investigating alleged malfeasance
We took it upon ourselves to reposition the organisation for optimal performance.
Subsequently, the board met with management, other external parties and experts. From here we developed the 10by10 turnaround blueprint.
This blueprint would guide us as we endeavour to
- Identify 10 chronic challenges
- Devise solutions for each of these 10 issues
- How do they reposition and recalibrate this institution to
-- Perform optimally
-- Build capacity
-- Restore public confidence
-- Improve staff morale
They could also not turn a blind eye to issues ventilated by the public, this Committee and the Minister.
They then met with Treasury and the Public Protector to ascertain precisely which matters these two bodies were handling. Of the more than 30 matters on the table, the NHFC was saddled with 25 matters.
On that basis, they appointed multi-disciplinary forensic investigation teams with skills in IT, governance, law and public finance, risk, etc. These teams established that over 30 matters were pointing to senior officials and functionaries on financial misdemeanour, corrupt processes, fraud, forgery, bullying, abuse of state resources, unauthorised travels, disciplinary actions, irregular increase of salaries and payment to executives and fruitless and wasteful expenditure.
Whilst these investigations are ongoing, the NHFC board remains committed to delivering on its mandate. It wants it to fire on all cylinders and build sustainability.
Hence, it is clear from their second quarter report that results are beginning to show. Whereas the overall performance was at 65% previously, it has grown to 74% first and now stands at 85%, which is above the mandate of 80% demanded by the shareholders.
He briefly explained how the prevailing macro-economic affected their profitability. There was a great need for them to diversify their income streams.
The challenges they faced were
- Disbursement over 200%
- High write-offs
- High vacancy rates
- Outdated IT system
In response they have done a few things
- Created a jobs fund
- Increase the approvals from R1 billion to R3 billion
- Improve their investment pipeline
- Strengthening their treasury function
- Revisit their equity portfolio and divest where no fruits are borne.
(See further information in the attached documents.)
Mr Satish Roopa, Non-Executive Director: Human Capital, addressed the suspension of its CEO. He said it was a painful process. The forensic investigations revealed the following
- Systemic weaknesses
- Unauthorised approval of executive travel expenditure
- Irregular recruitment and promotion practices
- Deficient record-keeping
- Poor governance and internal controls
- Over-reliance on undocumented processes
- Irregular expenditure
- Dereliction and acts of criminality
He continued and said the board had no alternative but to suspend the CEO. The board is not making any findings on the matter and is merely following processes aligned with labour laws, the PFMA, the Constitution and the principles of procedural fairness.
(See further details in the attached document.)
Response by the Department
The Minister said that the reports done by Nexia SAB&T compelled the new board to continue with investigations. The risk of running parallel investigations was real, and she urged Members to be patient as they await the final reports from Treasury and the Public Protector. It was necessary for these bodies to share information but not to infringe on each other's investigations.
Paramount for her was that the institution maintained its stability and attended to the vacancies.
This Committee also identified the NHFC as one of the entities where lifestyle audits had not done yet. She can confirm that such audits are now being run by the SIU.
She said that she approved the need for the board to have extra sittings due to the workload involved in these investigations. Some of the Department's staff also offered to assist the NHFC as they work on cleaning up the organisation and getting it to function as per their mandate.
She emphasised that she regarded the Committee's request as very serious and, for that reason, she made sure she was present to address all their concerns. Never was it her intention to protect any of the executives in the NHFC. She urged members to be patient as they await the reports from the three separate investigations.
The Chairperson thanked the Minister for her transparency and said that they had several people calling or emailing them about certain issues. However, since the new board took over, these communications stopped because, in his view, the right things were being done.
Discussion
Ms M Dlamini (EFF) said that her party had asked the Department for a list of the IT companies contracted to their entities. Three months later the Department is yet to provide such list. She would like to repeat her request and ask for the list to include dates of appointment and the end of contracts. She also wanted information on the financial exposure related to these contracts. Were payments to these contractors suspended given the ongoing investigation?
About the board members, she wanted to know how many of the new members were returning members.
She also found it puzzling that the entity with so many grand plans could degenerate into the mess it was in. Previously Member Mphithi brought the allegations to the Committee and the tone was that there was stability. And, now they hear of the suspension of the CEO. What did they miss?
She proceeded and asked about the deteriorating loan book and what was being done to reverse this.
Referring to the HDA, she asked what criteria were used to acquire the buildings mentioned and whether these acquisitions were aligned with the Integrated Human Settlement Plan. Do they really mitigate the housing demands?
Another concern for her was that the planning sometimes failed to cater for the bulk infrastructure needs, resulting in settlements without the basic services.
Ms B Kegakilwe (ANC) bemoaned the fact that it seemed the HDA neglected the rural areas. She urged them to pay more attention to areas like the North West.
She praised the NHFC board for the sterling work they had accomplished despite their short time in office.
She asked about the lifestyle audits and their progress.
She wanted to know if the NHFC could provide a figure for the high write-offs mentioned earlier.
Ms Kegakilwe asked for more details about the framework for the audit that was outsourced to Nexia SAB&T. Did the board not have its own internal audit staff?
She also needed more information on the employees identified for succession and what the criteria were to make these selections.
Response by HDA
Mr Rakgoale said that their presentation was about inner cities and not the rural areas. However, he could confirm that most of the land forming part of their land assembly programme was located in the North West. Several land parcels have been allocated for informal settlement and on some land basic services have already been provided.
The criteria used for the acquisitions in the inner cities are mainly due to the existing bulk services. These properties would also provide housing to disadvantaged people living in these cities.
Response by NHFC
Adv Jabu Fakazi, Acting Chief Executive Officer, NHFC, said that the NHFC has a succession pool from where staff are selected to receive mentoring and preparation to take up senior roles. When vacancies occur, they could then apply. The problem arose when members outside the pool are appointed. Such appointments would then be illegal.
Ms Hlengiwe Khumalo, NED: Audit and Risk Committee, replied that the contracting loan book is a matter of great concern and for that reason they were reviewing their credit policies.
They also plan to roll out incubator programmes for emerging developers. This will build capacity but also in turn contribute to increasing the loan book.
The write-offs amounted to about R80 million in the previous financial year. This happened when there was no board in place.
Internally the NHFC had no internal audit department so this was outsourced and under the management of the CFO and the general manager for enterprise risk management. They had looked at the risks involved in establishing an internal audit department, but with the things happening internally, it was decided to outsource it for the next two years at least. Thereafter the issue would be reviewed by the Audit Risk Committee.
Mr Mtho Xulu, NED: Investments, added to the comments on the loan book. He said what was driving the contraction was the over-collection on the performing book. The non-performing book has flatlined. They needed to increase the loan book by speeding up their approval processes. Of the approvals, only 40% disbursements take place. If they increase this to 40% of R3 billion it would increase the collection amount. Increasing the pipeline is also very important. They are appointing professionals as advisers to assist with the application process.
In the metros, there were state-owned housing companies that could provide thousands of bankable projects. These were low hanging fruits. They are also focusing on private sector developers to boost their book.
The NHFC would like to boost their national footprint, and to facilitate this, it is working hand in hand with MECs to establish meaningful partnerships on pockets of land.
Some of the policies were not responsive to the demographics of their applicants. This means approvals happen but disbursement cannot.
To increase their pipeline they are looking more and more at impact investment.
Mr Golding replied that 3 of 11 board members were previous members.
The NHFC will make available the list of the IT companies contracted.
The SIU was expected to conclude their lifestyle audit reports by 31 March 2026.03.02
A concrete intervention with regard to the loan book was to separate credit from collections and write-offs. This was to improve the management function.
Response by the Department
Adv Moemi said the Department never claimed that all is well at the NHFC. In fact, he said, the Department did its own assessment of the NHFC and raised serious concerns that performances were below par. They set the target at above 80% and they had not met it for two financial years. They had agreed with the Member claiming several reports of malfeasance. Where we did not agree was when he expected the Minister to intervene and act against the CEO. This function was mandated to the board. We were also emphatic when we said that we would not sweep anything under the carpet.
The criteria for land acquisition were based on the primary policy to bridge the special divide created under Apartheid. The townships were dormitories providing cheap labour to the city centre but these people could not reside in the centre. Land closest to the urban centre was most expensive, but if one bought land far away, it created other economic horrors for the people. Transport costs were exorbitant as you moved away from the centre. The state has therefore taken a policy decision to compete with the private sector for this urban land. The HAD was mandated to buy this land and develop it for social housing for the poor. The acquired land must have all the necessary amenities, failing which a special motivation needs to be made.
The leader of government business had a report that revealed that the DHS answered all the questions tabled. The recent questions have not reached their deadline yet.
He promised to share with this Committee a detailed report on the ICT companies contracted to the Department.
He replied to the comment that the Department act very slow in some cases. The example used was that of the town of Mkhondo. The Department acted with haste and even the Minister visited the region. However, this was a provincial competency. The Department is still waiting for the province to report on the investigation launched. Notwithstanding, they would make another spirited request for the report from the province.
In this regard, Treasury had issued a directive that approval to acquire land would only be granted where bulk was already available and services were in place. Should entities disregard this directive they are culpable of misconduct?
The NHFC will increase their pipeline if they diversify their offerings and target people who have not previously been targeted.
In tandem with the Department of Public Administration, they were in the throes of designing a Government Employee Housing Scheme (GEHS). The Minister was the champion of this scheme and it was hoped to have it in place within two years' time. The income would be secure because the PERSAL system would be used for collection.
The write-offs were those belonging to the previous board and CEO. The Department made it very clear that recent balances should not be written off and that the monies should be collected in whichever way possible. The schedule of debt was adjusted and given to the new board who in turn assessed what they could pursue and what had already prescribed. These write-offs were to create the correct picture for parliament that the NHFC was actually owed a certain amount and not the amount reflecting the historical apathy about collections.
The Minister said that the conditions of the grant and the framework did not allow them to apply the stop and reallocation to support the entities. When monies are returned to the fiscus it does not make sense when such monies could have been applied elsewhere wherever the need was greater. The conditions were rather stringent.
She bemoaned the fact that much of the grant was spent on chemicals and tankering services, and nothing was done to actually eradicate those challenges. Treasury was alive to this and planned to reduce the grant even further to avoid spending money unwisely. This "medicine" could be too bitter for municipalities because how does one simply stop a service when it was desperately needed daily.
One of the challenges was that houses were built on land where no services were available.
She urged the entities to compile the list of IT companies contracted to the entities. It should not take more than a week to submit such to the Committee.
She invited members to report anything that slipped through the cracks in the investigations by the three bodies. She expects members to help them identify any blind spots.
She concluded by congratulating the two entities present for the work they did. This is why she afforded them additional sittings to allow them ample time to do both the investigative as well as their normal mandated duties.
Discussion (Continued)
Mr C Poole (DA) said they welcomed the acquisition of strategically located land but the proof of the pudding would be in the number of families placed in houses. He also asked the HDA to provide an implementation schedule for all five sites mentioned. This report should reflect the timelines for construction and occupation dates. It should also reflect the cost per unit for each site. And, it would be helpful if this schedule also demonstrated how these projects measured against national benchmarks. He would also want to see which projects were fully funded and which still relied on future allocations.
Since these projects would only provide one thousand units, how did the HDA assess the impact relative to the national housing backlog of over 2.4 million?
Mr Poole deemed the suspension of the CEO of NHFC as a perfect example of how oversight should happen.
Mr S Dithebe (ANC) remarked that he was satisfied that the NHFC addressed all the issues raised in previous meetings. He wanted to know whether they regard the liquidity ratio as ample proof of their financial health. He asked whether the NHFC were using any artificial intelligence to help it get the entity back on track.
He asked the shareholder whether they had a road map for their plans to transition into a human settlement development bank.
He requested that the HDA provide updates on the progress made on the Bloemfontein building because the need was so great.
Ms Khumalo said that whilst the financial performance reflected a stable and resilient position it was definitely supported by strong liquidity. The NHFC alone have over R2 billion and this was due to their disciplined cost management plans and effective oversight.
She continued to explain how factors such as successful collections and the lower interest rates impacted the contraction of the loan book.
Importantly, she told members that they can rest assured that the liquidity ratios and solvency are solid.
Ms Noluthando Khulukazi Mashologu, Deputy Chairperson, NHFC, told the Committee that they were faced with quite a number of legacy issues when they took office in August 2025.
The notion of a human settlements development bank (HSDB) was a great idea and needed much work. As an organisation they made a decision to take the organisation through various stages in order to strive towards optimum performance. They had to be honest and assess whether they were ready for the HSDB – the answer was clear; they were not ready yet. When they engaged with the shareholder about this HSDB it was decided that the shareholder would take the lead in this regard.
Mr Roopa replied that when they were installed as board members, they were told that the CEO suspended a staff member for whistleblowing. The employee laid a grievance against the CEO. The board sought legal advice and was told that the actions of the CEO were irregular and even illegal. Instead of dealing with the complaint, she appointed a service provider to establish who the whistleblower had been. This was unacceptable especially given the fact that the Minister of Justice had announced measures for extra protection for whistleblowers. The employee has been reinstated.
At a policy level they are reviewing 20-odd policies with the assistance of organised labour in March 2026.
In terms of the change in culture, they had commissioned a culture climate survey with a service provider. This was aimed at establishing the extent of the breakdown in relationships and how best to restore a good climate.
They are exploring several ways to enhance employee wellness. Much of what they are doing is now reflected in the improved performance.
Mr Golding said the IT system was very old and could not cope with an integrated approach to improved performance.
Mr Khenisa explained that they provided services to the Department and municipalities. The HDA provided a pull-service which meant the end-user could pull any service they needed, for example the drafting of a title deed.
The HDA was constantly looking at ways to enhance its service offering and is in constant touch with the Department to devise plans to streamline the processes.
The Minister told the Committee that the idea of a bank was already tabled in 2023. The need does exist, and government included the plan in its reform strategy. The matter is not finalised yet and she would report back as soon as there were further developments.
The study tour to Brazil would help to create the framework for this bank.
She assured the Chairperson that she would meet up with the SHRA again to work through the issues present. She would take responsibility for the SHRA not putting their best foot forward. She apologised to members for what happened earlier with the SHRA.
Ms Z Abader (MKP) said that everything always sounded great on paper but implementation was the key. She wanted to know how it was possible for the irregularities to happen in the midst of sound governance structures.
She commented that a turnaround strategy did not equate to accountability. The true test would be consequence management, asset recovery and clean audits. The governance gaps might explain weaknesses, but they do not excuse the alleged misconduct.
We must ensure that precautionary suspensions do not become prolonged processes that quietly fade without decisive outcomes. And lastly, Ms Abader recommends that the board provide the Committee with a timebound consequence management matrix including criminal referrals, recovery actions, disciplinary timelines and responsible officials.
She also recommended that National Treasury and the Public Protector submit written progress updates directly to the Committee to ensure independent oversight of the forensic process.
She further recommended that the NHFC submit a quarterly financial stability report detailing loan book performance, non-performing loan reduction strategy and concrete steps to increase long term viability.
She wanted to know from the HAD how many of the acquired buildings have moved beyond packaging and concept design into actual construction with funded, approved timelines? Exactly when would the first beneficiaries receive keys? Several projects remain in planning stages years after acquisition.
What was the total cost of holding these properties? Security, rates, maintenance, and how much public money was being spent on buildings that were not yet delivering housing?
What consequent management mechanisms were in place if these projects missed deadlines, escalated in cost or stalled? Who was personally accountable within the HDA and the Department for ensuring delivery?
She said that communities did not live in feasibility studies. The spatial intent was correct.
If they continued celebrating pipeline projects instead of completed units, they risked normalising underperformance in a sector where people are waiting decades for dignified shelter. And it comes down to the humanity and the dignity of our people.
The Department must provide a timebound implementation schedule of each property including funding status, contractor appointment dates, and projected occupation timelines.
A quarterly progress report must be submitted to the Committee detailing physical progress - that is, percentage complete, expenditure against budget and risk mitigation measures.
And lastly, she suggested the Committee conduct at least two oversight visits to these sites within the financial year.
Mr T Gamede (MKP) wanted to know how the HDA made sure these high-density urban developments did not inadvertently displace or marginalise the most vulnerable households currently awaiting housing on municipal lists.
What comprehensive long-term property management models are being proposed to prevent these critical assets from deteriorating or succumbing to unauthorised occupation?
What specific legally binding safeguards have been integrated into the current recruitment framework to ensure that no single executive including the CEO could override the human capital or internal audit functions during the selection of their direct reports on the verification of cultural fit beyond technical competence.
What verifiable evidence can the NHFC produce to demonstrate that the newly appointed executive had a documented track record of successfully navigating and resolving, rather than suppressing, internal reports in their previous leadership roles?
He wished to share a critical reflection on the suspension of the CEO. He said it presented a complex case of governance tension and performance recovery. The Committee reflected on the systemic inconsistencies in the suspension process, the nature of the allegations.
A primary inconsistency lay in the timing and context of the suspension.
It was intellectually dissonant to hold the CEO solely accountable for governance collapses that occurred during a period where the institution's guardrails, the board, were absent.
Furthermore, the precautionary nature of the suspension, while standard in administrative law to protect the integrity of investigations, appears with procedural ambiguity.
The burden of proof lay with the accusers. The reliance on whistleblowers' reports had already cost the entity R385 940 in investigation fees, without a public verdict, suggesting the potential weaponisation of whistleblowing.
The Chairperson told Mr Gamede that he was getting into the merits of the case. He found this problematic because the case was still ongoing and there was no need to defend or decide on anything. The Committee was simply doing its oversight. A situation could develop where people quoted the Member as part of their defence.
Response by the Minister
Minister Simelane told Members that the board cannot respond to issues still under investigation. There would also be a parliamentary process where Members would be given ample opportunity to interrogate the three formal reports.
The presentation did not reveal any names of people responsible for the forensic findings. These finer details would all be revealed in the final reports.
She said that they would be discussing actual housing matters instead of governance issues if the entities were all doing well.
Her role was to protect the judicial responsibility of the shareholder. When she installed the new board one of the instructions to them was to exercise caution, to be fair and ensure their report was acceptable. The board concurred with her on the investigations. She implored members to wait for the reports before they consider any merits of the cases.
Due to the ongoing budgetary process, neither the NHFC nor the HDA were not able to provide the details of their project timelines as requested by members. Only once the funding has been allocated can the exact details be finalised.
Executive recruitment was happening where possible because not every vacant position needs to wait for the investigation to first conclude.
The board dealt with some of the negative media reports.
What was also noticeable was that some matters were beginning to resolve themselves as preliminary reports were available from National Treasury and the Public Protector.
Concluding remarks by the Chairperson
The Chairperson told Members it was not their place to judge but to provide oversight.
He reminded the Department to address the concerns with SHRA.
He encouraged collaboration between the NHF and the HDA on the use of student accommodation to achieve their respective objectives.
The Chairperson highlighted the problem of overcommitment in the North West. They cannot pay their bills. The Committee was happy to support the Minister in her efforts to get the funding model reviewed, especially as it relates to municipalities and metros.
Finally, he wished all Muslim colleagues a blessed Ramadan.
The meeting was adjourned.
