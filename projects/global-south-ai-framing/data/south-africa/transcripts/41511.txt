Title: Briefing on the shortage of clinical staff and the strategies to attract and retain clinical staff
Date: 03 Sep 2025
Committee:

Meeting Summary
Video
The Standing Committee on Health and Wellness (WCPP) convened for a briefing by the Western Cape Department of Health and Wellness on the shortage of clinical staff and the strategies to attract and retain clinical staff. The Minister opened the meeting by emphasising that human resource management cannot be considered in isolation from the Department's broader objectives. The briefing aimed to demonstrate how the people management strategy aligns with the Provincial Strategic Plan and the Department's five-year goals.
Members acknowledged the significant budgetary pressures faced by the Department and noted that certain legislative and procedural requirements, while well-intentioned, can impede effective service delivery. The Department’s adaptive processes were commended, particularly recruitment and retention strategies designed to mitigate staff churn. Questions were raised regarding whether recent improvements in staff numbers realistically reverse long-term turnover and the loss of critical skills could. Concerns were also expressed about staff shortages, morale, burnout, mental health, and strategies to curb migration to the private sector or abroad.
The Minister explained that high staff turnover is common due to retirements and resignations and that recruitment constraints from previous austerity periods exacerbated the problem. Strategic adjustments are beginning to reverse negative trends, with the province performing relatively well compared to others. The Deputy Director-General responsible for finance and human resources highlighted cautious optimism about sustaining these improvements, pointing to potential efficiencies through technology, including artificial intelligence, and emphasised the interconnected importance of morale, retention, and a people-centred culture.
Discussion then focused on service delivery challenges. Members sought clarification on the “life course approach,” service redesign, and staffing levels in response to ongoing issues such as overcrowded hospitals. The Minister explained that the framework integrates health, social services, and economic participation across all stages of life, requiring a whole-of-government and whole-of-society approach. The Deputy Director-General outlined the People Strategy’s 11 priorities, which address both structural components and workforce culture, ensuring that policies translate into improved outcomes for service users.
Further questions addressed the two-part system, the pool system, post allocation, and the impending retirement of nurses. The Department noted that some staff are returning through formal recruitment, exit interviews inform retention strategies, and transversal solutions optimise scarce resources across facilities. Progress in nursing training was highlighted, with accredited postgraduate programs addressing previous gaps and collaboration with higher education and the private sector enhancing specialist training. Employee wellness programmes were cited as influencing decisions around early retirement. Members welcomed the progress in nursing training and emphasised prioritising hospitals such as Paarl and Khayelitsha when allocating newly trained staff.
The Committee resolved to request a future report from the Department detailing nursing appointments and training, including the number of vacancies filled, geographic distribution, and planning for replacements by 2026.
Meeting report
Minister’s opening remarks
Ms Mireille Wenger, Minister of Health and Wellness in the Western Cape, introduced the presentation and stated that human resource management could not be viewed in isolation from the broader work of the Department. She explained that the presentation would demonstrate how the people management strategy aligned with the Provincial Strategic Plan (PSP) and the Department’s five-year goals. She acknowledged the challenges posed by austerity in recent years and highlighted the Department’s integrated response to people management under these circumstances.
Briefing by the Department of Health and Wellness on the shortage of clinical staff, especially nurses, in health facilities in the province and the strategies to attract and retain clinical staff
The Department of Health and Wellness briefed the Committee on the shortage of clinical staff, particularly nurses, in health facilities in the province, and on strategies to attract and retain clinical personnel.
The Department explained that its strategy was framed within the Western Cape Government’s life-course and whole-of-society approach, with emphasis on building a care system that functioned both “system-wide” by connecting across silos, and “system-deep” by shaping values, norms, and relationships. It stated that the aim was to align, learn, connect, and action its capabilities to strengthen healthcare delivery over the Medium Term Expenditure Framework (MTEF).
The Department reported that the People Strategy acted as a bridging enabler between operational focus and measurable people-centred outcomes for patients, their families, and staff. Eleven strategic priorities had been identified, including employee well-being, workforce planning, technology enablement, communication, service integration, organisational culture, collaboration, leadership development, training, alignment with business objectives, and performance accountability. The Department said the vision of the People Strategy was to empower excellence in healthcare by fostering a supportive, inclusive culture through professional people management services.
Recruitment and retention challenges were explained within the context of policy changes and austerity measures. The Department highlighted the Approved Post List as a tool for the equitable distribution of resources. New public service regulations required the national advertising of posts, reduced the validity of selection pools, and enforced stricter role specifications. These changes had increased the volume of applications and the frequency of advertising. To adapt, the Department pooled posts across facilities, streamlined interview processes, and introduced digital platforms for assessments and data sharing. It reported that despite initial challenges, headcount numbers were stabilising, with more staff entering than leaving since April 2025.
Budgetary constraints were reported to have resulted in a net loss of 1 862 posts, although responsible decisions had enabled 591 new posts to be added in 2025/26. By raising the attrition rate allowance, the Department had been able to advertise more posts, while protecting clinical roles, particularly in nursing. These measures had produced a steady month-on-month increase in filled posts.
The Department concluded by reaffirming that it had a clear implementation strategy, a people strategy, and responsible workforce stabilisation measures in place. With managerial commitment and the right tools, it expressed confidence that workforce numbers would continue to stabilise sustainably over the MTEF period.

Discussion
Mr T Walters (DA) acknowledged the significant budgetary pressures faced by the Department and stated that certain legislative and procedural requirements constrained effective service delivery, despite their underlying rationale. He observed that these regulations had unintended consequences that complicated decision-making. He commended the Department’s adaptive processes, comparing the effort to the agility of the Springboks on the rugby field.
Mr Walters expressed interest in the Department's approach to mitigating job losses through its advertising and recruitment strategy, which he described as almost algorithmic in design. He referred to the recent stabilisation in staffing numbers, noting that since April 2025, more staff had been entering than leaving the service. He asked whether this trend could realistically reverse the long-term problem of staff churn and the loss of critical skills that had been observed during oversight visits. While acknowledging that it might be too early to judge, he suggested that the trend could be a positive development for the Department. He further asked whether the Department regarded this as a sustainable reversal of staff churn, or whether it would be overly ambitious to consider it a "silver bullet" solution.
Ms D Stephens (PA) asked how the updated departmental strategy was addressing staff shortages and capacity constraints in the public health sector. She also sought clarity on how the Department was responding to issues of staff morale, burnout, and mental health among healthcare workers. She further inquired whether a retention strategy had been developed to curb the migration of healthcare professionals to the private sector or abroad
.
Response
Minister Wenger explained that, unlike most other Western Cape departments, the Western Cape Department of Health and Wellness (WCDHW) operated on a very large scale, which naturally resulted in constant staff churn through retirements and resignations. She stated that, during the austerity period, DPSA regulations had made recruitment following this churn extremely difficult. Combined with the two-part system, this had led to unusually high staff exits that could not easily be replaced, even where budget had been available. While acknowledging that these measures were designed to slow recruitment during South Africa’s fiscal emergency, she stressed that such constraints were impractical for a frontline service delivery department such as health and had placed enormous strain on operations.
She noted that the Department had taken considered actions to manage these challenges, including internal strategic adjustments around attrition rates, which were now beginning to reverse some of the negative trends. She highlighted that, as reflected in the presentation slides, the Western Cape was performing relatively well compared to other provinces in this regard.
Minister Wenger confirmed that staff morale and retention strategies were in place and indicated that colleagues would elaborate further. She emphasised that the Department invested significant effort to ensure staff felt valued and supported.
Mr Simon Kaye, Chief Financial Officer (CFO), WCDHW, stated that the Department was cautiously optimistic about sustaining recent improvements and noted that no policy shocks were anticipated in the near future. He indicated that greater process efficiencies could be achieved through investments in technology, including the use of AI to streamline recruitment by filtering large volumes of applications and reducing administrative burdens while still complying with selection processes.
He highlighted that the Minister of Finance had announced a substantial allocation to the health system over the next three years. While the exact figures were not yet available, he explained that these resources would need to be strategically invested in areas that strengthened the entire system. The Department was still finalising its resource strategy to support the framework, which would be followed by policy decisions and implementation.
Mr Kaye emphasised that staff morale, retention, and service demand were deeply interconnected. He stressed the importance of collective support, collaboration, and a people-centred culture to counter burnout and strain, particularly at frontline facilities. He identified employee well-being, organisational values, training, and development as central priorities in the strategy to support staff, strengthen morale, and retain healthcare workers. While acknowledging that many staff remained under heavy pressure due to service demand, he assured Members that the Department was mindful of these realities and committed to addressing them.
Further discussion
Ms R Windvogel (ANC) welcomed the Department’s presentation and referred to slide four. She asked for a simple explanation of the “life course approach” in the context of the daily realities of poor people in the Western Cape, many of whom faced unemployment and persistent social ills. She wanted to know how the Department was working with other government departments to address these challenges.
Turning to slide five, she inquired about what the Department’s service redesign entailed and how it would respond to ongoing challenges such as patients sleeping on the floors in facilities like Mitchell’s Plain and Khayelitsha District Hospitals. She stressed that her concerns were based on the lived experiences of vulnerable and unemployed communities who relied heavily on the public health system.
Lastly, Ms Windvogel referred to slide ten on the "theory of change" in the People Strategy, asking for further clarification. She posed this question in the context of staff and bed shortages across provincial healthcare facilities. She asked directly whether the Department intended to increase staffing levels to address this critical issue.
Minister Wenger explained that the Department’s strategic framework was based on the life course approach, aligned to the provincial goal of enabling residents of the Western Cape to “live a life that they value.” She outlined that this framework followed individuals from conception and early childhood, through adolescence and adulthood, and into their later years, with each stage of life requiring different forms of support. She said that these needs were shaped by family, community, public spaces, workplaces, and broader social structures.
She emphasised that effective service delivery depended on integrating services and infrastructure such as housing, mobility, education, healthcare, and social development, supported by government and linked to economic growth. She noted that health was interconnected with all these areas, contributing to individual well-being and participation in the economy, while also being influenced by external factors such as climate and ecosystems.
Minister Wenger stressed that this approach required breaking down silos within government and adopting a “whole-of-society” perspective, where departments worked together on shared initiatives such as safety, nutrition, and youth development. She stated that the ultimate aim was to ensure that children started well, adults lived well, and the elderly aged well, through coordinated services that reflected the realities of residents’ lives.
Mr Kaye emphasised that there was no single “silver bullet” to solving the complex challenges facing the health system, which was why the Department had developed a People Strategy with 11 priorities. He explained that this strategy underpinned the broader organisational strategy by addressing both system “hardware” (the building blocks of service delivery) and “software” (values, culture, and norms). He said the aim was to ensure that these translated into the lived experiences of service users, acknowledging that while there were many positive outcomes, there were also areas of persistent challenge that required continuous learning and adaptation.
He stressed that improving health outcomes could not be achieved by the Department alone but required tackling the wider social determinants of health. He noted that issues such as violence, alcohol abuse, and lifestyle choices placed enormous pressure on frontline facilities such as Mitchell’s Plain and Tygerberg emergency centres. He argued that health promotion and prevention required a “whole-of-society” and “whole-of-government” approach, where individuals had agency to make healthy choices, supported by effective communication, equitable policies, and stronger social conditions.
Mr Kaye highlighted that while it was easier to make healthy lifestyle choices in more affluent areas, disadvantaged communities faced greater barriers. He stated that equity must therefore guide investment decisions, with targeted support for vulnerable communities. He further stressed the importance of working collaboratively with patients, clinic committees, communities, and even the private sector to improve access and outcomes. He concluded that the Department’s strategy was centred on mobilising all stakeholders to work together towards a shared vision of improved health and well-being across society.
The Chairperson noted that his questions were based on issues observed during oversight visits to health facilities. He raised concerns about the two-part system, asking whether uncertainty around it had contributed to staff resignations and whether those who had resigned were later returning to the service, similar to engineers who resigned, took lump sums, and returned as consultants due to their specialised skills.
He sought clarity on the pool system, where the placement period for candidates had been reduced from six months to three months. He asked whether selection from the pool was based purely on the number of applicants for a post or whether candidates were ranked according to their interview performance.
He commended the Department on its turnaround in filling posts but highlighted concerns raised by facilities. He explained that when several staff members resigned, facilities often reported that only one post was replaced instead of all that were vacated. He asked how the Department determined allocations when filling posts—whether all vacancies in a single facility, such as Khayelitsha, were filled, or whether posts were distributed proportionally across different facilities, such as Worcester, based on broader departmental needs.
Mr Kaye noted that, anecdotally, some staff who had left under the two-part system were returning, although only through the formal recruitment and selection process. While no detailed study had yet been conducted, he stated that exit interviews were proving valuable in shaping retention strategies. He explained that under the two-part system, staff could only withdraw part of their pension on resignation, unlike before. He cautioned that a potential new policy allowing early retirement without penalty—still under discussion in bargaining chambers—could trigger resignations and contribute to a brain drain. However, he expressed confidence that succession planning efforts would help mitigate this risk, particularly in nursing.
On the pool system, Mr Kaye clarified that candidates were ranked through recruitment and selection processes to ensure suitability for posts, rather than being placed randomly. Turning to post allocations, he acknowledged the frustrations of facilities that lost multiple staff but only had one post replaced, explaining that replacements were constrained by available resources and budget decisions.
He highlighted how the Department was adopting new ways of working across ecosystems to optimise scarce resources. He cited the example of a urologist post funded within one facility but used to provide services across the Metro West platform, thereby reducing patient travel burdens. Similarly, partnerships with organisations such as the Children’s Trust had enabled new infrastructure such as a burns unit in Ceres, which significantly improved patient outcomes by reducing delays in treatment. These collaborations also facilitated staff training and decentralised specialist skills, supported by technology. Mr Kaye emphasised that such innovations demonstrated how resources could be leveraged collectively to enhance both patient care and staff capacity across the system.
Minister Wenger explained that the current budget decisions were beginning to reverse previous staff reductions at facilities. While some posts had previously been lost, the new allocations would restore more positions than before, reflecting a strategic effort to strengthen service delivery. She also noted challenges with the “three-month rule” for post applications, as high application volumes and processing times often meant the rule was insufficient, requiring processes to restart. She stated that overall, significant manpower was needed to manage the implications of these regulatory and policy decisions.
Staffing challenges and recruitment bottlenecks
Dr Tendani Mabuda, Director: People’s Development, WCDHW, highlighted that while pooling applications assisted in managing large volumes, some applications remained incomplete by the end of the process. An advantage, however, was that selected candidates could be quickly assigned to vacancies, such as in hospitals, thereby streamlining hiring. She confirmed that many former employees were returning to the system, as they realised they still had several years of work ahead, and if they remained employable, they were being rehired.
Mr Walters framed the recruitment and resignation challenges as "systems theory bottlenecks," noting that while the timing of resignations and advertising of positions could ideally prevent vacancies, this rarely occurred in practice. He asked whether processes around resignations, contracts and notice periods had been adjusted to help alleviate these bottlenecks. He also raised the importance of transversal solutions, such as sharing specialist staff between hospitals. He asked to what extent services were coordinated locally with municipalities and other departments to manage workload and improve morale. He emphasised that addressing vacancies and staff retention required both human impact management and system-level planning, including potential investment to expand capacity. He stressed the need for localised, whole-of-government and whole-of-society approaches rather than relying solely on the Department of Health.
The Chairperson highlighted the challenge of balancing patient demand and staffing across facilities. Using Grabouw as an example, she explained that patients travelled long distances for care, while some specialised wards had empty beds despite other hospitals, such as in the Eastern Cape, being overcrowded. This raised questions about how to better manage staffing and bed allocation, especially when shortages were reported in some areas even as other beds remained unused.
Planning for staff exits and system constraints
Mr Kaye explained that the only staff exits that could be properly planned for were retirements, which allowed recruitment processes to be completed before the retirement date. In contrast, unexpected resignations required only 30 days' notice, leaving little time to prepare. To ensure continuity of care, the Department relied on agency staff and commuted overtime for nurses and medical officers. However, such measures were not possible for administrators or chief executive officers. Pooling of resources, he noted, should be understood holistically, enabling teams to work together more effectively across facilities.
He stressed the importance of transversal solutions, highlighting that planning should start at the household and community level and scale up through sub-districts, districts and the province. Municipalities, he emphasised, were central to coordination, and conversations should include departments such as health, education, social development, the police and even the business sector. He pointed to examples where housing developments triggered new demands for schools and clinics, underlining the need for integrated planning through structures such as the Department of Infrastructure’s single-view pipeline strategy.
On systemic bottlenecks, Mr Kaye explained that hospital overcrowding and empty beds were often symptoms of broader constraints, such as delays in patient transport, lack of after-hours services or Emergency Medical Services backlogs. He stressed that the health system was a complex adaptive system where interventions in one area could have unintended consequences elsewhere. Service redesign, therefore, had to focus on placing patients at the centre and ensuring access to care as close to their homes as possible. He concluded that openness, adaptability and collaboration across departments were critical for managing pressures, with insights from frontline services such as Emergency Medical Services and the Violence Prevention Unit playing a key role in identifying root causes and shaping localised solutions.
Concerns about nurse retirements and training
Ms B van Minnen (DA) shared the example of Helderberg Hospital in Somerset West, noting how community growth over time had left the facility poorly positioned relative to current population needs. She raised concern about the looming retirement of a large proportion of nurses, warning that this could create severe staffing shortages. She highlighted existing blockages in the training and qualification of nurses, referencing Red Cross Hospital’s specialised services as an example of where this challenge was particularly evident.
She asked how the Department planned to manage the situation, given the hard retirement cut-off at 65 and the financial impracticality of relying heavily on nursing agencies. She cautioned that without a clear strategy, facilities risked situations such as Paarl, where an efficient emergency centre could only operate two beds due to insufficient staff.
Minister Wenger acknowledged the challenges around nursing training, explaining that changes in qualifications over the past decade had created a gap where old programmes had been phased out but new ones were not yet fully certified. This left a period in which neither the old nor the new qualifications could be pursued. She noted, however, that progress had been made, with the provincial College of Nursing successfully training nurses and specialists, and the first cohorts of graduates from the new specialist qualifications recently completing their studies. While there was positive momentum, she cautioned that timing challenges remained, particularly with the impact of early retirements, making it an area that required focused attention.
Dr Mabuda confirmed that the concerns about nursing training were valid, noting that since December 2019 there had been a gap in specialist nurse training due to the transition to new qualifications and alignment with the national qualifications framework. He highlighted that the Western Cape had taken the lead nationally, with seven postgraduate diploma programmes now accredited. He said that last month the province held its first graduation under the new system, producing over 400 graduates, including more than 100 specialist nurses.
He explained that this would help address critical gaps, such as at Red Cross, where child nursing training had stalled because the University of Cape Town (UCT) lacked accreditation and would only begin training again the following year. In contrast, the provincial college was already offering a range of postgraduate diplomas, including in critical care. While the challenges were significant, he stressed that the province was now better positioned to meet specialist nursing needs going forward.
Collaboration and staff retention strategies
Mr Kaye expressed optimism about the future of nursing in the province, noting that the Western Cape College of Nursing had received 8 500 applications for the following year, despite only having capacity for around 500 students. He stressed the importance of collaboration with higher education, civil society and the private sector to align training needs with service demands. He added that institutions such as Red Cross were not only training for South Africa but for the broader African continent, which reinforced the value of these partnerships.
He also highlighted insights from the Department’s employee wellness programme, where the majority of staff inquiries related to financial advice around retirement. Many employees considering early retirement withdrew their resignations once they understood the severe financial implications, often amounting to a loss of more than half their income. He cautioned that while enthusiasm for early retirement was common, the financial reality often led people to reconsider.
Lastly, Mr Kaye noted that although the general retirement age was 65, public service regulations allowed for limited extensions in exceptional cases. This practice was sometimes used to retain specialists, enabling knowledge transfer and mentorship of younger staff. Retired specialists could also return on sessional posts, providing continuity of care and supporting the training of new professionals.
Questions about staff attrition
Ms Windvogel asked whether the Department had conducted research into the reasons why professionals were leaving the system, noting the importance of understanding this trend. She welcomed the positive developments around the training of professional nurses, emphasising that staff shortages were consistently raised during oversight visits. She cited Paarl Hospital as an example where beds remained unused due to a lack of professional nurses, a problem also seen elsewhere.
She applauded the Department’s progress in nurse training and urged that, alongside Red Cross, facilities such as Paarl and Khayelitsha Hospitals be prioritised in the allocation of newly trained staff. She stressed that this initiative was encouraging news for addressing the critical nursing shortages in the province.
Mr Kaye responded to the question on why professionals were leaving the system, noting that while many previously did not disclose reasons, more staff were now willing to share this information. The emerging data showed a range of factors, including unaffordable housing in rural areas despite rural allowances, challenges related to children’s schooling, and heavy work pressures. Pay disparities also played a role, with municipalities often offering higher salaries, and some categories, such as paramedics, being actively recruited overseas with salaries the province could not match.
He further explained that structural issues affected retention, such as the absence of an OSD dispensation for nurse educators, which made training roles less attractive compared to clinical specialisations. These insights, drawn from data-driven analysis, allowed the Department to identify trends and tailor strategies, though some challenges extended beyond its control. Encouragingly, Mr Kaye observed a positive shift in staff morale, with many professionals choosing to stay because of improved workplace culture and collaborative environments, even when higher pay was available elsewhere. He described this as an important sign of system-deep investment beginning to bear fruit.
Proposal for a hospital in Bitou
The Chairperson proposed that the Department consider a legacy project for the next five years, specifically the establishment of a hospital in Bitou Local Municipality, which was currently the only municipality in the Western Cape without one. He acknowledged the financial and budgetary challenges involved but suggested that the idea remain a long-term goal for the team. He added that such an initiative would serve as a meaningful legacy project for the province, prioritising essential healthcare infrastructure over less critical expenditure.
Mr Kaye reflected on discussions during a visit to Bitou, where the community had called for a 90-bed hospital. He noted that while Bitou had no public health beds, there was an existing facility that could not currently be accessed, raising the need to think differently about creating access to safe care closer to where people lived. He pointed to examples such as Hermanus and Vredenburg, where public and private hospitals shared the same entrance, as models for more integrated approaches.
He cautioned that building a new 90-bed hospital might not be the most sustainable solution, as the resources required would have to be diverted from existing hospitals or primary healthcare services. Instead, he stressed the importance of exploring alternative models under universal health coverage reforms to ensure communities had access to quality care without overextending limited resources.
Minister Wenger thanked the Committee for the engagement.
Committee resolutions and actions
Mr Walters indicated that he had no new resolutions beyond those he had previously submitted to the Committee. He noted, however, one specific item related to the transversal issue that had arisen during the discussion.
Ms Van Minnen proposed that the Committee receive a briefing on nursing and nursing training, suggesting that such a session would be highly instructive for all Members.
Ms Windvogel requested an update from the Department on how nurses and health professionals would be deployed across various hospitals. She sought clarity on appointments, specifically asking who would be employed, where, and how these appointments would be managed, noting Dr Mabuda’s mention that some professionals might be allocated to Red Cross.
The Chairperson clarified that no appointments had yet been made and suggested that the resolution be phrased to reflect future planning.
Mr Walters proposed that the future report include a comparative progress report showing vacancies filled along with a geographic breakdown. This phrasing was agreed to by Ms Windvogel as sufficient to address the Committee's concerns.
Ms Stephens supported the same concern regarding the replacement of nurses by 2026 and emphasised the importance of knowing how hospitals in critical need, such as Cal Bremer Hospital, would be addressed. The Chairperson confirmed that this would be covered in the phrasing agreed upon with Mr Walters.
The Committee therefore resolved to request a future report from the Department on nursing appointments and training, including the number of vacancies filled, geographic distribution of staff, planning for nurse replacements by 2026, and specific updates on hospitals requiring urgent attention.
Adoption of Minutes
Minutes dated 06 August 2025
The minutes of 06 August 2025 were presented and adopted with no amendments.
Minutes of the oversight visit dated 13 August 2025
The Committee discussed the oversight visit of 13 August 2025.
Ms van Minnen proposed that the issue of allegations by the National Coloured Congress (NCC) needed to be revisited; however, it was noted that since the Member in question was no longer a Member of Parliament, it was unlikely that any substantive outcome could be achieved. She further proposed that the Committee could not proceed with any resolution or investigation regarding these allegations unless substantiated evidence was provided.
Ms Windvogel disagreed regarding the NCC allegations, noting that during the oversight visit, an official had confirmed that hospital equipment was being borrowed between facilities. The official emphasised that this issue, particularly concerning Red Cross, still required investigation despite the Member no longer being in Parliament.
Ms van Minnen cautioned against conflating separate issues. She clarified that the loaning of hospital equipment was distinct from unsubstantiated allegations made by a former member of the provincial Parliament. She noted that the equipment matter related to departmental procedures rather than complaints from staff and stressed the importance of keeping it separate from allegations submitted by ex-members, such as Mr D Jacobs (NCC), which fell outside the Committee’s current jurisdiction.
Mr Walters reminded the Committee that the discussion was part of the minutes review and suggested not sidetracking the current agenda item with matters arising from the minutes, as he was not present during the original discussion.
Ms Windvogel emphasised that the allegations were made while the Member was still in the House and argued that it was unfair to dismiss them solely because the Member was no longer in Parliament; she maintained that allegations should still be investigated.
Ms van Minnen responded by noting that it had previously been agreed that Mr Jacobs, as a Committee Member, needed to submit any allegations in writing for the Committee to consider them. Since Mr Jacobs had not done so, Ms Van Minnen stated that the matter could not proceed at this time in accordance with the recommendations of the original report
.
The Chairperson agreed with Ms van Minnen, noting that nothing had been received in writing from the relevant parties. He confirmed that the Committee would take action only when written submissions were received, and the matter would then be brought back for consideration. For procedural correctness, the Chairperson clarified that the issue would remain part of the minutes, even though no resolution could currently be taken, and he would report back to the Committee after seeking further legal guidance.
The meeting was adjourned.
