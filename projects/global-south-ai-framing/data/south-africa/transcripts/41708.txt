SCOPA RAF Enquiry
The Standing Committee on Public Accounts held the second day of its inquiry into the Road Accident Fund, focusing on governance, procurement, and accounting irregularities that have led to years of adverse audit outcomes. The Committee engaged the National Treasury, including the Office of the Chief Procurement Officer and the Office of the Accountant-General, and the Auditor-General of South Africa to clarify the Fund’s persistent financial and compliance failures.
The Office of the Accountant-General explained that the Road Accident Fund’s deviation from Generally Recognised Accounting Practice to the use of the International Public Sector Accounting Standard 42 was unlawful, as the latter has not been approved for local use. This accounting choice led to major misstatements in the Road Accident Fund’s liabilities and expenditure, contributing to repeated audit disclaimers and negatively affecting Treasury’s consolidated financial statements. The Office of the Accountant-General noted that while engagements with the Road Accident Fund have been ongoing since 2021, the issue remains unresolved and is currently before the courts. A draft GRAP on Social Benefits is being finalised to provide clarity by 2026.
The Chief Procurement Officer highlighted significant concerns regarding the Road Accident Fund’s supply chain management. Between 2022 and 2025, the Road Accident Fund reported R1.4 billion in procurement deviations and R719 million in contract variations, primarily in ICT and property leasing. Treasury confirmed that while deviations are allowed under the Public Finance Management Act for exceptional circumstances, the Road Accident Fund’s pattern suggested systemic weaknesses rather than isolated cases. Members raised questions about accountability, ethical subcontracting, and inflated pricing practices.
The Auditor-General’s presentation revealed deepening financial instability at RAF, with its deficit widening from R13.6 billion in 2020/21 to R27.8 billion in 2024/25. Governance failures, unfilled senior vacancies, irregular and fruitless expenditure, and delayed disciplinary processes were identified as persistent risks. The Auditor-General also flagged concerns about the reliability of RAF’s new Integrated Claims Management System, citing implementation delays, weak oversight, and potential wasteful expenditure.
Members questioned the Road Accident Fund’s board decisions, fiduciary accountability, and the adequacy of ministerial oversight. The Committee will continue its inquiry to determine whether the entity’s leadership and governance structures exercised their responsibilities in line with public finance and accountability standards.

Office of the Chief Procurement Officer Presentation
Mr Sibusiso Nene, Acting Director in the OCPO, took the Committee through the presentation. The country’s public procurement landscape is anchored in a strong legislative framework designed to promote transparency, accountability, and integrity in the use of public funds. This framework is established through key laws such as the Constitution, the Public Finance Management Act (PFMA), the Municipal Finance Management Act (MFMA), the Preferential Procurement Policy Framework Act, and anti-corruption statutes. Together, these instruments form the foundation of Supply Chain Management (SCM) across all spheres of government.
Significant reforms have taken place through the replacement of Instruction Note 3 of 2016/17 with Instruction 3 of 2021/22, which strengthened institutional accountability. Under the new provisions, Accounting Officers and Accounting Authorities (AO/AAs) are granted direct authority to approve procurement by alternative means, such as single-source or emergency procurement, without first seeking Treasury approval. However, all such decisions must be properly documented and reported within defined timeframes. This shift ensures that accountability remains firmly with AO/AAs, in line with constitutional principles of legality and the rule of law.
Procurement through deviations remains a regulated but necessary mechanism when open competitive bidding is impractical. Treasury Regulation 16A6.4 allows AO/AAs to procure goods and services “by other means,” provided that reasons are recorded and justified. This includes limited bidding processes, single or sole-source procurements where competition is constrained, and emergency acquisitions. Similarly, contract variations – such as extensions, scope expansions, or price adjustments – are permitted within prescribed thresholds to accommodate operational realities, though variations beyond certain limits must be reported to both the NT and the Auditor-General (AG).
Between 2019 and 2025, procurement deviations and contract variations showed a notable upward trend. From 2022 to 2025 alone, deviations totalled R1.4 billion across 86 cases, while contract variations amounted to R719 million across 81 cases. RAF accounted for the bulk of these transactions, particularly in ICT and property leasing.

Accountant-General Presentation
Mr Khan, Ms Karen Maree, Chief Director: Accounting Support and Reporting at the Accountant-General, Ms Lindy Bodewig (Responsible for Technical Support services at the Office of the Accountant-General), and Ms Jeanine Poggiolini, Chief Executive Officer of the Accounting Standards Board, took the Committee through the presentation.
The Office of the Accountant-General (OAG) provided an in-depth briefing on the accounting issues underpinning RAF’s audit challenges, particularly its deviation from the prescribed Generally Recognised Accounting Practice (GRAP) to the use of the International Public Sector Accounting Standards (IPSAS) 42. The Fund’s 2024/25 Annual Financial Statements were notably overdue, further compounding concerns about compliance and transparency.
Accounting standards in the public sector are set by the Accounting Standards Board (ASB), approved by the Minister of Finance, and implemented by the NT, while the Auditor-General audits entities against these standards. The Minister also determines the implementation dates of new GRAP standards. Authoritative guidance stems from Standards and Directives issued by the ASB, whereas guidelines and staff communications serve a non-authoritative role.
RAF’s use of IPSAS 42 was contentious because GRAP 19, currently applicable to provisions and contingent liabilities, explicitly excludes social benefits and insurance contracts. Directive 5 guides entities to use International Financial Reporting Standards (IFRS) or IPSAS only when no GRAP exists. However, IPSAS 42 was excluded from Directive 5, as the ASB is developing a local GRAP standard on social benefits. This gap has created interpretative challenges for RAF, which administers benefits that straddle the line between social assistance and social insurance.
Since 2021, the OAG has been in continuous engagement with RAF to resolve the dispute over accounting policy. Despite multiple correspondences and meetings, the matter remains unresolved and is currently before the courts. In July 2025, the RAF requested renewed discussions on applicable standards, and the OAG has since sought internal legal advice to guide future engagement.
RAF’s accounting deviations have materially affected NT’s consolidated financial statements, contributing to disclaimer opinions due to understated claims, liabilities and expenditure. The ASB has since issued a draft GRAP on Social Benefits for public consultation, with final approval expected by March 2026. Once implemented, RAF will need to re-evaluate its classification of benefits and ensure alignment with the new GRAP standard, anchored in the principle of economic substance over legal form, to restore audit credibility and financial integrity.

Auditor-General of South Africa Presentation
Mr Siphesihle Mlangeni, Senior Manager: Road Accident Fund at AGSA, took Members through the presentation. Over the past five years, RAF has shown persistent stagnation in its audit outcomes, receiving consecutive disclaimers and adverse audit opinions. The AG attributes this trend primarily to RAF’s continued use of IPSAS 42, a framework not approved by ASB for South African public entities. Despite guidance from the ASB to apply GRAP, RAF persisted with IPSAS 42 for claims liabilities, leading to pervasive material misstatements in financial reporting. These included inaccuracies in claims expenditure, liabilities, and related disclosures, as well as irregular expenditure and non-submission of key management documents.
The Fund’s accumulated deficit has deepened over the years, rising from R13.6 billion in 2020/21 to R27.8 billion in 2024/25, reflecting ongoing solvency challenges where liabilities far exceed assets. Despite disclosing these financial risks, RAF continues to face significant going concern uncertainties that threaten its ability to meet obligations and sustain operations.
Compliance issues have persisted throughout the period, with recurring findings related to poor financial statement quality, procurement irregularities, and weak consequence management. Procurement processes often deviated from Treasury regulations, including the awarding of contracts to non-compliant bidders and single-source deviations totalling hundreds of millions. Irregular expenditure for 2024/25 reached R36 million, with an incomplete assessment of prior-year irregularities valued at R52.7 million. Most irregular expenditures – over 95% – remain unresolved or not condoned. Fruitless and wasteful expenditure also escalated to R6.4 million due to duplicate and incorrect payments, underscoring weak internal controls in claims processing.
Governance and institutional capacity constraints further exacerbate RAF’s instability. Key executive positions, including Chief Claims Officer and Head of Legal, remained vacant or filled by acting incumbents for extended periods, weakening strategic oversight. Capacity shortages in legal representation have contributed to an increase in default judgments, rising from R1.6 billion to nearly R4 billion in one year.
Two material irregularities were also reported in 2025 – unauthorised changes to banking details and overpayments of claims – resulting in losses exceeding R42 million. Concurrently, the RAF’s litigation against the AG, challenging audit outcomes, continues before the Constitutional Court.
The AG recommends that RAF revert to GRAP-compliant standards, strengthen internal controls, expedite the filling of key vacancies, enforce consequence management, and ensure effective oversight to restore accountability and financial stability.

The causal link has to be there, and then also, you have to, in line with the process, I think it is on slide number one, where Mr Mlangeni outlined the process of the material irregularities process, you have to first issue the recommendation to the accounting officer. The tool was always meant to augment the PFMA and not to assume the responsibilities of the accounting officer or the accounting authority. So they have the first bite at trying to do the right thing. And so you issue the MI, you give them space and opportunity to go do the right thing. If that does not happen, you then issue a binding recommendation in your audit report as a follow-up action. And then if the recommendation is again further not implemented, you then issue the remedial action, which is binding. And then if they still do not act, you then issue a certificate of debt. So it is quite a lengthy process, because obviously, you want to make sure that you are administratively just and fair in your processes. And then also, I think our institution has been quite transparent in saying that the process has had numerous things that we have had to grapple with, such as changes in accounting offices, changes in accounting authorities, as and when you reach a particular stage. And in some instances, given the steps that we have to comply with, some of the accounting officers and the boards have been responsive in dealing with some of the matters where indeed they have been raised. But in this particular instance, not yet, Chair, because you would have had to do all the work that I have just outlined. And then the conversation on fruitless and wasteful expenditure also becomes one where, if you look at the definition of fruitless and wasteful, in line with the PFMA requirements, it says that the expenditure incurred is in vain. But also, coupled with that, there is another criterion that says it could have been avoided had management, you know, applied themselves with due regard. So both criteria have to apply. And in this instance, it becomes difficult to say you believe that you are taking a course of action for the benefit of the institution, whether that belief is crooked or not. But it is not in vain, because the lawyers came and rendered the services, and you engaged in court action. But certainly, those are also conversations we are exploring in terms of the final outcomes and the final implications of this court case. I can confirm that without making confirmation that it might result in fruitless and wasteful expenditure. So those are the two, and then I am going to hand it over to my colleagues now, who will deal with some of the other issues that were raised. Thank you, Chair.
