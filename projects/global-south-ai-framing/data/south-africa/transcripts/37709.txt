Meeting Summary
Video
2023 Budgetary Review and Recommendations Report (BRRR)
In a virtual meeting, the State Information Technology Agency (SITA) presented governance and performance-related matters to the Portfolio Committee on Communications and Digital Technologies.
It explained its ambitious 90-day plan to tackle the immediate challenges within the entity. The plan had been developed after the identification of key points internally, as well as the conduct of stakeholder surveys and engagement feedback. The five focus areas consisted of creating a client-centric organisation; strengthening internal business excellence; fostering a culture of partnerships; investment in emerging technologies; and securing business sustainability through strategic acquisitions, expansions and partnerships. Some of the initiatives had been achieved, and some were in progress. The challenges that existed included the lack of adequate understanding of client priorities; the customer satisfaction index being below average; difficulty in attracting skills to an organisation which had suffered reputational damage through corruption allegations; tender processes being largely manual and too slow; the lack of a partnership framework; and not tapping into the emerging technology market for revenue opportunities. The impact of the 90-day plan and efforts to stabilise the organisation were trending upward. There was still a long road ahead in terms of winning back client trust, improving organisational agility, and repositioning SITA back to its original mandate.
SITA has received qualified audit opinions for the past four financial years. The key audit issues had been analysed to determine the key focus areas for improvement. Solutions had been developed to respond to the root causes, with specific responsibilities, timelines and budget implications. SITA would focus on business process and records management, and would workshop with the Auditor-General on audit action plans and documentation requirements. It would be training finance and co-dependent officials on business process and records management. There would be quarterly reviews of financial controls; a bi-annual review of interim financials; a review of procedures in place to determine the effectiveness of controls; the provision of training on the control framework; and investigations into irregular expenditure.
SITA wanted to improve citizens' experience of government service delivery through digital channels, and to improve the efficiency of government operations through the provisioning of secure and cost-effective information communication technology (ICT) solutions, products and services. The challenges it faced involved the rising public service costs and backlogs in service delivery; lack of easy access to government services; insufficient integration of operational processes; government departments having a low level of digital maturity; silo approaches in government to digital transformation; lack of capacity by departments to deliver digital government initiatives, and a lack of security in government systems. The aim was to enhance citizens’ access to digital services, improve efficiency and effectiveness of government operations, ensure security and privacy of citizens' data, foster innovation and digital skills, and ecosystem development. The recommended approach to digital governance and digitisation included clean audits, statutory compliance, utilisation of e-services, and time and cost efficiency. SITA wanted to develop an enabling framework that would focus on marketplace business, cyber-security business, testing and certification business, service management, and data and artificial intelligence business.
The platform economy was the driving force in global ICT sector growth. The platform business model focused on helping to facilitate interactions across a large number of participants. Its value creation was on facilitating transactions and exchanges at an unprecedented scale. Other countries, such as the United Kingdom, Australia, New Zealand, China and Singapore, have successfully used the platform business model. SITA, as an enabler of the platform economy in the Republic of South Africa, would help with the ICT sector network, economies of scale, government digitalisation and automation, establishment of an ecosystem, government self-processes, and the use of government data and artificial intelligence, to improve services.
Members were concerned about the current strike at SITA, and sought assurance that the necessary steps were being taken to address the situation. The Committee was impressed with SITA’s 90-day plan, hoping it would improve performance. Concerns were raised about SITA not being able to attract people who wanted to be associated with the entity; the low level of customer satisfaction; the limited communication to citizens, and the fact that the Auditor-General had observed that accounting standards were not being applied properly.
Meeting report
The Chairperson welcomed everyone to the meeting, and said that the Committee would not be dealing with the Budgetary Review and Recommendations Reports (BRRRs) because the Committee wanted to process the matter adequately. Therefore, it would be dealt with next week.
Minister's opening remarks
Mr Mondli Gungubele, Minister of Communications and Digital Technologies, said that he would be short so that the Committee could deal with the real issues on the agenda which was a briefing on the governance of the State Information Technology Agency (SITA). At the recent annual general meeting, he expressed his disappointment at the target performance of the entity. In the 2022/23 financial year, there had been an overall performance rate of 44%, compared to 72% in the 2021/22 financial year. He was pleased with the surplus of R450m, which was 3.2% higher than the net surplus of R436m in the previous financial year. It indicated that SITA was in a good financial space, compared to other challenges relating to service delivery.
He had spoken to the team at SITA about improving its position. It had analysed government's plan on information technology and the capital market share, and realised that the organisation could do better. There was a R7.5 billion market share in a space where hundreds of millions were spent. The board had committed to earning the respect of government in order to be its preferred service provider.
SITA had shared information with the Department of Communications and Digital Technologies (DCDT) on its mitigating plans to deal with issues, especially those challenges affecting government departments and service delivery matters. This included the 90-day plan, which would be presented to the Committee in this meeting to interrogate. The DCDT had stated that SITA needed a board of highly competent professionals, which had been recommended to Cabinet for approval.
The board had been tasked to improve SITA’s performance in the current financial year. This was underpinned by the resolution of the audit findings and improvement in the control environment. Information and communications technology (ICT) remained the greatest area of opportunity for improving availability and efficiency in the provision of government services to citizens. However, evidence was clear that the adoption and use of ICT had been rather haphazard and uncoordinated. This was a matter that had to be improved as quickly as possible.
The need to remodel SITA was long overdue. The Managing Director of SITA would be presenting its platform strategy as it related to the remodelling of SITA, and equally ensuring that citizens benefited from the digital economy. Some studies would show how to enhance citizens' access to digital services; how to improve efficiency and effectiveness of government operations; create a singular marketplace for sourcing and selling products, inclusive of small, medium and micro enterprises (SMMEs); how to secure the privacy of citizens' data, and how to foster innovation and digital skills.
The DCDT was leading in technology, especially modern technology. Trading globally was an area that it needed to lead differently. It had to be on the cutting edge as far as other sectors were concerned in actually taking a step forward and being able to be upfront and have a sense of what the opportunities were, and what would make the country competitive. No other department could do this for all the sectors, whether it was transport, automotive or agriculture. The DCDT had adopted the master plan for the digital economy. This would be implemented, instead of being caught up in a mode of planning and producing new strategies. He hoped that with this implementation mode, things would be moving.
SITA on its six months of achievements and progress
Mr Kiruben Pillay, Chairperson of the Board, SITA, said there would be three presentations. SITA would reflect on its six months' achievements, which focused primarily on escalation management. There had been quite a number of topics that SITA had been dealing with. It was supposed to focus on customer engagement, internal process review and partnership evolution. These were all short-term actions being maintained by SITA to stabilise the organisation. The annual performance plans had evolved from year-end to a 44% level, and some of the actions being done already gave SITA a potential of 64%. There were still another five months left, and SITA had every expectation and desire to get between 80-100% of its targets.
The second presentation would cover the audit plan. SITA had had quite a dramatic year-end. It had received its fourth qualified audit. A detailed audit plan had been crafted, with specific dates and specific actions. It had been grouped thematically so that SITA focused on the root causes. The audit and risk committee tracked that every month.
The third presentation was on SITA’s reorientation repurposing. SITA always focused on procurement, but would be adding public service, security and government deficiency. The focus was not so much on strategy definition, but it was building on the strategy crafted in 2013 on the fourth industrial revolution (4IR). SITA primarily focused on implementation, extending its ecosystem by working better with partners and engaging better with customers. The Managing Director would be taking the Committee through the three presentations.
Dr Bongani Mabaso, Managing Director, SITA, said that there were three presentations with a storyline and was built on the topic of governance and performance matters. The first presentation would be related to performance of the first two quarters of the new financial year. At a physical meeting on 1 May, mention had been made of the 90-day plan that was meant to stabilise SITA, employee wellness and improve performance. The second presentation would speak on issues of governance matters. There was a qualified audit opinion in this financial year, and it had been like this for four years in a row. An audit action plan had been developed to address challenges around governance, and how to improve the failing internal controls. The third presentation was about how SITA wanted to move forward in terms of transformation from a digital transformation perspective, and how it would repurpose SITA to help with the digital transformation journey.
On 1 May, an ambitious 90-day plan with the objective of tackling immediate challenges, demonstrating short-term progress and building momentum for further change, had been developed. It was developed after identifying key problem areas internally in the organisation, and also as articulated by stakeholders in surveys, engagement feedback, and other strengths, weaknesses, opportunities and threats analysis conducted. This was also an opportunity to engage and listen to their stakeholders' needs, expectations and concerns. The engagement sessions would inform the development of a revised strategic approach for SITA to meet its value proposition to clients and other stakeholders.
The 90-day plan was aligned to five focus areas:
create a client-centric organisation;
strengthen internal business excellence;
foster a culture of partnerships;
invest in emerging technologies; and
secure business sustainability through strategic acquisitions, expansions and partnerships.
To date, some 90-day initiatives have been achieved and some are in progress. Some focus areas were intended to be achieved beyond 90 days.
Dr Mabaso went over a detailed table indicating the five focus areas, the challenges, the plans to address the challenges, results, progress to date, the next steps and responsibility.
The impact of the 90-day plan and efforts to stabilise the organisation were trending upward. There was still a long road ahead in terms of winning back client trust, improving organisational agility, and repositioning SITA back to its original mandate of public, government efficiency and security.

SITA's audit action plan
Dr Mabaso said that SITA had received qualified audit opinions for the past four financial years. The seven qualification areas included revenue, cost of sales, operating expenditure, property plant and equipment, intangible assets, trade and other receivables, and prior period errors. SITA analysed the qualification areas to determine key audit issues. These key audit issues were analysed to determine the key focus areas for improvement. The key focus areas were unpacked to determine why these findings surfaced and to understand the root causes for these findings, which were repeat findings in certain cases. Solutions were developed to respond to the root causes, with specific responsibilities, timelines and budget implications. There were also general areas of focus identified to further contribute to improving the audit outcomes.
SITA would focus on workshopping with the Auditor-General of South Africa (AGSA) regarding audit action plans and document requirements. Furthermore, it would focus on change management, reporting and monitoring monthly, training, internal audits monthly, quarterly and bi-annually, and irregular expenditure. The presentation included a detailed table.

SITA’s approach to digital transformation
Dr Mabaso said that the problem statement consisted of the rising public service costs and backlogs in service delivery; lack of easy access to government services; insufficient integration of operational processes; government departments having low levels of digital maturity; silo approaches to digital transformation in government; lack of capacity by departments to deliver digital government initiatives, and lack of security in government systems.
The value proposition was to enhance citizen’s access to digital services, improve efficiency and effectiveness of government operations, ensure security and privacy of citizens' data, foster innovation and digital skills, and foster ecosystem development. The recommended approach to digital governance and digitisation included clean audits, statutory compliance, utilisation of e-services, and time and cost efficiency.
There were a lot of other countries that were implementing the platform economy, such as the United Kingdom, Singapore and China.

Discussion
Ms T Bodlani (DA) thanked Dr Mabaso for the very informative and elaborate presentation which addressed the state of SITA. She said that the biggest elephant in the room was what was currently unfolding at SITA, where the Public Servants Association (PSA) had threatened a full-blown strike. She said this should be addressed so that the Committee could know where management was in terms of finding each other with the union. Everything that had been presented hinged on the workers. She asked for guidance from the Chairperson.
The Chairperson said that this was a matter that the management at SITA and the Ministry could address. It had to take the Committee into confidence on matters of public interest, and give an assurance that the plans would be implemented. This was not something that would prevent Ms Bodlani from raising her other points so that SITA and Minister Gungubele could respond to the issues raised.
Ms Bodlani said that the presentation showed that SITA was literally restructuring its philosophical practices. These were the sort of fresh ideas that SITA needed to have if it really wanted to claim its market share as an IT industry leader. She hoped that what had been presented would come to fruition. This presentation should be made to every SITA stakeholder, or anybody who had an association with SITA, to get an appreciation of what it was doing. It had been said that SITA had been seen as just a procurement mechanism, and not necessarily as government's IT agency. If stakeholders could see SITA from both sides, it would make it a great entity.
The AGSA had used the words “regression” and “stagnant” when giving SITA a qualified audit opinion. SITA would be addressing these issues and incorporating the comments of AGSA. It was said that the goals of AGSA seemed like a moving target. In other words, when one thing was achieved, the operating practices then changed. She asked for clarity on this. What did this mean for future audit reports?
In the 2022/23 financial year, only 44% of the targets had been achieved. The Committee would love to see SITA perform better. The 90-day plan looked good on paper, but it should be translated into achieving higher targets in the next financial year. SITA had not been able to fill positions. This reminded her of the Media Development and Diversity Agency (MDDA), which had informed the Committee that it had to readvertise positions because it could not find suitable people. What did this mean for the IT sector if it said that there was no talent and that because of SITA’s reputation, people did not want to be associated with the organisation? She noted the immediate interventions in the presentation, but this was a state entity that really should be the pride of government insofar as IT was concerned. Her biggest worry was not being able to attract people who wanted to be associated with SITA. After interviews, appointments should be made. If people came for an interview but said "no" when an offer was made, what was missing? What could be done to improve this situation?
AGSA had also spoken about the accounting standards not being applied properly. In an oversight visit, the Committee had seen that SITA had a young workforce. If there were people at the executive level and AGSA still pointed out that standards were not properly applied, it takes one back to the fact that it had been raised that AGSA’s expectations kept changing, so it would be receiving this audit outcome. She was really lost in this regard. The AGSA had also recommended refresher training on Generally Recognised Accounting Practices (GRAP), and she hoped it would be implemented.
The customer satisfaction rating was only 54%. She wished that it was higher, because it would speak to SITA being an entrusted service provider in relation to government IT services. This 54% meant that there was a real gap -- reputational issues and possibly a service issue. This pointed to the need to broaden stakeholder engagement. She welcomed the fact that there had been a roadshow and everything else. There had to be a deliberate process to intensify those attempts so that the low ratings by clients could be improved. There should have been more emphasis on timelines for everything, but she appreciated the fact that there was a living plan. These timelines would help the Committee measure the promises and obligations made at the meeting.
Mr L Molala (ANC) said that the last presentation on the approach to digital transformation had been really impressive. He asked Dr Mabaso what his assessment was regarding the change management since he arrived. SITA had presented quite good and hopeful presentations regarding implementation. There had been some progress. How much did the citizens know about its initiatives, particularly in establishing apps for cell phones that the majority of South Africans had? Citizens must be the first to know about whatever new programme SITA was implementing.
He asked for more details on public relations, and said that the Committee should be concerned about the current strike at SITA. The presentation indicated that the strike was about salaries. The placards, however, indicated a different story. He did not want Dr Mabaso to go into the political space, but rather to speak administratively about this concern. It was important to take the Committee on board on this process. How far was the strike? What were the issues? How far was SITA in addressing these issues?
Responses
Minister Gungubele asked that Mr Pillay speak on the matter relating to the current strike action. He said that Ms Bodlani must understand that this was a matter of public interest and that the Committee was entitled to an update, but there was a limitation to the extent to which information could be shared due to its nature. He did not want to make comments here which would be referred to at the negotiating table. An update would be given, but he would be reluctant to go into detail.
Ms Bodlani asked if SITA could provide an indication on what mitigation was being done to ensure that services would be provided. This would help to ensure that South Africans were not being compromised.
Mr Pillay said that the main issue around the strike was salaries. The Public Service Association (PSA), on behalf of the staff, had requested 12% and SITA had offered 3%. The PSA demand had dropped to 7% and SITA had offered 5%. There was a 2% differential. SITA employed about 3 300 people, of which 900 were potentially affected by this particular strike action. The discussions were still ongoing. SITA wanted to open up discussions around multi-year agreements so that it did not have to go through this process again in the future. From an operational perspective, there had been no impact. There were business continuity plans in preparation for the strike action. These plans had been taken down and reviewed. The plans had been updated and subsequently implemented, where there were hourly and daily check-ins for progress purposes. There had been no impact on customers and service reception areas. There would be two briefing sessions to see how the gaps could be closed, and to look at other opportunities.
Minister Gungubele indicated that the DCDT encouraged negotiation in this regard.
Dr Mabaso said he had the same concern as Ms Bodlani on SITA not being able to attract the right level of skills. This was something that could not be undermined, but was not something that could change overnight. This was something that had been raised to the board. He had raised the matter many times before that the executive committee (EXCO) skills level needed to be significantly increased. It needed to bring in the right level of expertise into the organisation. It was an ICT organisation, and needed to be primarily about ICT skills. It should attract people in the five “horses” marketplace business; cyber-security business; testing and certification business; service management, and data and artificial intelligence business. These were skills that did exist in the country. It was not about people not being there -- it was about changing the perceptions, mindset and image that SITA portrayed in the market. SITA had a lot to do to change these mindsets, and it started with those efforts. He had made many pleas in interviews to attract skills. This was something that would be done, but it would not be easy. It had been difficult for him to decide to come to SITA, and this might also be the case with other people. SITA was now in the beginning phase where it had to climb the mountain and eventually, the slope of the cliff would become less and allow SITA to attract more people at a faster rate. The low levels at SITA, indicated that more aggressive campaigns were needed for people to come in and help SITA to deliver. SITA had been able to bring 150 people in, of whom 50 were graduates and the rest interns. There had been some renewals, and people in the Western Cape had been happy. So, this was something that was being done at all levels of the organisation.
He agreed that there needed to be a culture akin to what was out there in the market with some of the top technology companies, but SITA was not there yet. It was determined to make it. Even the buildings were not on par yet. This was part of the things that spoke to the culture and the “smell” of the place. The culture issues were critical, hence the launch of culture campaigns. SITA was trying its best to communicate a positive culture right from the top, at the board, exco and management level.
SITA acknowledged the comments and concerns that Ms Bodlani had raised. It was not something that could be overturned overnight.
The client satisfaction survey to which Ms Bodlani had referred was a big challenge. When he joined SITA in the first month, he received 12 escalations and received a lot more since then. SITA responded to these escalations by establishing a client exco structure to manage much more effectively and to hold people accountable. This would help to ensure client issues were resolved. It was about bringing back client-centricity, and this was not something that could be done overnight. It had to show delivery consistently. He had not engaged with a client whose issues were unresolved in an amicable time. It did show that clients were willing to engage and place trust in SITA. In the past, clients had been disappointed, but this was not something that he could get into.
He said there was no fault with the AGSA audit, but rather with SITA. SITA had not addressed the root causes. If it were a matter of SITA having the right systems or controls in place, then it would have been able to make a case that AGSA was unfair, but this was not the case. The issues were on the side of SITA, and it had to own up to their issues and resolve them. SITA was owning up to the fact that it had not addressed the issues, hence the repeat findings. It wanted to address all of these issues, but the only challenge was time, because there was less time to implement all of the plans. SITA did understand the root causes and was busy addressing them. It had the right monitoring structures in place to ensure that these plans were being monitored even at the board level.
Change management spoke mostly to the employee level, such as whether there was resistance to change from various employees and the like, as it brought in new and fresh thinking. It was looking at how to make SITA perform like a technology company. SITA must look and behave like a technology company. There were no easy answers. One could not just flick one's fingers and change things overnight. It took a concerted effort to be able to engage with employees and bring them on board. He had had engagements with employees in the Western Cape, Eastern Cape, Kwa-Zulu Natal and Gauteng. He wished that he could have engaged with all 3 300 employees. There were plans in place to meet with employees across all other offices to have these conversations around change management, and to reduce the distance between the very top of organisation with the lowest level of employee. There had been tough engagements with difficult employees who had raised their challenges and concerns. Many times, they had been valuable and valid, but they had always been able to find each other and leave the employees with a great deal of excitement. The change management issue was being addressed, hence the running of the culture plan and employee engagement sessions.
It was repurposing SITA to take it back to its original mandate, which was speaking to the public/citizens, government efficiency and cybersecurity. Thus far, it had been focusing on government departments, and not the citizen side of things. It had done a lot these last six months in terms of publicity and speaking through both the public and government departments, but a lot still needed to be done, especially in directing citizens to use services that SITA had already publicised. This would allow citizens to access government services more effectively. SITA did not necessarily want to be the front-runner for this, because it did not have a mandate to do public relations, advertising or marketing. It wanted to use the government services that were there. There was a shareholder department in the DCDT that could assist in ensuring that the word got out. He and the Director-General had already put a plan in place to increase the use of e-services by citizens. Some stakeholders were very willing to help get the word out. SITA wanted to use the machinery of government already there to put the word out because SITA was not sufficiently equipped to do so. So this was the plan SITA had in the background to increase publicity from a citizens' perspective.
Mr Molatlhegi Kgauwe, Chief Financial Officer (CFO), SITA, said that AGSA was not moving the goalposts. SITA wanted to ensure that its collection plan for recording information and running business processes was dynamic enough to enable it to respond to AGSA’s requests within the timeline that was provided. SITA would be workshopping the planning details with AGSA regarding the volume of information. This engagement had already started. There would be more engagements to ensure that SITA was able to move the goalposts and address the root causes going forward. It would continue with the training on accounting standards to ensure that all officials involved refreshed their memory and were able to manage the smaller issues that arise during the audit processes. He was confident that SITA would be able to achieve the expected results.
Minister Gungubele said this was all it wanted to share with the Committee. It did want to see a difference in the e-services on an ongoing basis. There was a potential for 60 million interactions if this was turned around. There was a need for digital transformation in the country. He hoped that SITA would have the people to succeed in this regard. The negotiations would continue.
Chairperson's concluding summary
The Chairperson thanked the Minister and SITA for taking the Committee through the issues of governance and performance, as they provided more clarity on the commitments made in May. The results were beginning to show. He encouraged SITA to continue on that trajectory of changing things for the better. The Committee appreciated the Minister’s commitment or assurance that the people on the board were professionals -- by implication, these people would be able to get things done because of their oversight role, and there should be some improvement. However, there should be caution and the Minister should keep an eye on things. There was a tendency when professionals were together in an oversight body to think they could do it themselves, when they were supposed to only monitor. This had happened in one entity before, and the Chairperson had had to write a letter about this. He said the oversight body should also not be misled. He made an example of Postbank, where unrealistic targets were approved by those who had to do oversight. The Committee appreciates the fact that it was bringing people in who understood the space, but the rules should be clear. There would always be unavoidable tensions among people who knew the space better. These unavoidable tensions, in the main, should be about how best to move forward.
He appreciated the fact that SITA could learn from other entities about what could be done better. He also showed an appreciation for automation, which should be at a level where both clients and people within SITA would be able to use technology to enhance the supply chain. Automation could lead to realisation of an open tender system, where citizens could see it from entry to the final decision by using the same technology that was available. It would help with building the SITA brand, because there were professionals at an executive level that did not want to associate themselves with the organisation because of the damage of the past involving corruption and so on. This was something that should be worked on to ensure that SITA was the entity that it needed to be. It needed to assure South Africans that processes were competitive and transparent. This was where people would be able to see why a particular contract was not awarded. It should also be about going into the future and ensuring that the idea of an open tender is realised.
He noted the statement that it was not about bringing in new strategies, but about ensuring implementation of those already in place. The Ministry had intervened by establishing a caretaker executive at a particular time. If this was not managed, this was what happened. At the time when the intervention was implemented, SITA had been taken from a 25% performance level to almost 75%. The performance was now around 64%, with the intention of getting it to between 80,-100%. This assurance was quite important because sometimes more time was spent on bringing in new ideas instead of implementing the programmes that would have been on the table. So, when the Committee did its oversight, it would not refer to something else when the board was actually trying to do something else that it thought would work. This had also been the experience with strategies in other entities now struggling within the portfolio. There were problems in some of these entities.
The Committee really wanted a speedy resolution to the strike so that services were not compromised, and to ensure everyone was onboard in implementing discipline. The Committee was confident that both SITA and the DCDT would give the necessary support to ensure that these matters were resolved speedily. He raised the point that Mr Molala had mentioned that when people were talking about salaries, there was an opportunity to raise political matters. He hoped that this separation could be made so that matters that revolved around affecting work at that level should also receive attention on the appropriate platforms.
He said that this was the summary of the discussions that had taken place.
Follow-up question
Ms Z Majozi (IFP) said that she was also part of the Portfolio Committee on Police, and the reason given by the South African Police Service (SAPS) as to why it could not go digital was because they were claiming that SITA was the one that should be providing the services to them. Had there been any engagement or agreement on providing such a service?
Dr Mabaso said that he had met with General Masemola, the Police Commissioner,  last month to discuss a whole range of issues, including case management solutions. SITA had promised to come back to him with a proposal on how to fast-track delivery and the implementation of solutions. There was constant communication in this regard.
The Chairperson said the Committee would handle the South African Post Office Amendment Bill next Tuesday.
The meeting was adjourned.
