Title: Solidarity and Cape Forum Petition; DEL Revised 2023/24 Annual Performance Plan (with Minister & Deputy Minister)
Date: 15 Nov 2023
Committee: 
URL: https://pmg.org.za/committee-meeting/37985/
============================================================

Meeting Summary
Video
The Portfolio Committee on Employment and Labour met on a virtual platform for a briefing by the Department of Employment and Labour on the Petition sent to the Speaker in June 2023 by Solidarity and Cape Forum regarding regulations to facilitate the Employment Equity Act. The Revised Budget and the Revised Annual Performance Plan of the Department were presented following budget cuts by National Treasury and additional financial implications for the Department as well as an obligation to increase and adapt performance indicators.
In his remarks, the Minister reminded Members of the words in the Preamble to the Constitution, that apartheid had been labelled “a crime against humanity” and that laws addressed the injustices of the past. Following the signing of the Employment Equity Act No 4 of 2022 by the President, the Department developed draft regulations to facilitate the Act to the distress of those who stood for injustice and anti-transformation. The petition was irrelevant because it was sent to the Speaker by a group that had not read the word "DRAFT" on the regulations. The Department was fine-tuning the Revised Draft Employment Equity Regulations on the proposed Sector and Numerical Targets for Employment Equity, which would include those comments and proposals that were consistent with the Constitution. It would be made available to the public for further comment.
The Department of Employment and Labour rejected the validity of the petition as the regulations were still in draft form and yet to be re-published for further public comment. The Solidarity Labour Court case on the alleged unconstitutionality of certain sections of the EE Amendment Act, No 4 of 2022 and referenced in the petition had been withdrawn by Solidarity and a Settlement Agreement had been signed between Solidarity and the government in June 2023. The contents of the Settlement Agreement would form part of the final Employment Equity Regulations.
Members asked for confirmation that the contents of that settlement agreement would be contained in the new draft regulations. Were the three provisions set out upfront in the government’s settlement agreement with Solidarity going to be taken into account and reflected when the regulations were published again? When would the new regulations be gazetted? When would the Employment Equity Amendment Act be signed into law?
The Department of Employment and Labour presented amendments to the Annual Performance Plan for 2023/24 that had been tabled in March 2023. Following an alignment of the Annual Performance Plans of all government departments to priorities of government and the addition of further priorities had necessitated the revision. The Department was requested to add some indicators to the Annual Performance Plan, some of which were already contained in the Annual Performance Plans of the branches within the Department but had to form part of the departmental Plan for 2023/24. One or two new indicators had been included. The additional indicators had an impact on the work of the Department, especially in matters where the completion date had been brought forward to April 2024. In addition, the departmental budget had been impacted by funding cuts imposed by National Treasury and several allocations within the Department had consequently been reduced. In addition, the Department was required to pay the Public Service Wage Agreement increases from its compensation of employees’ budget, with no additional funding being granted, which meant that the budget would no longer cover the current vacant posts. Those posts became unfunded and could not be filled.
Members asked why the only effective entity in the Department, the Commission for Conciliation, Mediation and Arbitration (CCMA), had been selected for a budget cut of R10 million. Why had the government caved into the demand for the Public Service increase when the Minister of Finance had strongly urged the government not to agree to the increase because the government did not have the money, nor could it sustain the increase? Would the R15 million cut in Inspections and Enforcement Services mean that no inspectors were going to be employed? What would be affected in Administration as a result of the R24 million cut to that division? Could the Department provide more specific information on the impact of the budget cuts?
Meeting report
Opening Remarks
The Chairperson recapped events for Members of the Committee. A petition had been sent to the Office of the Speaker by Solidarity and Cape Forum on the issue of the Employment Equity Act regulations in June 2023, reflecting their dissatisfaction with issues raised in the Employment Equity Act. The Portfolio Committee was obliged to respond to the petition via the Office of the Speaker. The Committee had to look into the memorandum but also had to request the Department to appear before it because the regulations referred to had not been through the parliamentary process to date. The Department was requested to take the Committee through the process.
She had requested Ms Sueanne Isaac, Parliamentary Legal Advisor, Constitutional and Legal Services Office (CLSO), to attend the session as it was a legal matter and she might have to advise the Committee.
The officials of the Department of Employment and Labour were introduced and the DDG for Employment Services, Mr Sam Morotoba, indicated that representatives from four provinces were in attendance:
KwaZulu-Natal, Eastern Cape, Limpopo and Northern Cape.
The Chairperson called on the Minister of Employment and Labour to make his opening remarks.
Opening remarks of the Minister of Employment and Labour
Minister Thulas Nexi first apologised for his absence at the last Portfolio Committee meeting but South Africa's membership and participation in international organisations was crucial to advance national interests. It had to be more than just affiliation to an organisation; active participation was required in a manner that was beneficial to the growth and the interests of the country. When he was away at the International Labour Organisation (ILO), the Acting DG, Dr Moemi joined the meeting on
8 November 2023
. He had been DG in the Department of Sports and Recreation and later was DG in the Department of Transport as well as the Department of Public Works and Infrastructure. He was now a DDG in the Department of Employment and Labour to make his own contribution to the fulfilment of the mandate of the Department of Employment and Labour.
The Minister reminded Members that the Constitution of the democratic South Africa states that: “We the people of South Africa recognise the injustices of our past and honour those who suffered for justice and freedom in our land; respect those who have worked to build and develop our country and believe that South Africa belongs to all who believe in it, united in our diversity…”. He reminded those who had forgotten that the United Nations had labelled apartheid as “a crime against humanity”. That was fundamental for some of the denialists today. When the government formulated laws or repealed them, it did so ensuring that the injustices and imbalances of the past were being addressed. If it did not do that, the fledgling democracy would be empty and totally meaningless. That was what some did when they wanted to protect the privileges of apartheid.
He reminded the Portfolio Committee that it had put together the Amendments to the Employment Equity Act and the entire Parliament had adopted the Amendments and sent them to the President for assent. The President did exactly that in April 2023. The subsequent process of developing draft regulations to facilitate the Act had followed. What was remarkable in the law of the country was that everyone was given an opportunity to comment and engage. Everyone commented on the Employment Equity Amendment Bill and all comments were scrutinised. There was no way that regressive input would have made it into the final text, but all constructive amendments found space in the law and that was what happened in the Employment Equity Act, No 4 of 2022. Those who stood for injustice and anti-transformation were still crying. Some of them were against the concept as early as 1998. When the Department published draft Employment Equity regulations, those people jumped up and distorted the content and the purpose of the documents. That was the reason that the Committee had received a petition from the National Assembly Speaker, which it ought to respond to. The petition was from the group that had not read the word "DRAFT" in big, underlined capital letters, which was an invitation for views and comments, and he was glad to see that some had submitted comments. Having received all the comments, the Department, together with the Commission for Employment Equity, was fine-tuning the Revised Draft Employment Equity Regulations on the proposed Sector and Numerical Targets for Employment Equity. Once the process on the proposed Sector and Numerical Targets for Employment Equity had been completed, the draft would include those comments and proposals that were consistent with the Constitution, as the court had instructed the Department. The Revised Draft will be available to the public for further comment. The Department had committed to monitor and report on the targets set by the Presidential Job Summit Agreement and in its Annual Performance Plan, the Department's plans spelt out how it would develop employment.
The Minister added that the Department's APP would be presented by the Acting DG, Dr Alec Moemi, and his team. He would provide details but it involved what one could only refer to as cutting one's jacket according to the size of the cloth. The APP would ensure that irregular expenditure was eliminated but, importantly, it would ensure that key priorities contained in Performance Agreements signed with the President were indeed implemented. The priorities had to be featured in the 23/24 Annual Performance Plan.
The Chairperson thanked the Minister for his insight. She invited the Department to make a presentation on its response to the petition.
DEL’s Response to the Petition by Solidarity and Cape Forum on EE Regulations, 2023
Dr Moemi informed the Committee that Mr Mkalipi would take the Committee through the Department’s response. The Minister had covered the political overview on the matter, but Mr Mkalipi would provide details of the response. He requested that the Chief Operations Officer, Ms Marsha Bronkhorst, be permitted to present the changes to the Annual Performance Plan (APP).
Mr Thembinkosi Mkalipi, Chief Director for Labour Relations, DEL, made a fairly brief presentation, rejecting the validity of the petition as the Regulations had not yet been passed; the Regulations were currently a draft. He explained that the key features of the Employment Equity Amendment Act, assented into law by the President and promulgated in 2023, were to empower the Minister to regulate Sector-Specific EE Numerical targets and to promulgate section 53 of the EEA for the issuing of the EE Compliance Certificate as a prerequisite for doing business with any organ of the state.
Public comments had been called for and incorporated into the Revised Draft EE Regulations on proposed Sector EE targets. It was yet to be re-published for further public comment to extend the consultation process. The referenced Solidarity Labour Court case on the alleged unconstitutionality of certain sections of the EE Amendment Act, 2022 had been withdrawn by Solidarity and a Settlement Agreement had been signed between Solidarity and the government in June 2023. The contents of the Settlement Agreement would form part of the final EE Regulations.
Mr Mkalipi stated that the petition by Solidarity and the Cape Forum had been overtaken by developments. It was a misuse of the parliamentary processes.
(See Presentation)
Dr Moemi added that essentially the petition was premature. He noted that the petitioners were asking Parliament to curtail the work of another branch of the state. The separation of powers was important to note and the Executive should be given space to do its work.
Discussion
Mr M Bagraim (DA) had read the petition and also the settlement agreement entered into between the ministry and Solidarity and quite clearly what had happened in terms of the settlement agreement was that Solidarity's claims in the court had been agreed to by the Department. All he was looking for was for the Minister to confirm that the contents of that settlement agreement would be contained in the new draft regulations. In other words, the current draft regulations would be withdrawn and a new set of draft regulations would be issued for comment and the new regulations would contain the contents of the settlement agreement between Solidarity and the Department of Employment.
Mr M Cardo (DA) asked when the new regulations were going to be gazetted. Building on from Mr Bagraim's point, he thought that the settlement agreement with Solidarity, which was facilitated by the CCMA under the supervision of the ILO, was absolutely key. What interested him about that settlement was that there were three provisions upfront that stated essentially that affirmative action had to be a coherent packet of measures: firstly, they must be temporary in nature in line with the Constitution; secondly, the settlement agreement states that affirmative action shall be applied in a nuanced way; thirdly, it says that no absolute barrier may be placed upon employment practices. None of those provisions was reflected in the draft regulations that were published in May and he wanted to know whether those three things were going to be taken into account and reflected when the regulations were published again.  One had to face it that the so-called targets were an absolute misnomer, they … [break in connectivity] unconstitutional. Would those particular provisions of the settlement agreement be reflected in the regulations when they were gazetted again?
Ms H Denner (FF+) indicated that most of her questions had been covered by her two colleagues. When would the Employment Equity Amendment be signed into law? She asked for a ballpark date.
Mr Mkalipi responded by referring to the presentation. Firstly, the settlement agreement with Solidarity happened well after the draft regulations had been published and, consequently, whatever was in the settlement agreement could not have been in the draft regulation. Secondly, the Department had entered into a settlement agreement with Solidarity in good faith and the Department understood the issues. Part of the settlement argument said that the Department would incorporate the issues in the regulations, but it did not say that a draft regulation should change policies. He had already said that the regulations would be re-published for public input and comment. The Department would include all the issues in the law as well as the issues that were agreed to in terms of the settlement agreement.
On the issue of when the law would be promulgated, he said that was a decision for the President. The Department was working on the issue and would go back to the President when it was ready, having drafted all the regulations, and knowing that everything was in order. Even after the Department had asked the President to promulgate, it would depend on the President when the law was promulgated. However, the Department had not finalised its processes and could not give the date.
Minister Nxesi repeated that having received all the comments, the Department, together with the Employment Equity Commission was fine-tuning the Revised Draft Employment Equity Regulations and Sectoral Employment Equity Targets. Once that process was completed, those revised (underlined) draft employment regulations would be published for further comment. There was no talk about withdrawal of what the Department had published: it was about revision. Withdrawal was in somebody's mind, but the Department was not going to withdraw anything. The comments and proposals that were consistent with what the Constitution instructed the Department to do would be considered. It was still a process. He emphasised that the Department had agreed to what was in the settlement; it was a court order, declared by a court of law.
The Chairperson stated that the key thing was that it would not be correct for the Portfolio Committee to be involved in a process of the Department. The Committee could not, by default, discuss regulations that were not yet in front of the Members. The Committee could also not be involved in a process in which an organisation brought a petition when the processes were still at the draft stage. The Committee would allow the Department to finish its processes and table the regulations in front of the Committee. At that stage, the Committee would be advised on what to do as Parliament, but for the moment, the Committee should allow the Department to finish its processes. The secretariat would prepare a report for the Office of the Speaker.
The Chairperson moved on to the second item on the agenda, which was the presentation by the Department on the APP and the impact of the budget cuts by the Treasury.
Presentation of the DEL Strategic Plan 2020/2025 and Amended Annual Performance Plan 2023-2024
Ms. Marsha Bronkhorst, Chief Operations Officer (COO), DEL, presented only the amendments to the Annual Performance Plan (APP) 2023/24.
Ms Bronkhorst explained that the APP had been tabled in March 2023, but the Department of Public Planning, Monitoring and Evaluation had undertaken an alignment of the APPs of all government departments to several priorities presented in the State of the Nation Address. The APPs were also aligned to the Ministers’ Performance Agreements signed with the President. After subsequent consultations between the President and the Deputy President, further priorities were identified and the Department was requested to add some indicators to the APP, some of which were already contained in the Annual Performance Plans of the branches within the Department. They had to be lifted to form part of the APP.  One or two new indicators had been included. She referred to slides 17, 19, 22, 23 and 24 where the amendments had been highlighted in red.
Slide 17 showed that the Department could not fill its vacancies and so could not meet targets, especially in respect of the percentage of women in senior management. Slide 19 showed that the reconfiguration of the Department and the unbundling of the Unemployment Insurance Fund (UIF) and the Compensation Fund (CF) was to be finalised within the term of the Sixth Parliament, i.e. April 2024. Slide 22 showed an increase in the number of job or career fairs that the DEL held annually to nine, i.e. one per province. That was because the President had been highly impressed with the benefits that such shows offered to people who attended the fairs. A National Labour Migration Policy (NLMP) and the Employment Services Amendment Bill (ESAB) were to be finalised and approved by the Minister for submission to the Cabinet by the end of the year.  Draft five-year Sector EE targets were to be developed and published for public comment by 31 March 2024 and also by that date, a Framework Agreement for a Social Compact was to be adopted at NEDLAC.
Presentation- Chief Finance Officer
Mr Bheki Maduna, CFO, DEL, informed the Committee that the National Treasury had asked the Department to implement a reduction in the voted funds for the current financial year 2023/24 and also in the outer years. The outer years had not been confirmed as the Department was currently engaged with the Treasury on what the position would be if the Department implemented 6% and 8% cuts, respectively, in the outer years. In the current year, the reduction in funds was 3% of the budget, which amounted to R79.4 million. The cut was distributed across Administration (R24m), Inspection and Enforcement Services (R15m), Public Employment Services (R23m), Labour Policy and Industrial Relations (R17m). The allocation of R4 092 225 000 was reduced to R4 012 776 000. Cuts were made, inter alia, in advertising, audit costs, operating leases on machinery and equipment, the Compensation Fund and the Commission for Conciliation, Mediation and Arbitration (CCMA).
He added that, as the CEO had indicated, there was a moratorium on filling vacancies as departments had been asked to fund the government wage agreement within their budgets, which had consequently wiped out all the funding for the vacant posts.
The Chairperson asked Mr Maduna to explain the position regarding vacant posts on slide 7 more clearly.
Mr Maduna explained that the slide showed the funded vacancies in the Department in June 2023 because at the time there was funding in the compensation of employees’ budget. Based on the calculations that Treasury had made regarding the wage agreement, the cost to the DEL was R80 million. That amount should have been added to departmental budgets. However, the fiscus did not have that amount, so departments were asked to find the Public Service Wage Agreement increases within their own budgets. When there was a wage agreement and the public servants in the Department received increases, Treasury normally provided additional funding but now there was no additional funding despite the compensation of employees’ budget increasing by R80 million. That additional amount had eaten into the funding for vacant positions, which then became unfunded.
(See Presentation)
Dr Moemi added that it was a matter beyond the Department’s control: Treasury had issued an Instruction and so the Department would have to cut where necessary. None of the areas being cut were making their jobs any easier compared to what was expected to be delivered. Nevertheless, the government had to collectively address the challenge that was on the table. What was presented to the Committee was the Department’s effort to contribute to the bigger picture, although, as Members could see from the earlier presentation by Ms Bronkhorst, it impacted the Department’s ability to deliver on commitments.
Discussion
Mr Bagraim stated that it was a bleak report. It was actually a bit depressing. He understood that the Department's hands were tied behind its back because of the wage increase that the civil servants were given. He had a couple of concerns. First of all, R10 million was taken off the CCMI budget. Every year when there was a budget cut, the CCMA had its budget cut and, effectively, it was the only entity within the Department that was actually working. The public was starting to suffer from the budget cuts at the CCMA which was a vital organisation for the public; it was going to be very vital going into a period with the mines going through retrenchments, disputes and all sorts of other things like that. Even the Minister had referred to the Solidarity agreement that was sorted out by the CCMA. So it was a vital institution and for the Department to choose to take R10 million off the CCMA was folly. The Department could have taken money from Productivity which did very little or even from Supported Employment Enterprises (SEE). He was begging and pleading that the Minister had a rethink about taking the R10 million off the CCMA budget.
Mr Bagraim's second point was about the wage agreement. The Committee would recall that, at the time, the Minister of Finance strongly urged the government not to agree to that increase for public servants because he did not have the money; the government could not sustain the increase. The current cuts were not where it would end because the following increase in the next financial year had to be a percentage of what had already been increased in the current financial year. The Minister of Finance had pleaded with the country that it was not affordable. And yet the government had caved into the demand for the increase which was now affecting every single department and that was a really bad situation for service delivery in South Africa. The Department of Employment and Labour was supposed to give citizens service delivery but they were not getting it – the only entity that gave service delivery was the CCMA. So he had a problem, on top of which vacancies could not be filled. Members would recall that the vacancies that appeared in all entities within the Department of Employment and Labour became a keynote of the Committee's observations and it had subsequently urged the Department to fill the vacancies. Now it could not do so because it did not have the money and it was probably going to have to create more vacancies because it did not have money to pay people because of the wage agreement. He reiterated that the public service wage agreement was going to create havoc, not just in the Department of Employment and Labour; probably all the other Portfolio Committees were having a similar debate.
The Chairperson said that her concern was the budget cuts addressed on slide two. Inspections and Enforcement Services were suffering a R15 million cut. Did that mean that no inspectors were going to be employed? How would the Administration be affected by the R24 million cut? It would have been much better to show where the impact of the cuts would be felt as that would give Members a proper understanding of the impact of those cuts. The danger was that, down the line, the Department would point to the impact of the cuts on service delivery. She asked again whether the R15 million cut would affect the employment of inspectors. She understood that wage agreements were the terrain of the Department of Public Service Administration but what did the increase mean in terms of posts in the Department of Employment and Labour?
Dr Moemi responded that the DEL had to do what it was required to do; it was obliged to set priorities. The Department had to be realistic about its commitments and the work that it had set out to do and then it had to make decisions on what could be done within the confines of the budget. The ministry had to set priorities, together with what came from the government, Cabinet and the key priorities set for the Sixth Administration.  Those priorities had to be considered when looking at what to sacrifice or to compromise. That methodology had to consider the financial viability of the institution as a whole, as well as its functional ability, so that services did not collapse. It was a science to manage the tightrope in terms of how far one could go and on which key issues. It was not easy. Everyone had their own opinions about what they perceived as the most important service. However, the Department moved from the premise that all the services were important and Parliament had given it the responsibility, via legislation, to roll out services. While every service could be seen as important, the law was binding in terms of what the Department was required to do; the Department could not pick and choose which legislation to implement, and which not to implement.
Dr Moemi believed that the Department had faced up to the real issue and had made difficult decisions about what had to be taken forward and what not. The Department had presented the budget cuts to the Committee in the reverse, but he was willing to be guided and to listen to what was being posited by Members. The Department would relook at the budget cuts to understand the impact of what was being suggested and the impact of not doing what the Committee requested it to do. Ultimately, the Minister would make a decision as far as that was concerned. He reiterated that the principle was that everybody had to sacrifice and the bottom line, before considering the viability test, was that the DEL had to look at its principal position which was that everybody had to sacrifice a percentage and from there the budget was built based on the priorities of the Administration. The Department had looked at cross-subsidising, but there were no specific rules. The decisions taken were scientific in the sense that a clear economic viability test had to be undertaken to ensure that the Department remained afloat, but it was also an art because one had to undertake a balancing act while making difficult choices of where to cut.
He assured the Committee that the Department would consider the input of Members and would also reflect, with the Minister, on the issues raised. It was not as difficult as one would think but there had been a lot of work behind the Department’s arrival at those figures. If the Committee wanted to know what the total impact would be, that could be done. He could also draw an exception report that would show the impact on goods and services; he could show the reduction and what it might mean in real terms.
Regarding the availability of inspectors, he said that the solution did not, in the main, rest with bigger increases in the numbers of inspectors, it had more to do with becoming more efficient and introducing monitoring technologies that would have a multiplier effect. The Department was engaging with law enforcement agencies, including the State Security Agency. DEL was quite targeted and undertook intelligent light inspections; it could not be all over the show with the number of inspectors it had, considering the number of registered companies in South Africa and considering the spread and the diversity of industries in South Africa which was one of the most developed and diversified economies on the African continent. It would be impossible to reach all companies across the length and breadth of the Republic. Being much more targeted, the DEL was using its limited resources much more efficiently, tending towards technology, using things like drone monitoring, particularly for construction sites. Artificial intelligence programmes were emerging and becoming stronger and cheaper. To that extent, DEL would seek to diversify and modernise its Inspectorate. Discussions had been undertaken with the Inspector General on the matter. The latest equipment and technology could also reduce the burden on administration, reporting and that type of work; it would free up inspectors to spend more time on site. Such advancements would contribute to a much greater extent than simply increasing the number of individuals.
Dr Moemi stated that one had to look at the situation, and even if it was a crisis, one should never let a good crisis go to waste. if all things were considered, one could at least come out of the crisis even better, more resilient and more able and agile to achieve what was necessary. He would share how DEL had arrived at the numbers. In terms of the impact on future employment within the Department, the CFO could respond.
Mr Maduna addressed the issue of the CCMA. The allocation for the CCMA was R1.051 billion and the allocation for the Department was R4 billion, so it stood to reason that the CCMA received the biggest share of the budget. If there were a budget cut as crude as the compensation of employees, funds for which were allocated separately, and the rules for it were different from other sectors of the budget, DEL was left with no other choice than to look at CCMA. If the biggest line item was the compensation of employees and the second one was the transfer to CCMA, DEL could not just overlook CCMA.
Concluding remarks
The Chairperson said that the executive authority and the officials had to understand that when Members saw the budget cuts, they naturally panicked because, day to day, they were on the ground and could see what was happening. No one complained about the services and the queues at a bank but they complained about service in hospitals and at the Department of Employment and Labour, etc. While the Department was dealing with the technologies for inspectors, Members received calls and emails from people who were not paid on time, about the UIF, etc. The revised APP was really going to create a serious problem, not only for Members but for the country at large and for workers. The Members were awaiting the date for the Department’s Budget Vote debate; it had not yet been received. She adjourned the meeting.
The meeting was adjourned.
