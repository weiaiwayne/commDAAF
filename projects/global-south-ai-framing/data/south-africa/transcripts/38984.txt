Title: Election of Chairperson; DoJ&CD / Chief Justice 2024/25 Performance Plan, with Ministry
Date: 09 Jul 2024
Committee: 
URL: https://pmg.org.za/committee-meeting/38984/

Meeting Summary
The Portfolio Committee on Justice and Constitutional Development elected Mr X Nqola (ANC) as Chairperson.
The incoming Chairperson noted and listed the many outstanding matters that the Sixth Administration had left and said the Committee had to design a programme that allowed it to finish the necessary work.
The Committee received presentations from the Office of the Chief Justice and the Department of Justice and Constitutional Development on their 2024/25 Annual Performance Plans and Budgets. Both highlighted budget cuts and their negative impact on their ability to deliver services and fill vacant posts.
The Committee was concerned about the budget reductions that OCJ would experience and how this would affect its operations and functioning. The Committee was concerned that the budget cuts would impede the work of the OCJ and limit the implementation of the OCJ’s ideas and programmes. There was concern that the budget cuts would inhibit the roll-out of the ICTs and modernising projects of the OCJ. The Committee was interested in understanding what mitigating efforts would be done to address the impact of the budget cuts. The Committee was committed to working more proactively to address the budget cuts experienced by the OCJ.
The Committee was also concerned about the OCJ’s vacancy rate and how this affected its functioning.
Due to time constraints, the Committee could not fully engage with the DoJ & CD on its APP and Budget. In addition, Members had only received the presentation document that same day and could not fully prepare for the meeting. As a result, it was resolved that a follow-up meeting would be necessary for the Committee to engage with the Department and review the documents received.
The Department highlighted that a key priority is reviewing and replacing colonial and apartheid-era justice-related legislation.
Meeting report
Opening Remarks
The Committee Secretary welcomed members and officials to the meeting. The first purpose of the meeting was to elect a Chairperson for the Committee.
The support staff and members introduced themselves.
Election of the Chairperson
The Committee Secretary indicated that according to National Assembly (NA) Rule 158, the Committee had to elect a Chairperson. He called for nominations.
Mr O Mathafa (ANC) nominated Mr X Nqola (ANC).
Mr C Dugmore (ANC) seconded the nomination.
Mr Nqola accepted the nomination.
A member indicated that if Dr J Hlophe (MK) had been present, she would have liked to nominate him for the position, but he was not present to accept the nomination.
No other nominations were received.
Mr Nqola was duly elected as the Chairperson of the Committee.
Chairperson’s Remarks
The Chairperson thanked the members and support staff for their support. Previously, the Committee oversaw two departments, but as a result of the President’s Cabinet appointments, the Committee had to be formulated in accordance with the portfolios designated by the President.
The Chairperson indicated that the Sixth Administration had left many matters that the Committee needed to conclude. The appointment of the Deputy Public Protector had been deferred to the Seventh Administration. The Thabo Bester matter had been moved to another committee. The Sixth Administration had not processed a number of bills, and they had to be dealt with by the Committee. The Committee had to design a programme that allowed it to finish the necessary work.
The Chairperson asked the Committee to work cordially together to ensure that the core business of Parliament would be completed. The members should put aside their different political approaches and not allow them to hinder the work of Parliament. The Committee had a big load, and it was likely to meet several times a week.
The Department of Justice and Constitutional Development (DoJ & CD) and the Office of the Chief Justice (OCJ) will brief the Committee on their Annual Performance Plans (APP) and budget. The Minister and Deputy Minister of DoJ & CD will join the meeting later.
The Chairperson invited members to introduce themselves.
The members shared their congratulations for the newly elected Chairperson.
Adv G Breytenbach (DA) said that the Committee had worked exceptionally well in the sixth administration. She was hopeful that the Committee would be as productive as the previous one. The previous Committee was largely able to leave party politics behind and focus on its job. As a returning member of the Committee, she welcomed new members to engage with her regarding the committee's work.
Mr S Nomvalo (MK) extended an apology on behalf of Dr Hlophe, who was not present at the meeting. MK was committed to contributing to the work of the Committee and applying high standards of professionalism and respect. He indicated that MK expected to receive respect in return from all members of the Committee. He was hopeful that the Chairperson would act in his capacity as the Chair and not as a politician. Members of MK would treat the Chairperson as he treated them.
Adv B Mkhwebane (EFF) expressed her hope that the Chairperson would act objectively and serve the Committee in the best interests of the public. She agreed with Mr Nomvalo that depending on the treatment received, so would the EFF treat the Chairperson. The Committee had several issues to address, including resolving some Truth and Reconciliation Commission (TRC) cases and non-performance by various courts. The Committee had to prioritise service delivery that impacted the public. She hoped that the Committee could work together effectively and be responsive to the public. A key issue would be how the Committee holds the Department and various institutions to account. The legacy report of the Sixth Committee highlighted several meetings but did not indicate what decisions had been taken or whether they had been implemented. When members of the public complained about the various institutions, she suggested that there should be dedicated members within the Committee to ensure that the complaints were attended to.
Ms H Denner (FF+) indicated that she was a new member of the Committee. She agreed that the Committee should make decisions that are in the best interest of South Africans.
Adv S Swart (ACDP) was grateful that the elected Chairperson had previously served on the Committee and had valuable institutional knowledge. There was a lot of work ahead for the Committee. It was important to improve the Committee’s oversight. Justice was important in society. The Committee should continue to improve on the work of the Sixth Administration. He requested that the documents for the briefings be sent electronically before the Department presented.
Mr Mathafa agreed that the Committee would be very busy. The Committee dealt with issues that attracted a lot of public interest.
The Chairperson indicated that the meeting would break for lunch and reconvene for the Department and OCJ presentations.
The Chairperson stated that the Committee would receive presentations from the Department and OCJ on their APPs and budgets for 2024/25. The OCJ would present first, followed by a discussion by members and officials; following this, the Department would present, and members would be given the opportunity to engage with the presentation. The Chairperson requested that the presenters be mindful of time.
Office of the Chief Justice 2024/25 APP and Budget Presentation
Ms Memme Sejosengwe, Secretary General (SG), OCJ, presented OCJ’s APP and Budget.
The Peace and Security allocation for the 2024 Medium-Term Expenditure Framework (MTEF) accounts for 12.4% of the government’s consolidated expenditure. The function is allocated R765 Billion to combat crime and ensure territorial integrity. Approximately 22.3% of the overall allocation is directed to ‘Law courts and prisons.’
The OCJ is allocated R2.39 Billion in 2024/25, which has decreased by -11.2% from 2023/24. R1.2 Billion is allocated to programmes, and R1.7 Billion is allocated to Judges’ salaries. Some of the main cost drivers of the OCJ include the Cost of Employees (CoE), the legislated benefit for Judge’s motor vehicles (expected to increase), travel and subsistence expenses, costs relating to the activities of the Judicial Service Commission (JSC), costs relating to Information and Communication Technology (ICT), and the appointment of acting Judges.
The OCJ experienced budget reductions amounting to R446.6 million over the medium term. The allocated budget for 2024/25 is 6% (R160.5 million) lower than the 2023/24 actual expenditure. The impact of the R138 million budget reduction will result in overspending the allocated budget for 2024/25 by R329.3 million, compromised support to the Judiciary and Superior Courts that will affect the realisation of set performance targets, challenges meeting payments in terms of existing contractual obligations, challenges in filling vacancies, and a negative impact on service delivery and access to justice.
Over the MTEF period, OCJ aims to strengthen access to justice by ensuring effective administration through modernising processes and infrastructure in the Superior Courts. A cloud-based digital system would be implemented to provide online case initiation, case management and evidence presentation. The Court Online system is expected to be rolled out in 2024/25 to the Labour Court, Labour Appeal Court and the Land Court in Gauteng.
As of June 2024, the OCJ's vacancy rate was 10.27%, excluding 15 ICT posts. Including the ICT posts brings the vacancy rate up to 11%.
The presentation highlighted the various OCJ Programmes, including administration, superior court services, and judicial education support, and the targets under these programmes.

Discussion
The Chairperson thank OCJ for the presentation and invited members to share any questions.
Mr Dugmore said that the members had not received the presentation before the meeting. He requested that moving forward, presentations be submitted to the Committee before the meeting. This would allow for a more robust engagement.
Mr Dugmore referred to the motor vehicle policy for Judges. He noted that, according to the presentation, this policy would impact OCJ's operational budget. He requested clarity on the previous policy. What about the new policy would create such a big impact on the operational budget? What was the cost of this change?
Mr Mathafa raised the issue of the budget cuts. Were there any particular criteria or level of involvement from OCJ when the Treasury made the budget cuts?
Mr Mathafa asked what mitigating factors were being put in place to ensure that the budget cuts did not render the Department ineffective. When engaging with Treasury, what was being put in place to ensure that OCJ would continue to fulfil its core mandate?
Mr Mathafa highlighted the six senior management vacancies. What measures were in place to ensure that OCJ could still perform at the committed level?
Adv Swart expressed concern with the budget cuts. Parliament had to pass the budget, and it was unacceptable that every year, members made resolutions that the budget cuts were unacceptable. The budget cuts would have a dire impact on the OCJ and all the entities it supported. The impacts would be felt across the Judiciary. The Committee had to exercise its powers and make submissions on the Appropriations Bill. Time was very limited, but the Committee could not allow the Judiciary to suffer because Treasury did not appreciate the work of the Judiciary. When the Judiciary made their annual presentation, it was said that Judges themselves could not make a presentation on their budget – they had to work through the OCJ. It was important to work to mitigate the risks of the budget cuts.
Adv Breytenbach agreed with Adv Swart’s comments. The presentation set out ‘lofty’ ideas, but there would likely not be sufficient funds to implement them. It was likely that the ideas would not have been implemented next year, the OCJ would be subject to further budget cuts, and the OCJ would be no further than what it is. It was time for the Committee to act more proactively. It was unacceptable that the Judiciary was in a place where they could not properly do their work. The judiciary was expected to work with infrastructure that was falling apart – this had to be done by the Department of Public Works and Infrastructure. It was unlikely that the rolling out of technology would not happen. She noted the OCJ’s plan for the roll-out but said it had not happened last year and would likely not happen this year.
Adv Mkhwebane referred to the level of vacancies within the OCJ. There were funded positions that were vacant. What was the cost of delay in filling the vacancies? The Judiciary and the operations of the courts were labour intensive, and each person was necessary.
Adv Mkhwebane said that the OCJ needed to do more to train new judges. The issue of the Lower Courts Bill still had to be dealt with. The calibre of judges was important. How good was the calibre of the current Judges? Supporting judges would be more successful if there were better systems. Better and more advanced systems, such as artificial intelligence, could fast-track the work of judges. Has the OCJ looked into this?
Adv Mkhwebane highlighted the Labour Court. The vacancy of the Registrar had been an issue in the Sixth Administration. Another registrar was being used to assist the Labour Court with administration issues.
Adv Mkhwebane highlighted the Integrated Justice System. How would OCJ ensure that the system works to its benefit? The OCJ needed to utilise the systems available to assist with the challenges created by the financial constraints.
Adv Mkhwebane asked, in terms of the budget cuts, if the OCJ had utilised its resources fully. It was one thing to have the budget cut, but it was another thing to spend 100% of the budget allocation. She agreed that the Committee had to act proactively against unnecessary budget cuts.
Ms Denner referred to slide 29 regarding quasi-judicial functions. It was indicated that the workload increased by 56%. What was the reason for this increase?
Ms Denner was concerned about the State Information Technology Agency (SITA). Many departments have expressed that they are experiencing problems with SITA. She suggested that the issue of SITA hampering departments' work be put on the Committee’s agenda. It was important to get the proper procurement, but SITA stood in the way.
Ms Denner agreed with the other members regarding the budget cuts and that the Committee had to address this issue proactively.
OCJ Response
Ms Sejosengwe responded to the concerns regarding ICT. Both the SG and the Director General of the Department were part of the Integrated Justice System (IJS) board. The IJS was a central fund that attempted to fund the transversal integrated system within the criminal justice system. The challenge was that there needed to be funds within the institution to roll out the project. SITA procurement issues created a challenge with this. The delays in SITA and the costs were too much.
The OCJ has always spent above 90% of its budget. The OCJ has always been above 98% regarding 30-day payments. The problem came from the austerity measures introduced by Treasury and the Department of Public Service and Administration (DPSA). The OCJ could not fill positions as they became vacant due to the processes in place. The Minister had to go to the Minister of DPSA; DPSA would then assess, and Treasury had to determine if the funds were available. It was a very tedious process. The OCJ could not advertise posts as they became vacant due to this process. It did not matter that the post was funded; it could not be filled until authorised. Judges were extremely unhappy about the process; it was difficult for them to have secretaries or researchers leave and be unable to fill the position.
Using ICT resources to assist the impact of the vacant positions was problematic due to the expenses of ICTs and the delays by SITA. OCJ has paid a lot of money for software licenses, but the budget for ICTs did not allow for much. The courts had very low bandwidth, and upgrading them came at a high cost.
Ms Paula Morapedi, Chief Financial Officer (CFO), OCJ, responded to the question regarding Judges’ vehicles. It was legislated that whenever a judge was appointed, a vehicle had to be procured for them. The issue was that Treasury had reduced the budget, and the vehicle costs did not fall under the direct costs. If Treasury did not assist OCJ, they would likely engage with the Standing Committee on Public Accounts (SCOPA) because it was legislated that the vehicles had to be provided to newly appointed judges. The OCJ was engaging Treasury on the matter.
The Chairperson asked what the threshold was for vehicles procured for judges. What was the upper limit?
Ms Morapedi indicated that the threshold was R2 million. The vehicle was a Mercedes Benz and the price of the vehicle determined the amount.
Ms Morapedi responded to the question regarding the mitigating actions put in place to address the impact of the budget cuts. Regarding the vacancies, many officials were appointed in an ‘acting’ capacity. The OCJ was engaging with the National Treasury as the budget would significantly limit OCJ.
Ms Sejosengwe said that interventions were necessary. She had recommended that the Minister engage with the Minister of Finance. The vacancies at the senior management level would not allow for the successful running of the OCJ. DPSA had not authorised these positions to be filled, but these were critical posts.
In terms of mitigating the budget cuts, she said the nature of the institutions supported meant that funding could not come from anywhere else. Donor funds were not permitted. The OCJ was attempting to use internal staff where possible.
Adv Marelize Potgieter, Deputy Director-General (DDG): Court Administration Services, DoJ &CD, said that operations were being normalised. The comparisons made referred to the COVID-19 period. There was also the implementation of the National Credit Act, which was initially referred to court but, due to case law, was now being dealt with. She said that the increasing workload put unnecessary pressure on staff.
The Chairperson thanked OCJ for their responses. He invited the DoJ & CD to begin their presentation.
DoJ & CD 2024/25 APP and Budget Presentation
Adv Doctor Mashabane, Director-General (DG), DoJ & CD, presented the Department’s 2024/25 APP and Budget.
The 2024/25 APP highlighted ten outcomes and 71 output indicators.
Regarding Programme 1: Administration, it was indicated that the modernising project was still a key priority for the Department to improve the quality and speed up the provision of services. The Department aimed to roll out Recording Audio-Visual Solution (CRAVS) to 626 courtrooms and develop three digitalised administrative solutions to implement the modernisation and digitisation strategy.
The Department planned to finalise 75% of disciplinary cases within 90 days of the first hearing, resolve 80% of grievances within 60 days of lodging and investigate and finalise 81% of reported corruption cases. Further, the Department planned to reduce irregular expenditure by 75% and eliminate 100% fruitless and wasteful expenditure.
In terms of Programme 2: Lower Court Services, the Department indicated that it would continue to implement interventions to address the scourge of Gender-Based Violence and femicide (GBVF). Regarding physical access, the Department plans to refurbish and upgrade 82 court facilities through minor capital works.
Programme 3: State Legal Services planned to address the Masters Service. The Department would ensure that 80% of Guardian Fund applications are paid within 40 days of the date of receipt of all documents. The Department planned to examine 90% of liquidation and distribution accounts in bankruptcy matters within 15 days of receiving all documents. The Department would register 20% of newly deceased estates on the deceased online registration system.
The Department’s priority was the review and replacement of colonial and apartheid-era justice-related legislation.
Under Programme 4: Integrated Justice System (IJS), the Department planned to hold 14 IJS Governance intervention sessions and asses two IJS Operational sites for cyber-attack vulnerability. The Department also planned to deploy Integrated Person Management (IPM) to 40 South African Police Services (SAPS) police stations and connect one additional department or entity to the transversal platform.
The Justice and Constitutional Development Vote (Vote 25) is allocated R24.1 billion for 2024/25, including the direct transfer for Magistrate’s salaries (compared to R 22.96 billion in 2023/24). In real terms, the Vote allocation increases by 0.3% when compared to 2023/24. The total allocated for programmes in 2024/25 is R21.6 billion (compared to R20.6 billion in 2023/24), while R2.5 billion is for Magistrate’s salaries. R53.6 million and R117.3 million are cut from the allocations to the South African Human Rights Commission and the Public Protector South Africa respectively. In the case of the PPSA, the institution will implement cost-containment measures and use cash reserves. The SAHRC plans to freeze the filling of vacant posts and rely on natural attrition to reduce its head count.
The Department noted that the 2024/25 allocations faced several budget pressures and indicated that it was investigating ways to address this. The Department is committed to pursuing efforts to ensure efficiencies. However, these efforts alone would not be sufficient to address the Department's strategic priorities.
During the presentation, an alarm indicating a National Council of Provinces (NCOP) meeting interrupted the meeting.

Discussion
Adv Swart noted that the Committee was being expected to engage with the presentation but had only seen the document during the presentation and had not had time to review the documents beforehand. He suggested that the presentation be treated as a preliminary engagement and that the Committee call the Department again for an additional engagement. This would allow for a more robust engagement with the documents and presentation. He noted that the time constraints had not been anyone's fault.
The Chairperson noted this proposal.
Mr Nomvalo agreed with the suggestion. There was no time in the current meeting to engage on the serious matters of the presentation properly. He requested that the documents be sent to members so that members may engage fully with the documents before engaging with the Department again.
The Chairperson said that the Department now knew that the presentations and documents had to be submitted to the Committee seven days before the actual sitting. This would allow members to engage fully with the documents.
Adv Breytenbach said that it had always been the practice for the Committee to have documents submitted seven days before a particular meeting and that the Department was aware of this. It was unfair and undoable for the Department and OCJ to expect members to be able to adequately engage with a presentation that they had not seen until it was presented and summarised in a meeting.
The Chairperson noted the concerns of members and recognised the need to re-engage with the Department. He would engage with the Committee Secretary to organise another meeting, which may occur virtually if necessary. The Committee Secretary would engage with the Department to set a date.
Mr Mathafa requested that the documents be sent to the Committee. This would allow the members time to review and develop questions. It would also allow members to submit any urgent questions to the Department, which they could respond to in the follow-up meeting.
The Chairperson indicated that the documents should be sent to the Committee Secretary, who would circulate them to members.
Adv Mkhwebane said that the OCJ had not mentioned the number of cases processed through the court. What was the state of the backlogs, and how were they being addressed?
Ms Sejosengwe responded that the judiciary's APP was done independently. Therefore, the requested information did not form part of the OCJ's strategic plan.
Adv Mkhwebane asked if it had been submitted to the Committee. She requested that it be provided to the Committee.
The Chairperson said any other questions could be addressed in the follow-up meeting.
Minister’s Remarks
Ms Thembi Simelane, Minister of Justice and Constitutional Development, apologised for her and the Deputy Minister’s late arrival. She expressed her appreciation for some work that could be done. She was committed to the process suggested by the Committee regarding re-engaging with the Committee. The programme for the Committee was finalised very late and did not allow for the submission of documents beforehand. She indicated that in the future, documents would be submitted to the Committee before meetings.
The meeting was adjourned.
