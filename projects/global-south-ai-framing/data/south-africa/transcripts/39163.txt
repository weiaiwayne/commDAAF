Meeting report
The Committee heard submissions from Youth Capital, the Congress of South African Trade Unions (COSATU), the Indaba Foundation, Equal Education, and the Equal Education Law Centre on the 2024 Appropriation
Bil
l [B5-2024].
Youth Capital
Youth Capital highlighted the youth unemployment crisis, noting that 9.1 million young people were not in education, employment or training. One in five people in the country did not have enough food, and child malnutrition was a significant challenge.
The organisation called for youth employment creation to be prioritised. It urged the government to prioritise spending on creating opportunities for young people. It emphasised the need for investment in quality employment programmes, including extending and protecting the Presidential Employment Stimulus (PES) programme.
Youth Capital proposed amalgamating the PES, the Expanded Public Works Programme (EPED), the Community Work Programme, and the National Youth Service to create a more cohesive strategy. It highlighted the lack of sufficient measures to monitor the effectiveness of youth employment initiatives. It advocated long-term job guarantees to absorb young people into labour-intensive roles, thus stimulating economic growth.

Indaba Foundation
The Indaba Foundation reported that approximately 25 percent of children under six attended early childhood development (ECD) centres regularly. Only 45 percent of children in early learning programmes started school with the right learning foundations. Among the poorest children, only 3 out of 10 of the poorest children attending early learning programmes started school on track, compared to 8 out of 10 from the richest households. In the poorest 80 percent of schools, learners were about 2.5 years behind the curriculum by Grade 3. By Grade 12, 50 percent of learners had dropped out.
The 2023/24 total budget for the education sector in South Africa amounted to R457 billion, but only R3.7 billion, or 0.81 percent of that budget, went to ECD.

COSATU
Mr Mathew Parks, COSATU Parliamentary Coordinator, said the Appropriation Bill had both positive aspects and missed opportunities. He stressed the need to stimulate the economy, reduce unemployment, provide relief to the poor, rebuild the state, and address crime and corruption to achieve economic growth beyond the projected 1.8 percent over the Medium Term Expenditure Framework (MTEF).
Regarding revenue proposals, COSATU welcomed the release of R150 billion from the South African Reserve Bank (SARB) to ease fiscal pressures. Government's efforts to rebuild the South African Revenue Service (SARS) were also acknowledged. The additional allocation of R1 billion to SARS to boost tax compliance was welcomed.
However, COSATU expressed disappointment that National Treasury had missed the opportunity to reduce the budget deficit by not increasing taxes on wealthy individuals, large corporations, and luxury imports. The organisation also expressed concern that lower income tax bands were not adjusted for the Consumer Price Index (CPI). Nonetheless, they noted that the R4 billion fuel levy relief would help mitigate the impact of fuel price hikes on commuters.
See attached for full submission
Equal Education and Equal Education Law Centre
Equal Education and the Equal Education Law Centre called for improvements in school infrastructure, addressing overcrowding, supporting learners with necessary teaching materials, and enhancing ECD support. They urged that budget cuts and other regressive measures be scrutinised more closely, and advocated the ring-fencing of the children's budget. Equal Education proposed increasing the ECD subsidy to at least R46 by 2029, asserting that budget cuts violated children's constitutional right to education.

Discussion
The Chairperson thanked the presenters for their comments and asked Members to be succinct in their questions to make time for responses.
Mr K Wakelin (DA) expressed strong interest in learning more from Youth Capital about various issues related to youth development. He inquired about the organisation's stance on labour legislation and its impact on small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) in creating job opportunities. This question was also directed to COSATU. He asked about the effectiveness of the Expanded Public Works Programme (EPWP) and whether there could be a more consolidated approach to programmes to ensure more meaningful employment. Additionally, he suggested engaging with the Department of Employment and Labour to ensure that funding was directed towards the acquisition of the right skills.
Regarding ECD, as mentioned by the Indaba Foundation, some organisations did better work than government and needed support. Many people in the NGO sector were passionate about creating meaningful change on the ground. However, there was a concern about the trickle-down effect, where a million was allocated at the top, but only R20,000 reached the actual work. Government must ensure that 80 percent of the money is spent where it is needed.
Mr Wakelin acknowledged COSATU's past and present contributions but expressed concern about the organisation's lack of self-reflection and its impact on SMEs. He raised the issue of COSATU's support for labour legislation that might harm the economy and asked what COSATU planned to do about it. He also noted a decline in COSATU's membership and asked about the reasons behind it and its impact. Additionally, he questioned whether COSATU members performed optimally in their jobs. He expressed concern about the increase in contracted services due to lack of performance. He suggested that the funds used for these services could be better utilised to create employment opportunities.
COSATU should propose a more sustainable approach regarding the Social Relief of Distress (SRD) grant. The current approach was not viable, and the focus should be on getting people back into employment. Instead of pressuring the government, COSATU could consider partnering with it. This partnership could be crucial in advancing the country.
Regarding ECD, he commended the Indaba Foundation. He emphasised that child fostering was a major issue. He stressed the importance of ensuring that a child's education was supported in addition to their care. This presented a significant challenge that required urgent attention and resolution.
Lastly, government could make non-cash contributions to the ECD centres. Many of these centres did not get funding from the government because they did not have direct lease agreements, and this red tape must be cut.
Dr M Burke (DA) thanked the presenters and said it took bravery to come and present before the Committee, but it took greater bravery to dedicate one's life to serving the public.
He understood the Indaba Foundation’s comments to say that R3.8 billion was allocated for ECD. More than 60 percent of funding went towards tertiary salaries. He emphasised the disparity in funding, pointing out that it seemed unreasonable for a 19-year-old at university to require 20 times the amount needed by a 15-month-old baby. He believed that there was a significant lack of investment in ECD. He suggested that if the impact of such investment on GDP could be demonstrated, it would help in reallocating the funding appropriately. The noise in the higher education funding sector, sometimes for private political gains, had channelled resources disproportionately to that sector instead of ECD.
Ms H Neale-May (ANC) appreciated COSATU’s presentation. She agreed with COSATU’s view on the Road Accident Fund (RAF). She asked about Youth Capital’s relationship with the National Youth Development Agency (NYDA).
Ms N Gcaleka-Mazibuko (ANC) commented that government needed to invest in children’s development. When this was done, it must ensure that there was compliance as that had been a serious challenge. Government funding was not addressing the depth of the problem. There was no mention of school transport which posed many challenges.
Government was currently the biggest employer. What was COSATU doing to assist the government in fast-tracking the creation of employment? She also asked COSATU about its contribution to investor attraction and productivity.
Ms N Hlonyana (EFF) asked Youth Capital if they believed that the NYDA was sufficiently helping the youth in South Africa. She asked COSATU about steps to be taken to stop the illegal flow of money into the country, which reportedly amounted to 62 billion dollars in 2023. Additionally, she sought COSATU's views on industrialisation. She also questioned whether the appropriation for Defence was adequate.
She found the Indaba Foundation's presentation impressive and asked about the appropriate budget allocation for childhood development, given that about 0.81 percent of the budget was spent in this area. She echoed Equal Education's concern about the reduced allocation for the Funza Lushaka Bursary. She advocated increased funding, emphasising the importance of educators being well-educated.
Mr D Van Rooyen (MK) welcomed the presentations but expressed disappointment at the limited time for engagement. He emphasised the importance of public participation in a participatory democracy and stressed the need for thorough engagement rather than just ticking the boxes. Due to time constraints, he was unable to ask all his questions and requested permission from the Chair to communicate directly with the organisations.
Youth Capital stated that only about 1.2 million jobs were created, while the President mentioned 2 million. He inquired about the disparity. He praised COSATU for its views on neoliberal policies and agreed that these policies were among government's major challenges.
The Indaba Foundation had not specified what it proposed for the current budget, but highlighted their priority areas, which received positive feedback from the Members.
Stakeholders acknowledged the country's crisis, with concerns about the ticking time bomb of youth unemployment and the fact that the country was not on track to meet the National Development Plan (NDP) targets even 30 years after democracy. He suggested that organisations clearly outline their proposed actions to address these issues in the future.
The Chairperson commented that Members could submit written questions to the Secretary, who would circulate the questions to all the presenters. The written questions and responses would be included in the report to be tabled as minutes of the meeting.
Mr W Douglas (MK) said that in Africa, the focus was more on charity than on finding long-term solutions. A major challenge was the lack of recognition of the rights of regional and indigenous people. Significant funds were available from the United Nations and other sources for ECD, youth development, school development, and for the benefit of regional and indigenous people. However, South Africa did not use the language of rights when it came to accessing these funds. Instead, the focus was on charity and asking for handouts. He stressed that regional and indigenous people had rights under the United Nations Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous People, and these rights needed to be recognised. He also emphasised the need to acknowledge the presence of the Khoisan people in South Africa.
Mr M Lekganyane (ANC) suggested that written responses be furnished. He emphasised the need to start discussing infrastructure development. Despite having some of the best facilities, the country was unable to produce the desired results. Additionally, there were contentious issues such as access, redress, admission, language policy, exclusion, and artificial intelligence. The country still failed to produce the skilled individuals it needed; some matriculants failed exams and never returned to school, rendering them unemployable and posing a problem for the country. These were critical developmental issues and microcosms within society, significantly impacting social stability and economic productivity.
Ms M Bartlett (ANC) asked Youth Capital if they were representing the youth of the country, given that the numbers on employment statistics were not the same as those presented by the President. She suggested that young people who were not at school and in employment should be taken into account. They should be educated to be employed.
She sought clarity on COSATU’s views on placing the RAF under administration. Regarding ECD, not all provinces were covered in the statistics. She suggested that monitoring and regulation of ECD and fostering must be revised. Perhaps once they were more formalised and legalised, they could be held to account.
Responses
Indaba Foundation
The Foundation said it would be easier to respond to the questions in writing. There was no question that they supported registration of every centre. There was a need for
en masse
training of the highest quality possible. Without the training, registration was impossible.
The Chairperson recalled the FeesMustFall era. When the appropriation was made for higher education, it was made post-FeesMustFall, and the mid-term budget responded to that. As the Committee consolidated and tabled its report, it must not be the loudest wheel that got the grease; developmental objectives should be considered to ensure that appropriations were made accordingly.
It was decided that due to time constraints, entities could not respond fully but would respond in writing to all the comments and questions of Members.
The meeting was adjourned.
