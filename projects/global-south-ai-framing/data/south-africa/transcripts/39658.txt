The Portfolio Committee on Health (the Committee) convened virtually for the 2023/24 Annual Report briefings by the Council for Medical Schemes (CMS), the South African Health Products Regulatory Authority (SAHPRA), the Office of the Health Ombud (OHO) and the Office of Health Standards Compliance (OHSC).
The overarching theme of the discussions was the lack of funding to enable the various entities to deliver on their statutory mandates. The National Treasury's continuous grant reductions were detrimental to capacity building, efficient service delivery, and keeping management information systems updated. The Committee offered to assist the entities in obtaining adequate funding from the fiscus.
The CMS struggled to achieve some of its targets and generate quarterly reports due to challenges in information and communication technology (ICT). This contributed to unresolved irregular expenditure dating back to the early 2000s. The matter was raised as a consequence management finding by the Auditor-General of South Africa (AGSA). The Lost Control Committee (LCC) has been working on transactions related to the 2022/23 and 2024/25 financial periods. The CMS appointed an external service provider to augment internal capacity and clear the irregular expenditure backlog. A proposal for R55 million over four years was submitted to National Treasury for a once-off revamp of the ICT infrastructure and to fund three permanent IT-related positions. The digital strategy envisaged revamping technologies regarding data migration and replacing old infrastructure with Cloud technologies. The Committee requested to be provided with a quarterly report on strengthening controls related to the audit findings.
The finalisation of regulatory compliance investigation reports within 30 working days is one of the targets the SAHPRA could not meet. The complaints backlog was due to delays in stakeholder contributions to conduct the investigations. Another contributing factor is the lack of resources. The staff complement of 318 fell short of the total of 500. A new case management and document management tool was implemented, forming part of the overall regulatory document ecosystem. To date, only two of the 35 business processes have been digitised. External funding is being sourced to digitise the remaining business processes. The SAHPRA appealed to the Committee to support their funding request with the National Treasury, which was needed to complete the digital transformation project.
The complexity of cases and inadequate capacity to resolve the cases reported to the Heath Ombud resulted in a backlog dating back more than six years. Compliance monitoring is limited to high-risk cases due to the limited number of staff members. The staff, composed of nine members, would have to be doubled to conduct more investigations. A special project was launched to deal with the backlog within 12 to 18 months. The Chairperson agreed to discuss the staffing requirements with the Minister of Health.
The ability of the OHSC to inspect more health establishments across the country is restricted due to staff shortages. Of the 50 000 health facilities across the country, the OHSC can inspect at least 12 500 annually over a four-year period. The staff complement of 132 members needs to be increased by a further 181 members to expand the number of inspections. In the interim, contractors have been employed to assist with inspections. A decentralised model was introduced to speed up the rate of inspections by placing staff members in the various provinces. Additionally, smart inspections were developed to increase the speed of conducting inspections and ensure that reports are released more speedily.
Meeting report
The Chairperson called for reflection and silence before opening the discussion. He thanked members for their support during the previous day's meeting. Important issues emerged from the discussions. He invited the CMS delegation to introduce themselves and proceed with the presentation.
CMS Presentation
Dr Thandi Mabeba, Chairperson, CMS, was honoured to address the Committee on behalf of the CMS and expressed her gratitude for the opportunity to engage Members on healthcare issues. She commended the Committee for their dedication to issues impacting the healthcare sector. The entity achieved an unqualified audit and 86% overall performance for the year under review. The CMS managed to resolve 93.94% of complaints beyond the turnaround time. She briefly highlighted the achievements of the CMS for the period under review. As of 20 March 2024, the entity regulated 71 medical schemes, 34 administrators, 42 active managed care organisations, 2193 broker organisations, and 7,718 individual brokers. These regulated bodies represented 9.04 million medical scheme members and beneficiaries. Healthcare expenditure 2022 totalled R198 billion, and R232 billion in revenue was reported. The industry solvency ratio was calculated at 47.21%. Out-of-pocket payments amounted to R40 billion. She stated that the CMS was steadfast in delivering its mandate.
Mr Mfana Maswanganyi, Acting Chief Executive (CE), CMS, presented the entity's 2023/24 annual performance outcomes. Only six of the 58 applicable indicators were not achieved. The CMS managed to overperform in 23 indicators. As of 31 March 2024, the workforce totalled 140 employees. The staff turnover rate of 10.48% was within the 15% industry norm.
Ms Andiza Zinja, Chief Financial Officer (CFO), CMS, reported on the Annual Financial Statements (AFS) and audit outcomes. Levies from medical aid schemes constituted 88% of the income. Staff costs represented 68% of total expenditure. Irregular expenditure decreased from R1 475 million in 2023 to R893 million in 2024. The LCC is anticipated to conclude the work on irregular expenditure by the end of the current financial year. The financial position is reported to remain stable.

In such cases, the period for completing inspections would be extended to 12 months. The ICT systems were old and poorly maintained. The applications were internally developed by people who had since left CMS, which made it difficult to fix problems when they occurred. The ICT problem had an impact on achieving some targets, such as generating quarterly reports.
He told Ms Clarke that plans are in place to mitigate litigation costs. The general principle is to use internal forums and to reach out to each party to resolve matters amicably to avoid costs. Employees can appeal internally; if unsatisfied, the matter is referred to an Appeals Board. Legal assistance is obtained for external matters only. On-site visits are being conducted to detect the abuse of prescribed medical benefits (PMB) and irregular claim payments.
He replied to Dr Letlape that the labour matter involved former executive members challenging their exit from the CMS. The dispute about performance bonuses involved payments not aligning with HR policy due to affordability and budget constraints. The NHI Act allowed the CMS to play a particular role because of the continued need for the sector to be regulated.
He replied to Ms Shabangu on the issue of consequence management. The LCC had concluded testing recent matters, which were referred to HR for disciplinary procedures. A process to investigate irregular expenditure dating back to 2021/22 is underway.
He advised Mr Van Staden that the restructuring process had been completed and fully implemented. A review process is underway to assess performance since implementation.
Dr Mabeba said the CMS is committed to playing a role in the NHI and has actively participated in the NHI Working Committee. The first phase of implementation started in 2023. The CMS is in a position to shepherd the medical scheme industry, which is an ongoing process.

Mr M Motubatse (MK) enquired about the turnaround time for the ICT challenges.
Dr Michael Willie, Executive Manager: Research and Monitoring, CMS, explained that the data concerning the R40 billion out-of-pocket payments was collected through statutory returns from medical schemes. The amount presents the difference between service provider claims and medical scheme payments. The amount could be understated because members do not always report payments that they have made. Out-of-pocket payments were a function of benefit design and scheme options and were based on premium payments. The NHI landscape is anticipated to bring changes from an operating, contracting and service perspective. The CMS would work with the National Department of Health (NDoH) for guidance on membership profiles and premiums through collaboration with the NHI Task Team dealing with benefit structure. PMB is a function of benefit design. A benefit structure is cost-centric. The CMS was developing PMB definitions on key priority areas to assist medical aid schemes with prescriptions. PMB is important so that members do not have to make out-of-pocket payments.
Due to limited funding, Ms Zinja drew attention to certain vacant positions in the new organisational structure. Concerning consequence management, she replied that non-compliant findings were registered in the early 2000s. A committee of internal staff members analysed each transaction on the register, but the process was delayed due to capacity issues. With the assistance of a service provider, matters were quality-assured and closed. Some of the closed findings resulted in disciplinary actions. The audit finding was related to a contract management issue, which would be closed as the old contract was not renewed. She explained that the reason for the salary increases was based on a benchmarking exercise that resulted in back pay of nine months related to the 2022/23 financial year.
Due to financial difficulties, the entity could not cover all the financial benefits. Since 2019, payments to suppliers and staff have not been fully made. An attempt is being made regarding the new funding model to pay benefits in compliance with the HR policy. However, it was still not possible to fully pay bonuses due to competing priorities such as ICT challenges and the filling of critical positions. The increase in administration costs was due to telecommunication expenses, which are ICT-related. Training and staff development costs include the costs of upskilling staff. The rental increase comprised an annual increase element and adjustments in previous years.
She replied to Ms Senne that the ICT proposal submitted was for R55 million over four years. The amount included three permanent IT-related positions and once-off infrastructure funding to resolve the ICT challenges. As part of the rollover request submitted to National Treasury, money was identified to fund some of the cost pressures within the CMS.
Dr Denisha Jairam-Owthar, CIO, CMS, said the CMS had started the digital transformation journey to revamp technology to help resolve ICT challenges. The current systems would be managed in parallel and with upskilling the team to manage the revamped systems. The digital strategy envisaged a turnaround time of between three and five years because it involved revamping large technologies in terms of data migration and moving towards the Cloud to replace the old infrastructure.
The Chairperson drew attention to questions about the AGSA findings, which must be addressed in writing. He argued that extending a contract beyond its expiry date is a choice. A contract must be reviewed at least four months before it expires to avoid becoming an irregular expense. He encouraged the entity to continue applying consequence management. He requested a quarterly report on strengthening oversight of the audit outcomes and findings. He was pleased to hear that the CMS continued working with the NDoH and other stakeholders to reshape the future role of the CMS in universal health coverage. He urged the CMS to continue public health education on medical scheme options or to encourage and support medical schemes to educate the public on the available products. He thanked the team for their availability and commended them for being knowledgeable about their areas of responsibility.
Dr Mabeba thanked the Chairperson for his guidance. The CMS is committed to delivering its statutory mandate and will continuously engage and communicate with the Committee on the entity's progress and development.

She asked what was needed to achieve 100% functionality, requested a description of an on-site inspection, wanted to know how hospital self-inspections compare to inspections of private and public facilities, and asked how long it would take for all facilities to receive ideal status.
Dr Letlape asked whether the four outstanding appointments were also reviewed within the moratorium period. He wanted to know if internal training involved tertiary institutions. He asked when all facilities would be expected to comply fully with the health standards. He enquired about the different compliance levels. He asked if information about personnel needs compared to funded posts was available. Given the lack of staff and information systems, he enquired about the synergies in inspection findings between the Ombuds Office and the OHSC. He asked how the entity would ensure that shared services would not compromise the autonomy of the Health Ombud.
Ms Khumalo inquired about improving healthcare quality and safety in facilities. She asked for an explanation about the specific AI and data analytics applications being considered. She wanted to know how effective the mechanisms were to ensure compliance with norms and standards and deter non-compliance. She also inquired about the observations regarding enforcing compliance.
Dr Le Roux welcomed the shift towards clinical-based assessments. Given the limited budget and constrained environment, he asked how much an inspection every two or five years would cost. He believed that it would be a useful exercise to expand inspections more widely across the whole country and to consider the costs involved.
Ms Shabangu asked how the OHSC is assisting in strengthening the implementation of the NHI and inquired about the plans to enhance communication between the public, private, and NHI funds regarding data sharing and collaboration for improved healthcare outcomes.
The Chairperson asked if the OHSC could assess the impact of inspections, given the many challenges within the entity. He wanted to know if there was enough time to check on the implementation of recommendations and whether the entity could measure the interventions' success. He asked which facilities are being prioritised. The AGSA report indicated that the entity was missing out in some areas, such as issuing compliance certificates. He enquired about the rationale for the decentralised model. He commended the OHSC for achieving clean audits.
Dr Mndaweni said there are 50 000 health establishments in the country. The entity would be able to inspect at least 12 500 health establishments annually over four years. The decentralised model is meant to speed up the rate of inspections by placing staff in the various provinces. The organogram was revised to include contractors who have been trained and are being supervised by the OHSC. The staff complement of 132 needs to increase by 181 to expand the coverage.
He replied to Ms Clarke that the self-assessments often result in higher scores. The OHSC would be encouraging establishments to be more truthful in their assessments. He resisted making comparisons between the public and private sectors. The norms and standards would be revised to determine the actual outcomes regarding health quality indicators.
He replied to Dr Letlape that the appointments were considered after the moratorium was lifted. It is taking longer to fill some of the posts due to the specialised field of work. Tertiary institutions are developing the in-house training material based on information provided by the entity. He assured the Committee that the standards would not be compromised. Compliance certificates are issued based on an assessment of vital measures. Non-compliant facilities are given time to become compliant. The Act permits the right to challenge any finding. To date, the OHSC has not been challenged on its inspection findings. Provinces are encouraged to use the early warning system to report instances of non-compliance based on the indicators. The concept of smart inspections was developed to enhance the speed of inspections and to release reports speedily. The effectiveness of enforcement is measured in terms of responses to written warnings. The OHSC has started engaging provinces in enforcement actions, which include pre-enforcement workshops to explain corrections for breaches of norms and standards. The OHSC developed an internal programme to review the readiness for the NHI implementation and to have regular contact with the NHI unit. Despite some resistance to the entity's work, the health sector understands the need for regulation. Inspections are shared with the NDoH and provinces to expose the sector to the importance of regulatory work. The cost-benefit of the decentralised model would require a cash injection at the initial stages but it is envisaged to expand the footprint.
Dr Mathabo Mathebula, COO, OHSC, said the OHSC regularly engages with executives of private healthcare groups and with HODs of government departments to encourage compliance with healthcare standards. The results are showing an improvement.
Mr A Phiri, Acting Director of Inspectorate Compliance, OHSC, explained that the inspection process is defined in terms of the level of care provided at a facility. For example, the functional areas of a clinic include four divisions, i.e. management, clinical services, provision for medication, and infrastructure maintenance. An inspection at a clinic could take six hours to complete. In addition to the four clinic divisions, the functional areas at community healthcare centres include a maternity section, a rehabilitation section, and dental services. An inspection by a team of five members would last a whole day. A hospital inspection would last between three and four days, depending on the size of the team. Different methodologies were developed to conduct inspections. Reviewing documents, standard operating procedures, and guidelines form part of the inspection. Observations are made to assess the cleanliness and availability of materials and equipment. Patient records are reviewed, and patient and staff interviews are conducted. The data collected is captured on tablets. The team leader is responsible for ensuring the quality of the data. The entire team validates the data before a report is issued. He could not provide the number of complaints for the 2023/24 period but offered to forward the data to the Committee. Of the 139 re-inspections conducted during the 2022/23 period, 66% were compliant, and 33% were recommended to work with the enforcement until they are ready to comply.
Mr Mapatha replied to Dr Le Roux that a standard cost per inspection had not been determined. The cost would depend on the facility's location; e.g. the cost in the Western Cape would be more than in Gauteng. Other factors include the type of facility and the duration of the inspection; e.g. an inspection at a clinic would last a day and five days at a hospital. The costs at a hospital could vary between R70 000 and R130 000 a week, depending on the number of inspectors.
Dr Kenoshi, said the reports were assisting in protecting the state from litigation. Inspections are done according to standards promulgated and signed off by the Minister of Health. They are discussed with the healthcare establishments and provinces and agreed upon with the NDoH to ensure that the reports are accepted by all stakeholders. The entity has a good legal team that also provides assistance to the Health Ombud.
Dr Mndaweni said the activities of both entities are managed separately, so implementing shared services would not compromise either office's independence. For example, both entities reviewed the Helen Joseph Hospital incident, but the findings were based on the different jurisdictions.
The Chairperson expressed his appreciation to the entire board and the executive team of the OHSC for the engagement. He thanked Members for their commitment, which had started the previous day. He hoped that Members would have questions ready for the Minister's presentation at 18:00 the following day after the President’s question-and-answer session in the National Assembly.
The meeting was adjourned.
