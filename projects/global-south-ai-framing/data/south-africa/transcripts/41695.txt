Meeting Summary
Annual Reports 2024/25
The Portfolio Committee convened to be briefed on the 2024/25 audit outcomes of the Department of Forestry, Fisheries and the Environment (DFFE) and two of its entities, the Marine Living Resources Fund and the South African Weather Service (SAWS).
Members expressed concern about the South African Forestry Company Ltd's (SAFCOL’s) repeated failure to submit its financial statements, questioning whether the Department had received any report explaining the delay, and when the matter would be resolved. They also raised issues regarding the cyberattack on SAWS, noting the Auditor-General’s report, which contradicted earlier assurances that data had been safely backed up. They stressed the importance of receiving accurate information and ensuring that such incidents did not recur.
The Committee also discussed the challenge of municipalities awarding tenders to their own officials, with the Chairperson asking whether a system could be implemented to detect conflicts of interest in real time rather than relying on the Auditor-General’s findings. The Department responded that this would be difficult due to the use of transversal systems and inconsistent municipal platforms, adding that only National Treasury could enforce a unified system.
Members addressed skills shortages in supply chain management, noting that universities did not offer qualifications specific to the needs of government procurement officials. The Department confirmed that the National School of Government and National Treasury were engaging to close this gap.
Finally, Members commended SAWS for improving revenue and resilience after the cyberattack, but raised questions about data reliability, system recovery costs, and long-term cybersecurity measures.
Meeting report
Chairperson's overview
The Chairperson welcomed everyone to the meeting, and said that the Budgetary Review and Recommendations Report (BRRR) informed the Standing Committee on Appropriation's decisions regarding the mid-term financial allocations. In this process, the Committee was required to provide an assessment of the Department of Forestry, Fisheries and the Environment's (DFFE's) service delivery performance in relation to the resources available. The Committee was also expected to evaluate the effectiveness and efficiency of the Department’s utilisation and forward allocation of these resources and, lastly, to make recommendations concerning their future use.
She said that in preparation for the 2025 BRRR, the Committee had reviewed and deliberated upon the quarterly performance and expenditure of the Department and its entities. On the present occasion, the Committee had convened to consider the audited financial and non-financial performance reports of the Department and its entities for the 2024/25 financial year.
She said the Committee was unable to discuss the South African Forestry Company Limited’s (SAFCOL's) annual performance and expenditure report for 2024/25 because the entity had failed to submit its report by the statutory deadline of 30 September. This mirrored the situation from the previous year, when SAFCOL had also failed to submit its 2023/24 audited annual report on time, blaming its delay on operations in Mozambique and the Auditor-General (AG). No explanation had yet been provided for the current delay. However, she confirmed that the Department and its remaining entities had met the 30 September submission deadline.
Regarding performance, the Department had achieved 83% of its targets for the year under review, representing a slight decline from the 88% attained in the 2023/24 financial year. This lower performance had occurred despite a reduction in the total number of targets from 66 to 40. Over recent years, the Department has progressively reduced its annual targets, from 149 in 2021/22, to 78 in 2022/23, 66 in 2023/24, and now 40 in 2024/25. This continuous decrease, including the removal of targets central to the Department’s mandate, was a matter of concern and ought not to be normalised.
Repeated findings relating to weaknesses in financial management controls, performance reporting, compliance with key legislation, irregular expenditure and consequence management, continued to overshadow the Department’s achievements. However, she acknowledged the reduction in material irregularities.
The South African Weather Service (SAWS) had reduced its targets from 28 in the 2023/24 financial year to 12 in the year under review, achieving 75% compared with the previous 82%. The Marine Living Resources Fund (MLRF) had lowered its targets from 11 to seven, but had improved its performance from 80% to 86%. She praised the MLRF for maintaining its clean audit outcome, and emphasised that the Committee’s goal was for the Department and all its entities to attain clean audits, as these reflect transparency, credibility, and sound financial management.
Clean audits must be accompanied by tangible improvements in service delivery and environmental outcomes to enhance the lived experiences of South Africans. She expressed appreciation to the Office of the Auditor-General for its invaluable support in strengthening parliamentary oversight, both through capacity building and on-site verification activities.
The Chairperson asked for a mover and a seconder for the agenda.
Ms N Makasi (ANC) moved the adoption of the agenda, and Mr W Peach (DA) seconded.
Deputy Minister’s remarks
Mr Narend Singh, Deputy Minister of Forestry, Fisheries and Environment, expressed appreciation for the Committee’s observations regarding SAFCOL, noting that the Department shared the Committee’s concern. He informed Members that discussions were underway with SAFCOL’s management and the Chairperson of its Board to resolve the matter, and to ensure that such delays did not recur, as the current situation was unacceptable.
Regarding departmental performance, he said the Department had achieved 83% of its planned annual targets, exceeding 18 of them. This included notable progress in the finalisation of criminal dockets referred to the National Prosecuting Authority (NPA), expansion of the conservation estate, rehabilitation of wetlands, processing of waste tyres, and the planting of trees outside forested areas. Three programmes, Programme 2 (Regulatory Compliance and Monitoring), Programme 6 (Environmental Programmes), and Programme 7 (Chemicals and Waste Management), had achieved 100% of their targets.
The Department had received an unqualified audit opinion for the second consecutive year, marking an improvement in financial management and successful implementation of its audit action plan to prevent repeat findings.
On job creation, the Department had made significant progress through the Expanded Public Works Programme (EPWP), creating 34 319 full-time equivalents and 72 022 work opportunities. These initiatives had primarily targeted vulnerable groups, including women, youth, and persons with disabilities. Municipal support interventions continued to address littering, illegal dumping, and waste diversion, with eight municipalities supported during 2024/25, and 29 municipal cleaning campaigns conducted nationwide.
He added that 226 bursaries had been issued to students, 58% of which were awarded to women, reflecting the Department’s commitment to skills development and gender equity within the environmental sector. The DFFE's scientific expeditions to Marion Island, Gough Island, and Antarctica had yielded critical data for ocean governance, climate policy, and marine biodiversity protection. The publication of the Highveld Priority Area Air Quality Management Plan in March represented a major policy intervention aimed at combating industrial pollution and protecting public health.
He informed the Committee that a total of 176 659 indigenous and fruit trees had been planted during the period under review, supporting efforts to combat deforestation, restore ecosystems, and mitigate climate change.
Turning to the Marine Living Resources Fund (MLRF), Deputy Minister Singh welcomed its achievement of a clean audit for the third consecutive year, which he described as a testament to sound financial stewardship. However, he emphasised that service delivery remained as important as financial prudence. Performance highlights included the submission of the Aquaculture Development Bill to Cabinet for consideration by Parliament, the conduct of over 5 900 inspections and 600 verifications across six key fisheries -- hake, abalone, rock lobster, lionfish, squid, and pelagic species -- thereby exceeding the annual target, and the creation of 1 300 full-time equivalent jobs and 1 500 work opportunities within the fisheries sector. Although one target had not been achieved and another had been removed from the annual performance plan (APP), overall performance remained strong, with four targets exceeded and two achieved.
In respect of the SAWS, he reminded Members that its performance must be viewed in the context of a major cyberattack in January 2025, which had adversely affected its ability to meet certain targets. He highlighted SAWS’s increasingly critical role in protecting lives, property and infrastructure amid the growing frequency of climate change-induced extreme weather events. The 2024/25 financial year had been marked by several such events, including tornadoes, the first recorded in KwaZulu-Natal (KZN), as well as severe snowstorms, cut-off low-pressure systems, and heat waves affecting communities in the Eastern Cape, KZN and the Free State.
He expressed regret that many of these events had resulted in loss of life and damage to infrastructure, despite SAWS having issued 1 267 impact-based weather warnings throughout the year. He described this as evidence of SAWS’s proactive approach, and urged that the WeatherSmart campaign, which promotes public awareness, resilience and timely action, be more widely adopted across society. Early warnings could save lives only if heeded.
On governance, the January cyberattack had disrupted operations and reversed SAWS’s previous clean audit record. Nevertheless, he commended the entity’s leadership for its transparent handling of the crisis and for the significant progress made in strengthening cybersecurity resilience.
SAWS had made commendable progress in generating non-regulated revenue, nearly reaching pre-COVID-19 levels, a positive development given the ongoing need for investment in infrastructure, innovation, and human capital. He called upon the Committee to continue supporting SAWS in budgetary considerations, emphasising its vital contribution to public health, agriculture, disaster risk reduction, and the broader economy.
Deputy Minister Singh observed that the 2024/25 reporting year had been one of both resilience and achievement. The Department and its entities had demonstrated a collective commitment to transparent governance, environmental sustainability, job creation, and disaster risk reduction. He expressed his sincere appreciation to the leadership and staff of the DFFE, the MLRF and SAWS, their respective boards and accounting authorities, and the Members of the Portfolio Committee for their continued oversight and partnership.
He expressed the hope that the Committee would undertake more field visits in the forthcoming financial year to observe developments on the ground, rather than confining discussions to boardrooms. He encouraged continued collaboration in building a greener, safer, and more climate-resilient South Africa, and concluded by thanking the Committee for the opportunity to present the reports.
AGSA briefing on Committee's audit outcomes
Ms Thabelo Musisinyani, Business Unit Leader, AGSA, and Mr Nathan Lawnet, Deputy Business Unit Leader, AGSA, took the Committee through a briefing of AGSA’s outcomes for the Portfolio Committee on Forestry, Fisheries and the Environment.
The overall audit outcomes were:
The portfolio remained largely unchanged -- three audits were unqualified with no findings, three were unqualified with findings.
The DFFE and South African National Parks (SANParks) showed minor improvements in their annual financial statement (AFS) preparation and compliance, while SAWS had regressed due to a cyberattack, causing loss of performance data.
The South African National Biodiversity Institute (SANBI) had achieved an unqualified audit opinion with no findings.
Key root causes:
DFFE: No policy on implementing investigation recommendations, causing delays.
SANParks: Inadequate review of supply chain management (SCM) recommendations led to non-compliant approvals.
SAWS: Information technology (IT) vulnerabilities and a lack of backup testing exposed the organisation to data loss.
Key recommendations:
DFFE's chief financial officer (CFO) to align action plans with legislation.
DFFE and SANParks to report quarterly on investigations and include timeframes for implementation.
SANParks to train procurement committees and ensure internal audit review.
SAWS to strengthen IT governance, systems, and backup controls.
Air quality management by provincial departments
Six provinces -- the Eastern Cape (EC), Free State (FS), Gauteng (GP), KZN, North West (NW) and the Northern Cape (NC) -- had reported that some municipalities lacked air quality management plans (AQMPs), with only FS taking adequate action to ensure compliance. Three provinces (EC, FS, GP) do not have provincial AQMPs. Only four provinces (GP, KZN, LP, WC) had budgets for air quality monitoring stations, three of which were below R1 million. Vandalism, power interruptions, and poor maintenance prevented stations from meeting the required 75% data capture.
Causes and impact
The main causes are a lack of coordination across government spheres and low prioritisation of air quality management by municipalities.
These deficiencies had resulted in inaccurate measurement of air pollution, delayed or inappropriate interventions, and potential public health risks. Ineffective management of monitoring stations further undermined air quality oversight.
Recommendation
All responsible parties should be compelled to allocate budgets for air quality functions. Provinces and municipalities must develop and implement AQMPs as mandated by the Air Quality Act. Monitoring stations should be relocated to secure sites and equipped with power backup to ensure consistent data transmission.
Overall message to Portfolio Committee
Provincial departments must prioritise air quality management, ensure compliance with statutory requirements, and secure functional monitoring stations to enable accurate measurement and effective interventions.

AGSA's response
Mr Lawnet responded that with regard to the question on whether there had been a regression or improvement in overall audit outcomes between the 2023/24 and 2024/25 financial years, slide six of the presentation had illustrated the comparison clearly. When considering the distinction between unqualified and qualified audit opinions, there had been a stagnation in outcomes between the two years. However, upon closer examination of the underlying details, there was evidence of improvement. The number of non-compliance issues identified had decreased when compared to the previous year, during which material adjustments and irregular expenditures had been noted. Furthermore, issues relating to consequence management within certain entities had since been addressed. Thus, while the overall audit outcome had remained unchanged, there was clear progress in resolving material findings previously raised by the Auditor-General.
Addressing the question on the possible manipulation or misstatement of performance information, he said no such instances had been identified, based on the Auditor-General’s audit processes. No evidence of data manipulation had come to their attention. The Auditor-General maintained a constructive working relationship with both the DFFE and its entities, and if any such irregularities were to arise, they would be escalated appropriately.
In response to the question regarding whether weak leadership had contributed significantly to the audit findings within the DFFE, he said that the findings were primarily related to the accuracy and occurrence of performance objectives, and issues of consequence management, where ongoing investigations and lengthy processes were contributing factors. It could not be concluded that weak leadership was directly responsible. Rather, there was scope for improvement in the control environment and the provision of clearer guidance to prevent such findings. He added that if leadership were weak, there would likely be no evidence of remedial action or improvement, yet in this case, there was a clear focus on addressing identified issues, indicating that the Department was taking the necessary steps.
On matters of conflict of interest and procurement irregularities, Mr Lawnet explained that the Auditor-General routinely conducted checks to identify such risks. Had any material cases of conflict of interest been detected in the awarding of contracts, these would have been reported formally in the audit outcomes. While the audit process did occasionally identify instances where employees conducted external business activities, such matters were referred to the Department for internal management, and did not affect procurement conducted within the DFFE portfolio. He said no specific procurement-related conflicts of interest had been identified in the current audit cycle.
Mr Ziphezinhle Biyela, Audit Manager, AGSA, said that regarding the key programmes whose targets had not been achieved, Members could refer to the section of the report outlining performance against planned targets within the audited programmes. The report contained a summary indicating the extent of achievement across the DFFE and its entities.
For the DFFE, the indicator not achieved concerned the proposal for the establishment of the Climate Change Response Fund. The target had been to submit the proposal to Cabinet for recommendation, but the reported achievement had been that the document remained under consideration by the Minister, and was still the subject of further engagement with National Treasury.
With respect to SAWS, the entity had failed to meet its target for capacity assistance availability, achieving 68% against a target of 80%. A further indicator not achieved related to the percentage of priority area air quality monitoring stations operating on circuits that met the minimum data requirements. The target had been 80%, but only 69% had been achieved.
Turning to the MLRF, he said that the indicator related to the number of small-scale fisheries sector local management structures established had not been achieved, with the target number not being met.
He added that while the briefing note provided to Members contained additional indicators marked as not material, these had not been included in the presentation slides.
On the issue of fraud, he said that as Mr Lawnet had indicated, the audit process had not identified any such cases. Although instances of potential conflict of interest had been reviewed, none had met the threshold for escalation as fraud. Findings of non-compliance should not be construed as fraud, as they typically relate to procedural or legislative irregularities. By way of example, he referred to SANParks, where certain procurement irregularities had been noted. However, these were classified as non-compliance with legislation, rather than fraudulent conduct.
Ms Musisinyani said that in reflecting on the governance processes within the portfolio, the Auditor-General had observed a generally positive tone. The DFFE had routinely provided updates on commitments quarterly without prompting, demonstrating accountability for previous findings and the implementation of corrective action plans. The Department had engaged robustly with the AG throughout the year, including the Accounting Officer and their team, and displayed a genuine commitment to rectifying issues and implementing necessary measures.
There had been no indication of intentional manipulation of information, although errors had occasionally been identified and subsequently corrected through discussion. Regarding ongoing investigations, while progress was being made on legacy irregular expenditure matters, there remained delays between the conclusion of investigations and the implementation of recommended disciplinary measures. The Department had committed to defining timelines to ensure these processes were concluded efficiently.
No evidence of political interference had been detected. The portfolio had demonstrated a consistent willingness to address outstanding issues, engage constructively with Treasury, and implement sustainable solutions in response to the AG’s recommendations, rather than merely adopting measures to satisfy audit requirements.
On the issue of competitive bidding, she referred to SANParks, highlighting a case in which contracts had expired, and appropriate procurement processes had not been followed due to an ongoing court case. Services had continued under the expired contracts during the legal proceedings, but following the resolution of the case, SANParks had put in place the correct procurement process. This matter had been formally elevated as part of the compliance review.
Ms Makasi conveyed her appreciation to the Office of AGSA for the reports regularly submitted to the Committee, observing that they provided a clear and comprehensive account of the performance of the DFFE and its entities. The responses furnished by the Office were adequate and sufficiently clear for the Committee’s deliberations.
On the matter of political interference, she said that it was a relevant concern, given the recurring issues raised by Members of the Portfolio Committee. She proposed that it might be appropriate for the Ministers themselves to respond directly to this issue, noting that the saying “where there is smoke, there is fire” highlighted the necessity for transparency and clarity.
She commended the report presented, particularly for its inclusion of reflections on previous recommendations made to the Committee. She emphasised the importance of ongoing updates from AGSA to maintain entity accountability and to enable the Committee to discern which entities were performing effectively. She said that the overall message of the report offered valuable guidance to the Portfolio Committee.

DFFE's response
Ms Tshabalala stated that she would return to the issue of SAFCOL, noting its critical importance. She indicated that she would address other matters later, while allowing the DDGs to respond to Members' questions.
Ms Jass observed that irregular expenditure was an inherent risk within the finance function, and emphasised that the Department continuously sought to improve the control environment. According to the AG’s audit, there were no material findings identified in supply chain management (SCM) for the financial year. Any irregular expenditure incurred had arisen from legacy non-compliant contracts, primarily within the Waste Management Bureau subunit, resulting from the withdrawal of an industrial plan previously referenced by Ms Tshabalala. As these contracts had been investigated, payments on them had become classified as irregular, but no new irregular expenditure had been identified for the current financial year.
Ms Jass acknowledged that fraudulent payments occasionally arose, but mechanisms were in place to detect them and strengthen controls, including access controls. She described SCM as a high inherent risk area, and commended the AG for auditing 100% of SCM, which had allowed the Department to enhance its control environment.
Regarding procurement reforms, she highlighted measures to verify employees in government before contract awards. However, while verification was feasible in the national and provincial spheres, municipalities relied solely on AGSA-provided information. Through a proactive agreement with AGSA, the Department received regular reports, enabling timely interventions, such as identifying municipal employees conducting business with the Department and taking corrective action, including verifying resignations. These measures reflected the Department’s ongoing efforts to improve procurement integrity and prevent irregular expenditure.
Ms Mashala said that in addressing concerns raised by Members regarding disciplinary processes, she wished to provide a comprehensive response aligned with the explanation already given by the CFO. She indicated that the Department had experienced several bids that were deemed irregular, originating from as far back as the 2018/19 financial year, and that payments made under those contracts continued to constitute irregular expenditure.
The Department had reviewed all outstanding disciplinary cases over five years, and had dealt with approximately 572 officials. These cases had been addressed through various corrective measures, including capacity-building interventions, disciplinary action, coaching, and other appropriate outcomes, depending on the seriousness of each case. Owing to capacity constraints, the Department had developed a strategy to prioritise more recent cases, covering the 2022/23, 2023/24, and 2024/25 financial years, before returning to older cases to prevent a continuous backlog.

Mr Mzizi expressed appreciation for the Committee’s acknowledgement of progress in generating commercial revenue. Activity-based costing had already been completed for marine operations, and the organisation was awaiting guidance from the aviation authority.
While SAWS valued the integrity and ownership of its data, there was an increasing global shift towards broader data accessibility, which the organisation was considering within its operational framework.
Ms Dekker said that there had been one outstanding question regarding the number of beneficiaries. Approximately R6 million had been spent on training initiatives, from which 409 employees had benefited. However, the specific number of employees who had benefited from bursaries would be confirmed in writing.
Further discussion
Ms Makasi referred to the response provided on the cyberattack, and said she did not believe that the entity had misled the Committee. She was referring to an earlier discussion involving Dr L Managa (EFF), who had not been present at the meeting. She recalled that during a previous engagement, Members had enquired whether the cyberattack could have been prevented, to which the response had been both affirmative and negative. When the Committee had further enquired about the security of data backups, it was indicated that the data was solidly backed up and inaccessible to unauthorised persons. Members had taken note of such responses for future reference and comparison, and it was therefore important to ensure consistency and accuracy in all submissions to the Committee.
Her remarks were not intended as a question, but rather as a comment, noting that the Committee kept detailed records of discussions and would refer to them in subsequent meetings. She reiterated that the Auditor-General’s report had highlighted data accessibility issues, notwithstanding previous assurances that the data was securely protected, and she expressed the hope that precautionary measures recently implemented would indeed prevent a recurrence.
The Chairperson said that the Committee would review its recommendations. National Treasury would provide a briefing on the allocation of environmental funds for their intended purposes. The DFFE and its entities should continue to include progress reports on audit action implementations when presenting quarterly performance reports.
She added that the Committee would schedule a briefing to understand the process for allocating funds derived from environmental levies or fines, and how such funds were dedicated to personnel serving in the sector. Regarding SAWS, written responses would be expected by 24 October on three matters: remedial action on the technical indicator, relocation timelines of air quality monitoring stations, and information on bursary beneficiaries, both internal and external.
Committee programme
Ms Tyhileka Madubela, Committee Secretary, and Mr Nhlanhla Ginindza, Content Advisor, took the Committee through the consideration and adoption of the Committee programme.
The Committee discussed the planning and execution of forthcoming oversight visits, which had been significantly impacted by changes in the parliamentary programme and external events such as the G20 activities. The original oversight schedule had had to be adjusted due to the unavailability of departments and entities, lapsed service provider contracts, and the need for additional approvals for international travel, particularly for the proposed leg via Botswana. Logistical challenges were compounded by the absence of state-provided venues and infrastructure in key locations, including the Madikwe Game Reserve, necessitating the exploration of alternative accommodation and meeting arrangements. It was recommended that the Botswana component be cancelled to simplify travel, reduce costs, and focus on South African oversight locations, with Pilanesberg, specifically Bakubung or Kwa Maritane, identified as the preferred accommodation base to minimise travel time while maintaining cost-effectiveness.
The oversight programme would focus primarily on elephant population management, concession agreements, biodiversity economy strategies, and community benefit sharing, with site visits including meetings with provincial departments, game reserve management, concessionaires, and assessments of environmental damage. Waste management oversight at Rustenburg sites would also form part of the programme, with the provincial government assisting in stakeholder mobilisation. Committee Members emphasised the importance of witnessing conditions on the ground rather than relying solely on presentations. Engagement with communities would be confined to matters within the Committee’s mandate, particularly small-scale fisheries and ocean economy issues, with efforts made to ensure that community representatives were properly briefed and able to provide informed input.
Further discussions highlighted the impact of the parliamentary calendar on scheduling oversight activities, noting that plenary sessions, mini-plenaries, and legislative priorities placed constraints on available time. Specific dates were proposed for the debriefing on elephant management and related provincial task teams, while longer-term planning was suggested for fisheries, aquaculture, forestry, and illegal mining oversight. International travel, particularly to Brazil, faced additional logistical challenges due to accommodation shortages, high costs, and intermediary arrangements, resulting in uncertainty regarding participation and prompting the Committee to prioritise domestic oversight activities.
The Committee programme was formally adopted, with amendments to include reporting on elephant management, and it was agreed that future oversight must be carefully planned, adequately resourced, and coordinated to ensure meaningful engagement and actionable outcomes.
The meeting was adjourned.
