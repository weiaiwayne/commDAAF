Meeting Summary
Video
The Portfolio Committee held a virtual meeting with the Auditor-General of South Africa (AGSA), the Department of Communications and Digital Technologies (DCDT) and its entities, to receive briefings on their performance and financial reports for the third and fourth quarters of 2023/24.
The Committee noted the many challenges faced by the DCDT entities, and agreed that further engagements were needed, especially with the South African Post Office (SAPO) being placed under business rescue. The Committee recommended that the quarterly reports should indicate the progress entities have made in implementing the AGSA recommendations.
Members sought explanations about the underspending of R300m by the Independent Communications Authority of South Africa (ICASA); the looming strike at ICASA; the vacant positions at the DCDT; and the National Electronic Media Institute of South Africa's failure to train people. They questioned whether the Films and Publication Board was still a competent entity. Questions were also asked about whether the SAPO would be able to stand on its own feet after receiving a R3.8 billion bailout; the resolution of the South African Broadcasting Corporation's outstanding debt to Sentech; how the State Information Technology Agency would revise its targets; the merger between Sentech and Broadband Infraco; and the relationship between the AGSA and the internal audit committees of entities and departments.
Members were disappointed that the reports focused only on what had been done, but said nothing about the impact or substantive objectives of the entities.
Meeting report
AGSA briefing on DCDT quarterly performance
Ms Thabelo Musisinyani, Business Unit Leader, Auditor-General of South Africa (AGSA), said the presentation covered the Department of Communications and Digital Technologies' performance for the third and fourth quarters of the 2023/24 financial year. She and Ms Joyce Nkonyana, Senior Manager, AGSA, took the Committee through the report.
Referring to the Department's medium-term strategic framework (MTSF) targets, they said that by 2024, South Africa aimed to have 80% internet access, be a top ten cheapest country in Africa for data, and train 2.5m people focusing on the future of work learning programmes. Plans included implementing 4G coverage, establishing an artificial intelligence (AI) institute, and developing a national e-Government strategy and roadmap.
Key targets of the annual performance plan(APP) that had been achieved were the seven national days of South Africa's history being broadcast by the South African Broadcasting Corporation (SABC), 970 funded sites connected in Phase 1 to the SA Connect model, the AI Institute established, and spectrum licences issued.
Key targets not met included the implementation of the recommendations from the cost-to-communicate study, and the Broadcasting Digital Migration (BDM) targets. The South African Post Office (SAPO) had failed to achieve regulated mail delivery standards, and the Postbank did not become a retail bank.
Some root causes included misalignment of targets for the BDM project, delays in the state bank establishment, inadequate customer attraction processes, and lack of evidence for internal controls.
The AGSA's recommendations included the DCDT overseeing role players, Sentech and the Universal Service and Access Fund (USAF), tracking the set-top box (STB) installations, and careful planning for the IPS rollout.
The DCDT had reported coordinating household migration and analogue switch-off for households registered after 31 October 2021. The SABC had achieved 66% of analogue television transmitters switched off in collaboration with Sentech. The Universal Service and Access Fund (USAF) had funded 83% of Broadcasting Digital Migration (BDM) installations, with 48 962 out of 58 380 installations being funded. Sentech had exceeded the target with a 102% balance of STBs installed.
Key audit observations and root causes were that the annual target changes were not improving the outcomes, the STB installations were not advancing for spectrum release, and there was no alignment between role players. The delay in implementing the BDM project could lead to potential international non-compliance with the International Telecommunication Union’s (ITU's) deadline, hindering the introduction of newer technologies and services. This could result in limited internet access for citizens, impacting health, education, and the overall realisation of the National Development Plan's (NDPs) vision by 2030.
Financial stability of SAPO, Postbank, SABC and Broadband Infraco (BBI)
The South African Post Office (SAPO) was in a dire financial position and had been placed under business rescue. The root cause was due to the lack of implementation of turnaround strategies and instability in leadership.
The Postbank was currently financially stable. However, the bank’s reliance on outdated and fragmented technology systems increased its vulnerability to cyber threats, and compromised its ability to respond effectively.
The SABC had completed its three-year turnaround plan, but its financial position had not improved, while the bailout funds had been almost fully utilised. The root cause was that the turnaround strategies were not output-driven to deliver financial results.
Broadband Infraco (BBI) had shown signs of revenue growth, but though the revenue had grown, the operating loss had widened due to high operating expenses. In other words, the revenue generation was not sufficient to cover operating costs.
The presentation also set out the material irregularity process. The SAPO's grant payment system had internal weaknesses. As far as the Postbank was concerned, the South African Social Security Agency (SASSA) cards were stolen and used for fraudulent payments, resulting in the cards being written off. The security contract at the SABC did not follow the procurement process, and rent was being paid for unoccupied spaces. At the State Information Technology Agency (SITA), the payment made for excess licences was not utilised, and payment was made for an event that did not occur.
There was inadequate procurement at the SAPO, Postbank and the BBI. Consequence management was identified at the DCDT, the Universal Service and Access Agency of South Africa (USAASA), the Independent Communications Authority of South Africa (ICASA), SITA, SABC, SAPO, Postbank, BBI and USAF. There was inadequate revenue management at the SAPO, Postbank and the BBI. There was inadequate oversight and governance at the SABC and BBI. There was also inadequate strategic planning and performance management at the SAPO and Postbank.
The AGSA said that there was a stagnation in the overall audit outcomes of the portfolio.

There was a peer review and peer comparison of performance across the portfolio. The DCDT hoped that the peer review mechanisms would assist in showcasing the best-performing entities so that lessons could be learned from them. This would assist those entities that were not performing or were having difficulties. The targets must be smart -- simple, measurable and realistic.
All types of feedback were given to entities. There had been interaction with ICASA. There was the development of the performance management system framework which was submitted to the Sixth Administration of Parliament. According to the law, this framework must be endorsed by the National Assembly. There were performance agreements, and a panel must be put together to consider these agreements. It would be looking at the targets individually and collectively. The panel was required to be appointed by the Minister in consultation with the National Assembly. The Minister had now been put in a position to submit the proposed names for the panel to the National Assembly, which the National Assembly would delegate to the Committee to assess before final approval. Once approved, the panel would assess the performance in the previous financial years of ICASA. The performance agreements for the current financial year were in the process of finalisation. This was a circular process that would also be considered by the evaluation panel once approved by the National Assembly.
Ms Jordan-Dyani said that the Department was currently busy with the R3.8 billion for SAPO. There was a meeting scheduled for tomorrow with the National Treasury. The DCDT had been meeting with the SAPO to ensure that the plan responded to the issues that the Minister of Finance had raised. The matter would be tabled tomorrow with the National Treasury. The DCDT was hoping that the R3.8 billion would be considered favourably. There had been some unforeseeable and unavoidable events. Both the DCDT and the SAPO would be making an appearance on this matter. The DCDT had met with the SAPO the day before to finalise what the R3.8 billion would look like. The DCDT would have more details by next Tuesday.
She addressed the issue of the accounting authorities, and who had to take full responsibility. There was an integrated audit action plan that was assessed by the CEO Forum to ensure implementation and progress. A discussion was held last year to start looking at the material irregularities. Some matters affect the audit reporting against the portfolio. Several of the entities were being tracked, such as the SAPO, SITA and the Postbank. The matters were tracked to see if they were resolved or unresolved. The DCDT was happy with some of the unqualified audit opinions, but matters still affected the audit reporting.
She said that matters were being looked at quarterly. At a high-level, there was a chairperson and ministers' forum to check whether there was implementation. The merger of Sentech and the BBI was still underway. There was a delay due to BBI’s financial viability, which has since been improved. Sentech had now been able to do due diligence. The merger would now proceed, and the DCDT would look at all the revisions, prescriptions, and relevant acts.
The ECA was an amendment about the dissolution of USAASA. There had been progress, and it was going through the Cabinet process. It would soon come to the Committee, and the necessary public consultation would be done.
Deputy Ministers closing remarks
Deputy Minister Gungubele said that it was not easy to know whether an entity was moving forward or not. This was especially so if one did not have a sense of where a number of the entities were coming from. For example, the SABC had received a disclaimer audit opinion, but he had been advised that the SABC was now at an unqualified level and that their targets had improved. Despite the revenue challenges, the DCDT’s assessment was that the SABC was stabilising and no longer sliding down. Despite its technical insolvency, the oversight unit had indicated that the entity was no longer going down. The SABC had tabled a turnaround strategy that it was implementing. The entity continued to have challenges, but not in a negative direction.
SITA had challenges, but the targets had improved. The BBI was not where it was, and the report showed that there was a positive balance. The BBI was not where it was a couple of months ago. There was some joint work being done by the BBI and Sentech to connect people in poor communities. He suggested that a site visit could be done to some of these entities if there was another opportunity.
There was correspondence being sent to the Director-General and the oversight unit to ensure that written/supplementary answers were submitted no later than Thursday.
Due to the time constraints, SAPO could not give the Committee a lot of important information. Regarding the board matter, there was legal pursuit of the matter. Deputy Minister Gungubele said the Minister felt that it was no longer wise to pursue a legal war in this matter. The parties had to find each other in that regard. The legal team would be looking at the implications of the view of not pursuing the matter. The latest update in that regard was that the Minister would have opted that the three members who had initiated the court proceeding should come back and be part of the board. The Minister had advised that the former chair would have to take over from the current chair. He said that the DCDT would have another opportunity to update the Committee on this matter.
The Chairperson allowed Ms Mathys an opportunity to speak.
Ms Mathys asked that when the DCDT and the entities respond to the Committee in writing, they should include what consequence management was being taken when targets were not met. She acknowledged what had been told, but this was not "the first rodeo." There could not be people earning salaries every month and year, and there were no consequences when targets were not being met.
Chairperson's closing remarks
The Chairperson said the Committee would be going through the recording of the meeting to identify which questions had not been responded to. These questions would be sent to the DCDT, with a return date of no later than Friday. The adoption of these quarterly reports was on the agenda for next week. The Committee could not adopt these reports if some pertinent questions were outstanding. She recommended that the strategic plans include the issues raised here. The Committee did not want to come back again and lament these issues. The targets should not be too quantitative, but should also speak to the impact and outcomes. The Committee could not be expected to rubber-stamp the strategic plans. If the DCDT wished to engage with the Committee in this regard, it could do so. The quarterly reports should reflect how the AGSA’s recommendations were going to be implemented, including corrective measures and consequence management.
This was the Committee’s first virtual meeting, and there should not be many of them. However, virtual meetings had some benefits, such as greater participation from the public.
Committee minutes
The Committee adopted the minutes of 23 July, 20 August and 27 August 2024.
The Chairperson said that in the minutes of 3 September, the statement that the "presentation was available on request" may be removed. This would not take away any of the details that had been discussed. She also said that the minutes should include the Committee’s concerns about the quality of the KPMG reports.
She thanked everyone again for attending the meeting. She appreciated the diligent indulgence of the Members for the meeting that went over time. There would be a non-statutory meeting to discuss the Committee’s programme going forward.
The meeting was adjourned.
