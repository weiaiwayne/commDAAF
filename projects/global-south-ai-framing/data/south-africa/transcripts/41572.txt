Meeting Summary
Video
The Standing Committee on Public Accounts (SCOPA) met to discuss its oversight priorities with the Auditor-General of South Africa (AGSA). The Committee agreed that its focus should be on poorly performing entities such as water boards, municipalities, Sector Education and Training Authorities (SETAs), the Passenger Rail Agency of South Africa (PRASA), Transnet, Eskom, South African Airways, the Post Office, and key departments such as Health, Education, Defence, and Social Development. Members highlighted the need to strengthen oversight by addressing delayed audit submissions, improving collaboration with other oversight bodies, and ensuring credible research-informed citizen-focused decision-making. Concerns were also raised about governance in state security structures, the public procurement system, and accountability for medical-legal claims and social grants.
The AGSA reported that while clean audits had increased in the Seventh Administration, major departments and state-owned entities continued to struggle with financial management, accountability, and audit outcomes. Late submissions remained a serious problem, undermining transparency and raising borrowing costs for municipalities. AGSA highlighted systemic failures in infrastructure delivery, including delays, poor-quality work, and unutilised facilities due to staffing shortages. The Auditor-General urged a stronger focus on leadership, financial discipline, and consequence management, while noting that reliance on consultants, weak internal audits, and recycling of senior officials were persistent challenges.
The Committee engaged on issues of unqualified audits with findings, penalties for late submissions, and the link between audit outcomes and service delivery. Members also questioned the role of private auditors, professional bodies, and the use of artificial intelligence in strengthening audits. Concerns were raised about Eskom’s tender practices, consultant misuse, and systemic failures in municipalities. AGSA committed to improving transparency, strengthening enforcement of material irregularities, and enhancing the use of data-driven oversight.
The Committee further considered and adopted reports on unauthorised expenditure from various departments, with recommendations that most of the overspending be offset against future allocations, except in cases such as emergency social grants during COVID-19, which were to be charged to the National Revenue Fund. The meeting concluded with a discussion on an international study tour, with most members supporting the United Kingdom as the preferred destination due to its similar parliamentary oversight model.
Meeting report
Prior to welcoming the Auditor-General of South Africa (AGSA), the Standing Committee engaged in discussion on what questions would be posed to the AGSA.
The Chairperson urged the Committee to focus on areas of the worst performing Water Boards, district municipalities, worst performing Sector Education and Training Authorities (SETAs), the Passenger Rail Agency of SA (PRASA), such as its train refurbishment, the Road Traffic Management Corporation (RTMC), Transnet, South African Airways (SAA), Basic Education and its scholar transport and national nutrition programme, defence portfolio which was the worst-performing, Post Office and Post Bank, medical legal claims, the SA Social Security Agency (SASSA), the Department of Public Service and Administration (DPSA) and its vetting of officials, Government Pension Agency (GPA), etc.
Members all agreed that those were the areas that they needed to focus on.
He suggested that the Committee also discuss with the Minister of Finance the public electronic tender system. He recalled a system in Canada in which three unrelated groups evaluated entities in the bidding process to prevent collusion, which he thought was a good idea.
He also suggested that the Committee have a working relationship with the SIU to get regular updates on the investigations related to DPSA and the Department of Cooperative Governance and Traditional Affairs (COGTA) before it received outdated reports.
The Committee emphasised that getting credible research data to reach citizens' information was difficult, to understand citizen satisfaction with government policies and decisions. The Committee collaborate with research and academic institutions to address this.
He further highlighted the need for the Committee to review the financial governance issues in intelligence structures.
Mr F Essack (DA) commented that state security seemed to be a closed book. He proposed that the assistance of the Chief Whips’ Forum be sought to help the Committee move this matter forward.
The Chairperson reminded him that the Committee had agreed that it would first get legal advice from the Parliamentary Legal Advisor before taking further steps.
Although he understood the sensitivity issues regarding the State Security Agency (SSA), he was also aware that a brief outline of the budget should be made available to SCOPA, similar to the one that the AGSA had often done without revealing detailed information.
AGSA on key focus areas and priority issues that the Committee should consider in its oversight work, update on the AGSA’s role in the audit of Eskom Holdings SOC Ltd and its subsidiaries
Ms Tsakane Maluleke, Auditor-General of South Africa, led the presentation on the key focus areas and priority issues that the Committee should consider in its oversight work.
The AG's Office comprised of 4 000 people, including over 1,500 professional auditors. The AG's Office issued 1 000 audit reports annually covering all public institutions. In addition, the Office had contributed an additional 300 new Chartered Accountants (CAs) to the market, having been trained by the National Audit Office, adding to its already 2 000 plus CAs that had been trained through the same Office over the years.
She encouraged SCOPA members to utilise AGSA’s audit reports and engage with civil society organisations for better oversight. She also encouraged better communication and coordination with other portfolio committees.
AGSA tabled its 2024 General Report for Public Finance Management Act. The Office’s analysis showed progress in that in the Seventh Administration, there were 144 clean audits, a significant increase from 93 at the end of the previous administration in 2019. Ms Maluleke disputed the narrative that some officials had been uttering that getting unqualified audit opinions, unqualified reports with findings indicated issues in governance, controls, and compliance despite correct financial statements. She reiterated that an unqualified audit was not bad but indicated that there were some issues to be addressed.
She urged the Committee to pay attention to late auditees. There were 38 late audits identified during the analysis, with ongoing issues noted for Transnet and Eskom.
She pointed out that there was a tendency to tolerate late audits among state-owned entities which significantly impacted accountability processes. Late audits prevented proper accountability and performance discussions, leading to a financial year lag in reporting.
The AG also recalled her latest conversations with some Johannesburg Stock Exchange (JSE) listed companies, which expressed similar concerns on late auditees.
The Chairperson interjected and enquired about the listed instruments and why they mattered in this instance.
Ms Maluleke explained that municipalities, especially metros, could raise long-term debt from capital markets through instruments listed and regulated by the JSE. To do this, they had to comply with strict financial regulations, including audited statements and consistent financial discipline. Poor financial performance or failure to comply led to higher borrowing costs, as lenders viewed them as risky. Research showed a clear link between poor audit outcomes and expensive debt in local government, which ultimately raised costs for citizens. Cape Town was highlighted as a metro that has successfully raised funding from capital markets, unlike Johannesburg, suggesting differences in financial discipline and performance. A lack of sound financial management discipline among state entities led to poor audit outcomes and service delivery issues.
She recommended that the Committee focus its oversight on the departments and entities with big budgets, big service delivery requirements and mandates, and the ones with the highest risk to the fiscus.
She expressed AGSA’s concern that big state-owned entities and departments hardly get clean audits. Only the Development Bank of Southern Africa (DBSA) received clean audits among state-owned entities, while major departments like health and education struggled with audit outcomes. Others, like PRASA, sat between disclaimers and qualified opinions for many years. Furthermore, she indicated that many government departments had uncompleted audits, indicating a lack of management stability and competence.
She emphasised the importance of getting senior managers, the executive authority, the internal audit, and the audit committees to focus on controls and disciplines to ensure transparency, accountability, good performance and financial management.
She indicated to the Committee that AGSA had a specific audit office that focused on infrastructure, looking at what the problems were in dealing with new projects, ensuring that they were delivered on time at the right quality, at the right price, etc. Poor procurement practices often led to the selection of inadequate suppliers, affecting project quality. Far too many departments of infrastructure in metros and State-Owned Enterprises (SOEs) did not have the capacity to monitor the implementation of projects properly.
86% of inspected projects had problems, including delays averaging 34 months and poor-quality work. Completed infrastructure projects, like clinics and neonatal intensive care units (ICUs), were not being utilised due to staffing issues. Systemic issues in planning and budgeting led to the underutilisation of healthcare facilities. When the auditors tried to engage with the accounting officers on material irregularities (MIs), the responses were usually about the lack of funding to recruit the personnel to staff those facilities. Repeated instances of unutilised infrastructure diminished public trust in healthcare services.

Discussion
Following Ms Maluleke’s input, the Chairperson remarked that the lack of consumables and budget for necessary medical equipment caused operational challenges. Budgeting assumptions in healthcare often failed, leading to situations where contracts were in place but funding for critical staff, like specialist nurses, was denied. This left healthcare workers in distress, unable to provide necessary care, and at times resulted in patients’ conditions worsening, or even death.
He inquired if there were any formal procedures to ensure that all smaller items were budgeted for before procurement.
Ms Maluleke concurred and emphasised the need for these disciplines to be clarified and enforced. Standard operating procedures ought to be clear about at what point different types of expenditure are approved. For instance, she was aware that in the Free State there was a completed hospital but had not yet been used. Her Office had flagged the same issue to the relevant accounting officers for three consecutive years, but they hardly received satisfactory responses.
She indicated to the Committee that as AGSA, they had put together a multi-year audit plan and identified particular themes to focus on at different periods.
She reported to the Committee about a situation at a district municipality in the Eastern Cape. They had opted to hire consultants instead of employing staff for basic tasks as fruitless and wasteful expenditure. Despite having communicated with the municipality a few times, the latter did not respond positively. Noticing that the municipality did not oblige, the AG referred the matter to the SIU. In response, that municipality had now taken the AGSA to court for a review of its decisions.
She assured the Committee that the AGSA checked the reasons for the hiring of consultants during audits and would in certain instances flag the use of consultants as fruitless and wasteful expenditures.
Mr A Beesley (Action SA) commended AGSA’s important work in ensuring accountability in public governance. However, he noted that AG seemed hesitant to issue certificates of debt. What caused this?
He noted Ms Maluleke’s input on unqualified audit opinions with findings and agreed that no one should be dancing around when they got such. The narrative had to be changed and everyone in the public sector had to know that such opinions were not good enough.
He noted that there was no information around Ithala Development Finance Corporation Ltd (IDFC) but was aware of the irregularities that had occurred in that entity.
Ms Maluleke confirmed that they were aware of the issues at Ithala and were closely monitoring the matter. It was not listed as an SOE because it was a provincial entity rather than a listed entity in terms of Schedule 2.
She commented on unqualified audit outcomes with opinions. It was fine to have debits and credits done right, but those opinions indicated that there were some significant management issues. Her Office was working hard to ensure that everyone understood the importance of getting a clean audit.
She explained that in terms of standard procedures, issuing certificates of debt was the last resort. It was not that they were reluctant to issue out certificates of debt.
Ms H Neale-May (ANC) asked if the AG was piloting or planning the use of artificial intelligence (AI) or machine learning to detect patterns of corruption, fraud or mismanagement that might not be visible during traditional audits. Could AI tools help identify red flag indicators such as inflated pricing, duplicate invoices or conflicts of interest in procurement systems?
She asked how they planned to future-proof audit processes against increasingly sophisticated fraud schemes that used technology to cover tricks. What role could Parliament and SCOPA play in supporting AGSA to scale up data-driven and AI-enabled auditing?
On ESKOM, what risks did AGSA foresee in terms of audit readiness, financial reporting and accountability at the entity?
Could the planned restructure create blind spots or loopholes that weaken the audit assurance? Given the complexity and high stakes of this restructuring, would it not be prudent under the circumstances to consider a control deviation, keeping the current auditors in place temporarily to ensure continuity, prevent audit gaps and maintain the financial report's integrity?
Ms Maluleke responded that the Office had started to use some of the AI tools. Internally, it had set up a data academy and had data analysts who worked alongside the audit team. For instance, in Social Development, the Office was highlighting gaps around people getting grants when they should not be and highlighting instances of fraud. Overall, she expressed enthusiasm for the use of AI.
She urged the Committee to help them ensure that audit fees were collected to support future investments in technology and skills. Currently, AGSA has over R1 billion of debt owed to it and generated just under R5 billion in audit fees. There was a continued lack of discipline in paying audit fees, especially among municipalities and state-owned entities.
She noted the concerns around the issues at Eskom. She expressed her own concern regarding stability in governance arrangements amidst changing executives. She also pointed to regulatory complexities with independent private power providers (IPPs).
The Chairperson indicated that the Committee had had discussions about devising a mechanism where part of the budgeting process involved transferring directly to the AG the outstanding audit fees.
Ms T Bila (ANC) said that the Committee should follow up on the commitments made to it by the different departments.
She agreed with previous speakers that progressing to an unqualified audit opinion was not something that should be celebrated and suggested a more meaningful conversation to be had with officials on the meaning of the different opinions.
Mr D Skosana (MKP) noted a pattern that it seemed difficult for municipalities to move from unqualified with findings to clean audits, indicating a systemic issue. Was there any reason for this?
He asked if government departments could maintain unqualified status without annual assistance from the AGSA. He questioned the effectiveness of internal audit committees in those departments and entities as they were expected to assist auditees daily.
He enquired about the role of private auditors that audited public entities, raising questions about checks and balances in the auditing process. AGSA was held accountable by Parliament, but the same accountability model did not apply to private auditing firms. He sought the AG’s comment on that.
Ms Maluleke attributed departments and entities staying in the yellow zone to complacency. She highlighted the government’s error in not ensuring this clear message got sent through years ago that a clean audit was important.
She acknowledged the regression in audit outcomes once those entities operated independently without the assistance of the AG. She used the eight big metros as an example. Only three out of the eight had the ability to prepare quality financial statements regularly. For those three metros, they even did an interim audit to go and check on the controls before the AG started the final audit and were often able to confirm that those controls were still in place. The other five heavily relied on the audit process to at least get into unqualified, and many still ended up with qualified audit opinions. Even the City of Johannesburg could not produce credible financial statements.
She observed that those entities’ internal audits were not functioning properly due to lack of resources and oversight. To address that, she suggested that internal audit committees be invited to future hearings, so they provide feedback on audit processes.
She responded to Mr Skosana that the Independent Regulatory Board of Auditors (IRBA) monitored and regulated all auditors annually. Every year, they took a sample of audit files conducted by the private sector firms and checked them for quality. They then issued a report to a firm directly to provide feedback on audit quality, and then compiled a consolidated report. For the AG, the consolidated report gives her Office a good view on how the private sector was doing on audit quality. She said they still insisted on getting the individual firm’s report to confirm their standards of quality management. She welcomed the Committee raising concerns about specific audits to ensure accountability.
Ms C Nontenja (UDM) asked whether there were any penalties for late or non-submission of reports. She noted that some entities fell behind, and in the absence of consequences, there seemed to be complacency.
She further raised concerns about the extensive use of consultants, highlighting that millions of rands were spent on them, yet the outcomes often did not justify the cost. In her view, whether the work was done internally or by consultants, the results were still unsatisfactory. She therefore asked what measures should be taken to regulate or limit the use of consultants, questioning the value of spending millions on reports and statements that did not achieve the intended results.
On the issue of penalties for late or non-submissions, Ms Maluleke indicated that they had begun issuing MIs, which had led to improvements in provinces such as the Free State and the North West, particularly at local government level. In addition to issuing MIs, AGSA raised the matter sharply with provincial treasuries, offices of the premiers, and provincial COGTA departments to emphasise their responsibilities.
She explained that while the threat of penalties (via MIs) had an impact, greater success was achieved by ensuring that institutions understood the importance of timely submission. It was further noted that political leadership played a crucial role. As an example, the Free State Premier at the time took a direct role by calling mayors and municipal managers to his Office and requiring them to submit financials in his presence. This intervention improved compliance. However, with a change in leadership the following year, that practice was not repeated and submissions declined again.
Regarding the use of consultants, Ms Maluleke reported that they had also made use of MIs and other enforcement powers to compel accounting officers to act. In many cases, this resulted in contracts being cancelled, funds recovered where overpayments had been made, and permanent staff being appointed to perform the work internally. In other cases, entities resisted and resorted to court action. Nonetheless, AGSA continued to use its enforcement powers to address the misuse of consultants.
Mr T Kubheka (MKP) commented on the joint oversight with COGTA and SCOPA on municipalities, noting that there had been significant resistance from mayors and municipal managers in defending their positions. A recurring issue was the defence of unfunded budgets, where municipalities initially presented budgets as funded, but upon closer examination, they were revealed to be unfunded.
He commended the AGSA’s information and analysis, stating that it was accurate “99 per cent of the time” and left municipalities with little room to evade accountability. He also appreciated the concise and precise notes provided by the AGSA technical team during oversight visits, which were described as “spectacular, straight to the point, and very short,” and therefore very helpful given the time constraints faced by Members.
He raised a concern about the recycling of senior municipal officials, such as Chief Financial Officers (CFOs), Municipal Managers, Directors of Corporate Services, and Directors of Technical Services. It was noted that individuals implicated in corruption or maladministration in one municipality were often moved or seconded to another, sometimes with the involvement of municipal councils or even MECs of COGTA.
He expressed concern that this practice undermined accountability, as the same officials implicated in failures were repeatedly placed in positions of responsibility. He therefore asked what the AGSA’s view or comment was on having to audit municipalities under the leadership of individuals already known or alleged to be corrupt, and how such systemic failures should be addressed.
The Chairperson asked AGSA how it dealt with entities or departments whose CFOs AG had previously dealt with and was found to have not been good.
Ms Maluleke acknowledged the difficulty of dealing with municipal officials who might have allegations of corruption or maladministration against them. It was explained that, in terms of the audit process, the AGSA was required to engage professionally with the accounting officer or CFO in office at the time, and to rely on the representation letters signed by these officials confirming the accuracy of information provided.
She emphasised that they could not prejudge matters, as many allegations remained uninvestigated and without consequences. This limited AGSA’s ability to act beyond the audit process. However, it was noted that in at least one province, AGSA teams were collating information on officials who were repeatedly recycled into senior positions despite prior concerns. This information was intended to be shared with oversight bodies, highlighting individuals of concern and the risks posed to municipalities.
AGSA stressed that while it could not dismiss or prosecute officials, it could flag such patterns with oversight structures to strengthen accountability. Ultimately, AGSA remained limited to auditing “whoever was in the seat” at the time of the audit.
Mr K Madlala (MKP) raised the issue of accountability for CFOs registered with the South African Institute of Chartered Accountants (SAICA). The Committee had recently met with that body and asked about its role in ensuring that members who repeatedly failed to deliver were held accountable. While SAICA had indicated that not all CFOs were members, he questioned why there was no system or database to “red flag” CFOs who consistently received disclaimers, especially in the public sector. In his view, such individuals demonstrated an inability to implement the Municipal Finance Management Act (MFMA) or PFMA and should not be reappointed. This was linked to a broader concern about the lack of consequence management and accountability in municipalities.
He stressed that many of the governance failures were fundamentally leadership and corruption problems. Examples were given from oversight visits where completed projects remained unused, and where poor coordination between departments led to situations such as houses being built without water infrastructure. This reflected both a lack of strategic leadership and mismanagement of public resources.
He further asked for AGSA’s view on the relationship between audit outcomes and service delivery. While audit improvements were welcomed, he cautioned that they should not come at the expense of delivery. Oversight visits in North West and Gauteng revealed under-expenditure on infrastructure grants, raising concerns that some municipalities achieved better audit outcomes by withholding expenditure rather than delivering services. He argued for a balance between compliance and implementation so that improvements in audit outcomes translated to tangible benefits for communities.
He also welcomed AGSA’s work on improving the use of data to inform implementation, noting that systemic weaknesses in government extended beyond municipalities. For example, the National Student Financial Aid Scheme (NSFAS) beneficiaries often leave university without any monitoring of employment outcomes or repayment of loans, undermining the sustainability of the funding system. He suggested that AGSA could play a role in highlighting such systemic issues to strengthen accountability and governance.
On the issue of data and policy, Ms Maluleke clarified that they could not pronounce on policy but could provide credible and independent insights to highlight pitfalls. This enabled decision-makers to take informed decisions.
In terms of the correlation between audit outcomes, service delivery, and the use of grants, she explained that clean audits reflected institutions with strong controls, a clear leadership tone, and well-established systems. An analysis of grant expenditure showed that institutions with clean audits tended to use their grants properly, while those with poor audit outcomes often misused funds. Examples of the latter included paying salaries under unfunded budgets, settling old accruals, or overpaying contractors who failed to deliver.
She stressed that a clean audit was the minimum standard that should be expected, as it enabled a real conversation about mandates and performance planning. While isolated service delivery issues might still occur in clean audit environments, the broader pattern showed that well-governed institutions had both the capability and discipline to deliver. By contrast, auditees with poor audit outcomes consistently displayed weak spending and delivery capacity.
It was noted, however, that improvements in audit outcomes were most visible in smaller departments and oversight bodies, while the larger, high-impact auditees with significant budgets and service delivery mandates showed less improvement. This limited the extent to which improvements in audit outcomes translated into visible improvements in service delivery for communities.
On the role of professional bodies such as SAICA, she emphasised that they should be pressured to enforce disciplinary processes where failures occurred. Nonetheless, professional qualifications alone would not address the problem. Where the political and administrative leadership tone was poor, even qualified professionals faced significant obstacles, often leading to high turnover among CAs in such environments.
She shared reflections from internal roadshows with staff, noting that many professionals would only accept senior leadership roles (e.g. as a Head of Department) if there was confidence that the executive authority would support efforts to clean up governance. This underscored the centrality of political alignment and leadership commitment in driving sustainable improvements.
Ms V Mente-Nkuna (EFF) expressed appreciation to the AG, noting that in a context of widespread dishonesty and institutional decline, the AG’s integrity and professionalism provided Parliament with a reliable basis for its oversight work. She further commended the inclusion of women in audit teams despite the risks and intimidation auditors often faced at municipal level.
She raised concern regarding the lack of financial management capacity in major metros such as Johannesburg, where the AG had reported an inability to produce credible financial statements. It was emphasised that South Africa had sufficient accounting professionals, and that such failures were the result of mismanagement rather than capacity shortages. She argued that municipalities could not claim to budget effectively or deliver services without first producing sound financial statements.
She criticised the notion that achieving a clean audit was a “special achievement,” arguing that it should be the norm. The gap between the AG’s findings and parliamentary or executive action was identified as a key contributor to ongoing governance failures. She highlighted the importance of expanding the real-time audit initiative, which proved effective during COVID-19 and the KwaZulu-Natal disaster, but warned that without sufficient budget allocations, this tool would remain underutilised.
On the handling of MIs, she cited the example of Johannesburg, where several previously reported MIs had been reduced to a single outstanding matter, with the remainder dismissed without proper investigation. She observed that municipalities often treat the process as a “tick-box exercise,” attributing irregularities to accruals with entities such as Eskom or Rand Water, instead of investigating budgeting failures that led to unpaid invoices.
She further raised concerns regarding Eskom tender practices. It was noted that certain contracts above R50 million stipulated payment terms of 60 days instead of the standard 30. This practice was described as exclusionary, as only large firms were able to absorb two months of salary and operating costs in advance, thereby disadvantaging smaller contractors and undermining economic transformation. She requested clarity on whether this exemption had a legal basis and what role the AG's Office could play in addressing it.
Finally, she referred to Deloitte, noting that while the firm had faced legal consequences for shortcomings in one service area, it continued to conduct other business with the state, including auditing. She questioned the fairness of this arrangement, pointing out that officials were dismissed for errors, whereas private firms were allowed to continue operating despite serious failures. She urged greater consistency in accountability between public officials and private contractors.
Ms Maluleke noted that the AGSA continued to strengthen its systems to ensure that oversight bodies were equipped with timely and substantive information on how MIs were being dealt with. This would prevent situations where irregularities appeared to be resolved before Parliament had had sight of them.
It was acknowledged that, while specific instances of irregularity might be resolved, systemic problems often remained. An example was given of health facilities where wards had been built but left unused; once such wards were opened, the MI was deemed “resolved,” although the underlying systemic failures persisted. Ms Maluleke emphasised the need to move beyond a “tick-box” approach by municipalities and to insist that root causes were addressed.
The authority to write off irregular expenditure rested with councils, and AGSA could not override such decisions. However, the Office undertook to improve transparency by ensuring that Parliament was alerted to the underlying issues when irregular expenditure was written off.
She drew attention to challenges created by the irregular expenditure framework. Current provisions allowed auditees to classify certain matters as “irregular expenditure under assessment,” which should be disclosed in the annual report but not in the annual financial statements. As a result, oversight committees might not see the full picture. AGSA committed to providing detailed briefings on such cases to assist Parliament in holding accounting officers accountable.
Regarding Eskom, it was explained that the company’s decision to extend payment terms on certain large tenders from 30 to 60 days was a policy decision made by its management and board. She indicated that this was not an audit matter, but rather a policy issue requiring accountability through parliamentary engagement. Concerns about exclusion of smaller contractors were acknowledged as legitimate.
On Deloitte, Ms Maluleke explained that the firm had previously faced sanctions relating to its consulting business at Eskom but had since paid fines and resolved those matters. Deloitte currently serves as Eskom's external auditor, with its audit quality confirmed by the IRBA. She stressed that South Africa’s audit profession relied heavily on a small pool of large firms, making it important to monitor their quality and ethics while recognising the need for their continued participation in the public sector. It was noted that other firms, such as KPMG and PwC, had also undergone remediation following past failures.
She highlighted that the approach should be consistent: problems should be identified, consequences enforced, and demonstrable corrective action taken. However, it was not considered helpful to exclude entire firms from the audit environment given capacity constraints in the profession.
Consideration and adoption of unauthorised expenditure reports of departments
The Chairperson indicated that there were unauthorised expenditures which the Sixth Parliament SCOPA had approved, which this Committee needed to effect. The National Treasury had to brief the Committee on how those expenditures were to be treated. However, in the Sixth Parliament, that report had been prepared but was never ATC’d. The National Assembly did not process, nor vote on the report. National Treasury now wanted to implement the decisions made by the previous Committee.
He indicated the time pressure to approve decisions by Friday for the October budget. The Committee had to approve the decisions made by the Sixth Parliament’s SCOPA before the budget changes could be made. Treasury should present again to the Committee to express an opinion on outstanding unauthorised expenditures. The unauthorised expenditure totalled R1.1 to R3 million, with specific amounts allocated to different programmes.
He highlighted the change of s34(2) which was a typo.
Case 1: Department of Cooperative Governance (2006/07 financial year)
The Committee considered unauthorised expenditure totalling R1.123 million, consisting of R430 000 under Programme 1 and R693 000 under Programme 7. The over-expenditure was incurred on travel and subsistence relating to the Commission on Traditional Leadership, for which no budget provision had been made.
Having considered the matter, the Committee recommended that the total amount of R1.123 million be financed as a reduction in the Department’s future allocation, in accordance with section 34(2) of the PFMA. Members concurred that this was an appropriate decision under the circumstances.
Case 2: Department of Communications and Digital Technologies (2013/14 financial year)
The Committee considered unauthorised expenditure of R4.74 million. The expenditure arose due to a 21 per cent depreciation of the rand against the US dollar between March 2012 and March 2014, which resulted in higher-than-budgeted membership fees to international organisations, payable in US dollars.
The Committee recommended that the full amount of R4.74 million be financed as a reduction in the Department’s future allocation, in terms of section 34(1)(a) of the PFMA. The Committee affirmed that this was a sound decision in line with legislative requirements.
Case 3: Department of Women, Youth and Persons with Disabilities
The Committee considered the unauthorised expenditure of the Department of Women, Youth and Persons with Disabilities, which incurred R27 million in 2011/12, largely on compensation of employees (R11.4 million) and goods and services (R12.4 million). This overspending arose during the transition of the Department from the Presidency to a standalone ministry. Further unauthorised expenditure was incurred in 2015/16, amounting to R2.2 million, and in 2019/20, amounting to R3.2 million. Having deliberated on these matters, the Committee recommended that the expenditure be financed as a reduction of the Department’s future budget allocations, in line with section 34(1)(2) of the PFMA.
Case 4: Department of Justice and Correctional Services
The Committee also considered the unauthorised expenditure of the Department of Justice and Correctional Services, which recorded overspending of R121 000 on employee-related costs. The Committee found that this could have been avoided with proper financial management, as the compensation budget had not been fully spent. The Committee therefore recommended that the amount be financed as a reduction in future allocations.
Case 5: Department of Social Development
With respect to the Department of Social Development, the Committee considered the significant unauthorised expenditure of R15.134 billion incurred during the COVID-19 pandemic. This amount arose from emergency social grant payments which could not await parliamentary appropriation processes. The Committee noted that this expenditure did not arise from negligence or poor planning, but from an emergency response to the crisis. It therefore recommended that the expenditure be charged to the National Revenue Fund.
Case 6: Statistics South Africa
The Committee considered the unauthorised expenditure recorded by Statistics South Africa, which amounted to R37.8 million, R56.7 million and R47.6 million across successive financial years. These amounts arose primarily due to budget reductions in compensation of employees, leading to overspending at the economic classification level. The Committee recommended that part of this expenditure be financed as a reduction in future budget allocations, while the remainder be treated as a direct charge against the National Revenue Fund.
Case 7: Department of Public Service and Administration
The Department of Public Service and Administration incurred unauthorised expenditure of R8.8 million during the 2013/14 financial year. This expenditure was incurred in respect of advisory staff appointed to the Minister's Office and related operational costs, which exceeded the approved budget. The Committee found that the overspending was avoidable and recommended that the expenditure be financed as a reduction against future allocations.
Case 8: Independent Police Investigative Directorate (IPID)
The Committee also considered the unauthorised expenditure of the Independent Police Investigative Directorate (IPID), which amounted to R91 000 in 2005/06 and R800 000 in 2008/09. These amounts resulted from payroll system interface errors that led to overspending on compensation of employees. The Committee recommended that these amounts be financed as a reduction in future allocations.
Case 9: Department of Basic Education
In the case of the Department of Basic Education (DBE), unauthorised expenditure was incurred in 2015 in relation to the National Teaching Awards ceremony. The Department overspent by shifting savings from other areas within the vote without obtaining Treasury approval. While no official benefited unduly, the Committee found that the expenditure could have been avoided through proper financial management. The Committee recommended that the expenditure be financed as a reduction in future allocations.
Case 10: Department of Minerals and Energy
The Committee also considered the unauthorised expenditure of the Department of Minerals and Energy (DME), which amounted to R14.8 million in 2009/10. The expenditure arose when transfers to municipalities were rejected due to unverified or incorrect banking details, and the Department failed to request a rollover. The Committee noted that corrective measures had since been put in place but recommended that the amount be financed as a reduction in future allocations.After the briefing, Ms Neale-May proposed that the reports be forwarded to the House.
Mr Skosana supported the proposal.
Mr Madlala did not understand why the report had to be returned to the Committee. Was this Committee in a position to contradict the decision of the previous Committee? He thought there were areas in which the departments could have administered consequence management measures. He was not in agreement with a lot of the issues raised.
The Chairperson clarified that this Committee was simply directing the Minister of Finance to deduct the monies from their future allocations or otherwise draw them from the National Revenue Fund, which effectively was an addition to their budget when they should have done better.
The Committee agreed to send the reports for publishing in the ATC. See reports here https://pmg.org.za/tabled-committee-reports
Discussion on Study Tour
The Committee agreed on the suggestions of the shortlisted countries being the United Kingdom (UK), Canada and Australia. These were based on the similarity of the parliamentary systems, the relationship with the AG, and the presence of state-owned companies and agencies, etc.
Mr F Essack (DA) supported going to Australia.
Ms Neale-May, Mr Skosana, Ms Bila, and Mr Kubheka all supported going to the UK.
Ms V Mente-Nkuna emphasised the choice should be informed by what the Committee intended to achieve. Since she was not privy to that knowledge, she would just listen.
Mr Atkinson had a cousin who worked at the public accounts [committee] in the UK Parliament, which he said was very similar to how South Africa’s Parliament exercised oversight.
In his submission, the Chairperson considered the travelling time and how much time that Members would spend on the ground and thus agreed that the UK was a better option. His personal view was for Australia because of the similarity and its resource-based economy, which was similar to South Africa. He emphasised the meticulous record-keeping methods which made auditing a lot easier in Australia.
Ms Neale-May enquired about the cost consideration of flying to the UK or Australia, highlighting cost-effectiveness of the study tour and that all Members should go.
The Chairperson also indicated that should the Committee opt to go to Australia, they would focus on the federal government instead of going to the states.
The meeting was adjourned.
