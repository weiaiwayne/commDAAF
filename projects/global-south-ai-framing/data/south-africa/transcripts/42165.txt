Date: 28 Nov 2025

Meeting Summary
Video
The Portfolio Committees on Home Affairs and Cooperative Governance and Traditional Affairs met jointly to discuss the Independent Electoral Commission’s (IEC's) preparations for the 2026 local government elections. The joint sitting received a comprehensive briefing from the IEC on operational planning, voter registration, technological systems, voter behaviour trends, and anticipated legislative amendments. The discussion was driven by the Committees’ earlier reflections on the 2024 national and provincial elections, where Members had identified several weaknesses that needed to be addressed ahead of the next local government elections.
The IEC outlined its preparation framework, highlighting improvements made following challenges experienced in the 2024 elections. A significant focus was placed on the performance of the voter management devices (VMDs) and the steps taken to stabilise the software, including independent end-to-end testing. The Commission also explained measures to enhance voter registration, improve staff training, strengthen information integrity, and address declining voter participation.
Members raised extensive questions relating to ward demarcations, technical glitches, the functioning of the VMDs, the suitability of voting stations, voter roll integrity, youth participation, voter intimidation, misinformation, and the need for inclusive access for persons with disabilities. Concerns were also raised about uncollected ID documents, the effectiveness of the remote registration app, and the possibility of combining elections or transitioning towards automatic voter registration.
The IEC responded to each issue, clarifying the purpose and limitations of the VMD, explaining the Commission's collaboration with the Department of Home Affairs (DHA), outlining legal timelines for the anticipated Electoral Amendment Bill, and detailing the systems in place to combat misinformation. The DHA confirmed strengthened coordination with the IEC, ongoing campaigns to reduce uncollected IDs, and extended operating hours during the election period.
In closing, Members agreed that several unresolved issues, especially those concerning the VMD, must be revisited early in 2026, including a planned demonstration of the device. The Committee noted the importance of continued joint oversight as the country moved toward the 2026 local government elections.
Meeting report
Chairpersons' opening remarks
The Chairpersons welcomed the Members of both the Portfolio Committees -- Home Affairs and Cooperative Governance and Traditional Affairs -- as well as the Deputy Ministers, senior Departmental officials, and the leadership of the Independent Electoral Commission (IEC).
Chairperson Mkhize said that Chairperson Chabane would preside over the meeting in full, and would guide the session through the agenda, chair the discussions, and oversee the presentations.
On behalf of the Committees, Chairperson Chabane took a moment to congratulate Mr Mosotho Moepya, Judge Dhaya Pillay and Ms Joyce Pitso on their recent appointments as IEC commissioners following the National Assembly’s recommendations. He expressed confidence that their collective expertise would strengthen the Commission and further support its ongoing work.
He reminded attendees that the purpose of the joint meeting was to receive an update on the IEC’s preparations for the 2026 local government elections. This engagement had arisen from the Committee’s reflections on the 2024 national and provincial elections report. The Committees had previously engaged with the IEC about both the progress achieved and the challenges encountered during those elections, and Members had taken note of the weaknesses identified. The current session was therefore considered essential as the Committees begin early oversight to ensure that the upcoming local government elections run smoothly and without incident.
He added that the IEC might introduce possible legislative amendments in preparation for the elections, which the Committees would need to consider and engage with as part of their oversight responsibilities. Although the IEC had already held press briefings on its readiness for the 2026 elections, he emphasised that several matters still required direct engagement with Parliament. The meeting would thus provide an opportunity for deeper discussion and clarity.
He said the IEC had been consulting provincial legislatures on matters within its mandate, and he anticipated that the commissioners would brief the Committees on these engagements as well. He confirmed that the joint meeting had been convened to enable both Committees to collectively understand and navigate the issues the IEC was working on.
Independent Electoral Commission on preparations for local government elections
Commissioner Moepya, Chairperson, IEC, said the Commission welcomed the opportunity to update the Committees on the work currently underway, the challenges being addressed, and the progress made in resolving issues identified during the previous year’s national and provincial elections. He invited the CEO to deliver the presentation on the Commission’s preparations for the local government elections.
Mr Sy Mamabolo, Chief Executive Officer (CEO), IEC, introduced the IEC's presentation by noting that his colleague, Mr Masego Sheburi, Deputy CEO: Electoral Operations, would take the Committees through the detailed briefing. As the presentation was being prepared for display, he offered several preliminary comments.
He reflected on the operational challenges experienced during the previous year’s elections, explaining that these issues had been thoroughly examined at both the administrative level and by the Commission itself. He emphasised that the IEC had implemented a range of amelioration measures which, in their view, would ensure that such challenges did not recur on the next election day.
A key issue highlighted was the malfunction of the voter registration application used on the Voter Management Devices (VMDs). Mr Mamabolo explained that a specific software bug had resulted in the creation of multiple voter records and had caused delays in verifying voters efficiently at voting stations. He informed the Committees that the bug had been identified and addressed, and the IEC believed the matter had now been resolved.
However, the Commission intended to go further than simply correcting the fault. It planned to subject the app to independent testing, covering its full operational journey, from the initial scan at a voting station, through the entire network, to its integration into the IEC’s databases. This independent review would provide assurance regarding the functionality, reliability, and performance of the system. After outlining these measures, Mr Mamabolo concluded his introductory remarks and invited Mr Sheburi to proceed with the formal presentation on the IEC’s preparations.
Mr Sheburi said the IEC’s presentation would cover four themes:
the planning and preparation framework for local government elections;
factors influencing voter behaviour;
the Commission’s building blocks for election delivery; and
the critical path to ensure readiness once the election date was set.
He stressed that the IEC did not set election dates -- the Minister of Cooperative Governance and Traditional Affairs held that authority -- but the Commission had to be fully prepared. The next local elections were constitutionally required between 2 November 2026 and 30 January 2027, with the IEC assuming a Wednesday election to maximise turnout.
Focusing on voter behaviour, Mr Sheburi highlighted declining participation as a global trend exacerbated by economic challenges. Research showed that only 49% of eligible voters would currently vote, with disillusionment and dissatisfaction with democracy as key drivers. Trust in national and local government had sharply declined, and many citizens increasingly viewed voting transactionally, seeking tangible personal benefits. However, 68% still saw voting as a civic duty, offering a foundation for engagement.
The IEC’s preparation included 27.7 million registered voters out of 38 million eligible citizens, with urban areas, especially Gauteng, showing registration gaps. Women register and vote at higher rates, and youth outreach was a priority. The Commission manages 23 292 voting stations and was improving operational efficiency through enhanced training, station suitability reviews, and management criteria for presiding officers. Multi-station voting centres were being considered in high-density areas.
Voter education remained central, using traditional media, community radio, schools, tertiary institutions, and digital tools. Municipal outreach coordinators and district field workers ensured inclusivity, particularly for disabled communities. The IEC was also working with telecom companies to remove data barriers for online services.
Necessary technical amendments to the Electoral Act had been proposed to improve clarity around objections to the voters’ roll, without affecting operations or training. The IEC had undertaken national planning reviews and “train-the-trainer” programmes to ensure consistent election preparedness.
Preparations for the 2026 elections included recruiting registration staff via online platforms, finalising ward boundaries, intensive outreach during voter registration weekends, and training electoral staff, starting in June 2026. Following the Minister’s proclamation of the election date, the voters’ roll would close, candidate nominations would open online, and the formal election timetable would be adopted. Special voting, counting, and results announcements would follow legally mandated deadlines.
Finally, Mr Sheburi emphasised collaboration with political parties, educational institutions and youth-focused campaigns to increase registration, participation and engagement, presenting elections as a tool for citizens to shape their communities and country.

Discussion
Ms H Mkhaliphi (EFF) described the IEC’s presentation as “very thorough and progressive.” She then asked whether the Commission had considered combining elections to reduce resource strain, and whether legislative or constitutional amendments could support this approach.
She sought the IEC’s position on a proposed private member’s bill to do away with voter registration, noting that registration processes contributed to lower turnout.
She requested clarification on the IEC’s timeline for delimitation revisions (February–March 2026) and how the Commission aligned with the Municipal Demarcation Board, particularly in municipalities where court cases had arisen.
She asked about the role of VMDs, highlighting practical challenges such as charging issues and electricity interruptions at voting stations.
She questioned whether the Commission verified the suitability of all voting stations, citing instances where stations were in poor condition, which could affect voter participation.
She raised concerns about voters without formal addresses and the resulting confusion or disputes at polling stations, asking how the IEC manages such situations.
She shared examples of alleged irregularities during past elections, including presiding officers colluding with political parties, and asked about the IEC’s protocols for addressing complaints and vote-rigging incidents.
Ms Mkhaliphi concluded by emphasising the need for clarity on these operational and procedural issues to ensure free, fair, and credible elections.
Mr T Mogale (EFF) focused on several operational and legislative issues regarding voter registration and election timing. He highlighted challenges with the remote voter-registration app, which requires a cell phone number. In areas where residents lacked mobile phones, registration was constrained, as one person may become the sole intermediary for multiple registrations. He asked what measures the IEC was taking to address this limitation. What had the IEC done to specifically increase registration in urban areas, where registration gaps were most pronounced?
He appealed to the IEC to ensure voting stations were adequately sized and comfortable, as some current stations were too small and overcrowded, potentially hindering voters from exercising their rights. He questioned whether legislative or constitutional amendments could allow for moving the upcoming local government election from November 2026 to a more favourable period, noting that the November timing had contributed to a historically low turnout in 2021. Mr Mogale concluded by emphasising the need for practical solutions and legislative flexibility to improve voter participation and the overall election experience.
Mr S Subrathie (ANC) welcomed the IEC’s presentation, noting its value in improving voter participation to protect democracy. His comments focused on voter communication, operational issues, and election integrity. He suggested the IEC should leverage its database of registered voters’ phone numbers to communicate effectively about election plans, arguing that even partial outreach (e.g., 50% via SMS) could enhance participation.
He raised concerns about the terminology used in the presentation to describe unsuccessful candidates (“not so famous”), and recommended using neutral language. He also queried how the IEC could further refine candidature criteria to balance inclusion with operational feasibility. He asked what factors had led to public loss of faith in the IEC during previous elections, and how the IEC planned to address operational shortcomings, including delays in finalising voter lists and impacts from the Municipal Demarcation Board (MDB). He questioned whether voter reallocation due to new demarcations could be managed electronically to reduce confusion on voting day.
He expressed concern about voter intimidation at voting stations, including party vans, songs, and large groups of supporters, asking what the IEC would do to ensure a pressure-free environment for voters. He inquired about plans for voter registration weekends in 2026 and whether targeted campaigns would be implemented, particularly in collaboration with Home Affairs, to increase voter registration and turnout. He emphasised that improving communication, operational efficiency and voter protection, were key to restoring trust and participation in South African elections.
Ms A van Zyl (DA) sought clarification on the process for submitting candidates, noting that the presenter had mentioned submissions may need to be done locally rather than centrally as in the past. Highlighting the voter participation survey indicating only 49% of eligible citizens intended to vote, and a continued decline in trust in democratic institutions, she asked what concrete strategies the IEC would implement, beyond generic civic education, to reverse voter apathy, particularly in municipalities where service delivery issues discouraged voting.
She noted a decrease of 61 442 voters compared to the 2024 National and Provincial Elections (NPE) roll. She asked what factors contributed to this decline and what targeted strategies the IEC would employ to register eligible but currently unregistered citizens, especially youth. She inquired about Home Affairs’ role in ensuring identity documents (IDs) were available for registration, particularly for vulnerable voters whose IDs might be withheld due to financial circumstances. She raised concerns about the 23 292 voting stations and potential delays caused by MDB ward changes, asking if the IEC was confident that delayed finalisation of ward boundaries would not compromise the suitability and readiness of voting stations, especially in rural municipalities where venue options were limited.
Ms Van Zyl emphasised the need for practical solutions to ensure voter participation, registration readiness, and the effective functioning of voting stations ahead of the upcoming local government elections.
Ms M Mathopa (PA) recognised the IEC’s solid progress in legal, operational and logistical preparations for the 2026 local government elections, including expanded voting districts, strengthened staff recruitment, and upgraded technology systems. She emphasised that the biggest threat was not logistical but voter apathy, noting that only 49% of eligible citizens intended to vote, posing risks to the legitimacy of municipalities. She also stressed the need to urgently address misinformation, voter roll accuracy, ward boundaries, and to finalise outstanding legislative amendments.
On legal challenges, she requested clarification on whether the IEC anticipated legal challenges arising from ward demarcations or voter roll objections. She also asked what percentage of voters currently had verified residential addresses, especially in high-mobility and informal communities, and what interventions were in place to improve address accuracy in municipalities with weak data systems. She sought details on the safety and operational procedures of mobile voting stations, citing concerns about votes cast in mobile stations in remote areas like the Kruger National Park.
She inquired about the capacity and training of voting station staff, emphasising that presiding officers and staff should provide consistent information and act impartially, without favouring any political party. She reiterated concerns regarding intimidation and other irregularities at voting stations, urging the IEC to investigate and address these issues. She concluded by reinforcing the importance of operational and electoral integrity to ensure smooth and credible elections.
Inkosi N Buthelezi (IFP) highlighted concerns over declining institutional trust, noting that all categories in the IEC survey had shown a downward trend. Despite a 68% belief in elections, translating that belief into actual voter participation remained challenging. He stressed that declining trust contributed to voter apathy. Mr A Roos (DA) asked about the validation of the coded voters' roll app, including timeframes and access to reports. He also suggested analysing VMD data using big data and artificial intelligence (AI) to identify recurring issues and the potential need for additional devices in areas with past challenges. He emphasised the critical nature of VMD functionality, noting past voter delays due to device downtime.
He asked for clarity on deadlines for Parliament to complete relevant legislative processes to support the elections. He requested details on the IEC's approach to combating misinformation, including whether they planned a proactive monitoring strategy or a reactive reporting system.
Chairperson Mkhize re-entered the meeting's proceedings, and commented that the Committees were at the forefront of supporting the Commission in navigating the critical task of preparing for elections. He emphasised the importance of strengthening the IEC’s relationships with political parties, pointing out that effective engagement could help address grievances, disputes, and doubts expressed by parties, which in turn could mitigate concerns about the credibility and integrity of election results. He also stressed the need to tightly manage disinformation and fake news, urging political parties to actively discourage their members from disseminating false information that could undermine public confidence in the electoral process.
He then turned to the broader challenges of voter attitudes and public disillusionment, observing that the IEC alone could not change how citizens perceived the electoral system. He highlighted that voter participation was strongly influenced by socio-economic conditions such as service delivery, unemployment, cost of living, corruption and crime. He noted that even though there may be a high number of candidates standing for office, public disillusionment remained significant, creating a divergence between the enthusiasm of political aspirants and the engagement of the electorate. He also pointed out a worrying trend toward localised or parochial forms of governance, referring to recent statistics indicating that a notable proportion of citizens expressed support for a military takeover, underscoring the seriousness of declining trust in institutions.
Regarding candidacy, he observed that the pursuit of public office could be motivated in part by economic opportunities, which explained the high number of candidates in contrast with low levels of voter confidence. He highlighted that this misalignment could create instability in governance if not addressed over time.
The Chairperson raised concerns about the stabilisation of coalition governments at the municipal level, noting that delays in processing legislation intended to structure coalitions could perpetuate instability, particularly in light of frequent votes of no confidence in municipalities.
Finally, he addressed the issue of legislative amendments, stressing the need for clarity on timelines and milestones for any changes. He warned that parliamentary processes, including public hearings and consultations, could introduce delays that might affect the IEC’s preparations for the upcoming local government elections. Throughout his contribution, he emphasised the Committees’ readiness to support the IEC while highlighting the interplay between effective electoral management, public confidence, and legislative readiness, noting that all these factors were critical for a credible and stable election process.
IEC's responses
Mr Mamabolo first addressed concerns about the voter management devices (VMDs). He said that a previously identified bug in the voters’ roll application had been corrected, and the operational plan had reached the stage where no further changes could be made to applications used during the registration weekends. The only remaining step was the procurement of an independent testing entity to conduct end-to-end testing of the VMDs and their network, ensuring full functionality prior to use. In addition, handheld scanners would be introduced to improve scanning speed, supplementing the built-in VMD cameras. While the acquisition of additional VMD units was under consideration, the Commission currently had just under 40 000 units, some of which had been damaged during previous operations. He emphasised that staffing at registration weekends would now include at least one person per station with sufficient operational knowledge of the devices, addressing challenges caused by insufficient technical proficiency among personnel.
He then moved on to legislative matters, confirming that the drafting of the bill had been completed, consultations with the relevant political committees had taken place, and the bill had been submitted to the Minister of Home Affairs for approval before tabling in the National Assembly. He highlighted concerns about the tight timelines for processing the bill, emphasising the importance of passing it by April next year to allow for institutionalisation and socialisation within the election environment, including among staff and political committees. He also suggested the possibility of joint briefings and public consultations between the Home Affairs Committee and the Select Committee to streamline the legislative process.
Mr Mamabolo addressed concerns about social media, misinformation and disinformation, noting the significant impact these could have on the credibility and fairness of elections. He said that the IEC was building internal capacity to monitor social media proactively and respond where clarification or corrective communication was needed. This included creating content tailored for different platforms and demographics -- for instance, using LinkedIn for rational debate among professionals and X (formerly Twitter) for more robust engagement, ensuring communication strategies were suited to the nature of each platform.
He outlined the Commission’s multifaceted approach to managing social media and misinformation in the context of elections. A key component involved formal cooperation agreements with major platforms such as Facebook and TikTok, designed to place responsibility on these platforms to prevent their services from being used in ways that compromised the fairness of elections. The agreements required platforms to act on reports of content that was patently false or incited violence, applying their internal policies to remove such content. The Commission also continued to collaborate with Media Monitoring Africa, which operates an independent platform for reporting misinformation. Complaints submitted through this platform were reviewed by an independent panel, and substantiated complaints may lead to requests for removal by the platforms or referrals to the Electoral Court, which was the only body authorised to adjudicate violations of the electoral code of conduct. The Commission was also exploring the constitutional viability of introducing an additional code of conduct to strengthen information integrity, which would be subject to public consultation if implemented.
The CEO acknowledged challenges identified in the 2024 elections, particularly regarding the deployment of young graduates at voting stations. The Commission aimed to strike a balance, ensuring that presiding officers, deputy presiding officers, and other critical roles were filled by experienced personnel while providing opportunities for young graduates to gain practical experience. Recruitment for these positions would begin at the start of the new year, with provincial “train-the-trainer” sessions commencing in December and training for voter registration officers following thereafter. The Commission was also developing mechanisms to monitor the quality of training beyond standard competency assessments.
Regarding voting stations, approximately 60% were situated in schools, but the quality of facilities varied. The Commission sought to ensure that all voters could exercise their rights in decent conditions, supplementing inadequate infrastructure with basic amenities such as ablution facilities and water where necessary. A recent assessment of voting stations had evaluated factors such as seating, accessibility for persons with disabilities, restroom facilities, water availability, and mobile phone coverage. Decisions on the establishment or relocation of stations within a district were made in consultation with local party committees, taking local knowledge into account to address tactical and logistical considerations. The CEO emphasised the importance of active participation by local political committees in this process, noting that delays or low attendance could impact critical logistical decisions.
The Commission was also addressing issues related to activities immediately outside voting stations. While low-key political activities like voter encouragement were permitted, organised rallies, marches, and other large public events were prohibited. Regulations governed the placement of tents and tables to ensure they did not obstruct voters or the free flow of people and vehicles. Authority within the voting station rested solely with the presiding officer, while the South African Police Service was responsible for maintaining order beyond station boundaries.
Mr Mamabolo provided an update on the website, indicating plans to enhance accessibility, particularly for persons with disabilities. On administrative matters, the Commission was working closely with the Municipal Demarcation Board to finalise electoral boundaries by 10 December, with contingency planning in case of litigation. The Commission maintained a close working relationship with the Department of Home Affairs, coordinating on ID verification and other logistical matters, while candidate nominations would continue to be processed centrally.
Further discussion
Ms Mkhaliphi addressed the CEO’s response, clarifying that her question specifically concerned the VDM. She expressed a need for a clear explanation of its role, noting that in some instances the VDM failed to function, yet voting continues. She highlighted the practical challenges this created, as party agents frequently reported malfunctions, which could cause confusion and disruption at voting stations. She emphasised that understanding the VDM’s purpose and limitations was important for Members of Parliament to better grasp its role in the electoral process.
Mr Sheburi withdrew an earlier statement he had made about candidates, clarifying that it was not meant to be disparaging but was a limitation in his expression. He then responded directly to the question regarding the functional value of the VMD at voting stations.
He explained that the VMD had originally been introduced following the 2016 Constitutional Court decision which required that voters appear on the voters’ roll with an address where one exists. The VMD was therefore designed to capture voters’ addresses during registration and to allocate each voter to the correct ward. This was its primary purpose when it was rolled out in 2019.
He added that after allegations had emerged suggesting that some voters might have voted more than once, the system had been expanded. The VMD was adapted to support a voters’ roll application that checks ID numbers against ballots issued at a station, ensuring that each ID number was scanned only once, and that a voter could receive ballot papers only a single time. With real-time updates, where internet connectivity allows, the VMD prevents a scenario where a voter casts a ballot at one station in the morning and then goes to another station later in the day.
He noted that this safeguard assumed a voter could remove the indelible ink, but since section 24A voting was no longer available upon demand at the station, the risk of multiple voting had been significantly reduced.
Mr Mawethu Mosery, Deputy Chief Electoral Officer: Outreach, IEC, said he wished to address three main issues. He first spoke about the distinction between the population register and the voters’ roll. He emphasised that relying on the population register would not resolve the challenge of low voter turnout, and might in fact worsen the appearance of poor participation because it included everyone irrespective of their intention to vote. He said that individuals who registered to vote demonstrated a deliberate intention to participate, and the voters’ roll also had the advantage of geographically locating voters, an important aspect of building an accurate and functional electoral roll. He noted that the IEC continued to work closely with the Department of Home Affairs to ensure the accuracy of ID numbers and to support ongoing voter registration efforts, especially among young people.
He then turned to the broader issue of voter attitudes and perceptions. He confirmed that research consistently showed a downward trend in voter participation, partly driven by a group of disillusioned voters, as previously referenced by Mr Sheburi. According to the IEC’s recent study, one of the strongest influences on this disillusioned cohort was the prevalence of negative campaigning and negative messaging by political contestants. He stressed that this was not something the Commission could remedy on its own; rather, it required political parties to shift towards more constructive campaigning. He added that negative rhetoric, including messaging that undermines the authority of the IEC, had a direct effect on how the public perceives the Commission and on overall confidence in the electoral process. He urged Committees and political stakeholders to help cultivate a positive electoral climate that strengthens the freeness of elections and encourages disillusioned voters to return to participation.
Mr Mosery noted that their research on declining democratic participation also revealed that some voters were beginning to show a preference for alternative forms of governance, including authoritarian models, an indication that South Africa must actively protect and uphold the value of its democratic system.
He went on to address trends in voter behaviour, confirming that higher participation among women was consistent both on the voters’ roll and on voting day. Although the IEC had not studied the reasons behind this trend, he affirmed it as a consistent pattern.
He concluded by outlining the Commission’s ongoing engagements with persons with disabilities. He described the practical measures put in place to enhance accessibility, such as the Universal Ballot Template (UBT), which assists dyslexic voters, partially blind voters, and individuals with literacy limitations, to vote independently and preserve the secrecy of their ballot. He added that the IEC’s website had also been made accessible to people who were deaf and blind, as confirmed during recent stakeholder engagements. These initiatives reflected the Commission’s commitment to ensuring inclusive participation.
Ms Mkhaliphi returned to the issue of the VMD, indicating that she remained concerned despite the earlier explanations. She said that the Committee had been told that the VMD’s purpose was to verify whether a voter had previously voted, which was a critical safeguard in the electoral process. Her concern, however, was that in many instances the device collapsed or failed during voting. She questioned why voting was allowed to continue when the VMD was not functioning, given that it was meant to prevent multiple voting. She emphasised that this had been a recurring problem, and asked directly what happened when the device collapsed, and why the process continued despite the malfunction.
Chairperson Chabane noted Ms Mkhaliphi’s concern, and said the commissioners would address the matter. However, he emphasised that the Committee would need to return to several of the issues raised during the meeting, including the functioning of the VMDs. He indicated that these matters would form part of the Committee’s next engagement with the IEC, which was scheduled for early 2026. He stressed the importance of returning to the issue of the VMD at a future engagement and noted that a demonstration of the device would also be necessary. He then asked the commissioners to address the matter raised by Ms Mkhaliphi.
Commissioner Masuri thanked Members for their detailed and frank questions and acknowledged the constructive nature of the discussion. He emphasise that the VMD contains five applications, with only one having experienced challenges. While this issue could be resolved immediately, he agreed that Members should still have the opportunity to observe a full demonstration in the next engagement.
He went on to address three remaining issues. Firstly, he noted a reference to a private member’s bill concerning voter registration. The Commission was not yet aware of the bill, but once it was officially tabled, they would engage with it and provide their honest assessment. Secondly, he discussed the recurring question of combined elections, explaining that this was ultimately a policy matter for Parliament. Once Parliament finalised its policy position, the Commission would provide its considered views, but currently no formal proposal existed. Thirdly, he addressed concerns about credibility issues, including allegations of vote rigging and challenges with the compilation of results. He assured Members that lessons from previous elections had been studied and translated into a checklist of risks, with corresponding mitigation measures. He stressed that the Commission’s priority was to ensure accessible elections that did not impede citizens’ right to vote. He also pointed to research indicating that while some voters may lose confidence if issues were not resolved, a larger percentage was affected by broader disillusionment linked to political trust and service delivery. He urged continued collaboration to address these concerns.
DHA's responses
Mr Tommy Makhode, Director-General, Department of Home Affairs, confirmed that the interministerial committee responsible for local government election preparations had already been activated and was meeting regularly. Coordination was ongoing between the Department, the IEC and other key structures. He also confirmed that the new Electoral Amendment Bill had been sent to the State Law Advisor for an opinion, and the Department was awaiting additional documentation from the IEC to complete the socio-economic impact assessment.
On uncollected IDs, he reported that the number had dropped from around 550 000 to approximately 390 000, largely due to efforts by the Department and the Committee. However, young applicants still accounted for a significant share of uncollected documents. From 8 December, DHA offices would operate extended hours, including overtime shifts and Saturday hours, with additional services provided through mobile units, mall-based offices and certain bank branches. These operational hours would align with IEC activities during registration and voting periods.
Mr Njabulo Nzuza, Deputy Minister of Home Affairs, added that no registered voter would be prevented from voting due to lack of an ID, since temporary identification certificates would be issued when necessary. He emphasised continued campaigns to reduce uncollected IDs, and highlighted ongoing collaboration with the IEC to maintain an accurate and clean voters’ roll.
Closing remarks
In closing, Chairperson Chabane thanked all participants, and noted that several issues raised would be revisited in future joint meetings as preparations for the local government elections progress. He then invited Dr Mkhize to summarise the meeting.
Chairperson Mkhize highlighted key issues requiring attention, including the timely distribution of identity documents, addressing technical glitches within the IEC systems, and the need for future engagement on voter registration processes, including the possibility of automatic registration. He also emphasised the importance of coordination across committees, departments and political stakeholders to reduce misinformation and protect the integrity of South Africa’s electoral processes, which broadly remained credible.
The meeting was adjourned.
