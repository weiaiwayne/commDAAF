Meeting Summary
The Minister of Home Affairs and the Government Printing Works (GPW) briefed the Committee on the entity's turnaround strategy. Other agenda items included deliberations on the upper limits and disclosure threshold to fund political parties, consideration of the motion of desirability (MOD) on the Immigration Amendment Bill, and responses from the Border Management Authority (BMA) to questions posed at last week’s Committee meeting.
The GPW's presentation covered its response to the Auditor-General's recommendations, as well as progress on the recommendations of the ministerial review. The Minister made it clear that the entity had to adapt to the technological advances in the printing industry, or face a bleak future. He said the GPW faced risks as the world became more paperless, and repositioning the organisation and addressing long-standing issues could unlock value and create opportunities. His view was backed by the GPW management, which highlighted its recent progress, including manufacturing 3.6 million identity documents in the past year against a target of 2.6 million. Its plans included relocating and upgrading its facilities, improving the organisational culture, and taking a firm stance against corruption.
The BMA responded to the Committee's questions as to how they were dealing with the illicit trade in cigarettes; travellers who did not have travel documentation to cross the country's borders; and their level of capability from an advanced technology viewpoint to deal with the current breed of technologically advanced criminals. Members expressed disappointment at the responses, stating that they were lacking in meaningful detail.
The Committee considered the draft resolution on the spending threshold and limit to fund political parties. The current recommendation was to keep the upper limit of donations at R15 million and the lowest threshold at R100 000.
The motion of desirability on the Immigration Amendment Bill aimed to amend sections of the Immigration Act, address the arrest and detention of illegal foreigners for deportation, align with constitutional principles, and provide related measures. However, there was disagreement on the amendment's scope -- another unrelated aspect had been introduced -- so further public consultation was required.

David
Chilembe, Deputy Commissioner, BMA, who went over three questions that had been asked previously.
Mr Ngubane had asked whether the problem of farmers who owned part of the borderline and had erected a gate or a fence that made it impossible for the border guards to patrol had been resolved.
Mr Chilembe said the matter was before the courts, led by the Department of Public Works and Infrastructure (DPWI). In the interim, the BMA was working with the Transport Research Activity Centre (TRAC), the N4 route concessionaire, to utilise part of the land called KM7. TRAC was renting directly from the alleged owner, not the DPWI, so that there was a smooth passage of traffic to the Lebombo port of entry as part of their 30-year contract with the two governments of South Africa and Mozambique.
As the BMA worked with the Defence Force, they were able to engage with the farmer, and he allowed law enforcement authorities to conduct patrols on that route. He had erected the boom to control the movement of small vans that moved illicit goods from his farm. The BMA had partnered with the farmer and worked with him to deter and intercept some smugglers trying to move goods in and out of South Africa.
Mr Ngubane’s second question had been about measures put in place for travellers who travel between Lesotho and South Africa without the necessary travel documentation, making use of a bridge to cross over to another country.
Mr Chilembe responded that the BMA had deployed border law enforcement officers to address the Lesotho/RSA vulnerable segments of the borderline at Ficksburg, Maseru, Van Rooyen, and Caledonspoort. Joint operations and collaboration with the South African National Defence Force (SANDF) were taking place as well. However, the resources were not adequate since the BMA did not have a footprint at all the ports of entry and vulnerable segments between the two countries.
Mr T Mogale (EFF) had asked what the BMA was doing about the illicit trade in cigarettes, and whether they were engaging with other jurisdictions that were believed to be producing these cigarettes?
Mr Chilembe said criminal cases had been opened with the South African Police Service (SAPS) and investigations were ongoing. Manufacturers from neighbouring countries were yet not known.
Mr Mogale’s second question asked if the 35 vehicle "hits" had been stolen.
Mr Chilembe said these were vehicles that had been stolen and detected through the SAPS database, or through physical inspections and verifications with the Electronic National Administration Traffic Information System (e-NaTIS).
Mr Mogale also asked how the BMA was dealing with the conveyors/ transporters of travellers who did not have travel documentation and what the names of these companies were.
Mr Chilembe said that airlines and bus companies that transport illegal travellers were issued with fines in terms of the Immigration Act 2002, section 50 (3) and (4). The BMA had entered into a multi-agency memorandum of understanding (MOU) on cooperation in respect of the administrative fines and penalties levied against conveyors issued in terms of the Immigration Act, 2002 (Act No.13 of 2002) with the Department of Home Affairs (DHA); the Department of Transport (DoT); the Airports Company of South Africa (ACSA) and the Cross-Border Road Transport Agency (C-BRTA).
He added that a third question had been asked by Ms F Muthambi (ANC), who had pointed out that within the border environment, they were dealing with technologically advanced criminals, some of whom moved guns, so the BMA also needed to rely on technology to address these problems. How was the BMA implementing the national cybersecurity policy framework?
Mr Chilembe said the BMA oversees both strategic and operational information communication technology (ICT) risks through its enterprise risk management framework, which includes cybersecurity risks. High-level risks had mitigations applied to them. The effectiveness and adequacy of these risk mitigations were assessed. A risk register was maintained, monitored, and updated regularly. For information security, the BMA has implemented network security devices, access control management tools, anti-malware, and encryption to safeguard against threats and ensure confidentiality, integrity, and availability of sensitive information. The BMA continuously monitors its ICT assets for potential vulnerabilities, whether external or internal. Identified vulnerabilities were addressed, and 30% of the budget received would be allocated to IT provision.

Government Printing Works' turnaround strategy
The Minister said the Government Printing Works (GPW) would brief the Committee on the turnaround strategy. The presentation would cover Auditor-General's (AG's) recommendations, as well as progress on the recommendations of the ministerial review. It would include the names of executives and managers who were members of the union, the impact of downtime, the cost of machines, when the machines were purchased, and how many. He would provide an assessment of the situation and outline the way forward.
Manufacture of Smart identity documents (IDs)
The target of 3.6 million smart IDs manufactured this year has been met. This was the highest number ever, as the target had been 2.5 or 2.6 million. There were roughly 18 million South Africans who still had the green ID book, which was vulnerable to fraud. The GPW needed to significantly improve performance to potentially issue millions -- even 10 million -- IDs on an annual basis.
External review of GPW
The GPW was actively working on the Committee's recommendation for a broader,
external look into the entity. There was a need to draw up terms of reference and find appropriate people. The goal was to pull together experts with clear terms of reference, and avoid repeating existing
investigations. A broader strategic assessment of where the organisation was going and how it would
function in the digital future was needed.
Challenges and opportunities
There was a need to address current challenges and focus on strategic questions. Positioning the GPW to benefit from the digital transformation of government was important. The President envisioned "government at a touch" and a single platform for government services. Confirming user identity was crucial.
Minister Schreiber commented on the future of the GPW, and said "it's either going to be an opportunity or a threat if we don't get out of the mud." The GPW faced risks as the world became more paperless, and repositioning the organisation and addressing long-standing issues could unlock value and create opportunities.
GPW's strategic repositioning and turnaround of operations
Ms Alinah Fosi, Chief Executive Officer (CEO), Government Print Works, said that the presentation was aligned with the contents of the letter received from the Portfolio Committee on 12 March. She started by focusing on the strategic repositioning and turnaround strategy for operations at the GPW.
Alignment to priorities
She said the strategy was aligned to the medium-term development plan (MTDP) priorities as stated by the President on last year:
Drive inclusive growth and job creation;
Reduce poverty and tackle high cost of living;
Build a capable, ethical and developmental state.
GPW's actions
The organisation had developed a tax plan for 2025 to 2030, and an annual performance plan (APP) 2025/26 to support the ministerial priorities of digital transformation. Documents would reflect its repositioning as an entity for authenticating and certifying secured digital products and services using E-Government platforms.
Vision and priorities
The revised vision was its role as a "safe, mandated security document enabler and digital trust centre."
Its priorities were to transform into a digital trust centre through digital products and services, reposition operations as an enabler and verifier of digital products for viability and sustainability, and improve customer experience through timely services, quality, and quantity via hybrid digital offerings.
Organisational strategy
The GPW's focus was on national identity management, now including digital products, and data protection. There was a strong focus on cybersecurity and anti-fraud measures for E-passport certificates and national ID verification. In the area of expansion and capacity building, it was investing in G cell printing presses for increased operational efficiencies. It was also collaborating with the DHA to revise and upgrade the ePassport into the new ePassport series.
E-government integration
E-government integration would function across all government departments to certify and verify documents. The GPW would verify services for citizens on one side, using e-government platforms. Efficiency and automation of business processes would be central, with a focus on optimisation.
Strategic pillars
There would be a revised business and operating model, and asset recapitalisation would remain key to determine the use of existing buildings and equipment. There would be a focus on local businesses, as well as those in African countries.
HR development
Training and retraining/upskilling/reskilling of officials were needed as the GPW embraces
technology. "No one was going to lose their jobs," and a hybrid model of coexistence between people
and technology was planned. There was a plan to train 80% of officials to build an agile workforce that could respond to digitisation challenges.
Infrastructure and relocation
The focus would be on ICT, safety and the environment as a manufacturing and printing entity. Officials would be moved out of the "dilapidated postmaster's office." The Development Bank of Southern Africa (DBSA) had promised that officials would gradually start moving into the new headquarters
building in Visagie Street. Officials would also move to the new commercial printing facility, vacating the old building.
GPW business model
Digital identity management would be key. Revenue management would focus on printing services and sales. The market would be local and international.
Performance analysis
Audited performance data from the Auditor-General of South Africa (AGSA) was being used. Organisational performance has averaged around 70% since 2019, ending at 78% in
2023/24. The goal was to improve performance and demonstrate an upward projection.
ID production
The GPW had printed a total of 3.6 million IDs to date, exceeding the set target. Since October 2013, it has printed a total of 28.4 million ID Smart cards. Production would be ramped up to prepare for the 18 million citizens still to be printed for. A total of 12.6 million passports had been printed since 2009.
Statistics and future plan
Statistics from 2019 to today showed an upward trajectory. The organisation had plans to ramp up and
improve ID documents, and move more into the E-examination space to service more customers with printing.
Addressing critical areas
This section of the presentation addressed two critical areas:
Strategic level: Ensuring the organisation was responsible and positioned for digital transformation.
Operational level: Turning around operations to deal with culture change and implement change management.
Strategic level: situation analysis
The history of the GPW's performance showed it was not an ailing organisation on the verge of collapse. It was a sustainable government entity that had performed through difficult periods, including COVID-19 and data loss. It had been contributing to the National Revenue Fund (NRF) through its surplus, which had been used to bail out other entities.
Operational level: internal challenges
The GPW would be focusing its energies on internal operational challenges to ensure stability. Its challenges included:
Turning around the organisational culture through the implementation of a change
management strategy.
Redefining the organisation's role to adapt to new technological investments and reposition operations for digitalisation.
Investing in people, creating high-performance teams, and managing performance through performance management and development system (PMDS) processes.
Reward and recognition strategies aimed at encouraging officials to continue to do better in the organisation.
Ms Fosi concluded by stating that officials would be held accountable and responsible for acts of misconduct in order to avoid a recurrence of such acts.

Immigration Amendment
Bill
Consideration of the motion of desirability (MOD)
For Members new to legislation, a motion of desirability was a technical requirement for a
Committee before processin legislation. Submissions, discussions, and answers on the immigration amendment were complete, allowing bill revision and progress.
The Committee, with a quorum, needed to adopt the motion of desirability to proceed with
deliberations and various bill versions. The motion of desirability on the Immigration Amendment Bill aimed to amend sections of the Immigration Act, address the arrest and detention of illegal foreigners for deportation, align with constitutional principles, and provide related measures.
The parliamentary legal team, the DHA, and the chief state law advisor addressed a few matters outside the meeting to formulate input and outline the next steps. After adopting the motion of desirability, the Committee would review the bill's content, aligning with bylaws. Public hearings had taken place, and the bill had been published for comments. The Committee had been briefed on responses from the
Department and advisors, and the next step was to deliberate on the bill.
The Office of Legal Services had met with the Department and the state law advisor. They had tried to find middle ground, but disagreements remained. They wanted to brief the Chairperson and meet with stakeholders again.
Disagreement on amendment scope
The disagreement was that the amendment was solely about the provision of that section, but other aspects not related to that section were being brought in, and bringing in another matter required another process. Public consultation was also required.
The purpose of the bill was to address the constitutional court judgment and cure the constitutional defect.
Public comments had identified gaps in the legislation, such as:
The input of the justice factor.
The issue of children.
The issue of section 35 rights.
The issue of children was not dealt with in the judgment, but came from the public participation process. Parliament was obliged to meaningfully consider public input. Recommendations from the public hearing had been brought to the Committee's attention for consideration.
The court had provided guidance, but did not want to step on the principle of the separation of powers. The legislature could amend or cure the defect as they see fit, and the Committee could expand on the board if it deems it fit.
Amendments that were material and not in the public domain may require the bill to be re
advertised. Introducing new elements that deviate from the Constitutional Court's mandate could
affect the timeframe.
Focusing on the Constitutional Court mandate
The DG mentioned a separate process (White Paper) to address broader issues.
The Chairperson suggested focusing on the Constitutional Court's specific requirements and
addressing other matters through the White Paper process.
A meeting with the Chairperson and the team would be scheduled to bring clarity on what needed to
be done.
The meeting was adjourned.
