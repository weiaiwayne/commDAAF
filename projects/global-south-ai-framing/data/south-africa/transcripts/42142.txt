Meeting Summary
Video
The Portfolio Committee meeting focused on provincial and national readiness for disasters during the rainy season, with particular emphasis on emergency housing interventions, coordination challenges, and long-term solutions. Throughout the session, Members expressed concern about recurring disasters, slow or uneven responses, and the need for stronger preventive strategies.
Several Members highlighted that despite annual readiness plans, implementation failures continued to leave victims struggling for assistance long after disasters occurred. They stressed that many communities remained in danger zones year after year, particularly along riverbanks and floodplains, because of limited housing options and systemic failures by government structures. Members argued that the government should prioritise making suitable land available for proper housing to minimise repeated disaster exposure and save lives and public resources.
Questions were raised about national and provincial coordination systems, the verification of affected beneficiaries, and compliance with emergency housing criteria to prevent temporary accommodation from becoming permanent. Members also asked why readiness plans did not explicitly incorporate climate-change considerations, despite worsening weather patterns, frequent flooding, and increased fires in certain regions.
In response, national and provincial officials provided detailed explanations. They noted that national teams were guaranteed to be on the ground within 48 hours of a reported disaster, although beneficiary verification, done by municipalities, often caused delays. In some areas, municipalities struggled to provide suitable land for temporary residential units (TRUs), or the land available was unsafe for construction. Officials acknowledged that contractors sometimes failed to deliver TRUs, but emphasised that most projects progressed effectively.
National Department of Human Settlements representatives also explained that people living in low-lying or flood-prone areas were regularly warned and encouraged to relocate to mass care centres, but many refused due to fear of losing their belongings, proximity to jobs, schools, and familiar community spaces. Officials further highlighted that forced removals were legally constrained and could occur only with a court order, which municipalities had to initiate.
A recurring theme was the need for increased public education on disaster risks and preventive measures. National Treasury had allocated funds specifically for education and prevention, and officials confirmed that these programmes were underway but hampered by community reluctance to relocate.
Provinces requested better standardisation of practices and technologies across the country. Some provinces, such as Gauteng, were more advanced in using data analytics, artificial intelligence, drone footage, and mapping technologies to model risk and inform planning. The Department confirmed that it had begun using drones and polygon-mapping technologies to identify the number, size, and risk exposure of informal settlements. These tools supported decisions on which settlements could be upgraded in situ and which required relocation.
The Chairperson raised concerns about inconsistent TRU designs across provinces and encouraged improved quality to ensure dignity for affected communities. DHS officials reported that although one broad specification existed, contractors sometimes exceeded or adapted requirements, leading to variation. The Director General also announced a forthcoming pilot project in the Eastern Cape to test permanent 40 m² houses as a direct disaster response, replacing TRUs. Treasury had given in-principle support for this model, which could reduce long-term costs and eliminate “double dipping” caused by providing both temporary and permanent structures.
Provinces concluded by expressing appreciation for national support and reaffirmed their commitment to readiness, community awareness, and collaboration during the upcoming disaster season.
Meeting report
Chairperson’s Remarks
The Chairperson outlined the purpose of the meeting, emphasising that the Committee sought updates from the national Department of Human Settlements (DHS) and assurances from the provinces regarding their state of readiness for the upcoming festive period. He said the agenda included a briefing from the Department on the Emergency Fund, followed by readiness presentations from the provinces of Limpopo, Mpumalanga, North West, Northern Cape, and the Western Cape. The Committee would meet with the remaining provinces the following week to ensure full oversight and comfort regarding national preparedness.
He said the purpose of engaging provinces this week, and the following Wednesday, was to enable the Portfolio Committee to receive key highlights on national and provincial readiness for the upcoming disaster period. Signs of disaster-related risks were already becoming visible in several provinces, including Limpopo around Sekhukhune, the North West, and the Eastern Cape. He cautioned that if not properly managed, these early signs could quickly escalate, and therefore, the Committee needed to receive timely briefings on preparedness measures. While acknowledging that disasters were inherently unpredictable and often outside the direct control of government, he emphasised the Committee’s responsibility to understand the level of readiness from a human settlements perspective.
The Chairperson addressed concerns raised by Members regarding the late submission of reports. He said that DDG Dumalisile had briefed him about the challenges experienced in coordinating inputs across all provinces, noting that obtaining consolidated information from multiple provincial departments was significantly more complex than receiving a single report from the national Department. He highlighted that Portfolio Committee reports were not only for individual Members, but must also be shared with their political parties to allow sufficient time for internal analysis, guidance, and preparation ahead of meetings. Receiving reports only a day before the meeting limited this process and created operational challenges for Members. However, understanding the DDG’s constraints for this specific meeting, he indicated that the Committee would proceed, while the concern remained valid for future engagements.
Minister’s opening remarks
Ms Thembi Simelane, Minister of Human Settlements, highlighted several key issues she felt were important to underscore before the Director General's (DG’s) presentation. She reminded Members that the Department had previously committed to assessing provincial performance during recent disaster events, including reviewing the status of families affected by disasters who had subsequently been added to blocked project lists or who had not yet been assisted. She confirmed that this exercise had been completed under the leadership of the DG, with strong cooperation from provinces, and that meaningful progress had been made. She said the North West and Free State had faced significant challenges. While the relevant Members of Executive Councils (MECs) were not present, she acknowledged their constructive engagement in jointly revising their business plans and categorising priority blocked projects, including those affecting disaster-affected beneficiaries.
The Minister said the DG would provide a more detailed account of the state of readiness across provinces. The Department had reviewed disaster-related spending quotas, governance concerns, administrative processes, and accountability mechanisms, all of which had previously contributed to challenges in managing disaster funding and informal settlement interventions. She reported that the DG, along with the executive management team and provincial heads of departments (HODs), had conducted technical engagements across provinces, culminating in discussions at the most recent Ministers and Members of Executive Councils (MINMEC) meeting. During that session, a new percentage-based mechanism had been introduced to enable provinces to respond to disasters as they occurred immediately.
Minister Simelane also updated the Committee on recent disaster incidents in the provinces listed earlier by the Chairperson, adding that KwaZulu-Natal (KZN) had been affected as well. In the Hanover and Umzimkhulu areas, two family members remained missing as of the previous evening, though search efforts were ongoing. One body had been recovered, and both national and provincial teams were on site providing support.
Department of Human Settlements' emergency response framework
Dr Alec Moemi, DG, DHS, said that DDG Dumalisile would be delivering the presentation on behalf of the Department, while he would contribute further information or clarity on specific issues as needed during the course of the presentation.
Ms Ngaka Dumalisile, DDG: Affordable, Rental and Social Housing, DHS, reminded Members that the Department had previously briefed the Portfolio Committee on its responsibilities and performance since assuming control of the Emergency Housing Programme, and that several of the elements outlined then also formed part of the current presentation. Some repetition was therefore unavoidable, as the same policy and operational framework underpinned the Department’s current readiness measures.
She confirmed that South Africa had entered the period in which disaster incidents typically increased, particularly over the summer season, and that the DHS was already observing events in various provinces. In response, the national Department had developed a comprehensive framework to guide disaster response within the human settlements sector. This framework was informed both by recent incidents and by the resolution of MINMEC to enable provinces to implement portions of the emergency housing intervention directly. The Department had engaged all provinces on the framework to clarify expectations. Many provinces were already guided by their provincial disaster management centres, and in some cases, such as the Eastern Cape, a pre-season “dry run” of preparedness had already been undertaken. Limpopo had a plan in place, and provinces such as Gauteng, North West and Mpumalanga had also been engaged within their own institutional arrangements, including through the Department of Cooperative Governance and Traditional Affairs (COGTA) where relevant.
Ms Dumalisile said the key objective of the framework was to ensure clarity and consistency in disaster interventions across all affected areas, so that national and provincial responses were aligned and comparable in approach. The Department aimed to assist provinces to intervene timeously and effectively, while still complying with existing disaster management legislative prescripts. The framework was anchored in the existing legislative and institutional arrangements of the National Disaster Management Centre and the Inter-Ministerial Committee (IMC). Human Settlements participates in these structures at both political and technical levels -- the Minister serves on the IMC, and the DG serves on the technical IMC. At the provincial level, incidents reported at the municipal level were escalated through provincial disaster management structures to the national centre, with the Department of Human Settlements receiving parallel reports and responding in line with its mandate.
She then outlined the provincial hazard profiles and patterns that informed planning. In the Eastern Cape, a mixed rural-urban province with extensive informal settlements, many communities were located along the coastline, near rivers, or in areas prone to recurring heavy rains and flooding, such as Mthatha, Port St Johns and Mzamba. Informal and formal settlements, as well as ageing township infrastructure, were all affected. Provinces were therefore expected to conduct pre-season identification of hazards down to the lowest level, map high-risk informal settlement zones, conduct public awareness campaigns, register for early warning systems with the South African Weather Service, and ensure that response teams were on standby. Similar principles applied in the Free State, where the main hazards included summer heat waves, thunderstorms, and flooding in low-lying areas, affecting both rural settlements and old townships with ageing infrastructure.
In Gauteng, a high-density urban province with a large footprint of informal settlements, hazards included intense thunderstorms with hail and flash flooding due to overloaded drainage systems, as well as settlements located near rivers, such as those around the Jukskei River. In KwaZulu-Natal, a coastal province, risks included tropical storms, heavy summer rainfall, storm surges and fires in informal settlements, along with settlements situated along rivers, as seen in the recent incident referred to by the Minister. Limpopo, predominantly rural with peri-urban settlements, faced risks associated with summer rains, flooding along rivers, and informal rural settlements built in low-lying areas with poor drainage and ageing bulk infrastructure. In Mpumalanga, a mixed urban-rural province, hazards included thunderstorms, hail, veld fires affecting structures near forests, and informal settlements and villages near rivers, wetlands and mining areas.
Ms Dumalisile noted that a separate exercise was underway to profile mining areas that may be at risk of sinkholes. North West, with a semi-rural and peri-urban profile, experienced summer thunderstorms, flash floods, informal settlement fires, and vulnerabilities in informal settlements near streams and in some mining communities. The Northern Cape, sparsely populated, was exposed to extreme heat waves, thunderstorms, flash flooding and fires, especially affecting informal dwellings in remote rural locations. Finally, the Western Cape, a coastal province, faced chronic informal settlement fires, summer wildfires and flooding in both informal settlements and houses near forests and low-lying areas. It was one of the provinces with the highest number of repeat incidents and featured prominently in the Department’s hotspot list.
She then presented the national disaster “hotspots” identified by the Department based on recurring incidents and patterns.
In the Eastern Cape, these include areas within Buffalo City Metro such as Duncan Village and informal settlements in Nompumelelo, parts of the OR Tambo District including Mthatha, Port St Johns and Makhanda, and areas around Bizana in the Winnie Madikizela-Mandela Local Municipality.
In KwaZulu-Natal, areas such as Kennedy Road, informal settlements in eThekwini, Umlazi and other frequently affected localities had required repeated intervention.
In the Western Cape, hotspot areas include Khayelitsha, Philippi, Lwandle, Overcome Heights, Dunoon, Langa, Joe Slovo, Wallacedene, Masiphumelele and others, where fires and flooding occurred regularly.
The Department expected provincial disaster and human settlements plans to elevate these identified hotspots explicitly and to plan targeted interventions accordingly, so that responses were swift and better coordinated when incidents occur.
Turning to institutional arrangements for intervention, Ms Dumalisile explained the division of responsibilities between national and provincial levels. Following a MINMEC decision, provinces were responsible for responding to incidents where the impact was minimal or minor, and affected between one and 50 households. The national Department was responsible for significant and severe disaster impacts affecting 51 or more households, regardless of whether the effect was categorised as significant or severe. Once an incident was reported, the Department received early warnings from the South African Weather Service (SAWS) and deployed staff and community liaison officers to assess and verify incidents.
In preparation for the summer season, the Department had supplemented its capacity with 48 volunteers who had been trained and had agreed to remain available over the festive period, in addition to existing community liaison officers. These teams were responsible for confirming incidents on the ground, profiling affected households, and identifying the appropriate interventions. The range of interventions included the provision of fire kits and building materials for informal settlements, temporary residential units (TRUs), and temporary emergency accommodation (TEA) where families were displaced, as well as structural assessments in cases of partially damaged formal houses, undertaken by the NHBRC. The NHBRC advises on the scope and cost of repairs, and material for self-repair was provided where appropriate. Provinces were expected to mirror these practices for incidents within their 1–50 household threshold and to pre-identify temporary emergency accommodation facilities such as student residences, boarding facilities or other suitable buildings in high-risk areas, so that resettlement was not delayed. Ms Dumalisile cited previous challenges, such as in Mthatha, where a lack of integrated planning had complicated transport and relocation, stressing that better pre-planning and interdepartmental coordination were now expected.
To strengthen technical capacity, the Department had also secured two additional engineers from the Housing Development Agency (HDA) to support inspections and certify the structural soundness of TRUs in line with departmental specifications. These engineers had already been active on the ground for approximately two weeks. The Department had also ensured that service providers on its existing panel for the construction of TRUs were available during the summer period and not on extended holiday, to prevent delays in response. Similarly, arrangements had been made to ensure that suppliers of fire kits and building materials were on standby.
On the financial side, Ms Dumalisile reported that the Department had dedicated funding for emergency housing interventions, including allocations for TRUs, fire kits and associated support. The National Treasury had approved the rollover of certain funds and considered an application for “unforeseen and unavoidable” expenditure submitted to the Cabinet. Although the amount approved was less than what the Department had requested, the Department was proceeding with verifications in the affected provinces, especially Mpumalanga, Limpopo, KwaZulu-Natal and the Eastern Cape, where substantial outstanding needs had been identified and had not yet been fully addressed. Teams from the chief financial officer's (CFO’s) office had already visited Limpopo and engaged with the Eastern Cape to confirm that conditions on the ground still warranted intervention, and further verification visits to other provinces were planned.
She concluded by clarifying, in response to a question raised at a previous meeting, how and where disaster incidents could be reported, noting that the Department had provided detailed contact information and reporting channels. She closed by submitting the report and framework to the Portfolio Committee for noting, emphasising that the national framework had been put in place, guidance had been provided to the provinces, and provincial plans had been prepared to demonstrate their level of readiness to respond to disaster incidents during the summer period.

Dr Moemi added that in relation to the funding arrangements being discussed, the resources available for disaster response formed part of the existing grant framework. He said that following extensive engagement with the National Treasury, it had been confirmed that no new funding would be made available specifically for this process. Apart from the national funds centralised at the Department’s head office, an agreement had therefore been reached that provinces may utilise a defined percentage of their existing grant funding, specifically under the Informal Settlements Upgrading Partnership Grant (ISUPG), to intervene in disaster incidents affecting between one and 50 households. The Department had also explored the possibility of reallocating some funding from the national level to provinces, but Treasury had advised that such an approach would not be permissible. As a result, it was relying on a ring-fenced percentage within the current grant framework, with a provision allowing for adjustments under exceptional circumstances. This arrangement was intended to support provinces and metropolitan municipalities in responding more effectively to disasters that impacted smaller numbers of households.
The Chairperson requested that either the DG or the DDG provide a summary on the decentralisation of emergency funding. He noted that the Committee had previously received a detailed presentation on this matter, but asked for a concise recap clarifying whether the emergency funding function remained centralised at the national level, or whether it had now been decentralised to provinces or municipalities.
Dr Moemi clarified that the matter he had just presented addressed the Committee’s question regarding the decentralisation of emergency funding. He said the MINMEC had resolved that the National Emergency Housing Disaster Response Fund would remain centralised at the national level. This meant that funding for significant and severe disasters affecting 51 households or more would continue to be administered directly by the national Department. However, in order to ensure that provinces and metropolitan municipalities were able to respond swiftly to smaller-scale incidents, the MINMEC had agreed on a mechanism to decentralise responsibility for minor disasters.
He said the key question had always been what funding provinces and metros should use when responding to disasters affecting between one and 50 households. To address this, MINMEC has approved the ring-fencing of a percentage of the ISUPG, which provinces and metros may now use specifically for emergency response within this threshold. This arrangement, he emphasised, empowered the “coalface” institutions -- those closest to affected communities -- to intervene timeously in minor disaster events.
He said the Department had explored the possibility of sharing or transferring a portion of the national disaster fund to the provinces, but Treasury had advised that this would not be permissible, as the fund was not appropriated in a manner that allowed transfers. It must therefore remain a national resource for major disaster response only.
Dr Moemi added that in situations where a province or metro exhausts its ring-fenced ISUPG allocation for minor disasters, the grant framework now includes a special condition permitting the transferring officer to consider increasing that percentage on a case-by-case basis. This consideration would take into account the number of disasters experienced in the province or metro, as well as the size of its ISUPG allocation, which differs across provinces.
He concluded by stating that the MINMEC had unanimously endorsed this arrangement as the first protocol to be implemented, with the understanding that it may be reviewed in the future. The current position was therefore that the emergency response for incidents affecting one to 50 households was decentralised to provinces and metros, while major disaster funding remained centralised at the national level.
The Chairperson requested that the Minister share her views on the potential use of the Housing Development Agency (HDA) in supporting emergency housing interventions. He emphasised that he was seeking her perspective, specifically on whether HDA could or should play a greater role in emergency housing.
Minister on emergency housing interventions
Minister Simelane said the Department was currently engaging with its entities to explore additional internal capabilities within the broader human settlements family that could be utilised to strengthen service delivery. As an example, she highlighted the assessment of informal settlements, an activity that the Department would ordinarily outsource. Instead, the Housing Development Agency (HDA) had been carrying out this function, and provinces were being encouraged to broaden their use of departmental entities beyond traditional roles to support human settlements delivery more holistically.
She said the Department had not yet considered the use of the HDA specifically for disaster response. However, some provinces were already utilising it for programmes such as mud house eradication and asbestos-related interventions. While these programmes were not always disaster-driven, they often arose from disaster conditions, and thus presented an opportunity for further internal strengthening. She indicated her openness to exploring the role of the HDA in disaster contexts, subject to careful assessment and the establishment of appropriate systems and processes.
She emphasised that as provinces improved their internal capacity, the reliance on the HDA tended to decrease in programmes such as the Breaking New Ground (BNG) and other alternative housing interventions. However, she maintained that the potential role of the HDA in supporting disaster-related work was worth examining in more detail.
Turning to the broader funding and governance framework, the Minister reiterated that the DG had addressed the Committee’s earlier concerns regarding the management of the disaster fund and the delegation of responsibilities to provinces. She stressed the importance of the updated framework, particularly in light of findings from the Auditor-General (AG), which revealed instances, especially at the municipal level, where grant funding intended for informal settlement upgrading had been misapplied toward temporary services such as ablution facilities and mobile toilets. The new measures were therefore intended to strengthen governance, ensure appropriate use of funds, and prevent the misuse of grant allocations.
The Minister also emphasised the need for vigilance to ensure that Temporary Residential Units (TRUs) provided for smaller-scale incidents (affecting 1 to 50 households) remained temporary and did not become permanent structures by default. She commented that in some cases, provinces had classified old incidents as newly occurring disasters, despite money having already been allocated for earlier events. In KwaZulu-Natal in particular, a large backlog remained from flood incidents dating back to 2020 and 2022, and the national Department continued to assist the province in addressing these.
The Minister expressed her appreciation to the Committee for providing an opportunity to present the Department’s state of readiness for the festive and broader summer season. She reaffirmed the Department’s commitment to strengthening disaster response capabilities across the country.
Provinces' state of readiness for floods and disasters
Limpopo
Dr Modjadji Malahlela,
Head of Department (HOD)
: Cooperative Governance, Human Settlements and Traditional Affairs (CoGHSTA), said that Limpopo’s profiling showed that thunderstorms and floods typically characterised the period between October and February. Capricorn, Mopani and Sekhukhune were the districts most susceptible to severe thunderstorms, although pockets of flooding occurred across the province. The most flood-prone districts were Vhembe and Waterberg. Between October 2024 and November 2025, the province had already experienced frequent floods and thunderstorms, and current patterns suggested that further flooding was likely during December and January.
She elaborated on the province’s previous response model, noting that concerns had previously been raised regarding the use of tents during disaster interventions. As a result, the province had begun procuring TRUs, which were distributed to district municipalities for rapid deployment during emergencies. Between 2021 and 2023, more than 100 TRUs had been procured. These structures were movable and were intended to remain district assets that could be reused once affected families repaired their homes or benefited from permanent solutions. However, in many instances, families required permanent structures, and districts were often unable to recover the TRUs. Despite this challenge, the TRU system had significantly improved the rapidity of the provincial response during severe weather incidents.
She reported that more than 4 000 households in Limpopo had been affected by disasters in a relatively short space of time, underscoring the increasing intensity of climatic events and the urgent need for proactive prevention measures. A breakdown included in the presentation showed that most incidents involved damaged roofs caused by thunderstorms, rather than complete structural collapse, hence the high demand for building repair materials. TRUs were used where structural integrity assessments indicated the need for full demolition or temporary relocation.
Dr Malahlela then highlighted recent flood incidents in Vhembe and Waterberg, illustrating the recurring nature of these events. She described the province’s two-phase response model -- immediate emergency relief followed by medium-term housing interventions. In the immediate phase, disaster centres and municipalities provided blankets, mattresses, temporary shelters and other relief items, while the South African Social Security Agency (SASSA) provided food parcels after conducting its own assessments. A detailed annexure listing the available stock of emergency materials across all districts had been attached to the presentation.
Regarding housing interventions, she confirmed that Limpopo still had a backlog of households requiring material for repairs, TRUs, or replacement housing -- an issue compounded by funding delays previously outlined by the national Department. This backlog placed vulnerable families at higher risk as the province entered the peak rainy season. Photographs included in the presentation illustrated TRUs already provided by the national Department in some areas, along with structural integrity assessments conducted to guide further interventions.
Dr Malahlela reported that Limpopo had approved its provincial contingency plan for the summer season in September. The plan was based on hazard analysis and established a multidisciplinary team with clearly defined institutional arrangements. The plan outlined coordination mechanisms from the community level up to the Provincial Disaster Management Centre (PDMC), ensuring that information flowed rapidly from community development workers (CDWs) and ward councillors to municipalities, and then to provincial authorities. Depending on the nature of the incident, reports were then directed to the relevant national Departments, including Human Settlements, where appropriate.
Rapid mobilisation protocols were also in place. In cases requiring evacuation, particularly during floods, the South African National Defence Force (SANDF) was deployed through established mechanisms. The province was fully registered with the SAWS for early warning alerts, and Limpopo’s disaster centres operated an active WhatsApp communication group used to disseminate warnings and coordinate responses in real time. The contingency plan included a detailed outline of available resources at provincial and district levels, which was attached to the presentation for the Committee’s reference.
Dr Malahlela added that both the PDMC and the district disaster management centres maintained continuous monitoring of conditions and were always prepared to activate Joint Operations Centres at either district or provincial level when required. She also addressed a matter previously raised by the Committee regarding temporary emergency accommodation sites. She confirmed that all districts in Limpopo had identified and prepared temporary emergency areas that could be activated immediately should evacuations become necessary. Although the province had not yet experienced an incident that required mass evacuation into these facilities, the infrastructure was fully in place, and districts were ready to deploy them at short notice.
She reported that at the provincial level, Limpopo had finalised a draft disaster management coordination framework aimed at ensuring quicker response times and strengthening coordination among the various sectors and stakeholders involved in disaster management. Building on ongoing work within the province, the Provincial Executive Council had also approved a centralised provincial revenue disaster contingency fund to enable a more rapid response during emergencies. It was this fund that had allowed the province to respond swiftly during the February floods, primarily in the roads, health and education sectors, where approximately R500 million had been released for urgent infrastructure interventions.
Dr Malahlela explained that the rationale for establishing this provincial contingency fund was grounded in the recognition that delays often occurred, even during emergencies, before funds became available from national processes. The province, therefore, needed to ensure that it had its own immediate resources to respond effectively, in line with the requirements of the Disaster Management Act. These provincial resources were intended to complement any additional allocations that may later come from the national government, depending on the severity and impact of the disaster.
She outlined the strategic challenges faced by the Limpopo Province in responding to disasters. She highlighted as a key concern the slow turnaround time in providing relief materials. She said that when districts previously maintained their own stock of emergency materials, particularly TRUs, the province had been able to respond far more rapidly. However, when a disaster struck, and procurement processes still had to be initiated, delays could occur at times when immediate intervention was critical. For this reason, the province recommended maintaining a minimum level of pre-positioned stock to enable swift relief, especially in difficult or life-threatening situations. This proposal aligned with the discussions held at the national level and the strategic direction communicated by the DG.
Dr Malahlela also identified challenges in the coordination processes between the National Disaster Management Centre (NDMC) and the National Treasury. The current financial decision-making system took time, and by the time funds were approved and released, the immediacy of the disaster may have passed, raising concerns about whether the intervention could still be classified as a disaster response. She stressed that disaster response, by definition, must be timely.
Another strategic challenge was related to the absence of a ring-fenced budget specifically dedicated to providing permanent houses for affected households. At present, provinces are required to rely on existing allocations to support families whose homes have been destroyed or severely damaged, even though these needs were not planned for and may far exceed available budgets. Given ongoing fiscal constraints, she emphasised that future planning must consider establishing a dedicated funding mechanism for permanent reconstruction where warranted.
She also referred to the importance of working closely with traditional leaders and municipalities to improve settlement planning and reduce risk exposure, particularly in relation to settlement location and development patterns, echoing issues raised earlier by the DDG.
Dr Malahlela recommended that the Portfolio Committee note the progress made in implementing the emergency housing programme, as well as Limpopo’s overall state of readiness to respond to potential disasters during the summer season.

Mpumalanga
Dr Bongane Ntiwane, Acting Chief Director: Development and Planning, COGTA, indicated that he was serving as the Acting Head of the Provincial Disaster Management Centre, and would be leading the presentation. He said the presentation would focus on the province’s state of readiness, preventative measures, human settlement interventions, challenges and recommendations.
He said Mpumalanga was highly prone to disasters throughout both winter and summer seasons, and that the province had adopted a summer contingency plan outlining readiness across all sectors, especially in high-risk areas. Since the start of the summer season, incidents have already been recorded. At the time of submitting the report, 28 houses had been damaged in Chief Albert Luthuli Municipality, a figure which had since risen to 35. He added that 87 houses had been affected in Dr JS Moroka, leaving 20 people displaced and accommodated at a community hall. In total, 127 houses had been affected since the beginning of the rainy season, excluding long-standing, unrepaired houses from previous disasters.
Working with the National Disaster Management Centre, the province had been using the disaster management grant for response and recovery, particularly to rehabilitate roads and bridges. This intervention had reduced the impact of recent events. He also reported that the MEC had convened the Provincial Intergovernmental Committee on Disaster Management to strengthen readiness.
He emphasised the daily weather alerts and early warnings received from the SAWS, which were circulated through emergency groups, ward structures, CDWs and a provincial app. He indicated that all disaster management centres remained on high alert. All joint operations committees had been activated since mid-November, and were meeting weekly to track incidents and responses. On humanitarian relief, he reported that stock levels were being replenished and distributed through district disaster centres, with additional procurement expected the following week. He added that TRUs were not included in provincial stock because they relied on the national Department.
He said the province continued to issue public awareness messages before, during and after disasters, and that CDWs were sharing information and reporting incidents. As part of risk reduction, the MEC had instructed all sectors to clear stormwater drainage systems, with commitments from Public Works, the South African National Roads Agency Ltd (SANRAL) and municipalities. The Expanded Public Works Programme (EPWP) and CWP workers were assisting with this work.
He said rehabilitation work followed the “building back better” approach by upgrading gravel roads to paving and raising low-lying bridges. The provincial flood management strategy had been approved by Cabinet, identifying high-risk areas, mainly in the Ehlanzeni District. Implementation had already started, focusing on information dissemination, capacity building, infrastructure resilience, land-use enforcement and clarifying sector roles.
Regarding human settlement interventions, he said the province relied on emergency housing funds. Between 2018 and 2023, many houses had been repaired, but from 2024 onwards, 2 326 affected households had been reported. The National Home Builders Registration Council (NHBRC) had been appointed to conduct assessments and costing, and service providers had been appointed to provide TRUs. However, he highlighted serious delays -- technical assessment reports for 1 629 houses were still outstanding, even though the new rainy season had begun. He gave examples of incomplete work, including slabs left without TRUs. He recommended urgent action from the NHBRC on outstanding assessments, the termination of a non-performing service provider, and expedited feedback on households affected in June and September. He emphasised that poorly constructed houses -- both government-built and self-built -- contributed significantly to repeated damage, due to sub-standard materials and lack of compliance. Municipalities had been asked to strengthen by-law enforcement and building standards.
He concluded by noting that resource mobilisation and timely assessments needed improvement, citing delays in the latest incident, where household lists were being submitted only that day. He warned that without reform, displaced families would remain in community halls indefinitely.

North West Province
Ms Kgomotso Mahlobo, HOD: Human Settlements, said that the Department’s disaster management approach was guided by the Disaster Management Act 57 of 2002, which obliges all spheres of government to maintain effective systems that focus on prevention, mitigation, and preparedness. She emphasised that the North West Department of Human Settlements was mandated to develop and implement relevant disaster management plans, particularly in line with Section 38 of the Act.
She reported that institutional arrangements were in place, with the Department participating in both provincial and district disaster management advisory forums. Two departmental nodal officials served on the provincial forum, while district directors were part of the district structures. She added that the Department participated in joint coordinating structures whenever they were activated.
She outlined the province’s hazard profile, noting that the North West was a semi-rural and peri-urban environment exposed to hazards such as summer thunderstorms, flash floods, heat waves, informal settlement fires, and failures of water and power infrastructure, especially during festive-season influxes. Vulnerabilities were particularly pronounced in informal settlements, peri-urban areas, and mining towns with significant service and housing backlogs.
Ms Mahlobo summarised the province’s state of preparedness, stating that the Department had mapped flash flood risk zones, upgraded drainage in settlement areas, conducted pre-season fire safety audits, carried out fire-risk awareness campaigns, and promoted heat-wave precaution and water conservation messaging. Hotspot areas had been pre-allocated emergency housing resources, and response teams were on standby to conduct incident assessments.
She said weather alerts, municipal reports of fire or flooding, or severe heat events triggered the response plan. Regarding disaster response and recovery, she noted that the Department participated in all relevant coordination structures and supported municipalities in applications for TRUs, a function currently lying with the national Department. She added that geotechnical studies were conducted for both TRUs and reconstruction and development programme (RDP) developments, and that municipal housing units were assisted in identifying mass-care centres for displaced families.
Providing an overview of the province’s risk and vulnerability profile, Ms Mahlobo said that the North West was prone to both natural and human-induced hazards, including floods, thunderstorms, sinkholes, and mining-related seismic activity. She outlined risk-reduction measures such as Section 38 disaster plans, provision of standard RDP houses, informal settlement upgrades, eradication of mud houses, proper environmental assessments before development, geotechnical studies, and continuous consumer education.
She then reviewed previous disaster incidents, noting gaps in TRU provision. Several municipalities had outstanding TRU needs due to contractors withdrawing or failing to deliver. In some instances, only a portion of the requested TRUs had been provided, leaving affected households stranded.
She highlighted several challenges, including inadequate disaster response equipment across government spheres, poor infrastructure maintenance, especially stormwater systems, underfunded disaster management centres, delayed costing of damaged infrastructure, poor coordination in integrated development planning structures, and failure to enforce building regulations and municipal bylaws. She emphasised that communities continued to occupy floodplains illegally, and many houses built with mud were structurally weak and easily damaged by rain.
She concluded with recommendations, including a request for the National Department of Human Settlements to fast-track the implementation of TRUs for affected households, prioritise funding for disaster-related areas, improve community participation and awareness campaigns, and ensure that all sector departments develop disaster management plans in accordance with Section 38 of the Disaster Management Act.

Gauteng
Mr Tshepo Motlhale, Head: Gauteng Provincial Disaster Management Centre (PDMC), said he would present on the province’s state of readiness for the rainy season, guided by current seasonal forecasts and recent early warning information from the SAWS.
He reported that Gauteng had been receiving several weather warnings over recent weeks, with a significant escalation in the last seven days, including alerts ranging from Level 4 to Level 9, the latter indicating highly destructive rainfall. In response, the province had reviewed its contingency measures and activated relevant structures.
He said that in terms of its legislative mandate, the Gauteng PDMC was responsible for coordinating and managing disasters in the province, supporting the provincial executive, the National Disaster Management Centre (NDMC) and municipalities. The Centre carried two key functions -- disaster management (Schedule 4A) and fire services (Schedule 4B) of the Constitution. The PDMC coordinated disaster management at the local government level (Section 44 of the Disaster Management Act) and provided secondary support to municipalities in line with Section 35, with further escalation to the national level where necessary.
On the seasonal forecast for 2025/26, he said that observations confirmed SAWS's projections of a La Niña year, associated with increased precipitation and heightened flood risk from November to March, and potentially beyond. He indicated that while preparing the provincial summer season contingency plan, submitted to the NDMC and shared with stakeholders, Gauteng had taken these forecasts into account and already observed impacts at various warning levels. A recent Level 9 warning had led to an incident in the City of Tshwane, where a vehicle had plunged into a low-lying bridge -- emergency services had been activated, and all passengers were successfully rescued. He used this example to underscore the importance of strengthening community engagement, training and education around flood risks.
In terms of impact analysis, he identified high-risk areas within the City of Tshwane (including Mamelodi, Soshanguve and Olievenhoutbosch), the City of Ekurhuleni, and the City of Johannesburg (notably Alexandra along the Jukskei Stream, and parts of Soweto along the Klip River). In the West Rand District, he highlighted Randfontein Local Municipality as particularly vulnerable due to sinkholes, noting that a state of disaster classification for that area had already been gazetted. He reported that previous disasters, including those classified in 2022, had caused significant infrastructural damage, and that certain bridges and other structures, already known as critical, continued to require ongoing monitoring and maintenance.
On the province’s preparedness, Mr Mothlale stated that during consultations with municipalities, Gauteng had developed flood contingency plans and broadened engagements to ensure adequate resourcing for rapid emergency response. The province had strengthened municipal capacity through support for specialised rescue functions such as swift-water rescue (particularly in metros) and had facilitated inter-municipal cooperation with districts. As an example, he mentioned the handover of a speedboat for water rescue to mid-Vaal municipalities in the previous financial year, and collaboration with an SA Rescue team based in the Vaal area. He indicated that fire and rescue services were also being capacitated with equipment and communications tools.
He confirmed that all municipalities in Gauteng had disaster management plans, and that the PDMC conducted periodic functionality assessments of municipal disaster management centres. Only one district had not yet established such a centre; a business case was being developed to enable support from the next financial year. He stressed that the contingency plans promoted an integrated and uniform approach to preparedness and response across the province.
On risk and hazard ranking, he reported that flash floods, thunderstorms, heatwaves, hailstorms, strong winds and occasional tornadoes all had a high likelihood and high impact, with tornado incidents in recent years leaving households homeless. Regarding early warnings, he explained that the PDMC had conducted a study of the effects of early warnings and identified dissemination gaps. The Centre received weather information from the SAWS via an integrated system that interprets alert levels and associated impacts, and this was then channelled through the provincial communications unit to municipalities and various media platforms. He indicated that Gauteng was enhancing this system through additional “intelligence capabilities” in collaboration with the Council for Scientific and Industrial Research (CSIR) and was developing a mobile application that would enable residents to view warnings and report emergencies directly.
Mr Mothlale described the institutional coordination structure under which various sector departments and entities support municipalities and enhance capacity. He outlined the disaster response architecture linking the PDMC and municipal disaster management centres (MDMCs), with clear escalation to the national structure when the province cannot cope. He referred to the example of the Eastern Cape, where national air-support capabilities had been activated. He noted a signed service-level agreement between the NDMC and the SANDF for air rescue.
He emphasised that the province was focusing not only on response, but also on prevention and mitigation, particularly for road and stormwater infrastructure. The PDMC’s response structure included defined escalation levels:
Level 0 for incidents that a municipality could contain;
Level 2 for incidents where municipalities request provincial assistance; and
Level 3 for incidents requiring activation of national structures.
He explained that the incident coordination process involved incident reporting, activation of primary responders, possible activation of a joint operations centre, repeated assessments, planning and implementation of rehabilitation and reconstruction measures, often in conjunction with the NDMC, including applications for disaster grants where necessary.
Outlining roles and responsibilities, he said that the PDMC was responsible for overall coordination, while COGTA led on central communication and liaison with municipalities. Municipalities retained primary responsibility for disaster response. The SAWS provided forecasts, updates and warnings; the Department of Water and Sanitation (DWS) supplied dam and river level data, with work underway to enable real-time data retrieval. He highlighted the role of trained disaster management volunteers and wardens, who assist with damage assessments and humanitarian support, and noted active cooperation with the SAPS's water wing and with non-governmental organisations (NGOs) providing relief. He added that internal sector departments, such as infrastructure development, health, education, roads and transport, and social development, provided functional services as part of the broader response system.
He then drew the Committee’s attention to several challenges requiring further intervention. Many municipalities, he said, struggled to finalise stormwater master plans, which hampers resilient infrastructure planning and mitigation. Some areas remained underdeveloped and lacked adequate infrastructure. In others, especially central business districts (CBDs), poor maintenance of stormwater systems and illegal dumping caused blockages. Although municipalities reported efforts to clear culverts and drains, he stressed that more work was needed, particularly as part of the provincial CBD revitalisation programme. He further noted that some human settlements projects continued to be developed on unsuitable land, including floodlines, often without adequate involvement of disaster management structures.
He reported that community responses to early warning messages could be delayed, with some residents reluctant to act promptly. The province was seeking to address this through public awareness initiatives and simulation exercises, especially in metros, involving swift-water rescue teams.
Turning to emergency housing, Mr Motlhale acknowledged that communities expected immediate support during emergencies. Gauteng’s current contingency arrangements included tents and the use of municipal community facilities. He pointed out that these measures were adequate for a limited time, but once displacement exceeded seven days, serious challenges arose. On behalf of the province, he requested that the response time for providing temporary emergency shelters be improved, and that this be done through closer coordination with other structures. He concluded by recommending that the presentation be noted.

Ms Bele said she had met the previous day with the Eastern Cape HOD to address the issues raised in Umtata. The initial disaster list in the area had contained approximately 2 400 affected households. Because the funds requested from the national government were insufficient to cover the full needs, the national Department had asked the province to clarify how many units it planned to construct. According to Ms Bele, the province had committed to building 600 units within the OR Tambo District, of which 400 had been allocated to the King Sabata Dalindyebo (KSD) Municipality.
She further reported that both the national and provincial governments had agreed that the OR Tambo District Municipality should re-examine the beneficiary lists across its three local municipalities, as ongoing changes to the lists had been identified as a major problem. She emphasised that the HOD had acknowledged that the beneficiary list frequently changed, and that this created confusion regarding who had truly been assisted.
Ms Bele added that the national DHS had requested a thorough review to ensure that all legitimately affected households, particularly those not yet assisted, could be prioritised. She indicated that the HOD had assured the national that he would return to the Provincial Disaster Management Centre to undertake this verification work with the municipalities.
She concluded by noting that around 300 households had already been assisted by the province and that the national government would contribute a further 600 units. As a result, the combined total of 1200 households in the OR Tambo District would be assisted by the end of the current financial year.
Ms Z Abader (MKP) said that before discussing plans, budgets, and financial constraints, it was necessary to confront the human reality that repeatedly brought the Committee together. She argued that families living along riverbanks -- from the Jukskei in Pretoria to informal settlements across the country -- did not reside there out of choice, but because government systems had failed them. Every rainy season, those families’ lives, dignity, and futures were put at risk.
She expressed frustration that the same issues were faced every year. She said that although financial constraints were acknowledged, limited budgets could not continue to serve as the excuse for the ongoing loss of life among poor households. She argued that the government always found money to respond after disasters -- funds for tents, emergency food parcels, body bags, and post-disaster clean-ups. Yet, somehow, it lacked sufficient funding for the prevention that would save lives. She stressed that preventative work must be prioritised, because floods and fires were predictable seasonal events, and continuing to respond only after the fact perpetuated an annual cycle of trauma.
Ms Abader questioned why communities remained in the same dangerous areas year after year, why high-risk riverbanks were not declared red zones, and why the state could compensate for damage after disasters but could not secure relocation and protection before disasters occurred. She stated that poor communities were consistently exposed to the highest levels of risk while the government debated budgets, and she emphasised that these people were not spreadsheet numbers but human beings who went to bed fearing whether rising rivers might destroy their homes. She stated that accountability was required, rather than excuses, sympathy, or discussions about budget constraints. She called for firm commitments, timelines, and concrete actions that would remove families from danger and restore dignity to long-ignored communities.
Ms Abader then asked whether the Department had explored implementing community education and risk-awareness programmes to help residents living along riverbanks understand the dangers and perhaps voluntarily relocate. She also asked what reasons had been identified for communities’ refusal or inability to move to safer housing when relocation options were offered, noting that Departments often reported that affected communities did not want to move.
Mr S Dithebe (ANC) said that other Members had already raised some of his concerns, but he wished to focus on the speed and agility of response teams across the different spheres of government. He referred to the threshold system, where disasters affecting 1 to 50 households fell under provincial responsibility, and 51 or more under national, and pointed out that while resources were centralised, functional responsibility was effectively devolved. He asked whether the national government ensured that best practices were shared among provinces, noting that some provinces, such as Gauteng, appeared more advanced in their use of technology and artificial intelligence. He emphasised that modern disaster management required data-driven decision-making, modelling, simulation, and rapid action, especially in anticipating and preventing disasters.
He pointed out that the severity of disasters differed across provinces due to population density, and therefore, learning across regions was essential. As an example, he referred to comments earlier in the meeting that some departmental staff had volunteered to support disaster operations during the festive season, which he described as commendable. However, he argued that this also highlighted the need for strong institutional memory and well-established internal capacity within all disaster management structures.
Mr Dithebe also raised concerns about settlements situated within floodlines. He said that in some cases, pressure from communities had resulted in municipalities undertaking in-situ upgrades, which inevitably left some households inside hazardous zones. He asked how national, provincial, and local government, together with Human Settlements, COGTA, Water and Sanitation, and the Department of Forestry, Fisheries and Environment, could collaborate structurally to prevent recurring flooding in these areas.
He referred to issues raised by Gauteng regarding the absence of stormwater master plans, noting that this contributed not only to flooding but also to the loss of rainwater that could otherwise be channelled productively. He argued that this demonstrated the need for a whole-of-government approach.
He also emphasised that disaster reporting should include cases where homes were not washed away but were nevertheless severely flooded, resulting in damage to furniture and personal belongings. He noted having witnessed such a case two weeks earlier. He observed that municipal attempts at remediation, such as using tractor loader backhoe (TLB) machinery to redirect water, were often insufficient or poorly planned.
He concluded by stressing that better preventative planning would reduce waste, improve outcomes, and reduce the burden on government resources. He reiterated the need for provinces to share best practices, especially regarding equipment for ward committee members and community workers who were often expected to inspect flooded areas without adequate protective gear. He asked that the Department and the provinces address these concerns.
Ms Bele said that many affected households had experienced flooding where their structures were not destroyed, but were inundated with water. She reported that in Umtata, these families were moved to mass care centres and placed in safer temporary accommodation. After about a week, most returned to their homes, meaning there was no need to provide temporary shelters, TRUs, or building materials, as what they required was immediate safety rather than reconstruction. The national Department, together with the province, OR Tambo District Municipality, and KSD Municipality, had coordinated the move to B&B facilities. Many of the affected households had expressed appreciation for the fast response and returned home once they had secured their belongings and cleaned their houses.
Ms Bele said that the households most severely affected were located next to the riverbank. KSD Municipality had offered additional land situated along the same riverbank to construct TRUs, but the national Department and the Minister had rejected this option, insisting that communities needed to be moved away from high-risk areas. She added that the National Treasury had allocated funds specifically to strengthen education and preventative measures, emphasising that the government must shift from merely responding to disasters to preventing them.
She stated that although suitable land had been identified further away for temporary residential units, and the province had constructed TRUs, the challenge had emerged during relocation. Many residents refused to move, arguing that they had lived in those areas for years. She highlighted that this situation also exists in Nelson Mandela Bay, where TRUs had been built in safer areas, but residents still preferred to remain at the riverbanks. According to her, similar resistance was found among communities living near the Jukskei Bridge.
Ms Bele concluded that while national, provincial, and municipal structures were investing in education and prevention, community resistance remained a major obstacle. She emphasised that the government continued to work with communities to find sustainable solutions, despite these challenges.
Dr Moemi said that the Department did in fact communicate proactively with communities living in low-lying, flood-prone areas. He confirmed that they carried out awareness campaigns, which was why they were able to speak with certainty about how various communities respond. He added that when the National Disaster Management Centre receives a warning from the SAWS, particularly warnings above Level 5, local communities in danger zones are informed. In many cases, this information was disseminated through physical means such as loud-hailing, alerting residents that a severe storm or strong winds were expected and advising them to relocate temporarily to municipal mass-care centres situated in safer, elevated areas such as community halls.
Many residents refuse to relocate, and he emphasised that their reasoning was understandable. People feared leaving their belongings unattended because they worried that these possessions, often the only ones they had, might be stolen in their absence. He pointed out, however, that staying behind did not protect their property either, because when flooding occurred, these same belongings were often destroyed regardless.
He reminded the Committee that this was not a new problem. He cited the example of the residents of Jukskei, explaining that the Lufhereng Phase 11 project in Soweto had originally been initiated specifically to provide housing for those living along the Jukskei River. According to him, around 70% of affected residents had agreed to be relocated and to accept housing in Lufhereng. However, a portion of the community had remained unwilling to move. These residents had argued that Lufhereng was too far from their workplaces, schools and churches, and that transport costs from the new location were higher. Their preference was to remain where they were, even though those areas were unsafe.
Addressing the suggestion that the government should forcefully remove residents from such hazardous zones, Dr Moemi stated that any eviction would need to comply with the law, particularly the Prevention of Illegal Eviction (PIE) Act. He said forceful removals were not legally permissible without a court order. While he believed that obtaining such a court order was possible, he emphasised that the decision to pursue this course of action lay with the City of Johannesburg and its political leadership.
Finally, Dr Moemi reaffirmed that the Department conducted ongoing education and risk-awareness campaigns in informal settlements. These activities were part of the Department’s formal performance targets, and it reports on them in its quarterly APP submissions to the Portfolio Committee, particularly those initiatives aimed at communities residing in low-lying, high-risk areas.
The Chairperson asked two follow-up questions before the officials left the platform. He observed that the TRUs being supplied across different localities did not appear to be the same or even similar. He queried why there were variations in design and construction between provinces. He also asked whether the Department had considered shifting to a permanent solution even in the event of a disaster, suggesting that instead of constructing TRUs, the Department might provide permanent houses immediately after a disaster for affected families.
Dr Moemi responded that the Department issued one broad specification for TRUs nationally, but deliberately avoided prescribing overly rigid designs, allowing some flexibility for provinces and contractors within the set parameters. The national specification included the minimum size of 30 square metres, requirements for alternative building technologies, the tensile strength of wall panels, foundation standards, roofing guidelines, and other structural elements. However, he said that contractors often exceeded specifications — for example, building 40 m² units at the cost of 30 m², or installing aluminium windows or interior insulation panels — and sometimes did so competitively when multiple contractors were working side-by-side on the same site. He commented that this occasionally caused communities to reject TRUs that met the minimum specification, but appeared inferior to more elaborate units built nearby. He added that the Department had encouraged provinces to ensure contractors stayed within the allowed specification, although identical designs across the entire country were not required.
Addressing the Chairperson’s question on permanent solutions, Dr Moemi confirmed that the Department was already exploring this option. He said a pilot programme was being finalised, in partnership with the Eastern Cape, to replace TRUs with permanent 40 m² houses immediately after major disasters. He said the Emergency Housing Response Fund would cover an amount equivalent to the cost of a TRU, while the remaining balance needed for a full 40 m² subsidised house would be funded through the Human Settlements Development Grant. He confirmed that the Eastern Cape had already amended its business plan to incorporate this model, and that bilateral discussions were scheduled with the province to determine implementation mechanics. He added that the National Treasury had given in-principle support, and that the pilot could begin with a small number of houses in the current financial year, expanding into the next year. He concluded that this approach could be transformative in reducing duplication of expenditure and providing long-term, permanent solutions for disaster-affected families.
Mr Dithebe indicated that the DG had responded only to the portion of his earlier question dealing with community education and the reluctance of residents to relocate from flood-prone areas. He said the main thrust of his question concerned the standardisation of best practices across provinces. He clarified that although it was understandable that Gauteng possessed more human and material resources than other provinces, he was interested in whether the national Department ensured that effective practices were shared, even if resources themselves were not.
He emphasised that, with increasing reliance on artificial intelligence and data-driven decision-making, it should be possible to track weather-related incidents in real time and use that information to guide interventions, particularly in high-risk areas such as Jukskei, in a more uniform and technologically informed manner.
He also raised the matter of municipal by-laws, stating that without strict enforcement, especially at the level of local municipalities, many of the problems under discussion would persist. He cautioned that municipalities’ failure to apply and enforce by-laws undermined national and provincial efforts, and ultimately resulted in limited resources being repeatedly used to respond to preventable disasters.
He also expressed concern about the declining real value of the ISUPG allocation in the outer years of the medium-term expenditure framework (MTEF), noting that if the same grant had to be used to fund disaster-related interventions, it would become even more difficult to address long-standing issues such as informal settlement upgrading.
Dr Moemi explained that the Department had begun using technology extensively to map low-lying areas. He reported that, in collaboration with the Housing Development Agency, they were using drone footage to map all informal settlements. They had introduced polygon technology to count the number of households in each informal settlement, assess their size, and analyse hydrological studies to determine whether settlements were located in low-lying zones or within the 100-year flood line. This work, he said, enabled the Department to determine which informal settlements could be upgraded in situ, and which could not.
The DG stated that as a result of these technological advances, the Department could now say with confidence how many informal settlements existed, how large they were, what their development status was, and what type of intervention each required. This information was increasingly being used for planning purposes.
He acknowledged that there was still scope to use additional technologies and that technology continued to improve daily. However, he cautioned that the uptake and deployment of new technologies required significant resources, and the newer the technology, the costlier it tended to be. He explained that the Department’s approach was therefore one of balancing innovation with affordability.
The Chairperson noted that the meeting was moving towards its conclusion and invited the provinces to make their final remarks. g. He first called on the Northern Cape to check whether they wished to add any final comments. After confirming there were no further inputs, he proceeded to Limpopo, where there were also no closing remarks. He then moved on to the North West.
Ms Mahlobo said the North West Province really appreciated the help that it had been receiving from the National DHS and looked forward to working with them to deal with the expected disasters.
Dr Malahlela also wished to place on record the Limpopo province’s appreciation for the opportunity to present. She said that building on the work initiated by the national Department, Limpopo was committed to working closely with the national team to clear the existing backlogs and to ensure full readiness for the upcoming disaster season.
Mr Ntiwane aligned Mpumalanga with the other provinces in acknowledging the value of the engagement. He said that the province remained committed to working with the National Department to address outstanding issues and ensure readiness for disasters. He added that the province would continue to intensify its public awareness programmes and undertake relocations where necessary. He said they were also monitoring dam levels, recognising the flooding risks when dams exceed their capacity. He assured the Committee that the province would continue supporting communities and strengthening collaboration with the National Department.
The Chairperson thanked the Members and the provincial and national delegations for their participation. He highlighted several key takeaways from the meeting. He said that the Director-General had been asked to explore a permanent solution for disaster response, rather than relying solely on TRUs, and that the DG had indicated a pilot project was already underway in the Eastern Cape. He reminded the meeting that the Committee had previously requested provinces not to use a uniform TRU design, but to ensure that TRUs were habitable, as some units built in the past were of such poor quality that they appeared unfit for human occupation. He added that other provinces were constructing TRUs that were of a much higher standard, which made residents reluctant to move out of them because they appreciated the improved quality.
The Chairperson noted that the CFO had outlined the backlogs across all provinces and the process being implemented to address them. He acknowledged that provinces had raised several concerns. He stated that the Committee trusted that the DHS’s ongoing engagements with them would allow these issues to be addressed so that all stakeholders remained aligned.
Overall, the Chairperson said that the Portfolio Committee was satisfied with the readiness of the different provinces for the rainy season, especially regarding disaster response measures. However, he emphasised that backlogs remained a concern, warning that an intervention could no longer be regarded as an “emergency” if affected families had to wait six to eight months for assistance. He expressed appreciation for the proposed pilot aimed at delivering permanent structures within one to two months, noting that such an approach could help save resources while providing swift relief.
Next week, four provinces will brief the Committee on their state of readiness for floods and disasters -- the Eastern Cape, Free State, KwaZulu-Natal and the Western Cape.
The Chairperson then thanked the Members, the Director-General and his delegation, and the provincial representatives for their participation. He expressed appreciation for the attendance of the MECs from Limpopo and the Northern Cape, and noted with regret that the MEC from Gauteng had struggled to join the meeting. He further acknowledged his concern regarding the absence of the MEC from Mpumalanga, stating that the problem should be formally recorded and would be taken forward.
The meeting was adjourned.
