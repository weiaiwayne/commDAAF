Meeting Summary
The Portfolio Committee on Basic Education convened in Parliament for a roundtable workshop to be introduced, inducted and orientated on the basic education sector. The Minister of Basic Education said there should not be an antagonistic relationship between the executive and Parliament, and that the two should work symbiotically because Parliament played an important role of oversight, and the executive plays the role of delivering the service. It was important for the quality of the service that the executive delivers, that there was oversight over the work that it does.
The 7
th
Administration had done some work to identify the key priorities for the next five years. In the next few weeks, the Department would put together a five-year strategic plan and an annual performance plan, and would present it to Parliament. However, there were processes in the system that the Department was concerned about, including the literacy levels in the country, as well as the learning and teaching time in schools, so the Department would need the help of the Committee to protect learning and teaching time. The learners spend 200 days in school, and it must be ensured that in those 200 days, seven hours were spent in the classrooms with uninterrupted teaching time. The Department needed the Committee to ensure that learning and teaching time in schools was being protected during its oversight visits.
The Department was also embarking on a project to eradicate unsafe school infrastructure, including pit toilets and overcrowding in schools in metropolitan areas, to release them from the pressure. Linked to all of this, the Department was also looking at reducing its enhanced focus on the learner without a focus on the teacher, as it wanted to ensure that teachers were also safe in schools and treated with dignity.
The Committee heard presentations from the education departments of the nine provinces of South Africa. Members welcomed the presentations, but raised major concerns about the infrastructure budgets, school safety, the nutrition programmes, Early Childhood Development (ECD), and the existence of pit latrines in schools. They urged the provincial departments to find solutions to these issues, as they affected the learning and teaching time in schools and the well-being of learners and teachers.
The Members also stressed the need for the provincial departments to deal with the administrative, structural and financial challenges that they have, including the high vacancy rates of teachers in schools, the inability to fund the compensation of employees, the lack of infrastructure for ECD, representation in senior management positions within the provincial departments, the need to address unauthorised, fruitless, and wasteful expenditure, as well as prioritising the inclusion of agriculture in the school curriculum from grade R level.
The Committee urged the provincial departments to partner with the private sector in their respective provinces to assist with some of the programmes they wanted to implement, instead of depending only on their budget allocations.
Meeting report
Opening remarks
The Chairperson welcomed the Minister and Deputy Minister of Basic Education, the provincial Departments of Basic Education, the Chairpersons of the provincial Portfolio Committees on Basic Education, the leadership of Umalusi and the Southern Association of Independent Schools (SAIS), as well as other officials accompanying the Department to the induction workshop. The workshop was intended to comprehensively empower the Portfolio Committee on Basic Education. After the elections, there was anxiety among all Members of Parliament because they were entering uncharted territories, but they were now elated that a Government of National Unity (GNU) had been formed, and Parliament could establish the required structures to fulfil its social contract with the public.
Through the induction workshop, the Committee wanted to get a comprehensive lesson on the basic education space because it wanted to start with its oversight work. It would sit and outline its strategy and develop a mechanism, structure, and framework for its oversight work, understanding that it oversees the work of the provinces, hence it saw the need to invite the provinces to present the status of basic education in their respective constituencies.
The Committee and South Africans at large were concerned about various matters, including the quality of the curriculum content, the infrastructure of the schools in the provinces, the slow pace of integrating artificial intelligence (AI) and information communication technology (ICT) in schools, the safety of learners and teachers in schools, the quality of the nutritious meals provided in schools, and whether the schools were producing learners that would be able to compete in a rapidly changing world. The Committee had raised these issues in its invitation to the Department and the provinces. The Committee had also invited the Chairpersons of the provincial Portfolio Committees of Basic Education because it understood the concurrent nature of basic education, and when it did oversight in the provinces, it would be working with the provincial Portfolio Committees so that they did not duplicate their work, but worked as a team.
The Committee was determined to hold the executive accountable and to ensure that finances were spent as they should be. The Committee would also ensure that the public was informed, and that the public participates in the work of both Parliament and the executives.
The Chairperson noted apologies from Members and said that the programme would be amended for day two of the workshop, as the Department would be allowed to present on section B in the morning, and section C would be presented after lunch. The programme of the workshop was adopted.

The provinces were the true interface between communities, districts and the learners and teachers, more than the national Department of Basic Education (DBE). Provincial DBE presentations
Eastern Cape Education Department
Ms Nokuthula Ngcingwana, on behalf of the Head of Department (HOD), presented the Eastern Cape Education Department’s (ECED's) profile, including its policy imperatives, shape and size, the overview of its financial performance, learner performance and the education system’s transformation plan. She said the Eastern Cape Education Department was focused on the basic education sector priorities and the action plan 2029 towards the realisation of schooling 2030. The ECED was looking to improve learning outcomes in language, mathematics and science as measured in grades 3, 6, 9, and 12, to ensure full access to compulsory schooling, decrease grade repetition and retention, improve access to further education and training (FET) colleges, and improving the quality of Grade R.
It also aimed to:
Increase teacher supply and improve teacher utilisation (including post-provisioning);
Improve teacher development and job satisfaction;
Increase curriculum coverage;
Ensure a minimum set of books, materials and equipment;
Improve school management, funding and governance;
Ensure adequate physical infrastructure;
Ensure learner well-being, inclusive education, and community linkages; and
Improve district functionality.
Mr Qaphela Luthuli, Chief Director: Human Resource Management and Development, ECED, said the budget allocation for 2024/25 amounted to R44.441 billion, from the revised estimate of R41.190 billion, and increased by 3% due to a constrained envelope. In 2024/25, the compensation of employees (CoE) budget increased by 7.5%, which may be attributed to additional funding received for the wage agreement, post-provisioning norms, and the carry-through costs for implementing the approved annual recruitment plan. The goods and services budget declined drastically in 2024/25 by 22.6%, to R3.590 billion from a revised estimate of R4.635 billion in 2023/24, mainly due to budget cuts relating to fiscal consolidation.
Own revenue collection for the 2024/25 financial year was budgeted at R104.066 million. As at the end of July 2024, the revenue collected was R44.567 million, which represented 93.7% of the projections, resulting in an under-collection of R2.981 million for the period under review. However, measured on a straight line, the Department had already collected 42.8% of the total budget.
The Department had an allocated budget of R42.441 billion for the 2024/25 financial year. It had projected to spend R14.794 billion for the first four months, but had spent R14.809 billion, recording overspending on projections of R14.361 million. The underspending was reported in all the programmes except for infrastructure development and examination and education services, which overspent by R149.671 million and R14.500 million respectively due to the payment of accruals and payables.
In terms of the Education Systems Transformation Plan (ESTP) 2024-2029, the ECED’s five transformation pillars include capacity building, economic drive, 4
th
and 5
th
Industrial Revolution, inclusivity and social cohesion, as well as infrastructure development. The ESTP was the blueprint to enhance service delivery innovations and contribute meaningfully to a diverse, evolving economy. The Department was in the process of reengineering its digital strategy with robotics and e-learning programmes in schools, to be on par with the 5
th
Industrial Revolution.
Education was a social matter and cut across all other departments. The Department’s core business was teaching and learning, but many social ills spill over from communities, having an adverse effect on teaching and learning. The Department had already formed memorandums of understanding (MOUs) with various departments to address overlapping matters. It was one of the main job creation sectors and would continue to use its skills development funds and grants for employment opportunities. As a sector, the Department would annually resource maritime schools and agriculture schools to ensure they contribute to the ocean economy and the agriculture sector of the province.
Free State Education Department
Mr Tshwarelo Malakoane, HOD, Free State Department of Education, said the vision of the Department was to provide an education system that was free, compulsory, universal, and equal for all children of the province. Its mission was to be a department that strives to ensure the progressive realisation of universal schooling, improving the quality of education, and eliminating disparities among Free State citizens.
The province makes up 5.4% of the 13 439 683 learners in the country, and has 4.1% of the 24 836 schools. Over the past five years, as the province has strived to ensure that educational resources reach all learners, as the number of farm schools has decreased from 163 to 57. Independent schools had increased from 77 to 84, while the number of public schools -- which include farm schools -- had shown a decrease from 903 to 880. In total, schools in the Free State had decreased from 1 145 to 1 023.
Quintile 1 schools were those located in the poorest communities, while Quintile 5 schools were in the affluent areas. Quintiles 1 to 3 schools made up 84% of the schools, with 85% of all learners (591 607) in public schools. Thabo Mofutsanyana education district had the highest number of schools (163), with the highest proportion of learners (46%) in Quintile 1 schools, followed by Lejweleputswa with 59 schools and 23% of learners. Motheo had the highest number of Quintile 5 schools, with 31 schools and 54% of learners, and Thabo Mofutsanyana had 7 Quintile 5 schools and 8% of all Quintile 5 learners. Xhariep had one Quintile 5 school, with the lowest proportion of learners at 0.54%.
Mr Malakoane said Early Childhood Development (ECD) centres had increased by 161, from 1 249 to 1 410. In the second year of the Department taking over the ECD centres, 14 316 children benefited from the subsidy grant, and 54 456  children were funded from the equitable share, as agreed in the Service Level Agreement (SLA). A total of 3 546 ECD practitioners and 1 398 matrons had received stipends in 2023, and in 2024, a total of 4 073 practitioners and 1 398 matrons received stipends.
ECD practitioners were trained in the five learning areas of the National Curriculum Framework
(NCF) during the first quarter of the financial year. 596 (99.3%) Grade R practitioners were trained on Curriculum Assessment Policy Statement (CAPS) content, targeting home languages, mathematics and life skills during the June holidays, and 695 (115.8%) were trained on visual perceptual skills. Infrastructure had succeeded in upgrading 18 existing ECD centres to make the learning and teaching environment conducive, while 23 ECD services had benefited from the maintenance grant under the Elementary Education Development (EED) directorate.
The province has made strides in implementing the framework for "Skills for a Changing World" by expanding the training of both educators and learners to respond to emerging technologies, including the internet of things, robotics, and artificial intelligence. The technical occupational subjects were offered in eight Schools of Skill in eight towns of the province, with a total of 2 608 learners enrolled in the schools. Coding and robotics were now taught as a subject in selected grades, and DBE had approved the curriculum. Teachers were trained, and schools were provided with coding and robotics kits and laptops. Coding and robotics competitions were held for participating schools. A total of 23 secondary schools offered coding and robotics as a subject in Grades 8 and 9. In 100 primary schools, it was implemented as a co-curricular subject.
Gauteng Education Department
Mr Matome Chiloane, MEC for Education in Gauteng, said the provincial Department had received a clean audit in the previous financial year and in the current financial year, which showed that the province was being stabilised. The key issues that were a challenge in the province included unfunded mandates, and school safety had been a pressing issue, but there were no high-risk schools, but rather high-risk areas, which affected the schools. As part of its strategy to address this, the Gauteng Education Department deployed security entities to protect the schools and learners, and that programme yielded some results, but there were some challenges because some areas were difficult.
The Department also had a reorganisation of schools programme which had three components, including the Schools of Specialisation (SOS) which were informed by economic corridors, and which had been successful because some of the learners had gone on to compete in the world. The key thing about the SOS programme was their partnerships with the leading original equipment manufacturers (OEMs), such as BMW, partnering with a school in Soshanguve and donating a brand new X3 so that the learners could learn how to dismantle and fix it. These were high-performing schools that produced the best results in Matric.
He said school infrastructure was causing him a headache, because Gauteng was a growing area, and on average, it had about 500 people migrating from different provinces, meaning it was growing at a rate of about 250 000 people yearly. This puts a lot of pressure on the system, and schools were not built fast enough in the province due to budget constraints. The schools in Gauteng were oversubscribed, and the Gauteng population had a high confidence in the public education system over the private schooling system.
Mr Rufus Mmutlana, HOD, said the Gauteng province was also struggling with its budget allocation like the other provinces, but they ensure that they spend the funds allocated to them on their intended purposes. Gauteng was the second biggest education system in the country after KZN, with more than 15 million pupils. Given these huge learner numbers, the province finds itself under pressure to provide education services, and infrastructure was crucial for the province to provide those services.
Mr Albert Chanee, Deputy Director-General (DDG): Strategic Planning Management, said the Gauteng Education Department’s vision was to ensure every learner feels valued and inspired by its innovative education system. Its mission was to provide functional and modern schools that enable quality teaching and learning to protect and promote the right of every learner to quality, equitable, and relevant education.
Regarding the size and shape of Gauteng education, he said that in the 2024 academic year, 2 737 596 learners were enrolled across the sector in Gauteng schools. This showed a 69.6% increase in public schools over the period from 1995 to 2024, with an average annual increase of 2% per annum. Learner enrolment in public ordinary schools (POS) from Grades 1 to 12 had increased from 1 310 080 in 1995 to 2 342 448 in 2024. The learner-educator ratio in PO primary schools had changed from 35.9% in 2011 to 33.8% in 2024. The PO secondary schools experienced an increase from 30.1% in 2011 to 34.4% in 2024. There was a great demand for infrastructure to reduce overcrowding in the schools.
The Gauteng Department of Education's (GDE's) strategic goals for 2024-2029 included Early Childhood Development, the promotion of quality education across all classrooms and schools, the creation of safe schools that embody social cohesion, patriotism and non-violence, changing the education landscape to accelerate relevant and quality learning, and to address the needs of Gauteng youth through development programmes and increased youth employability.
The key programmes that were being delivered this year include:
Expansion of pre-grade registration;
Grade R practitioner training through a donor consortium;
Early grade reading programmes;
Secondary school support programme for Grades 10 – 12;
Schools of specialisation;
Teacher training;
Focus on underperforming schools; and
Building of 18 schools by the Department.
In terms of the budget trends over the 2024/24 Medium Term Expenditure Framework (MTEF), Mr Chanee said the school funding norms set a macro target of at least an 85:15 split between personnel and non-personnel in the education allocation, excluding conditional grants. Currently, the split between personnel and non-personnel was 75:25. Five percent was earmarked for delivery improvements and quality interventions. The Department’s budget had increased from a main appropriation of R59.7 billion in 2022/23, to R63.4 billion in 2023/24, showing a rand value increase of R3.6 billion which translated to a percentage increase of 6.2%. The Department’s budget would grow at an annual average rate of 4.3%, from R63.4 billion in 2023/24 to R67.6 billion in 2025/26. The 2024/25 budget totalling R65.8 billion was composed of the equitable share allocation (94%) and conditional grants (6%).
Regarding the audit outcomes of the GDE in 2023/24, he said it had sustained an unqualified audit opinion on financial statements, with no material findings in the audit of predetermined objectives and compliance. The financial statements were free from material errors. There were material corrections due to embedded controls and adequate reviews over supporting schedules to the annual financial statements (AFS). The annual performance report (APR) was free from material misstatements due to embedded controls and adequate reviews over supporting schedules to the report. An emerging risk was identified due to the Department’s conditional grant to build 18 schools. This indicator would need to be included in the APR from 2024-25 up to the completion of the projects.
Although material non-compliance was not identified, controls within supply chain management (SCM) had not yet been fully established to avoid incurring irregular expenditure. The GDE had further identified that the bid evaluation committee did not utilise the full terms of reference when considering the bid, which could lead to unacceptable bids in terms of Preferential Procurement Policy Framework Act (PPPFA) Regulation 1. Controls to avoid irregular expenditure should be implemented. The Department should continue its efforts to reduce irregular expenditure incurred, and continue to take appropriate action against instances of irregular expenditure investigated.
In conclusion, he said the GDE would continue to deliver quality education despite several factors which were forcing a change in the shape of schooling, including:
Demography – rapid population growth in developing countries was increasing demand for education, while the developed world’s ageing population would require lifelong learning.
Digital technology – the increased volume of knowledge; the ease of access to it; and transformed communication, collaboration, and connectivity.
Economic recession – education budgets across the developed world were contracting, while developing countries had no possibility of the kind of investment in public services historically enjoyed by the developed world.
Globalisation – communities, economies, and education itself were now globally connected.

Provincial Education Department's responses
Eastern Cape
Mr Luthuli responded on the Alfred Nzo District school issue, and said the province was looking at infrastructure by considering population trends before erecting new infrastructure in an area. One of the determining factors was that a school must be able to maintain a specific number of learners for at least a five-year period. The establishment of this new school in the Alfred Nzo District was because the district had arable land and good climatic conditions. The Eastern Cape was also introducing the cannabis story, so it also wanted to harvest on that, which was why the Alfred Nzo District was seen as one of the best areas where a school could be established.
The absenteeism of educators was regulated by section 8, but there was also a policy on Incapacity Leave of 2006, which operates differently from the Department of Basic Education to other sector departments. In basic education, when an educator was on temporary incapacity leave or long term incapacity leave, curriculum delivery compelled the school to replace them with a substitute teacher. Ordinarily, when a declaration was done, provision for those instances were made, so there was no such problem in the Eastern Cape where educators were on prolonged sick leave, and no substitute teachers were available in their absence. At every stage where a teacher was on incapacity leave, a substitute teacher was appointed, so teaching and learning were not affected.
Regarding the replacement of the ageing population of educators, he said the ECED did not have a problem replacing educators. This also included afternoon classes, weekend classes, winter school, spring school, and incubation sessions that would be in place, so with all of that, the target of the province was nothing less than an 81% pass rate. In terms of the model that it had implemented, it should be able to reach the target. In that plan, a detailed presentation spoke to the targets for each of the districts, circuits, schools, subjects, etc.
Unauthorised, fruitless and wasteful expenditure was a challenge for the Department, and it had set up a team between itself and the Treasury to investigate all these aspects, including the latest audit outcomes received by the Department. It was developing an audit implementation plan, and the Committee could rest assured that it was taking the matter seriously.
Regarding the skills development process for the energy sector, he said the Department was in the process of developing a human resource development plan that was related to renewable energy, especially in the space of hydrogen development, which would yield a lot of opportunities for young people. However, the skills base and the skills development level in the province were very low. To improve, the Department must interact with the higher education institutions, especially the universities and TVET colleges in the provinces. That process was already taking place, and there were also ongoing engagements with Sasol and other stakeholders in that field to develop a strategy for the renewable energy sector.
North West
MEC Motsumi confirmed that the advertisement for the HOD post had been signed off and was in the Office of the Premier as per the cost containment protocols, and it should be out by next week. The time frames were clear to say the new HOD must start at least by the beginning of November. The table of vacancies that was presented before the Committee was from the human resources (HR) department and was with the Office of the Premier, as there had been a concern regarding the vacancies in grade R not appearing on the table, but the province had no vacancies in terms of grade R posts. Four provinces were affected by the National Qualifications Framework (NQF) level diploma, and the unfortunate part was that the NQF level was not sufficient to qualify a person as a teacher, and the Department was clear in that it would not subject its learners to educators who were not qualified.
The mistake made by the Department was not appointing them as grade R practitioners, instead of as grade R teachers. This was a sensitive matter before the Council of Education Ministers (CEM), and had been discussed in the previous Administration, where the CEM had decided that it must go before the Bargaining Council. The Department was still waiting for the Bargaining Council, but it was continuously engaging the educators and encouraging them to apply for bursaries so that they could get their qualifications, to ensure that in the end, it became a win-win situation.
In terms of the temporary teachers, the Department was employing a policy where every three months, the teachers were absorbed by the system, but this depended on the principals of the schools because the Department could not make teachers permanent without a full report from the principals condoning these teachers.
Regarding the audit outcomes, the post-audit action plan was monitored weekly by the HOD, and to improve its audit outcomes, the Department appointed an audit committee which would monitor and intervene on issues of compliance. The mismanagement of funds was mostly from the schools, their principals and SGBs. The Department was supporting the principals and SGBs through capacity workshops to teach them the procedure of ensuring that procurements were done diligently.
Regarding the cross-border issue between the North West and the Northern Cape, the Department would start with the demarcation processes, and take note of its own proposals and see how both provinces could assist each other on the matter to ensure that it did not repeat itself. Regarding the infrastructure curriculum, some stakeholders had already stated their support, and that was how these incubation processes helped the province survive. Vodacom had donated over 400 laptops for learners, and they would be donated to the subject advisors so that they could have tools of trade. The Lekgotla was producing results through these partnerships, and the gaps were being closed.
The organisational structure was being reviewed because it did not speak to the needs of the Department, and had created serious tension among the employees because it was more clustered at the corporate level, but at the districts where service delivery happened, the human resources were limited. This was why the Department was trying to close the gaps where necessary to ensure that it delivered on its mandate.
Western Cape
Mr Walters said a disciplinary process was being followed regarding the Bellville Hoerskoel matter, and it would unfold in time. He accepted the points that were made by Members regarding representation in the organogram of the Department, noting that there were three members of African descent in the management team, and that there was much better representation at the level of professionals inside the Department. He acknowledged that the Department could do much better in terms of representation.
The Department did random drug testing and searching, as well as search and seizures at the schools that were identified as high-risk schools. Counselling support was also offered to learners via district psychologists and social workers. Social workers also made referrals to public health centres, and they had recently been trained to accompany learners to court in cases that ended up in court. Extra safety personnel were being deployed to high-risk schools.
The Department decided where it would build new schools based on growth, because the emigration of people in the province was not only to urban areas, but also to rural areas such as Saldanha Bay and Malmesbury. Regarding the sustainability risk, he said the point he was trying to make was that over time, as the numbers increased, the Department would be given money to service the sustainability risk, but the risk would push the learner-teacher ratio down. However, the actual risk would be that the ratio could go up because CoE was not financed enough.
Because of the nature of the province and the fact that the quintile system was based on areas, 75% of the schools were in quintiles 4 and 5, but many of these schools did not belong in quintile 4, as many of those in quintile 4 belonged in quintiles 1, 2, and 3. The quintile system currently looks at the areas to group the system, but if it looked at the learners and the homes that they came from, then the majority of the learners belonged to quintiles 1 to 3, and that was a disadvantage in the province when it came to how money was distributed throughout the system.
Regarding scholar transport, the province had 63 000 learners on scholar transport, and they travelled daily on 1 040 routes across the system. The major key was to line up local government before all the approvals were given. The Department would provide a report to the Committee regarding ICT integration in schools.
The Chairperson said the Committee would send additional questions to the provinces in writing, but the provinces must report back to the Committee on those questions.
Deputy Minister’s concluding remarks
Deputy Minister Mhaule thanked the provinces for availing themselves for the induction workshop and presenting their information to the Committee. She also thanked the Committee for the invitation and assured the Members that the Committee would have the Department’s full cooperation throughout the 7
th
Administration.
Chairperson’s concluding remarks
The Chairperson thanked the Deputy Minister and the provinces, noting that first impressions last and that she had hope that the provinces would work together well with the Committee. She said the Committee did not accept the apology from the MEC of the Western Cape, because it was unfair of him to miss the workshop because he was going to attend a CEM, as the other MECs were also going to attend the CEM and yet they had still availed themselves for the workshop and had travelled all the way from other provinces.
She thanked the provinces and added that seeing women in leadership positions in their respective provinces was refreshing, especially during Women’s Month. However, a lot still needs to be done to put women in these kinds of positions. She also thanked the provincial portfolio committee chairpersons for availing themselves for the meeting, and emphasised the importance of the national assembly and the National Council of Provinces (NCOP) working together on the oversight function.
The meeting was adjourned.
