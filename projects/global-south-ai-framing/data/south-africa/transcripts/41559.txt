Meeting Summary
Video
The Committee held an engagement with the Human Sciences Research Council (HSRC), the Academy of Science of South Africa (ASSAf), and the South African Council for Natural Scientific Professions (SACNaSP) on their 2025/26 Annual Performance Plans. The entities of the Department of Science, Technology and Innovation were questioned on a wide range of issues, including strategy, funding, digital innovation, regulatory functions, and public engagement.
Members asked the HSRC about strategies for talent development, infrastructure investment, transparency in research for vulnerable communities, climate change, and the role of digital tools such as artificial intelligence (AI) and chatbots. Concerns were expressed about the need for a measurable theory of change to ensure research contributed to reducing inequality and poverty, rather than remaining academic. Questions were raised about the HSRC’s capacity to address AI’s impact on jobs and data sovereignty, and how research would engage affected communities during transitions, particularly in the mining sector. Members also sought clarity on the dissemination of research findings, influence on policy, and the sustainability of initiatives in the context of constrained public resources.
ASSAf was asked about the sustainability of its science engagement activities, mechanisms for evaluating policy advice impact, the balance between scholarly publishing and strategic priorities, and digital equity for underserved communities. Clarity was also sought on the distinct roles of ASSAf and SACNaSP, and on ASSAf’s goals in hosting the Science20 event at the G20 Summit.
Members questioned SACNaSP about cybersecurity, professional development, public awareness of natural sciences, and impact evaluation. They also raised concerns about infrastructure deficits, registration backlogs, funding reliance, and the sustainability of its surplus.
Across all entities, they urged stronger collaboration with Sector Education and Training Authorities (SETAs), alternative funding strategies, internal cost-cutting, and a focus on employment creation and beneficiation. The Committee encouraged proactive efforts to support local economic empowerment and improve science accessibility, while ensuring that research had a tangible societal impact.
Meeting report
Opening remarks
The Chairperson said that the meeting had been convened to consider the five-year strategic plans and annual performance plans (APPs) of the entities under the Committee’s oversight: the Human Sciences Research Council (HSRC), the Academy of Science of South Africa (ASSAf), and the South African Council for Natural Scientific Professions (SACNaSP).
The meeting had become necessary due to recent changes to the parliamentary programme, which had disrupted the Committee’s original scheduling. She expressed regret that it had not been possible to engage with all entities before the conclusion of the previous term, and extended an apology for this. The strategic plan had recently been finalised, and the Committee had met the previous week to deliberate on it. Engagements with the Department and its entities were described as productive, with effective oversight having taken place.
The Chairperson urged the entities presenting to adhere strictly to the allocated time slots. In previous terms, time constraints had resulted in the Committee being unable to deal with all agenda items, especially during question-and-answer sessions. The agenda would begin with a briefing on the strategic and annual performance plans of the HSRC, followed by ASSAf and then SACNASP.
Before proceeding with the adoption of the agenda, the Chairperson informed the Committee that Ms Mchunu would assume the role of Acting Chairperson for the remainder of the meeting, as she had received news of the passing of her brother.
Dr W Boshoff (FF+) moved the adoption of the agenda. This was seconded by Mr V Nkosi (ANC).
Ms Gugulethu Zwane, Deputy Director-General (DDG): Institutional Planning and Support, Department of Science, Technology and Innovation (DSTI), said that the three entities presenting were each submitting their strategic and annual performance plans. She handed over to the HSRC, led by Dr Cassius Lubisi, Chairperson of the HSRC Board.
HSRC's strategic plan and APP
Dr Lubisi said the HSRC was presenting its strategic plan for 2025–2030 and its APP for 2025/26. These documents were the outcome of a detailed strategic process aligned with national priorities. They incorporated recommendations and outcomes from two years of institutional reflection, including external divisional reviews, an institutional review, and an organisational culture survey. The plans aligned with the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), the African Union’s (AU's) Agenda 2063, the National Development Plan (NDP), the Medium Term Strategic Framework (2024–2029), and the Decadal Plan (2022–2032). The five-year strategy was based on a theory of change, focused on research to address inequality, unemployment and poverty through evidence-based policy and social innovation. This involved identifying and analysing systemic barriers in South African society, such as structural inequality, skills gaps, and inefficiencies in the labour market.
He identified financial sustainability as one of the organisation’s key strategic risks, currently heightened by international geopolitical developments. The board, together with the chief executive officer (CEO) and executive management, had been monitoring the situation and engaging across various governance structures. The matter was also under discussion at the ministerial level through the Minister’s working group, on the impact of the United States funding freeze, where the HSRC was represented by the CEO.
He reported that the HSRC continued to manage compliance effectively. The organisation had received an unqualified audit opinion with no material findings from the Auditor-General of South Africa (AGSA). The material irregularity flagged in 2021/22 had been resolved through appropriate corrective action.
He advised the Committee that the current board’s term would end on 31 October. He added that efforts to stabilise risks were intended to enable the incoming board to focus primarily on strategic priorities and to strengthen the HSRC’s position locally, regionally and internationally. The goal was to build new partnerships and diversify funding.
He handed over to the CEO and her team to present the strategic and annual performance plans in detail.
Dr Lubisi, Prof Sarah Mosoetsa, CEO, and Ms Jacomien Rousseau, Chief Financial Officer (CFO), briefed the Committee on the HRSC presentation.
Strategic priorities and themes
The strategy focused on reducing inequalities and poverty through inclusive growth, rural development and innovation. It emphasised global partnerships, health, climate sustainability, education, and science diplomacy to create jobs and build an ethical, capable state supporting a future society driven by research, skills and international collaboration.
Flagship projects 2025-2030
The flagship projects from 2025 to 2030 focused on addressing inequalities, poverty and employment through eight key initiatives. These included social attitudes surveys, longitudinal inequality studies, and national health and innovation indicators. The projects also emphasised skills development, capacity building, internships, and publishing research to foster sustainable growth and social transformation.
Key initiatives 2025-2030
Key initiatives with partners aimed to enhance human resource development, workplace research, and democracy. They supported the African Continental Free Trade Area (AfCFTA), analysed Agenda 2063, reviewed 'Silencing the Guns,' and provided policy advice on the AU Agenda. Focus areas also included artificial intelligence (AI) risks, mental health research, and e-participation platforms.
Annual performance plan
The APP outlined five key outcomes. It aimed to position South Africa as a leader in knowledge production for poverty eradication and employment creation. It fostered collaboration with government and academic institutions to shape policy and enhance science communication. International partnerships promoted science diplomacy, while transforming research capacity and sustainable income streams supported long-term impact. These outcomes were driven by the LeaPPT+S framework -- leadership, policy influence, partnership, transformed research capabilities, and sustainability.
Leadership in advancing knowledge and research impact
This performance area supported national and global leadership in research to reduce poverty and inequality. Key indicators included collaborative research projects, peer-reviewed journal articles, books, and book chapters by HSRC researchers. New indicators measured strategic outputs, citation impact, and the number of curated datasets downloaded for secondary use.
Transformed research capabilities and national capacity building
This outcome focused on improving equity and inclusion in research. Key indicators tracked black representation among mid- and senior-level researchers, as well as black female senior researchers. New and revised indicators highlighted scholar support, training opportunities, and initiatives for unemployed graduates and interns across provinces to build national research capacity.
Sustainability and income diversification
Outcome five focused on building sustainable income streams by increasing extra-parliamentary funding. Indicators included the percentage of total income from non-parliamentary sources, as well as the number of multi-year collaborative projects funded locally and internationally. New indicators aimed to expand funding opportunities, ensuring long-term financial stability and institutional independence.

ASSAf's strategic plan and APP
Prof Thokozani Majozi, President & Chair of Council; Prof Himla Soodyall, Executive Officer; Dr Melusi Thwala, Manager: Science Advisory Programme and Strategic Partnerships; and Mr Morakeng Chiloane, Finance Manager, briefed the Committee on the Academy of Science of South Africa (ASSAf) presentation.
Transformation
The transformation agenda emphasises inclusivity, people-centric approaches, and addresses both global and national challenges. It prioritises skills advancement, poverty alleviation, and the empowerment of youth, women, and persons with disabilities. It aligns knowledge generation with equity redress, ensuring science serves all sectors of society for sustainable development.
Polycrisis
The current global context was marked by interlinked crises, ranging from the COVID-19 pandemic and geopolitical instability, to demographic shifts and digital transformation. These were compounded by climate change, resource insecurity, inflation, unemployment, and persistent inequalities. Understanding these dynamics was critical for shaping resilient and inclusive science policy responses.
Three pillars determine the direction of the Academy
The direction of the ASSAf was guided by three strategic pillars:
advancing the objectives of the White Paper and the Decadal Plan for Science, Technology, and Innovation (STI);
promoting evidence-based research aligned with the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs); and
strengthening science engagement and communication.
These pillars underpinned ASSAf’s commitment to driving impact through science for science, science for policy, and science for society, ensuring that scientific research informs national priorities, supports global development, and benefits all sectors of society.
Annual performance plan
Biosafety and Biosecurity improve laboratory safety and outbreak responses. Science, technology, engineering and mathematics (STEM) advances energy solutions and education. Humanities tackle gender-based violence (GBV) and education funding. Health and related matters address disease impacts and mental health. Science for the reduction of poverty and inequality promotes equity and sustainable development.

SACNaSP strategic plan and APP
Prof Khathutshelo Nephawe, Chairperson of Council; Dr Nompumelelo Obokoh (CEO); and Mr Terrence Mangalana, Finance Manager, briefed the Committee on the South African Council for Natural Science Professions (SACNaSP) presentation.
2025-2030 strategic objectives
Focus was placed on increasing registration and retention of natural science professionals, ensuring ethical standards, promoting lifelong learning, enhancing science communication, and fostering strategic partnerships for advancement.
Alignment with the medium term development plan (MTDP)
The 2025-2030 strategic objectives support the MTDP by driving inclusive growth, job creation, poverty reduction, and a capable, ethical, and developmental state through professional development, ethical standards, and effective science communication.

The plan aims to increase and retain registered natural science professionals by streamlining registration, upholding high professional and ethical standards through education campaigns, advancing professional development and transformation via lifelong learning, promoting the profession through science communication and engagement, and fostering strategic partnerships to enhance skills, global exposure, and workforce capacity.

HSRC's response
Dr Lubisi noted that most responses would be provided by the executive team, but he wished to make a few preliminary remarks.
In response to questions regarding alternative funding, he said that the HSRC had been impacted by recent geopolitical developments, particularly funding cuts implemented by the US administration in January 2025. These decisions, taken without warning, had led to the termination of several international research programmes, affecting institutions globally, including major US universities such as Harvard. The HSRC had historically relied on partnerships with the US, Europe, and other regions for research funding. However, it had also established collaborations with South African government departments and local institutions, which continued to provide alternative funding streams.
He indicated that the board, in coordination with the Ministry of Science, Technology, and Innovation, had resolved to accelerate the search for partnerships within BRICS, across the African continent, and in East and Southeast Asia. He cautioned that replacing lost funding would not happen quickly, and that significant effort would be required. This challenge was not unique to the HSRC, but affected other institutions, including the Department of Health, particularly in relation to HIV funding. He emphasised that there would be global competition for alternative funding, and that while domestic sources would also be pursued, results should not be expected immediately.
Regarding the accessibility of research findings, Dr Lubisi explained that the HSRC places significant emphasis on public communication of science, ensuring that research outputs are tailored to diverse audiences, including policymakers, academics, the general public, and funders. The organisation used multiple platforms to disseminate its work, including seminars, dialogues, workshops, and virtual engagements. Many of these sessions were recorded and made available on the HSRC’s website and YouTube channel.
He added that research outputs were published in various forms, such as general articles, policy briefs, books, blogs, conference papers, and research reports. In the case of policy briefs, the HSRC presented evidence-based recommendations but did not dictate policy, as its role was to provide policy advice, not policy-making.
He also referred to the establishment of the Policy Action Network, a collaboration between the HSRC and the DSTI, designed to bridge the gap between research and policy-making by facilitating stronger connections between researchers and decision-makers.
Finally, responding to a query about rental income, he confirmed that the HSRC owned one of the tallest buildings on Pretoria Street in Pretoria, which houses the headquarters of the Department of Social Development. The income derived from leasing this space constituted the reported rental income. However, he noted that the HSRC may soon sell the building due to longstanding challenges with its maintenance, a decision made before the appointment of the current board and CEO.
Prof Mosoetsa noted that Dr Lubisi had already addressed the themes of funding and stakeholder engagement. She added that many of the questions raised by Members went to the core of the HSRC’s vision and mission. Its mandate was not to make policy, but to influence policy through relevant, evidence-based research conducted in partnership with government, communities, and other key stakeholders, including the private sector. The impact of its research was often immediate due to these strategic partnerships. Research was frequently launched in collaboration with institutions such as the Independent Electoral Commission (IEC), the Department of Agriculture, the Department of Health, and National Treasury, among others. This approach ensured that research findings were taken up directly by policy actors.
On inequality, she confirmed that the HSRC was undertaking a comprehensive longitudinal study on inequality in South Africa. She acknowledged the Members’ concerns, and confirmed that the institution was actively pursuing this area of research.
Regarding climate change, she indicated that the HSRC was organising a follow-up conference as part of its ongoing programme, in partnership with Mozambique and other regional partners. The upcoming conference would be hosted in Namibia and would focus on the impact and responses to climate change.
With respect to support for emerging researchers, Prof Mosoetsa highlighted Strategic Outcome 4, which specifically addresses training and development. This includes not only academic skills, but also practical competencies ranging from soft skills to entrepreneurship, gender issues, and social cohesion.
She concluded by expressing support for further engagement with the Portfolio Committee, and welcomed their participation in upcoming HSRC events, where more of their questions could be addressed in detail.
Prof Sharlene Swartz, Divisional Executive: Equitable Education and Economies, responded to Mr Mjadu’s question on how to address digital neocolonialism in relation to the numerous AI questions. In brief, the answer had to be the development of Pan-African and global south solidarities, and that had been their ongoing focus. Their AI research and strategy followed a four-pronged approach. The first involved capacity development of their own staff, as well as emerging researchers at universities. They had been running capacity development workshops on AI and digitalisation across the country, depending on staff availability and time. Most of the research had been funded by the Facebook Foundation, the MasterCard Foundation, and the International Development Research Centre (IDRC), with some proposals still under review. They were being cautious about where they accepted research funding from, due to concerns over neocolonialism and data sovereignty.
The work was continuing through Pan-African collaborations and solidarities. One of the projects, focused on African youth futures and funded by the MasterCard Foundation, included countries such as Senegal, Ghana, Nigeria, Uganda and Ethiopia. The aim was to consider national sovereignty while also ensuring that young people had access to AI.
They were developing AI agents without using large language models from the United States, due to its stated goal of becoming the dominant global AI producer. They had instead been using Chinese search engines and large language models, and were also creating their own data servers to train these agents.
Regarding the question of when their AI bot would be ready, which had been raised during a previous parliamentary session, the challenge was the constant technological changes. Each time they believed the model was ready, new developments revealed its limitations. By creating their own large language model on a server at the HSRC, they expected to have a stronger model. They would inform Parliament once it was ready, as it was intended for public use to access their research.
On ethics and policy, Ms Mazzone had asked what the Committee could contribute. Prof Swartz said it felt like the right time for a cross-government, cross-entity forum to consider the types of policies needed to regulate AI and data more effectively, while also improving access for vulnerable and marginalised groups.
On the question of data infrastructure, she said they were working on it, though it remained costly. Many of their surveys were curated and offered as open access to the public and to university students, but further investment was still needed.
Dr Mzikazi Nduna (Divisional Executive: Research Impact, HSRC) responded to several questions by grouping them under the themes of ethics and the translation of research into policy and practice.
Regarding the HSRC’s recruitment and retention, she noted that recruitment policies were being taken more seriously. Recent advertisements had prioritised applications from South Africans. The language policy had been activated to ensure that scientists responsible for HSRC outputs were able to speak South African languages. She added that while the outputs remained in English, the meanings and understandings behind them would be influenced by this language alignment. For example, among the 8 000 reviewers, it was now required that each be able to speak at least one South African language. The expectation was that these revised recruitment and retention strategies would prove effective.
Internal capacity development projects had shown that staff turnover at the HSRC was low, and long service was high. This could be verified in the organisational report.
On the issue of inequity in AI, the importance of engaged research was highlighted, particularly in forming partnerships between the HSRC, the private sector, and the public sector. These collaborations were intended to unlock and harness resources beyond the parliamentary grant. She emphasised that a case -- a book on the Jukskei River, produced by HSRC researchers -- had been translated into practical action by a community NGO in Alexandra. This work has contributed directly to cleaning the river. She added that through collaborations with community organisations, research outputs were now being linked to ongoing work in the field. This approach would continue. Previously, books had been launched without integration into community projects, which meant there had been no clear evidence of translating research into practice or policy.
Regarding interventions currently underway, reference had been made to the HSRC’s flagship project on science diplomacy. This initiative responded directly to concerns about the unregulated influence of foreign AI systems in the country. South Africa’s position was viewed within the broader continental context. A recent engagement with the diplomatic community, held in partnership with the Department of International Relations and Cooperation (DIRCO), had focused on regional as well as national responses.
Dr Nduna said an annual 10-day science diplomacy training had been introduced. This was expected to support a more unified continental approach to foreign AI systems. The training curriculum included AI content designed specifically for the diplomatic community. Another question focused on how HSRC research had shaped state-led responses. One example was the recent policy shift on parental leave, which had been informed by research on families and fatherhood. A further example involved the national food security systems plan, linked to the HSRC’s surveys on food insecurity.
Dr Nduna noted that the HSRC’s research ethics committee had broadened its scope to include research integrity and governance. This work now went beyond ethical review, supporting the national regulation of research and reinforcing the independence of the HSRC’s output. Concerns about the independence and robustness of research had been addressed through a new governance section at the HSRC, which now oversaw these functions.
Finally, in relation to how this work would be sustained, a new approach to Memoranda of Understanding (MoUs) had been adopted. The focus was now on outputs, outcomes, and measurable impact. A tracking system had been developed to monitor and demonstrate the effectiveness of work carried out in partnership with communities.
Ms Rousseau, the HSRC's CFO, addressed several questions about the entity's sustainability.
She referred to a phrase introduced by the CEO that had been gaining traction within the organisation:
living within our means
. This approach was intended to ensure the HSRC operated within its financial limits and remained sustainable. She emphasised that the organisation was committed to spending only within its means. MoUs were being used as indicators, and building partnerships was identified as a key strategy for maintaining sustainability.
She referred to a number of cost containment measures that had already been implemented. The HSRC was spending prudently, and only where financial resources were available. Shared internal resources were also being used to stretch available funds more effectively.
On the issue of outsourcing versus insourcing, she noted that discussions had taken place around areas where the organisation lacked specific expertise. In the case of information technology (IT) services, outsourcing has proved more economical. For example, paying R42 000 per year for external services was more cost-effective than appointing someone permanently to that role.
Ms Rousseau also provided an update on the HSRC building. The organisation was currently based in a 14-storey building, with floors two to nine rented out to the Department of Social Development. She pointed out that the HSRC occupied less than 50% of the space, and that the building was in the process of being sold. Income from the rental had largely been used to maintain the building. Once the sale was completed, savings were expected. The HSRC intended to move into rented premises, as its primary purpose was to conduct research rather than manage property.

He identified working more closely with industrial partners as an untapped opportunity. He explained that policies that ASSAf sought to inform affected industrial partners, and mentioned the Quadruple Helix Model of innovation, which includes industry, academia, government, and society. He said the private sector had not yet been fully involved in their activities, but that industry was willing to assist scientific engagement efforts. ASSAf was therefore looking to bring industrial partners, particularly from the private sector, closer to its initiatives, believing this could lead to significant financial leverage based on mutual benefits. He described this funding diversification as short-term, and referred to it as low-hanging fruit.
Regarding a question from Mr Ramongalo on the end goal of the S20 chaired by the Academy of Sciences, Prof Majozi said that the aim was to produce a statement related to climate change and planetary sustainability, which would inform the G20 summit in November. The process had started in February with work on the statement, which was now close to obtaining signatures from all member countries after extensive deliberations. He expressed confidence that if the statement became policy within this global framework, it would influence actions at the ground level in all G20 countries. The ultimate goal was to ensure climate change was addressed with a focus on human and planetary well-being.
He mentioned that ASSAf had been cutting costs for a long time. He described it as a very small organisation compared to others, noting that despite limited revenue, they continued to reduce expenses wherever possible.
On the question of how to ensure their work leads to tangible policy changes, he said that ASSAf worked closely with the government, reporting to DSTI. All activities were aligned with government oversight to remain relevant to policy.
Before handing over, Prof Majozi emphasised the potential of beneficiation as a poverty alleviation strategy. He argued it was an option South Africa had not fully leveraged to address poverty, unemployment, and inequality. He noted ongoing work in this area, particularly at universities, but highlighted that it was not yet fully supported by national policy. He suggested ASSAf could play a major role in leading the development of policy to promote beneficiation as a means of alleviating poverty.
Prof Soodyall responded to the question regarding how ASSAf evaluates the effectiveness of science engagement in improving trust. She acknowledged that it was a difficult matter to quantify. While it was possible to measure average publication scores, revenue generated, and visibility through conventional metrics, evaluating the concept of trust was far more complex. However, she believed that being invited to co-partner with NGOs, community-based organisations and other entities, was a good indication that ASSAf was recognised and trusted for its work. These invitations reflected a level of confidence in the Academy's capabilities.
She mentioned that ASSAf had made deliberate efforts to move science out of academic spaces and into communities. In July, during Mandela Month, it engaged with an old-age home, facilitating activities to support the elderly and persons with disabilities. Science had been introduced into this space in a way that allowed the community to understand ASSAf's role and voice their own concerns, which would then inform future initiatives. She recalled that in previous engagements, Members of the Committee had asked what ASSAf was doing in such settings. ASSAf had taken that recommendation seriously, and was developing what she described as a legacy project. The aim was to build a long-term footprint for the Academy.
She also stated that colleagues had been engaging with schools, particularly in under-resourced areas that lacked internet access and other infrastructure. ASSAf members who had excelled in their scientific careers had been bringing science content to young learners, particularly in schools without laboratories. They attempted to facilitate practical learning through available resources within the membership and the secretariat.
She added that ASSAf worked in alignment with the DSTI’s science engagement framework. Their approach was not to act alone, but to work jointly with other stakeholders to ensure coordinated delivery in areas where increased visibility was required.
Prof Soodyall acknowledged that Prof Majozi had addressed issues related to sustainability, and also noted that the HSRC Chairperson had described the current operating environment, particularly the effects of international cost pressures, tariffs, and spending cuts. She thanked the Minister for mobilising expertise across entities and universities to advise the DSTI on navigating these challenges. She expressed hope that discussions were ongoing across other ministries to find collective solutions not only for individual entities, but also across the national system.
She referred to Dr Boshoff’s question about how the global south could perform more effectively than the global north in the future. ASSAf was working in partnership with the BRICS Academy and BRICS Plus, as well as with the InterAcademy Partnership, which was a global network of academies. These collaborations brought internationalisation into their daily work and allowed them to address shared concerns across the global south. ASSAf also worked closely with the Network of African Science Academies (NASAC), supporting less-resourced African academies and helping to secure small, collaborative grants to support sustainability.
She spoke about the importance of managing costs and resources across all programmes. As the executive officer, she worked closely with ASSAf’s council and staff when planning the annual performance plans. She stressed that the focus must be on output- and outcome-driven activities, guided by humanitarian values. She remained cautious about being drawn into projects outside of ASSAf’s mandate, as this would reduce the Academy's impact and strain resources unnecessarily.
In response to Mr Nkosi's question about balancing high costs with publication outputs, she explained that the Academy employed a set of collective strategies. She reiterated that ASSAf did not make policy, but conducted research to support policy advice. The recommendations from consensus studies were intended to inform the relevant government departments. She emphasised the Academy's reliance on the DSTI to communicate such ideas across departments due to its broader visibility in the national science, technology, and innovation landscape.
Responding to the question from Dr Boshoff about how ASSAf’s mandate differed from that of SACNaSP, she clarified that ASSAf was a membership-based organisation, with members elected by nomination. This was not the same as membership in professional councils like the Health Professions Council of South Africa (HPCSA) or SACNaSP, where registration and accreditation were central.
Responding to the Chairperson’s question about addressing unemployment through research, she said that ASSAf focused on advancing skills among early-career scientists and youth. The Academy hosted the South African Young Academy of Science, and worked closely with the Organisation for Women in Science in the Developing World to support women scientists. She explained that ASSAf's Standing Committee on Poverty was also involved in national dialogue aimed at reducing poverty, unemployment, and inequality. She described ASSAf’s role as one of bringing stakeholders together and cementing collective ideas.
She concluded by noting that due to budget constraints, the Academy continued to operate primarily on a web-based model with minimal travel. They had downsized office space to reduce rental costs and, like the HSRC, relied on outsourcing for some functions. She expressed appreciation to the Committee for its engagement on science issues. She suggested that joint webinars between the Committee and entities could be a valuable way to align activities and build stronger collaboration.
SACNaSP's response
Prof Nephawe expressed his appreciation for the comments and questions directed towards SACNaSP. In the interest of time, he requested that the CEO and the management team respond to the questions. He would provide concluding remarks and further clarification later in the proceedings.
Dr Obokoh explained that the questions had been grouped according to several key themes. These included registration and science awareness, continuing professional development (CPD) and impact, regulatory matters, the ICT system, finance, and the broader mandate of the SACNaASP.
Addressing the registration targets, he said that the growth figures were realistic, based on trends observed during the development of SACNaSP's five-year strategy. She said the registration database had grown to over 20 000 professionals. Over the previous five years alone, there had been 12 451 new registrations, suggesting that the Council was on track to exceed 30 000 registered scientists by the year 2030. She emphasised that several key strategic initiatives supported this target, such as the presidential mandate to professionalise the public sector, and current efforts to strengthen the Natural Scientific Professions Bill. These were expected to result in even higher registration numbers.
Dr Obokoh also referred to the occupation-specific dispensation (OSD), explaining that many government departments had already begun to implement it in alignment with the professionalisation of the public sector. She said that SACNaSP was also engaging with the private sector, where employers now increasingly required proof of SACNaSP registration when considering candidates for scientific roles. As a result, she confirmed that the Council was confident in meeting its projected targets.
Responding to a question about whether SACNaSP registration was mandatory or voluntary, she pointed to Section 18 of the Natural Scientific Professions Act. This section explicitly stated that no individual may practise in any of the designated fields without being registered with a recognised regulatory body -- in this case, SACNaSP. She clarified that the entity was a statutory regulatory body, not to be confused with voluntary associations. This distinction was also recognised by the South African Qualifications Authority (SAQA). SACNaSP was the only organisation in South Africa mandated by an Act of Parliament to register, regulate, and uphold ethical standards within the natural sciences. She compared its role to that of the Engineering Council of South Africa (ECSA) in the engineering sector, and the Health Professions Council of South Africa (HPCSA) in the health sector.
Turning to continuing professional development, Dr Obokoh said that CPD was central to maintaining professional competency. It was compulsory for all registered scientists. SACNaSP was working to increase participation by including candidate scientists, particularly given current developments in ICT and intellectual property. This was crucial for preparing scientists to address industry challenges and improve their employability. SACNaSP had begun engaging with candidate scientists to determine their developmental needs across its 26 recognised fields of practice. CPD programmes were designed not only to ensure professional growth, but also to keep participants up to date on ethical standards and technological advancements. She stressed that this approach enabled scientists to contribute meaningfully to key socio-economic challenges.
Dr Obokoh said that SACNaSP assessed the impact of its CPD initiatives primarily through participation rates, which were disaggregated by gender, youth, and people with disabilities. Feedback was also collected from the 49 voluntary associations recognised by SACNaSP, as well as from employers in both government and industry. A comprehensive survey had been conducted to identify areas for development, and the Council continued to adapt its CPD offerings accordingly. She noted that this inclusive and impactful approach was underpinned by a policy approved by the Council, which outlined the framework for CPD and ensured long-term effectiveness.
On science awareness, she said SACNaSP promoted public understanding of science through several programmes. A flagship initiative had been launched in partnership with various departments within the DSTI, other government departments, and provincial bodies. It focused on engaging previously disadvantaged schools and establishing student chapters at universities. It was also hosting a wide range of science engagement events, both online and in person. What had initially been an annual science week had now evolved into a monthly event to increase ongoing interaction with communities. Advisory notes and newsletters were made available to further support accessibility.
Regarding partnerships with SETAs, Dr Obokoh confirmed that SACNaSP had already begun securing additional funding. She cited a signed Memorandum of Agreement (MoA) with the Chemical Industries Education and Training Authority (CHIETA), which had provided funding of just over R600 000 in the previous year. SACNaSP had also engaged with the Agricultural Sector Education and Training Authority (AgriSETA) and the Food and Beverage Manufacturing Sector Education and Training Authority (FoodBev SETA). The Council was in ongoing discussions with several other sectoral and industry bodies to develop further collaboration.
She highlighted the Employer Champion Programme, which encouraged companies to support SACNaSP registration and CPD among their scientific staff. The initiative had been well received and was helping ensure that the private sector actively promoted professional development in the natural sciences.
On ICT and data protection, Dr Obokoh reported that SACNaSP’s database held information on more than 20 000 scientists. The Council had migrated its systems to a cloud-based platform, implemented firewalls, ensured daily data backups, and introduced multi-factor authentication. Access to systems was now tightly managed. She confirmed that SACNaSP was working closely with the Information Regulator to meet compliance requirements under the Protection of Personal Information Act (POPIA). Cybersecurity training for staff had been introduced, covering phishing and other risks. She acknowledged that cybersecurity threats continued to evolve, but stated that SACNaSP was taking appropriate measures to remain proactive and secure its data.
Finally, on finance, Dr Obokoh acknowledged the financial constraints caused by reduced government funding. While SACNaSP remained reliant on the R30 million received from the government, the Council had actively sought alternative funding sources. It had signed an MoU with the City of Cape Town and the Gauteng Department of Agriculture and Rural Development. It was also working with the Municipal Infrastructure Support Agent (MISA) to support municipal professionalisation efforts. In addition, SACNaSP was developing both local and international partnerships. Not all support would be monetary -- some collaboration, such as with the China Association for Science and Technology (CAST), focused on global exposure for young scientists and knowledge-sharing.
She concluded by inviting the finance team to expand on any issues related to sustainability and financial matters that she had not already addressed.
Mr Mangalana began by addressing the issue concerning the R30 million baseline funding. He acknowledged that the matter had already been partially covered, but reiterated that the core question was what contingencies were in place should the funding be delayed or not realised. He said that without this baseline funding, SACNaSP would need to scale down its mission and rely more heavily on project-specific support, which he pointed out was far less sustainable.
However, he emphasised that SACNaSP had already established strong relationships with SETAs and industry partners, as mentioned earlier by Dr Obokoh. These relationships had been leveraged to secure funding for key initiatives, such as the SACNaSP Information Technology System and professionalisation programmes. While these partnerships provided a useful safety net, he stressed that baseline funding remained absolutely critical. He added that SACNaSP maintained a close engagement with the DSTI to proactively manage any risks. These combined measures would help to ensure that the entity remained financially sustainable and able to fulfil its mandate, even under constrained financial conditions.
Mr Mangalana then addressed the second question, which related to cost-cutting measures. He said that SACNaSP planned to co-host stakeholder engagement and awareness events with universities, SETAs and government departments, to share costs. It would also prioritise hosting stakeholder engagements and other events online rather than in person, as this had proven to be both beneficial and cost-effective. Another cost-saving intervention would be to encourage senior professionals to volunteer for SACNaSP programmes and activities, thereby reducing the need to hire external consultants.
Lastly, Mr Mangalana turned to the final question regarding SACNaSP’s plans to remain profitable and avoid entering into a deficit. He said that the first key strategy was to diversify revenue streams by exploring multiple income sources, including offering professional development courses and forming partnerships with like-minded organisations. The second strategy involved collaborating with industry stakeholders, government agencies, and international organisations to access funding, resources, and expertise. The third strategy was to ensure ongoing market awareness by staying informed about industry trends and emerging technologies.
He concluded by stating that through the implementation of these strategies and best practices, SACNaSP believed it could work towards long-term profitability and sustainability.
Closing remarks
The Acting Chairperson expressed concern that the HSRC was now at the point of considering the sale of its building. Based on her limited financial understanding, she remarked that a building should be considered an asset. She acknowledged that it had not provided a full explanation for this decision, but in her view, it was important for an entity such as the HSRC to own an asset of this nature. She did not believe the building was a depreciating asset, but rather one that should appreciate. If it was in fact depreciating, she suggested that the Department should explore how it might assist the HSRC in ensuring that the building generated income, rather than becoming a liability. She concluded by thanking everyone, including the participating Members, for their contributions.
Ms Shiviti expressed her gratitude to Ms Mchunu for leading the discussions during the meeting. Also, she extended her thanks to the Portfolio Committee, the DSTI, and all the entities under the Department. She acknowledged the HSRC and ASSAf in particular for their thorough preparation of reports and for their availability in responding to the questions raised by Members of the Portfolio Committee. Although she had briefly been absent from the discussions, she had participated in some of them, and supported the direction taken. She endorsed the initiative to place greater focus on beneficiation, with the aim of industrialising the economy and creating employment opportunities.
The meeting was adjourned.
