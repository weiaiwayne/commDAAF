Meeting Summary
Video
2025/26 Annual Performance Plans
The Department of Science, Technology and Innovation briefed the Committee on its five year Strategic Plan and 2025/26 Annual Performance Plan. The Minister of Science, Technology and Innovation outlined the Department's vision of placing science, technology and innovation (STI) at the centre of South Africa’s development. The focus was on driving inclusive economic growth, building resilience, and promoting social equity. A major goal of the Department was to raise investment in STI to 1.5% of the country's gross domestic product by 2030, and increase access to science, technology, engineering and mathematics education through various initiatives.
The Director-General presented the Department's strategic plan for 2025–2030, contextualising it within the global crises of inequality, climate change, technological disruption, and geopolitical uncertainty. The plan aligned with the government's medium-term development plan, and supported three key priorities: inclusive growth and job creation, poverty alleviation, and building a capable, ethical state. Each of the Department's programmes had specific deliverables.
Several concerns were raised during the discussion. Members expressed dissatisfaction with the limited inclusion of rural and underdeveloped municipalities in DSTI programmes, with provinces like the Free State and Mpumalanga notably underrepresented. There was significant concern over the Department’s failure to meet equity targets for youth, coloured, white, and disabled groups, as well as calls for greater youth employment and involvement in STI initiatives. The need for better integration of STI in local government planning, especially in response to natural disasters and environmental management, was also highlighted. Members urged the Department to accelerate the decolonisation of science by integrating indigenous knowledge systems more deeply, and adopting African perspectives in research and development.
The DSTI responded with several reassurances and clarifications. It expressed commitment to expanding grassroots innovation and youth-focused projects, and explained that programme participation was currently limited by budget. The Department emphasised its existing collaboration with China and other international partners, and noted efforts to commercialise local innovations through partnerships with the Department of Trade, Industry and Competition, and the involvement of venture capital investors, including the Public Investment Corporation. To address funding constraints, it planned to request additional resources from National Treasury and develop new public-private funding models to raise an additional R4 billion. It would also improve monitoring tools such as reports and surveys to evaluate programme reach and the public's perception of science.
While Members welcomed many of the DSTI's initiatives, they called for broader participation across provinces, faster transformation, and greater impact at local levels. The Chairperson also invited written submissions from the Department for further clarity and oversight.
Meeting report
Minister's opening remarks
Dr Blade Nzimande, Minister of Science, Technology and Innovation, outlined the aims of the Department by first and foremost placing science, technology and innovation (STI) at the centre of South Africa's development, with its focus on inclusive growth, resilience and social equity. He proceeded to outline the Department's key priorities:
Driving economic and social development through innovation in energy, health, artificial intelligence (AI), agriculture, and climate-resilient infrastructure.
Better coordination of STI efforts at the provincial and municipal levels.
Investing in local scientific capacity, including vaccines, space technology, and digital services.
Promoting science, technology, engineering and mathematics (STEM) education, especially for women and black scientists, and launching a Presidential PhD programme.
Strengthening global partnerships through science diplomacy and platforms like the G20 and STISA 2024.
Raising STI investment to 1.5% of gross domestic product (GDP) by 2030 (currently at 0.6%).
He handed over to Dr Mlungisi Cele, Director-General, to present the DSTI's 2025/26 annual performance plan.
DSTI's strategic plan and annual performance plan (APP)
Dr Cele began by describing the context within which the Department's APP had been developed. He said the world was facing intersecting crises of inequality, climate change, technological disruption, and geopolitical instability. Science, technology, and innovation were central to solving these challenges. South Africa's socio-economic issues required STI-driven solutions.
The DSTI’s strategic plan was aligned with the government's medium-term development plan (MTDP) and contributes to three key government priorities -- inclusive growth and job creation; poverty reduction and cost of living mitigation; and building a capable, ethical, developmental state.
He highlighted the Department's programme commitments for the financial year:
Programme 1: Administration
Unqualified audit with no findings by July 2025.
50% women in senior roles, 4% disability representation.
Enhanced science visibility via national media campaigns.
Programme 2: Technology Innovation
Support for students, artisans, post-doctorates in clean energy, space, and bioeconomy.
Innovation Fund support for venture capital (VC) firms and commercialisation.
Intellectual property (IP) funding, outreach in space science, and new black farmer innovation support.
Programme 3: International Cooperation & Resources
Science diplomacy and economic partnerships.
Stronger support for historically disadvantaged institutions (HDIs).
Increased South-South cooperation and foreign investment attraction.
Programme 4: Research Development & Support
PhD ecosystem strengthened via Presidential Programme -- R1 billion from National Skills Fund (NSF).
Focus on foundational sciences, climate change, and indigenous knowledge.
Postgraduate equity funding maintained despite budget constraints.
Programme 5: Socio-Economic Innovation Partnerships
Grassroots innovation, living laboratories, and township small, medium and micro enterprise (SMME) support.
Enhanced district development model (DDM)
Launch of Municipality Innovation Fund (MIF) and promotion of STEM in schools.
Dr Cele referred to human resources concerns, saying the Department had a vacancy rate of 23.5% (117 of 495 posts unfilled), youth employment of only 12% against a target of 30%, and representation of whites and coloureds below target.
Risks and mitigation
Poor coordination: Strengthen STI committees and budget integration.
Funding gaps: Engage stakeholders and Treasury; public-private partnerships (PPPs).
International engagement decline: Revise strategies and boost global presence.
Organisational structure: Review and optimise regularly.

Discussion
Ms J Adriaanse (DA, North West) agreed with the Minister that South Africa ought to become producers of knowledge, and that the DSTI could produce this. She said that addressing the floods and other natural disasters called for innovation and better town planning, with a need to have local municipalities on board. She emphasised this point by stating that town planning required taking the changes in the weather into consideration. One also had to look at the protection of South Africa’s rivers and green belts in cities and towns.
Ms Adriaanse then turned her focus to enquiring whether the amount of money allocated to research programmes would be enough, considering the funding from Pepkor which had been cut. She expressed her concern regarding the DSTI’s employment targets, with the Department overachieving in many targets except in hiring ‘coloured’ and ‘white’ employees. She enquired whether there was a specific reason for that, and whether the Department was aiming to meet those targets.
She expressed further concern as to the disproportional allocation of DSTI programmes to municipalities, stating that municipalities that needed an improvement of basic services seemed to have been ignored. She used the example of the North West as such a location, with only one municipality -- Rustenberg -- being included in Project 1. She asked what the chances were of other municipalities being included in the above-mentioned project.
Minister Nzimande agreed with Ms Adriaanse, and commented that to assist in preventing floods, one must clean and maintain the stormwater drainage systems.
Ms L Arries (EFF, North West) first requested that the DSTI give examples of projects regarding service delivery and projects supporting initiatives in rural areas. She sought details confirming the involvement of the DSTI, the private sector and sponsors, in start-up initiatives and youth innovators in townships as part of its projects. She expressed concern and requested reasons for there being no service delivery programmes in the Free State, and no living laboratories in Mpumalanga, and enquired how the Department was supporting municipalities affected by natural disasters and the recent floods.
Ms Arries requested that the Department provide details on whether they were taking a decolonial approach to theory and research practices, and what their role in this approach was. What had been the impact of the collaboration between the DSTI, the Department of Basic Education (DBE) and the Department of Higher Education and Training (DHET), in investing in STEM education to enable lifelong learning? Did the initiatives from the collaboration form part of the DBE's and DHET's formal reporting process?
Dr Tshepang Mosiea, Chief Director: Innovation for Inclusive Development, DSTI, responded to Ms Arries' question on supporting youth innovators in rural areas by emphasising the number of instruments the DSTI needed to ensure access for youth innovators. He gave examples of instruments such as grassroots innovations, living laboratories and innovation centres, which would make South African technologies accessible, and commented that this was beyond the means of the Department’s budget.
Mr Imraam Patel, Deputy Director General (DDG): Research Development and Support, responded to Ms Arries' concern on the Department's decolonisation efforts. He said that the Department did world-renowned work on indigenous knowledge, but that it was also looking to expand decolonisation efforts into other areas, such as looking after the environment from an African perspective.
Ms N du Plessis (DA, Gauteng) began by commenting on China’s extreme consequences for corruption, and said their investments had been used to grow technology, science and innovation. She enquired whether the DSTI was relevant as a department, considering that South Africa was nowhere near where it needed to be in the 4th industrial revolution, which meant the talent in the country was not being used. She concluded by inquiring about outcomes 6 and 7, as found in the presentation -- improved National System of Innovation (NSI) governance and coordination, and increased integration of STI-based evidence into government decision making. She asked how the DSTI would be monitoring intergovernmental engagement to ensure that the money they were allocating to entities was being used properly.
In response to Ms Du Plessis' comment on how China handled corruption, Minister Nzimande confirmed that consequences were harsh, and acknowledged that reducing corruption did make a difference in investments.
Mr David Mmakola, Chief Director, DSTI, provided answers to both Ms Du Plessis' concerns. First, he said that the DSTI had mechanisms in place, such as comprehensive reporting systems, to hold entities accountable. These mechanisms were implemented regularly to ensure entities met their targets and allocated their budgets correctly. On outcome 6-7, he said that these outcomes were embedded in the Department's planning commitments and reporting requirements. He added that the Department was also held accountable for these outcomes by being invited by the Portfolio and Select Committees to report back on their progress.
Mr P Sibande (ANC, Mpumalanga) asked the Minister to elaborate on the reasons why transformation was so slow, particularly after the 1996 White Paper, the various pieces of legislation, and parliamentary meetings. He expressed concern regarding those who were still against transformation in South Africa. He also criticised the use of multiple acronyms by the DSTI, commenting that many did not know what they meant.
Mr Sibande then enquired as to whether there were laws that protected South African innovations, and whether the Department could give any advice on this. He asked about the DSTI's involvement in providing energy to rural areas so far. He then made enquiries about the programmes of the Department. In terms of programme 2 (technology innovation), was China willing to support South Africa? Was the R1 billion allocated to programme 4 (research, development, and support) enough, considering how big South Africa was? Turning to programme 5 (socio-economic innovation partnerships), he requested clarity as to why the provinces that usually received a lot of support and assistance had received more investment than the provinces with high levels of disadvantage. Lastly, he expressed concern over the low rate of youth employment in the Department and wanted to know whether the DSTI had any programmes that could assist the youth.
On Mr Sibande's concern about transformation, Minister Nzimande reassured him that South Africa would not go backwards, despite resistance from those who did not want transformation. The Department would push for transformation by readjusting subjects and holding government officials accountable.
Mr Daan du Toit, DDG: International Cooperation and Resources, DSTI, in response to Mr Sibande’s question on South African-China relations, proceeded to explain how China was currently assisting South Africa in STI. He said the DSTI aimed for a diverse portfolio of international partnerships to reduce the vulnerability in these relationships. He said South Africa had a solid partnership with China through several programmes and initiatives. These programmes and initiatives included joint research programmes, innovation projects, exchange programmes, joint research centres, etc. They were also collaborating with multinational corporations and with various Chinese provinces, such as with the Guangdong province, so China was supporting South Africa in STI.
Minister Nzimande further elaborated on this by asserting that only five countries were comprehensive strategic partners with China, with South Africa being one of them.
Dr Cele responded to Mr Sibande’s concern regarding provinces that already receive support being prioritised in the DSTI's programmes. He said that the Department did not like the current state of affairs, but hoped that start-ups would spread to other provinces. He emphasised that the Department was doing all it could, and hoped that as it grew, it would be able to take on more provinces.
Ms D Fienies (ANC, Northern Cape) asked the Department to indicate the roles of the National Treasury and the Department of Planning, Monitoring and Evaluation (DPME) in their collaboration with the DSTI, in determining how the gross expenditure on research and development (GERD) target of 1.5% of South Africa’s GDP could be achieved. She then enquired as to the departmental impact that the DSTI was facing due to having to transfer R72 million to the Department of Higher Education and Training (DHET), and how the relationship between the two departments was maintained with the funding programmes and targets attached to the R72 million. Lastly, she asked about the unemployed graduates from the technical and vocational education and training (TVET) colleges’ reskilling process -- specifically, how communication was done to ensure that the TVET curriculum was updated, reducing the need to reskill.
Dr Cele responded to her enquiry about the collaboration between the DSTI, National Treasury and the DPME, and said he would be meeting with the Director-General of the DPME in a few days’ time. The Department would therefore be able to respond only in the future as to the nature of the collaboration.
Mr Andries Shila, Director: Management Accounting, DSTI, responded to Ms Fienies' enquiry about the R72 million transfer to the DHET, and said that the money had been transferred to the DSTI in 2020 as a result of STI being in the DHET's portfolio. However, in 2024, these ministries had separated, and due to budget constraints of the DHET and costs associated with the department, the money had to be transferred back. This would be done over a few years, with the DSTI aiming to use savings from other national budgets to recover.
Minister Nzimande responded to Ms Fienies' inquiry on an updated curriculum at TVET colleges and supporting youth innovations. He said that if the DSTI was able to receive money from the Public Investment Corporation (PIC), it would be able to provide better support. Although the DSTI could help with the TVET curriculum, it remained the primary role of the DHET.
Mr M Mokwele (ANC, Limpopo) observed that it took a long time for government departments to process anything due to a lack of science and technology. The Select Committee needed to assist the Department in receiving and accessing available information regularly. In line with the views of other Members, he expressed concern over the lack of rural provinces involved in the DSTI’s programmes. He said that despite its limited resources, the DSTI had to make strides and prioritise STI at all spheres of government, not only at the national level. Only by doing this would one be able to see the impact of the DSTI's programmes.
The Chairperson commended Minister Nzimande's initiative as the Minister of the DSTI. However, he also expressed concern over the lack of youth employed in the Department, emphasising the need to attract young talent before a crisis developed. He enquired as to the causes of this challenge, and how the Department was aiming to mitigate them. He wanted to know when the DSTI would start commercialising its innovations to assist in addressing country-wide challenges
Dr Cele responded to the Chairperson's question on commercialisation, stating that the DSTI collaborates with the Department of Trade, Industry and Competition (DTIC) to commercialise their research. He gave an assurance that the Department would improve its joint efforts with the DTIC to ensure that commercialisation took place.
Minister Nzimande elaborated on the issue of commercialisation by stating that he was trying to strengthen technology innovation by increasing investments in STI and the Council for Scientific and Industrial Research (CSIR), and including venture capitalists from all sectors. He then named potential investment partners such as the PIC.
Ms Zanele Ngwenya, Chief Director: Human Resources, DSTI, responded to the concerns of Members on the DSTI's employment equity targets. She said that the failure of the DSTI to fill their vacant posts was due to budgetary concerns. The Department was aware of its shortfalls in employing those with disabilities, the youth, white and coloured persons, and would put posts aside for these persons. This would be done by conducting targeted recruitment.
Dr Mosiea responded to Members' questions on the participation of rural provinces in the DSTI’s programme on service delivery. He first explained that programmes were run from a bottom-up approach, with municipalities having to submit expressions of interest. There were 180 municipalities that submitted, but only 65 could be recommended for the programme. He said there would be another opportunity for rural municipalities to be included due to the two-part nature of the programme. He reassured Members that an expanded case for innovation service delivery would be presented, which would include other municipalities.
In response to concerns raised on natural disasters and city planning, Dr Mosiea explained that the DSTI had made available the South African Risk and Vulnerability Atlas, which would assist municipalities to identify vulnerable areas. This atlas could also be used to plan settlements. DSTI entities may submit projects to be used in the case of natural disasters.
On the issue of funding raised by several Members, Minister Nzimande explained how the DSTI would go about receiving more funding from the Minister of Finance. He said the Department would compile a case stating that it needed to unfreeze posts in critical areas and that in doing so, it would assist in socio-economic development.
Mr Patel added that the Department was looking for partnership models to increase funding by an additional R4 billion. This money would come from different sources such as the private sector, international partnerships and other departments.
He also responded to concerns regarding the monitoring of the Department’s programmes and the promotion of science to the youth. He said the DSTI did monitor the impact of its programmes in terms of how many people they reach. He drew attention to the survey done by the Department in 2024, which had scientifically looked at the public perception of science, and said it would use these results to determine the type of science promotion suitable for different ages.
Consideration and adoption of the draft Budget Vote report and draft minutes
The Chairperson asked Members to vote for the adoption of the Report of the Select Committee on Education, Science and Creative Industries on Budget Vote 16: Basic Education.
Mr Mokwele proposed its adoption, and Ms Fienies seconded.
