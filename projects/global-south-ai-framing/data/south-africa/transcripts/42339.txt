Meeting Summary
Video
The Committee convened virtually to receive briefings from the Department of Science, Technology and Innovation (DSTI) and the Human Sciences Research Council (HSRC) on research, measurement and analysis of research and development (R&D) and innovation in South Africa, with a particular focus on the work of the Centre for Science, Technology and Innovation Indicators (CeSTII). The DSTI emphasised the strategic importance of the HSRC and CeSTII within the national system of innovation, particularly in tracking system performance over time using internationally recognised frameworks and indicators to inform evidence-based policy.
The HSRC outlined its institutional structure and mandate, highlighting CeSTII as a key centre responsible for national R&D and innovation measurement. It was explained that CeSTII’s work aimed to ensure that data collection went beyond academic exercise and directly informed policy, strategy, and developmental outcomes. The presentation was said to have covered trends in South Africa’s R&D intensity, which had stagnated at 0.61% of GDP, well below the 1.5% target for 2030, reflecting persistent underinvestment and heavy reliance on government funding. Members were informed of findings on Fourth Industrial Revolution technologies, agricultural business innovation, informal sector innovation, mining modernisation and the development of science, technology and innovation (STI) compliance indicators for state-owned enterprises (SOEs), with emphasis placed on the need for more inclusive and sustainable innovation practices.
During the discussion, concerns were raised by Members regarding the absence of the DSTI Ministry, the limited translation of research outputs into tangible socio-economic outcomes, and accountability for the continued failure to meet national R&D targets. Members also questioned the independence and impact of CeSTII’s work, the effectiveness of innovation indicators, and the weak linkage between research, implementation and service delivery, including in areas such as water, agriculture and manufacturing. Further issues raised included the need for stronger private sector participation in R&D, better support for innovation in the informal sector, alignment of STI indicators with inclusive growth objectives, and improved collaboration across government departments, SOEs and regional structures such as the Southern African Development Community (SADC).

She offered a brief high-level background on who CeSTII was, together with the research it conducted, and provided an outline of the presentation. She added that the presentation would be conducted by Dr Mustapha.
Dr Mustapha, Head of CeSTII Centre, proceeded with the presentation. He highlighted the importance of the measurement of Research and Development (R&D), taking Members through the history of the South African National Survey on R&D, and the main objectives of R&D.
Some of the challenges highlighted included the stagnation of R&D intensity. Dr Mustapha highlighted that R&D intensity had stagnated at 0.61% of South Africa’s Gross Domestic Product (GDP), below the 1.5% target by 2030, indicating continued underinvestment relative to economic output. The business sector was the hardest hit during COVID-19. He further highlighted government funding dominated R&D, highlighting vulnerability to fiscal pressures and the need to crowd in private investment.
4IR Trends and other advanced technologies
The Internet of Things (80.2%) was the highest indicator, followed by business intelligence technologies (52.3%). While blockchain technologies and nanotechnology were at the lowest with 5.8% and 7.5% respectively.
South African Agricultural Business Innovation survey (AgriBIS)
He further took Members through the South African Agricultural Business Innovation survey (AgriBIS), highlighting some challenges facing agriculture in South Africa. These included climate change, digitalisation, institutional/policies, water scarcity, lack of support, global competition, and productivity. He highlighted that a majority of Agri-businesses engaged in about 67% of some form of innovation. Digital technologies remained unevenly adopted. Larger and better‑resourced firms showed higher innovation intensity.
Innovation in the Informal Sector Survey
Dr Mustapha said 70% of economic activity in Africa was informal, and the informal sector had poorly targeted innovation policy. He emphasised the need to understand the nature of innovation and learning in the informal sector and the need for targeted instruments (Decadal Plan) and the strengthening of existing programmes e.g. grassroots innovation.
Strengthening African STI Measurement
Dr Mustapha further highlighted the African Partnerships/Networks for Research, Measurement and Capacity Building. Some of the core activities included training African countries to conduct RDI surveys as well as capacity building in Science, Technology and Innovation (STI) policy reviews, data management and data governance, digital transformation and RDI survey methodologies.
Some key achievements in codifying expertise to co-create STI Policy, data and measurement toolkits included:
- African Innovation Outlook Reports, CeSTII provided expert support;
- SADC STI Indicators Reference Group, CeSTII offered expert representation, reporting to regional ministers;
- Transformative Innovation Policy Africa, Capacity building and development of transformative innovation policy models; and
- Regional Centre of Excellence in STI Measurement (SA to submit Proposal – early 2026 to SADC, together with other member states).
Modernisation of Mining
Mining remained vital to the national economy but faced sustainability pressures. Modernisation in the mining industry was a central theme in South Africa’s mining policy landscape. Modernisation signified a shift from legacy practices toward: Technology-driven solutions; Greater operational resilience; future-oriented strategic approaches responding to longstanding structural issues.
STI Compliance Indicators for State-Owned Enterprises
Dr Mustapha took Members through the framework for enabling innovation in State Owned Enterprises (SOEs). He highlighted dimensions for assessing SOEs' R&D together with innovation capabilities. These included human capabilities, technological capability, research infrastructure, networks/collaboration and governance. He concluded presentations by highlighting reporting indicators on STI for SOEs.
The HSRC CEO closed the presentation by highlighting the information provided spoke to three key ideas that HSRC had always pursued. Firstly, that it was not data for its own sake. When looking at the work performed by CeSTII it produced data which informed policy and strategy. Secondly, that there had always been a government uptake of the data to an extent that government had requested CeSTII to provide such data again for that policy and strategy to happen. Thirdly, institutional use was also something HSRC worked on through CeSTII. She made an example of the work done in the mining sector as well as SOEs.
She emphasised that the data provided was not showing doom and gloom in terms of R&D expenditure in the country or innovation expenditure. There were lessons learned, of course, but largely there were positive stories that were coming from South Africa through the data CeSTII unearthed.
CeSTII had further put into practice the idea of the Africa agenda research; she highlighted that the research done by CeSTII was regionally relevant but also continentally relevant and not just focused on South Africa’s R&D.

She added that she had heard Members’ frustrations around the lack of implementation of the recommendations provided. She added that not all HSRC policy recommendations would be taken up, and this was for various reasons. This was not because the recommendations were not credible, but precisely because of the timing of the recommendations, as well as the issue of resources.
She appreciated that the Committee would assist HSRC in engaging different government departments on various research projects and policy recommendations and look into what had been done after the research was received.

This was research which needed to be taken up by communities to also inform communities of where the country was, be it on health issues, R&D and innovation, or the Internet of Things. She added that this was an ongoing challenge for the HSRC to continue engaging with communities, informing communities of where the government was spending money and where the government was moving around in innovation. She emphasised the importance of research which empowered.

It was also required to go through many hurdles to even provide such services to government departments. This was also something which needed addressing to ensure research was funded.
Prof Swartz added on whether the HSRC had enough money to do the work it performed. She said the HSRC cut its cloth according to the money it received, and with more funding received it would be able to do more work. She added that sometimes the entity raised additional money from various foundations externally in order to make sure that the work happened. CeSTII believed in being a good citizen both of its continent and of the country. She added that a lot of the work the HSRC did in collaboration with the rest of the African continent was through conferencing, capacity development , and this further assisted in raising funds.
Regarding how the work of CeSTII impacted livelihoods, she gave the example of a workshop CeSTII had with the Western Cape Government last year, where it invited various sectors, where over the course of two days, everyone present interrogated the data that had been produced over the past decade. She said this was one of the most productive workshops she had been a part of at the HSRC where there was multi-stakeholder engagement, and people were trying to look at not just the data, but what the data meant and what action could be taken. This included policy leveraging, but much of it was practical action that could be taken by, for example, biotech innovation companies looking at the spend on innovation. She emphasised this needed to be repeated across the provinces to get as close to the ground as possible. The closer they were, the greater the possibility of using this data to impact livelihoods both through policy and through practice.
Dr II-haam Petersen, Chief Research Specialist, HSRC, responded to questions on the informal sector and how the HSRC ensured its data on the informal sector was internationally comparable but also contextually appropriate and relevant. She highlighted that this was the challenge faced by the CeSTII, which it acknowledged at the start of this process. She highlighted that the beginning of the design of the project was to firstly engage with the international guidelines as articulated in the Oslo manual and further engage critically and to test empirically how appropriate this would be for the South African context. The methodology that CeSTII adopted for this project did not follow a traditional innovation business survey.
She highlighted that CeSTII did have a survey element, but it also conducted mixed methods of research, where it added qualitative research, which enabled it to really get a grounded understanding of innovation in this context.
The language usage around innovation, such as the nature, the drivers, the outcomes, the barriers, the challenges to use, etc., the understanding thereof informed the survey instrument to ensure that CeSTII’s instrument was contextualised as well. She added that going forward, the plan was to continue with this kind of model, keeping the traditional conventional survey but refining it as they went along, developing a grounded understanding of the informal sector and using community-based methods, as well as to be able to develop that understanding.
Regarding collaboration with the DSBD, she highlighted that over the last two years CeSTII engaged with the department and particularly with the unit responsible for the informal sector. They were in the process of refining or revising the National Informal Business Innovation Strategy. CeSTII engaged the DSBD on the data that it collected through the survey and the My BIP app, together with the Toolkit, which were really designed to be able to engage informal business owners directly around innovation, and to promote that understanding at the local level and on the culture of innovation-oriented entrepreneurship within the informal sector. She explained this was a new collaboration and for now, it was still in the discussion phase. For now, CeSTII did not have a fully funded project or a plan on how to roll out the Toolkit and My BIP app at national and local levels.
Lastly, regarding aiding the informal businesses to graduate to the formal sector, she highlighted CeSTII had engaged DSBD to think about capabilities to formalise, and the importance of innovation capabilities in the informal business Toolkit to become a formal more sustainable employing business. There were certain capabilities that were needed and therefore, it was important to really think about how to build capabilities to formalise rather than focusing only or mainly on business registration and compliance.
Dr Atoko Kasongo, Research Specialist, HSRC, responded to questions on data. She highlighted that CeSTII had introduced a new project investigating and working on upgrading methodologies for better data analysis and policy dissemination. They were exploring web scraping as well as now casting. These were explored in terms of using the longitudinal time series data that it had to be able to fill in the gaps that might exist and be able to better predict the policy and economic movements going forward. While this was still in the preliminary stages, she said the project was moving.
Dr Kgabo Ramoroka, Senior Research Specialist, HSRC, addressed questions on agriculture, water and mining. He said agriculture in South Africa was essentially a two-sector type of agriculture where there were big commercial farms and small-scale farmers including subsistence farmers. He highlighted the talks of modernisation and adoption of advanced technologies in this space, and this spoke of a transition to a more advanced precision agriculture. This transition was affected by the nature and characteristics of these two groups. He further highlighted that the two groups did not achieve advanced precision agriculture at the same pace nor in the same manner.
He gave an example of commercial farms which were doing well. He added that these tended to have internal research development and innovation teams, whereas smaller-scale farmers relied extensively on extension services and also learning from each other. He emphasised the sector was complicated, especially when thinking about precision agriculture and precision technologies. There were a number of areas where precision agriculture needed to be implemented, especially in areas such as improving productivity, saving resources such as water, and using land in a sustainable manner and increasing profitability. There were farmers who were struggling to access profitable markets and support climate-smart technologies. These were just some of the areas he believed precision technologies could assist farmers.
He highlighted prevailing issues in terms of barriers in agriculture, such as issues linked to finance and funding. He highlighted that these precision technologies were expensive and gave an example of drones, adding that small-scale farmers could not afford drones or implement weather stations to predict weather and safeguard their produce.
Another prevailing challenge was regulations, such as water rights, which affect applications. If a farmer wanted to apply for a global gap, for example, there were issues linked to water rights. He recognised commercial farmers were also facing some of these challenges, but given their nature and how they operated, they were able to adapt and deal with some of these challenges.
Regarding the mining modernisation space and the role it played in re-industrialisation, he said the latter required a shift from raw mineral exports to value-added processing. He highlighted the importance of strengthening value chains through modernisation. There were also issues around building capacity for broader transformation. He added that the positive side of modernisation was the fact that it did not necessarily replace or create job losses but rather transformed the nature of employment. Traditionally, mining was labour-intensive and required physical strength, and women were excluded from this. However, with modernisation, the sector would be using technologies and subsequently create a space for women to also play a key role and work in the space. Another positive he highlighted on modernisation was in re-industrialisation through preventing premature or early mining closure. This would strengthen more efficient ways of mining and make it less risky, especially with people exposed to deep mining.
He emphasised that in all this, CeSTII tried to capture the issues around the local perspective or understanding of modernisation. Looking at how it was being used, how it was conceived on the ground before imposing or thinking about. He highlighted that, looking at the results of the study, there was a somewhat narrow perspective on the concept of modernisation, which was linked to operation and efficiency as well as health and safety. He said if modernisation was done in this way there was the risk of job losses, however a more inclusive approach and more inclusive modernisation looking at ways to include people in the modernisation process.
Dr Mustapha responded to the question on the link between R&D and economic growth and how to use the longitudinal data. He said that when attempting to answer these questions, it could not be done in a very straightforward way by simply using the R&D survey as a tool. The R&D survey only looked at institutions or enterprises that did R&D and that was a very small group of performers in the country, he estimated about 10% to 20% in the business sector. The impact of R&D was not just within the R&D performers themselves but was economy-wide and much broader. However, CeSTII was able to use longitudinal data and he said there were some very well-known studies that were done using econometric methods using data models and statistical models and so on. He added there were mixed results in these econometric studies amd there were anomalies which occurred in developing countries showing links between R&D and productivity were not really that direct but generally speaking the belief was that R&D did have an effect on especially GDP growth, as well as an effect on employment growth.
CeSTII was thus able to not only perform these econometric studies but also had the skills to look at the data and not only collect the data but further analyse it using sophisticated models.
Regarding questions on methodical issues using Oslo manuals and why CeSTII used international standards, he emphasised it was important for CeSTII to be able to not just collect the data but to use it in a manner which was beneficial to South African policy makers and to the country in general. Thus, CeSTII used these international standards to compare and benchmark against other countries. This, he added, was the only way it was able to do so in a very reliable way. He explained that when CeSTII created tools such as the informal sector innovation tool, it tried to align with the principles of the Oslo manuals. For the informal sector, he highlighted this was a very specialised sector and it required CeSTII to conduct grassroots research on exactly how one would measure innovation in the informal sector while still maintaining the international standards.
Regarding questions on the independence of the data, CeSTII produced especially the R&D intensity series. He said CeSTII was overseen by Statistics South Africa, and part of the quality criteria that it had to satisfy was that CeSTII must be independent from policy influence. This he added was specifically mentioned in the South African Statistical Quality Assessment Framework, which was gazetted as the national standard for assessing quality in statistics. This was something HSRC had to adhere to for it to maintain the official accreditation. He added that CeSTII made every effort to keep its data independent from policy influence. Thus, its data was simply what was collected from the various institutions and the various businesses which CeSTII collected from.
Regarding SOEs, he said the project was started more than ten years ago, and CeSTII first used its data to look at how SOEs were performing. Thereafter, CeSTII, led by the DSTI, had engagements with the Department of Public Enterprises together with several SOEs. He highlighted that most of the large SOEs did R&D. The engagement was well received, and as an outcome, CeSTII was asked to conduct further research. Subsequently, CeSTII performed case studies of three partners who were at the engagement. This was the amount of engagement CeSTII had with the SOs themselves. He highlighted that the utilisation of the indicators that it developed was something that would be in the purview of the DSTI which the HSRC could support through its indicators. CeSTII tried very much to involve those people it identified as the stakeholders or prime users of the research that it conducted. In this case it was the SOEs and the Department of Public Enterprises.
HSRC sought to increase its links with the regional areas as well as have dissemination activities at the broader African level as well as at the SADC level.
Prof Swartz responded to questions on the private sector and said these were big philosophical questions, however, the HSRC had been thinking about policy briefs and policy implications. CeSTII had done some work on policy levers to boost innovation and productivity in South African manufacturing firms. They further ensured that the private sector was at the workshops that it held when it spoke about the data. She highlighted that CeSTII’s more recent policy briefs look at the role of BBBEE on innovation. She emphasised that CeSTII was ensuring that they brought the private sector, SOEs, and the informal sector to the table.
She concluded responses by stating that CeSTII was a team of academics who were both highly trained in data collection and data analysis but also in the application of data. There were over 20 policy briefs which CeSTII wrote recently and would ensure the Committee was apprised of these policy briefs, as they were produced including mapping modes of firm innovation in the private sector through new indicators.
Concluding remarks by the Director-General
The DG concluded responses by addressing private sector participation. He said this was a challenge which the DSTI had openly admitted. He emphasised that the entire system acknowledged that it was quite hard in South Africa to get the private sector on board with activities of the national system of innovation, including making the necessary investment in R&D. He highlighted this which was contrary to what was happening elsewhere in the world, where private sector investments were over 50%. In South Africa, it was roughly 45%, he added that this was the main contributor to why DSTI were not achieving the 1.5% growth target. This was a point the Department was making for the private sector to reconsider their investment options and decisions.
Further, some analysis on this was being done by the National Advisory Council on Innovation. The DSTI Minister had also since directed the Department to assist in developing a multi-pronged funding strategy. The first element would be directed at the private sector, and the Minister intended to engage the private sector directly about the areas which the sector could invest in. The Department had recognised that holding breakfasts or dinners, as was previously done, had not always yielded positive results and had not always seen an inflow of investment into the system. The Department was currently in the process of identifying the areas or initiatives which were scalable, bankable, and viable which the Minister was to put on the table for discussion with the private sector. The DSTI hoped this would come with more traction, the Department was further engaging its entities such as the Council for Scientific and Industrial Research (CSIR) and Technology Innovation Agency (TIA), which were working closely with the private sector to re-engineer their own strategies to improve the way the Department engaged with the private sector. He highlighted these included discussions on the question of incentives. He made an example of the Innovation Fund which the Department was implementing through TIA. He highlighted that the Department had thus far managed to leverage about R1,2 billion with government investment at around R250 million. The Department sought to upscale this innovation. It engaged TIA to build the necessary capability to manage the Fund. Further, TIA recently appointed someone who was going to set up that capability within TIA to effectively manage the Fund. He stated that through this Fund the Department was seeing a positive reaction from the private sector, which showed a keen interest in investing together with Treasury and other development institutions.
He said the Sector Innovation Fund was also broadly welcomed by different economic sectors. The Department was in ongoing discussions with the DTIC and would continue to engage on a way forward as well as improved tax incentives.
Finally, the DSTI agreed to reorganise itself by creating a dedicated portfolio focusing on the private sector and consolidating all incentive instruments within the Department to have a much better and sharper focus on how to engage with the sector.
Lastly, he highlighted the correlation between economic growth and progress made with respect to R&D he said that in two years' time, there might be better results on R&D. The constraints and economic activities had contributed to DSTI’s inability to realise the 1.5%.
The Department was also working with National Treasury which was busy with changes to the Public Finance Management Act (PFMA), which would be going to Cabinet. He hoped these changes would assist the Department with respect to funding.
Regarding budget cuts, he requested that thorough engagement be held with the Minister and Deputy Minister when they came before the Committee, to talk through some of the implications of the budget cuts, and what the Department intended to do in response to these cuts. Suffice to say, the Department was looking at all its initiatives looking at those it considered catalytic, which required upscaling and more investment. The DSTI would be meeting with the Minister of Finance on the issue of funding, indicating areas that require such funding. The Department was in the process of identifying these critical areas and further looking at how to engage international funding, while ensuring the country owned technological sovereignty as a state. This work would also be brought before the Committee for engagement.
The DSTI was also in the process of renewing and revitalising its international STI approach as it sought to strengthen its regional and continental partnerships. The DSTI Minister was currently in Addis Ababa where the African Union was discussing the implementation of the Science, Technology and Innovation Strategy for Africa (STISA 2034). He added the Minister had expressed keen interest in engaging other Ministers to discuss how to better implement the 2034 STISA programme as part of South Africa’s commitment to the building of the science, technology, innovation system at a regional as well as the continental level, but also to exploit opportunities that were presented by the Africa Trade Framework. This was also to further ensure that Africa was freed of dependency and bondage on donor funding, which was a major concerning aspect affecting the ability of most African countries to define their own research agenda, and use science and innovation to address some of the pressing national priorities. This included discussions on how to reposition South Africa in relation to the emerging global relations dynamics, including how to build on the successes of the G20 ministerial research and innovation outcomes, while ensuring the existing partnerships with the European Union continued to be oiled and supported albeit with the challenges that it was confronted with.
Dr Cele said the Minister would brief the Committee officially on the development of the STI legislation since the Department was one of the few departments without legislation,all its entities were established by Acts of Parliament while the Department did not have legislation. Once drafted, this Bill would come before the Committee. He added that the Department envisaged that the Bill would also deal with some of the persisting and institutional architecture-type questions. That was whether the Department was structured properly, whether its institutions were fit for purpose, whether there was no mandate creep or duplication between the various institutions nor gaps in the system which might require the DSTI either to merge, integrate or create new institutions and facilities.
He emphasised the Department was pushing very hard. He cautioned that its science centres should not be forced to compete with the private sector in order to make available technologies or innovation solutions that had been generated as a consequence of publicly funded research. Unfortunately, in most cases the public entities were unable to compete with these private entities. He added that DSTI was pushing hard for its entities to be seen as the first point of call by government departments across all three spheres of government to be utilised to make sure that the solutions that they had generated were utilised to solve some of the pressing challenges that South Africa’s system was facing.
Indeed, there were a lot of solutions that the DSTI science councils had put on the table; however, it was up to government whether those solutions were utilised or not, including CeSTII. He emphasised that CeSTII was a research entity; it did not implement any intervention. It was up to the government to decide what to do with the recommendations emanating from the insights which CeSTII provided. This, he highlighted, was one of the reasons the Department kept on strengthening institutions such as CeSTII so that their work was robust, rigorous, and credible, and that its work informed evidence-based policy, not just to DSTI but all other departments that might stand to benefit from its work. The DSTI would continue to work with CeSTII and its other entities. It was important to take the system to the next level for it to respond positively to the socioeconomic challenges and further improve its impact. The HSRC itself also had reoriented its strategic outcomes to focus on outcomes and impact together with CSIR and other entities.
Dr Cele highlighted the importance of research institutions and said research institutions such as CeSTII would not always provide concrete solutions; however, their research was necessary to offer a sound technical base for any decision-making. This was one of the greatest contributions of these research institutions. It was again up to policymakers to decide on what to do with such evidence.
Further, it was institutions such as CeSTII which assisted the government in determining whether its policies were working or not. By implication, society too got to know whether government policies were indeed working or not, through the rigorous work of these bodies. It was through this very work that departments were also able to identify the emerging or persistent inequalities in society. It is through the work of bodies such as CeSTII which helped government and stakeholders to appreciate the widening inequalities or gaps.
Closure
The Chairperson thanked the DSTI, Members, and the HSRC delegation for their engagement.
The meeting was adjourned.
