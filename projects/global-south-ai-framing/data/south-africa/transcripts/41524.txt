Meeting Summary
Video
The Portfolio Committee held a strategic planning session to discuss its five-year plan in alignment with the NDP 2030, SDGs, Agenda 2063, and the Seventh Parliament priorities. Committee members welcomed the comprehensive presentation but raised strong concerns about the need for clear, measurable outcomes, stronger oversight mechanisms, and urgency in addressing critical education challenges.
Key issues raised included the dire state of school infrastructure (unsafe toilets, lack of roofs, water, sanitation, and fencing), with members stressing that infrastructure must be the top priority before reforms like the BELA Act or curriculum changes can succeed. They warned against vague performance indicators and called for specific, quantifiable targets such as schools rebuilt, toilets replaced and asbestos removed. Teacher shortages, overcrowding, underperformance in STEM, dropout rates, and weak support for rural and township schools were highlighted as urgent challenges, along with improving Early Childhood Development (ECD) delivery. Members pressed for stronger interventions on GBV, drugs and crime in schools, urging mandatory reporting of sexual offences and learner psychosocial support.
Concerns were raised about weak follow-through on oversight visits, with unresolved cases like Setotolwane Special School and Ngodini High cited as examples. Members called for a formal resolution tracker, better coordination with provinces, and possibly a dedicated DBE follow-up team to ensure that Committee recommendations are implemented. They also cautioned against repeated “tick-box” oversight, urging focus on outcomes, not activities, and the use of disaggregated indicators (by province, quintile, gender, disability) to measure progress and reduce disparities.
While acknowledging resource and budget constraints, members proposed cost-effective oversight methods, smaller delegations, and more public participation. International benchmarking was supported, with lessons from countries like Finland and Rwanda to strengthen ECD, curriculum design, teacher development, and technology integration, while adapting them to South Africa’s context. Members emphasised aligning oversight with ministerial performance agreements, addressing risks like under-expenditure and corruption, and ensuring education reforms contribute directly to poverty reduction and inclusive growth.
The Committee Content Advisor outlined risks such as budget shortfalls, lack of psychosocial support, and inadequate infrastructure monitoring. Oversight extends beyond school visits to include legislative reviews, departmental accountability sessions, and stakeholder collaboration, with public participation central to the Committee’s mandate. Tools such as a public dashboard and improved tracking systems were proposed to enhance accountability.
The Chairperson stressed that while the Committee cannot build schools, it must drive oversight on infrastructure, ECD, and systemic reforms. On the second strategic objective of strengthening the oversight model, she proposed a two-day workshop. She committed to refining the Committee strategic plan and APP with members’ written submissions and finalising these by the 19 September deadline.
Meeting report
Chairperson’s remarks
The Chairperson said she trusted that members had received the draft documents and had sufficient time to review them. The strategic plan is a critical process through which the Committee articulates our vision and outlines the intention to effectively implement the Committee’s objectives. It serves as a roadmap for the Committee’s executing oversight responsibilities, ensuring that the public is actively involved in the provision of equitable basic education, and guaranteeing that all entities under the Committee’s oversight are held accountable.
This strategic plan is not merely a document; it is a tool for defining the mission, mapping approach, and establishing clear mechanisms for achieving the Committee’s goals. She hoped that all members have had the opportunity to engage with the plan, analyze its contents, and are prepared to contribute meaningfully so that, by the end of this session, the adopted strategic plan reflects a collective vision and represents a shared commitment to fulfilling the mandate of this Portfolio Committee.
The key focus of this session is to hear from members of the Committee on how the Committee collectively define an approach to achieving the objectives. It is essential that the Committee work together as a unified team, rather than diverging in approach over time, to ensure consistent and effective implementation. In this session, the Committee will also take the opportunity to review and reflect on performance over the past year, assessing what has been done well and identifying areas where the Committee have fallen short. This reflection is crucial so it can strategize on improving areas of weakness and strengthen our overall effectiveness as a Committee.
This session is particularly important because it is about self-assessment, planning, and envisioning the Committee’s goals for the remainder of the Seventh Parliament. The Committee must consider what legacy it wants to leave behind. For what achievements and contributions does the Committee hope to be remembered?
Committee Strategic Plan 2024-2029 and Annual Performance Plan 2025/26
Ms Portia Mbude-Mutshekwane, Committee Content Advisor, introduced herself and her colleagues who had drafted the strategic plan. The members of the Committee work collectively to provide inputs and support on matters concerning the strategic plan, which will be refined as members contribute, and eventually deliberated on and adopted.
The strategic plan spans a five-year period from 2025 to 2030. The draft includes acronyms applicable to sector. The table of contents outlines the draft, covering the mandate of Parliament, the vision, mission, and values, as well as the Committee’s mandate. The Committee will also discuss the strategic planning process and its alignment with Parliament’s overall strategic plan. Further, we will explore alignment with national, regional, and continental policy priorities, tracing the strategic framework from 2019–2024 to 2025–2030 within the Medium-Term Development Plan (MTDP), which outlines the government’s priorities for 2024–2030. Subsequently, the five-year plan of the Committee will be unpacked with its goals and indicators, as well as the annual performance plan for 2025/26.
The session is intended to be interactive, allowing members to reflect on the Committee’s work over the previous year and plan forward for the next five years. The strategic plan is critical as it focuses on the institution’s key strategic issues, and it is reviewed periodically to measure outcomes against institutional objectives. The annual performance plan complements the strategic plan by focusing on specific outputs, output indicators, and targets for the coming year. Both the strategic and annual plans are aligned with Parliament’s strategic objectives and reflect priorities outlined in the MTDP and Medium-Term Fiscal Framework (MTF).
Introduction
The Strategic Plan is a five-year framework focusing on key institutional priorities, defining outcomes and impacts for performance evaluation. The Annual Performance Plan (APP) translates the Strategic Plan into measurable outputs, indicators, and targets for the upcoming financial year, aligned with the Medium-Term Expenditure Framework (MTEF) including annual and quarterly targets. Building on the Strategic Plan of the Seventh Parliament (2024–2029) and previous oversight work, the Portfolio Committee on Basic Education is developing its Strategic Plan and APP for 2025–2029. It aligns with the State of the Nation Address, MTDP, and the Basic Education sector’s priorities, including plans from the Department of Basic Education, SACE and Umalusi.
The Seventh Parliament’s Strategic Plan emphasizes the African philosophy of Ubuntu, promoting collaboration, inclusivity, and collective responsibility to build a harmonious society. It prioritizes fostering a capable, ethical, and developmental state through good governance, integrity, accountability, and oversight, aiming to improve service delivery, uphold the rule of law, and strengthen public trust.
Situation Analysis
External Environment:
The Committee engages in global and national education initiatives, including participation in G20 Education Working Group sessions in 2025, reinforcing South Africa’s leadership in education transformation, ECD implementation, gender equality, and sustainable development. South Africa faces persistent challenges of poverty, unemployment, and inequality, with unemployment reaching 33% in Q2 2025, affecting mostly youth and women—identified as a priority for the next five years.
Internal Environment:
The Committee uses an Oversight and Accountability Tracking Dashboard to monitor public matters and its strategic plan performance. The legislative mandate was strengthened with the BELA Act (2024), aimed at transforming the education landscape. Sector performance improved, with 90% achievement and 99% budget expenditure in 2023/24. Oversight visits in KZN, North West, and Gauteng ensured implementation of commitments in ECD, language policy, admissions, and infrastructure.
Opportunities and Successes:
Significant progress has been made in Basic Education, notably the NSC 2024 matric achievement of 87.3%. Achievements by girls in earning bachelor passes and in mathematics and science were noted. The BELA Act aims to improve attendance, admissions, and disciplinary practices, enhancing the education system.
Challenges:
- Underperformance in programmes like Funza Lushaka.
- Financial constraints due to budget cuts.
- Infrastructure backlogs and unsafe schools, despite progress in eliminating pit latrines.
- Need for a new funding model and accurate infrastructure data.
- Some sections of the BELA Act face contestation, particularly school admission policies, language policies, and compulsory Grade R resource allocation.
Department of Basic Education (DBE) mandate
It stems from the Constitution and the National Education Policy Act (NEPA, 1996), ensuring access to quality education, policy formulation, and oversight. It is responsible for establishing national norms and standards, monitoring implementation, and evaluating educational impact.
South African Council of Educators (SACE) is responsible for registering educators, promoting professional development, maintaining ethical standards, and continuing the professionalization of the teaching profession.
Umalusi (General and Further Education and Training Quality Assurance Council) oversees qualifications and quality assurance in general and further education and training. It monitors the adequacy and sustainability of qualifications, accredits private education providers, and ensures adoption of quality management systems.

Portfolio Committee Annual Performance Plan (APP) 2025/26
The Portfolio Committee processes and adopts the Children’s Amendment Bill [2025], holding two meetings and producing two sets of minutes and reports, including a briefing on the Bill and an update on the Department of Basic Education’s legislative framework. The legislative programme, including the Children’s Bill, presented and a draft report adopted. To monitor implementation, a framework developed for tracking the BELA Act, with oversight visits to provinces scheduled before February 2026 and a final report to be tabled by March 2026. Stakeholder engagement included a meeting with DBE and nine Provincial Education Departments, updates from ten schools, and a consolidated report to ensure ongoing communication and accountability.
The Portfolio Committee on Basic Education (PCBE) conducts effective oversight over the Department and its public entities by aligning its activities with the National Development Plan (NDP) and Medium-Term Development Plan (MTDP) priorities. The Committee develops and adopts its Strategic Plan for 2025–2030 and the Annual Performance Plan for 2025/26, following a strategic planning session that identifies key oversight priorities.
To ensure structured and continuous oversight, the Committee drafts and finalizes four quarterly programmes for the 2025/26 financial year and initiates the first-term programme for 2026/27, thereby establishing a clear mechanism for monitoring executive performance and accountability.
The Portfolio Committee conducts comprehensive oversight over the Department of Basic Education and its entities, SACE and Umalusi, through the assessment of annual and quarterly performance reports aligned with the National Development Plan (NDP) and Medium-Term Development Plan (MTDP). Four meetings held to scrutinize annual reports, culminating in the compilation, adoption, and tabling of the 2024/25 Budget Review and Recommendation Report (BRRR). Quarterly reports from 2024/25 and 2025/26 reviewed to monitor progress on key priorities. The Committee also tracks educator development through SACE’s CPTD programme, including international exchange opportunities in STEM subjects, and tables disaggregated data on training participation.
Oversight visits to three provinces conducted to evaluate exam readiness, school infrastructure, and early childhood development, ensuring alignment with national and provincial planning targets.
The Committee facilitates public participation in legislative processes by enhancing access and engagement through a structured Public Participation Model.
A Public Education Plan on statutory rape developed, including educational content, publications, and a programme aimed at informing the public—particularly young girls—about the issue. A comprehensive stakeholder engagement plan implemented, involving diverse groups such as learners, parents, civil society, government departments, traditional and religious leaders, and academia. A database of key stakeholders compiled, and a communication strategy put in place to raise awareness about the Bill and statutory rape.
The Committee holds briefings with the Department of Basic Education (DBE) on its reporting strategies, conducts update meetings on public involvement, and provides research summaries to members and the public to support informed participation.
To ensure cooperative and sound inter-governmental relations, the Committee enhances its oversight by conducting joint briefing sessions with other Parliamentary Committees, including those on Social Development, Public Works, Police, and Communications. These briefings focus on critical priorities such as the state of school infrastructure, delays in infrastructure programmes, and their impact on learning outcomes, as well as the implementation of the Early Childhood Development (ECD) programme.
The Committee holds one meeting with Portfolio Committees in provincial legislatures during oversight visits, inviting MECs to participate and contribute to discussions. A report from this meeting is compiled to document findings and strengthen collaborative governance across national and provincial levels.
The Committee actively participates in international engagements to strengthen its role in global education policy spaces. As outlined in its Strategic Plan, a study tour is conducted to countries identified for comparative learning, with a specific focus on the G20 Education Working Groups and their impact on equality, solidarity, and sustainability. A concept paper and research document developed to guide the engagement. The Committee attends one G20 session and participates in both the G20 Summit and the P20 Summit, contributing to international dialogue and drawing insights to inform domestic education strategies.

Mr L Komane (EFF) said his observation was the Committee has become Pavarotti the opera singer, who just sings when he opens his mouth. The Chairperson thanked members for their comments and submissions. She advised that the Committee should have a two-day strategic workshop with the support team. The first day would be for the Committee itself and the second day would include the entities the Committee is overseeing or vice versa. This will help the Committee to reflect on the comments made.
She emphasized the importance of holding a strategic session for the second strategic objective of strengthening oversight and accountability. The Committee must face reality and face facts in its role as one of the three equal arms of the state. No arm of the state is superior to the other, and each serves as a check and balance. The Constitution grants the Committee sufficient powers to conduct oversight, including summoning institutions and individuals to account. However, the concern lies not in the powers or mandate, but in the oversight model currently being applied, which has significant gaps. The current model schedules oversight in ways that limit its effectiveness, often confining the Committee to offices and weekly departmental presentations rather than allowing for practical, on-the-ground work. The Committee should use a workshop to critically reflect on and interrogate the oversight model that has been in place since the dawn of democracy, so that it can propose improvements to the Speaker’s Office.
While the Constitution grants the Committee powers to hold the executive accountable, the scope and definition of these powers require deeper interrogation. The concern arises when dealing with complex issues such as the Setotolwane School matter, where responsibility cannot rest solely with provincial authorities, as the national department has a monitoring role even within concurrent functions. In such cases, the Committee engages DBE through formal processes facilitated by the Office of the Speaker. For example, after a Setotolwane School debate, the Committee’s recommendations were adopted and communicated by the Speaker’s Office to the Minister of Basic Education, who then provided a progress update outlining steps taken in consultation with the province.
However, when the Committee is not satisfied with such responses, it is compelled to interrogate the broader structure, mechanisms, and strategies of oversight to ensure accountability. In addition to executive oversight, the Committee also recognizes the importance of enhancing public participation. Traditionally Committees only engage with the public when legislation is under consideration. To address this limitation, the Committee has deliberately established practices such as inviting stakeholders to Portfolio Committee meetings and including them in oversight visits.
This approach not only strengthens the quality of public participation but also ensures that public voices inform parliamentary oversight even in periods without active Bills under consideration, making accountability more inclusive and grounded in real issues. When the Committee conducts oversight in provinces, all stakeholders, including traditional leaders and community members, must be informed and involved, as such visits present valuable opportunities for public participation in the work of Parliament. However, there are serious gaps in the current model, structure, mechanisms, processes, and strategies that Parliament has adopted over the years in exercising its authority. These shortcomings highlight the need for a dedicated workshop where the Committee can reflect on and address these systemic issues.
The aim of such a session would be to ensure that this generation of parliamentarians can make a meaningful contribution by redefining or clarifying areas of oversight that remain ill-defined. The Committee intends to propose to the House Chair and the Speaker’s Office that these identified gaps be considered by the relevant parliamentary structures responsible for refining the oversight model. This approach would not only address longstanding bottlenecks but also reshape the traditional ways of operating in Parliament, enabling more effective oversight and stronger public engagement.
She made an example of the Committee’s attempt to conduct oversight in the Eastern Cape following the serious case of sexual assault at a school, which underscored the challenges faced in executing effective oversight. Despite the urgency and significance of the matter, the Committee’s request to the Office of the House Chair for an oversight visit was denied, with the explanation that no oversight was scheduled for the term due to plenary sessions and the budget process. While the Committee had formally applied, as is procedure, the request was declined on the basis of limited resources and scheduling constraints. The matter had been reported in meetings multiple times, yet the restrictive budget allocation and its utilization remain major obstacles to the Committee’s work. This illustrates the broader issue of how financial and procedural limitations hinder the Committee’s ability to act promptly on pressing issues, raising concerns about the effectiveness of the current oversight model and mechanisms available for addressing urgent matters of public interest.
Oversight work is often constrained by procedural hurdles and cancellations, as seen in the case where an application for an oversight visit was denied because the House Chair cited budget processes and scheduling conflicts. This raised concerns about the effectiveness of the current system in enabling timely oversight. In response to such limitations, the Committee took proactive steps to improve responsiveness through the creation of a “dashboard,” even though it is not a mandated function. This initiative was introduced to ensure that Parliament remains accessible and accountable to the public.
The Chairperson said that the dashboard was designed as a mechanism to process and track correspondence from citizens, many of whom raise critical issues, some labour-related, others technical, and some outside the Committee’s direct scope. Rather than ignoring or failing to acknowledge such inputs, the Committee channels these concerns to the Department of Basic Education, requesting that they be referred to the appropriate body for action, while ensuring that DBE responses are copied to the Committee for monitoring. This system reflects a deliberate effort to close communication gaps, enhance responsiveness, and strengthen the Committee’s role as a bridge between the public and government institutions.
The Chairperson said that during the August 2025 recess, the Committee had planned a meeting with all provincial education departments to discuss the functionality of the dashboard system, although it had to be postponed due to a bereavement. The purpose of that meeting was to address the Committee’s concern over the slow pace at which provincial departments respond to issues referred to them, especially those originating from the public, stakeholders, teachers, school governing bodies (SGBs) raising allegations such as corruption in schools. While the national Department of Basic Education has been more responsive—largely because it reports to the Committee weekly and is held accountable—provincial departments have often failed to provide timely feedback.
The postponed meeting would have been the second or third engagement specifically on this matter, underscoring the seriousness with which the Committee is tracking and monitoring the dashboard. In earlier discussions with DBE, the Committee had already emphasized the need for provincial departments to respond to queries within seven working days, warning that delays undermine the Committee’s oversight and the public’s trust in Parliament. The dashboard remains a critical tool not only for monitoring issues but also for demanding accountability and responsiveness from all levels of the education system.
The national Department of Basic Education has consistently responded to issues referred to it, with no instance where the Director-General has failed to reply. For example, in the recent case from Mpumalanga on exam results that remain unreleased, the DG’s Office provided a response, demonstrating the national department’s reliability. However, the real challenge lies with issues linked to labour relations and those that fall under provincial education departments (PEDs). PEDs often defer such matters to district offices, claiming they are awaiting district-level responses, which causes significant delays and undermines accountability. This recurring problem was precisely why the postponed meeting with all PEDs had been scheduled—to reinforce the Committee’s expectation that they must respond directly and not hide behind district processes. The dashboard, therefore, has become an essential mechanism created by the Committee to ensure that public complaints are acknowledged and followed up.
Yet another critical gap identified is the lack of a formal “resolutions tracker.” While the dashboard allows monitoring of complaints, there is no structured tool to follow up on the numerous resolutions adopted by the Portfolio Committee, particularly those arising from oversight visits or engagements with stakeholders. The Committee emphasized the importance of developing such a tracker to ensure that resolutions are systematically monitored, tasks assigned to DBE are followed through, and accountability is strengthened at both national and provincial levels.
The Chairperson recalled at the last meeting the Committee had tasked DBE with conducting an audit in the Western Cape, but to date, no clear response has been provided on when the audit will take place or when feedback will be given. This delay highlights the urgent need for a formal resolutions tracker, which should form part of the Committee’s implementation plan. Assigning a dedicated support staff member to manage this tracker would ensure proper follow-up on resolutions and accountability from DBE.
Another critical issue raised relates to the schedule and funding of oversight visits, which remain limited—only two or three have been conducted since the beginning of the Seventh Parliament. Oversight visits are vital for accountability, yet they are constrained by budgetary challenges, particularly the exorbitant costs associated with ground travel. Shuttle services, for instance, can cost as much as R10 000 for trips that would otherwise be a fraction of that price if alternatives like Uber were used. In some cases, service providers from other provinces have even been contracted to provide shuttle services in areas where local providers were available, unnecessarily inflating costs. These inefficiencies in procurement and expenditure undermine the Committee’s ability to maximize its limited budget for effective oversight.
The Chairperson said that the upcoming workshop was identified as a crucial platform to raise these concerns directly with the relevant parliamentary officials, as optimizing budget utilization would free up resources, enabling the Committee to conduct more frequent, creative, and impactful oversight activities.
The Chairperson noted that the Committee had highlighted the need for flexibility and innovation in how oversight is conducted, especially in cases where urgent matters arise in specific provinces. She suggested that instead of deploying the entire Committee, smaller delegations or even the chairperson alone, as was the case with Sudan, could be sent to reduce costs and allow for quicker responses. By transporting fewer members, funds can be utilized more efficiently, enabling the Committee to stretch its limited budget further and increase its reach. This approach would also make it possible to delegate members in smaller groups to cover different regions simultaneously, ensuring broader oversight coverage without straining resources. Such innovative methods are seen as essential for Parliament to effectively execute its constitutional mandate.
The Chairperson commended the Committee staff, particularly Ms Mbuda, for their excellent and comprehensive work, while urging that members’ contributions and comments be incorporated into the plan. She recommended that another meeting should be convened to review the final draft of the strategic plan before submission.
The Chairperson said that the Committee had emphasized the importance of strengthening its systems for tracking work and outcomes, responding thoroughly to members’ questions, and engaging meaningfully on the proposed international benchmarking trip, ensuring that it focuses on areas most relevant to the Committee’s oversight responsibilities.
The discussion had identified four key focus areas, with two being particularly emphasized. The first is the urgent need to study international best practices in Early Childhood Development (ECD). Currently, South Africa does not have a single, coherent ECD model but rather a mix of different approaches, which creates confusion and inconsistency in implementation. To address this, the Committee proposed visiting countries that are excelling in ECD provision to examine their models, learn from their successes, and identify practices that could be adapted to strengthen the local system.
The second area relates to the broader education curriculum, where there is a need for clarity on the overall vision and the type of young adults the system should be shaping. The curriculum must be meaningful and aligned with long-term career pathways, ensuring learners are better prepared for the future. This requires not only rethinking the content but also benchmarking against both international and domestic contexts, particularly regions where curriculum design is successfully linked to career development from the foundation phase. Together, these areas point to the necessity of developing a clear educational model and vision that can drive systemic improvements and align the education sector more effectively with national development goals.
The Chairperson noted that the Committee had emphasized the importance of studying international examples, such as China, where curriculum content from the foundation phase is deliberately linked to career paths and long-term exit strategies, beginning as early as grade one. This model provides learners with clear trajectories and purposeful learning outcomes from the outset, which could inform improvements in South Africa’s educational framework.
The integration of technology into education was highlighted as a critical focus area. Many countries have successfully leveraged technology to enhance learning outcomes and address infrastructural constraints, and South Africa could benefit from similar approaches, particularly through online learning platforms. The Committee noted that contemporary learners, including young children, increasingly rely on digital tools and artificial intelligence for research and homework, signalling a shift in the way education is accessed and applied. While acknowledging the potential risks associated with AI and technology, one needed to integrate these tools positively to improve educational outcomes and engagement.
These focus areas—career-linked curriculum and technology integration—should guide the decision on the proposed international benchmarking trip. The Committee must act swiftly to determine the destination, objectives, and budgetary implications to ensure the trip is cost-effective and yields actionable insights, without exhausting the allocated resources.
The Chairperson thanked the Committee staff for their excellent work and urged that all member contributions and feedback be incorporated into the final plan. It was important to convene another session to continue discussions and address outstanding matters.
Committee Content Advisor response
Ms Mbuda-Mutshakweni emphasized the teamwork that enables processes like these to succeed. The initial discussion served as an opportunity to gather feedback, comments, and identify gaps, while intentionally postponing the inclusion of risks at this stage. The intention is to incorporate these insights into a revised strategic plan, ensuring it reflects the Committee’s priorities and addresses identified shortcomings.
She summarised and responded to members’ concerns. Parliament operates under a clear oversight and accountability model, which guides committees in monitoring the implementation of laws, the application of budgets, and the governance and management of departments, in line with constitutional provisions. She acknowledged proposals to enhance the resolution tracking system, building on the dashboard initiative that allows the public to submit queries and receive responses. With the Chairperson’s guidance, these improvements will be explored further to strengthen oversight, accountability, and responsiveness to public input.
She highlighted the need to strengthen the accountability framework within Parliament, with an emphasis on standardizing and improving oversight mechanisms across committees. This effort is closely linked to the National Development Plan (NDP), as aligning oversight activities with NDP objectives ensures that Committee work contributes meaningfully to South Africa’s long-term development goals over the next five years. By focusing on the NDP, oversight becomes more strategic, targeted, and outcome-oriented, rather than purely procedural.
In line with this, the upcoming workshop, as suggested by the Chairperson, will provide an opportunity to delve deeper into the refined oversight and accountability model of Parliament. The session will allow members to gain a clearer understanding of the Committee’s oversight functions, the mechanisms at its disposal, and how best to apply them to achieve effective monitoring, evaluation, and impact.
She referred to Mr Shikwambana’s question on how the strategic plan aligns with other national and international frameworks and what informs the selection of study tours. The plan is guided by both the SDGs and the NDP 2030, which are closely integrated. Specifically, the Committee’s focus on quality education aligns with SDG Goal 4, while the NDP emphasizes education priorities in Chapter 9, from which the Committee has extrapolated key policy directives. This alignment ensures that by 2030, monitoring and evaluation can accurately track South Africa’s progress toward these goals. The work conducted by members during oversight activities provides critical evidence of progress, demonstrating accountability and the relationship between the executive and Parliament.
One identified risk, relates to sensitive matters affecting children, such as statutory rape cases, which require careful attention and oversight. Addressing such risks not only reinforces the Committee’s accountability function but also ensures that oversight is meaningful, responsive, and evidence-based, directly linking policy directives to real-world outcomes.
Issues affecting young children are especially delicate. She referenced the ongoing process of the Children’s Amendment Bill. In such cases, confidentiality and the protection of children’s rights remain paramount, as required by law. A key risk identified was the need for adequate psychosocial support when learners share their experiences during public hearings on sensitive matters such as statutory rape. She highlighted this as the first and most pressing risk requiring urgent attention.
The second risk centres on budget constraints that affect the Committee’s ability to effectively conduct oversight. While DBE is responsible for implementing programs and services, the Committee’s mandate is to oversee and hold the Department accountable. However, its oversight activities, including site visits, monitoring, and evaluation, are directly limited by financial resources.
Ms Mbuda-Mutshakweni emphasized that oversight extends beyond physical visits to include legislative reviews, report analysis, and accountability sessions, but budgetary constraints still hinder the Committee’s capacity to execute these functions comprehensively. Addressing these challenges requires stronger strategic planning, better coordination, and improved resource allocation to ensure that oversight remains robust while safeguarding vulnerable stakeholders, particularly children.
Oversight, as highlighted by the Committee, is a multifaceted function that goes far beyond physical site visits. It encompasses a broad spectrum of activities, including the review of legislation, tracking its implementation, analysing departmental reports, and holding government entities accountable to ensure compliance with both legal mandates and policy objectives. This comprehensive approach strengthens Parliament’s role in evaluating performance, addressing shortcomings, and driving accountability. On the matter of international benchmarking, the Committee reaffirmed its importance as a priority for the Seventh Parliament, building on practices from previous parliaments. South Africa’s role as a regional leader on the African continent, coupled with its current position in the G20, places an even greater responsibility on the country to learn from global best practices and adapt them to its own context.
The process of selecting study tour destinations begins with member proposals, followed by in-depth research to identify countries with relevant expertise. For example, during the Sixth Parliament, Finland was identified and studied as a model, particularly on Early Childhood Development (ECD) legislation and implementation. Once research papers are developed, members review the findings to determine which international experiences can provide the most valuable lessons. These lessons are then tailored to South Africa’s realities, ensuring that international benchmarking directly informs domestic policy improvements and strengthens the Committee’s oversight practices.
Another significant matter deliberated by the Committee was home-schooling, where members highlighted the importance of understanding how different countries regulate and implement policies, particularly ECD and associated aspects such as funding, provisioning, and oversight. During the oversight study visit to Finland, the Committee observed that South Africa’s legislative framework on home-schooling, especially as outlined in Section 37 of the Basic Education Laws Amendment (BELA) Bill, was broadly aligned with international standards, as most countries maintain regulatory measures governing home-schooling practices. Members also paid close attention to how Finland structures and delivers ECD services, identifying several innovative practices as valuable lessons for South Africa.
While Finland’s model demonstrated strong systemic integration and resource allocation, it could not be directly transplanted due to South Africa’s differing socio-economic conditions and resource constraints. Instead, the Committee emphasized the importance of adapting selected best practices to align with South Africa’s contextual realities, ensuring that policy adjustments are both practical and sustainable. This process reaffirmed the importance of international benchmarking as a tool to generate evidence-based insights, strengthen policy development, and support the adoption of globally informed yet locally relevant approaches to both homeschooling and ECD implementation.
Ms Mbuda-Mutshakweni said the Committee raised the critical issue of the shortage of teachers, one with far-reaching implications for the education sector. Members unanimously recognized this concern and acknowledged that while the Department would provide more detailed data and analysis, the Committee itself must remain vigilant in monitoring the situation. The shortage of educators is particularly pronounced in rural schools and marginalized communities, where learners already face structural disadvantages. Although the Committee could not yet identify specific provinces most affected, members emphasized that planning begins at a broader, strategic level, ensuring alignment with Parliament’s institutional plan, the National Development Plan (NDP), the regional African Agenda 2063, and the global Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs). The draft Strategic Plan already identifies rural schools and marginalized communities as priority focus areas, while the forthcoming implementation plan will provide detailed guidance on provinces, schools, and specific interventions, developed in collaboration with key stakeholders to ensure shared ownership and effective execution.
Beyond teacher shortages, the Committee reaffirmed that broader national priorities—poverty, inequality, and unemployment— at the heart of the NDP and cascaded into the Medium-Term Development Plan (MTDP), and wanted to ensure a coherent response to systemic challenges. On ECD, members expressed concern over the protracted delays in finalizing the Children’s Amendment Bill, which has been pending for more than a decade. Given its urgency, the Bill’s enactment has now been prioritized in both the State of the Nation Address (SONA) and the Department of Basic Education, placing it squarely within Parliament’s oversight agenda.
The Committee reiterated that while the Department is responsible for implementation, its role lies in robust oversight to ensure that progress is made and accountability is upheld. In doing so, the Committee ensures that strategic objectives, oversight activities, and development priorities are interconnected, focused on closing systemic gaps, and advancing South Africa’s long-term education and development goals.
Ms Mbuda-Mutshakweni said that it is important to note that several of the targets in the strategic plan are shared across different parliamentary committees, demonstrating the interconnected and collaborative nature of parliamentary oversight. She referred to Ms Sharif’s comment that the central focus must remain on quality indicators when evaluating education, since quality education is not merely about access but about improving learning outcomes for all learners. These quality indicators can be assessed across multiple dimensions, including the condition of school environments, classroom infrastructure, lesson planning and delivery, assessment methods, curriculum implementation, availability of teaching and learning resources, the strength of governance and leadership, and the adequacy of financing.
Concerns raised by schools on insufficient funding highlight a key challenge that directly impacts their ability to deliver quality education. Where infrastructure is identified as a priority, the Committee must ensure that the Department takes full responsibility for implementing improvements, while also fostering collaboration with other government departments—such as Public Works and Water and Sanitation—to optimize the use of shared resources. In addition, the potential of public-private partnerships and active community involvement must be harnessed, as schools are deeply rooted within and supported by the communities they serve. At the heart of these initiatives, however, the ultimate benchmark for success remains the delivery of quality learning outcomes. She emphasized that oversight efforts, collaborative strategies, and resource mobilization must collectively ensure that learners receive meaningful, equitable, and high-standard education that contributes to long-term national development.
Another critical matter raised in the discussions was on the specificity of targets in the Committee strategic plan, particularly on their feasibility and alignment with broader objectives. In responding to this, Committee work is informed by both baseline data and the realities of its budgetary constraints, which determine how far certain targets can be pursued. She urged members to actively engage in assessing whether particular targets should be increased, reduced, or maintained, ensuring that they remain practical and achievable within the available resources.
On infrastructure and quality education outcomes, the Committee support staff is currently reviewing the number of schools—both Section 20 and Section 21 institutions—that require monitoring and improvement. These insights, together with members’ proposals and contributions, will be integrated into the revised strategic plan for consideration.
In addition, the Committee emphasized the importance of gender-related indicators. It needs to work closely with the Portfolio Committee on Women, Youth, and Persons with Disabilities to ensure that planned targets are harmonized with both departmental and parliamentary priorities. Such collaboration is essential to accurately assess national progress against benchmarks set for 2023 and beyond, and to ensure South Africa’s performance is measured against reliable and consistent standards.
The Committee reaffirmed that the strategic plan is broadly aligned with the National Development Plan, the SDGs, Africa’s Agenda 2063, and the priorities of the Seventh Parliament. While there may be some differences in quantifying targets, the Committee is confident at a strategic level, the plan is coherent, well-integrated, and positioned to address national, regional, and global development imperatives.
Ms Mbuda-Mutshakweni said the Committee underscored the importance of public participation as a central component of its mandate, particularly in the dual role of law-making and holding the executive accountable. Public involvement ensures that laws are not only shaped by policymakers but also informed by the voices of citizens who are directly impacted by such legislation. To achieve this, the Committee works in close collaboration with units within Parliament which provide essential support to public participation programs. For instance, during the processing of the BELA Bill, Parliamentary Communication Services played a key role in disseminating information through media alerts, statements, and radio broadcasts, ensuring that the public was informed and engaged ahead of hearings. Similarly, the Parliamentary Democracy Offices in the provinces undertake public education initiatives to prepare communities for hearings and submissions, ensuring that when citizens voice their support or opposition to a bill, they are doing so from an informed position.
This collaborative approach highlights the critical role of communication, education, and outreach in strengthening democracy and accountability. However, one of the risks identified relates to resource constraints, as effective public participation requires sufficient financial and human resources to expand outreach and ensure inclusivity. The support team committed to refining these aspects in the revised strategic plan, incorporating all suggestions raised during this session.
Ms Mbuda-Mutshakweni thanked everyone as the plan is a collective effort and will continue to be refined to ensure it reflects the aspirations of Parliament and the people it serves.
In closing, the Chairperson thanked everyone and encouraged members to submit written comments that could be incorporated into the final draft. She acknowledged the length of recent meetings which reflects the dedication of Parliament to fulfilling its mandate on behalf of South Africans. Appreciation was extended to the support team for their diligent work in preparing the draft Strategic Plan. Members were reminded of the upcoming oversight visit to the Free State noting the importance of this program in strengthening oversight.
Meeting adjourned.
