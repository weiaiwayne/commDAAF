Meeting Summary
Video
WC Appropriation Bill 2025
Western Cape Appropriation Bill
The Standing Committee on the Premier and Constitutional Matters in the Western Cape Provincial Parliament convened virtually for deliberations and a public hearing on the Western Cape Appropriation Bill 2025 Vote 1: Department of the Premier (DotP), and also considered the Committee report on the Vote.
The Committee was satisfied with the proposed budget, but raised concerns about the ability of the DotP to deliver services, given the constrained fiscal environment. The Department acknowledged the impact of financial and non-financial risks on the sustainability of service delivery, but assured the Committee that measures were in place to mitigate both internal and external risks in meeting the growing demand for services.
The ANC and PA expressed the minority view to not support Vote 1, and GOOD elected to withhold its Vote pending the budget debate.
The Committee report supporting the Vote was adopted.
No member of the public was on the platform to make a submission.
Meeting report
Budget overview
Premier's opening remarks
Mr Alan Winde, Western Cape Premier, remarked that the team from the Department was well represented. The [Western Cape] Minister of Finance had
tabled
the budget the day before. The budget of R2.012 billion represented a 4.3% reduction in the adjusted budget, which meant that the Department did not receive more money. The biggest part of the budget was used in key focus areas, such as connectivity and e-governance.
The 82 mobile centres were providing connectivity to citizens. Public wi-fi hotspots have been increased to 1 600. The Broadband 2.0 agreement, which was concluded in this financial year, signalled the next stage of connectivity. The goal was to increase the footprint in the province to 2 150 connected sites over the 2025 medium term expenditure framework (MTEF) period. The broadband investment was meant to improve connectivity in rural areas, ensuring the availability of e-learning platforms in the education sector, it would also enable electronic record-keeping in the health sector to assist patients outside the city centre and in rural areas with better opportunities for consultations with specialists in the city centres. Further areas covered by the broadband investment included libraries and museums and assisting with the apex project of growing the economy and jobs in the province. The investment would help with managing security risks against cyber-attacks. Some departments and municipalities have been subjected to massive cyber-attacks.
The investment in training was important to remain relevant and innovative through the continuous training which had been taking place at the training institute in Stellenbosch and the satellite centre in George. The training was done in preparation for the increasing role of artificial intelligence (AI) in government. The continuation of the "culture journey" was to ensure government was continuously improving services and its interaction with citizens.
The provincial government received extra funding to host some of the G20, B20, and other related meetings. Meetings with the Presidency had been taking place to ensure the province was adequately prepared for visitors, and that it would benefit from the events. A multi-sectoral approach was being followed for the B20 meetings. The first B20 meeting had gone well. The anti-corruption G20 cluster meeting in Stellenbosch presented opportunities to showcase what was being done locally and learn from others about good governance practices.
The earmarked funding for the continuation of the energy plan was meant to proceed with the energy supply programme. The biggest risk to the economy was the instability in the energy supply, as proved by the latest load-shedding occurrences. As the fastest-growing province, the Western Cape government needed more resources to fulfil its core mandates. It had been agreed to include water sustainability as part of the committee that had been dealing with energy supply to prevent a day-zero scenario at municipalities. Treasury had provided R25 million extra to compensate for the volatility in the rand/dollar exchange rate. One of the fringe benefits of the B20 was the opportunity to engage with Microsoft representatives from the United States. Further meetings would be arranged to mitigate the volatility in the exchange rate when paying the licence fees. The Premier asked the Director-General (DG) to add any information that he might have missed in his introduction.
Director General's overview
Dr Harry Malila, DG, Department of the Premier (DotP), said that the vision and mission of the Department was to build a government that was trusted by the people. His intention was to connect the broader aspects in the Premier’s overview to the key portfolios, i.e. economic growth and jobs, safety, education, and health. As a transversal priority, the Department has been playing a key role in ensuring innovation, culture, and governance as a backbone to the key portfolios. The Department had been working daily to ensure delivery of all priorities.
The programmes being offered at the provincial training institute in Stellenbosch were geared towards building a capable state. The Western Cape Government had been certified as a top employer for a third consecutive year. More work was being done around human resource practices. The broadband initiatives were aimed at improving the lives of citizens. The big goal was to improve governance by working through a lens of service, in collaboration with the Corporate Assurance, Legal, and Treasury departments. It was envisaged to grow jobs in the infrastructure and development planning environments by using artificial intelligence (AI).
The DotP had a good relationship with national government on most programmes. The provincial strategic plan (PSP) reflected a close alignment with different priorities in the National Development Plan (NDP). The Department had been working closely with the National School of Governance (NSG), National Treasury, and other government institutions. The priorities included tackling issues of poverty, the high cost of living and youth unemployment, by growing the economy and creating jobs. The objective was to deliver on the five-year vision, ending in 2029, of the Western Cape Government.
Discussion
Mr K Sayed (ANC) asked the Premier to indicate how e-Governance had improved service delivery. He wanted to know how many citizens had benefited from e-governance and which specific areas were targeted. He asked if e-Governance was being used as a monitoring and evaluation (M&E) tool within the Premiers’ Office. He enquired about the synergy between the Infrastructure Department and the maintenance of water and energy infrastructure. He asked for an explanation about the housing backlog and questioned the shift from Human Settlements to Infrastructure.
Premier Winde replied that a record of the number of people making use of e-Governance through digital connectivity was kept. M&E was a separate responsibility, but the systems could be used to help with this function. The digital platforms being rolled out had different functions aimed at different people. Back-office data on the type of requests from users was available -- for example, the number of learners doing research for school projects, people looking for jobs, education platforms for higher learning, and how departments had been using the platforms for e-filing.
The Department had been working closely with the Department of Social Development (DSD) to enable social workers to work more efficiently and to be less onerous in their reporting so that files could be used from anywhere in the province. The data centre was making it possible to view the level of data use at schools. The Department of Education had been using more megabytes per day than the rest of the country.
The Energy Council in the Premier’s Office was setup to deal with energy sustainability, and was headed by an energy expert. The infrastructure, education, and health departments were key in using solar panels at parking lots and on school roofs as a means to supply energy to the grid. R50 million had been paid to citizens and businesses for transferring energy to the grid. It was still early days for the Water Council, but water sustainability would be dealt with in the same manner.
The biggest challenge in the country was the failure to enable economic growth because of failing infrastructure. Premier Winde explained that Human Settlements was a unit within the Department of Infrastructure. The idea was to coordinate all infrastructure projects across the province and in local government under one plan, and to manage it based one vision and one budget.
Dr Malila said the Internet Portal registered more than 100 million contacts within the Western Cape Government. The broadband environment was located across systems with more than 2 000 connected sites and more than 1 600 public wi-fi hotspots. The parents of more than 1.1 million children in the education system had been using the platform for school enrolments. More than 15 million contacts per year have been registered in the health environment. He said that human settlements were being managed in terms of an integrated strategy based on the developmental agenda. The 2050 vision for human settlements anticipated involving the public sector at the national and local government level, as well as the private sector. He undertook to send a formal invitation to the Committee for a visit to the data centre to gain a better understanding of the entire environment and for improved decision-making.
The Chairperson welcomed the invitation.
Mr Sayed sought clarity about the synergy between the work of the energy and water councils and the work of infrastructure departments in local government. He wanted to know why the councils were not located in the departments. He said the argument was being made that centralising mandates of various departments would increase the red tape on service delivery. He requested specific data on where the broadband sites were installed.
Premier Winde replied that the energy and water councils were under his chairmanship because it was his priority to reduce risks and to make decisions accordingly. Both energy and water were not the constitutional mandate of provinces, but of national and local government. The meetings were chaired by the Premier, who was taking responsibility for ensuring urgency in meeting the ultimate goal. The committees have been working with other provinces, such as the Northern Cape and Eastern Cape provinces, to ensure synergy.
DG Malila agreed to provide the Committee with data on the location of broadband sites. He explained that a separate ministerial infrastructure committee was in place, but feedback was provided to the special committees on energy and water to deal with issues such as climate change. The Department remained responsible for the implementation of energy and water projects. The energy council was taking responsibility, although the energy budget was spread across departments.
The Chairperson requested the DG to brief the Committee on Vote 1.
Vote 1
Dr Malila said the Premier had highlighted the key allocations in Vote 1. The total budget of R2.012 billion was spread across the key programmes of the Department -- Executive Governance and Integration, Provincial Strategic Management, People Management, Centre for e-Innovation, Corporate Assurance and Legal Services. The Premier elaborated on all these issues in his overview, and the details were set out in the budget. The annual performance plan (APP) had been tabled in Parliament and provided a more detailed presentation of the finances of the Department, supported by the five-year strategic plan of the Western Cape. All these plans culminated in the ultimate goal of innovation, governance and culture to the benefit of the citizens. The Department was primarily providing support to the entire system.
Discussion
The Chairperson assured the Committee of the briefing about the APP and the five-year plan, but the focus of this meeting was on Vote 1. She indicated that she had clustered the sections in the budget. She requested Members to apply a limit of five questions for the first round. [see attached document]

Main services, Core functions and responsibilities (pages 1 to 6)
Mr Sayed said that during the State of the Province Address (SOPA) address in the Karoo, the Premier had promised gender-based violence (GBV) and job creation programmes to the residents of Beaufort West. He asked if these programmes would be funded by the budget of the Department of the Premier, or by other departments.
Premier Winde replied that Growth for Jobs (G4J) programmes in any district were about the whole of government approach, but it would be funded mainly out of the G4J cluster budget of the Department of Economic Development and Tourism (DEDAT). GBV programmes would be funded either by the Department of Social Development or the Department of Health.
Revenue of the 2024/25 financial year (pages 12 to 16)
Mr Sayed noted that the Department had obtained a clean audit outcome for the 2023/24 financial year and had spent 98% of its appropriated funds. He asked if the Premier was confident of spending closer to 100% in this financial year and if measures were in place to ensure that it happened. He commented that the Western Cape government’s participation in the 11th Regional Leadership Summit in China, and the visit to Taiwan by the Member of the Executive Council (MEC) for Agriculture, was a political and not a departmental visit. Given the political context, he asked if the Premier should not be reviewing the pronouncements of the MEC that related to trade.
The Premier said participation in the Regional Leadership Summit had been in place since the first democratic government, and had not changed over the years. The gathering was taking place every two years. Cape Town would be hosting the event this year, and would be handing over the chair to the next province. The province had been benefiting from the Hisense investment, and had a number of agreements with China. He regarded China as an important trading partner. He said Dr Ivan Meyer (Minister of Agriculture) was not on an official visit to Taiwan.
He remarked that it was unrealistic to expect 100% budget expenditure. Strict management was applied to avoid massive accruals and to not allow dumping to get to 100%. He said 99% of unpaid invoices was due to people who submitted invoices, and not to the financial team. He was proud of the track record of the team.
DG Malila confirmed that invoices were paid within 30 days, and a monthly report was forwarded to the Premier. He explained that the underspending of more than R20 million was related to staff exits (R2 million), office accommodation for the Commissioner for Children (R12 million), and a capital asset delay attributed to the supplier (R9 million). He invited the Committee to an investment conference scheduled to take place in November 2025. The aim of the conference was to create an enabling environment for economic growth and employment.
Mr L van Wyk (DA) said he had been involved in the problem-driven iterative adaption (PDIA) initiative in the past and had found it a useful programme. He enquired about the progress of this programme.
The Premier replied that the PDIA team was the first to use the methodology which was developed by Harvard University and which had since been embedded within the methodology of the Western Cape Government. Officials followed online courses on a solution-driven approach that various departments were using. Successes to date included fast tracking the decision-making process for the approval of heritage programmes, in collaboration with national government, and reducing the number of Golden Arrow bus hijackings. Hotspots were identified and municipalities were tasked to fix potholes to ensure that buses could drive freely. In addition, steel boxes were provided in which cash was dropped.
Dr Malila said meetings were held recently to unpack and solve problems in the procurement and safety environments at municipalities. The fishbone strategy was applied by unpacking issues line by line to identify the areas where processes get stuck.
Outlook for the coming financial year (pages 17 to 21)
Mr T Walters (DA) wanted to know what the Premier considered to be the biggest risk given the constrained fiscal environment.
Premier Winde said the provincial budget had been tabled while the national budget had not been confirmed. The budget was based on the original allocation at the national level, linked to provinces. He regarded the biggest risk as the fast-growing population, accompanied by the increasing demand for more services in terms of food security, health and education services, especially for the most vulnerable people in society. Risk management was a strong focus in the Department, along with getting the job done.
DG Malila regarded the budget shortfall as a risk, if grants were to be cut. The cost of employment (COE) budget would also be at risk, because the money to fill vacancies would not be available. The rand/dollar exchange rate was an additional risk, because Microsoft licence fees were paid in dollars. He further highlighted transversal risks such as floods, which would have an impact on the Department should they materialise.
Ms Henriette Robson, Deputy Director-General (DDG): Corporate Assurance, DOtP, noted several external risks which were complex and not solvable in the short-term, including climate change, water and energy security, unemployment, regression of infrastructure, and safety. Actions were in place to mitigate these risks. Internal risks included the ability to sustain businesses due to budget cuts and staff safety concerns, which had been prominent in the media. The Department had been monitoring progress on action plans through the work of the water and energy committees, and submits quarterly reports to Cabinet.
Ms D Stephens (PA) asked how much of the budget had been allocated to the benefit of disadvantaged communities. She enquired about the measures in place to address irregular and wasteful expenditure.
The DG replied that R1.3 billion of the R2 billion budget had been allocated for broadband connectivity. Most of the beneficiaries of broadband connectivity were in poor communities. Almost every e-Centre was situated in poor communities and outside the metro, and most of the schools were in quantiles one to three. The broadband initiative was enabling young people to look for jobs and economic opportunities. He reiterated that a large part of the budget was targeted at the poor.
Receipts and financing (pages 22 to 24)
Mr B Herron (GOOD) commented that the budget allocation for e-Innovation over the past two years had been underestimated. He asked what the reasons were for the differences. He referred to page 52 of the budget, and asked if the R1.3 billion allocated for spending in Cape Town's boundaries was confined to the metro.
Response
Dr Malila said the biggest part of the budget had been allocated to the Centre for e-Innovation. The rand/dollar exchange rate was the biggest risk. In terms of an agreement with National Treasury, an adjustment was allowed when the allocation was insufficient. He explained that the anticipated number of broadband sites had not been rolled out due to safety concerns within communities, which had had an impact on the ability to deliver services. The new broadband contractual agreements would be coming into effect in the 2025/26 financial year. The spatial distribution on page 52 related to the areas where payments were taking place, and according to services as determined by the head office.
Programme 2: Provincial Strategic Management (pages 28 to 30)
Mr Herron enquired about the estimated allocation of R10 million for the Children’s Commissioner, which had been reduced to R6 million in the two outer years. He sought an explanation for the drastic cut and asked if the function was being managed by the DotP or if the Commissioner was operating as a stand-alone function. He wanted to know if the R36 million energy allocation was for the energy council.
The DG explained that the R10 million allocated in the first year was for office accommodation, and the R6 million in the outer years was for salaries, and goods and services. The accommodation was subject to a court process for the eviction of the existing tenants from the identified building. The R6 million was being managed by the DotP. The Commissioner was a statutory appointment but the staff were government employees, paid via the personnel salary system (PERSAL). The R36 million was not related to the energy council, but for demand-side management, municipal programmes, and capacity building programmes to provide specialised technical support to implement the programmes. A quarterly progress report was forwarded to Provincial Treasury.
Mr Sayed asked if the performance management model involved a programme to monitor progress and performance.
The DG confirmed that a monitoring programme was being used, and a quarterly performance report was forwarded to Cabinet. The APP indicators were a standing issue on the agenda.
Ms Z Ismail, Chief Director: Strategic Management Information, DOtP, replied that the national performance management policy was being reviewed. Preparations were underway to evolve from the integrated performance review model to a multidimensional performance review model, taking into account indicators on developmental factors such as improving society. The model was more advanced than using the APP indicators.
Mr Walters welcomed the deeper performance appraisal model beyond monitoring the APP indicators. With reference to the transversal element, he asked if the approach would be extended to local government.
The DG responded that the Department responsible for governance would soon be tabling the quarterly report on local government and municipalities dealing with financial and non-financial matters, as well as governance performance. DEDAT was playing a key role from a legal perspective, and corporate assurance was providing assistance on issues of risk management. The information technology (IT) department had been assisting municipalities with system-related problems. Working with municipalities was important to improve governance. The Department of the Premier was playing a strategic role. The Premier regularly meets with the Auditor-General (AG) on the performance of municipalities, and reports back to the local AG’s Office.
Premier Winde confirmed his regular meetings with the AG, which would take place prior to the AG visiting the legislature. The AG was prominent in the meetings with the municipalities. The challenge to the mayors was to get all municipalities to the same level, with unqualified audit outcomes. Proper service delivery is not possible without good governance.
Programme 4: Centre for e-Innovation (pages 33 to 36)
Mr Sayed asked how the earmarked allocation of R502 million would assist in rolling out the broadband ecosystem, and if it would deal with service delivery challenges. He wanted to know if the online learner application system would be reviewed to address the glitches in the system.
Dr Malila replied that the contractual commitment of Broadband 2.0 was allowing for the expansion of services to more than 2 000 sites in the Western Cape. The rollout was meant to enable better access to the more than 1 600 public wi-fi hotspots. The performance had improved since the previous year.
Mr Evan Alexander, Chief Director: Connected Government and Infrastructure Services, DOtP, said that the increasing rollout of broadband across the province had resulted in 2 150 sites being connected. He undertook to share the details with the Committee. As part of the Broadband 2.0 tender, more sites had been identified, as well as the departments which would be benefiting from the rollout. Access to online systems would be making improved service delivery possible for users of the system.
An official from the Department drew attention to the e-Kiosk pilot project, which involved placing service stations at facilities such as libraries. A personal computer was placed in the foyer and manned by someone to provide assistance. The online learner admission system had been open for two weeks. More than 109 000 applications had been registered on the first day, compared to the 61 000 applications on the first day in the previous year. This proved that the system was operating seamlessly and issues were easily being resolved.
Mr Sayed requested a breakdown of operational expenses compared to service delivery as it related to the programme on people management.
Dr Malila asked Mr Sayed to clarify his question. He explained that HR-related matters were dealt with in Programme 3 under sub-programmes -- organisation development involving health and wellness across departments, people training and empowerment for capacity building at the Stellenbosch and George institutions, goods and services for catering, and people management practices dealing with transversal policy development of recruitment and staff benefits, across departments.
Mr Sayed sought clarity on the percentage expenditure on operational matters compared to service delivery results.
The DG replied that this was not a service delivery department, but an enabling department to empower people to deliver services.
Paragraph 10: Other programme information (pages 41 to 53)
Mr Sayed referred to table 10.1 on page 41, and questioned the budget allocation of 32.1% for the Centre for e-Innovation compared to the 0.9% allocated for interns, the expanded public works programme (EPWP), and learnerships.
DG Malila replied that the 32.1% included most of the people in the DotP, and the complement of technical staff servicing all 13 departments. It was anticipated that the move to the digitised AI environment would result in people exiting the system to take up other jobs and new opportunities. Interns and learnerships were deployed across departments. A decrease in the salaries was based on the national framework and the salary levels as determined by the Department of Public Service and Administration (DPSA).
Mr Drikus Basson, Chief Financial Officer (CFO), DOtP, said the salary differences could be attributed to the difference in ratio between paid interns and graduate interns.
Mr Sayed noted the decrease in the earmarked allowance for the Children’s Commissioner. He asked why only two of the three vacancies in the Office of the Children’s Commissioner were filled, and requested an explanation for the underspending.
Mr Van Wyk referred to table A.3 on page 51. He wanted to know what the provincial payments to the Cape Town Metro and Stellenbosch were related to.
The DG replied that the Stellenbosch payment was related to the training institute mentioned in Programme 3. The Cape Town Metro payment represented the difference between the total appropriation and the Cape
Winelands' allocation.
He explained that filling the vacancies had proved to be difficult, but the Department had prioritised the appointment of staff for the Office of the Children’s Commissioner. The underspending is related to office accommodation for the Commissioner. The amount was rolled over to the new year due to challenges beyond the Department’s control. The normal budget of R6 million would apply once the premises were secured. The Office of the Commissioner had made a separate funding application to the Provincial
Treasury, but it had been rejected due to a lack of evidence that the full budget would be spent.
Public submissions
The Chairperson confirmed that no member of the public was on the platform to make a submission.
She thanked the Premier, DG and officials of the Department for their participation and for answering the questions, and allowed them to exit the platform.
Deliberations on the draft Committee report
The Chairperson invited Members to express their views on Vote 1.
Ms W Kaizer-Philander (DA) said that having perused the Vote and listening to the input and budget objectives of the Premier, the DG and the management team, she found that the strategic allocation in Vote 1 reflected a commitment to innovation, efficiency and effective governance. Despite the reduction in funding for the Centre for e-Innovation, it continued to be a priority in the critical role of digital transformation and enhancing service delivery. The investment in people management was further ensuring a balanced approach to meeting departmental demands. By advancing initiatives such as Broadband 2.0, and by strengthening data-driven decision-making, this budget appeared to support a future-ready government that was responsive, adaptive, and equipped to serve the people of the Western Cape effectively. She believed that, on average, the Western Cape's fiscal discipline had been better than the rest of the country. She was satisfied that the risk assessment strategy was beyond reproach, which was clear in the performance report. On behalf of the DA, she expressed support for Vote 1.
Mr Van Wyk supported the summary by Ms Kaizer-Philander. The amount of funding directed to the Centre for e-Innovation and the implementation of broadband and wi-fi systems should be accompanied by a publicity campaign to raise awareness amongst citizens about the access to free internet across the province. The benefits of an e-Innovation Centre had become clear on an oversight visit to the small town of Merweville a month ago. He fully supported Vote 1.
Mr Walters confirmed his support of Vote 1.
Mr Sayed noted the positive aspects of the Vote, but a number of challenges had been highlighted through the questions that he had raised. On behalf of the ANC, he elected not to support Vote 1. The reasons would be unpacked during the budget debate.
The Chairperson asked if he was registering a minority view at this point.
Mr Sayed confirmed he was expressing the minority view.
Ms Stephens said that the PA would provide reasons for not supporting the Vote in the budget debate.
Mr Herron said his party would have to consider the entire budget to decide on whether to support Vote 1. It was not a minority view, but a withholding of the view.
The Chairperson requested the Procedural Officer to screen the draft report, reflecting the views of the various political parties.
Ms Waseemah Kamish-Achmat, the Procedural Officer, presented the draft report for perusal by Members.
The Chairperson sought clarity from Mr Herron on how his view should be recorded.
Mr Herron said he wished it to be recorded that his party was not ready to support the Vote.
The Chairperson asked if all Members were in support of the draft report.
Ms Kaizer-Philander said given the views expressed by the various political parties, she supported the adoption of the report.
Mr Walters and Mr Van Wyk both confirmed the adoption of the report.
The Chairperson obtained confirmation for the adoption of the report by Members of the other political parties.
Adoption of minutes
The Committee considered and adopted the minutes of the meeting dated 25 March. No amendments were made.
Resolutions and actions
The Chairperson proposed that the Committee accept the offer of the Department to visit the Data Centre, and to attend the Investment Conference in November 2025.
The meeting was adjourned.
