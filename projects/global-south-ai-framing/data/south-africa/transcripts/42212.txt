Annual Reports 2024/25
The Portfolio Committee on Higher Education met to receive a briefing from the Auditor-General of South Africa (AGSA) on recent audit outcomes and to consider the annual reports of the National Student Financial Aid Scheme (NSFAS) for 2024/25 and the National Skills Fund (NSF) for 2023/24.
AGSA reported an adverse audit opinion for NSFAS and a qualified opinion for the NSF. In both cases, the audit findings pointed to serious weaknesses in financial management, performance reporting and regulatory compliance. These deficiencies were attributed to prolonged leadership instability, reliance on outdated and largely manual systems, and weak internal controls.
Committee Members expressed deep concern about the ongoing governance and operational failures at both entities. Discussion centred on the NSF’s inconsistent explanations for its failure to fill critical vacancies, its continued underspending despite a clear skills development mandate, and the lack of effective consequence management for officials linked to poor audit outcomes and irregular expenditure. Members also raised serious allegations of conflicts of interest within the NSF and criticised the absence of the accounting authority and the responsible Minister from the meeting, which limited the Committee’s ability to enforce direct accountability. The NSF’s failure to conduct a tracer study for the past two years was highlighted as a significant shortcoming, leaving it unable to demonstrate the employment impact of its funded programmes.
NSFAS presented a turnaround plan aimed at stabilising leadership, implementing a new loan management system and ensuring the timely closure of projects. The NSF reported that it had introduced a new digital system to address reporting weaknesses and was pursuing consequence management through external investigations. Officials from both entities acknowledged that previous reporting on recovery efforts had been overstated and that progress to date had been unacceptably slow.
The Chairperson described the NSF’s annual report as extremely poor and criticised the lack of decisive action on material irregularities identified by AGSA. He indicated that the Committee would adopt a more rigorous and consequential oversight approach in the coming year, stressing that officials responsible for the mismanagement of public funds must be held accountable.

AGSA
Presentation
Mr Ignatius Fourie, Senior Manager, AGSA, reported that the audit of the NSFAS for the 2023/24 financial year resulted in adverse outcomes, with material findings across the financial statements, performance reporting and compliance.
He said the financial statements contained significant misstatements, including inaccurately reported close-out project results that could not be verified against institutional records, as well as presentation deficiencies that did not comply with reporting standards. Performance information was found to be unreliable due to weak indicator definitions, poor measurement processes and indicators that did not reflect actual performance. Compliance findings included the late submission of financial statements, ineffective strategic planning, inadequate revenue collection, failure to prevent irregular expenditure and serious procurement weaknesses. He added that the irregular expenditure balance, disclosed as more than R84 billion, could not be verified for completeness and had therefore been disclaimed.
Mr Fourie reported that several operational and governance weaknesses contributed to these outcomes. NSFAS continued to operate on manual processes, outdated and fragmented information technology infrastructure and an unsuitable loan-book system. There was no integrated system linking NSFAS with institutions. Capacity constraints in the finance and information technology environments had resulted in a heavy dependence on consultants, while internal capability remained weak. Coordination of information from institutions was inadequate, action plans were insufficient, and leadership monitoring was limited. Strategic planning and quality control processes for performance reporting were poor, and procurement processes showed indicators of fraud risk.
He stated that several material irregularities had been identified or remained unresolved, including disbursements that exceeded contracted amounts, outstanding balances owed by institutions, and interest not charged on student loan accounts. Although implementation deadlines had been extended several times, key issues remained outstanding. As a result, AGSA issued remedial actions in October 2025, requiring NSFAS to act within six months and to submit progress reports every two months.
Mr Fourie said AGSA recommended urgent strengthening of the information technology environment, including the development of appropriate systems, a comprehensive loan-management system and the replacement of legacy infrastructure. Leadership stability was identified as essential to improving governance and driving reforms. He said action plans required strengthening, regular monitoring and supporting operational procedures. He further recommended regular reconciliations, stronger financial controls, stricter adherence to procurement requirements and timely submission of statutory information. He encouraged the Portfolio Committee to support and monitor the implementation of these reforms and to reinforce accountability.

NSFAS Presentation
Dr Johanna Maphutha, Board Member: Education, NSFAS, said the Board had noted the AGSA’s findings and would allow the Acting CEO to respond to the issues raised.
Mr Carrim said NSFAS acknowledged the adverse audit opinion issued by AGSA for the 2023/24 financial year. He reported that the period under review had been marked by significant leadership instability. The CEO had been placed on special leave and later dismissed, the Chief Information Officer (CIO) had resigned, and the Board Chairperson had stepped down. This culminated in the dissolution of the Board and the appointment of an administrator. He said these disruptions had affected governance, operational stability and the finalisation of the Annual Financial Statements (AFS), which required an extended post-audit review before submission.
Mr Carrim said NSFAS’s overall performance—reflected in only 41% of annual targets achieved—had been negatively affected by operational inefficiencies, late student payments, leadership instability and long-standing information technology system weaknesses. He noted that several performance indicators had been poorly defined, performance evidence had been unreliable, and legacy IT systems were not integrated with institutions, resulting in manual workarounds and human error. These shortcomings contributed directly to AGSA’s findings on unreliable performance reporting.
He acknowledged AGSA’s findings on irregular, fruitless and wasteful expenditure. Irregular expenditure had increased due to procurement non-compliance and failures related to the NSFAS Act. In contrast, fruitless and wasteful expenditure stemmed from unused assets such as laptops and Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP) licences. He added that investigations were ongoing, including a Special Investigating Unit (SIU) investigation and a separate probe into irregularities in the appointment of direct payment service providers.
Mr Carrim outlined several interventions underway to address the audit findings. A new strategy and Annual Performance Plan (APP) had been developed with clearer indicators, and standard operating procedures for performance reporting were being introduced. Quarterly performance reviews had been implemented, an operational plan aligned to targets had been developed, and work was progressing to strengthen planning and reporting capacity. He said NSFAS was prioritising leadership stabilisation by filling critical executive posts and improving internal controls, financial management and compliance systems.
He further stated that NSFAS accepted AGSA’s findings on outdated IT infrastructure and had committed to procuring an automated loan-management system and developing a broader information and communications technology and digital-transformation strategy. NSFAS was working to improve data reliability, restore normal reporting timelines and rebuild relationships with regulatory bodies. The close-out project was continuing, supported by updated data-extraction methods intended to resolve historical reconciliation discrepancies at universities and Technical and Vocational Education and Training (TVET) colleges.
Mr Carrim said NSFAS viewed its response as focused on restoring leadership stability, strengthening governance, improving internal processes and implementing long-term structural reforms to address the root causes of the adverse audit opinion.

He said that by working with key entities such as Home Affairs, the South African Social Security Agency (SASSA), the South African Revenue Service (SARS), and National Treasury, NSFAS aimed to verify funding decisions through multiple sources. He stressed, however, that NSFAS still had a responsibility to account for incorrect funding granted in the past and to recover those funds, including working with relevant stakeholders such as the SIU.
Second, he said NSFAS had allowed its loan system to become dysfunctional. He explained that steps were being taken to address this, including strengthening the internal capacity of the loan function, implementing a viable loan system capable of managing the loan book daily, and ensuring that recoverable loans were actively collected. He clarified that this did not involve pursuing poor South Africans but focused on individuals who had become employed and accumulated assets, and who therefore had the means to repay loans they had taken before 2017. He added that loans would increasingly become part of the policy mix and that NSFAS needed a functional loan system to manage the missing middle loan fund effectively.
Third, he highlighted the need for improved operational capability to complete the historical close-out project within the dates provided. He stressed the importance of implementing a methodology that would prevent the repetition of past delays, stating that by the end of the 2025 financial year, the academic year 2025 also needed to be closed. He emphasised that NSFAS could not continue a situation where, in 2027, it would still be closing the 2025 academic year. He said institutions that failed to comply with submission requirements would face punitive measures.
He concluded that these three issues were the major challenges affecting NSFAS and that the outlined steps would need to be monitored to ensure improvements in both operational capability and financial reporting.
Mr Louw said he thanked the Acting CEO for his response and explained that it would enable the Committee to hold him accountable the following year when they reached 2026. He then turned to the issue of the tracer study and said he had noted that for two consecutive years, NSF had failed to appoint a service provider. He explained that this failure implied that money had been spent without the entity being able to determine whether learners or students had transitioned into employment or placement programmes. He said that without this information, NSF continued spending money without knowing whether its interventions were effective. He asked what mitigating mechanisms had been put in place while the entity was still searching for a service provider and what had caused the delay in securing a suitable provider, especially since the tracer study formed part of NSF’s key performance indicators (KPIs). He stressed that if NSF could not trace how its funds were utilised, fruitless expenditure would result and accountability would be undermined. He asked what temporary measures were in place to compensate for the absence of a service provider and whether NSF could provide proof of interim activities, such as efforts to align and track beneficiaries. He added that spending and tracing needed to be reconciled, and he requested clarity on the entity’s tracing strategy.
Ms Erra acknowledged that the NSF had failed to meet the tracer study target for the past two reporting periods. She began with the mitigation steps and explained that it was essential for the NSF to track its beneficiaries, including whether they had obtained the correct qualifications and whether their stipends were paid on time. She said that while procurement processes for a service provider were ongoing and experiencing delays, the NSF had adopted interim internal measures. She explained that the NSF had reviewed the methodologies used by other SETAs and by the National Skills Authority (NSA), which had standing survey instruments.
The NSF had benchmarked these approaches and, by the end of March, had conducted a small-scale survey to engage with beneficiaries. She clarified that although the survey was limited in scale, it helped to build internal capacity. She added that the NSF had also approached the W&R SETA and others about partnering on research chairs, since various stakeholders in the PSET sector conducted research. She stated that if Indlela, for example, was already conducting research, the NSF should not duplicate those efforts. She said these partnerships would allow the NSF to share research services rather than building a standalone research unit and would prevent duplication across the system.
Regarding delays, she explained that the NSF did not have a dedicated SCM unit and that procurement remained part of shared services with the Department. She said the NSF had two outstanding matters with the DHET concerning SCM processes and that once a director for SCM was appointed specifically for the NSF, procurement would no longer have to rely on the Department’s processes.
Mr Louw then asked if the delay resulted from the absence of a dedicated unit and whether, despite DHET’s support, NSF still could not appoint a service provider. He sought clarity on whether she was suggesting the Department itself was causing the delay.
Ms Erra responded that the NSF could not appoint a service provider because tenders had to go through the DHET bid adjudication committees. She said the NSF could manage quotations internally through a small team but lacked the capacity to manage tenders.
Mr Louw asked directly whether the DHET was responsible for delaying the appointment and why the appointment had not taken place.
Ms Erra replied that she could not answer the question.
Mr Louw then directed the question to the Acting DG and asked why the Department had not appointed a service provider for the tracer study, emphasising that it was a key KPI linked to budgeting.
Ms Futshane said she was aware of delays in some SCM processes. She explained more generally that the CFO had previously reported the unavailability of members of the bid evaluation and adjudication committees. She said new members had recently been appointed to these committees. She undertook to consult the CFO for a specific response to this tracer study matter and said the initial part of her response addressed general issues that might have also affected this case. She noted that the CFO, who could provide more detail, had stepped out but that she would follow up and report back.
Mr Louw then asked whether the Department was effectively saying it struggled to appoint its own internal committees needed to handle procurement and service delivery processes.
Ms Futshane replied that this was not what she had indicated. She said the appointment of committee members had not been the issue; the challenge had been that appointed members were unavailable to meet. She explained that the solution had been to appoint additional members so that committee work could proceed without delays.
Mr Louw said the matter needed to be resolved because funds were being allocated without the necessary mechanisms to ensure accountability. He asked that the Committee be provided with a report when they next met so that they could reconcile what the NSF had spent with the corresponding outcomes of the tracer study.
Mr Louw then moved to the next issue and said that the CFO had previously reported on funding for Small, Medium, and Micro Enterprises (SMMEs). He said there had been a shortfall affecting 47 companies that were supposed to be funded under a training and education initiative. He explained that the shortfall had resulted from challenges experienced by the Small Enterprise Finance Agency (SEFA), which had led to non-reporting. He said the matter had been escalated to the DG, and he asked for an update. He wanted to know at what point during the 2024/25 period the NSF had become aware that the SEFA project was experiencing implementation challenges.
Ms Erra explained that, regarding the Small Enterprise Development and Finance Agency (Sedfa) project for SMMEs, the matter had already been escalated before she was appointed. She said her understanding was that it had been brought to the attention of the accounting authority and the former acting CEO sometime before September 2024. However, she did not know the exact dates. She reported that, upon her appointment, the project management team informed her of non-compliance issues and complaints relating to learner stipends. She stated that a non-compliance letter had since been issued to Sedfa, and a remedial plan had been initiated to determine what needed to be completed. She added that Sedfa had failed to submit eight quarterly financial reports and eight quarterly performance reports. She summarised that the NSF had been verifying the information, but the issues raised with Sedfa were significant. She said the matter would now be referred to the LJRC and the State Attorney.
Mr Louw then said he wanted to pose three final questions. He asked how much had been allocated to the project, whether the entity was able to report outside the reporting period, and why the NSF was outsourcing the function instead of performing it itself.
Ms Erra said she would need to verify the amount allocated, as she did not want to provide incorrect information. She explained that if a contract had expired, an entity could apply for reinstatement and then submit its information. She stated that the NSF always welcomed SDPs to submit performance and financial information. However, she added that in this particular case, there were serious issues she did not wish to detail in the meeting, and there were limitations that would not be resolvable from a reporting perspective, which was why the matter was being referred. She began to explain why the work had not been outsourced to another service provider, but Mr Louw interjected.
Mr Louw repeated that he was asking why the NSF did not simply implement the project itself rather than outsourcing it.
Ms Erra said the initial plan for the project had been to support SMMEs, and that Sedfa had been selected because it offered relevant services, which justified the partnership. She said the NSF could work with other small businesses if needed, but such an approach would require re-conceptualisation. She added that the NSF was willing to do this, given its existing artisan development work, noting that when an artisan, such as a plumber, was trained, the intention was often to support them in becoming a small business. She said this demonstrated the value of the partnership model. She explained that, based on the concept notes at the time, Sedfa had appeared to be the best implementing partner to help develop the businesses. She informed the Committee that Sedfa had, in fact, outsourced the project itself and had not implemented it directly. She agreed that, as Mr Louw had suggested, the NSF could implement the project internally if necessary.
The Chairperson asked what process was followed when appointing individuals to act in vacant positions.
Ms Erra explained that, depending on the level of the post, budget considerations applied, and HR was notified when a vacancy arose. She said the relevant manager would inform HR, which would then identify internal candidates who met the criteria. She noted that HR and the unit would engage on the proposed names, after which a submission would be sent to the Minister for approval.
The Chairperson then used the example of the HR Manager who was acting in the Skills Development Implementation unit. He asked what the reporting line looked like below the Chief Director for Skills Development.
Ms Erra replied that the structure consisted of a Director, Regional Managers, and a Director for Initiation and Evaluation. However, she added that they considered all directors across the organisation rather than limiting the search to a single unit.
The Chairperson questioned why someone from HR would be considered when directors were working daily in Skills Development Implementation, who were directly involved in the core functions of that unit. He asked why it would not make more sense to appoint someone from within that environment.
Ms Erra said that during the period under review, the Director for Initiation and Evaluation was not in place, leaving no candidate for that position. She added that DHET HR had informed the individual who had previously been acting that they could not continue acting for more than 12 months. She explained that the NSF had then been instructed to initiate the acting appointment process for the Chief Director: Skills Development Implementation across the organisation. She stated that the two directors within the unit had been appointed less than a year earlier and therefore did not meet the criteria. Based on these initial considerations, she said she instructed HR to review the CVs of all NSF directors. She noted that although she worked through Ms Williams, the task had been carried out by the Deputy Director responsible for recruitment.
The Chairperson summarised that she had handed the matter to HR, as policy required.
Ms Erra clarified that she had allocated the task to the Deputy Director for Recruitment.
The Chairperson asked how this applied to other units, such as if the CFO were on sick leave. He said it sounded as though the Deputy Director was always tasked with identifying potential acting candidates.
Ms Erra replied that the policy did not state that she must involve the Deputy Director, but that procedurally she gave matters to the head of the unit concerned, meaning she would normally send finance-related issues to the CFO and HR matters to HR.
The Chairperson said that in this case, she would therefore have given the task directly to HR, which then allocated it internally to the Deputy Director to identify possible candidates.
Ms Erra confirmed that the Deputy Director analysed the CVs of all directors.
The Chairperson asked how many recommendations had been produced.
Ms Erra said she needed to verify the exact number, but recalled that the assessment had involved several officials and had resulted in more than one possible candidate.
The Chairperson then noted that she had ultimately selected the HR Director.
Ms Erra responded that she had not chosen HR herself; rather, the Deputy Director for Recruitment had assessed the directors’ qualifications, experience, and project management background, and had compiled a submission identifying which candidates met the requirements and which did not. She said the submission included possible options for acting appointments based on this assessment.
The Chairperson asked her to provide the Committee with the written submission. He said he expected it to either recommend a list of suitable candidates or propose a preferred candidate.
Ms Erra said the submission recommended two candidates, as there were two chief director positions requiring acting appointments. She noted that the submission explained why the other directors did not meet the criteria and that she needed two acting appointments, including one for strategy, due to her own acting role as CEO.
The Chairperson said the submission had therefore given her one recommendation for Skills Development and one for the other chief director post.
Ms Erra confirmed this.
The Chairperson then asked whether she had not found it inappropriate that a subordinate of the HR Director had recommended their senior to act as a chief director.
Ms Erra said she had raised the issue with the Deputy Director and had reviewed the CVs herself because she had been concerned.
The Chairperson then moved to a different issue, noting that he was unsure whether he should raise it but indicating that he had received an email during the meeting alleging that Ms Williams, during her tenure as HR Manager, had facilitated the hiring of her brother at the NSF. He asked whether this was true.
Ms Williams confirmed that her brother had been hired as a contract worker.
The Chairperson asked what this meant, and Ms Williams explained that the NSF had approval to recruit 99 contract workers. She confirmed that the NSF recruited these contract workers.
The Chairperson said that the meeting had reached its conclusion. He stated that the NSF’s 2023/24 report was extremely poor and explained that the AGSA had sent him three letters alerting him to material irregularities at the NSF. He noted that similar correspondence has been forwarded to the NSF and the acting CEO, and that the letters indicated that material irregularities had been identified. Still, management had failed to act on them. He said the AGSA had since written back to him to report that no action had been taken, which underscored the seriousness of the situation.
He explained that he had been attempting to secure a meeting with the AGSA so that he could gain clarity on what was expected of the Committee in dealing with the identified material irregularities. He said that once this meeting took place, he intended to brief Members on the options available and on who held decision-making responsibility. He emphasised that these issues involved state resources, and that when individuals mishandled such resources—whether through deliberate misconduct or otherwise—Parliament had a duty to hold them accountable.
He expressed frustration that those responsible for the mismanagement of public funds were not being held to account, despite the AGSA’s formal findings. He said it was time for officials to face consequences for their actions, including the opening of criminal cases against those who had failed to manage state resources properly. He added that some individuals even used state resources to avoid accountability, hiring lawyers to resist oversight.
The Chairperson reflected that the year had been particularly difficult. He said it had begun with a university refusing to account to the Committee, which resulted in them being summoned. He noted that, rather than responding properly, the institution attempted to undermine his character while failing to carry out a basic responsibility: conferring a degree on an individual who had completed the required processes. He said they appeared unable to grasp foundational concepts such as the National Qualifications Framework (NQF) or articulation processes. Yet, they chose to attack him personally instead of addressing the substantive issues.

Concluding remarks
Mr Msezane said he wished to express his appreciation for the support provided to the Office of the Chairperson. He acknowledged the capable colleagues who assisted the Committee and encouraged Members to continue conducting themselves well, doing their homework, and holding accountable those who needed to be held to account. He added that the leadership of the Chair would continue to guide this work.
Mr Louw said he also wished to convey his thanks. He noted that the year had been difficult for the Committee, and he emphasised that, by January, the Committee would need to hold elections for the position of Deputy Chairperson. He thanked all Members for their commitment to the collective and remarked that those who appeared before the Committee should understand that oversight was not personal. He stressed that the Committee did not hate anyone, but was fulfilling its responsibility to ensure that the country functioned in alignment with economic imperatives and that learners and students received the opportunities they needed.
Dr Christians said she wished to echo the sentiments expressed by her colleagues. She reflected that, for the period she had served on the higher education portfolio, it had been a good year. She described the Committee as a strong team that had effectively held entities accountable. Addressing the DHET, she expressed the hope that the Department would appear before the Committee more frequently in the coming year so that accountability could be fully exercised. She extended well-wishes for the festive season, encouraging everyone to travel safely, enjoy time with their families, and look forward to a prosperous new year.
Closing remarks
The Chairperson thanked Members and expressed appreciation for the entire support team. He noted that the Committee Secretary, Research, and Content Advisory units had performed exceptionally well throughout the year by keeping the Committee informed on numerous matters. He remarked that, although recognition often went to others, it was these colleagues working behind the scenes who ensured important information was not missed. He acknowledged that, at times, he had overlooked emails, but the team had consistently reminded him and highlighted the implications of key issues.
He addressed Members directly, stating that each should reflect with pride on their contributions throughout the year. He emphasised that these contributions should not be taken for granted and that Members had performed well both individually and collectively. He acknowledged the strong working relationship among the Committee, noting that, despite representation from
six
different political parties, their unity had at times made party differences almost invisible. He encouraged Members to enjoy the Christmas period with their families, rest, and return in the new year refreshed.
The Chairperson cautioned that the work ahead would be even more demanding. He stated that those who had been challenged or held to account during the year should expect even firmer oversight in the future. He emphasised that the Committee would not apologise for holding entities accountable, as its responsibility was to ensure institutions performed their duties in ways that improved the lives of young people. He shared that, in his community, he regularly encountered young boys seeking money, often with the intention to purchase drugs, and noted that institutions such as the National Skills Fund (NSF) and the Sector Education and Training Authorities (SETAs) were mandated to reach these young people and provide them with real opportunities.
He reflected on the impact of delayed funding decisions, explaining that some students, despite performing well academically and qualifying for university admission, had lost opportunities because funding was not finalised on time. He noted that these students, unable to afford basic necessities such as food or accommodation, were sometimes forced to leave university before funding eventually came through. He stressed that the Committee’s questioning of entities was not motivated by personal animosity or a desire to remove individuals from their positions, but by the responsibility to ensure students were properly supported.
The Chairperson addressed the number of deaths at institutions of higher learning during the year, particularly among female students. He highlighted incidents of suicide and alleged rape at institutions including Durban University of Technology (DUT), University of Fort Hare (UFH), and Nelson Mandela University (NMU). He noted that some students reporting sexual assault experienced institutional inaction, leaving them vulnerable and traumatised. He referred to cases at Sol Plaatjie University (SPU), where alleged perpetrators of gender-based violence remained in their positions or were appointed to new roles despite confessions. He reiterated that he had made it clear in engagements with institutions that he would not defend perpetrators of gender-based violence.
He urged Members and departmental officials to reflect over the festive season on improvements needed in the coming year. He emphasised that parents entrusted their children to the higher education sector, yet too many were being returned in body bags. He expressed concern that he no longer knew how to reassure parents that their children would be safe and educated.
Addressing the DHET, he called on those attending church services in December to pray and fast for the Department and for the DG to recover fully, enabling accountability for departmental challenges. He stated that the Committee intended to address allegations concerning the hiring of foreign nationals at the NSF in positions not classified as scarce skills. He clarified that this concern was based on the Immigration Act, which requires employers to prioritise South African citizens unless a skill is proven to be scarce. He noted that universities and the Department had struggled to account for such appointments despite legal clarity.
He concluded by stating that this would be the last occasion the Committee offered prayers for the Department. From January onward, he said the Committee would take a far more assertive approach due to lives being lost and eroding confidence in the system. He urged all to take care during the festive season, wished a Merry Christmas to those who celebrated it, and expressed hope that the coming year would see improvements.
The meeting was adjourned.
