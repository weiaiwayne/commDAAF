Meeting Summary
Consolidated MFMA 2023/24 Report
The Select Committee convened to discuss the 2023/24 local government audit outcomes.
The Chairperson highlighted several challenges facing local government, including the problematic funding model, difficulties in attracting skilled individuals, and persistent governance issues. He expressed hope that the ongoing review of the White Paper on Local Government would address these concerns and the issue of unfunded budgets. The committee's recent oversight visit to the Free State revealed significant service delivery problems and instances where Section 139 interventions were being used without prior support as mandated by Section 154 of the Constitution. The importance of professionalising the public sector and addressing under-expenditure was also emphasised.
The AG's presentation revealed a concerning state of financial management and service delivery in municipalities, with a significant number receiving qualified, adverse, or disclaimed audit opinions. Key issues included poor quality financial statements, reliance on consultants for financial reporting, prevalent financial management risks, and weaknesses in performance planning for service delivery. The report called for mayors, councils, and executive authorities to enhance accountability and professionalise local government.
During the discussion, members raised various questions and concerns. The enforcement of provincial monitoring of municipalities, particularly in the Free State, and the sequence of interventions (Sections 106, 154, and 139) were questioned. The effectiveness of audit reports beyond "tick-box" exercises and the issue of unfunded budgets were also debated. The widespread use of consultants and their impact on institutional capacity was a major point of discussion, with some members advocating for the deployment of AG-trained professionals to municipalities. Concerns were also raised about municipalities' adherence to Eskom debt relief programs, the impact of the Public Audit Act amendments, and the lack of accountability for politicians. Members also questioned the AG's powers in cases of misuse of public funds, interventions in maladministration, and the potential downsides of clean audits. The lack of qualified staff, infrastructure maintenance planning, and the politicisation of appointments were highlighted as recurring issues.
The AG clarified that her office's mandate is to audit, report, and follow up on recommendations, not to step into the roles of other authorities. She outlined actions her office could take, including issuing stronger recommendations, taking remedial action, and referring matters for investigation. The effectiveness of the Public Audit Act amendments in fostering collaboration with other state institutions was affirmed. The AG emphasised that the viability of municipalities and the use of consultants were fundamentally leadership issues, stressing the need for strong, competent, and effective leadership at the municipal level. She provided recommendations for parliamentary oversight, suggesting a focus on provinces and adherence to constitutional provisions for supporting municipalities.

AGSA Presentation
Ms Tsakani Maluleke, Auditor-General (AG), led the committee through the presentation.
The presentation highlighted a concerning state of financial management and service delivery within South African municipalities. Overall audit outcomes show that while the number of clean audits slightly increased from 34 in 2022-23 to 41 in 2023-24, a significant portion of municipalities still received qualified, adverse, or disclaimed opinions. The late or non-submission rate of financial statements remained high at 5%.
Key issues identified included a lack of improvement in the quality of financial statements, with continued reliance on the audit process and consultants, and unreliable in-year reports. Major areas of misstatement include receivables, property/infrastructure/plant/equipment, irregular expenditure, revenue from services, and general expenditure. These issues are attributed to vacancies, lack of financial management skills, poor basic financial management processes, inadequate information systems, and ineffective internal audit units and committees. Consultants are frequently used for financial reporting, costing R1.47 billion across 219 municipalities in 2023-24, but their work often includes material misstatements, indicating that many municipalities are not adequately reviewing their output.
Financial management risks are prevalent, with 20% of municipalities facing going concern uncertainties, an average of 286 days to pay creditors, and insufficient spending on repairs and maintenance leading to infrastructure deterioration. Municipalities also owe substantial amounts to Eskom and water boards (R55.4 billion and R21.24 billion respectively). Unfunded budgets are a significant problem, affecting 44% of municipalities, with 76% of these experiencing it for three consecutive years. Unauthorised expenditure is R31.79 billion, largely due to overspending.
Performance planning and reporting for service delivery also show significant weaknesses, with many municipalities still relying on the audit process for reliable performance reports and a high percentage having material findings on compliance with strategic planning and performance management legislation. Infrastructure project delays, poor quality construction, and significant overspending are common findings, primarily due to inadequate monitoring, vacancies, ineffective procurement, and limited accountability.
Compliance with key legislation remains a challenge, particularly in areas like the prevention of unauthorised, irregular, and fruitless/wasteful expenditure, procurement and contract management, and expenditure management. Irregular expenditure related to supply chain management amounts to R28.72 billion. Material irregularities identified, totalling 446, have resulted in an estimated R8.74 billion in financial loss, but their desired impact is not being fully realised due to unresponsiveness from municipalities.
The report emphasised a call to action for mayors, councils, and national/provincial executive authorities to activate the accountability ecosystem, build capable institutions, professionalise local government, and instil a culture of ethics and accountability to address the root causes of these failures.

Discussion
The Chairperson thanked the Auditor-General, expressing his appreciation for what he described as a well-prepared, informative, and insightful presentation, which provided a clear picture of the state of audits in the Republic of South Africa, particularly at the municipal level. He added that they must also extend congratulations to the Western Cape for their performance. He emphasised that this recognition was important, as it could serve as an example for other municipalities to follow.
Dr I Scheurkogel (DA, Free State) said that he had reviewed the presentation again the previous day and reflected that morning, thinking to himself that although the Auditor-General was a very “pretty lady,” she was probably not the most popular person in South Africa. He added, with some humour, that this was likely due to her work in local municipalities. He noted that there were a number of issues, and that some of the matters he was raising did not necessarily require immediate responses, as they would probably receive reports on them in due course.
He stated that he was from the Free State, and posed his first question: how did one enforce provinces to monitor municipalities? Referring to Mafube, which he said was his allocated constituency, he pointed out that they had not submitted their financial statements and that the last update to the indigent register had been done ten years ago. He added that he would be conducting an oversight visit with the Deputy Minister of Water and Sanitation the following month, specifically regarding water and sanitation issues, because Mafube was directly polluting the Wilge River and the Vaal River daily.
He asked how they could enforce compliance, noting that the Free State was repeatedly mentioned and was a province with systemic issues, performing poorly in nearly every category. He questioned how the province could be held accountable, suggesting perhaps placing the Free State under a Section 100 administration, similar to what had been done in the North West. He speculated that the North West might be performing better now as a result of that intervention. He reiterated his question: how could they take effective action?
He went on to refer to Section 139 interventions and pointed out that, before invoking Section 139, there was the option of applying Section 106 of the Municipal Systems Act. Moreover, he emphasised that the Constitution mandated the use of Section 154 before more extreme interventions. He questioned how they could ensure this sequence was followed, expressing concern that these important preliminary steps were being overlooked.
Turning to the topic of audit reports, he acknowledged the visible improvement in financial statements but cautioned that sometimes moving from a qualified to an unqualified audit opinion might be nothing more than a tick-box exercise. He used a metaphor, saying, “I look very well today, but you don’t know I have cancer,” to illustrate how surface-level improvements could conceal deeper dysfunction. He stressed the need to move beyond ticking boxes to fixing the underlying problems.
He then shared his experience as a former ward councillor in Matjhabeng until the last election, saying he had firsthand knowledge of what was happening there. He explained that in his ward, the Integrated Development Plan (IDP) process was also a tick-box exercise. He recalled that the plan he had submitted to the MMC in 2021 remained unchanged until the time he left office the previous year—no new projects were introduced, and nothing was updated. He asked how this could be rectified.
He then raised the idea of deploying the well-trained professionals from the Auditor-General's office to municipalities. He suggested that if legislation was needed to make this possible, it should be considered. He argued that relying on consultants was part of the problem, and that this reliance was precisely why Matjhabeng had failed to function properly. He called for a more direct deployment of AG-trained personnel to help fix municipal systems.
Moving on, he said his “biggest” concern—and second-to-last point—was about unfunded budgets. He recalled that the Auditor-General had mentioned this toward the end of her presentation and asked how it could be addressed. He returned to the example of Matjhabeng, stating that from 2021 to the present, he had stood up repeatedly to highlight that their budget was unfunded. Nevertheless, the majority party in the council had pushed it through each time. As a result, he said, service delivery had collapsed.
He elaborated, noting that Matjhabeng had 13 wastewater treatment plants, but only three of them were operational. Of their fifty-four pump stations, only thirteen worked. He described sewage as being rampant throughout the area, attributing this to the continued passage of unfunded budgets. He emphasised that while the budget might be passed, there was no matching revenue collection to support it. He asked whether a policy needed to be developed to address this issue.
He further emphasised that some municipalities had not resolved these problems for three years or more, and the same ones listed in the AG’s presentation continued to be repeat offenders. He strongly rejected the idea that municipalities needed a new funding model, calling it “nonsense,” and pointed to the Western Cape as evidence that the current funding model could work. He stressed that municipalities did not need more money. Instead, the issue was with their spending and management—expenditure was high, indicating that funds were available. The problem, he said, was the lack of control over unmetered sites, the failure to fix indigent registers, and other basic management issues.
Turning to infrastructure, he urged the AG to consider the widespread backlog on Eskom payments. He pointed again to Mafube as a successful case, citing “rural maintenance,” which was responsible for the electricity supply in the municipality. He said they had paid off their Eskom debt and had a 99% collection rate—even in rural and township areas. He suggested that rural maintenance in Mafube could serve as a model to help other municipalities improve revenue collection. He noted that they had even applied to be removed from the national load-shedding schedule during peak times because they had managed their electricity supply so well.
Lastly, he said that the real issue was governance and the absence of direct consequence management for politicians. He explained that in many municipalities, responsibility was shifted to Chief Financial Officers (CFOs) and Municipal Managers (MMs). Councillors were not held accountable for decisions passed in council, even though they held the power to pass those decisions. He questioned how this governance gap could be fixed.
He stated that litigation costs against municipalities were soaring because of poor decisions pushed through by councils, often linked to unfunded budgets passed over several years. He cited the example of the Section 139 intervention in Matjhabeng, asking who was paying for the court case resulting from it. His answer: the ratepayers. He asserted that the real responsibility lay with the politicians who had repeatedly pushed through unsustainable budgets.
He concluded by asking whether new legislation was needed or whether existing structures, such as the Systems Act, should be revised to ensure greater political accountability. He emphasised that the only function that artificial intelligence was unlikely to replace in the next decade was that of political leadership, because accountability and governance could not be automated. He acknowledged that the AG had asked whether better legislation was needed and agreed that over-legislation could be harmful to the economy. However, he stressed that, in his view, nearly every issue raised in the presentation was not the fault of officials, but rather the fault of politicians. He asked for the AG’s perspective on how this could be fixed, as ultimately, the issue was about delivering services to the people.

She said that during their oversight visits, they often found infighting between municipal leaders, for instance, between the mayor and the speaker. Sometimes councils did not sit at all. In many instances, the chairpersons of the Municipal Public Accounts Committees (MPACs) did not understand their responsibilities. She stated that although they held the title, they did not know how to assist municipalities properly.
She referred to the offices of Chief Financial Officers (CFOs), acknowledging that while some CFOs were qualified, others were not. She raised the issue of consultants and the lack of skills transfer. She asked whether there was any regulation stipulating that municipalities could only use consultants for a limited period, such as five years, after which there must be a transfer of skills to internal staff. She noted that, according to the report, some municipalities had been using consultants for over eight to ten years. If there were no such regulation, she asked what could be done to address the issue and reduce reliance on consultants. She concluded by noting that although consultants could assist in some municipalities, in other cases, despite high spending on consultants, there was no improvement in performance.
The Chairperson said he wanted to join his colleagues in welcoming the Auditor General's report, noting that it was truly enlightening in terms of the work they were charged with discharging as part of their responsibilities.
He began by stating that although it was not within the Auditor General’s mandate to comment on the role of political parties, he believed that political parties could not be excluded from the broader ecosystem. He explained that when people vote for local government, they do so through political parties, which in turn appoint politicians. Therefore, he argued, political parties as institutions had a role to play in supporting the work of the Auditor General, rather than being disruptive.
He gave an example of a specific municipality, which he chose not to name, that had previously led the entire country in terms of credit status, outperforming even the Western Cape as well as national and provincial departments. However, rating agencies downgraded this municipality due to a reckless political statement that was made publicly. He emphasised that the downgrade was not based on any factual shortcomings, but rather on the political remarks, which illustrated how the political arm could negatively impact municipal performance. He further noted that the municipality he was referring to did not owe Eskom or any bulk water authority, yet it had suffered consequences due to the politicisation of its affairs.
He expressed concern over non-viable municipalities. He referred to Kopanong and mentioned a new proposal from the Demarcation Board to remove four towns from the municipality to form a new one. He suggested that this was an issue that required urgent collective attention. He then cited Mooi Mpofana Municipality in KwaZulu-Natal (Mooi River) as another example of a non-viable municipality. He described it as small and lacking revenue, with even the local industries no longer functional. He emphasised that these situations demanded intervention.
He then addressed the issue of value for money. He said that in many municipalities, there was a serious challenge regarding service payments, suggesting that the amounts being paid for services were often inflated beyond what was appropriate. He illustrated this with an example, stating that when three quotes were requested for a project, a service provider might propose a cost of R30 million, but officials would insist on doubling or multiplying that amount. He clarified that this was not hearsay but something that was actively happening across national, provincial, and local governments. He stressed that as a result, money that could have been used to build more houses or provide essential services was being misused.
He explained that the inflated R60 million amounts did not go to the actual service providers, who would still only receive R30 million. The remaining R30 million was distributed among corrupt individuals who contributed nothing to the work. He argued that the extent of corruption needed to be addressed for municipalities to function effectively.
He apologised for speaking at length, acknowledging his role as chairperson, and concluded his remarks.
Finally, he requested that the Auditor General provide written responses to some of the questions raised, as a number of members had left the meeting early due to caucuses. He noted that some members were fortunate not to have caucuses scheduled on that day.
Ms Maluleke responded that she would have asked her colleague to address the question about the outcomes in the Western Cape, but assured that the information could be provided in writing if that was acceptable. While addressing the Western Cape, she referred to a question about the use of consultants. She explained that their report contained a detailed analysis of the use of consultants by different provinces, which could be found on page 88. According to her, the Western Cape once again spent the least amount of money on consultants. She clarified that the province used consultants primarily to "top up" their internal capacity, particularly in dealing with complex accounting matters, but largely relied on internal capability for financial reporting.
She provided specific figures: in 2024, the Western Cape’s 27 municipalities collectively spent R37 million on consultants. She then compared this to other provinces—North West spent R344 million across 21 municipalities; Limpopo spent R286 million across 27 municipalities; KwaZulu-Natal spent R222 million across 47 municipalities; and another reference to Limpopo indicated R169 million spent across 18 municipalities. She concluded that while the Western Cape did use consultants, it was only for technical support, which she emphasised was the ideal approach.
She went on to summarise her responses under several sections, being mindful of the time.
-Viability of Municipalities: Some municipalities were fundamentally unviable because they lacked the revenue base assumed by the municipal funding model. This issue aligned with concerns raised about Mooi Mpofana. She acknowledged that this challenge was receiving attention in the current review of the White Paper on Local Government. She expressed satisfaction that the discussion document recently gazetted by the minister was asking the right questions regarding these issues.
-Public Audit Act Amendments: She addressed questions about the amendments to the Public Audit Act, first clarifying that their powers did not allow the Auditor General’s office to step into the roles of the accounting officer, mayor, council, or any other authority. She emphasised that their mandate was to audit, report, and now, follow up on whether their recommendations were being implemented.
She outlined three actions they could take if accounting officers failed to act on audit recommendations:
First, they could issue a stronger recommendation as part of the annual audit report. This would then become a transparent matter for Members of Parliament, councillors, and legislatures to follow up.
Second, they could take remedial action, which would compel accounting officers to act, such as initiating disciplinary action against staff.
Third, they could refer matters for investigation, which had already been done in several cases where financial losses were suspected.
She cited Matjhabeng Municipality as an example. A few years prior, they had identified issues with a construction contract involving payments for dam infrastructure that did not exist. The matter had initially been issued as a Material Irregularity (MI). When the accounting officer delayed action, the case was referred to the Hawks, who eventually made arrests. Similar referrals had been made to the SIU, the NPA, and the Human Rights Commission. For instance, the Rooiwal wastewater treatment plant had been referred to the Human Rights Commission due to environmental and public harm.
-Landfill Sites and Environmental Compliance: The AGSA conducted a compliance analysis using its audit powers. They would inspect and report where landfill sites were not maintained or did not meet license conditions, highlighting non-compliance and environmental harm. Accounting officers would then be instructed to address the issues. While some complied, others did not, and in such cases, the matters were referred to the Department of Environmental Affairs.
She explained that similar approaches were applied to wastewater treatment plants, where non-compliance was reported and referred to the Department of Water and Sanitation. She emphasised that these efforts were not intended to replace anyone’s job but were meant to nudge and support others in performing their responsibilities.
-Effectiveness of the Amendments: She affirmed that the amendment to the Public Audit Act had been effective, as it helped the Auditor General’s office to collaborate more meaningfully with other state institutions. It had enabled them to link audit outcomes with tangible realities on the ground and prompted real action. It had also helped involve provincial leadership in addressing issues like disclaimers and late audit submissions.
She reiterated that although they could not prosecute, fire anyone, or intervene directly, their role was to remain independent and catalyse action through reporting, follow-ups, and collaboration.
She continued by providing an example to address the issue of value for money. In the City of Tshwane, consultants had been appointed to help compile a fixed asset register under a contract valued at R600 million, which had been in place for about three years by the time it was reviewed. She said the Auditor-General's office had examined the matter and found that the amount appeared excessive. They also discovered that the municipality had not effectively monitored the consultants’ work. As a result, they issued a Material Irregularity (MI).
Following the MI, the accounting officer conducted a value-for-money audit on the contract and identified an overpayment of R26 million. Of that, R12 million had already been recovered, and efforts to recover the remaining amount were ongoing. She emphasised that such use of their powers could help drive action, although it would never be the sole remedy—audits would never pick up everything. The goal, she stressed, was for the accounting officer to learn from these instances, investigate similar contracts, implement better controls, and act differently in the future.
She noted that the MI report becomes available to the mayor, council, and oversight bodies like the Municipal Public Accounts Committee (MPAC). Therefore, it was expected that they would also engage actively with internal audit, audit committees, and MPAC to ensure appropriate oversight and follow-up. Without such engagement, she warned, the system would never improve.
-Leadership in Local Government: The local government system relies on strong, competent, and effective leadership, especially at the levels of the mayor, municipal manager (MM), and senior managers. She questioned whether those appointed to such roles had the necessary capacity to perform their duties. She pointed out the complexity of municipalities, citing their Integrated Development Plans (IDPs) and the scale of their budgets, particularly in the metros.
She said the expectations set out in the White Paper on Local Government were extensive. Mayors were expected to lead participatory processes in the development of IDPs, and speakers were to ensure engagement not just with communities but also with stakeholders, including the private sector, to attract investment.
However, she observed that many IDPs did not reflect genuine engagement with business. Instead, they simply listed ward meetings as evidence of consultation. It was no surprise, then, that the private sector often operated outside municipal frameworks and that IDPs failed to provide a coherent strategy for local economic development.
She highlighted Mhlontlo Local Municipality (likely referring to what she heard the chairperson mention earlier) as an impressive example. They had managed to crowd in investment through infrastructure partnerships with businesses, aligning closely with the vision of the white paper.
She reflected on the situation in metros, asking how many of them had the discipline, competence, and stature to properly engage stakeholders. Without these qualities, she warned, municipalities would struggle to attract investment, maintain transparency, and retain the trust of lenders. These were not policy failures, she argued, but rather leadership gaps.
She posed questions about MPACs, internal audits, and audit committees, asking whether the mayors were ensuring they were properly constituted, resourced, and integrated into the governance rhythm. She expressed concern that while the governance structure had been designed well on paper, insufficient attention had been paid to the people tasked with making it work.
She clarified that she was not a statecraft expert, but an auditor deeply invested in the well-being of municipalities. She said she even took time to read IDPs to understand the local government environment better. Her team was actively reflecting on these issues, asking where the real problems lay and how their audit insights could help.
-Oversight Recommendations: She suggested that members could use the AGSA’s reports to identify provinces that required greater attention. Oversight could begin with engagements at the provincial level, involving the Premier, MEC for Finance, and MEC for Cooperative Governance and Traditional Affairs (COGTA). She recommended asking critical questions about how these leaders were delivering on their duties, giving effect to provisions in the Municipal Systems Act and Municipal Finance Management Act (MFMA), and ensuring compliance with Section 154 of the Constitution, which compels national and provincial governments to support and strengthen municipalities.
-Consultants and Capacity Building: The issue of consultants was fundamentally a leadership issue. If a municipality continuously hired consultants for the same services each year without any sign of internal capacity being built, mayors, MMs, and CFOs should be held accountable.
She cited Matjhabeng Municipality as an example. Despite years of disclaimers and heavy reliance on consultants, progress was made only when a new mayor arrived and took decisive action. The mayor insisted on hiring qualified accountants to build internal financial management capacity. As a result, the municipality began moving out of disclaimers and reduced its reliance on consultants.
She also referenced Ngquza Hill Municipality in the Eastern Cape, where leadership responded positively to audit findings. After being advised that their consultant expenditure was too high, the accounting officer decided to cut costs and invest in internal capacity, saving R3.4 million. She acknowledged that while this may seem small, it represented a significant step toward sustainability.
She emphasised that skills transfer would not happen unless mayors started asking the right questions—Why are we still hiring consultants? Why is there no visible skills transfer? What resources does the CFO need? She stated that there was no rule or policy banning the use of consultants every year, but rather that effective leadership was required to change the trajectory.
She concluded by saying that some municipalities ignore audit recommendations and continue as before, simply because no one in the leadership deems it important to change course. But others respond constructively, and that made all the difference. In her view, matching responsibilities to capable individuals was the critical issue—without this, the system would continue to struggle.
Closing Remarks
The Chairperson believed the Auditor-General and her team could be encouraged by the fact that the Minister of Cooperative Governance and Traditional Affairs (COGTA) was considering a standardised approach to the delivery of water and electricity services in municipalities. The idea being discussed was the establishment of utilities as standalone entities, which he believed would help address many of the challenges the Auditor-General had raised.
He explained that one of the problems leading to service delivery failures was that some functions within municipalities had effectively become “spaza shops”—where capacity was intentionally weakened to enable outsourcing. Once everything was outsourced, contractors became embedded in the system. In some cases, these same contractors were believed to be vandalising infrastructure to ensure continued work opportunities. This, he argued, created a perverse incentive structure. He reiterated that municipalities were constitutionally mandated to provide services like water, so it was important to build internal capacity while also exploring the utility model to manage those services more sustainably.
He clarified that the municipality he had been referring to earlier was eThekwini, not uMhlathuze. He praised eThekwini for its efforts to engage closely with businesses, referring to a World Bank report that had highlighted improved business confidence in the city. He attributed this progress to deliberate efforts by the municipality to involve businesses through platforms such as the Economic Council.
At the same time, he acknowledged that some municipalities were regressing, and he took responsibility on behalf of the political leadership. He said it was the job of politicians to ensure that governance issues did not devolve into micromanagement and that municipal executives were given the freedom to perform their roles effectively.
He emphasised the importance of supporting local government, calling it the “coalface” of development and service delivery. For this reason, political leaders needed to continue investing in their growth and capacity. He then expressed gratitude to the Auditor-General and her team, thanking them for engaging with the committee and sharing valuable insights. He said the interaction had been beneficial, especially in enhancing the committee’s ability to carry out its oversight responsibilities.
He noted that the team from the AG had committed to ongoing engagement, including accompanying oversight visits. This, he said, would allow the AGSA to educate and guide municipalities directly on what was expected of them. He assured the AG that oversight planning would incorporate a stronger focus on calling in municipalities, premiers, and MECs to ensure progress in service delivery and financial governance.
He welcomed the improvement in audit outcomes, noting that the number of disclaimers had dropped from 48 to 14, which he considered a significant achievement. However, he stressed that this progress must not plateau—the aim was continued improvement, and therefore, more concerted effort would be required.
In closing, he congratulated the Auditor-General for the leadership she had demonstrated. He said her conduct and that of her dedicated team had shown the country what effective oversight looked like. According to him, no municipality could claim the AG’s office had been unresponsive. The teams were always available for meetings, engagements, and support.
He also commended the AG’s office for investing in infrastructure and internal capacity, saying that such developments would further enhance their ability to support governance. For example, he raised the issue of infrastructure costs, questioning why some road projects were so expensive despite the bills of quantities not justifying the expenditure. He said these were the kinds of details and inefficiencies that needed to be scrutinised to address leakages and waste in government.
The meeting was adjourned.
