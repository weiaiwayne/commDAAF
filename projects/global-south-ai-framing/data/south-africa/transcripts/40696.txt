Title: Digital solutions to assist government in standardising HR processes in the public service; NW & GP Oversight Report
Date: 14 May 2025

Meeting Summary
Video
The Portfolio Committee on Public Service and Administration convened virtually to adopt the oversight report on the North West and Gauteng provinces, and to deliberate on the digitisation of human resource management (HRM) processes in the government's public service departments.
The meeting opened with an acknowledgement of the global shift towards digital transformation and the pressing need for the public service to modernise accordingly. The Committee unanimously adopted the oversight report with minor amendments. A key focus of the meeting was the presentation of the inspection in loco report by the Public Service Commission (PSC), which assessed the state of HRM digitisation in selected departments and provinces. The study found that most departments were unprepared for the demands of the fourth industrial revolution, and highlighted budgetary constraints, inadequate information and communication technology (ICT) infrastructure, and a lack of upskilling for HR professionals as significant challenges. Recommendations included decentralising ICT management, establishing a unified digital HRM portal, and enforcing accountability for non-compliance with digitisation mandates.
The Centre for Public Service Innovation (CPSI) showcased innovative digital solutions, including in-house developed e-recruitment systems in the Eastern Cape and Western Cape. These initiatives demonstrated practical progress in streamlining HR functions, despite prevalent challenges such as technophobia, inconsistent funding, and a lack of cybersecurity expertise. The Western Cape’s strategic digital transformation plan and the Eastern Cape’s system interface processes were praised for their effectiveness. However, concerns were raised about accessibility for rural applicants and ongoing reliance on paper-based systems in other provinces.
During the discussion, Members of the Committee raised concerns about the exclusion of key departments like Health and Education from the PSC study, the inefficiencies within the State Information Technology Agency (SITA), and the inconsistencies in digital infrastructure across provinces. There were strong calls for centralised support, equitable access, and uniform standards in digitisation. Concerns about the diversion of ICT budgets, lack of consequences for non-compliance, and the need to support marginalised communities were emphasised. The Committee highlighted the importance of collaboration between the Department of Public Service and Administration (DPSA), National Treasury and other stakeholders, to achieve interoperability and cost efficiency.
In response, representatives from the PSC and CPSI acknowledged the systemic challenges, but expressed confidence in the transformative potential of existing in-house solutions and centralised systems. They stressed the need for executive decisions and performance-linked accountability measures to accelerate digitisation. The Committee underscored the urgency of the matter, referring to their oversight visits to hospitals that illustrated the dire need for digital systems. The meeting concluded with consensus on the importance of a cohesive strategy, improved resourcing, and the continuous evolution of the public service through digital transformation.
Meeting report
Chairperson's opening remarks
The Chairperson opened the meeting by saying that the world was fast-changing towards embracing technology, including easier access to government services. The digitisation of human resource management (HRM) processes had become an integral part of how the public service should be administered, and Members of the Committee had called for advances in the way that HRM was done in government, including its centralisation. The public service should not be left behind in bringing digital transformation to the country. This was the context from which the day’s meeting was scheduled.

PSC inspection of digitised HRM processes in selected public service departments
Mr Vusumuzi Mavuso, Commissioner, Public Service Commission (PSC), prefaced the presentation by saying that the advent of the fourth industrial revolution (4IR) brought with it a variety of opportunities that required the state to harvest the benefits it came with. Amongst these was the utilisation of artificial intelligence (AI) and the extent to which it could be used within public administration. An observation worth noting was that for the public service to have a greater impact, the human resources practices needed to be the first port of call in terms of readiness, to see these benefits become a concrete reality. Consequently, the PSC had undertaken a study to find out precisely where the public service stood in that regard. Most public service departments had been caught unprepared and poorly equipped to effectively adopt or exploit the 4IR during and after COVID-19, and had struggled to maintain the business-as-usual operations. The study was carried out during the 2023/24 financial year to ascertain the progress made by departments in terms of digitising HRM systems and upskilling employees to improve the efficiency and effectiveness of core operational processes.
Ms Amanda Kelengeshe, Director: Human Resources Best Practices, PSC, led the presentation. She said the study had been conducted in six departments, including Forestry, Fisheries and the Environment, International Relations and Cooperation, the National School of Government and five provinces, including the Eastern Cape, Gauteng and KwaZulu-Natal. The findings of the study were outlined, which included reference to electronic/digital recruitment and selection, where participants believed that sending a curriculum vitae (CV) and supporting documents by email or completing an e-application and an HR practitioner manually printing for prescreening and shortlisting purposes, qualified as automation and/or digitisation. A major concern was the limited information communication technology (ICT) budget allocation, and diversion of the limited budget for non-ICT-related purposes by top management.
At the provincial level, digitisation of the HRM processes landscape in the few selected provincial departments in the Eastern Cape, Limpopo and Gauteng provinces was more multi-colourful, advancing, stimulating and trailblazing, compared to most national and some provincial departments. However, disregarding ICT and HRM professionals’ upskilling and reskilling remained a dire challenge across many provincial departments, yet they were expected to perform miracles when faced with a crisis.
The presentation outlined the recommendations of the study, amongst which were:
Decentralisation of government ICT management (including its related budget) and coordination by one-time pins (OTPs) with relevant national offices to mitigate the challenges experienced with the alleged inadequate State Information Technology Agency (SITA) support and slow response by National Treasury (NT) to departmental requests for procurement deviations.
Establishment of a single government digital HRM system and portal that can connect provinces (via OTPs) and national departments using existing enterprise resource planning (ERP) solutions in departments, instead of the Department of Public Service and Administration (DPSA) reinventing the wheel. This would assist in reducing the possibility of cost duplication relating to licence fees and easing the burden on the fiscus, as well as systems incompatibility, which could also create maintenance challenges.
Set a digital transformation timeframe. Accountability and consequence management should be implemented by the Office of the President and the OTPs, assisted by the DPSA, against executive authorities and accounting officers for failing to embrace HRM digitisation in their departments.
The presentation concluded that the findings indicated a general consensus amongst participants that the DPSA should cease to explore or develop a new HRM module, which might take forever -- like the discontinued Integrated Financial Management System (IFMS). The focus should be on adopting and/or integrating existing proven systems that were used by some departments. The PSC was convinced that once the listed recommendations were carefully explored and implemented, efficiency and effectiveness would be achieved in the HRM components of the public service.
In closing, Commissioner Mavuso said that the PSC had its own flaws and weaknesses and was lagging behind in some instances due to budgetary constraints and limitations. However, it was still moving ahead in terms of e-systems and performance management and development systems (PMDSs) and making good progress. He said there were pockets of excellence in some provincial and national departments, and what needed to be done was to find a central system that could ensure that they moved in par.

Standardising digital human resource management processes in the public service
The Centre for Public Service Innovation (CPSI) made a presentation showcasing digital innovative interventions/solutions to assist government in standardising digital human resource management processes in the public service
Ms Lydia Sebokedi, Acting Executive Director, CPSI, led the presentation. She emphasised the necessity of developing in-house digital skills to reduce reliance on external solutions for HR processes. She pointed out the challenges of technophobia among public servants and the lack of cybersecurity specialists.
She then introduced the Eastern Cape and Western Cape teams who would presentation on their e-recruitment systems which, amongst others, aimed to address previous recruitment challenges and improve efficiency across public sector departments.
Eastern Cape demonstration
Mr Siyanda Vusani, Office of the Premier in the Eastern Cape, detailed the application process for external applicants, emphasising the importance of registering and creating a CV. The process included uploading academic qualifications and filling out the required information, such as secondary and tertiary qualifications, work experience, language proficiency, and references. Each step must be completed to be eligible for job applications.
Mr Vusani outlined the functionalities of the recruitment system, including how applicants can edit their submissions and view job advertisements. He also described the backend processes for recruitment personnel, including compiling job details and the approval workflow involving the departmental approver and the Office of the Premier.
Western Cape inter-provincial coordination and demonstration
Ms Aneesa Basha, from the Western Cape Government, presented on the digital transformation strategy that began in 2017, highlighting its implementation through a digital transformation plan in 2019. The strategy aimed to improve public service delivery by prioritising citizens' needs and integrating technology while also focusing on upskilling employees in digital skills and cybersecurity to adapt to changing demands.
Ms Basha presented the e-Recruit system for educators, developed in-house by a dedicated team. She noted its similarity to a public service recruitment system managed by a service provider. She also introduced the performance management information system (PERMIS), which was maintained by in-house developers, and explained the advantages of a centralised model for resource management. She also mentioned the pilot phase of the My Portal application, aimed at consolidating HR services for employees in the Department of Health and Wellness.
She discussed the significance of benchmarking in demonstrating South Africa's digital transformation achievements, citing awards received for the I-Plan initiative and other projects. She stressed the need for a centralised support approach and innovative thinking in the IT space.
Mr Pierre Schoonraad, Head: Research and Development, CPSI, concluded by highlighting the provinces' commitment to resource sharing and the potential for cost savings through a bottom-up approach to human resource management.

Discussion
Mr P Ndamase (ANC) posed some questions to both the CPSI and the PSC. He asked why they were looking at the human resource management solutions instead of migrating out of the Basic Accounting System (BAS) and Personnel Salary System (PERSAL) to the Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP) system. Referring to the PSC presentation, he commented that huge departments like Education and Health, which were labour-intensive, were not part of the study sample. Was there a reason for this? He also noted that the Eastern Cape presentation had picked up certain aspects of recruitment in relation to those departments, specifically the Department of Health in the Eastern Cape. He asked to what extent the manual approach in a number of activities, not necessarily involving recruitment only, had resulted in challenges of medical legal claims in that department. He commented that even the Department of Health was not in a good space in Gauteng, as was described in the media. Could this be clarified and confirmed?
He then turned to the role of the SITA. He asked the PSC to what extent the challenges that were known in the public space were related to SITA, and what role did SITA have to play in any IT-related acquisitions that were done by departments. To what extent did the challenges of SITA have a negative impact on what the PSC and CPSI were trying to do in the digitisation process? What was the role of National Treasury, and were there any challenges with the Public Finance Management Act (PFMA) in that regard?
He asked the PSC to shed some light on the issue involving the SAP/Oracle cloud-based solution for Gauteng.
Ms L Potgieter (DA) said she was glad that parts of the recommendations were for a centralised HR system. It did not make sense to her that operations were on all of these different kinds of systems that did not speak to each other. She said that instead of giving departments a choice on digitisation, a stronger tone on the need to centralise was perhaps needed. She then sought clarity on what the issues in the rural areas were. Was it a lack of resources because of funding or connectivity, or was it the inability to use technology, or a combination of these two?
She then asked the CPSI if there would be a move away from the need to rewrite CVs online. It was very cumbersome, and she had noticed that many government departments still used it. Did the systems that they were looking at have the ability to flag or isolate applications that did not meet the criteria of a particular application to reduce the number that were being received by departments?
Mr T Langa (IFP) asked the CPSI about what they called the bloated administration/programme 1 (slide 3) caused by manual processes. How did they propose to balance the reforms within the public service employment priorities in provinces with high unemployment rates? On low-code and ‘small’ cloud-based solutions that were integrated into a uniform digital public infrastructure (DPI), he enquired if the CPSI had undertaken a cost-benefit analysis comparing these approaches to traditional procurement models, particularly in under-resourced provinces? What mechanisms were in place to ensure equitable access to digital HR solutions across all provinces, especially where capacity was unequally distributed?
He asked to what extent the CPSI was assisting provinces in localising and sustaining the digital solutions developed centrally, such as e-recruitment and e-lift systems. On the call for lifting the moratorium on in-house HR systems, what risk mitigation strategies were being considered to ensure security, interoperability and auditability in the absence of traditional procurement controls?
Mr Langa said the Western Cape had indicated it emphasised citizen consultation and feedback. What framework did the CPSI have in place to promote similar citizen engagement in the digital transformation processes across all provinces? He then asked the Eastern Cape how the needs of the rural applicants were factored into the design of the e-recruitment and e-lift systems, particularly regarding accessibility outside of government networks. What performance metrics were used to evaluate the impact of the e-recruitment system on the recruitment timelines and applicant experience? Finally, he said some departments still, as it reflected on presentation, outsourced HR functions. What support was being given to these departments to internalise and adopt in-house systems in line with the in-house first principle stated on the slide?
Ms P Xaba-Ntshaba (ANC) wished to know, based on the in-loco inspection and the Commissioner’s assessment, which system should be considered to improve human resource management practice for the entire government, despite the failed IFMS by National Treasury. It had been mentioned that the National School of Government's e-recruitment system had been discontinued due to the piloting of the IFMS. When was the IFMS piloted, and how successful was the process? Was the IFMS fully functional in the public service since it was introduced by Cabinet in 2025? Why were certain departments stopped from implementing their own in-house digitised system, while others were implementing their own systems? What was the role of the DPSA in programmes for e-government in digitising and developing a common HRM system for the public service? What were the risks associated with their own individual system?
Was there any interface between all the developed digitised systems with PERSAL or the IFMS for purposes of government recording-keeping?
Ms S Gcilishe (EFF) began with a question to the CPSI, saying she had noted that the report highlighted digital solutions like e-recruitment and an e-leave system, but wanted to know how government would ensure that applicants from rural areas who lacked access to the internet or digital literacy were not excluded. She cited the example of the Eastern Cape which, in their e-recruitment systems, required uploading certified documents online. What measures were in place to prevent discrimination against people in rural settings? As much as the report acknowledged the digital transformation, it did not address the aspect of online alternatives for marginalised communities. She had also wondered why government was always obsessed with outsourcing critical HR systems instead of building in-house capacity, as had been done in the Eastern Cape.
As far as CPSI was concerned, she had taken note of the report’s admission that some departments still used paper-based processes despite the mandate to digitise. Why were there still no consequences for officials who resisted, despite institutions like the Auditor-General (AG) flagging manual leave systems, for example? What punitive measures existed? The Eastern Cape e-Leave system had been implemented to stop the manipulation of leave records. The Western Cape boasted about its advanced digital services, while there were provinces like KwaZulu-Natal which lagged behind. How did government justify this inequality? The Western Cape had this citizen portal, which offered the Small and Medium Enterprises (SME) funding and e-procurement, yet there were rural provinces that lacked similar infrastructure. What measures were in place to address this?
Ms Gcilishe said the report highlighted the diversion of limited ICT budgets for non-ICT purposes by top management. She would have asked the executive authority to provide concrete examples of how these funds were misused and what consequences had been imposed on the people responsible. She said the government should consider centralised funding to uplift departments in the rural provinces. She proposed that the minister be asked to confirm the number of accounting officers who had been held accountable, and why there was no transparency as far as this was concerned. She spoke of the need for the Committee to address the challenges of SITA, which had been repeatedly cited as a major obstacle to efforts to transform. There was also a need for benchmarking amongst provinces.
Ms M Pholwane (ANC) asked if government's human resource practitioners were capacitated on digital transformation by their own departments.
Ms W Tikana-Gxothiwe (ANC) expressed reservations about the implementation of digitalisation across government departments, citing challenges in compliance and a lack of clear plans to address these issues. She stressed the importance of capacitating ICT personnel to ensure effective digitalisation, and recommended that the Committee invite SITA to present on their programmes.
Responses
Commissioner Mavuso appreciated the questions raised by Members, which he found to be incisive but would probably not be answered fully on the day. As they marched on, they would be able to provide responses. There had been similarities in the presentations to a very large degree, and this showed that there were indeed pockets of excellence here and there, and a diverse kind of appreciation of what digitisation really implied and meant.
Ms Kelengeshe, for the PSC, said that SAP applications could run on top of the Oracle database. They were used together because Oracle was a database vendor which stored and managed data. Gauteng offered shared digital services throughout the province. On why the departments had been stopped from procuring their own systems by the DPSA was because the IFMS had the HRM module in it, so they could not be allowed to procure what was already built into the IFMS project. However, because of the delays that were happening with the IFMS project, other departments had been allowed to do their own procurement. Others had used their in-house developers and resources to make sure that the HR processes were digitised.
She said the study was interested in seeing how the departments were recruiting all the life cycles of the HR field, and not the line functions or business work processes of departments. The focus of the PSC was on cutting down the amount of administrative work in the HR space so they could begin to play a strategic role in supporting the line functions to ensure that the strategies of departments were being implemented. They were currently bogged down with a lot of applications that they needed to sit through, like capturing leave applications manually.
On the piloting of the IFMS, she understood that there had been a plan to do a pilot for 2024/25 again. There was a recent pilot that was supposed to be done, but it could not be implemented because of the issues that were happening with the IFMS project, which the Committee should perhaps ask National Treasury and the DPSA to provide feedback on. Regarding how the government would ensure rural areas were not excluded, she would leave the CPSI to respond to the diversion of ICT budgets to non-ICT items, and the associated consequence management. In the presentation of PSC, they had advocated that this area must be monitored in departments and consequences should happen, as those departments had not been able to move in terms of automation and digitisation.
Dr Kholofelo Sedibe, DDG: Leadership and Management Practices, PSC, responded that the core challenge was the decentralisation of financial management and human resource management practices. There were pros and cons to centralisation and decentralisation. On the current recommendations, whilst they were not advocating the reversal of all centralised functions to a central position, because there was a reason why the country had decentralised certain functions and particular stages, the time had come for certain things to be done from a central perspective. By so doing, government would be able to use economies of scale to achieve what it wanted to, including things like programming. It was not necessary for every government department to employ programmers. A pool of resources across departments and provinces should be in place, so government would be able to achieve what the CPSI had referred to as the interoperability of systems. These were some of the issues that needed to be looked at quite closely by National Treasury, the DPSA and CPSI. There would be obvious implications for certain amendments to prescripts, which would take away certain autonomies in terms of decentralised functions.
She said there were issues around openness in outsourcing. Annual reports of government departments required them to publish details of service providers they used for whatever different reasons. The PFMA required openness in terms of procurement, but there were competing systems within different departments. This caused many challenges in this area. They were also of the view that when systems had been proved to work, there was no reason why they could not be replicated, especially in instances where there were provincial and certain national departments that had created systems in-house that could be made to work. In the background, humans would be able to ensure that systems coordinated and integrated with each other. This was shown in their report, where some departments had developed systems and were able to get an interface with PERSAL in a way that minimised the maintenance of the system on HRM and other systems. Where they were unable to do that, one had HR officials and even line function managers having to deal with a double system, which diverted attention away from their work.
Dr Sedibe wished to indicate at a high level that National Treasury had put in place the frameworks and the systems for financial and supply chain management. However, it was known that HRM was the domain of the DPSA. It was thus important that both these institutions worked closely together to achieve the economies of scale needed when facilitating the integration of all line functions within government. The PSC believed it was possible for all departments to be digitised, as others had already done things that would take years to perfect. She said it was a question of executive decisions being taken, and not wasting resources by starting to develop systems that existed elsewhere. From their experience as the PSC, many departments were already paying licence fees for certain services that they did not use.
Ms Kelengeshe responded to why the education and health departments were not sampled in this inspection. She reminded the Committee that there had been a previous study that was done on this subject. What they had done was to assemble only those departments that indicated that they had partially or fully automated and digitised their HR processes, which were quite few. The Western Cape had digitised their centres of education and health before the previous study, so they did not need to go there. She noted the question on medical claims which she said was a supply chain question within the Department of Health, and they would not be in a position to respond to it.
She then referred to the SITA challenges in the public domain, and how they negatively impacted the digitisation space. Any department that relied on SITA for connections, the core business processes would be impacted by what was happening as a result of the inefficiencies of that institution. She knew that the PSC was currently running a study to look into the functioning of SITA, and she would allow the Commissioner to respond to the matter.
Commissioner Mavuso concluded by saying it would be worth the 7th Parliament's while to demand that all departments should digitise and automate within the next three to four years, and hold the entire executive accountable, as the PSC believed there was no reason why that should not be the case. The country’s state of readiness must be in line with what is happening throughout the world. He said the issues raised pertaining to the medical claims that had emanated from actual or alleged claims of medical negligence, which resulted in medical complications, and in some cases death, could easily have been resolved through the digitisation and automation processes, as opposed to doing things manually. This was because there were leakages in the system that had been exploited by entities who wanted to make a quick buck out of the misery of vulnerable people.
He said the actual resourcing that was related to the digitisation ought to be enhanced and expedited to deal with much more serious and critical issues that were related to service delivery. There was an ongoing investigation at SITA. Until that process was concluded, he would reserve his comments because they were still investigating and hoping to conclude in the next two months or so.
The Committee Chairperson said they had recently gone on an oversight trip to Gauteng and the North West provinces, where they had visited public hospitals and seen the desperate need for digitisation first hand. People’s experiences at service delivery centres were not pleasant because of the manual processes involved. He agreed with the presenters on the urgent need to turn this around.
Mr Mandla Ngcobo, DDG: eGovernment and Chief Government Information Officer, DPSA, said the Public Service Act provided for the Ministry of Public Service and Administration (MPSA) to establish norms and standards on information management, e-commerce and innovation. He said they hoped for an opportunity to come and present the strategy to the Committee in the near future, and indicate the extent to which there was a level of compliance on some of the things that were assessed, as well as other activities discussed. He cautioned against the thinking that there was a destination to transformation. The extent to which technology was changing was continual, and the transformation journey would never end.
He would recommend that the digitalisation transformation be made part of the performance agreement targets of accounting officers, so that it did not sit with the chief information officers. The latter should lead departments to think strategically about the business side of a department.
The meeting was adjourned.
