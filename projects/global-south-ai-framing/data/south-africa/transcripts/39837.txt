Meeting Summary
Video
The Standing Committee on Education received a briefing from the Western Cape Education Department (WCED) on the progress of its STEAMAC (Science, Technology, Engineering, Arts, Mathematics, Agriculture, and Coding) initiatives. The meeting focused on assessing WCED’s integration of those subjects into the curriculum to address skills gaps and improve learner preparedness for emerging job sectors, particularly in data analytics and artificial intelligence.
The WCED highlighted its prioritisation of foundational skills in STEAMAC subjects across schools in the province. Notably, coding sessions had been introduced to cultivate interest in STEM fields at an early stage. The Department also shared data indicating that 44% of youth in the Western Cape fall within the NEET (Not in Education, Employment, or Training) category. To address that, WCED had piloted vocational pathways for Grades 8 and 9, with options for learners to transition into either vocational or academic streams by Grade 10. Collaboration with TVET colleges was reported as a core component of WCED’s strategy to align educational outcomes with market demands.
During discussions, Members inquired about the criteria for expanding Marine Science programmes to additional coastal schools, strategies to improve mathematics pass rates in underperforming districts, and equitable access to STEAMAC subjects for disadvantaged communities. Members also raised the issue of participation rates among learners from marginalised backgrounds and queried WCED’s plans to align skills development with industry requirements.
The Committee resolved to support the formation of an advisory board, including key industry stakeholders, to guide curriculum alignment, assist with the recruitment of teachers for specialised subjects, and secure additional resources. Members also agreed to monitor mathematics and science performance, particularly within underperforming districts, to evaluate the impact of WCED’s interventions.
The meeting concluded with the Committee’s adoption of the draft report on WCED’s annual performance, along with the minutes of previous meetings held on 15 October and 10 September 2024.
Meeting report
The Chairperson invited Committee Members to introduce themselves. Present were Ms W Kaizer-Philander (DA), Mr M Booysen (DA), Mr F Christians (ACDP), Ms A Cassiem (EFF), Ms D Stephens (PA), and Mr T Walters (DA).
Apologies
The Chairperson conveyed apologies from Minister David Maynier, who was unable to attend, and acknowledged Mr Bertram Loriston, DDG: Curriculum and Assessment Management, WCED.
Mr Loriston then allowed his team to introduce themselves, starting with Mr Juan Benjamin, Chief Director: Curriculum Management and Teacher Development, who then introduced
Mr Don Haripersad
, Director: Curriculum FET, and Ms Karen Dudley, Director: GET Curriculum.
Opening remarks
The Chairperson thanked the officials and provided an outline of the meeting. He stated that the WCED would brief the Committee on the progress of Science, Technology, Engineering, Arts, Mathematics, Agriculture, and Coding (STEMAC) programmes, including the Department’s efforts in emerging areas such as data analysis and artificial intelligence, which are becoming increasingly significant in the employment market. He expressed the Committee’s interest in seeing learners prepared to meet these demands.
He concluded by requesting confirmation of attendees for the record, acknowledging Ms Wasiema Hassen Moosa, Procedural Officer for the Committee. This step underscored the Committee's commitment to thorough procedural documentation in their oversight of educational developments in the Western Cape.
The WCED’s update to the Committee on its STEAMAC Programme.
Mr Juan Benjamin, Chief Director: Curriculum Management and Teacher Development, thanked the Committee for the opportunity to present WCED’s initiatives, providing an overview of the WCED’s strategic focus on Science, Technology, Engineering, Arts, Mathematics, Agriculture, and Coding (STEAMAC) as a means to meet the rising demand for skills in technology and science. He highlighted recent Statistics South Africa figures, showing that 44% of young adults aged 15-24 fell under the Not in Education, Employment, or Training (NEET) category, with the Western Cape facing particular challenges in addressing youth unemployment. WCED concentrated on foundational STEAMAC subjects such as mathematics and science across all phases to address these. Mr Benjamin noted additional STEAMAC initiatives, such as coding and robotics "power hours," to build interest in STEM subjects among learners.
Mr Benjamin went on to discuss WCED’s approach to technical and agricultural subjects within the Further Education and Training (FET) band, which prepares learners for roles in key sectors such as agri-processing, energy, ICT, and tourism. He explained that WCED had piloted a vocational curriculum in Grades 8 and 9 across 44 schools, impacting about 17 200 learners, with pathways into vocational or academic streams by Grade 10. This vocational curriculum is aimed at aligning learners’ skills with market needs, ensuring that they are prepared to support economic growth in the province.
Mr Benjamin confirmed ongoing collaboration with Technical and Vocational Education and Training (TVET) institutions to facilitate a smooth transition into specialised training.
Mr Benjamin further acknowledged the Department’s progress despite fiscal constraints. He highlighted the need for additional resources and partnerships with TVET colleges, Sector Education and Training Authorities (SETAs), and industry stakeholders to expand these efforts. He reaffirmed WCED’s commitment to developing a skilled workforce to bolster the Western Cape and South African economies.

Discussion
The Chairperson expressed gratitude to Mr Benjamin for an enlightening presentation and commended him and the WCED team for their thorough work. Before inviting questions and comments from Committee members, he reflected on how the Committee could best support WCED’s initiatives, particularly by increasing public awareness of the Department’s progress. He suggested that showcasing the strides made could serve as inspiration for learners, demonstrating the broad spectrum of opportunities available to them.
The Chairperson then shared his recent experience meeting with the Chairman and Chief Executive Officer of a major international contact centre company with operations in Cape Town and George. He recounted that a question raised during this meeting concerned the extent to which young learners are being equipped for roles in the fast-evolving fields of data analytics and artificial intelligence. Acknowledging that these advancements place increasing demands on the teaching environment, the Chairperson noted the importance of aligning educational practices with global technological shifts. He then opened the floor to Members to provide comments and ask questions to delve further into the topic.
Ms A Cassiem (EFF) thanked the Chairperson and the Department for the presentation, raising a question regarding the identification of schools for the introduction of Marine Science. She sought clarification on the criteria used to select the five or six schools set to implement Marine Science as an additional subject or programme, noting that this information may have been missed during the presentation. Ms Cassiem inquired whether the Department intended to expand Marine Science programmes to schools along the province's coastal regions. She mentioned a visit to Saldanha, where she observed interest among both young and older fishers in educational programmes covering marine science and fishing. She asked if there were any plans to roll out such programmes in coastal schools across the province.
Mr F Christians (ACDP) appreciated the presentation and raised two points for further clarification. Referring to Slide 43, he noted the low pass rate for Mathematics distinctions in the Metro East region, which has consistently struggled compared to other regions. He asked if the Department had specific plans to address this ongoing issue in Metro East and improve performance in mathematics. Mr Christians also acknowledged the fiscal constraints faced by the province and country, acknowledging that budgetary limitations might impact the rollout of programmes. He requested further insight into the benchmarks and goals set by the Department to measure progress on skills alignment, particularly in addressing the misalignment of skills and preparing learners for the job market. He asked about the Department’s turnaround strategy, its timeframe for evaluating results, and the measures used to assess and adjust programmes based on effectiveness, considering both resource constraints and the need for sustainable, long-term impact.
Mr M Booysen (DA) thanked the Chairperson and expressed appreciation for the inspiring presentation by the Department. Noting the remarks that Provincial Minister of Agriculture made on limited agricultural colleges in the province, he inquired whether the Department shared the sentiment that these resources were insufficient. Given the introduction of innovative agricultural subjects, he asked if these subjects could serve as a feeder system to support the Minister’s vision of expanding agricultural education within the province.
The Chairperson then commented that certain high schools had already begun offering agriculture subjects. He was interested in hearing how similar progress might be expanded across other schools in the province.
Ms R Windvogel (ANC) apologised for her late arrival due to an extended prior meeting. She welcomed the progress reported on implementing the STEAMAC initiatives by WCED. Referring to slide 4, she highlighted concerns regarding unemployment, social inequality, and the rising number of NEET youths, especially among Coloured and African communities. Ms Windvogel expressed interest in understanding how WCED defines equity within the STEAMAC framework. Specifically, she asked about the targeted measures the Department had implemented to ensure that learners from disadvantaged socio-economic backgrounds, particularly in Coloured and African communities, had equitable access to these critical educational opportunities. She also inquired about how WCED ensured these initiatives translated into tangible, lasting outcomes for at-risk youth.
Ms Windvogel proceeded to ask about the breakdown of learner participation in STEAMAC subjects, referring to Slide 9. She requested details or statistics on racial and quintile breakdowns of learners participating in STEAMAC subjects, seeking clarification on the Department’s steps to address any disparities observed. She then noted that, while participation in mathematics appeared high in foundation and intermediate phases, there was a significant decline by Grade 12, with lower participation at the matriculation level. She asked the Department for insights into when and why this drop-off occurred, seeking details on specific stages where learners were opting out of Mathematics and factors contributing to this trend. She further requested information on any challenges or barriers affecting learners’ continuity in Mathematics through to Grade 12.
Responses
Mr Loriston expressed appreciation for the opportunity to address the Committee’s questions. He clarified that all learners in the General Education and Training (GET) band, from Grades 1 to 9, are required to study mathematics. However, at the Grade 10 level in the Further Education and Training (FET) phase, learners must choose between Mathematical Literacy, Pure Mathematics, or Technical Mathematics. He noted a challenge in the lack of a clear standard at Grade 9 that mandates learners to achieve a specific outcome in mathematics as a requirement for progression. Although current policy does not enforce this, he emphasised that the Department has implemented various measures to strengthen mathematics teaching from the foundation phase through to the FET phase, aiming to improve systemic results and participation rates annually.
Mr Loriston highlighted efforts to establish career clubs in schools, where external speakers are brought in to inform learners about the practical value of studying mathematics and its role in future career opportunities. He noted that these interventions primarily target quintiles 1 to 4 schools to encourage broader participation and drive transformation. Additionally, the Department committed to sharing data on learner participation rates across quintiles, as indicated by Mr Benjamin. On the topic of agricultural subjects, Mr Loriston acknowledged the financial constraints associated with establishing agricultural colleges but explained that the Department is promoting agricultural subjects across the province. This approach, he clarified, aims to increase awareness of diverse opportunities within the agriculture sector beyond traditional farming roles.
Mr Hariprasad responded to Ms Cassiem and Mr Christians' questions on marine sciences. He explained that school selection for Marine Sciences programmes is influenced by spatial planning and the availability of qualified educators, particularly in proximity to the province’s 14 ports and harbours to support the ocean economy. He noted the Department’s cautious approach in expanding these programmes due to the need for teachers with specialised skills in marine science, highlighting the challenge in re-skilling educators for this field. Currently, schools with staff members interested or trained in Marine Science, such as those in Athlone and nearby townships, have been prioritised. The Western Cape also benefits from having a senior curriculum planner with expertise in Marine Sciences, which supports the expansion of these programmes.
Mr Hariprasad further elaborated on the Department’s broader strategy, which includes plans to introduce subjects such as Maritime Economics and Agricultural Management focusing on aquaponics. He mentioned ongoing consultations with the National Department of Basic Education to reframe certain practical subjects, including Tourism, Hospitality Studies, and Computer Application Technology, as vocational rather than strictly academic. This reclassification aims to align these subjects more directly with the needs of the job market and support entrepreneurship. He also discussed challenges related to the drop-off in Mathematics enrolment at the Grade 12 level, attributing some of this to systemic issues within higher education. Specifically, many universities offer admission and bursaries based on distinctions in Mathematical Literacy rather than Pure Mathematics, making Mathematical Literacy a more attractive option for some learners. He concluded by reaffirming the Department’s commitment to expanding skills-based programmes in disadvantaged communities, with a focus on increasing accessibility for learners in African and Coloured communities.
Mr Benjamin addressed the Committee regarding the timeline and strategy for improving educational outcomes. In response to Mr Christians' question on the expected duration of these initiatives, he stated that a precise timeline of five or ten years could not be confirmed. Instead, the Department is developing an all-encompassing strategy, requiring engagement with other government departments, TVET colleges, and Higher Education Institutions (HEIs). He explained that these forums are actively being explored, with Mr Loriston leading efforts to ensure a cohesive strategy. Once the conceptual framework is finalised and endorsed by the Executive Council (EXCO), milestones will be set to guide the programme’s development in alignment with the Department’s capacity amidst fiscal constraints. Addressing Mr Booysen’s question about agricultural education, Mr Benjamin confirmed ongoing collaboration with the Department of Agriculture, particularly with facilities such as Elsenburg. This partnership aims to upskill teachers and expose learners to agricultural technologies like drones. He acknowledged that expanding agricultural education requires cooperation with the Department of Agriculture to effectively develop these offerings.
Mr Loriston then provided an update on the Metro East mathematics initiative. He noted his recent visit to the District, where focused efforts were underway to enhance Mathematics and Physical Science performance. He expressed optimism for improved results, emphasising that the programme also builds sustainability by supporting learners from Grade 10 through Grade 12 to reduce dropout rates in these subjects. He further addressed Ms Cassiem’s query on the Marine Sciences initiative, explaining that this programme was now in its third year and had stabilised after initial challenges, which would support broader implementation.
On the topic of equity in education, Mr Loriston acknowledged Ms Windvogel’s question, noting that the Department is conducting spatial analyses of schools and their surrounding industries to determine the most suitable subject offerings for each area. He explained that the Department is reviewing subject choices in schools, especially within the same community, to diversify offerings and expand learners’ career pathways. This approach includes collaboration with TVET colleges and industry partners to optimise resources (such as the Western Cape Government through the Growth for Jobs initiative) and ensure that learners have access to relevant skills. Mr Loriston highlighted the importance of partnerships, particularly with the Department of Agriculture, to ensure learners pursue diverse pathways within the three-stream education model.
Further, Mr Loriston noted the necessity of aligning with higher education institutions and TVET colleges to address the challenge of teacher supply, especially for specialised subjects. He explained that the Department is exploring frameworks to ensure a sustainable pipeline for training qualified teachers. He concluded by emphasising the Department’s commitment to building partnerships that will enhance the educational experience for vulnerable learners, recognising that external expertise and skills are essential to making education more relevant and impactful.
Further discussion
The Chairperson thanked the Department’s team for their responses, noting that several of Mr Loriston’s comments aligned with his thoughts on the importance of partnerships. He emphasised the need to build relationships with various economic sectors and business stakeholders, suggesting the formation of an advisory council or body that could collaborate with the Department to identify industry needs. Such a council could assist in generating additional funding to support educational initiatives, especially given current fiscal constraints.
The Chairperson remarked on the enthusiasm evident in programmes like mathematics, coding, robotics, and the science fair. However, he observed a potential drop in participation numbers in certain areas, such as The Garden Route District Municipality (formerly known as Eden District). He expressed the need to maintain high levels of engagement. He suggested that economic sectors could play a valuable role in advising on the types of skills and subjects learners should prioritise to better align with job market requirements. He then invited further questions from the Committee.
Ms Windvogel thanked the Chairperson and inquired about the Department’s collaboration with the Two Oceans Aquarium. She asked if a partnership existed to support the expansion of marine science education and whether specific resources or activities were planned to enhance and sustain this offering.
The Chairperson acknowledged her question and invited the Department to respond. He expressed appreciation for the briefing, noting that it provided valuable insight into the Department’s dynamic and multifaceted work. He recognised the ongoing challenges in preparing learners for careers and further education, which required continuous attention to multiple aspects within the education system. He then handed over to Mr Loriston to conclude.
Further responses
Mr Loriston assured the Committee that the Two Oceans Aquarium played an integral role in the development of the marine sciences curriculum and had been actively involved in the subject’s pilot phase. He confirmed that the aquarium continues to provide practical engagements for schools offering marine sciences. Additionally, Mr Loriston highlighted that Marine Sciences is one among various scientific subjects offered within the marine environment, mentioning that the Department also conducts examinations in nautical sciences, though uptake remains low. He expressed a commitment to promoting these subjects further among learners.
Closing Statements
The Chairperson thanked Mr Loriston and his team for their presence and presentation, acknowledging the Department’s dedication to advancing the STEAMAC programmes. He conveyed the Committee’s appreciation for the Department’s ongoing efforts to expand and enhance these subject offerings, with the aim of engaging more learners across various fields. Wishing the Department success in their continued work, he expressed confidence that their sustained efforts would further develop these initiatives. On that note, he excused the Department so they could continue with their tasks for the day.
Mr Loriston thanked the Committee on behalf of his team, both present and absent, and expressed appreciation for the engagement. He noted that these discussions served as a valuable opportunity to gain new perspectives, helping to broaden their approach to ongoing improvements within the Department. Acknowledging the benefits of outside insights, he thanked the Chairperson and Committee members for their constructive input and the collaborative tone of the session.
Committee Matters
Actions and Resolutions
The Chairperson thanked the officials for their participation and proposed that, with the Department now excused, the Committee continue with its agenda, beginning with the consideration and adoption of the draft Committee report on the Western Cape Education Department’s annual report. This would be followed by a review of draft minutes and further discussion on resolutions and actions stemming from the briefing.
Ms Windvogel sought clarification on whether any formal resolutions would be adopted based on the day’s presentation. She expressed interest in specific data on Mathematics and Physical Science performance, requesting a breakdown by race and quintile, along with details on the number of matric learners set to write exams in these subjects, comparing this year’s figures to the 2023 cohort.
The Chairperson confirmed that comparative data from 2023 and 2024 would be useful and suggested refining the request to include breakdowns by District, as proposed by Ms Windvogel. He asked the Procedural Officer to ensure these specifications were recorded.
Mr Christians supported Ms Windvogel’s request and added that he would like to monitor the results in Metro East, where Mathematics performance has historically lagged. Acknowledging that targeted interventions have been implemented, he proposed that the Committee track their impact when results are available in January 2025. He expressed hope that these measures would show meaningful improvement and asked that Metro East’s progress be evaluated to understand the effectiveness of the support initiatives.
The Chairperson agreed and proposed an additional resolution, encouraging the Department to establish a formal advisory board with representatives from key economic sectors and future employers in the Western Cape. This advisory body could help guide curriculum development and provide input on the skills needed for emerging job markets. He noted that such a body could also support teacher recruitment and training in these specialised areas. Furthermore, the Chairperson suggested the advisory board could contribute to funding initiatives aimed at enhancing learner engagement in activities such as youth arts festivals, Mathematics and Science Olympiads, and coding and robotics programmes, as well as in agricultural subjects. He invited additional comments on this resolution from Members.
Ms Wasiema Hassen-Moosa, Procedural Officer, sought clarification on Mr Christians' request regarding interventions in Metro East. She proposed that the Committee request each school’s improvement plan, detailing specific interventions, to allow Members to monitor Metro East’s performance and assess the effectiveness of the interventions.
The Chairperson agreed, suggesting that a consolidated report from the Department might provide a district-level summary of school improvement plans, rather than results for each individual school, to give the Committee a comprehensive yet manageable overview. He expressed hope that such a report would highlight necessary areas for further improvement across the District.
Mr Christians concurred, noting that Metro East has faced persistent challenges in mathematics and science over several years. He acknowledged the interventions already in place and reiterated his interest in examining post-exam outcomes to determine whether these measures have yielded improvements, especially in mathematics and science. He thanked the Chairperson and Procedural Officer for facilitating this approach.
Mr Booysen was pleased with the recommendations.
Consideration and Adoption of Draft Committee Report on the WCED Annual Report 2023/24
The Chairperson noted that the Committee appeared to be in unanimous agreement and proposed moving forward with the next agenda item. He invited Ms Wasiema Hassen-Moosa, the Procedural Officer, to guide the Committee through the draft report on the Western Cape Education Department’s annual report. He acknowledged the report’s thorough summary, which effectively captured the key points of discussion, resolutions, and information requests made during previous meetings.
The Chairperson requested that the Procedural Officer review each page, inviting members to provide comments or suggestions. He noted that the key points covered a comprehensive range of topics and were well reflected in the resolutions and requests for information. Once the review was complete, he asked for any proposals and seconders for the adoption of the report.
Ms Stephens proposed the adoption, and Ms Booysen seconded.
Adoption of Minutes
Minutes dated 10 September 2024
Mr Christians moved for the adoption of the minutes and was seconded by Mr Booysen.
Minutes dated 15 October 2024
Mr Booysen moved for the adoption and Mr Christians seconded the motion.
Committee Announcements and Closure
The Procedural Officer informed the Committee that there were no further announcements. She reminded Members that the World Bank presentation on the education sector analysis would take place the following week, followed by the Western Cape Education Department (WCED) Adjustments Appropriation discussion on 29 November, which would be the final Committee meeting of the year.
The Chairperson confirmed the upcoming World Bank presentation, noting that it would provide valuable insights into the education sector analysis conducted for the Department and offer useful advice for the Committee’s work. He then acknowledged the forthcoming Appropriations Bill discussion as the last meeting of the month, and expressed his appreciation to Committee members and officials for their participation.
Meeting adjourned.
