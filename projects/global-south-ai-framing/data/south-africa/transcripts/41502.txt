Meeting Summary
Video
The Standing Committee on Appropriations met virtually to discuss the Committee’s Strategic Planning Overview for the Seventh Parliament. The Content Advisor presented the proposed strategic plan, highlighting planning challenges, particularly the mismatch between Parliament’s and government institutions’ planning cycles. Key strategic priorities discussed included strengthening oversight, improving public participation, and enhancing appointment processes.
The Content Advisor outlined the government’s strategy to achieve microeconomic stability and boost infrastructure investment. The unemployment rate, particularly youth unemployment at 46.1%, was noted as a major concern.
One Member raised concerns about the effectiveness of oversight over institutions that lacked the capacity to implement recommendations. She argued that, similar to the local government level, institutions such as the South African Post Office (SAPO) and the Department of Water and Sanitation (DWS) had demonstrated ongoing capacity issues, which undermined the impact of oversight.
The Committee also emphasised the need for greater coordination with other committees to avoid duplication and improve synergy. The Committee suggested that oversight should be more strategic and consistent, with a focus on specific priorities through 2029.
Further, another Member stressed the importance of using the Committee’s R3 million budget effectively, pointing out that public hearings had already consumed these funds in the past. She also called for clear differentiation between the roles of the Appropriations Committee and the Standing Committee on Finance.
Another Member expressed strong support for strengthening oversight mechanisms and suggested that the Committee should focus on conducting spending reviews to identify inefficiencies, particularly in light of fiscal pressures. He also raised concerns about corruption oversight, proposing that the Committee prioritise consequence management for unauthorised, irregular, fruitless, and wasteful (UIFW) expenditure.
The Chairperson discussed the limitations of the Money Bills and Related Matters Act and proposed pursuing amendments to enhance the Committee’s role in amending or refining money bills. He also emphasised the importance of digitisation in improving the Committee’s oversight capacity, including the use of artificial intelligence (AI) to process vast amounts of parliamentary data efficiently.
He proposed developing a digital dashboard to track appropriations and spending outcomes in real time, which would allow immediate accountability and more focused engagements. He also recommended using technology for international engagements to reduce costs.
The Chairperson concluded by reinforcing the importance of accountability, public participation, and enhanced inter-committee collaboration as strategic objectives moving forward.
The meeting was adjourned after confirming that all Members' inputs would be incorporated into the draft strategic plan and Annual Performance Plan for adoption at the next meeting.
Meeting report
Committee’s Strategic Planning Overview for the Seventh Parliament
Mr Sifiso Magagula, Committee Content Advisor, presented the proposed strategic plan for the Seventh Parliament, covering the period from 2024 to 2029, alongside the annual performance plan for 2025. He highlighted the planning challenges faced by the Appropriations Committee, particularly the discrepancy between the annual performance plan cycle of Parliament and that of government institutions. Mr Magagula then discussed the strategic priorities aimed at strengthening oversight, improving public participation, and standardising appointment processes.
Economic growth and infrastructure strategy
Mr Magagula outlined the government's economic strategy, which focuses on achieving microeconomic stability and increasing public infrastructure investment to stimulate growth. He pointed out that the official unemployment rate currently stands at 33.2%, with youth unemployment notably higher at 46.1%. He also referred to the issues raised in the Legacy Report of the Standing Committee on Finance regarding infrastructure projects and public procurement, underscoring the need to address these concerns.
Strategic priorities and oversight enhancements
Mr Magagula discussed the strategic priorities for the Seventh Parliament, which include strengthening oversight, improving the legislative process, and enhancing public involvement. He urged Members to align their submissions with Parliament’s strategic objectives and emphasised the importance of using quarterly expenditure reports from the National Treasury to inform oversight activities. He also called for greater collaboration between committees and the facilitation of public participation in budgetary processes.
Enhancing legislative processes and public involvement
Mr Magagula outlined several strategic objectives aimed at improving the legislative process and increasing public involvement. These included enhancing scrutiny of statutory appointments and fostering greater engagement with external stakeholders. He stressed the importance of transparency and fairness in public office appointments and proposed regular training and international cooperation to bolster the Appropriations Committee's capacity.
Infrastructure coordination and committee collaboration
Mr Magagula proposed a plan to improve oversight of government infrastructure by collaborating with the Presidential Infrastructure Coordinating Commission (PICC) and conducting regular briefings. He recommended quarterly expenditure reports and two joint meetings per year with other committees to encourage greater collaboration. Furthermore, he emphasised the need for public participation in statutory appointments and addressed concerns regarding risks associated with capacity and resources.
Funding allocation
Mr Magagula outlined the financial constraints the Committee is facing, noting that the R3 million budget will be reduced due to prior expenditures. He suggested organising a meeting to break down the budget allocated for oversight activities and reconcile past spending.

Discussion
The Chairperson reported that, regarding the R3 million allocation, he had already engaged with the House Chairperson, noting that Committee funding was standardised across Parliament. While this alignment was beneficial, he emphasised that the Appropriations Committee undertook a far greater volume of work than most other committees. As a result, its allocations for research support, secretariat functions, and other resources were significantly higher than those of other committees.
The Chairperson pointed out that, as Members prepared to discuss the allocation of funding, careful consideration should be given to how additional activities would be financed. He drew a comparison, explaining that the Committee on Sport was coded as "green" in the parliamentary system because it typically met once a week or less. In contrast, the Appropriations Committee was coded as "red," due to the frequency of its meetings, which could occur up to three times a week depending on the programme. These concerns had already been raised at the previous Committee Chairpersons' meeting and would be revisited during the upcoming budget interface discussions.
The Chairperson clarified that it was not necessary to engage in a detailed debate on financial provisions at this stage, as further work was still needed in this area. He invited Members to offer substantive inputs, some of which would need to be submitted in writing to reflect party positions. He requested that contributions be made in rounds of discussion, rather than proceeding slide by slide, to ensure the Committee could produce a coherent strategic document rather than a collection of individual monologues.
Ms C Mkhonto (EFF) asked about the effectiveness of parliamentary oversight when it was directed at institutions that lacked the capacity to fulfil their constitutional mandates. Oversight in such circumstances, she argued, had often proved futile. She cited the example of the Auditor-General of South Africa (AGSA). The AGSA was properly capacitated to perform audits and was discharging its functions as required. However, despite the AGSA’s work, institutions often failed to implement recommendations. Ms Mkhonto expanded on her concerns regarding institutional capacity. She said that in the local government sphere, municipalities had consistently performed poorly in terms of financial management. Audit outcomes had often been unsatisfactory, and instead of improving, some municipalities had now reached the point where municipalities were unable to pay the salaries of their employees. This reflected a worsening situation, despite the AGSA being fully capacitated to conduct audits.
The problem, Ms Mkhonto argued, was that oversight was being conducted over institutions that lacked the capacity to implement recommendations. She cautioned that the Committee faced the same challenge in its own work, since many of the institutions over which it exercised oversight similarly lacked capacity. The question, she said, was how the Committee could formulate recommendations that were corrective and proactive, rather than reactive. Ms Mkhonto pointed out that a number of entities — including the South African Post Office (SAPO) and the Department of Water and Sanitation (DWS) — had demonstrated a lack of institutional capacity for years. Even if the Committee itself was capacitated to carry out oversight, the absence of capacity within those institutions meant that the same problems would persist, as had been seen at local government level.
Turning to a second point, Ms Mkhonto welcomed the proposals regarding statutory appointments. She stressed, however, that respective portfolio committees needed to exercise proper diligence in appointing boards, since those boards themselves required sufficient capacity to steer institutions effectively. In her view, cadre deployment had undermined this process, but she expressed hope that the new proposals, if properly implemented, would lead to progress and improvement in institutional performance.
The Chairperson thanked Ms Mkhonto for her contribution but reminded all Members that, to continue participating in the discussion, their cameras must remain on. While acknowledging that Ms Mkhonto had been given an indulgence this time, he emphasised the importance of compliance with this rule going forward.
Mr K Wakelin (DA) raised several concerns during the meeting. He first pointed out the need for greater coordination with other parliamentary committees, such as the National Council of Provinces (NCOP) and Finance Committees, to avoid duplication of efforts and ensure that oversight was complementary. He questioned how engagement with these counterpart committees could be better structured and suggested there was significant potential for synergy, particularly regarding appropriations and expenditure.
He went on to reflect on the Committee's engagement with various issues, such as oversight visits to hospitals and interactions with Eskom. While he acknowledged these efforts, he felt the approach had often been superficial. He proposed that the Committee take a more strategic and consistent approach, with clearly defined oversight priorities extending through to 2029. Mr Wakelin also mentioned that he had submitted specific operational proposals to the Chairperson the previous day, which he hoped would be incorporated into the Committee’s plans.
Turning to the Committee’s strategic objectives, Mr Wakelin emphasised that the first priority—strengthening oversight mechanisms—should receive a significant portion of the Committee’s focus. He requested clarification on whether this work was already being carried out, considering the broad nature of the targets set out in the report, and if not, who was taking responsibility for it.
He argued that the Committee's foremost concern should be the monitoring of Unauthorised, Irregular, Fruitless and Wasteful (UIFW) expenditure, as highlighted by the AGSA. Noting the lack of detail on this in the current planning documents, he suggested that the Committee should focus more specifically on reducing instances of irregular expenditure across departments and entities.
Regarding capital project scope creep and cost overruns, particularly in relation to projects funded by the Budget Facility for Infrastructure (BFI), Mr Wakelin mentioned he had submitted written proposals to the Chairperson, requesting that mechanisms be included in the work programme to closely monitor such risks.
He also raised the issue of material irregularities (MIs), noting that these had not been explicitly addressed in the Committee’s oversight agenda. He argued that, since the AGSA had developed a system for identifying and addressing MIs, it was crucial for the Committee to actively engage in this process to ensure thorough oversight.
Mr Wakelin stressed the importance of structured engagement with National Treasury, as the Committee regularly received in-year reports, the majority of which were Treasury-related. He suggested that the Committee should establish its expectations ahead of time to ensure that the presentations from Treasury aligned with the Committee's mandate, rather than leaving it to Treasury's discretion.
He also asked about which specific pieces of legislation the Committee intended to prioritise for public participation, citing the White Paper on Local Government as an example. He encouraged the Committee to identify such legislation early on and ensure it was tracked within the programme.
Mr Wakelin stressed that, where hearings with departments were planned, those departments should already be identified to ensure that they were aligned with the current budget cycle.
Ms N Gcaleka-Mazibuko (ANC) apologised for her initial connectivity issues and confirmed that she would turn off her video to improve audibility. She raised a concern regarding the Committee’s R3 million budget allocation, recalling that this had already been used for public hearings the previous year. She asked for clarification on whether the funds used came from the previous or the current financial year.
Ms Gcaleka-Mazibuko stressed that the Committee should lead by example when it came to the prudent use of resources. Given that the Committee often criticised departments and entities for underspending, she highlighted the importance of using its own oversight budget effectively and appropriately to ensure the delivery of its mandate.
She also cautioned that the Committee needed to clearly distinguish its oversight role from that of the Standing Committee on Finance. While the two committees were interrelated, she reminded the Committee that the Standing Committee on Finance was responsible for the fiscal framework and debt management, whereas the Appropriations Committee’s mandate focused on spending matters, including the Division of Revenue Bill, Appropriation Bill, Supplementary Appropriation Bill, and Adjustments Appropriation Bill.
Ms Gcaleka-Mazibuko noted that, as some Members served on both committees, there was often duplication of work and confusion about which committee was responsible for which tasks. She stressed the need, particularly for new Members, to clearly differentiate the two committees’ roles. She also confirmed that the African National Congress (ANC) would submit further inputs in writing, including comments on the timeframes outlined in the programme of action presented by the Committee Secretary.
On the issue of international study tours, Ms Gcaleka-Mazibuko agreed with the Committee’s planned tour to South America but also urged that the Committee focus on examining best practices within Africa. She reminded Members of Agenda 2063, which sets out a developmental vision for the continent, and emphasised that the Committee’s oversight should align with assessing Africa’s progress towards those goals. She also stressed the need for oversight to be aligned with the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), as many Millennium Development Goals had not been achieved. Ms Gcaleka-Mazibuko argued that departmental spending should be evaluated in relation to poverty reduction, inequality, and unemployment.
She further suggested that oversight visits should not be limited to inviting stakeholders to Parliament but should also include site visits to stakeholder institutions. Direct engagement, she believed, would provide Members with a better understanding of conditions on the ground. Regarding quarterly stakeholder invitations, she supported the approach but underlined that infrastructure development should be assessed not just through reports but by evaluating its measurable impact on people's lives, especially within local government. She cautioned that reports should not simply be “glossy” presentations but should reflect whether interventions resulted in tangible improvements in service delivery. She concluded by confirming that the ANC would supplement its oral contributions with written recommendations.
Mr A Bateman (DA) expressed strong support for the call to strengthen oversight mechanisms and proposed that the principle of ensuring value for money should apply beyond infrastructure expenditure, covering all public spending. He suggested conducting Spending Reviews to identify waste, duplication, and inefficiency, especially given current fiscal pressures. He also raised the issue of corruption oversight, proposing that consequence management related to irregular, fruitless, and wasteful expenditure should be prioritised.
Mr Bateman recommended that National Treasury provide the Committee with quarterly reports on consequence management and the implementation status of recommendations made by the Committee, particularly concerning legislation such as the Division of Revenue Bill and Appropriations Bills.
Lastly, Mr Bateman recommended refining the strategic plan's wording on economic transformation and redistribution. He cautioned that overly redistributive policies could undermine investment, growth, and employment. He argued that government should instead prioritise measures that promote growth, job creation, and education as the most effective way to address unemployment, poverty, and inequality.
Mr Musa Zamisa, Committee Researcher, expressed strong support for the importance of collaboration with stakeholders, emphasising that such partnerships enhance primary research, reduce costs, and improve the quality of oversight. He cited the example of Equal Education and the Equal Education Law Centre, whose collaboration during an oversight visit to the Eastern Cape had enabled the Committee to identify schools with inadequate infrastructure. Mr Zamisa argued that these collaborations also help build public trust in Parliament’s work.
He went on to suggest that the Committee could strengthen its capacity-building efforts by incorporating technology to conduct virtual study tours and training sessions, including collaborations with international institutions such as the International Monetary Fund (IMF) and World Bank. He also supported Ms Gcaleka-Mazibuko’s proposal for study tours within Africa, recalling that South Africa’s past engagements with Uganda and Kenya had provided valuable insights.
Mr Zamisa then highlighted that South Africa could learn useful lessons from other African countries on strengthening budgetary and financial management systems. He cited Uganda and Kenya as examples where the Committee had gained significant insights. However, he pointed out a recurring challenge: despite the Committee adopting numerous reports and making impactful recommendations each year, many departments fail to implement them effectively. This lack of follow-through, he argued, undermines the Committee’s oversight efforts, erodes Members’ momentum, and diminishes the value of their work. He stressed that unless Parliament develops mechanisms to enforce responses to its recommendations, the Committee's energy would risk being wasted. Mr Zamisa linked this concern directly to the issue of consequence management and echoed earlier calls for remedies to ensure accountability when recommendations are ignored.
Mr M Lekganyane (ANC) observed that while departments and municipalities appear before the Committee during the appropriations process, there is often little follow-up until the next budget cycle. He proposed that the Committee introduce mid-year follow-ups to assess whether allocated funds were being used effectively. He specifically mentioned municipalities like the City of Johannesburg and Mangaung Metropolitan Municipality, which had appeared before the Committee, suggesting that they should be called back during the financial year to account for how appropriated funds had been applied. This, he argued, would help prevent oversight processes from being incomplete.
Mr Lekganyane also underscored the need to strengthen public participation in the budget process. While he welcomed the Committee’s initiative to hold public hearings in Limpopo, he argued that communities should be consulted before the budget proposals are finalised, rather than after they have already been drafted. He explained that early public engagement would allow citizens to shape the priorities meaningfully, ensuring that the budget reflects local needs and follows a strategic approach. He concluded that aligning priorities with budget allocations through participatory processes would significantly enhance Parliament’s service to the people.
Mr Mashile Maubane, Committee Secretary, expressed his support for Mr Zamisa’s earlier remarks on the importance of collaboration and expanded on the discussion by highlighting the need for better coordination with other committees. He cautioned against the risk of duplicating oversight work, noting that the Committee’s planned oversight visit to the Eastern Cape had coincided with a similar visit by the Portfolio Committee on Higher Education.
Mr Maubane suggested that, before undertaking oversight visits, the Appropriations Committee should engage in joint planning sessions with relevant portfolio committees to ensure that their efforts are aligned, avoid overlapping work, and make the best use of available resources. He also pointed to the North West Province, where he believed that better collaboration with portfolio committees could have identified overlapping issues earlier. He argued that such coordination would prevent the Committee from "rediscovering" issues that should have already been addressed by line committees and would ultimately strengthen the oversight function across Parliament.
Chairperson’s input
The Chairperson acknowledged that while the Money Bills and Related Matters Act provided the legislative framework for the Committee’s work, it had significant limitations. These were not flaws in the Act itself, but stemmed from how its provisions functioned in practice, particularly regarding timelines and resources. He proposed that one of the Committee’s strategic objectives should be to pursue amendments to the Act to enhance the Committee’s ability to amend, reject, or refine money bills more effectively.
The Chairperson then stressed the importance of digitisation and the potential of artificial intelligence (AI) and machine learning to enhance the Committee’s information management. He argued that Parliament produces vast amounts of information, including Budgetary Review & Recommendations Reports (BRRR) reports, legislation, and oversight findings, which often remain underutilised. By incorporating digitisation and AI, the Committee could distil this information into actionable insights.
He proposed developing a rubric and digital dashboard to streamline Committee engagements. This system would enable Members to track key priorities, budget allocations, and implementation progress in real time, while preventing departments from overwhelming the Committee with repetitive or irrelevant presentation slides. Such a tool would ensure that engagements remain focused on strategic oversight objectives.
Looking to the future, the Chairperson envisaged a system where, at the click of a button, the Committee could access live data on appropriations and expenditure by sector—such as infrastructure spending in transport. This would enable Members to hold departments accountable immediately and efficiently. He further emphasised that digitisation would not only improve the Committee’s internal oversight but also provide Parliament with a modern, transparent, and user-friendly tool to demonstrate accountability to the public.
The Chairperson then expanded on the Committee’s strategic oversight framework, using the example of Mangaung Metro. He explained that while issues such as governance and management fall under the mandate of other portfolio committees, the Appropriations Committee’s focus should remain on appropriations. He noted that digitisation could play a central role in tracking such allocations directly, making it one of the Committee’s key objectives going forward.
Turning to state-owned enterprises (SOEs), the Chairperson highlighted that these entities are multi-disciplinary by nature and often straddle several committees, with some falling under the Presidency, which has no dedicated oversight committee. He proposed the adoption of a rubric-based system for Committee engagements with SOEs, ensuring that departments and entities respond to specific, pre-set questions rather than providing broad, repetitive presentations. He cited the South African Post Office as a topical example. He noted that the Committee's oversight should link appropriation decisions to strategic objectives outlined in the Medium Term Strategic Framework (MTSF), the President's State of the Nation Address (SONA), and cross-committee discussions. This would enable the Committee to be well-informed when considering future special appropriations or adjustment budgets for SOEs, fulfilling its trusteeship role on behalf of the people of South Africa.
The Chairperson stressed that Members were not there to impose personal views but to fulfil their constitutional responsibility under the Appropriations Act. This role required a narrower, more focused, and comparative oversight framework, which could also be used to align the work of the Committee with that of the Standing Committee on Finance and the NCOP Appropriations Committee.
He also proposed strengthening relationships with key fiscal institutions, such as the Financial and Fiscal Commission (FFC) and the Parliamentary Budget Office (PBO), to ensure their research and expertise are integrated into the Committee’s strategic objectives. He noted that these engagements should not be seen as “box-ticking” exercises but as opportunities for deeper collaboration, particularly in the area of spending reviews, which provide essential evidence on value for money and programme efficiency. Partnering with the PBO and FFC, he argued, would significantly enhance the Committee’s research capacity.
On public consultation, the Chairperson cautioned against reducing the process to mere roadshows. He criticised the tendency of stakeholders such as Business Unity South Africa (BUSA) to approach the President directly on budget matters, pointing out that the drafting of money bills and appropriations is Parliament’s constitutional mandate, not the executive’s. He emphasised that the Committee must instead create targeted, structured opportunities for stakeholders to engage directly with Parliament.
The Chairperson welcomed the suggestion to embed the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) into the Committee’s framework and reminded Members that the Committee had previously agreed to strengthen its global engagement. He recommended using technology to host webinars with institutions such as the IMF and World Bank, allowing Members to access global economic perspectives and growth forecasts without the need for costly travel. He noted that such comparative analysis would enrich the Committee’s deliberations, especially during the budget cycle in January and February, when institutions like the IMF, the Reserve Bank, and National Treasury release their forecasts.
Arguing that digitisation was essential for future oversight, the Chairperson proposed the development of a live digital dashboard to track appropriations, spending reviews, and programme outcomes in real time. This would not only capacitate Members but also empower the public to access information directly, making them informed stakeholders in the budget consultation process. He cited the Limpopo public hearings as an important but insufficient step, stressing the need for expanded educational and informational outreach surrounding the “people’s budget.”
In conclusion, the Chairperson stated that strengthening accountability, digitising oversight, embedding public participation, and enhancing inter-committee collaboration must all be strategic objectives for the Committee moving forward.
Mr Magagula thanked Members for their contributions and confirmed that all inputs would be incorporated into the draft strategic plan and Annual Performance Plan (APP) for adoption at the following week’s meeting. He encouraged Members to submit their written inputs by Friday afternoon to allow sufficient time for revisions.
Mr Magagula further cautioned against duplication of work, noting that certain matters, such as material irregularities, were more appropriately handled by the Standing Committee on Public Accounts (SCOPA). However, the Appropriations Committee could still engage with them where relevant. He supported greater collaboration with NCOP committees, recalling that joint meetings had previously been held with the Select Committee on Appropriations.
The Chairperson also raised concerns about Parliament’s long-standing failure to implement a resolution-tracking mechanism, despite repeated calls since 2015. He proposed that the Committee set aside a quarterly meeting specifically to review departmental responses to recommendations. This would prevent vague or dismissive replies from being accepted without scrutiny and ensure that recommendations are treated seriously.
Closing remarks
The Chairperson endorsed the proposal for Members to submit their inputs by Friday. He reiterated that vague recommendations lead to vague departmental responses, stressing that the Committee must sharpen the specificity of its resolutions, particularly when engaging with National Treasury. He cited the Value-Added Tax (VAT) hearings as an example where the Committee had been able to frame specific, actionable proposals.
The Chairperson thanked Members across parties for their contributions and confirmed that the consolidated document would be finalised for adoption and submission to the broader parliamentary process by mid-September.
Meeting adjourned.
