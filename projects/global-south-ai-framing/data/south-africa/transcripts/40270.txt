Title: Impact of USA foreign aid cuts and the implications for healthcare in the province; Khayelitsha District Hospital Oversight Visit Report
Date: 21 Feb 2025
Committee: 
URL: https://pmg.org.za/committee-meeting/40270/

Meeting Summary
Video
The Standing Committee convened to address the impact of U.S. foreign aid cuts on the Western Cape’s healthcare services. The Department of Health and Wellness (DHW) provided insights into the funding crisis, its effect on key health programmes, and the Department’s response strategies.
Key issues discussed were:
The financial impact. The loss of R365 million in donor funding, including a five-year $10.5 million USAID grant, had created significant challenges.
Service disruptions. Cuts had affected HIV and TB programmes, immunisation campaigns, and community-based healthcare services, with 698 healthcare workers impacted, primarily from non-profit organisations.
Legal considerations: The Committee discussed whether the U.S. funding withdrawal constituted a breach of contract, potentially providing a legal basis for action.
Alternative funding sources: Suggestions included seeking assistance from Provincial Treasury, the Global Fund, and the National Lotteries Commission.
Public communication and transparency: Members emphasised the importance of clear public messaging to prevent unnecessary panic and to maintain confidence in anti-retroviral (ARV) and TB treatment availability.
Key resolutions adopted were:
Legal advice: The Committee would seek legal clarity on whether it could call national government agencies to appear and provide updates on mitigation plans.
Transparency measures: The DHW had to provide memorandums of understanding and detailed non-governmental organisation (NGO) funding data, including lost amounts and affected programmes.
NGO engagement: The Committee would invite representatives from affected NGOs to discuss challenges and mitigation efforts.
Monitoring and communication: The DHW must ensure continuous public updates on the aid cuts’ impact and alternative funding efforts.
The Committee said it remained committed to mitigating the impact of these funding cuts and ensuring continuity in essential healthcare services. The next steps involved engaging Treasury, exploring legal avenues, and securing additional funding sources while keeping the public and stakeholders informed
Meeting report
The Chairperson said the main topic of discussion for the day was the global impact of the United States' foreign aid policy changes under the administration of President Donald Trump, and stressed the importance of understanding how these changes would affect healthcare services in the province.
Minister’s introductory remarks
Ms
Mireille
Wenger, Western Cape Minister of Health and Wellness (DHW), provided an overview of the province’s long-standing collaboration with international partners, particularly the United States. She said funding from these partnerships had been critical in supporting programs for HIV and TB management, strengthening healthcare services, and enhancing technical expertise.
However, she highlighted concerns regarding recent policy shifts, which had created uncertainty and funding gaps. The Department, in collaboration with non-governmental organisations (NGOs), was actively seeking clarity and exploring ways to mitigate potential risks. She assured the Committee that they would be kept informed of any further developments.
Department on impact of US foreign aid cuts
Dr Saadiq Kariem, Chief of Operations, DHW, emphasised the rapidly evolving nature of the situation. Updates were occurring on a near-weekly basis, and the Department was working hard to keep up with these developments.
He provided an overview of the current challenges faced by the (DHW), highlighting financial pressures, which had been discussed in previous meetings; the growing burden of disease, including the diphtheria outbreak, linked to declining immunisation rates, and an ongoing measles outbreak; and emerging health threats, including the first-ever documented outbreak of rabies in seals and other exotic viruses appearing across Africa. He said that maintaining an immunisation coverage rate of at least 80% was critical to preventing outbreaks, yet the province was currently at 74%.
Dr Kariem provided a timeline of the financial cuts and executive orders that had led to the funding crisis:
24 January: The Trump administration issued a suspension of foreign assistance with a 90-day review period.
30 January: An executive stop order was issued for the Government-to-Government (G2G) grant, a unique $10 million direct award from the US government to the Western Cape, covering five years.
8 February: Reports emerged that all United States Aid for International Development (USAID) financial assistance to South Africa had been cut.
12 February: Rescission orders were issued for Centre for Disease Control (CDC)-funded programmes, including those supporting voluntary medical male circumcision through Right to Care.
13 February: A similar rescission order was issued for TB/HIV Care, significantly impacting its operations.
The G2G grant was a rare subnational award, demonstrating the Western Cape’s strong partnership with USAID. However, the stop order placed an indefinite hold on this funding.
Dr Kariem displayed copies of the executive stop orders, illustrating the seriousness of the situation.
Direct impact on healthcare services
Dr Kariem outlined the direct impact on healthcare services due to these cuts:
Preventive services: Condom distribution and voluntary medical male circumcision programs had been affected.
Medicine disruptions: Challenges in medicine collection and dispensing.
Data collection: Affected by the halt in G2G funding.
Clinic closures: The Men’s Clinic and Transgender & Sex Worker Clinic had shut down, non-profit organisations (NPOs) responsible for these services had begun removing their assets and complaints from affected individuals had been increasing.
Testing and treatment: Disruptions to testing adherence and counselling services, delays in the TB emergency response plan, and an impact on the antiretroviral therapy (ART) enrolment campaign and the 95-95-95 treatment targets.
Mitigation strategies implemented
The Department had engaged with service providers and NPO partners to mitigate the impact of these disruptions. The key strategies include:
Fast-tracking the digitisation of HIV and TB registers to maintain essential reporting data.
Expanding electronic prescribing (E-Scripting) to reduce congestion in clinics.
Implementing six-month ARV dispensing to limit clinic visits for stable patients.
Redirecting affected patients to alternative healthcare facilities.
Enhancing collaboration with NPO partners to find solutions for service continuity.
Assessing the impact of discontinued services to identify gaps and address them accordingly.
The national Department of Health issued a directive to ensure uninterrupted HIV services, focusing on:
Prioritising repeat prescriptions for HIV patients.
Ensuring continued access to treatment by identifying alternative service points.
Strengthening mobile services to reach affected populations.
Maintaining ART enrolment targets despite the funding shortfall.
Additional strategies for ART continuity included:
Multi-month dispensing (three-month and six-month ARV script refills).
Extending the grace period for late ART collections by 28 days.
Automatic renewal of chronic medication scripts through the Chronic Dispensing Unit (CDU), reducing the need for physical clinic visits.
Financial Implications
Dr Kariem provided a breakdown of the financial impact, stating that R365 million had been lost for this financial year due to the funding cuts. This affected the ten NPOs listed in the report and the G2G programme, which was directly funding 700 healthcare workers. Engagements with Treasury were ongoing to explore alternative funding sources. However, the Department was operating under the assumption that none of the funding would be restored.
He reiterated that this crisis came at a time of immense financial strain and an escalating disease burden. The Department expected an increase in patient complaints, which the Minister had already been forwarding to senior officials.
He reassured members that:
Governance structures were in place, including daily monitoring and risk assessments.
The Minister was engaging at the Cabinet level to advocate for additional resources.
Ongoing communication with NPOs and stakeholders was essential for a unified response.
Mitigation plans would be continuously adjusted, based on new developments.
Dr Kariem concluded by stating that while the situation remained fluid, the Department would keep the committee informed of any updates.

Discussion
Legal developments in the US
Ms B van Minnen (DA) referred to a federal court ruling on 14 February that temporarily lifted the aid freeze and restored funding for certain programmes. She inquired whether this legal decision had any impact on the Western Cape’s funding situation, and whether similar contracts had been reinstated at the provincial level.
She acknowledged that separation-of-powers disputes within the U.S. government could further complicate the situation, but sought clarity on whether any relief had been granted to the province.
Financial shortfall and budget pressures
Mr L van Wyk (DA) emphasised the significant impact of the R365 million funding loss, noting that while it represented 1% to 1.2% of the total budget, it remained a substantial shortfall, given the province's existing financial constraints. He highlighted recent site visits to the Karl Bremer Hospital, where Members had observed firsthand the pressures on service delivery and resource allocation. He asked how the DHW planned to address the shortfall, particularly regarding budget reallocations and risk mitigation strategies.
He further noted that financial constraints were not unique to South Africa, as budget pressures had also contributed to the U.S. government’s decision to reduce aid. He stressed that the Western Cape must prepare for continued fiscal challenges, regardless of external funding decisions.
Ms D Stephens (PA) raised concerns about the broader impact of the funding cuts on healthcare services, particularly regarding HIV treatment and research initiatives. She specifically referred to the halted African-led HIV vaccine research programme, questioning whether the province was adequately prepared to navigate the challenges ahead if additional healthcare disruptions occurred.
Financial transparency and provincial dependency on foreign aid
Ms R Windvogel (ANC) expressed appreciation for the six-month ARV dispensing initiative, noting that it would help alleviate long queues at clinics.
She requested clarity on financial disbursements across provinces, particularly in relation to the "recession award" mentioned in the presentation.
She also inquired whether the Western Cape government was disproportionately reliant on U.S. funding, compared to other provinces. She asked whether a contingency plan beyond communication efforts was in place, particularly in the event of ARV stock shortages.
Impact on vulnerable communities
Ms A Bans (ANC) expressed concern about the impact of the funding cuts on marginalised communities, particularly HIV and TB patients who rely on community-based healthcare services.
She emphasised that the poorest communities would suffer the most from longer clinic wait times and reduced service availability. She sought clarity on contingency plans in response to the withdrawal of U.S. funding.
She also questioned the province’s reliance on donor funding, asking how provincial allocations compared to external funding sources and whether the province had sufficient financial independence to sustain essential healthcare programmes.
Long-term healthcare planning
Mr T Walters (DA) acknowledged the immediate impact of the funding cuts, but encouraged the Committee to also focus on long-term healthcare sustainability.
He emphasised that South Africa was entering an era of fiscal constraints, requiring the Western Cape to prepare for ongoing financial pressures.
He suggested that the Committee schedule a future presentation from the Department on long-term healthcare strategies, particularly regarding financial sustainability and service efficiency over the next decade.
DHW's response
Minister Wenger acknowledged the concerns raised by Members, and reiterated that the Western Cape had gradually moved away from relying on foreign aid for core healthcare services. She clarified that while donor funds had historically been used for technical support, data collection, and system strengthening, essential services such as ARV procurement and salaries for healthcare staff were funded through the provincial budget and conditional grants.
She assured the Committee that while the biggest impact would be felt in community-based care, the province had already begun engaging national and provincial stakeholders to mitigate risks. She confirmed that all provinces except the Northern Cape were affected by the funding cuts, and emphasised the need for a coordinated response at a national level.
Dr Kariem followed by addressing the specific questions and concerns raised by committee members.
Legal challenges in the US and potential reinstatement of funds
The Department was aware of the federal court ruling on 14 February, which temporarily lifted the aid freeze, but no formal communication had been received indicating that funding for the Western Cape would be restored.
The situation in the US remained complex, with ongoing separation-of-powers disputes between the federal government and the judiciary.
The Western Cape could not rely on legal resolutions in the US, and would continue planning as though the funding would not be reinstated.
Financial pressures and budgetary adjustments
While the R365 million cut represents 1-1.2% of the total budget, the implications for specific programmes were significant.
The Department was currently reallocating internal resources, but this came amid existing financial pressures.
The site visit to Karl Bremer Hospital highlighted the strain on service delivery, and the Department was aware that absorbing these financial shocks would be difficult.
Treasury engagements were ongoing to identify alternative funding streams.
Impact on service delivery
The loss of funding directly affects preventive services, community-based care, and NGO-supported initiatives.
The HIV vaccine research programme, which was an African-led initiative, had been put on hold.
While ARV medication access would remain stable, the cuts would impact support services, including testing, counselling, and outreach programmes.
Western Cape’s dependence on US funding
The Western Cape’s approach had always been to use donor funds for system strengthening, rather than direct service delivery.
Other provinces used donor funding to cover operational costs, meaning they would face a more severe impact.
The “recession award” affects the flow of funds to provinces, but the Western Cape had a lower reliance on foreign funding than some other regions.
A contingency plan beyond communication includes fast-tracking digitisation, six-month ARV dispensing, and automating ART renewals.
Mitigation measures for vulnerable communities
The biggest impact was on vulnerable populations, especially HIV & TB patients who rely on NGO-run community services.
The Department was working with NGOs to ensure continuity of care, even as funding was reduced.
Discussions with Treasury continue, but the Department had to prioritise core healthcare services within provincial budgets.
Long-term planning for healthcare
Fiscal constraints were a long-term reality, and the Department was already looking beyond 2030 to a 2050 vision for healthcare.
This includes artificial intelligence (AI)-driven diagnostics, telehealth, and digital innovations to improve efficiency.
The Department was open to presenting a detailed long-term healthcare strategy at a future Committee meeting.
Further discussion
Ms Windvogel asked whether the current financial situation meant that there would be no budget reallocation from non-essential programmes to sustain critical health services. She sought clarity on whether that was the message being conveyed.
Dr Kariem responded that the Department had already been engaged in a process of reprioritisation, not only due to the US aid cuts, but as part of an ongoing effort to assess and allocate resources effectively. He explained that the "Start Well, Live Well, Age Well" strategy, within the broader "Run Well" framework, was being used to analyse and prioritise services.
He emphasised that immunisation remained a crucial priority, alongside TB and HIV treatment. While the Department wished to provide care for all patients, financial realities required them to focus on essential services.
Ms Bans followed up by asking whether the Department could confirm that there was no need for concern over TB and HIV medication, given that donor funding was understood as a supplementary "top-up" rather than a primary budgetary reliance. She sought confirmation that core funding was secure, meaning that healthcare services would continue uninterrupted.
Dr Kariem agreed, affirming that the Department had always operated with the understanding that donor funding was conditional and could be withdrawn at any time. He reiterated that while donor contributions -- such as those from USAID and MasterCard -- aided with technical support, health system strengthening, and data management, the responsibility for core healthcare services lay with the provincial allocations provided by the Committee.
He said that while the Western Cape had structured its healthcare financing accordingly, other provinces were more reliant on external funding and were likely to experience a more severe impact from the US aid cuts.
The Chairperson posed two of his own follow-up questions.
The first, which he described as “serious”, concerned the legal challenge in the United States regarding aid funding. He asked whether contractual law would apply between the US government and South Africa, and whether a breach of contract could be argued if the funding agreement was suddenly withdrawn. He speculated that if such a challenge were successful, it could potentially buy the Department additional time to secure alternative funding.
Dr Kariem responded that while the Chairperson's “wild” question was somewhat unexpected, it was nonetheless relevant. He confirmed that the Department had raised similar concerns with their US counterparts, believing there was a potential breach of contract. He explained that the Western Cape had a formal five-year agreement with USAID worth $10.5 million, which Premier Alan Winde had been present to sign. The funds were not disbursed in full upfront but rather in tranches, dependent on the province meeting specific performance outcomes. This was why data tracking and compliance were so critical in ensuring funding continuity.
Regarding job losses, Dr Kariem confirmed that 698 individuals had already been informed that their employment had been terminated. These job cuts primarily affected employees working under non-profit organisations, with 27 of the affected staff members directly funded through G2G (government-to-government) grants.
He emphasised that this situation underscored why the Department viewed the withdrawal of funds as a contractual breach, given that the agreement had set terms and expectations that had abruptly been disrupted.
The Chairperson raised the idea of National Lotteries Commission (Lotto) funding as a possible alternative, acknowledging that the demand for Lotto funding was high, but suggesting that it might still be worth exploring.
Minister Wenger appreciated the suggestion, and acknowledged that the Department was actively engaging with both local and international partners to mitigate the impact of funding cuts. However, she cautioned that the situation was rapidly evolving, and further developments were expected.
She assured the Committee that the Department was committed to transparency and service continuity, and would keep members informed of any updates.
Committee actions and resolutions
NGO engagement and transparency
Ms Windvogel proposed that the Committee invite representatives from affected NGOs to discuss their challenges.
The Chairperson asked whether she was referring to representative bodies, rather than all NGOs. Ms Windvogel confirmed that she meant representatives.
Ms Bans recommended that the Committee obtain a detailed list of all affected NGOs, including:
Funding amounts they were supposed to receive.
Total amounts lost due to the U.S. aid cuts.
The number of job losses incurred.
She also called for a report on public agreements related to U.S. funding, including memorandums of understanding (MoUs) and funding structures, to ensure transparency and public reassurance.
The Chairperson acknowledged the points, commenting that transparency was key, especially in preventing unnecessary panic.
Legal advice on national government’s role
Ms Van Minnen cautioned that discussions should be handled sensitively, as international contracts were under a 90-day review period. She stressed that the Committee must be careful not to jeopardise aid negotiations.
Mr Walters emphasised the need for clear public communication, ensuring that people understood that ARV distribution remained intact. He proposed that legal advice be sought to determine whether the Committee could summon national government agencies for discussions.
Ms Bans questioned the need for legal advice on MoUs that were already public documents, arguing that transparency was essential, and that the Committee had a duty to keep communities informed.
Key resolutions
The Chairperson summarised the key resolutions. These were:
To seek legal advice on whether the Committee could call national government agencies to appear.
Obtain relevant MoUs for review before engaging externally.
Gather data on affected NGOs and funding shortfalls.
Ensure clear and ongoing public communication.
With no objections raised, the resolutions were adopted unanimously.
Khayelitsha District Hospital oversight visit report
and Committee minutes
The Committee proceeded to review the Committee report on the unannounced visit to Khayelitsha District Hospital on 31 January, and the minutes of 14 February.
The report was adopted, proposed by Ms Stephens, seconded by Ms Bans.
The minutes were approved, with no objections.
The Chairperson thanked the Department team, and acknowledged the difficult challenges ahead. He encouraged Members to continue working together to find solutions.
The meeting was adjourned.
