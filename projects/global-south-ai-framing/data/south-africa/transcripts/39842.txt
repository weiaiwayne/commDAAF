Meeting Summary
Video
The Committee convened in a virtual meeting to receive an update from the Department of Mineral Resources and Energy on the Renewable Energy Independent Power Producers Procurement Programme and the Risk Mitigation Independent Power Producers Procurement Programme. The Committee was also briefed by private sector entities representing aspects of the renewable energy sector: the Energy Council of South Africa, the South African Wind Energy Association, the South African Photovoltaic Industry Association and the South African Independent Power Producers Association.
The Department and the Independent Power Producers Office presented key achievements, such as milestones in renewable capacity, job creation, and carbon reduction, alongside challenges like grid constraints and budget limitations. They discussed strategies, including targeted procurement to improve grid access and support for socio-economic development in affected communities.
Members raised questions about government payments to independent power producers, local community ownership in energy projects, and Eskom’s monopoly over grid access. Discussions included renewable energy intermittency, the regulatory framework for independent power producers, and local employment in energy projects. Members asked whether the socio-economic commitments that are required of independent power producers are standardised.
Representatives from the industry associations highlighted South Africa’s significant wind and solar capacity, discussing issues like regulatory bottlenecks, supply chain limitations, and skilled labour shortages. They proposed solutions such as improved community involvement, support for local manufacturing, and end-of-life management for solar photovoltaic modules. The underscored the need for the delayed regulatory reforms to facilitate grid access to be finalised.
Members asked about localising the manufacturing of components for the generation of renewable energy, the prospects for generation closer to the point of use, and the roles of Eskom and the Regulator. Had the associations engaged with education institutions to establish programs that would equip individuals with the skills required for wind energy plant operations? What measures were being taken to push Eskom to stop anti-competitive practices? What was the potential for direct power sales to municipalities and could this alleviate the impact of existing grid constraints? What interactions had occurred with National Treasury and the Department of Trade, Industry and Competition to eliminate import duties on components for renewable energy projects?

Update by the Department of Mineral Resources and Energy (DMRE) and the Independent Power Producers (IPP) Office
Mr Bernard Magoro, Head of the IPP Office, updated the Committee on the Renewable Energy and Independent Power Producers Procurement Programme (REIPPPP) and the Risk Mitigation Independent Power Producers Procurement Programme (RMIPPPP). The presentation covered four main areas: the economic development contributions of IPP programs, current realities and options for future procurement, program status updates, and the governance framework of the IPP Office.
The IPP Office operates under a tripartite agreement involving the Department (DMRE), National Treasury, and the Development Bank of Southern Africa (DBSA). It manages various procurement programmes such as the REIPPPP and RMIPPPP, as well as the Battery Energy Storage Independent Power Producers Procurement Programme (BESIPPPP), and the Gas Independent Power Producers Procurement Programme (GASIPPPP). These programs are designed to address South Africa’s electricity shortages by procuring renewable energy capacity from independent producers. IPPs bear most project risks, covering construction, site management, and operational costs, while government support through policy oversight and certain financial provisions.
The presentation reported significant milestones, including increased operational power capacity, new projects under development, and socio-economic impacts like job creation and carbon reduction. It also highlighted key challenges, such as grid constraints in high-demand regions and a limited budget for expansion, alongside proposed solutions like geographically targeted procurement and potential new models for bid evaluation.
The IPP Office outlined a commitment to meeting specific social and economic goals, including promoting local ownership, supporting black South African stakeholders, and improving community engagement. The presentation concluded with future recommendations, stressing the importance of infrastructure development, consistent communication with potential bidders, and the ongoing evaluation of procurement strategies to align with evolving market conditions and grid capacities.

DMRE and IPP Office responses
Mr Magoro said no government guarantees have been called so far, meaning that Eskom has honoured all the IPP payments since the start of the program. As a result, government did not make any payments.
Karpowerships had not reached commercial or financial close because the companies involved had failed to meet the required conditions for appointment. One of these conditions was securing the necessary environmental authorisations, and another was ensuring the importation of goods. As a result, no Karpowerships are operational in the country today.
Local community ownership [in renewable power projects] was structured through community trusts. The communities and project companies created these trusts, with the communities being represented within them. The trustees were then registered, and the process was managed between the communities and as shareholders in the project company. On government’s side, the socio-economic commitments made by the projects were monitored. These commitments typically focused on supporting the communities where the projects were located. There was a distinction between community ownership in terms of shares and the socio-economic benefits communities received from the development and enterprise commitments.
He confirmed that no guarantees had been triggered so far, and reiterated that Eskom had fulfilled its obligations in previous years of the program. Regarding the reduction of the sovereign guarantee, he explained that two years previously, they began working with National Treasury to reduce the government guarantee. This involved consultations with stakeholders, including developers and the lending community. Following these discussions, National Treasury decided to start reducing the guarantee, which was implemented for Bid Window 7, which was currently being evaluated. The Bid Window 7 program was considered very promising, with the guarantee kept at 80% of the project cost, giving the project company the option to take over the project and find an alternative buyer. The impact of this on tariffs would be assessed once the evaluation of Bid Window 7 was completed. He said the plan, as agreed with National Treasury, was to continue reducing the sovereign guarantee going forward.
He was unsure about the question of how much had been paid excluding the cost of electricity and asked that the question be repeated. The average cost of IPP generation for the diesel-operated IPPs was above six Rands per unit the last time he checked. The average cost for renewable IPPs in earlier rounds was around four Rands per kilowatt-hour, as shown in the figures. The cost had dropped to about 56 cents per kilowatt-hour for the latest round. The most recent Eskom annual report stated that the average cost [for Eskom generation] was approximately 226 cents per kilowatt-hour.
A point has been raised about the intermittent nature of renewable energy. The IPP Office run programs based on the mandate they received from the Department, as outlined in the Integrated Resource Plan (IRP). For example, in risk mitigation, projects were designed to meet the challenge of supplying energy between 5 a.m. and 9 p.m. There were some innovative solutions, where project companies combined renewables with energy storage to meet the required energy profile. For future procurement, there was a possibility of running similar programs to help manage the intermittent nature of renewables while also contributing to peak demand. This approach would focus on solution-based procurement rather than technology-based procurement, and he was working with Eskom’s system operator on this.
He was not in a position to respond on behalf of Eskom on grid access rules. He was aware that there was an interim set of rules in place. These rules were meant to be elevated to a grid code, which would then be regulated at the national level. Currently, they were still working with these interim rules, and he was unsure about plans to codify them into national rules.
Socio-economic commitments [for IPPs] were standardised. The requirements were in alignment with the Broad-Based Black Economic Empowerment (BBBEE) codes, so that projects would commit on the same basis, making it easier to compare them during evaluation. However, since 2022, following the promulgation of regulations under the Preferential Procurement Policy Framework Act, they were no longer allowed to set minimum thresholds. As a result, they had observed a decline in project commitments, which was a concern. There was a process within these commitments to monitor these commitments of projects on a quarterly basis. In the past, some projects had not fully delivered on their commitments. However, the IPP Office has since strengthened its monitoring efforts, which have led to improvements in how projects were addressing the challenges faced by communities. They were constantly encouraging projects to meet their commitments and preferred that they invest any penalties into uplifting the communities rather than paying fines. The Office had recently appointed forensic auditors to support internal monitoring efforts. These auditors helped verify the information provided by the projects and the feedback received from the communities. This process was a priority for the IPP Office, and external forensic service providers were supporting their efforts to enhance monitoring. It was important to recognise the vulnerability of certain communities in the process. He deferred some details to the Deputy Director-General (DDG), who was present on the call, as there was ongoing work to address the needs of communities that were not economically viable to electrify through the standard electrification program.
On refinancing, he explained that debt financing had been expensive, especially in the earlier rounds due to the programme’s lack of track record and high perceived risk. However, with refinancing, project companies were able to secure cheaper funding. The requirement, from government’s side, was that at least 50% of the benefit from the lower financing costs be shared between the consumer and the project company. This reduction in cost would directly translate into a decrease in tariffs. For example, if a project could save 5% on debt costs, that would result in a 2.5% reduction in tariffs for consumers.
He said that he was not qualified to discuss the details of the supply of gas from Mozambique, except for what had been reported in the media. There was potential for more gas imports from Mozambique. Other entities were exploring the possibility of building a terminal in Mozambique to tap into the Republic of Mozambique Pipeline Investments Company (ROMPCO) pipeline for gas delivery into South Africa. He hoped someone from the Department could provide further details on this.
In response to the comment about the need for transparency in the pricing mechanism, he explained that the process was highly competitive, with no set floor or ceiling price. Bidders competed, and over the years, the prices dropped, reflecting the competitive nature of the process. The prices were determined by the bidders, not by the government.
The responsibility for acquiring land and securing environmental authorisations rested with the developers. However, the government supported them by engaging with various stakeholders, particularly the Department of Forestry, Fisheries, and the Environment (DFFE), to help prioritise the projects. If a large bidding window was closing, the government would engage with DFFE to see if there was a need to grant more time to projects based on their environmental approval timelines. Nevertheless, the government did not take on the responsibility of acquiring land or handling environmental issues directly.
He already discussed how the IPP Office monitors and enforces compliance with the commitments made by the projects. However, if there were any specific issues related to any wind farm, they had a dedicated team of Economic Development (ED) managers who were responsible for monitoring and investigating those concerns.
Mr Thabo Kekana, DDG: Programmes and Projects, DMRE, said that the concept of microgrids was not new. Eskom had already conducted pilot-scale work on microgrids, installing a few of them around the country, though on a small scale. Testing had been done at Komati, among other locations. He believed that microgrids would play an increasingly important role in South Africa’s energy mix in the future. Two main reasons were: first, the rising electricity tariffs, which made it difficult for some communities to afford grid connection despite being geographically close to [the grid]; and second, in rural areas where grid connection was not feasible due to the geographical distribution of communities.
To address these challenges, the Department introduced an additional component to the electrification program, targeting communities that are far from the grid. Currently, they are rolling out mini solar home systems, but in the future, they plan to accelerate the introduction of microgrids. This would help ensure that even communities far from the grid could benefit from lower tariffs. Two microgrids had been installed in a pilot programme for microgrids in Musina, Limpopo. One of these microgrids could supply power to around 13 communities or houses. The results from this pilot would inform future plans to expand microgrid deployment through the Integrated National Electrification Program (INEP).
Eskom and Sasol signed a Memorandum of Understanding (MOU) about a month ago. The objective of the MOU was to track and manage gas reserves and supply. The gas reserves in Mozambique, which South Africa relies on, were expected to be depleted by around 2027, a situation referred to as the "gas cliff." As a mitigating strategy for the future gas supply challenges, they were exploring options to import Liquefied Natural Gas (LNG) through some ports, particularly in Mozambique. Mozambique was ahead of South Africa in this regard, as they were currently building an LNG terminal in Matola. South Africa was looking into ways of importing LNG via the existing gas infrastructure, specifically through the Sasol gas pipeline, to continue supplying downstream consumers.
While Eskom was moving slowly, it was taking steps to diversify its energy mix. Eskom had plans to introduce a new gas-to-power plant in Richards Bay with a capacity of 3,000 megawatts. Eskom had plans at Komati to repurpose some of its existing coal fleet. The utility was not stuck in the past but was indeed working on incorporating a more diverse energy mix. However, he said that he was not speaking on behalf of Eskom.
He did not have information on the question about grid capacity and access but would ensure that Eskom provided a comprehensive written response to all the members.

ECSA’s response
Mr Mackay said regarding Eskom and what is perceived as uncompetitive behaviour, the first thing to note in response is the complexity and pace of change in the energy transition. These matters are difficult to deal with and are not uncommon. International comparisons and experience show similar challenges have occurred in many other jurisdictions. Eskom faces a very challenging position, particularly the utility debt spiral. As a centralised utility that has historically held all the generating assets, Eskom now needs to facilitate the move into a competitive environment where generating assets are decentralised and held by multiple private-sector parties. Typically, this shift is guided by clear rules and regulations, which he believed is where part of the challenge lies. A strong Eskom is critical to ensure a reliable energy system and electricity services through the transition. But Eskom needs to look very different in the future. This presents a challenge not only for Eskom's clients but also for its executives. If there are no appropriate regulations and rules to guide everyone, this can lead to difficulties. The CEO and the board chair of Eskom have emphasised the need to protect the
status quo
and push for clarity on rules and regulations. From ECSA’s engagements with both the executives and the board, he observed that Eskom is committed to the transition, although ECSA recognises the complexity involved at this early stage.
How [could the country] engage on these issues better? He clarified that NECOM, which has been established to deliver the energy action plan under the Presidency, is a subcommittee of the Cabinet. As businesses, ECSA members engage with and support the objectives of the energy action plan but do not drive those objectives. NECOM, as an extension of government, has been addressing issues such as grid access and trading, which had been raised. The focus should be on establishing a governance mechanism to engage Eskom through the shareholder compact, which has shifted from the traditional Department of Public Enterprises (DPE) to the new Ministry of Electricity and Energy. The structural change is very positive, but the shareholder compact has not yet been rewritten. This is an important issue for the transition rules and it is an opportunity to clarify those rules. NERSA, as the regulator, also plays a crucial role in clarifying rules and ensuring that changes requested can be implemented more swiftly. In the past, there had been a lack of response and a very slow reaction to requests for confirmation of changing rules, which added to the complexity of the situation. This has led to a standoff between increasing the number of rules and increasing competition, while also needing to protect revenue streams to ensure Eskom remains financially stable. Significant work is now being done in this area. There is strong alignment among all parties involved, with everyone committed to the transition. However, the critical challenge lies in getting through the short-term by creating clarity, rules, and structure around the delivery of the transition. Dealing with the grid is not only about rules and access rights but also about having clarity on the expansion, and the capacity needed to deliver that expansion. Further work needs to be done on this matter.
In the immediate term, a lot of work needs to be done. It is necessary to optimise the available capacity and balance this between the public and private sectors. Mechanisms could be included in requests for proposals (RFPs) from both sectors that focus on the objective of connecting power, rather than being limited by BQ and grid reservation rules. Recommendations and papers have been written through NECOM, and ECSA would be happy to engage in more detailed discussions offline.
The concept of curtailment of capacity would support the effort on the grid. This concept is technically well understood and proven, but it needs to be embedded in the rules. It could unlock an additional 3,400 megawatts of wind connection capacity in the Cape, which is a high-capacity area and a great resource, but where grid capacity has been constrained. There is a need for alignment between the Eskom Grid Access Unit, the National Transmission Company South Africa (NTCSA), and the Regulator to fast-track and optimise the alignment of curtailment capacity, as well as the RFP process. This process involves the IPP Office, which has been somewhat covered, as well as other entities, with the goal of unlocking short-term capacity. Such an approach would address the constrained growth that both SAWEA and SAPVIA have been discussing. This approach would ultimately be in the best interest of the country.
SAWEA’s response
Mr Govender said he would respond to three questions. The first was about employment and skills development. As an association, SAWEA is working on a wind energy skills roadmap to identify existing gaps and ensure they can fill those gaps in the short term, which is crucial for the sustainability of the industry. They have partnered with the Energy and Water SETA to ensure a programmatic approach to skills development, collaborating with training providers and TVET colleges to offer wind energy skills programs. Additionally, they are working with the Quality Council for Trades and Occupations (QCTO) to improve existing qualifications, ensuring they align with international best practices. New qualifications are being developed to help not only graduates but also matriculants and school leavers transition directly into trades, enabling them to participate in wind farm projects across the country. Each wind farm differs on the employment issue. As an industry association, SAWEA has developed community engagement guidelines specifically for wind farms. These guidelines are designed to ensure that engagement with local communities includes training opportunities, job creation, and employment options that extend beyond the construction phase. By following these guidelines, they aim to create more local job opportunities within the communities hosting wind farms. They are continuing to refine these guidelines to improve local employment prospects further.
90% of the balance of plant for South Africa’s wind projects is already produced locally. This limits opportunities for further expansion in plant manufacturing, but it offers a chance to enhance current manufacturing capabilities to local increase production for the industry. More than 10% of critical [windmill] components, such as concrete and some steel towers, are produced locally, although steel quality issues in South Africa present certain challenges. Based on research into local manufacturing potential, it has been determined that smaller transformers, gearboxes, aluminium cages, and tower internals could feasibly be produced locally, given the right conditions. For larger-scale manufacturing, he noted that nacelle assembly could potentially be done within South Africa if there is sufficient demand and scale in wind energy projects.
Significant progress is being made on offshore wind in South Africa. Feasibility studies conducted with the World Bank and CSIR have confirmed substantial offshore wind potential, with estimates indicating a capacity of over 90 kilowatts of wind per person per hour. The first offshore wind project is currently being developed near Richards Bay, and the industry is closely observing its progress as it moves toward implementation.
SAPVIA’s response
Dr Melamu said that two Members had asked questions on localisation. SAPVIA and SAWEA have been instrumental in developing the South African Renewable Energy Master Plan (SAREM). Both are actively participating in the planning process to shape localisation in South Africa’s renewable energy value chain. She was encouraged that the Deputy Minister of Electricity and Energy is leading the implementation of SAREM.
SAPVIA had commissioned two studies over the previous three years specifically to guide which areas of the value chain are most suitable for localisation. This was crucial because the industry had faced challenges in the past. She recalled the mid-2010s when numerous factories were set up in South Africa during a growth period. However, many eventually closed due to the unpredictable start-and-stop nature of the REIPPPP programme. This time, the industry wants a strategic approach to identify which parts of the value chain to prioritise and estimate the time required to achieve localisation in each area.
The first study, which SAPVIA would share with the Portfolio Committee, provided a comprehensive view of the sector, identifying key players and assessing manufacturing activity as of 2022. The second study, conducted with Solar Power Europe, evaluated the potential for exporting components manufactured locally, as Europe looks to diversify its solar supply chain away from reliance on China. Certain components, such as inverters, could be localised more easily than others, such as solar modules. Slide 20 of the SAPVIA presentation detailed the ease of localisation for various components.
Localisation should be approached with a long-term vision. Full localisation would require substantial investments in both demand and supply, and could take four to five years to achieve. In the meantime, as Mr Mileham pointed out, South Africa still needs to deploy a substantial amount of [renewable energy generation] capacity. This deployment must proceed even if local manufacturing capacity is not fully established. In the solar PV value chain, only a small portion of jobs (less than 15%) are generated through manufacturing components. The bulk of job creation comes from deploying and maintaining technology. Many industries are highly automated, which influences the number of jobs generated. South Africa must decide whether it should invest heavily in highly automated factories—requiring years to establish—or focus on areas of the value chain that could produce a greater number of jobs, such as manufacturing inverters and combiner boxes. This is a key policy decision for government.
The end-of-life management of solar PV modules and components is an essential consideration for the industry. She appreciated the question, noting that it is something SAPVIA has started to explore, especially with the guidance of a concept developed alongside Solar Power Europe. This concept aims to provide a roadmap for managing waste in the industry and includes developing a secondary market for solar modules. Many modules from utility projects remain in good condition and retain around 85% of their original efficiency, which makes them viable for reuse. SAREM includes provisions on recycling, end-of-life management, and the secondary market for solar PV modules and components.
SAPVIA’s engagement with the government over introducing a customs import duty [on PV modules] and temporary rebate has posed significant challenges for the industry. The sudden implementation of the duty led to considerable project delays, as the industry was not given adequate preparation time. Even after five months, there is no streamlined process for obtaining rebates, requiring members to apply individually for each consignment. A more supportive approach could have been taken to assist local manufacturing. SAPVIA would have liked to provide input to DTIC or other relevant parties on implementing such an instrument, but this opportunity was not afforded. Currently, SAPVIA is working with the International Trade Administration Commission of SA (ITAC) to help members understand how to obtain rebate permits for regular imports. However, SAPVIA’s board plans to engage with various structures to question whether the duty makes sense, given that local assembly currently supplies less than 10% of the total demand for modules in the country.
SAIPPA’s response
Mr Day first discussed grid access rules. NERSA is the regulatory authority responsible for setting these rules, based on input from the Grid Code Advisory Committee (GCAC), [a body of stakeholders whose function is to review proposed changes to the Grid Code and make recommendations to NERSA regarding the Grid Code.] Although the NTCSA has implemented interim rules, NERSA needs to finalise the grid access rules promptly. Around 18 months have passed since the interim rules were introduced, and the delay has created significant challenges, placing much of the burden on NERSA rather than on Eskom or NTCSA.
A question had been posed on energy generation closer to users, particularly through rooftop solar. He mentioned an image and link he shared, indicating a substantial potential for rooftop solar in Gauteng and beyond, estimating that Gauteng alone could support over 10 gigawatts. This figure exceeds the current REIPPPP-installed capacity by two to three times, illustrating a vast opportunity, especially in urban regions like KwaZulu-Natal, the Western Cape, and the Eastern Cape. However, financing these rooftop systems requires appropriate tariffs and [expansion] would largely depend on municipalities rather than just Eskom. Gauteng’s winter PV production exceeds that of the Northern Cape, which has higher summer output. Therefore, facilitating rooftop PV closer to demand could reduce grid losses and better align energy production with consumption needs.
Technology cost curves, particularly for battery energy storage, have seen significant cost reductions in recent years. While PV and wind cost curves have stabilised somewhat, battery storage costs continue to decline rapidly. Households and small businesses increasingly install battery backups, often combined with inverters, but typically only use them during load-shedding. These systems could serve a broader purpose by discharging stored energy for the grid’s benefit, not solely for backup purposes. Charging these systems from PV or even from grid power during off-peak times could maximise their utility. Artificial Intelligence (AI) and apps could play a role in optimising these energy storage systems for grid support.
The Chairperson thanked the entities for their presentations. She acknowledged the clarity they provided on the path forward, noting how they outlined what needs to happen, where the sector currently stands, and the goals for future action. She extended her gratitude and excused the entities from the meeting, as the meeting addressed internal Committee matters.
