Meeting Summary
Video
The Portfolio Committee commended the South African Police Service (SAPS) for its planned information and technology interventions in the fight against crime, but expressed concern at the absence of specific implementation timelines, costings, and detailed strategies to address the longstanding challenges posed by the State Information Technology Agency (SITA). The Committee continued to view the SITA as a significant obstacle to technological advancement within the SAPS.
Another area of concern was the lack of integration and suboptimal functioning of several key platforms, including the automated vehicle location system, the global emergency mobile communication, and command and control systems. These issues hindered the SAPS's ability to effectively fight crime.
While welcoming the SAPS's intention to collaborate with local government and entities such as the South African National Roads Agency to enhance system integration, the Committee stressed that the lack of tangible implementation deadlines limited its ability to ensure oversight and accountability. It emphasised that technological advancements, which served as a force multiplier, must be prioritised and expedited.
The Committee urged the SAPS to improve its communication regarding its mobile application, which facilitated anonymous crime reporting with video uploads and safety information. It was important to make this application known to the public to enhance information sharing between the SAPS and the community.
Regarding drones, the Committee acknowledged the commendable training of 60 police officers, but emphasised the need to expand these capabilities, especially in densely populated and inaccessible areas such as informal settlements.
Members welcomed the SAPS’s request for information from service providers on body cameras for officers. The Committee had long advocated body cameras to ensure accountability and improve officer safety, recognising their potential to reduce civil claims against the SAPS. Regular updates on the rollout of these technological advances were requested.
Progress in implementing an integrated and online firearms control management system was also welcomed. The Committee considered this system essential for efficient adjudication of applications and progress tracking, and urged the SAPS to adhere to the March 2026 migration timeline.
The Committee resolved to schedule a meeting to receive updates on findings from an oversight visit to the SAPS's Philippi Training Academy, expressing ongoing concerns about corruption allegations at the facility.

Chairperson's opening remarks

He prefaced the presentation with remarks on the crucial role of technology in modern law enforcement, particularly within South Africa’s resource-constrained environment. He said that effective integration of technology, such as data analytics, Global Positioning System (GPS) tracking, and mobile tools, could significantly enhance the SAPS’s ability to prevent, detect and respond to crime in real time. He mentioned the recent incident on the N2 involving a cash-in-transit heist, where the lack of an efficient communication system had illustrated serious capacity issues in some areas.
He acknowledged the challenges the SAPS faces with developing digital policing systems, which are compounded by reliance on other departments like the State Information Technology Agency (SITA) and the Department of Public Works and Infrastructure (DPWI) for technological infrastructure and maintenance. Despite these challenges, the Chairperson stressed that technology had significant potential as a "force multiplier" in the SAPS’s fight against crime. By leveraging surveillance systems, data analytics, and real-time communication, the SAPS could improve response times, crime prevention, and community trust. However, he emphasised that for technology to fully benefit the police force, the SAPS must also manage its existing resources effectively to avoid unnecessary complaints regarding resource shortages.
He mentioned several recent incidents to underscore the complex crime landscape in which the SAPS operates. He referred to incidents on the N12, where seven people had been killed in separate attacks, as well as a series of brutal assaults in Cape Town on 11 November. The Nelson Mandela Bay Flying Squad’s successful arrest of a suspect involved in illegal abalone trafficking, following a high-speed chase, was also commended as a proactive example of the SAPS combating organised crime.

SAPS on technology integration for enhanced crime fighting and firearm control management
Lt Gen Edith Mavundla, Head: SAPS Technology Management Service, introduced the SAPS' strategic use of technology to combat crime and the progress of the Firearm Control Management System (FCMS). Her presentation focused on technological advances in crime prevention, public engagement, and digital crime detection.
Community self-service Access to SAPS technologies
Lt Gen Mavundla introduced the SAPS mobile application, an essential tool for the public to report crimes, submit tips, and provide feedback. Mirroring features of the SAPS website, the app facilitated community engagement, crime reporting, and safety awareness. Its "Rate Our Service" feature allowed users to evaluate the SAPS station services, which supported service improvement and addressed public complaints to enhance overall service quality.
Enhanced community policing through technology
In the community policing domain, Lt Gen Mavundla discussed the global emergency mobile communication command and control system. This system logged calls and incidents in real time, optimising dispatch and incident management. The automated vehicle location system further enhanced response times by identifying the closest police vehicle to a scene, reducing vehicle misuse. Additionally, remotely piloted aircraft systems (RPAS), or drones, were employed in large events and search-and-rescue operations, providing coverage for high-risk or inaccessible areas and increasing officer safety. The automated number plate recognition system enabled the SAPS to track vehicles associated with criminal activity, a vital tool for real-time detection that operates in collaboration with partners through initiatives like the E2 project.
Digital crime detection and investigation capabilities
The SAPS has made considerable strides in digital crime investigation through the case administration and docket management system (CAS/ICDMS), which supports case management and crime data collection. The national photo image system stores visual records of suspects, aiding in investigations, while the person verification system cross-references fingerprints with the Department of Home Affairs’ (DHA's) database, enabling identity confirmation and linking suspects to prior convictions or wanted status. The integrated person management systems, a key part of the Integrated Justice System, ensure secure identification and verification of individuals from booking to release across the criminal justice process.
Circulation and forensic systems
Lt Gen Mavundla presented the circulation system, a tool for tracking stolen property, missing persons, and wanted individuals. This system enables real-time checks that support crime detection and property recovery using identifiers such as serial numbers and licence plates. Further forensic advancements include the automated fingerprint identification system (AFIS) and the automated ballistic information system (ABIS), which strengthen investigations by linking suspects to evidence. The geographical information system (GIS) provides visual data on criminal activity locations, assisting the SAPS in crime trend analysis and resource deployment.
Cybercrime tools and CCTV systems
Cybercrime tools enable the SAPS to gather data from digital devices connected to cybercrimes, reinforcing investigation efforts. Lt Gen Mavundla highlighted the importance of SAPS’s partnerships with CCTV operators for situational awareness in public areas, enhancing crime monitoring and response capabilities. Advanced AI-driven tools allow SAPS to perform behavioural analysis on CCTV footage, bolstering crime prevention strategies.
Information and communication technology (ICT) infrastructure
Lt Gen Mavundla underscored the need for robust ICT infrastructure to support SAPS’s systems, especially as they transition from analogue to digital communication systems. Essential services like network support for inter-station communication, file sharing, and administrative tasks require continuous upgrades to sustain operational reliability and efficiency.
Development of the firearm control management system (FCMS)
Lt Gen Mavundla detailed the phased rollout of the FCMS to digitise the SAPS’s central firearm registry in accordance with the Firearm Act. The first release, due by 30 November, focuses on dealer and manufacturer requirements, allowing for digital submissions and electronic applications. Subsequent phases would address firearm storage, transportation, and amnesty processes, with the final release set for March 2026. The new system would ultimately replace the enhanced firearm registry system, aiming to simplify firearm management processes with ten system integrations and improved public access.
Conclusion
Lt Gen Mavundla highlighted the SAPS’s commitment to strengthening its technological infrastructure for enhanced public safety, crime prevention, and data-driven policing. She emphasised that reliable infrastructure and advanced technology were crucial to combating various forms of crime, including gang violence, cybercrime, trafficking, and financial crimes.

SAPS's response
Lt Gen Mavundla explained that Ms Kohler had referred to the firm registry status, which seemed to relate to the portion of the Firearm Registry Environment that would later be addressed by visible policing. She acknowledged that the cameras in areas like the eThekwini District had been non-operational for some time. She confirmed that the SAPS collaborated with municipalities through the signed corporate cooperation agreements. They were engaging with the Metro Police and other businesses that had cameras covering these areas, which Mr Gana also mentioned in relation to the planned collaboration.
In their engagement, they examined cameras located in municipalities and businesses and considered the use of streaming these cameras to the police. She explained that the purposes for monitoring these cameras could differ. For example, in the eThekwini area, the cameras were being used for disaster recovery, not for criminality, but the goal was to stream this information to the police and emergency services. This would allow for timely responses to crime scenes. These engagements were ongoing, with collaborations in the process of being implemented.
Regarding body-worn cameras, she said that they were currently testing the market. They had issued a request for information from suppliers who could provide body-worn cameras for testing. This would allow them to evaluate how these cameras would fit into the organisation's business processes and enable better policing. The specifications had been compiled, and the supply chain was ready to advertise for this solution. She highlighted that audio and body-worn cameras were part of the technologies they were exploring.
Lt Gen Mavundla further explained that technology infrastructure was key to supporting these initiatives. The infrastructure must be upgraded to handle the new technologies, including body-worn cameras. She said that the police stations' telephone systems were previously reliant on outdated copper lines, but they were now transitioning to more modern telecommunications technologies, including Vodacom’s network, as part of a broader initiative led by National Treasury. They also issued cell phones where infrastructure had been compromised due to theft.
She touched on face recognition technology, noting that such technologies were still relatively new but could help the police monitor crime through streaming cameras and artificial intelligence. The aim was to enable the police to search through databases of businesses and municipalities for individuals of interest. She added that private security companies were also advancing in this area, and corporate agreements would help to integrate these technologies.
Lt Gen Mavundla acknowledged that there are gaps in coverage, particularly in rural areas, where newer technologies could not yet be implemented due to infrastructure limitations. In these regions, radio communication technology was still used to cover areas newer technologies did not reach. While some areas were not yet fully covered by modern infrastructure, the police were working to fill these gaps through analysis and cooperation with other entities.
She then discussed the implementation of technologies, emphasising that some technologies were functioning well, while others were hindered by outdated infrastructure, particularly in rural areas. They were upgrading their infrastructure to accommodate new technologies and enhance their effectiveness. This would require significant budget allocation to expand and maintain the infrastructure necessary for future technology rollouts.
Regarding the circulation system for missing persons, Lt Gen Mavundla explained that when someone was reported missing, their details were captured in the system and shared through the app, which allowed the public to identify missing persons. The app displayed faces and details of those of interest, and if a person was identified, it could be reported directly through the app.
She closed by addressing concerns about corruption in the administration of these technologies. She described how security features in the systems limited who could access, capture, or delete information, ensuring that only authorised personnel could carry out various actions. She added that a service complaint system was in place for citizens to report poor service or corruption, allowing them to upload videos, images or audio to support their complaints.
Gen Masemola said that before he left the meeting, he would like to ask Lt Gen Mamotheti to speak further about the number of drone pilots they currently have, as well as on matters related to the central firearms register. From his side, he would like to mention that they had begun rolling out CCTV in the KwaMashu and Inanda areas. The project had already started there, with the goal of integrating these systems with municipal and infrastructure systems, including the Road Traffic Management Corporation (RTMC) systems. He emphasised that they aimed to link all these cameras and eventually implement facial recognition, which would help them to track criminals more efficiently. He said many of the cameras on major national roads or farm roads had already been very effective, with arrests having been made as a result. If they could integrate these systems, including into shopping malls, it would be much easier for them to trace criminals when needed.
Lt Gen Maropeng Mamotheti, Divisional Commissioner: Visible Policing and Operations, SAPS, addressed the issue of the central firearms register, explaining that there had been drastic improvements since the Committee's previous visit, which was two to three years ago. The performance for the current financial year was above the set target, and they had achieved a clean audit for the CFR. The building housing the CFR had undergone some improvements by its owner, and they had also secured an alternative space for filing papers. Although they were still using paper in some instances, they expected to implement a new system in the next financial year that would solve many of these issues. Despite still being in the same building, they had been managing the process well. She attributed the improvements to the implementation plan they had developed, which had led to the positive results they were seeing now.
Moving on to the matter of training drone pilots, she confirmed that they had trained 60 members so far, and she was waiting for a breakdown of the provinces involved. She hoped to provide this breakdown before the end of the meeting, but offered to submit it later if necessary. She also mentioned that special allowances for drone pilots had been introduced to create a conducive working environment and retain skills, though she acknowledged that they were competing with the private sector and international employers. She stressed that while financial incentives were important, they were not the sole motivator, as many members left for better pay elsewhere despite the special allowances.
Turning to the issue of firearms, Lt Gen Mamotheti explained that the Firearms Control Act governs the process, and firearms coming into the country had to go through the proper channels. Firearm manufacturers and dealers must apply for an import permit that specifies where the firearms were coming from, and what types of firearms they were. Once the firearms arrived in the country, they were entered into their system, where the name of the exporter and details of the transaction were recorded. She assured the Committee that they were able to track firearms through the entire process, from export to sale to licensing under an individual or private security company. She confirmed that their system was working well and had been praised internationally. She mentioned that the Firearms Control Amendment Bill would help address any remaining issues, but added that the system would not replace the need for police officers to conduct interviews, inspections, and competency checks before firearms were licensed.
She also addressed concerns about non-compliant dealers, stating that their inspection programmme was thorough. If dealers were found to be involved in criminal activity or failing to comply with regulations, their licences were revoked and their businesses shut down.
She concluded by stating that the only outstanding issue was the breakdown of the 60 drone pilots, which she hoped to provide before the end of the meeting or submit later.

SAPS's response
Lt Gen Mavundla said that the SAPS prioritised system integration efforts according to the operational value chain, focusing especially on processes essential to service delivery. For example, external integrations, such as the link between the Integrated Criminal Case Docket Management System (ICDMS) and the National Prosecuting Authority (NPA), facilitate the exchange of case-ready information. This allowed prosecutors to access relevant case data, which aided in the efficient analysis and preparation of cases for court. Integrations were similarly extended to other entities within the justice system, such as Legal Aid, based on the prioritisation of operational needs.
Internally, while some SAPS systems remained in the process of full integration, Lt Gen Mavundla said that the SAPS was addressing this incrementally, aligning system integrations with priority business processes. Many of these processes necessitated updates to comply with regulatory or legal requirements. She highlighted the integration of the forensic laboratory system with the case management system, which enabled investigators to incorporate forensic evidence, such as 212 statements (also known as affidavits), into case dockets. These integrations were part of a continuous effort to identify gaps and enhance systems to better meet the needs of the organisation.
Discussing infrastructure maintenance, she underscored the importance of maintaining and upgrading critical systems, including the national radio communication network and IT infrastructure. The SAPS worked closely with the SITA to ensure a robust network and hosting infrastructure. She explained that older technologies, often referred to as legacy systems, required ongoing maintenance to stay operational until they could be replaced. This was especially true for rural police stations, where limited connectivity hampered the deployment of newer systems. Such challenges called for phased upgrades to ensure more widespread access to essential digital tools, such as case management systems.
Lt Gen Mavundla acknowledged that as technology continued to evolve, systems previously upgraded in recent years may soon require further updates. These needs were addressed through strategic planning, as outlined in the SAPS annual performance plan (APP) and the annual operational plan (AOP). However, due to resource limitations, SAPS often relied on maintaining existing technologies for extended periods, conducting repairs and updates rather than opting for full replacement.
She also highlighted the tangible benefits of these technological advancements, citing the circulation system used to track stolen vehicles, which had contributed to numerous vehicle recoveries and arrests. The technologies applied in case reporting, forensic investigations, and case management have collectively improved the SAPS’s capacity to resolve cases and enhance service delivery. However, infrastructure limitations, particularly in rural regions, remained a challenge, with ongoing upgrades addressing these gaps.
On the MySAPS application, Lt Gen Mavundla elaborated on its role in empowering communities to report crimes and submit evidence, including videos of incidents or police misconduct. Submissions were assessed for
prima facie
evidence before further action was taken. She confirmed that the app was actively promoted through public events and by provincial offices to raise awareness and encourage usage.
She also discussed the challenges associated with system rollouts, citing delays caused by the SITA procurement process and the shortage of skilled personnel. These factors slowed the swift deployment of new systems, requiring the SAPS to rely on existing infrastructure for longer than desired. Despite this, the SAPS continued to work with its partners to maintain current systems and prioritise upgrades in areas with the greatest need.
Lastly, she noted that the SAPS communication division was responsible for managing public awareness campaigns related to technology initiatives. This included promoting tools such as the My SAPS app to foster public collaboration in crime prevention efforts.
Lt General Lineo Nkhuoa, Acting Deputy National Commissioner: Support Services, SAPS, addressed the Committee regarding a query about the number of SAPS employees deemed incompetent to carry firearms. She said the requested information was not readily available, as it required retrieval from the system. She asked for the Committee's permission to submit the data in writing at a later stage, to ensure that the figures provided would be accurate.
Ms Kohler requested that the information on firearm competency certificates be provided, focusing on high-crime areas. She wanted to examine any correlation between areas with high crime rates and a lack of competency certificates.
The Chairperson acknowledged Ms Kohler’s request, and proposed setting up a process to gather the required details. He invited input from other Committee Members, stating that he would forward any additional points on the Committee’s behalf, and would seek feedback in the next session.
He then concluded the day’s session, agreeing with Members’ recommendations to include a future session for further discussions on the topic.
Ms James raised an outstanding question, inquiring if there was at least one police station in the country where all the technological infrastructure was fully operational. She clarified that she was not looking for a general response, but wanted the specific name of one station where systems were functioning as presented.
Lt Gen Mavundla confirmed that certain stations had the necessary infrastructure to support operational technologies.
However, Ms James reiterated her request for the name of a specific station with all systems in full operation.
Lt Gen Mavundla mentioned Johannesburg Central as an example.
Closing remarks
The Chairperson noted one additional matter concerning the extortion hotline, which had been raised in Parliament. He shared his experience of testing the hotline and encountering technical issues, suggesting getting technical feedback on the hotline’s functionality might be beneficial. He requested that this matter be added to the agenda for a brief update in the next session.
He commended Lt Gen Mavundla for a thorough presentation, and reiterated the importance of foundational improvements at the station level to achieve technological advancements. He said that issues with communication systems persisted at stations like Cape Town Central and others nationwide.
He confirmed that the agenda for the following week would be shared shortly, and reminded Members that the next session would be held in person at Parliament.
The meeting was adjourned.
