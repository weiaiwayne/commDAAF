2024 Medium Term Budget Policy Statement (MTBPS)
The two Appropriations and Finance Committees convened to receive a briefing from the National Treasury on the Medium-Term Budget Policy Statement (MTBPS) presented to Parliament the previous day, which emphasised, once again, the need for continued containment of government debt and expenditure in tandem while at the same time proceeding with the government’s structural reforms to unlock the bottlenecks currently restraining the economic growth in the country.
In his opening remarks, the Minister of Finance said the National Treasury has identified four pillars for economic growth: maintaining macroeconomic stability, implementing structural reforms, building state capability, and supporting growth-enhancing public infrastructure investment. He went further to say that the National Treasury will only be able to allocate more funds to different sectors of the economy once it has managed to constrain the country's debt levels, as debt service costs are currently the biggest expenditure item.
The Committees heard the National Treasury forecasts the government’s budget deficit narrowing from 5% of gross domestic product in 2024/2025 to 3.2% of gross domestic product in 2027/2028, while debt will stabilise at 75.5% of gross domestic product in 2025/2026. Consolidated government spending is expected to increase from R2.4 trillion in 2024/2025 to R2.77 trillion in 2027/2028, growing by an annual average rate of 4.9%. Over the same period, revenues are projected to underperform compared to the 2024 Budget projections.
Members attributed the underfunding of the South African Revenue Services as the main reason for the under-collection of revenue, and they called for the National Treasury to address this funding gap. The South African Revenue Services repeated the Members’ call and asked the National Treasury to review the institution’s funding model.
Members also questioned the accuracy of the National Treasury’s forecasts on the budget deficit, as they seemed to change each year.
While Members were pleased with Operation Vulindlela's progress in addressing some of the economy's structural defects, they wondered when citizens would begin to experience the impact of the work done so far.
There was uncertainty around whether the 4.7% public sector wage hike offered by the government was final because the Medium-Term Budget Policy Statement forecasted a 4.5% increase. Members asked the National Treasury to remain firm in its offer and not cave into the unions' demand for a 6% wage increase, which may adversely impact the government’s ability to deliver services.
Due to time constraints, the department could not answer all of the questions. As such, it was agreed that the deliberations will continue next Tuesday.
Meeting report
Co-Chairperson Legwase welcomed everyone in attendance. Afterwards, she asked if any apologies had been received.
Apologies were received from Ms Ntokozo Hlonyane MP and Ms Wendy Alexander MP.
Co-Chairperson Legwase noted the apologies and invited the Minister to make his opening remarks.
Opening remarks by the Minister
Mr Enoch Gondongwana (Minister of Finance) indicated that 60% of non-interest expenditure was spent on the social wage. To sustain government expenditure on redistributive policies like the social wage will require the participation of all relevant stakeholders in improving the country’s economic growth environment. As part of its contribution to this agenda, the department has drawn up four pillars. The first one is to create macroeconomic stability that is conducive to growth. The second is strengthening state capacity so that it is better able to provide the necessary services to stimulate the economy. The third pillar relates to implementing structural reforms, which will remove the economic bottlenecks. The last pillar seeks to change the composition of expenditure from one that is consumption-based to one based on capital investment.
If the growth challenges are addressed, unemployment and poverty will begin to decline. The National Treasury (NT) will first have to constrain the country's debt levels, as debt service costs are currently the biggest expenditure item, in order to allocate more funds to different sectors of the economy.
After making those brief remarks, he invited the Director-General (DG) to take the Committee through the presentation.
MTBPS Presentation
Mr Duncan Pieterse (DG of the National Treasury) took the Committee through the presentation.
Overview
The economy is forecast to grow by an annual average of 1.8% over the next three
years, underscoring the need for continued implementation of structural reforms.
The fiscal strategy remains on course.
The consolidated budget deficit will narrow from 5% of gross domestic product (GDP) in 2024/25 to 3.2% of GDP in 2027/28, and debt will stabilise at 75.5% of GDP in 2025/26. Economic reforms are beginning to bear fruit:
Electricity availability has improved;
The logistics system is stabilising; and
The cost of doing business is declining in some areas of the economy.
Global economic outlook
Global growth is expected to remain steady at 3.2% in 2024 and 2025 and is expected to converge to 3.1% after 2025
Persistent geopolitical challenges and the impact of elevated sovereign debt represent significant risks to the global outlook
Inflation has continued to ease, paving the way for the policy rate-cutting cycle
However, services inflation and lagged wage adjustments continue to interrupt the normalisation of policy rates
Domestic economic outlook
Real GDP growth projected to average 1.1% in 2024, up from 0.7% in 2023
A weaker first half has led to a downward revision of the 2024 GDP forecast, with notable compositional shifts
Contractions in foreign trade and investment are now projected, while household and government consumption is holding up.
SA’s economy faced challenges in the first half of 2024, including power cuts, freight rail and port failures, and high living costs. However, reduced power cuts, improved sentiment, and quicker inflation relief will boost activity going forward.
Medium‐to‐longer‐term growth is dependent on resolving the supply constraints and reducing barriers to economic activity
The NT believes that by alleviating load shedding, improving the performance of the logistics system, reducing the cost of data, improving water supply, and enabling the country to attract the skills it needs, the reforms already underway through Operation Vulindlela Phase I will provide a significant boost to the economy in the medium term. The government will continue assisting state-owned enterprises (SOEs) by strengthening their balance sheets, enabling higher levels of private investment in infrastructure, and improving their operational performance. To achieve higher and more inclusive long-term growth, Phase II of Operation Vulindlela will target additional reforms that will address both long-standing and emerging constraints, such as the deteriorating performance of local government and spatial inequality.
The NT’s medium-term fiscal strategy focuses on achieving fiscal sustainability, supporting economic growth and critical social services, and addressing significant fiscal and economic risks. The strategy prioritises, among others, stabilising government debt by maintaining sufficiently large primary surpluses over the rest of the decade, ensuring higher levels of capital investment by stabilising and reducing borrowing costs as well as directing a growing share of public spending towards capital projects and initiating wide-ranging infrastructure reforms; protecting critical services in the context of limited budget resources; controlling the growth in the public service wage bill; and limiting further financial support to SOEs, while completing the resolution of the debt obligations of Eskom and the South African National Roads Agency Limited (SANRAL).
Revenue collections over the first half of 2024/25 were 5.2% higher than the same period last year.  Falling import value-added tax (VAT) and fuel levy collections contribute to a weaker near-term revenue outlook. Compared to the 2024 Budget, the gross tax revenue estimate for 2024/25 is projected to be R22.3 billion lower.
Despite underperformance in near-term revenue collections, the tax-to-GDP ratio remains resilient, and tax collections are expected to remain buoyant, averaging above one over the medium term. Slower renewable energy-related imports lowered import VAT collections, while weaker in-year fuel levy collection will limit growth over the medium term. An improved profitability outlook raises expectations for medium-term corporate tax collections.
The NT has proposed spending adjustments for rollovers, the South African National Defence Force (SANDF) troop deployment in the Democratic Republic of the Congo, unforeseeable and unavoidable expenditure, the repayment of SANRAL debt relating to the Gauteng Freeway Improvement Project (GFIP), and spending announced in the 2024 Budget, which includes an increase in the COVID-19 social relief of distress (SRD) grant from R350 to R370 per month.
Consolidated government spending is expected to increase from R2.4 trillion in 2024/25 to R2.77 trillion in 2027/28, growing by an annual average rate of 4.9 per cent. Over the same period, revenues are projected to underperform compared to the 2024 Budget projections. In this context, major spending pressures must be funded mainly from current baselines, reprioritisations or shifts between programmes. Despite slower-than-expected revenue growth, the government is on track to achieve primary surpluses in 2024/25 and over the medium term. Debt is expected to stabilise at 75.5% of GDP in 2025/26. This will enable the government to arrest the trend of mounting debt-service costs, which will peak as a proportion of revenue at 21.7% in 2025/26 and decline thereafter.
The NT identified various risks to the fiscal outlook. Some of these are lower revenue growth due to an unexpected global or domestic slowdown, a higher-than-anticipated public-service wage settlement, higher borrowing costs driven by a prolonged elevation of the risk premium and a slower-than-expected reduction in global interest rates, as well as persistent deficits and the accumulation of liabilities in other areas of the public sector, such as SOEs, potentially leading to increased demands for budgetary support.

Following the presentation, Co-Chairperson Ndhlovu opened the floor for discussion. Each Member was allocated two minutes to comment or pose questions to the department.
Discussion
Adv S Swart (ACDP) was disappointed to hear the figures announced by the Minister of Finance (MoF) relating to the budget deficit. Nevertheless, he acknowledged that the government would take some time to address the country’s economic challenges.
Over the years, the African Christian Democratic Party (ACDP) has argued that there is room to collect more funds and taxes due to the state. He asked the South African Revenue Services (SARS) to unpack the difference between the debt on its books and what is actually collected.
The ACDP has also argued that if the NT were to provide the Asset Forfeiture Unit (AFU) and Special Investigating Unit (SIU) with more funds, both would be able to seize tens of billions of Rands sitting in foreign bank accounts. He asked if the NT is looking to increase the budget allocation to these two agencies.
To him, the NT regularly changes its forecasts for the deficit each year. While he welcomed the primary surplus, he was concerned about the accuracy of the forecasts.
Mr B Radebe (ANC, Free State) was also disappointed by some of the figures announced in the MTBPS. Nonetheless, he encouraged the NT to continue reducing the budget deficit so that it does not have to seek out loans from international agencies.
The consolidated budget framework worried him, as the NT has repeatedly said the debt will stabilise in the third year's outlay, yet this is always revised. He, too, questioned the accuracy of the department’s forecasting formula.
Even though he was pleased by the impact the department’s decision to provide Eskom with debt relief in the prior year has had on the entity’s ability to keep the lights on, he was worried by the lack of details in the MTBPS about what will be done to assist municipalities in honouring their debts to water boards, which was needed to ensure water was provided to local communities. He stressed that the country would not have had economic development without water.
Dr M Burke (DA) commended the NT for making realistic expectations for GDP growth for the first time in a while. Previously, it overestimated GDP growth and thus incurred more than the country could afford. He also commended the MoF for working hand-in-hand with the other Government of National Unity (GNU) partners when drafting the MTBPS.
An article published in BusinessDay two days ago highlighted uncertainty around whether the 4.7% public sector wage hike offered by the government was final because the MTBPS has forecasted a 4.5% increase. He asked if the expenditure on public sector employees was realistic; if not, what would be done to ensure a more practical number.
Mr A Beesley (ActionSA) said the ultimate barometer of any budget is economic growth, and the 1.8% projected by the department in four years had missed the mark and would not assist the unemployed or those going to bed hungry at night. In his opinion, bolder initiatives could have been announced in the MTBPS to stimulate economic growth in the country.
The underfunding of SARS—which collects 90% of the country’s revenue—considering the estimated tax gap of R800 billion was concerning. He implored the NT to address the funding gap as it was an easy way for the government to increase its revenue.
Mr K Wakelin (DA) applauded the Minister for his bold announcements in the MTBPS.
The unions have rejected the government’s offer to increase wages and asked for a 6% wage increase. In his view, the risk lies not in the cost application but in the destabilising impact such an agreement will have on service delivery. As such, he implored the department to find a way to resolve the matter.
Given the trend in municipalities and some government departments using the budget allocations for employee benefits for operational costs, he wondered how the department would prevent the funds set aside for the conditional grants that will form part of the provincial equitable share from being abused.
Ms O Maotwe (EFF) indicated that in the past decade, the economy had not grown more than 2% except for the 4.7% growth it achieved in 2021, following a decline of 6.7% in the prior year due to the Covid-19 hard lockdowns. Initially, in the 2023 Budget Speech, the NT projected that the economy would grow by 1.9%, which was later revised in the MTBPS to 1.4%. Yet the actual growth was 0.6%, which the NT, despite the large revision, celebrated. Considering the NT’s revision, she did not believe the country would achieve 1.8% growth over the four years.
In her opinion, Operation Vulindlela’s objectives contradict the government’s intention to rebuild state capacity, as the former intends to privatise SOEs. She wondered why the department wanted to accelerate visa applications as if there were no skills among the country's 11 million unemployed people.
Ms A Siwisa (EFF, Northern Cape) mentioned that local municipalities are unable to collect revenue because a large proportion of ratepayers are unemployed in the country, which is leading to their collapse. Given that the average household basket sits at R5 300, the government should implement a bolder approach to job creation. The NT’s plan to accelerate visa applications to bring foreign skills into the country would not assist the 11 million unemployed South Africans.
The MTBPS announced that due to the country’s 75% debt-to-GDP ratio, the government will need to rely on the private sector to augment the state's deficiencies. She believed the government would eventually outsource all its responsibilities to the private sector. In her view, the conversation should not be about outsourcing the government’s functions to the private sector to rescue the country. It should be around job creation because, at this rate, the government will eventually outsource all of its functions to the private sector.
She pointed out that R600 million was allocated to the Northern Cape (NC) provincial government as a conditional grant to assist in clearing the housing backlog. Yet, the money has not been utilised, and the site has not been surveyed.
Mr D Van Rooyen (MK) expressed concern about the two minutes allocated to Members to comment or pose questions.
Co-Chairperson Legwase noted the concern and said if there is enough time at the end, Members will be allowed to ask follow-up questions.
Mr Van Rooyen felt that the MTBPS should have touched on the medium-term development plan (MTDP) because this would have given the public a better understanding of the government’s priorities for the Seventh Administration. The omission of this plan was a problem, considering that there are only six years left until the final report on the government’s performance in implementing the National Development Plan is to be submitted. Without the MTDP, Parliament could not understand what informed the departments in the MTBPS.
In his view, the department was adamant and uncompromising in its call for the government to investigate the sustainability of renewable energy projects. These projects may pose a risk to the country's fiscal stability as they take up much of the country’s contingent liability provision. Globally, the country urging South Africa to look into renewable projects is moving away from them themselves as they harm fiscal sustainability.
Mr W Douglas (MK) outlined that the MK rejected the MTBPS with contempt as anti-poor and unsustainable. The MTBPS showed that the DA-Led ANC coalition government had lost touch with the struggles of the poor. The NT’s policy of fiscal consolidation has failed to deliver equitable growth. Austerity and fiscal consolidation cannot effectively stabilise national debt without exacerbating inequality, economic stagnation, and poverty. The MK supports a balanced approach, calling for a shift from austerity to measures prioritising people’s needs. The party wants to see the nationalisation of the South African Reserve Bank (SARB) and key assets to create jobs and increase the country’s tax base.
The drop in the budget revenue estimate has been lowered by R31.2 billion, signalling significant revenue underperformance that could impact government services, social support programmes, and the overall economic trajectory. Considering the shortfall, he asked the department what specific strategies and measures are being implemented by the NT and SARS to mitigate the revenue gap, whether there are plans to enhance revenue collection methods, target untapped or under-allocated revenue sources, or implement reforms aimed at improving compliance with efficiencies in SARS; and what contingency plans are in place to ensure critical government services and investments in economic growth are not compromised due to the revenue reduction.
He expressed deep concern about the socio-economic challenges faced by South Africans. He asked if the department could provide insight into plans or support mechanisms it will put in place to support those receiving the SRD grant, which is set to end in 2025.
Mr C Dugmore (ANC) rose on a point of order.
Co-Chairperson Ndhlovu asked what point he was rising on.
Mr Dugmore thought the comment about the unity government being a DA-led coalition was unnecessary and distracted from the matters at hand.
Co-Chairperson Ndhlovu noted his point of order and asked Members to desist from debating who said what.
Mr J Britz (DA, Eastern Cape) said the MTBPS highlighted that the consolidated budget deficit will narrow from 5% in 2024/2025 to 3.2% of GDP in 2027/2028. He asked what measures have been put in place to achieve this and how the provincial and local government spheres will contribute to achieving these goals.
After that, he asked if the provincial and local governments were expected to contribute to implementing the four pillars of economic growth.
Mr D Ryder (DA, Gauteng) applauded the department's commitment to investigating further debt relief for Eskom. The presentation highlighted some concerning issues, such as the R2 billion of unspent funds set to be returned by the local government and the fact that the strategic fuel reserves will be depleted by sale.
After that, he asked the department questions about what progress has been made in prioritising the justice and prosecutorial capacity needed to sort out the remaining items flagged by the Financial Action Task Force (FATF) if there had been a policy shift to align the money supply closer to growth to ensure inflation remains under control; further details on the R5 billion allocation to SANRAL in the appropriations bill; and for further details on the R6 billion which will be used for debt.
Ms S Nxumalo (ANC, Mpumalanga) said the government’s performance in the Seventh Administration will be linked to the performance of local municipalities. After that, she asked the department two questions. One, she asked if there were plans to introduce measures to implement a one-third write-off to water boards or subsidies to provide municipalities relief from the debt they owe water boards. Two, she asked what specific areas Operation Vulindlela has targeted to assist local municipalities.
Mr M Lekganyane (ANC) asked what has happened since the department first announced its intention in Parliament to draw monies from the Gold and Foreign Exchange Contingency Reserve Account (GFECRA). He also asked how far the structural reforms implemented in Phase I of Operation Vulindlela have enhanced the government’s mission to create an inclusive economy.
Ms L Makhubela (ANC) applauded the NT for announcing that it will take steps to intervene in the issue of municipal debt to water boards.
She asked what measures the department will implement to improve data connectivity across the country.
Mr S Sekoati (ANC) asked what the department would do to ensure that public-private partnerships succeed in meeting their targets speedily and what lessons it had learned from the failed sale of South African Airways to Takatso Consortium.
Ms N Gcaleka-Mazibuko (ANC) mentioned that services have deteriorated in different areas of the country. She asked how long it would take for citizens to experience the impact of Operation Vulindlela's reforms. She also asked what sustainable disaster risk funding model the department was developing and whether it was working with other African countries to develop such a model.
Ms H Neale-May (ANC) indicated that the MTBPS was a positive step forward, given the current economic situation. The Pension Funds Act regulations were critical in shaping the asset allocation patterns of pension funds. She asked what impact having the majority of assets for foreign investment relevant to domestic investment—which is 25%—has had. She also asked what fiscal policy interventions would be implemented to increase the capital allocation by the private and public sectors in supporting the country’s industrial policy.
Ms M Bartlett (ANC) asked what plan the department had in place to follow through with the President’s commitment in his Opening of Parliament Address that the government will ensure that the institutional structure and funding model for local government is fit for purpose and municipalities are financially sustainable, noting the marginal increase of the equitable share in 2025.
Mr A Bateman (DA) was encouraged by the MTBPS and the collaboration that went into developing it. He asked what decision-making framework is used to distinguish growth-enhancing public infrastructure spending from growth retarding public infrastructure spending. The danger with state-led infrastructure spending is that if top-down decisions are not made carefully and wisely, they can negatively affect the economy's overall health.
Mr S Majola (MK, KwaZulu-Natal) felt that the MTBPS did not touch on the impact of fuel prices on the rise in prices. (His initial questions were not covered as no sound came from his device).
Co-Chairperson Maimane said the Eskom debt was the elephant in the room. Even though the department's debt relief had assisted Eskom in resolving load shedding, it unintentionally created a problem for SARS in that the under-collection of VAT, as per the presentation, has decreased the import of energy-related goods that consumers required at the height of load shedding.
His main concerns were that Eskom had not complied with the conditions in the previous debt relief package and that municipalities owed Eskom ballooning debt. As such, he encouraged the government to seriously consider how best to manage Eskom’s debt, as it may pose a significant fiscal risk over the medium term.
Expenditure is estimated to increase by R65 billion and revenue by R19 billion, meaning the budget deficit will widen. He asked how the department planned to address the gap and why the country deployed troops to the Democratic Republic of Congo.
Mr Edward Kieswetter (SARS Commissioner) explained that various studies have been done on modelling the tax gap. If that is adjusted for time/value for money, the country will have a theoretical tax gap between R450 and 500 billion. An additional R334 billion has been added to the gap because the SARS balance sheet—which is contingent liabilities that belong to the state and are collectable—has 54 million outstanding returns, each valued at R1 500.
SARS was certain of the accuracy of its estimation that the tax gap currently stands at somewhere between R450 and R500 billion because it knows that the country's compliance level in the known universe is only 65%, which means that 35% of South Africans are not compliant. Tackling the compliance culture has not proved easy and is beyond SARS's remit. It is the mandate of every public sector worker.
SARS has noted an uptick in illicit trade, general crime and corruption, and criminal syndicates—which are also cross-border. Low economic growth, high unemployment, and poverty and inequality continue to characterise the country’s socio-economic environment, directly impacting the culture of compliance and taxpayers' ability to honour their tax commitments.
SARS also has to compete in the open market for critical skills needed in the evolving areas of digital science, technology, and artificial intelligence; hence, it has pleaded with the NT to review the agency's funding and funding model. SARS's caseload has grown by 260% in the last ten years, while revenue has only grown by 3.5%. The MoF is aware of the trend and has committed to addressing it within the current fiscal constraints.
SARS staff complement in customs has declined by 28% in the past ten years, and in that same period, the institution's overall staff complement has declined by 20%. SARS believes that greater resources should be allocated towards tackling base erosion, profit shifting by big business, tax and financial crime, and specialised skills to better deal with aggressive tax planning. Big corporations can now spend millions of Rands to challenge SARS in the courts.
There is also a need to continue with the modernisation project. The first phase was dedicated to digitising the institution’s processes, whereas the next phase will focus on embedding data science and artificial intelligence (AI) into the administration platform. Using AI and data science, SARS prevented the outflow of impermissible and fraudulent refunds amounting to nearly R50 billion. With the appropriate investment, SARS will develop a unique digital identity for all South Africans and entities. In addition, it will be able to continue the progress made with the SARB in increasing financial inclusion and reducing cash in the system by re-looking the payment system. Furthermore, SARS will also be able to modernise its VAT administration. He felt the current pace of modernisation is too slow compared to criminals and businesses.
Mr Pieterse said an allocation of R627 million in the budget was tabled earlier this year over the medium term to the Department of Justice and Constitutional Development to address the remaining FATF obligations and the AFU and SIU. The six outstanding items for FATF include the country demonstrating its ability to prosecute complex money laundering and other crimes. The NT is working with other departments to finalise these six matters and will report on progress by January.
Regarding the concerns raised on shifting the budget deficit forecasts, he pointed out that since the 2023 MTBPS, the department has consistently maintained that debt will stabilise at 77% in 2025/2026. During his Budget Speech earlier this year, the Minister revised this down to 75.3% in the same period. The main variable influencing how the debt-to-GDP ratio shifts – which occurs as and when there are revisions – is the nominal GDP (this has been slightly revised upwards in the MTBPS).
The budget deficit forecast over the next three years has slightly deteriorated to around R30 billion. The changes in the nominal GDP will offset this R30 billion. In total, the current budget deficit is between R300 and R400 billion. The NT has outlined that increasing the primary surplus over time and the four pillars of its economic growth strategy will be pivotal in controlling the deficit.
Regarding the questions related to the water boards, he said the department has engaged extensively with the Department of Water and Sanitation (DWS) on this matter, and there are two areas they have aligned on. The DWS has been working with the water boards to ensure that they forcefully implement debt collection and credit control enforcement for municipalities guilty of not paying their debt. In some cases, that will include attaching the bank account of the municipalities. Creating a payment culture amongst municipalities will be difficult without empowering water boards to implement forceful debt collection and credit control mechanisms.
A provision in the Constitution states that the MoF can consider a request from the Minister of Water and Sanitation to withhold the equitable share of specific municipalities to address consistent financial delinquency. However, withholding a municipality's equitable share has unintended consequences.
He asked that the question regarding the public sector wage agreement be referred to the minister. He could only say that the department has slightly increased the contingency reserve from R5 billion to R7 billion and will reach R20 billion in the next year to mitigate against any unforeseen disasters.
Adding on to that point, he indicated that the NT is also working on a parametric insurance product for disasters at the request of the Treasury Committee chaired by the President. This will define certain thresholds, and if a disaster meets that threshold, the department will automatically pay out funds like an insurance company does, shortening the current process. The department is also reviewing the local and provincial government disaster grants. It is also working closely with the Development Bank of South Africa, which accompanied the department to the disaster sites in the previous week.
On how the department will prevent the funds set aside for the conditional grants that will form part of the provincial equitable share from being abused, he said there are four elements to reforming the provincial equitable share. The first is that certain conditional grants which perform the same function will be merged, such as the Comprehensive Agricultural Support Programme and the Ilima Letsema Programme. Then, there are grants the department wants to provide better support for, like the Municipal Infrastructure Grant, which will be increased to adjust with inflation and align with the shifting composition of spending. Other grants are being restructured through building incentives. Only one grant, the Community Library Services Grant, will be incorporated into the provincial equitable share.
Regarding the SRD grant, he assured the Committee that it was included as a provisional allocation in the fiscal framework's baseline.
On the R5 billion to be allocated to SANRAL, he said the Gauteng Provincial Government has already paid R3.8 billion of the R20 billion debt to SANRAL for E-Tolls, in line with the agreement that 70% of the debt will be taken up by the national government and 30% for the provincial government. The R3.8 billion includes the provincial government’s contribution to the debt and backlog maintenance, and it will be appropriated to the Department of Transport, which will then pass it onto SANRAL. The balance of the R5 billion, which is R1.2 billion, will be covered by the national government. The provincial government decided to pay its debt over five years to save on interest costs, and the national contribution will kick in only after that.
Regarding the specific areas Operation Vulindlela has targeted to assist local municipalities, he mentioned that the Department of Cooperative Governance and Traditional Affairs, NT, and the Presidency are currently discussing those areas. Once those discussions are finalised, the department will return to the Committee with further details.
The NT has often emphasised Operation Vulindlela's impact in unlocking the bottlenecks in network industries, which directly impacts inclusivity. It has also addressed long-standing issues like spectrum auctions to reduce the cost of data. The department will provide a detailed update on Operation Vulindlela’s achievements in the future.
On GFECRA, he explained that GFECRA assisted in reducing the country’s gross borrowing requirement this year by R100 billion, thus improving the positive sentiment in the bond market and lowering yields. However, the impact has been slightly constrained due to the under-collection of revenue. An additional R50 billion will be used from the account to reduce the country’s gross borrowing requirement over the next two years.
Regarding the 45% offshore allowance, he indicated that the department is currently assessing its impact, particularly on the domestic savings industry. Once the assessment is completed, it will brief Parliament on its progress.
On public-private partnerships, he explained that municipalities and SOEs do public infrastructure projects. The focus going forward should be on improving their capacity to implement the projects. Currently, the department is refining its infrastructure data management system to better track, monitor, and evaluate projects as they are being delivered.
Regarding the department’s growth forecast concerns, he stated that its model was the most prudent estimate of what the department thinks the growth forecast will be. The forecast is updated four times a year.
Co-Chairperson Legwase noted that the department had little time to respond to all the questions, and she requested that the Committee allow the Minister to make his concluding remarks.
Dr David Masondo (Deputy Minister of Finance) said the government wanted a reliable and affordable energy supply. Eskom has been a liability not only to the fiscus but also to the economy. Through Operation Vulindlela, the government expressed its intent to liberalise the energy sector to allow the private sector to provide additional electricity and to fund the expansion of the country’s transmission infrastructure. The same principle has been applied to the other network industries.
He believed it was important for the joint meeting to consider how China developed its economy. China did so by liberalising the energy market and privatising some energy generation stations. Vietnam followed the same example. In his opinion, the reforms undertaken through Operation Vulindlela will assist the country in growing its economy.
Co-Chairperson Legwase said the Committee would continue deliberating on the MTBPS statement next Tuesday.
Minister Gondongwana said he would respond to the questions at the meeting on Tuesday.
The meeting was adjourned.
