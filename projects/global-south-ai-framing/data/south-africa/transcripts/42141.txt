Title: Catalysing MSME Development in SA; Small Business Growth Index
Date: 26 Nov 2025
Committee: 
URL: https://pmg.org.za/committee-meeting/42141/

Meeting Summary
Video
The Portfolio Committee received briefings on the state of micro, small, and medium enterprises (MSMEs) in South Africa.
The Bureau of Market Research briefed the Committee on the Small Business Growth Index compiled in partnership with ABSA Bank and the University of South Africa. The research found that the sector was under significant strain, with more than half of the 1 601 businesses surveyed found to be contracting or in distress. Key challenges included cost pressures, infrastructure instability, limited access to affordable finance, and critical skills gaps. The overall national index score of 51.08 out of 100 indicated a vulnerable sector. Businesses had called for reduced bureaucracy, improved finance access, and possible VAT reductions.
A presentation by CSC Advisory proposed a suite of integrated reforms to catalyse MSME development. It argued that incremental approaches were insufficient and outlined six key initiatives. These included a Unified National Business Package to simplify formalisation of small businesses, a data-sharing initiative to improve credit access, a reduction of red tape, a credit insurance scheme, incentives for skills and capital, and a national programme to promote an entrepreneurship culture. It was emphasised that coordinated, high-level leadership between government and the private sector was essential to implement these measures.
Committee Members said the data confirmed widely recognised sectoral challenges. Questions and comments focused on the need for actionable solutions, the specific impact of regulatory burdens and labour costs, the misalignment of government funding instruments, and the importance of ensuring that support reached informal, township, and rural businesses. Concerns were also raised about the representativeness of the survey methodology and the framing of policy recommendations.
In their responses, the presenters clarified the robustness of the research methodology and reaffirmed that the data provided a clear roadmap for targeted intervention. They emphasised that issues of affordable finance, administrative burdens, and infrastructure stability were critical. A strong, structured partnership between government and the private sector, modelled on the response to national crises, was required to drive integrated solutions and create a more enabling environment for MSMEs.
Meeting report
The Chairperson opened the meeting and welcomed all present. The agenda was adopted, and the day’s apologies were noted. The Committee Secretary introduced the two delegations that would brief the Committee.
The Chairperson then handed over to the delegation from the Bureau of Market Research (BMR) to deliver the first presentation.
Briefing: Small Business Growth Index
Dr Requier Wait, Chief Researcher, BMR, explained that the Small Business Growth Index (SBGI) was a national partnership between ABSA Bank and the BMR at the University of South Africa (Unisa). It intended to provide a credible real-time barometer of the health and resilience of South Africa’s small business sector. The study surveyed 1 601 small business owners and managers across all nine provinces, using a nationally representative sample. It focused on enterprises employing between one and 50 employees. The SBGI assessed business perceptions across eight performance metrics and aimed to generate evidence-based insights for policy intervention. The results being presented reflected the first half of the year, with a second report forthcoming.
The findings pointed to significant strain in the small business environment. More than half of small businesses were found to be contracting or in distress, driven by cost pressures, rising inflation, infrastructure instability and limited access to finance. Many businesses indicated that they might not survive another year without support. Skills gaps were identified in key areas such as digital management, cybersecurity and export readiness, with many respondents uncertain about where to access appropriate training. Limited access to capital, increased competition, and administrative burdens were highlighted as major obstacles. The overall national SBGI score of 51.08 out of 100 signalled a vulnerable sector, although some improvement in future stability was anticipated.
Small businesses regarded policy support as essential, with most calling for reduced bureaucracy, improved access to affordable finance and possible value-added tax (VAT) reductions. Businesses also expressed concern about escalating operational costs, including transport, utilities and raw materials, which had led many to plan price increases. Despite these challenges, some optimism remained, with around a quarter of businesses expecting growth. Following the report, the research team developed practical guidance for businesses, offering tips on cash-flow management, diversifying funding, improving online presence, using data to understand customers, and prioritising skills development, all aimed at strengthening resilience in a difficult operating environment.
Professor Paul Kibuuka, Researcher Director, BMR, noted that the initial workshop, where the Minister contributed inputs on the eight pillars of the SBGI, had been critical in shaping the project’s framework and partnerships. These discussions enabled the BMR to establish memorandums of understanding with the Department of Small Business Development (DSBD) and the Small Enterprise Development and Finance Agency (SEDFA). The SBGI’s uniqueness lay in its reliance on direct survey responses from small businesses, its implementation by organised business rather than solely academics, and its purpose as a tool for policy intervention rather than academic research. The SBGI was a biannual initiative, with the first report completed and the next scheduled for release at the beginning of December.

Discussion
Mr H Kruger (DA) said he appreciated the presentation. The data helped to quantify issues the Committee was already aware of regarding the pressures facing small businesses. He asked that the document containing the small business tips be shared with Members and that they be included in future distribution lists when updated reports and additional guidance were released. He asked whether the new Business Licensing Bill would affect the ease of doing business for micro, small and medium enterprises (MSMEs), given that many small firms were already in a state of significant distress and doubted their ability to survive beyond the year. Mr Kruger asked whether the Bill might add to existing bottlenecks and burdens and requested guidance on how the government should address the situation of small and informal businesses, such as spaza shops, that were not operating within legal requirements.
Ms S Singh (DA) said she found the presentation informative and noted, as Mr Kruger had, that much of the material confirmed challenges the Committee already understood. The evidence now places clear responsibility on the government to reassess its initiatives. Existing programmes were not producing the thriving MSME sector needed to drive employment. Despite billions invested, many supported MSMEs had either collapsed or remained in survival mode, as confirmed during oversight visits. Support programmes needed to provide a comprehensive package of financial and non-financial assistance, grounded in viable business plans and continuous guidance, rather than partial funding that left businesses unable to complete production or access markets.
Ms Singh asked the presenters what specific feedback had been received on labour costs and regulatory burdens, noting from Members’ own engagements that many potential employers, particularly in labour-intensive industries such as clothing manufacturing, struggled to comply with labour regulations and bargaining council agreements that were automatically extended to them. She also queried whether the study had differentiated the impact of different support mechanisms. Supplier development programmes run by corporates and businesses supported under enterprise incubation schemes appeared more sustainable and successful. Distinguishing which programmes were effective, and which were not, would offer valuable insight into what worked at ground level.
Mr C Malematja (ANC) said the presentation was concise and clear. He emphasised that while shortcomings in small businesses were often identified, such as limited capacity, weak pricing strategies, or the inability to sustain operations, entrepreneurs themselves bore the real burden of these challenges and required practical support rather than repeated criticism. Small operators, including spaza shops, worked in highly demanding environments where even minor errors could undermine profitability. Running such enterprises required real skill. Research should increasingly provide actionable, hands-on solutions to help small businesses build capacity rather than simply highlighting their weaknesses. Survey findings on constraints such as capacity and competition were useful, but needed to be translated into meaningful assistance for those operating in difficult circumstances.
Ms N Hlazo-Webster (BOSA) explained that her professional background was in enterprise and economic development, where she had worked with various government and private sector agencies. From that experience, she had observed a recurring problem: officials within departments and agencies responsible for supporting small enterprises often lacked direct experience in enterprise development. This gap in practical understanding weakened the impact and relevance of support programmes. She suggested that the Committee should consider how the DBSD might, over time, incorporate sector-specific expertise into its staffing and planning, as some international case studies showed that this approach improved outcomes.
She noted that the data indicated that 100 percent of MSMEs identified inadequate capital as their primary constraint. Which elements of the current government funding architecture were misaligned with MSME realities? She highlighted her long-standing concern that government funding instruments frequently applied commercial banking criteria that were unsuitable for MSMEs. She also referred to the finding that most MSMEs could survive less than a year without support, and she asked which financial instruments, such as grants or working-capital facilities, would be most effective for businesses with such short survival horizons.
Regarding market access, there was insufficient collaboration between government and the private sector in this area. Ms Hlazo-Webster asked whether the researchers could propose models of public–private partnership that would create stronger market-access opportunities, particularly given that MSMEs faced competition from better-resourced formal businesses. Certain opportunities might even need to be ring-fenced for participants in government programmes to strengthen both financing and market access, the two most critical areas highlighted in the report.
Ms B Mathulelwa (EFF) said the presentation had highlighted several serious concerns that required further clarification from the presenters. The data confirmed that the small business sector was in crisis, facing government failures in economic management, energy supply, logistics, and financing. While the private sector often criticised government performance, the presenters needed to state their own position clearly, particularly on issues such as state-led industrialisation, the proposal for a state bank and the call for a reduction in VAT. What was their stance on the state assuming greater responsibility for supporting MSMEs?
She added that the Committee needed to push the DSBD to improve skills development, infrastructure stability and the removal of red tape. She asked the presenters to clarify their view on red-tape reduction, given their finding that over half of small businesses were contracting or at risk of closing, with many likely to shut down within a year under current conditions. She highlighted the steep increases in transport, utilities, raw materials and finance costs, which made the sector unsustainable. She noted that more than 55 percent of MSMEs could only survive 12 months or less, and that 75 percent believed urgent policy intervention was needed.
Ms Mathulelwa pointed to major skills gaps, with extremely low digital and strategic capacity and large numbers of businesses not knowing where to access training. She emphasised the problem of inaccessible finance for start-ups and struggling enterprises. Most cited lack of capital as their main barrier, while more than half struggled to access formal funding due to bureaucracy and exclusion. She asked the presenters to provide clear figures on the percentage of black-owned small and medium businesses they assisted, especially those owned by women, youth and persons with disabilities, noting that the DSBD’s own statistics reflected limited support for these groups. Government intervention had to focus on easing access to finance and grants, reducing red tape and lowering VAT. She asked the presenters to outline their position on these key areas.
The Chairperson also welcomed the presentation and emphasised the need for clarity and understanding of the findings. The Committee had to be certain about whether the results accurately reflected the realities of all small businesses, not just those with internet access or in particular provinces or sectors. She questioned the representativeness of the 1 601 businesses surveyed, asking for details on where the survey was conducted and how participants were selected. She requested that the researchers outline the main weaknesses or gaps in their study to avoid misinterpretation or overstatement of the findings.
She raised concerns about the inclusion of small businesses in townships, rural areas, informal sectors and under-resourced communities. Limited access to stable internet and digital tools could have excluded or misrepresented these businesses if the survey had been conducted solely online. Had any physical fieldwork been conducted to reach these groups? She also highlighted the issue of red tape, requesting practical suggestions for removing unnecessary delays to make it easier for entrepreneurs to start, operate, or expand their businesses. How could support programmes be designed differently to ensure sustainability for informal or township microenterprises?
The Chairperson noted additional challenges that were not fully captured in the report, including the difficulty small businesses faced in accessing information to improve operations, and the risk of theft or loss of goods funded by support programmes. She requested guidance from the presenters on how these issues could be addressed, particularly for businesses that were already struggling to survive under current pressures.
Responses
Dr Wait said the survey, while not a census of all businesses, was robust, nationally representative, and statistically stress-tested to ensure validity and reliability. He stated that 1 601 SMMEs employing 1 to 50 people across all nine provinces were surveyed, drawing from 12 business registries and covering different economic sectors. He noted that the report provided insights into provincial variations and sectoral prospects, with agriculture, forestry, hunting, fishing, electricity, gas, and water supply showing poor prospects, while mining and construction showed higher prospects, based on respondents’ feedback.
Prof Kibuuka said diagnostic tests had been conducted on the 1 601 respondents to assess reliability, validity, and structural integrity, all of which confirmed that the sample was representative across the nine economic sectors and nine provinces. He emphasised that the study focused exclusively on businesses employing between one and fifty people, and that all respondents interviewed were either owners or managers of these entities.
Regarding the limitations of the study, Dr Waite said that, as was clearly stated in the report, the research was a cross-sectional online survey rather than a full census. Mobile penetration had enabled respondents to complete the survey even in remote areas, provided they had cell phone coverage.
The key takeaway from the SBGI was that it provided a clear roadmap indicating where support would have the greatest impact, based on the responses of businesses. The precise formulation of interventions in those areas did not have a simple solution and would require further deliberation and research. He identified the critical issues highlighted by the study such as access to affordable financing, reducing administrative and regulatory burdens, stabilising core infrastructure such as energy and water, and strengthening skills development pathways. The research would continue, with follow-on results to be published, allowing developments to be tracked over time and providing useful information for policymakers.
Dr Wait stated that he could share the report and supplementary tips with the Committee. On the Business Licensing Bill, he clarified that the SBGI did not make recommendations on specific legislation, as that was not the core focus. However, consideration needed to be given to the potential impact of the Bill, depending on how it was structured and implemented. Administrative burden and red tape were key concerns cited by small businesses and should be considered in relation to the Bill. Trade-offs would need to be weighed, as with most policies.
Issues related to bargaining and labour agreements fell outside the scope of the research. Similarly, supplier development, market access, and state-led industrialisation programmes were also beyond the study’s scope, although they remained important considerations for policymakers. He acknowledged comments regarding skills development, emphasising that small businesses were often uncertain where to access the right skills, which was an issue for the Committee to consider.
Dr Wait concluded by stating that the study focused on identifying key issues raised by businesses and tracking their evolution over time. He emphasised that access to finance and compliance were consistently identified as concerns. He assured the Committee that he would share the report and tips and that the BMR would be available to provide additional clarification or respond to follow-up questions by email, within the scope of the research.
Briefing: Catalysing small business development
Mr Charles Chemel, Principal, CSC Advisory, addressed the Committee on strategies to improve access to finance for MSMEs and catalyse small business development in South Africa. He emphasised that the sector was under strain and that incremental “business as usual” approaches would not achieve the necessary transformation. Despite recent positive economic sentiment, urgent intervention was required to improve economic fundamentals, particularly given global competition, technological disruption, and South Africa’s flat economic trajectory.
Mr Chemel highlighted the heterogeneous nature of MSMEs, noting that micro and small enterprises faced different constraints than those of medium-sized businesses. Micro and small enterprises were typically highly informal, constrained by limited capital, skills, and high compliance costs. These factors contributed to low bank utilisation, poor recordkeeping, limited access to credit, high interest costs, low scalability, and poor sector performance. This created a vicious cycle, making the sector unattractive to investment and trapping MSMEs in low-growth, low-credit environments.
At the macro level, South Africa had a weak culture of entrepreneurship, with many people dependent on employment or social grants. Regulatory red tape, fragmented support structures, slow policy implementation, and the absence of high-level coordination further discouraged entrepreneurial activity. He cited South Africa’s low ranking in pro-product market regulation among OECD countries and the long lead times for initiatives, arguing for more visible leadership, urgency, and ecosystem-wide orchestration.
Mr Chemel said bold reforms were needed to reorganise the MSME ecosystem. There should be a focus on a small number of transformative initiatives rather than dispersing effort across multiple fragmented projects. He outlined six integrated initiatives, which he said could radically change the trajectory of small business development over two to three years:
Unified National Business Package:
Mr Chemel proposed creating a national platform to encourage MSMEs to formalise and enter the banking system. Drawing on the successful 2004 Mzansi initiative for financial inclusion, the package would simplify registration, tax compliance, and data integration, providing a streamlined, end-to-end solution. Collaboration among government, banks, and mobile networks would be essential, with a focus on automating registration with the Companies and Intellectual Property Commission (CIPC) and South African Revenue Service (SARS), reducing compliance costs, and encouraging cash to enter the formal financial system.
Data Sharing Initiative:
Building on work by the South African Credit and Risk Reporting Association (SACRRA), Mr Chemel suggested a system to share credit and risk data among financial institutions, improving access to credit, reducing its cost, and supporting risk management. Effective implementation would require ecosystem-wide coordination and investment from both the public and private sectors.
Reduction of Red Tape:
Simplifying compliance procedures, particularly the Financial Intelligence Centre Act (FICA) process, and improving grant processes would reduce bureaucratic burdens. Regulatory simplification would make it easier for informal businesses to formalise and operate efficiently.
Credit Insurance and Risk Sharing:
Mr Chemel proposed a simple, optional scheme for businesses financing other businesses, utilising shared data to reduce the cost and risk of credit. This could be implemented through public-private partnerships to ensure accessibility and scalability.
Incentivising Skills and Capital:
Mr Chemel advocated targeted credit and capital incentives to attract investment and skills into the MSME sector. This approach would integrate investors and entrepreneurs, particularly benefiting historically disadvantaged groups, including women, youth, and people with disabilities.
Entrepreneurship Culture Programme:
There was a need to promote entrepreneurship as a desirable and viable path to wealth creation through marketing, education, and awareness campaigns. This programme would broaden participation in the MSME sector and encourage a culture of enterprise in South Africa.
Mr Chemel explained that these six initiatives were interdependent, each reinforcing the others to improve access to finance, operational efficiency, and formalisation. He proposed a national bank account as a central unifying mechanism, integrating automated registration, tax collection, recordkeeping, mobile data, digital skills training, and potential artificial intelligence (AI) tools. It would simplify operations for MSMEs and enhance financial inclusion.
Validation and buy-in from government, banks, and mobile networks would be critical to ensure the initiatives delivered intended benefits. While medium-sized enterprises were not the primary focus, collaboration between large and medium-sized businesses, inspired by international B20 initiatives, could complement these efforts.
Implementing these integrated initiatives could transform South Africa’s economy by making it more inclusive, competitive, and self-sufficient. Benefits would include improved investment attractiveness, GDP growth, employment creation, increased tax revenue, reduced informality, enhanced data availability, and broader access to finance. Coordinated, focused, and bold action was essential to realise this potential.

Discussion
Mr Kruger said he had found the presentation very informative. He highlighted two main factors that hindered the success of small businesses: red tape and ease of doing business. Although some academics equated the two, they were different concepts, each requiring a distinct definition.
He emphasised the need for a framework from the private sector to help eliminate red tape and promote the ease of doing business for MSMEs. He noted, however, that the private sector in South Africa was currently very fragmented, with a multitude of ideas on how to improve the MSME ecosystem. He expressed concern that many private-sector solutions were driven by self-interest rather than the needs of small enterprises, which contributed to fragmentation.
Mr Kruger referred to previous presentations and Committee discussions, observing that solutions proposed often already existed, and that oversight visits to very small and informal businesses revealed ongoing challenges. Fragmented solutions were not conducive to the greater good. A “whole-of-society” approach was necessary to address the problems in the small business sector, which was, according to the first presentation, in significant trouble.
He urged the development of a framework for solutions that was agreed upon by politicians, the private sector, and government. Without such coordinated action, MSMEs would remain in a precarious position.
Ms M Mafagane (MKP) asked how the DBSD, the Committee, and the MSME sector should prepare for the disruptive impact of artificial intelligence, noting that AI presented both significant opportunities for innovation, efficiency, and new career paths, as well as challenges including job displacement and ethical concerns. She questioned whether the MSME sector was likely to collapse or whether it might experience growth in light of these developments.
What were the main obstacles to developing the national business package referenced in the presentation? Could more be done to support MSMEs beyond current measures, such as the National Credit Act regulations and the MSME and Cooperative Funding Policy? The government was already encouraging banks to increase lending to small businesses.
Ms Mafagane asked for an example of the obstacles to successful partnerships between the private sector and government in the MSME ecosystem. The private sector was often perceived to be holding back investment due to the government’s failure to create a conducive environment. She emphasised the Committee’s interest in seeing greater private-sector involvement.
Ms Mathulelwa thanked the presenter for the report but also expressed concerns. While the report highlighted real challenges, it appeared to favour the private sector over the government, creating a negative perception of state entities. As researchers or experts, presenters were expected not only to provide data but also to share their own observations and positions, particularly on issues such as the role of state entities versus privatisation.
The presentation offered an alternative approach to accelerating MSME development. The sector was stagnant, fragmented, and unable to contribute meaningfully to economic growth and job creation in its current form. She was worried about the framing of reports, which, she argued, often highlighted private-sector efficiency while overlooking the impact on poorer communities and informal businesses. She gave examples, such as network coverage and fibre internet, which, while presented as technological improvements, were largely inaccessible to low-income households and small informal entrepreneurs.
Such presentations, while informative, could mislead communities by showing solutions that did not address structural inequalities. Ms Mathulelwa stressed the need to critically examine the causes behind the challenges faced by entrepreneurs and to ensure that reports included feasible solutions, particularly regarding nationalisation and support for informal businesses.
Ms Mathulelwa noted that global economic instability, rising protectionism, weak infrastructure, high unemployment, fiscal pressures, and slow SME expansion rendered the current “business-as-usual” approach inadequate. MSME development in South Africa lagged behind other countries, with low levels of formal business growth and limited scaling of small enterprises. The Committee needed to understand the root causes of the challenges faced by entrepreneurs and to demand practical, actionable recommendations from presenters, rather than reports that merely described problems.
Ms Mathulelwa noted that the presentation outlined a cycle of stagnation and expressed dissatisfaction with the way issues were being addressed. She acknowledged the value of the research but stressed that presenters should also provide their own positions and recommendations on how to address the challenges identified. The Committee could not simply accept negative portrayals without actionable solutions, as they were actively engaged in addressing these issues at a national level.
The presenter had argued that South Africa could significantly transform the SMME landscape within two to three years if the ecosystem were simplified, integrated, and supported by a few high-impact interventions led jointly by government and the private sector. She expressed frustration with the private sector, stating that it was not sufficiently committed to supporting entrepreneurs. She concluded by expressing hope for a follow-up session, noting that time constraints had prevented her from fully addressing her concerns.
Ms M Mmolotsane (ANC) asked Mr Chemel how participation in entrepreneurship could be broadened and the environment made more enabling, given South Africa’s weak culture of entrepreneurship, high dependency on employment and grants, and the perception that starting a business was often a last resort due to bureaucratic hurdles. What strategies or policy interventions could address systemic barriers, particularly restricted access to credit, and create sufficient incentives for entrepreneurs of all backgrounds to invest and grow within the MSME sector?
Ms Singh noted that the presentation reaffirmed widely recognised challenges within the small and micro business sector, including stagnation and regulatory hurdles, and that South Africa was lagging behind peer countries in terms of economic growth and employment. She stressed the importance of supporting initiatives that were backed by evidence, regardless of personal viewpoints on issues such as nationalisation.
She encouraged the presenter to provide more direct and specific recommendations, particularly regarding red tape, labour legislation, tax disincentives, and the ease of doing business. Ms Singh emphasised the need for urgent action. She called for clarity on exactly which legislation should be amended, which regulations should be removed, and what specific measures could improve conditions for small businesses.
Mr Malematja aligned himself with the presenter’s remark that tinkering was not a strategy and stressed the need for urgent action to address the long-standing exclusion of the black majority from the South African economy. He commended Mr Chemel for a well-structured presentation that incorporated international considerations, including conflicts, tariffs, and climate change.
He cautioned against the narrative that exempting businesses from tax constituted a growth strategy. Sustainable entrepreneurs should be nurtured to graduate into fully-fledged private-sector participants capable of supporting themselves and contributing to the broader economy. A sustainable and effective private sector was essential, particularly because the government could not accommodate everyone.
Mr Malematja referred to earlier points raised by Ms Mmolotsane regarding South Africa’s weak culture of entrepreneurship, high dependence on employment, and the need to broaden participation and create an enabling environment for business growth. He highlighted concerns about the fragmented MSME ecosystem, noting limited high-level coordination and leadership, and questioned how the ecosystem could be simplified to improve accessibility and achieve meaningful outcomes rather than merely administrative results.
Any intervention should aim to support MSMEs from start-up to fully integrated private-sector entities, with compliance and sustainability at the core. He urged researchers and policymakers to ensure that recommendations were clearly understood, actionable, and driven by a genuine commitment to transformation, rather than producing reports that failed to deliver tangible impact. He concluded by thanking the presenter for his informative presentation.
The Chairperson referred to slide seven of the presentation, which noted a lack of strong leadership on MSME reform, and asked what kind of leadership structure was needed to address this. She said that the presentation suggested high-level leadership was limited and, where it existed, focused on timelines and project management rather than thought leadership. She questioned this assertion, pointing out that the Department and institutions had demonstrated leadership in addressing past crises, such as the 2008 financial crisis and the COVID-19 pandemic, and asked the presenter to specify what was meant by the claim.
The Chairperson also requested examples of instances where micro and small businesses were benefiting despite existing challenges. She agreed with a previous speaker on the need to invite the state to conduct regular surveys of small businesses, rather than leaving this responsibility solely to the private sector. She referred to a point in the presentation about Chinese firms benefiting from flexibility and cost advantages but highlighted that in South Africa, many small businesses felt marginalised or “bullied” by larger firms. She asked the presenter to explain how the issues raised could be addressed within the South African context.
Responses
Mr Chemel emphasised that there should be no competition between the private sector and government, and that a strong partnership between the two was essential to achieve meaningful outcomes. Neither the government nor the private sector could resolve the challenges alone. Their respective roles complemented each other.
He drew a comparison to the electricity crisis in South Africa, noting that during that national emergency, all parties, including the President, relevant ministers, and senior executives in the private sector, worked together at a centralised level with clear priorities and resource mobilisation. The MSME sector currently suffers from fragmentation, with government departments, banks, and networks operating in a disjointed manner, lacking the intensity and coordination required to drive results.
Mr Chemel proposed the establishment of a structured, accountable process, potentially through a national convention or a national business package, supported by a strong steering committee comprising senior executives from government and the private sector. Activities, timelines, and responsibilities should be clearly defined to ensure integrated outcomes, high accountability, and urgent progress.
Addressing concerns about tax, he clarified that no one was suggesting exempting businesses from tax, but that many small businesses generated insufficient revenue to pay significant tax. The goal of proposed initiatives was to grow these businesses to become meaningful contributors to the fiscus, while reducing red tape and automating tax collection through an integrated national business package.
The proposals aimed to increase access to affordable credit, particularly for black entrepreneurs, through different initiatives and programmes which allowed data sharing to enhance competition and lower credit costs. While these measures were not a cure-all, they could create momentum in the MSME sector, improve participation, and contribute to broader economic transformation.
Mr Chemel added that revenue policy was clearly the domain of government, and that any integration of appropriate revenue instruments into a national business package would require discussion between the revenue authorities and the banks regarding the structure of the product. The key objective was to ensure that whatever solution was adopted could be executed efficiently through a national business package, without placing a burden on small businesses to manage records or hire accountants. The ultimate decisions regarding the extent of tax collection from various parts of the economy would rest with National Treasury.
The person thanked Mr Chemel for the informative presentation and suggested that the Secretariat organise a follow-up meeting with him as time had been constrained.
The meeting was adjourned.
