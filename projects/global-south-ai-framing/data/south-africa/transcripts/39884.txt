Electric Vehicle White Paper
The Portfolio Committee on Trade, Industry and Competition and the Portfolio Committee on Science, Technology and Innovation met jointly in Parliament for a briefing by the DSTI on the National Hydrogen and Fuel Cell Technologies Research, Development and Innovation Strategy and the Hydrogen Society Roadmap; a briefing by the DTIC on the Green Hydrogen Commercialisation Strategy and other Green Fuels; and a briefing by DTIC on the Electric Vehicle (EV) White Paper. The Minister and Deputy Minister of Trade, Industry and Competition attended, along with the Deputy Minister of Science, Technology and Innovation. The combined meeting created an opportunity for the two Portfolio Committees to receive briefings from DSTI and DTIC on their respective strategies and policies, how they fit into one another, and on progress in implementing those programmes.
The focus was on the implementation of the respective plans of hydrogen strategy of the Department of Science, Technology and Innovation (DSTI) and the Department of Trade, Industry, and Competition (DTIC), where the two Departments had been implementing the National Hydrogen and Fuel Cell Technology Research Development and Innovation Strategy, which was represented as the Hydrogen South Africa Strategy.  They also developed and implemented the Hydrogen Society Roadmap. The DTIC was responsible for implementing the Green Hydrogen Commercialisation Strategy, one of the pillars that supported the Hydrogen Society Roadmap and enabled the commercialisation of green hydrogen and the associated technology development through the Hydrogen South Africa Strategy.
The DSTI declared its support for commercialising locally developed and publicly funded Intellectual Property developed through DSTI initiatives. The governing structure would be a DSTI/DTIC Commercialisation Task Team. The first round of proposals comprised 30 projects amounting to approximately R1.7 billion (energy-related, amounting to R415 million). The expected next steps would be mapping the DSTI's existing incentives (both financial and non-financial), developing a new policy instrument, and submitting the Commercialisation Fund for the National Treasury's approval. The project proposals would be short-listed for piloting the Commercialisation Fund. It was anticipated that the President would launch the DSTI/DTIC Commercialisation Fund during the February 2025 State of the Nation Address.
The Minister explained that the reality was that the demand for internal combustion engine vehicles was significantly reducing. He had received notification from BMW that they were closing their internal combustion engine production capacity in the Eastern Cape in favour of plug-in hybrid engine production because of demand in the European Union and other markets to which South Africa exported those vehicles. The transition was happening at a fast pace. He was beginning to see a significant reduction in demand for internal combustion engines and a move towards new energy vehicles, so SA had to adapt its automotive strategy to be responsive to the reality of the demand.
The main argument for green hydrogen was that it did not compete with grid electricity because, to be considered ‘green’, the hydrogen had to be made with additional renewable electricity - existing renewable and coal-based electricity did not qualify. Green hydrogen did not compete for scarce water because desalinated seawater could provide the feed water to make the hydrogen. The markets for South African producers of green hydrogen included export markets in the European Union and the Far East. Locally, consumers, such as those in the mining industry, would be prepared to pay the premium for clean fuel while fertiliser made of green ammonia would find a market in agriculture. Finally, the Border market would entail refuelling aviation landing in South Africa and refuelling shipping. Fuels options for new engines and vessels would be Green Ammonia and Green Methanol.
A White Paper on Electric Vehicles was approved in 2023 in response to developments in the global space. The transformation was happening in the auto industry globally in response to climate challenge concerns. Seeking to promote fewer environmental emissions, a shift from internal combustion engines to electric vehicles of different kinds, from traditional hybrids to full battery electrics and fuel cell electric vehicles, had taken place. The White Paper outlined a commitment to ensuring that the transition was not just about decarbonisation but was also leveraged for growth. Several key stakeholders would have a role to play in implementing key policy interventions to support the transition to electric vehicles over the next decade. The implementation of the White Paper would commence after publication, which was expected shortly.

Presentation – Green Hydrogen and Commercialisation
Mr Gerard Fourie, Chief Director for Green Strategy and Green Hydrogen Commercialisation, began with the main argument for green hydrogen (GH2), which did not compete with grid electricity because to be considered ‘green’, it had to be made with additional renewable electricity. Existing renewable electricity and coal-based electricity did not qualify. Green hydrogen did not compete for scarce water because desalinated seawater (not surface water) could provide the feed water to make the hydrogen. Green hydrogen was a way of exporting sunshine and wind, which South Africa had in abundance. He confirmed that green hydrogen-based fuels were more expensive than fossil-based fuels, but sustainable bunker fuel for maritime shipping was the most attractive green-hydrogen-based fuel market.
His research indicated that South Africa and Chile were two very competitive countries. The manufacture of green hydrogen in SA was probably the second cheapest in the world, surpassed only by Chile, largely because the infrastructure was in place and there was abundant wind and (sea) water.
The presentation covered specific details of power fuels based on H2 from water electrolysis using renewable energy. SA GH2 could approach the $1/kg GH2 mark by 2050, equivalent to indigenous low-cost energy, making South Africa one of the most competitive industrial economies. South Africa would differentiate itself by using proprietary Fisher Tropsch technology to target the export of sustainable aviation fuel. It would manufacture electrolysers and fuel cells using platinum-group metals (PGMs), which are available locally. Commercialisation would leverage the Hydrogen Society Roadmap.
Ms Joanne Bates, Industrial Development Corporation COO, said that when the IDC started the commercialisation strategy, there was an intent to maximise the export markets, particularly where there were contracts with different opportunities where SA could be paid a premium for the export product, which would offset the higher cost at the front end of the development curve. Over the last two years, there have been significantly increased local applications, so the focus has always been on domestic and international markets. Locally, aviation fuel was more likely to be a shorter-term opportunity than originally anticipated, so that was a key area of focus.
The markets for South African producers of GH2 included competition in price-delivered export markets in the EU and the Far East. Locally, consumers, such as mining, would be prepared to pay the premium for clean fuel and fertiliser (green ammonia) would find a market in agriculture. Finally, the Border market would entail refuelling aviation landing in SA and refuelling shipping. Fuels options for new engines and vessels would be Green Ammonia (CMB, Fortescue) and Green Methanol (Maersk), while the existing fleet would utilise Green Fischer-Tropsch PtL.
Presentation on eVehicles White Paper
Mr Mkhululi Mlota, Chief Director of the Automotives Unit at the Department of Trade, Industry and Competition, indicated that a White Paper was approved the previous year (2023) in response to developments in the global space. A transformation was happening in the auto industry globally in response to climate challenge concerns. To promote fewer environmental emissions, there was a shift from internal combustion engines to electric vehicles of different kinds, from traditional hybrids to full battery electrics and fuel cell electric vehicles. South Africa’s automotive industry faced risks due to upcoming UK and European bans on fossil-fuel vehicles by 2035. With nearly 50% of SA's automotive exports going to Europe, the shift endangered a sector that contributed 2.6% of GDP and supported 115 000 jobs, particularly in the Eastern Cape. Transport emissions contributed 11% of SA’s greenhouse gases. Transitioning to electric vehicles (EVs) was essential to reduce those emissions, but significant investment in EV infrastructure and cleaner energy was required.
The White Paper outlined a commitment to ensuring that the transition was not just about decarbonisation but was also leveraged for growth. By deepening the automotive value chain, fostering local industry growth, and aligning with economic priorities, the approach aimed to be pro-growth and pro-investment. South Africa's Just Transition Framework integrated climate mitigation, adaptation and inclusive economic development, emphasising a 'just' transition that fairly distributed the benefits and burdens across society. It avoided disproportionate impacts on vulnerable communities and fostered an environmentally sustainable economy.
Regarding the way forward, several key stakeholders would need to participate in implementing key policy interventions to support the transition to EVs over the next decade.
The implementation of the White Paper would commence after publication with the following:
a) DTIC to publish the architecture of the incentive package;
b) Amendments to the Auto Production Development Programme (APDP) Regulations and Guidelines, initial draft and commencement of stakeholder engagements;
c) National Treasury to announce details of the Original Equipment Manufacturer (OEM) incentives;
d) Amendments to the Automotive Investment Scheme guidelines to be published.
The implementation of the remaining Policy Actions would be coordinated as follows:
a) SA Automotive Masterplan (SAAM) Executive Oversight Committee (EOC): The EV transition set out in the White Paper supported the achievement of the objectives of SAAM, and the EOC supported the implementation of the SAAM initiative;
b) Just Energy Transition Implementation Programme Coordinator for EVs: The JET Implementation Plan Programme Coordinator for Evs, namely the IDC, would support the implementation of the market development initiatives relating to the expansion of grid capacity and scaling up charging infrastructure.

Engineers and technology specialists require the same skills and competencies to navigate the production of green ammonia and green hydrogen. He said the best thing was to call for a 30% shareholding. That helped in the short to medium term, but in the long run, one did not want a situation where South Africans themselves were the lead innovators and investors on that front. The technological transfer was the best bet because sometimes capital followed the IP. Capital would follow if an IP was marketable and commercialised, and one could invest in those areas. But at the heart of it, one had to build a skills base. That was why the government was trying to partner with various universities, including TVETs, to build that pool.
Chairperson Masina stated that the Committees had enjoyed a fruitful engagement but allowed one further comment.
Mr Zondo stated that he felt they were too technical when he looked at the presentations the previous day. It was going to become a battle of intellectuals. Committee Members needed the Department to bring about the outcome of the mandate which was to drive the change. When providing answers in writing, the Department had to keep in mind that the responses should not be too technical for a room of Committee Members. The responses should show the processes that would drive the country to the desired outcome. For example, China should be brought on board. Most of the things that were happening in China were local. China drove its initiatives and localised production. Any partnerships had to ensure localisation in the process. The SA economy would never thrive or reach its destination through partnerships that attracted foreign investors to come and just take from South Africa, manufacture outside of the country and then sell the goods back to SA. Members wanted to hear where the country was going and how it would reach that destination.
Chairperson Masina stated that the Committees appreciated the presentations and the issues of emphasis that had been raised. The Minister had responded to some of the issues. The emphasis was that the just transition had to be just. Secondly, they could speak about the small things, such as a car at a university, but the point was that South Africa had no product. The Committees sought a higher ambition, not kids making cars in the township. SA wanted a greater influence on the continent but needed a higher ambition. They were challenging the officials to show that higher ambition, whether it was driven by the private sector and supported by the government or the other way around.
He added that future loans had to be affordable. SA should not continue to take the money and be instructed to do this or that because future generations would not be able to afford the loans, and those loans would not go away. It was essential to think about the future when drafting the financial model for all those things. Also, there had to be thinking about adapting existing infrastructure, including re-skilling those who were there to not displace one industry with a new one. There had to be a transition, even there. There had to be adaptation, re-skilling, etc., so that SA could move forward. Finally, transformation issues through building capabilities downstream were a key starting point.
Chairperson Masina thanked everyone for a really good conversation and hoped that it would help to direct the thinking of the Ministers and Deputy Ministers, their teams, and other institutions like the IDC and the Space Agency that were reshaping the discourse. A common agenda was important for the country to grow its economy, create employment for the people, and move to a higher level of industrialisation. There would be follow-up interactions.
He noted that he had received correspondence from the DTIC DG the previous night on the department's rationalisation, and he was removing that from the agenda. It would be discussed at a more convenient time when the DG was ready.
The Trade, Industry, and Competition Portfolio Committee Members were reminded of the meeting in Parliament on Thursday, 21 November, and the virtual meeting on Friday, 22 November 2024.
Closing remarks
The Chairperson concluded the business of the day.
The meeting was adjourned.
