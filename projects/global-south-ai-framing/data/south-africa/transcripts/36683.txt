Meeting Summary
Transport
SA National Road Agency Limited (SANRAL)
The Department of Transport and the South African National Roads Agency Limited (SANRAL) briefed the Portfolio Committee on Transport on their annual performance plans and budgets for the 2023/24 financial year.
The Department said its current plans considered budget cuts and the reprioritisation of resources over the medium term. Safety and security had remained its top strategic and organisational goal. Each mode had its own safety posture, but there were common themes across all modes. These included the need to work effectively with all spheres of government, address human behaviours, employ life-saving infrastructure counter-measures, improve safety data analysis, ensure innovative measures that bring safety benefits, and pursue performance-based rather than prescriptive regulations. The turn-around time for the issuance of driving licence cards was now targeted to be ten to 14 days.
The public transport priority was to achieve a seamless integration of all modal public transport operations that delivered a public transport system that was efficient, affordable, safe and reliable. The Department was targeting to have the revised public transport subsidy policy approved and implemented, and to have the framework for the taxi industry ownership of the taxi scrapping entity developed and implemented. On the revised bus rapid transport (BRT) specifications and technical norms and standards, cities had been instructed to scale down on infrastructure. They were implementing the Integrated Public Transport Network (IPTN) suite of offerings guide. Nine district municipalities have been assisted with the development of implementable IPTN plans. The Shova Kalula programme has seen 108 682 bicycles distributed. Other issues highlighted in the Department's presentation included its job creation successes; compliance in the Gauteng  freeway improvement project; and  the proposed integrated fare system utilising inter-operable bank cards operational on the IPTN system in Johannesburg, Cape Town, Tshwane, George, eThekwini and on the Gautrain;
SANRAL took the Committee through its focus areas for the 2023/24 period, which included asset management systems for the timely maintenance of national roads; transformation within the entity and the construction industry; the development of a new toll roads policy context for future projects; and conclusion of the resolution process regarding e-tolling in Gauteng. Its investment in routine and non-routine road maintenance would provide increased opportunities for black-owned small, medium and micro enterprises (SMMEs). The target was to have 1 800 SMMEs working for SANRAL. Another target was to create 12 000 jobs on community development projects.
The entity said that currently 1 882 km (8%) of SANRAL roads were in poor to very poor surface condition, which was well within the international norm of 10% for a well-maintained network, and the current average Overall Condition Index (OCI) was 69.35 -- only marginally below the international norm of 70. The condition of the remainder of strategic and primary roads under provincial administrations was substantially worse and needed to be addressed as a matter of priority. To address the basic requirements and also meet the expansion requirements of its current network, SANRAL would require a minimum of R15.753 billion per year over the next ten years should the option of toll funding no longer be available. Not doing expansions would result in increased congestion and associated increased road user costs on parts of the network.
Members asked if the Department had adequate equipment to produce driving licence cards; how sure it was that parts from scrapped taxis were not going to be recycled for use on South Africa's roads; how many schools had been given the Shova Kalula programme bicycles and their locations; how many bridges in rural areas have been built and their locations; what the Department was planning to do differently about fixing potholes; and what measures were in place to ensure fatalities were reduced, seeing that it was prioritising safety and a reduction of fatalities. It commented that the target of an 80% reduction in vandalism was not achievable when looking at the current rate of vandalism.
The Committee asked SANRAL when its projects could be expected to be completed if maintenance had been given priority, because it appeared the budget allocation was less than the need; what the state of the roads that had been affected by floods was; a breakdown of the SMMEs that were providing a service to the entity; and how many learners had been taken up in the entity's learnership programme -- and if it had started already.
Meeting report
Department of Transport Annual Performance Plan 2023/24
Mr Bosa Ramantsi, Chief Director: Strategy and Planning, Department of Transport (DoT), informed the Committee that the current plans considered budget cuts and the reprioritisation of resources over the medium term. Five strategic thrusts guided the focus of the Department:
Safety (including security) as an enabler of service delivery;
Public transport that enables social emancipation and an economy that works;
Infrastructure build that stimulates economic growth and job creation;
Building a maritime nation, and elevating the oceans economy; and
Accelerating transformation towards greater economic participation.
He said safety and security had remained the Department's top strategic and organisational goal.
Each mode had its own safety posture, but common themes crossed all modes. These included the need to work effectively with all spheres of government, address human behaviours, employ life-saving infrastructure counter-measures, improve safety data analysis, ensure innovative measures that bring safety benefits, and pursue performance-based rather than prescriptive regulations. An additional 1 200 000 driving licence cards were expected to be produced at the end of the 2021/22 financial year. Ten to f14 days were targeted to be the turn-around time for the issuance of driving licence cards.
Law enforcement functions were shared between the Road Traffic Management Corporation (RTMC), the Cross-Border Road Transport Agency (C-BRTA), the National Public Transport Regulator (NPTR) and the Road Traffic Infringement Agency (RTIA) with the provinces. Municipalities were undertaking law enforcement in line with their constitutional mandates. The target was to have rationalised entities where law enforcement was centrally coordinated to strengthen regulatory oversight, and to have a dedicated public transport  law enforcement capacity established countrywide.
The Department was targeting to have fewer than 977 rail safety occurrences, to have an 80% reduction in incidents of personal safety, theft and vandalism of infrastructure in the passenger rail environment, and to achieve 100% implementation of the national strategic plan to end gender-based violence and femicide (GBVF) in the rail transport sector. On maritime transport safety, the target was to reduce the reportable maritime safety incidents' rate and maritime fatality rate by 50%. The public transport GBV strategy had been developed and had been in the process of being incorporated into the South African National Taxi Council (SANTACO) constitution and code of conduct. Partnerships had been entered into between the taxi industry and the government (Department of Women, Youth and Persons with Disabilities) to promote the fight against GBV in the taxi industry. A memorandum of understanding (MoU) had been signed to formalise the initiative.
In the public transport focus area, the desired outcome for this priority was to achieve seamless integration of all modal public transport operations that deliver a public transport system that was efficient, affordable, safe and reliable. The Department was targeting to have the revised public transport subsidy policy approved and implemented, and to have the framework for the taxi industry ownership of the taxi scrapping entity developed and implemented. It was further targeted to have the re-imagined taxi recapitalisation programme framework that defines economic empowerment value chain opportunities, developed and implemented.
On implementing the revised bus rapid transport (BRT) specifications and technical norms and standards, cities had been instructed to scale down on infrastructure. They were implementing the Integrated Public Transport Network (IPTN) suite of offerings guide. Nine district municipalities have been assisted with the development of implementable IPTN Plans. 108 682 bicycles had been distributed, and the target was 120 000 distributed. The target was to have 90.07 million passenger trips instead of the current figure of 16.7 million passenger trips on passenger rail trips.
Regarding the infrastructure and job creation focus area, the transport sector had been identified as one of those that were key to creating decent jobs through the implementation of capital infrastructure projects. Key to optimising jobs created was adopting labour-intensive methods in public infrastructure programmes and providing an enabling environment for private sector employment.
100% compliance had been maintained at conventional toll plazas, while there had been 20% compliance in the Gauteng Freeway Improvement Project (GFIP) network. The Cabinet was yet to make the final decision on the GFIP. The Gauteng Provincial Government had agreed to contribute 30% to settle the debt and interest obligations of SANRAL, while the national government would cover the remaining 70%. Government proposed an initial allocation of R23.7 billion from the national fiscus, which would be disbursed under strict conditions.
18 955 jobs had been created against a target of 33 500 created through the public infrastructure projects of SANRAL's national road maintenance programme, while 515 914 jobs had been created against the target of 1 111 151 through public infrastructure projects -- the provincial road maintenance programme. The draft Public Sector Participation (PSP) framework has been approved for submission to the Economic Sectors, Employment and Infrastructure Development (ESEID) cluster. The draft High Speed Rail (HSR) framework has been approved for submission to the ESEID cluster. Both the PSP and HSR would be developed and implemented. 59 892 jobs have been supported against a target of 69 103 created through public infrastructure projects -- the Airports Company of SA (ACSA) infrastructure programme.
In the maritime focus area, the draft Merchant Shipping Bill and draft Maritime Development Fund Bill had been re-submitted to the state law adviser. The target was to have the Maritime Development Fund Bill promulgated. The terms of reference for the business case for the draft national shipping company model had been developed. The target was to have the national shipping company established.
Regarding acceleration of transformation for economic participation, the Department and its sector entities would focus on gender mainstreaming within its programmes. For all the areas identified, beneficiaries would be disaggregated into women, youth and persons with disabilities. The Economic Regulation of Transport (ERT) Bill has been approved for submission to Cabinet. The Transport Economic Regulator (TER) has been established and operationalised. Five merchant vessels against a target of ten had been registered on the South African ship register.
Concerning the innovation focus area, the transport sector was rapidly evolving into one of the economy's most innovative and dynamic areas. Significant developments and convergence of artificial intelligence, mapping, data and communications were driving innovation in transport. Consultations on the amendment of the 2011 regulations on an integrated fare system utilising inter-operable bank cards operational in Johannesburg, Cape Town, Tshwane, George and eThekwini IPTNs and the Gautrain, had been concluded. Consultations with the ten cities implementing IPTNs, SANTACO, PRASA and the Gautrain had been finalised. The target was a single integrated electronic ticketing system rolled out in selected government-subsidised public transport operators.
Detailed concept documents addressing all elements relating to the Remotely Piloted Aircraft System (RPAS) have been developed. Reviewed RPAS regulations would be approved and implemented.
When it came to environmental protection, the desired outcome was to ensure that the sector advanced environmentally sustainable policies and investments that promote a reduction of carbon and other harmful emissions from all sources of transport. The green procurement guidelines have been finalised. The target was to reduce transport sector emissions from 10.8% to 9.8% of the country's total emissions. The Department aimed to reduce the maritime pollution rate from 1.23 to 0.62 (50%).
Regarding governance for greater efficiency and accountability, the Department would be open and transparent, demonstrating to the public how it was furthering its strategic goals and effectively using its statutory and administrative authority. It would also target to build a departmental workforce that meets the challenges of today and tomorrow by improving employee engagement, recruiting talent from all segments of society, investing in workforce development and training, and enhancing the tools and technologies that employees rely on to meet the vision and mission of the Department. It planned to reduce the vacancy rate by 10% and below. Against a target of 30%, 30% of employees had been trained and 160 bursaries had been managed. 50 interns had been employed. No irregular and unauthorised expenditure had been reported for the 2020/21 financial year. The target was to have a 75% reduction in these two expenditures, and to achieve a clean audit opinion with no material findings because an unqualified audit report  with findings had been recorded in the 2020/21 financial year.

DoT's response
Mr Mathabatha Mokonyama, Deputy Director-General (DDG): Public Transport, DoT, said the conversion of panel vans was not illegal. For example, Nissan and Mercedes-Benz vehicles could be converted. The matter involved the Toyota Hiace panel vans that the SA Bureau of Standards (SABS) did not approve because of the anchorage on the metal sheets. The same applied to ambulances because they could be converted and were subjected to the same SABS standards and vehicles in the gaming industry. The Department agreed with the Public Protector on scrapping the Toyota panel vans, because even the Toyota company agreed they could not be converted into taxis. Unfortunately, not all the identified vehicles had come in. The conversions that happened had been illegal. They got impounded whenever those vehicles were identified because their owners were known. Concerning the recycled parts, he said that in terms of value added, some parts could be used but the vans had to be compacted. There had been an attempt to tamper with the panel vans, but that had to be stopped immediately.
He said the subsidy provided to the busses had been meant to make public transport cheaper. If the entire country was to be covered, an investment four times higher than the current one would have to be made. The subsidy level was very little. According to the White Paper, people should not spend more than 10% of their disposable income on transport. South Africa was not at the stage where the operator could recoup 100% from the industry.
Mr Ngwako Makaepea, DDG: Rail, DoT, said a framework had been developed to standardise what had become the parameters when they decided on the high-speed rail network corridor. The Department had developed the framework and consulted on it. The corridors had been identified. The Johannesburg – Durban corridor was the first one, the Tshwane – Komatipoort was the second, and the third was the Johannesburg – Musina corridor. He acknowledged that there were challenges with the long-distance passenger trains, as they were old and needed an overhaul. He also noted there were challenges in the report on locomotives. The first batch of trains had been auctioned, but the process had not been finalised and the Department was trying to ensure no violation of the rules. The matter could be better explained by PRASA.
Mr Prasanth Mohan, Chief Director: Roads, DoT, said that when it came to the road infrastructure budget and the imbalance in the budget allocation between the provinces and SANRAL, it should be noted that the budgeting system was that the budget of the provinces was from the provincial equitable share (PES) budget, and there was a shortfall. That was the very reason that the Provincial Roads Maintenance Grant (PRMG) had been established to supplement and assist the provinces. The Department was looking at improving the efficiency measures that could be put in place. A condition where there would be a rand-for-rand budget would be introduced into the budgeting system.
Referring to heavy vehicles, he explained there was a project that had been endorsed by the government and implemented by the Council for Scientific and Industrial Research (CSIR). It was called the Smart Truck Research Project, and was looking at the vehicles that had been designed according to the prescripts of the Road Traffic Act. The project was not about what the vehicle looked like, but how it performed and operated when it was on the road. This was based on international standards, and the participating countries were Canada, Britain, New Zealand and South Africa. 800 trucks from South Africa participated in this project under a controlled environment. The vehicles had permits to operate under abnormal load conditions. The Minister of Transport endorsed the project in terms of Section 81 (1) of the Road Traffic Act. The project was fully tracked by the global positioning system (GPS). Project managers and law enforcement authorities from the respective provinces had access to the routes used and the loading data. The vehicles were measured upon their departure and arrival at their destinations. Law enforcement authorities and project teams had access to the records, and if there were any deviations, the system would flag them and SMSes would be sent to the operators and law enforcement to ensure compliance. This was a temporary initiative, and the outcome was to see what improvements could be considered going forward when having vehicles of this nature. The objective was to look at productivity, safety, fitness and driver awareness. Another key aspect of the project was that operators had to comply with South African National Standards (SANS) 1359 of the Road Safety Traffic Management Systems adopted in South Africa. There were processes in place to look at deviations and project terminations. Once the project evaluation process was completed, a determination would be made on what could be used going forward in the long term.
Mr Mlawu said the licence cards matter was a moving target, driven by the patterns that had been observed in terms of the demand.

SANRAL 2023/24 annual performance plan
In his introductory remarks, Mr Themba Mhambi, SANRAL Board Chairperson, informed the Committee that the board was now complete, with two additional members -- Ms Buthelezi, an engineer, and Ms Noxaka, an accountant. The board had been given ten focus areas and four of them had been identified as critical. The financial stability of the entity was relatively secure. The maintenance of the existing infrastructure would be given consideration. The latest additions had got to do with potholes. Major projects were taking place on the Wild Coast and the N2 freeway. The entity would like to invest more in some projects, like Moloto and the N3 in KwaZulu-Natal (KZN). With new roads being built, the focus had been on community development, especially focusing on access to roads where communities were experiencing challenges.
He indicated the entity's supply chain management (SCM) had been reviewed to continue assisting big companies and designated groups with their small enterprises. On road safety, the focus was on looking at inputs from engineers to make roads safer. SANRAL has also strengthened the governance aspects in terms of consequence management to address fruitless, wasteful and irregular expenditure. Stakeholder engagement was another area of importance to the board.
Mr Reginald Demana, CEO, SANRAL, took the Committee through the focus areas for the 2023/24 period. These included implementation of the Horizon 2030 strategy; asset management systems for the timely maintenance of national roads; the rollout of SANRAL flagship projects; good cooperative relationships with relevant government departments, provincial and municipal authorities to implement Horizon 2030; transformation both internally and within the construction industry; acceleration of the Operating Model rollout; ongoing communication and stakeholder management efforts; implementation of business development focus areas; development of a new toll roads policy context for future projects; and conclusion of the resolution process of e-tolling in Gauteng.
He said SANRAL had been aiming, amongst other things, at providing and managing a safe national road network, the primary avenues of mobility, to enable and contribute to economic growth and social development; democratising the provision of the road network through broad-based black economic empowerment (BBBEE) and transformation; pursuing adequate government funding to sustainably operate and maintain the national road network; exploring, developing and implementing a diversified funding strategy and exploiting opportunities for the use of private finance and own revenue; and utilising technology, research and innovation to advance the provision, operation and management of the national road system in order to meet road users' needs.
For economic transformation and job creation, and in accordance with the 2023/24 annual performance plan, there would be a rollout of engineering and construction projects and a provision of a stimulus package for the N2 (Durban: EB Cloete; Umdloti to KwaMashu) and N3 (Durban to Pietermaritzburg) and N2 Wild Coast. Flagship projects include the R573 Moloto, the N2 Wild Coast and the Huguenot Tunnel. Investment in black economic empowerment (BEE) would enable opportunities for black-owned small, medium and micro enterprises (SMMEs) -- 75% of routine road maintenance expenditure and 60% of expenditure on non-routine road maintenance. The target was to have 1800 SMMEs working for SANRAL. There were 14 community development projects in construction, or completed. The target was to create 12 000 jobs on these projects.
In the field of education, skills and health priority, 200 internships were planned for the year, with 135 external bursaries and 250 scholarships targeted. There were a number of Sector Education and Training Authority (SETA) learnerships, and employment opportunities were provided for SETA graduates on SANRAL projects, where the target was 1 000. Regarding social cohesion and safer communities, the plan was to improve 12 hazardous locations through engineering standards; conduct 12 road safety audits; and implement road safety education and awareness programmes for primary schools and road users at 30 sites. Regarding capable, ethical and developmental state priorities, 440 positive messages in national and regional media were targeted, and 260 stakeholder engagement sessions were planned.
Mr Demana said that currently, 1 882 km (8%) of SANRAL roads were in a poor to very poor surface condition, which was well within the international norm of 10% for a well-maintained network. The current average overall condition index (OCI) was 69.35, which was marginally below the international norm of 70. This was largely due to the preventative maintenance first strategy followed by SANRAL. The impact of all preventative maintenance on roads incorporated since 2012 was evident in the 2017 to 2022 condition results. The condition of the remainder of strategic and primary roads under provincial administrations was substantially worse, and needed to be addressed as a matter of priority.
The current projected medium term expenditure framework (MTEF) allocations would enable SANRAL to address the basic budget requirements required to sustain the network, but not to address the growing backlog or expansion requirements of the current network under its jurisdiction. No network increase had been assumed. To address the basic requirements and to address the backlog as well as the expansion requirements of its current network, SANRAL would require a minimum of R15.753 billion per year over the next ten years should the option of toll funding no longer be available, provided the length of the SANRAL network remains unchanged. Not doing expansions would result in increased congestion and associated increased road user costs on parts of the network. Mobility was one of the most fundamental and important characteristics of economic activity, as it satisfied the basic need of going from one location to the other, a need that both passengers and freight shared. Apart from the direct cost of the congestion on the economy -- hours lost, productivity and vehicle operating costs -- the social impact of congestion on society was probably the biggest concern.

Regarding the unqualified audit report of 2022, he said the Auditor General (AG) had been satisfied that the revaluation was appropriate, reasonable, fairly valued and disclosed appropriately. The AG was satisfied that the expected credit losses were reasonable and disclosed appropriately, and satisfied that e-toll revenue was accounted for correctly and disclosed appropriately.
Key strategic risks were around the compromised delivery of projects due to the economic downturn, COVID-19 and threats of natural disasters; limited opportunities for funding diversity due to forced reliance on government funding; road funding policy uncertainty; inclusive participation in projects not being achieved and an inability to fully implement the transformation policy; challenges with navigating SCM regulations and thereby experiencing difficulty in sustaining the national road network; human capital capacity constraints; negative public perceptions; limited agility in responding to rapid changes in road technology and environmental disruptions; and uncertainty in the regulatory environment governing SANRAL.
Referring to mitigating key risks, Mr Demana said processes would be followed to ensure roads were suitable candidates for incorporation, and they would be transferred with sustainable and long term funding. The annual index targets would be adjusted in line with the negative index impact of the incorporated roads. Positive and unique features would be emphasised about SANRAL governance and how the entity was managed, and there would be intense communication about the transformation strategy and the profiling of the transformation beneficiaries of SANRAL.

Discussion
Ms Khumalo wanted to know when the projects would be expected to be completed if maintenance had been given priority, because it appeared the allocation was less than the need. She asked about the location of the 10 000 jobs that had been created, the focus areas of the jobs, and their beneficiaries.
Mr Mhambi said the Tongaat bridge would be fixed, but the procurement process was being finalised and the management of SANRAL would take care of that.
Ms Lehlohonolo Memeza, Corporate Affairs Executive, SANRAL, added they were embarking on the fifth procurement process concerning the Tongaat bridge, because the first four had failed. There was now approval from the board to select a number of service providers, and work had already started on the ground.
Ms Ramadwa asked how much had been budgeted for maintenance, because it appeared to be better than repairing; enquired about the status of the roads that had been affected by floods; asked for the breakdown of SMMEs that were providing a service to the entity, and wanted to know the number of both males and females on the board; enquired how many learners had been taken up in the learnership programme, and if it had started already. She remarked that in the village of Bende Motale in Limpopo, a scholar bus did not enter the village because of a narrow road, but dropped the children off on the main road. The children had to walk some kilometres to the school and back.
Mr Mhambi said SANRAL had done work on behalf of the KZN province, using the money of the province. Procurement for other projects was still being finalised. There were 1 000 targeted learnerships, which would be awarded in the fourth quarter of the year. It had been agreed that every SANRAL project had to have a learnership. This had been discussed with the Construction SETA as well.
Community development would take place in the communities close to the roads SANRAL had been working on. SANRAL had already done some work in the villages close to the areas where it had executed projects in Limpopo. The Eastern Cape had been very active in community development regarding access to roads that were close to the main projects. He added that the matter of the R74 would be raised with the relevant province so that it came to the attention of SANRAL. On the issue of females on the SANRAL board, he said the two new board members were female chairpersons of various committees.
Ms Memeza said that they had prioritised the locals when it came to the 11 000 created jobs. A minimum of 30% got allocated to the targeted groups of the projects, especially women-owned companies. The target for the learnerships was 1 000, which would be ramped up in outer years. SANRAL had entered into a memorandum of understanding (MOU) with the Construction SETA, and the focus would be on youths with matric and those who were semi-skilled, and they would be certificated so that they could find work in other projects. They wanted to establish a scheme to identify black industrialists in the value chain so that they could get work from SANRAL. This was another form of prioritising transformation because there was a radical need for it in the construction sector, including the property management area.
Mr Louw Kannemeyer, Engineering Services Executive, SANRAL, said that in the past year, 11 000 jobs had been generated. The jobs were created nationwide, and labour for semi-skilled work came from the targeted areas where the projects were. 71% of the 11 000 jobs went to males and the rest were shared between female youths and females. 1 914 SMMEs had been employed in the projects, and 1 682 of these enterprises had black ownership. A total of R82 million had gone to the SMMEs.
He said the learnerships were not something new, because they had been in place already. The only thing that had been introduced was the Construction SETA.
The Chairperson asked for the Committee to be given a breakdown per province of the roads that SANRAL had taken over. He also pointed out that some parts of the N1 in Limpopo were very bad, and needed to be resurfaced.
Ms Ramadwa enquired how the need versus allocation was going to be balanced, and how much would need to be added to the allocation.
Mr Demana replied that where the allocation was not meeting the need, there was always the maintenance of the road that was being covered, and new roads were not being developed. Maintenance was not being sacrificed, because it was the priority of SANRAL. Budget allocations prioritised maintenance, and expansion had always been a tertiary matter. Maintenance was the core business of SANRAL. The challenge was that it had been accepted that certain roads needed to be expanded and the allocation increased. In such cases, SANRAL had to approach National Treasury if the expansion was on a toll road.
Ms Khumalo suggested SANRAL should consider looking at the R61 road in the Eastern Cape, and wanted to know when it would be finished.
Mr Kannemeyer said that the floods had caused damage to the R61 road. There were restrictions on heavy trucks. SANRAL was busy with assessments and designs to finalise procurement for the work to be implemented. On the Mthetho Bridge, the contract had been awarded to a new contractor which had already moved on to the site. It would take four years to complete it, and it tied in with the N2 road project.
Mr Mhambi, in his closing remarks, said SANRAL was one of the entities they were proud of, amongst many others. He appreciated the input from Members and indicated the Committee would be brought up to speed about road maintenance development.
The meeting was adjourned.
