Date: 23 May 2023

Meeting Summary
Video
In a virtual meeting, the Public Service Commission (PSC) presented the findings of their roundtable discussion on the services offered by the State Information Technology Agency (SITA) to the Portfolio Committee. It identified several challenges affecting the effectiveness of government support for service delivery through information communication technology (ICT) in the public service. These challenges included outdated infrastructure, inadequate governance frameworks, frequent downtimes, lack of integration between departments, IT security weaknesses, and unjustifiable IT expenditure.
To address these challenges, the PSC proposed measures such as amending the SITA Act to enable digital transformation, reviewing the procurement and pricing model, devolving procurement to government departments, resolving tensions around the placement and role of SITA, and harmonising the policy environment and legislative framework. The roundtable report also highlighted the need for improved accountability, consequence management, and a stronger cybersecurity framework.
SITA acknowledged the challenges posed by ageing legacy systems, inter-departmental coordination, cybersecurity risks, skills development, procurement processes, and change management. They emphasised the importance of aligning digital transformation goals with government priorities, evaluating programme performance, protecting sensitive citizen data, investing in training and upskilling, and developing comprehensive strategies to prioritise investments and adapting to emerging technologies.
Committee Members expressed support for the PSC's recommendations and raised additional concerns on the need for cost analysis and pricing adjustments based on departmental requirements; the potential establishment of multiple small SITAs within departments compromising cybersecurity; the importance of change management and culture assessment within SITA; the significance of stricter monitoring and consequence management for procurement processes; and the role of client departments in supporting SITA's funding and sustainability.
In response, the PSC emphasised the complexity of cost analysis; the potential for in-house solutions to reduce costs; the need for capacity building within departments; and the role of the PSC in supporting SITA's efforts.
SITA highlighted their approaches to digital transformation; the challenges related to legacy systems; interdepartmental coordination; cybersecurity; skills development; procurement; change management; their plans for automation; collaboration with stakeholders; and developing a comprehensive strategy for successful government digitisation.
Overall, the roundtable discussion shed light on the challenges faced by SITA and the public service in delivering effective ICT services. The proposed measures and recommendations were aimed at addressing these challenges and improving service delivery in the future.
Meeting report
Public Service Commission (PSC) feedback on roundtable discussion on services offered by SITA.
Ms Yasmin Bacus, PSC Commissioner, led her team in presenting the Round Table Report on information communication technology (ICT). The presentation outlined that the PSC, through its various studies, had noted reports of inefficiencies with ICT support, and the negative impact on service delivery in the public service. Broadly, these were manifested in the following:
ICT infrastructure assets
Ageing and/or outdated infrastructure, which affected the speed of ICT transformation.
Despite significant investment in ICT, the government had not seen the benefit of technology, an impact and improvement of efficiencies, especially in front-line service delivery.
2. ICT governance
Inadequate/ineffective ICT governance frameworks.
Ineffective governance structures and policies.
Inadequate/ineffective system-related controls.
3. Performance and capability
Frequent and/or long connectivity downtimes.
Lack of, or poor integration of, ICT between departments in departments such as Home Affairs, the South African Social Security Agency (SASSA), the South African Police Service (SAPS), Justice and Constitutional Development, etc.
IT security weaknesses manifesting in, among others cyberattacks (Department of Justice)
IT expenditure not justifiable and government not receiving value or benefits from the money spent or IT projects or contracts.
Against this background, the PSC embarked on a project to assess the effectiveness of government support for service delivery, focusing on ICT in the public service. Therefore, the overall aim of the project was to delve into the whole ecosystem of the provision of government ICT and to propose measures to ensure effective and efficient support within the Public Service.
The specific objectives were to:
Assess how the State Information Technology Agency (SITA) was organised in executing its role of providing effective support to national government departments.
Identify the challenges experienced by government departments in terms of the provision and management of ICT, and the impact thereof on service delivery; and
Assess the underlying reasons for such challenges, review the interventions put in place to deal with them in relation to ICT, and propose measures to ensure effective and efficient service delivery.
The findings of the roundtable discussion were:
Functionality of SITA
Outdated SITA act, which required changes to enable digital transformation in government.
Complaints of over-pricing of both network and end-user computing services.
The ineffective and inefficient procurement process was often delayed over multiple years and fraught with irregularities.
Responses and proposed solutions
Amend the SITA act to harmonise the policy environment and legislative framework and repurpose SITA, which should include a review of the procurement and pricing model.
Devolve procurement of non-mandatory goods and services to government departments.
Resolve tensions around the placement and role of SITA to support the implementation of e-government strategies.
(See presentation for further details)
SITA feedback on roundtable discussion on the services it offers
Dr Bongani Mabaso, Managing Director, SITA, led his team in presenting the round table report on services offered by SITA.
The presentation outlined that the Public Service Commission (PSC) was established in terms of Section 196 of the Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, 1996. It was supported by the Office of the Public Service Commission (OPSC), headed by the Director-General, who was the Accounting Officer.  The mission of the PSC was to promote the constitutionally enshrined democratic principles and values of the public service by investigating, researching, monitoring, evaluating, communicating and reporting on public administration.
The initial meeting with entities/mandate-owners, including SITA, was on 12 July, and was attended by the Acting Director-General (ADG), Department of Communication and Digital Technologies ( DCDT), the Director-General (DG), Office of the Public Service Commissioner (OPSC), the interim Managing Director: SITA, as well as representatives from the Presidency, the DG: Department of Public Service and Administration (DPSA), and the DG: Department of Performance Monitoring and Administration (DPME). DG).
The OPSC was updating the meeting participants on the effectiveness of government support for service delivery, focusing on ICT. It had already been engaging some of the public-facing departments, like the Department of Home Affairs (DHA), the SAPS, the Department of Justice and Constitutional Development (DoJ&CD) and the Department of Mineral Resources and Energy (DMRE), which had raised some of the challenges they faced in delivering services to the citizens.
The objectives of the Roundtable were to:
Establish the challenges experienced by line departments in terms of the provision of ICT, and the impact thereof on service delivery.
Assess the challenges and underlying reasons and review the interventions put in place to address critical issues to provide ICT.
Propose measures to ensure effective and efficient service delivery
(See presentation for further details)
Discussion
Mr L Molala (ANC) said that while SITA was providing short-term solutions, they would hopefully present their broader plans to take the entity to the next level. The PSC had done commendable work, but it seemed that their efforts had come late in the Committee’s term, and it would have been better if they had acted earlier. The Committee should have seen changes in SITA and various departments by now, considering the historical challenges they had faced with certain departments regarding SITA. One important aspect that he believed the PSC should have addressed in their recommendations was the tendency of most departments to establish their own SITAs, which had been a longstanding issue. Nevertheless, it was pleasing that SITA was now determined to address these concerns.
Mr Molala said the PSC had mentioned the discrepancy between ICT professionals in the public and private sectors, which was related to the price model review and SITA's funding model. They had also proposed that the Treasury fund the modernisation of SITA's ICT. He questioned how these three elements were interrelated. Were the mini-ICT units in client departments also lacking capacity? If so, how did this relate to SITA and the price model review? Would the pricing model be adjusted upwards or downwards? He asked this question, considering the complaints received from clients who always wanted lower prices. However, would it be cheaper if SITA modernises and adopts advanced technology? Did the review account for the modernisation efforts and the resulting impact on ICT prices?
The PSC highlighted the limited funding available to departments for ICT modernisation. If the client departments could not provide funding assistance to SITA, as Treasury often required certain conditions for funding, the recommendation should include a role for client departments. If they did not provide more business to SITA, its sustainability would be compromised. Another issue was the procurement turnaround, which fell under SITA's responsibility. Considering the Auditor-General's (AG's) report on poor controls and widespread fruitless and wasteful expenditure, the reforms should include consequence management. Accountability should be emphasised, particularly in the supply chain. The AG's findings indicate that reforms alone may not be sufficient.
Mr Molala commended the PSC's presentation on system vulnerability and the issue of cybersecurity across both client departments and SOEs. However, he was concerned by the PSC's recommendation to devolve certain procurement powers to departments. He feared that this approach might lead to the establishment of numerous small SITAs within departments, potentially compromising national cybersecurity. He sought clarification on this matter.
Given that the chief operating officer (COO) would be appointed, could the Department provide an overall assessment of the culture and explain how they planned to effectively implement change management? This would help the Committee understand the direction SITA was heading. He hoped that the same level of assessment that applied to SITA's ICT structures in departments, would also be applied to client departments who filed complaints. It was important to determine the capacity levels of client departments and whether they were on par with, or lower than, SITA.
Ms T Bodlani (DA) said the PSC roundtable report had broadened the understanding of the expectation and mandate of SITA. It was a true reflection of the state of SITA and ICT infrastructure in South Africa, and offered solutions. The presidential fourth industrial revolution (4IR) Commission put the use of ICT back in the spotlight. However, changes and a lack of stability in the Ministries of Communications and Public Administration had hindered progress. Lack of political will and inconsistent leadership have contributed to the current state of SITA and ICT in South Africa.
Ms Bodlani said the weak state of the ICT environment indicated a failure of accounting officers and authorities to fulfil their responsibilities. The report on page 35 highlighted the challenges faced by chief information officers and the need for streamlined processes. Proper handling of ICT as a value chain was necessary to maximise investment and overcome the low investment percentage compared to the global average. One concern was the recommendation to relax procurement processes in the ICT sector. While reports of looting and poor consequence management were received, it became difficult to strike a balance between acquiring ICT infrastructure efficiently and ensuring accountability. Stricter monitoring processes and consequences for wrongdoing could have led to better returns on investment.
Ms Bodlani said ICT had the potential to create jobs, improve the quality of life, and this should be used to transform education in South Africa. Collaboration and knowledge sharing among government departments were crucial to harnessing the full benefits of ICT. Breaking down silos and barriers should be a priority. Lack of coordination, insufficient representation of information officers in decision-making, and uncompetitive salaries for ICT employees hindered progress. Involving all stakeholders was essential to meet technological priorities and fulfil the needs of citizens. Regarding legislation, the Deputy Minister requested clarity on the processes of amending the SITA Act. Empowering SITA with suitable legislation would enable proactive and efficient functioning, improving the funding model and procurement processes.
Ms Bodlani said in terms of oversight, a deeper understanding of the work of SITA and its impact was crucial. Desktop oversight alone was insufficient. The report emphasised poor controls and susceptibility to fraud, which should be addressed through effective processes and value for taxpayers' money. The goal should be to ensure the findings of the report translate into tangible improvements in the lives of South Africans. Measurable outcomes, effective oversight, and continuous evaluation would contribute to the success of ICT development and its positive impact on the country.
Responses
PSC
Ms Bacus said that as new commissioners, they had reviewed historical issues and subsequently identified flagship projects to pursue in the sixth administration. The current focus is on one of these flagship projects. Regarding costs, she emphasised the complexity of the issue, stating that the pricing would depend on a case-by-case analysis, as different clients and departments required varying solutions. Larger departments like Home Affairs and the Department of Health would require significant solutions, while smaller sectors like sports, arts, and recreation may require smaller ones. However, implementing simple in-house solutions with skilled ICT professionals could lead to substantial cost savings.
Ms Bacus mentioned that they had appointed an ICT director who had identified certain areas where in-house solutions could be implemented, instead of relying on external entities like SITA. This approach has proved effective in reducing costs. Funding for ICT projects could be reprioritised by departments, reallocating funds from areas of under-spending to support ICT initiatives. She acknowledged that the capacity of ICT structures within departments was currently insufficient, as highlighted by chief information officers and confirmed by their report. Simplifying procurement processes without compromising control was a possibility through engagement between SITA, Treasury and the DPSA.
A comment had been made in the plenary meeting that strategic plans often speak to 4IR, but unfortunately, the necessary human and financial resources were not being allocated to it. It would be critical for the PSC to see how SITA could pull together stakeholders and move in one direction. The PSC could support where possible and work alongside the Government Information Technology Office (GITO), the DPSA and Treasury towards a common purpose.
SITA
Ms Stella Bvuma, Deputy Chairperson, SITA said that as a board, they had observed two approaches to assist the government in incorporating digital concepts into their strategies. The first task was to align the goals for digital transformation with government's overall priorities, and appoint the right person to lead the entity. The second approach involved regularly evaluating the performance of digital programmes and adjusting them according to changing conditions.
She said legacy systems posed a significant challenge, as outdated systems in various government departments often lacked compatibility with modern technology. Upgrading and replacing these systems could be time-consuming and expensive. Interdepartmental coordination is vital for successful digitisation, but challenges may arise in aligning goals, priorities and resources among entities. Disparate systems, differing policies, varying levels of IT maturity, and increased cybersecurity risks, were some of the obstacles faced.
Ms Bvuma said protecting sensitive citizens' data from unauthorised access was a critical concern. SITA was committed to ensuring robust security measures across government systems by establishing and maintaining a strong cybersecurity framework. Skills and capacity development was another area of focus. Investing in training and upskilling programs was crucial to bridge the digital gap and successfully deliver services. Procurement processes and vendor management posed challenges as well. Complex procurement regulations could delay procurement cycles and managing relationships with external vendors could be difficult.
Ms Bvuma said that change management was critical during digital transformation initiatives. Resistance to change, lack of awareness, and inadequate change management strategies, could hinder the successful adoption of technologies. A holistic approach was necessary to address these challenges. Collaboration with various stakeholders was crucial. SITA must develop a comprehensive strategy to prioritise investments and adapt to emerging technologies and evolving needs to ensure successful government digitisation for the benefit of citizens.
Dr Mabaso addressed the issue of procurement turnaround times, and said that the lack of automation led to poor controls and opportunities for nefarious ends to come into the process. This was reflected in the AG's report on poor controls and fruitless and wasteful expenditure. Additionally, there was also the issue of poor contract management. All of these issues could be traced to the lack of automation.
SITA had partnered with Microsoft to automate the full end-to-end procurement value chain in the next two quarters, starting from 1 May. A parallel audit process would be implemented to ensure compliance at every step of the implementation. SITA would also work with the AG to look at the various controls as they were automated into the system with Microsoft. This would include consequence management processes to handle attempts to circumvent processes.
Dr Mabaso said that the role of group head of governance, risk, and compliance would be filled shortly to drive this at an executive level, and handle issues such as consequence management. In the short term, these were some of the measures being taken by SITA. In the long term, SITA was investigating artificial intelligence ( AI)-based procurement, where the entire process could be automated with minimal human intervention. However, basic automation must be achieved first before moving towards AI-based procurement. AI-based procurement was a version of procurement where there was no human intervention required in the process. As automation was implemented, bid evaluation and other processes would still require relevant levels of governance and committees. In the future, SITA envisions an AI-based procurement environment where there are only very few intervention points where a human is in the loop.
He said there were widespread weaknesses across various departments concerning ICT. This was not just a SITA problem, but a problem across various departments. SITA had the strongest capability compared to the departments, and was collaborating with the AG and Treasury to develop guidelines and controls for the process. In the past, departments were given exemptions to do something on their own, but when something goes wrong, they return to SITA for help. For example, in the Office of the Chief Justice, they had done something on their own and then encountered a cyber security issue. They had then come back to SITA for help to close those gaps.
Dr Mabaso said this model had not worked, and a new model was being developed where SITA would still be involved in mandatory services to ensure that guardrails were in place for cyber security and networks. This would allow for seamless and fast innovation without delegating powers away. This issue was receiving attention from SITA's lawyers, the AG, Treasury and the Department, to ensure that guidelines were written clearly.
Dr Mabaso also addressed the issue of change management, and said that clients wanted speed and price. To achieve this, SITA was building a culture of client-centricity and establishing forums to ensure that processes were client-centric. Various blogs around the SITA building talked about client-centricity, but in the actual processes, they were not client-centric. This was being addressed by bringing client-centricity back into the process of how SITA runs its organisation. Price could be lowered by having the right skills at SITA to deliver things at speed. Some successful models had been seen where this had been achieved.
He recommended that the Committee visit SITA's Cape Town office, which partnered with the University of Western Cape (UWC) to bring in 100 students as interns over three years. These interns had developed an inmate management system for the Western Cape Department of Correctional Services at a fraction of the cost of what had been done nationally with other providers. Dr Mabaso had met with all of them, and stated that this office was the future of what SITA was going to be. This was the future of how SITA would change its culture by bringing in young people and partnering with universities to refresh their thinking and partner them with experienced staff. This would ensure that there was a skills transfer.
Dr Mabaso also addressed the issue of collaboration, and stated that SITA had a technical consulting department that provided advisory services to government information technology officers (GITOs) and DGs before any tasking or government order. This department helps GITOs and DGs build their digital strategies and provides advisory services on their infrastructure. This role was currently not expanded to cover all GITOs, and was deployed on a case-by-case basis. However, in the new SITA, it would play a more coordinated and advisory role to the state. SITA wanted to bring in the concept of the chief information officer (CIO), and had a young lady who leads the department that would be building capacity to match every single GITO. This would ensure that every GITO had an advisor from SITA on a full-time basis to help them build their strategies and provide advisory services on how to transform their landscape. This would result in a more coordinated approach to transforming the state than it currently was, where things were very siloed.
Deputy Minister’s response
Mr Philly Mapulane, Deputy Minister of Communications and Digital Technologies, referred to the question from Ms Bodlani on the recommendation by the PSC to amend the SITA Act, and said that the process had already started. The Minister was looking into repurposing SITA and there had been progress, including developing a business case which had been consulted upon. The Development Bank of Southern Africa (DBSA) had given inputs, and the Department was working on developing a response. The intention was to respond to most of the issues that had come out of the roundtable process. The plan was to repurpose and reposition SITA as a state digital services company that would provide a bouquet of services to government and address shortcomings identified in the past, and in the roundtable report. Following Cabinet's determination, the Minister convened an inter-ministerial committee on digitisation to address the modernisation of ICT infrastructure across government and attend to issues of risk skilling.
Mr Mapulane said the issues around procurement challenges were also a concern and were being addressed with SITA to reduce delays in procurement matters. The goal was to repurpose SITA as a provider of choice, where clients receive services because SITA had given them value for money.
Follow-up questions
Ms N Mazzone (DA) expressed concern about adherence to the Public Finance Management Act (PFMA) and how the DCDT, together with stakeholders such as SITA, could get around problems in the PFMA without breaking or bending its rules. She emphasised the importance of adhering to the PFMA after state capture, and would like to delve more into how it could be adhered to without holding SITA to ransom for one whole financial year.
She mentioned her involvement in unravelling state capture and laying charges related to a lack of adherence to the PFMA. She was worried about looking for shortcuts around the PFMA, which could lead to dangerous situations where unintended or intended forms of corruption unfold. She had noticed that SITA had mentioned being held back on a financial year basis by the PFMA due to issues that had arisen. She asked how exactly the DCDT, together with stakeholders such as SITA, intended to get around problems in the PFMA without breaking or bending its rules.
Ms Mazzone said that AI was becoming increasingly prevalent, not only in South Africa, but worldwide. This was inevitable as technology advanced. However, AI could be quite scary for people whose jobs may become obsolete in a country with a high unemployment rate. She questioned whether the country had realised the technology and computer literacy rates needed for AI to take over certain assignments. She gave an example of motor vehicle licences, and suggested setting up a row of 30 machines where humans could input their data and receive their card within 20-30 seconds. This would mean no long queues or waiting periods, and would make life easier.
She asked how far along the country was with computer literacy rates for AI to become viable in assisting various departments. Where was the training for AI programmers, and where would it start? University graduates may not be interested in AI programming because they might think they have to be geniuses to enter the sector. She mentioned her own computer skills, and said it worried her that school education was not pushing towards the fourth industrial revolution and the completely technological world that it would bring. She questioned how many children had access to computer systems so that young South Africans could start learning from an early age. She shared her experience with a three-year-old learning to use an iPad better than she could within a few minutes. Children learn very fast, and teaching computer skills could be of great value and be added to curriculums.
On cyber security, which remained a big problem in South Africa and internationally, she asked if there were intercontinental and intergovernmental discussions about worldwide cyber security and what kind of interactions were happening with international organisations and governments to secure cyber security.
Ms Z Majozi (IFP) said departments blamed SITA for not helping them with developing and implementing digital systems. What were the challenges that SITA was experiencing? Was SITA helping these departments, and what challenges were they experiencing so that the blame did not always come back to SITA as the one giving them problems? It was correct for departments to have in-house ICT to create employment and put systems in place for all departments to run smoothly. She emphasised the importance of ICT in a department for capturing data and information.
Mr Molala said that in his budget speech, the Minister had spoken about addressing connectivity in rural areas by identifying schools, clinics and community centres. Could SITA comment on that and how far the process was? On the aging and obsolete ICT infrastructure and its possible cost, he questioned whether SITA would be financially sustainable, and what the impact would be without sufficient support from Treasury and client departments.
SITA's response
Dr Mabaso clarified that the intention was not to flood, short circuit or circumvent the PFMA process, but to automate as much of the procurement process as possible to facilitate adherence to that policy. He mentioned that perhaps the wording of being held back by PFMA was unfortunate. He said one of the gaps identified was that SITA did not have a procedure that interprets the PFMA and contextualises it to the organisation. Many state-owned companies have a procurement procedure manual (PPM) that interprets the PFMA and contextualises it to the organisation for clear operationalisation. This was one of the things that needed to be looked at as part of their policy universe review process, which was underway.
He said that AI did displace some jobs, but also created opportunities for many other jobs. The opportunity and upside were far higher than the downside, and their task as accounting officers was to ensure skills transformation. SITA's work skills plan (WSP) trains not just citizens or people who work for SITA, but also youths, small and medium enterprises (SMEs) and others, in collaboration with the Media Information and Communications Technologies (MICT) Sector Education and Training Authority (SETA) to ensure that they were skilled in this way and could take them to the future concerning the area of AI. SITA had a hackathon with the MIC SETA happening around 22 May, where they invited youths to participate in artificial intelligence because they believed that was the pathway they needed to take to add economic value to the ICT sector in the country.
There were intercontinental and international discussions around cybersecurity, and the US Consulate had invited SITA for a roundtable discussion on cybersecurity where they would be one of the keynote speakers to share lessons from the US and South Africa. SITA sends their people who run cybersecurity to international conferences to share information. Collaboration and sharing from an inter-departmental perspective were lacking. When he was engaging with various DGs and asking about cybersecurity, he could sense that it was not necessarily sitting at the right level in terms of receiving attention, understanding what it meant and, importantly, what to do to close the gaps that had been identified. He said that each GITO and each area was dealing with cybersecurity on their own, and more work needed to be done at SITA to create collaboration inter-departmentally around this issue. SITA had committed to working with various departments to evaluate a number of government sites from an ICT cybersecurity assessment perspective, identifying the gaps that were there, and then assisting the departments on how to close them. This was something they had identified as a gap and would be addressing.
Dr Mabaso referred to the challenges SITA experiences when working with various government departments as they try to execute their mandate. He said that this was a very important issue and most likely, on its own, could have its own PSC report regarding the quality of GITOs across various government departments, unclear requirements when engaging with various individuals, sometimes changing requirements, and other issues that impact delivery. It was not a one-sided thing, where people could just say it was SITA and it was a popular excuse. SITA, as part of their solution, was looking at this concept of the CIO, where they could ensure that their technical consulting team -- the pre-sales team that really provides advice -- could partner well with every GITO to ensure that by the time there was a tasking or a government order, that collaboration advisory had actually happened. He gave an example of a successful collaboration between SITA and the Regional Commissioner of Correctional Services in the Western Cape, where they had used young people who were interns from the UWC, working with the Department of Correctional Services, and built a solution at a fraction of the cost of what was found in the industry.
Dr Mabaso said that part of the challenge was that advisory and collaboration did not happen in many cases. A tasking letter was received that requested certain things because there was a need to discharge a budget within a certain time frame. This leads to many challenges. Fixing this part of the daily chain could solve half of all the problems in terms of ICT delivery. The focus was on beefing up the technical consulting function. If the team could advise every single tasking that came to SITA and the requirements were made clear, it would smooth many processes. Currently, there is not enough of this happening. SITA was trying to beef up from their side regarding having a CIO. Further work needed to be done to look at capacitating government departments and their GITO environments.
He responded on state capture and how SITA was working with the private sector to ensure there were checks and balances to prevent another form of capture from occurring. SITA wanted to move from being a reactive organisation to being more proactive in advising clients and engaging with the private sector. Instead of waiting for a tender to come from a client and then publishing something, SITA wanted to proactively vet various private sector solutions, and publish them in the SITA marketplace. An example was video conferencing solutions. SITA could engage Microsoft Teams, Zoom, Google Meet, and other providers to vet them for cybersecurity, data hosting, and whether they met government requirements from an ICT perspective within South Africa. These solutions could then be made available in the marketplace for clients to interact with competitively. This would also benefit SMEs by allowing them to publish their solutions in the marketplace after being pre-vetted by SITA. Clients could then choose between big tech solutions and SME-developed solutions that had been vetted for cybersecurity and other requirements. This could stimulate economic growth by providing cheaper, locally developed solutions that work just as well.
Dr Mabaso said it was believed that clients would start to choose SME-developed solutions as much as they would choose big tech solutions. The model being pivoted towards would play on both sides of the economy -- on the supply and demand sides. This would lead to stimulating economic growth and industry transformation, especially the participation of SMEs by putting them on an equal footing with big tech players. On the issue of the R6 billion mentioned in the Minister's budget vote speech around modernisation concerning broadband, a firm timeline could not be given at this time as it was a new development, and a thorough project plan was being put together. The team was working on high level planning to understand how to approach this. One thing being looked at was breaking it up into various regions so that a R6 billion tender was not issued all at once. Contracts would have clauses that talked to SME development and the upliftment of previously disadvantaged persons so that they could participate in the project.
Dr Mabaso said that SITA had experience rolling out broadband, with two projects currently running -- one in the Western Cape with their broadband 2.0 project, and one in Gauteng with the Gauteng Broadband Network (GBN) phase three project. Both were multi-billion rand programmes, where SITA was involved with the Western Cape and the Gauteng governments. SMEs had been involved, and hundreds of youths had been trained to roll out fibre and install access point names (APNs) in government buildings.
On ageing infrastructure and whether upgrading it might cause an issue of lack of sustainability from a SITA perspective, he said upgrading one data centre to tier three would cost R1.5 billion. SITA's cap was only about R1 billion in a good year. There was a significant cap and funding challenge that needed to be addressed. A new strategy around funding was being put together to ensure modernisation across the entire landscape. This included data centres and network infrastructure, where R400 million had already been invested, but more investment was needed. The private sector angle came from looking at co-investing, where it makes sense for private sector players to help lower the cost of running data centres and other infrastructure. Various options were being looked at to develop a proper proposal that could be taken to Treasury to address the challenge of ageing infrastructure.
Deputy Minister’s response
Deputy Minister Mapulane said the issue of the R6 billion raised had been clarified by the MD as being for the national broadband project of SITA. This should not be confused with the SA Connect project, which received a R1.3 billion allocation from National Treasury this financial year. The SA Connect project was part of the ambition to ensure 80% of South Africans had access to the internet in the next three years through connectivity and bridging the digital divide.
The President had developed an action plan to address recommendations arising from the Zondo Commission. The Department was working on procurement reform to prevent pillaging of state resources. SITA facilitated a lot of procurement and required procurement reforms. Comprehensive attention was given to the plan shared with the nation by the President.
Committee Minutes
The minutes of14 March; and 2, 5, 9 and 16 May were adopted.
The Chairperson adjourned the meeting.
