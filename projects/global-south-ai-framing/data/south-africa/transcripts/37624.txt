In a virtual meeting, the Portfolio Committee on Communications and Digital Technologies met with the Auditor-General of South Africa (AGSA), Department of Communications and Digital Technologies (DCDT), Government Communications System (GCIS), Media Development and Diversity Agency (MDDA), Independent Communications Authority of South Africa (ICASA), Films and Publications Board (FPB), State Information Technology Agency (SITA), .za Domain Name Association (ZADNA), Sentech, Broadband Infraco (BBI), National Electronic Media Institute of South Africa (NEMISA), Universal Service Access Agency of South Africa (USAASA), and the Universal Service Access Fund (USAF) on the 2022/23 annual reports.
The Committee heard that the FBP, NEMISA and Sentech received unqualified audit opinions with no findings for the 2022/23 financial year, resulting in clean audits. They submitted financial statements that were credible, reliable and free from material misstatements. They complied with key legislation in conducting day-to-day operations. The DCDT, ICASA, BBI and USAASA received unqualified audits with findings. The DCDT and the entities produced financial statements that could be corrected. SITA and USAF received qualified audits with findings. They could not produce credible and reliable financial statements. Postbank, the South African Post Office (SAPO) and the South African Broadcasting Corporation (SABC) received disclaimers of audit opinion. They could not provide evidence for amounts and disclosures in the financial statements.
The SABC achieved 32 percent of its targets. The DCDT achieved 77 percent. Sentech achieved 87 percent. ICASA achieved 88 percent. Postbank achieved 20 percent. SAPO achieved 18 percent. USAASA achieved 100 percent. USAF achieved 25 percent. SITA achieved 44 percent of its targets.
Members were told that there were large amounts of irregular expenditure in the financial statements of some of the entities. Some were not implementing AGSA recommendations. Action plans were not monitored properly. The absence of permanent leadership in some organisations leads to many things going wrong. There had been many resignations at the MDDA.
Members asked for more details on the issue of Sentech fees imposed on community radio stations and the progress in obtaining additional funding for the MDDA. They were worried about SAPO and Postbank receiving disclaimer audits. The Committee congratulated FPB, NEMISA and Sentech for receiving clean audits. Members said that if NEMISA could do so well, other entities could too. They raised concerns about the lack of consequences for those responsible for financial mismanagement, poor governance structures, unfilled senior posts and the BBI debt to Eskom and Transnet. Concerns were expressed that the state was being looted because people knew they could get away with it and that urgent intervention was needed to improve oversight mechanisms.
Meeting report
AGSA Briefing on Audit Outcomes: of Communications and Digital Technologies Portfolio
Mr Andries Sekgetho, Business Executive, Auditor-General of South Africa (AGSA), said the AGSA had a key role in working towards a culture shift in the public sector which would result in greater accountability and delivery of services to citizens. Everyone had a role to play in achieving this. The executive authority had to provide strategic leadership on the policies and agreements reached in Parliament. Accounting officers in departments had to ensure that the strategic aspirations of government were achieved. There were also oversight structures, including the parliamentary committees and the Department of Planning, Monitoring and Evaluation. At the centre of it all was the AGSA and it was talking to all of these stakeholders to try to influence all the key role players in the accountability ecosystem.
Mr Sekgetho said there were significant challenges at some of the DCDT entities. Some audit outcomes had regressed to disclaimers. These entities were supposed to be self-sustainable and to contribute meaningfully to the economy by implementing turnaround plans. There had been instability in senior management.
Audit outcomes and recommendations
Ms Surette Taljaard, Senior Manager, AGSA, said that the Film and Publications Board (FPB), the National Electronic Media Institute of South Africa (NEMISA) and Sentech received clean audits. Best practices included proper reviews of financial statements, performance reports and adherence to laws and regulations.
The audit outcomes at the Independent Communications Authority of South Africa (ICASA) and Broadband Infraco (BBI) remained unqualified, with material non-compliance matters. Preventative controls should be implemented.
Management at the Department of Communications and Digital Technologies (DCDT) did not have adequate internal controls in collecting, collating and verifying the data related to the Broadcasting Digital Migration (BDM) project. This resulted in material findings on the reliability of the reported performance information. Management should enhance oversight of the BDM project.
Audit outcomes at the Universal Service and Access Agency of South Africa (USAASA) improved due to the effectiveness of action plans developed to address previous qualification areas. However, internal financial controls should be enhanced. There were still material misstatements and a lack of consequence management for fruitless and wasteful expenditure.
The Universal Service and Access Fund (USAF) improved from a disclaimer of opinion. However, inventory limitations resulted in a qualified audit outcome.
The audit outcome at the State Information Technology Agency (SITA) remained unchanged at qualified with findings.
The South African Post Office (SAPO) again received a disclaimer of opinion. Significant findings were raised on the quality of supply chain management. Financial sustainability remained an issue.
Postbank again received a disclaimer of opinion. It did not achieve its planned target of becoming a retail and business bank. It reported a R2 billion loss in the 2022/23 financial year, and its financial position weakened. It continued to be vulnerable to cyber breaches. It recently took over cash pay points from SAPO which increased fraud at Postbank. The AGSA could not determine the full extent of losses. Management should implement key reconciliatory controls and complete investigations. Postbank had recently filled key executive positions.
The South African Broadcasting Corporation (SABC) regressed due to going-concern matters that remained a challenge. Management should direct initiatives driving the turnaround strategy.
The SABC achieved 32 percent of its targets. The DCDT achieved 77 percent, Sentech 87 percent, ICASA 88 percent, Postbank 20 percent, SAPO 18 percent, USAASA 100 percent, USAF 25 percent and SITA 44 percent.

Broadband migration
Digital migration was behind target. Installation of set-top boxes was not advancing as it should to allow for spectrum release. The oversight and controls were not sufficient. The DCDT should monitor all role players and keep them accountable. Sentech and the USAF must ensure that all set-top box installations are on track.
Financial health
SAPO recorded a net loss after tax for the year ended 31 March 2023 of R2.2 billion (2022: R2.2 billion) and a negative net asset value of R4.4 billion (R6.4 billion). Revenue decreased by 23 percent from R3 billion in the prior year to R2.3 billion. Mail revenue continued to be low due to a decline in mail and logistics volumes and loss of customers. The mail revenue business represented 61 percent of total company revenue.
The SABC reported a deficit of R1.1 billion for the year. The creditors’ payment period was 115 days. This meant that the SABC was not settling its debts when they became due.
BBI’s loss for the year increased from R119 million in 2022 to R121 million in 2023. There was significant doubt about the ability to continue operating as a going concern in the foreseeable future.
At Sentech, there was over reliance on one customer. There was a sustainability risk due to the lack of diversification as well as the fact that Sentech was in the process of acquiring 74 percent of BBI. Due diligence was completed, and the transaction was anticipated to be concluded by the end of September 2023.
Ms Joyce Nkonyana, Senior Manager, AGSA, said that some state-owned enterprises were not achieving financial sustainability. These included SAPO, Postbank and the SABC. There were also a number of repeat findings of non-compliance with legislation.
Irregular expenditure
An audit qualification on the completeness of irregular expenditure at the SABC was resolved by changes in the reporting framework, but the SABC still needed to ensure investigations took place and that there was a proper consequence management process to address the irregular expenditure identified.
SITA  incurred R452 million in irregular expenditure. The main contributor was expenditure on ongoing multi-year contracts incurred in prior years.
Postbank incurred R165 million in irregular expenditure. The main causes were approval of agreements without following proper supply chain management processes and the purchase of a software license which was not used.
SAPO incurred R155 million in irregular expenditure, due mainly to transgressions where contracts amounting to R62 million were amended without appropriate approvals. There were also a number of material irregularities.
MDDA and GCIS
Ms Taljaard also briefed the Committee on the audit outcomes for the Media Development and Diversity Agency (MDDA) and the Government Communication and Information System (GCIS).
The Media Development and Diversity Agency (MDDA) regressed to a qualified audit with findings. There was a significant regression in the financial statements and compliance with laws and regulations. This was attributed largely to the loss of key senior personnel during the 2022/23 financial year and a lack of controls over the daily and monthly processing of transactions in the general ledger and suppliers ledger. Procurement processes were not being followed as required by National Treasury and supply chain management policy. Management should implement strict measures to improve controls and monitor that they are operating effectively. The MDDA achieved 74 percent of its targets. One of the key targets not achieved was the evaluation of funded projects.
The GCIS audit outcome improved to an unqualified one without findings. The internal controls should be consistently reviewed and monitored to ensure that a clean administration is sustained. The GCIS achieved or over-achieved 97 percent of its targets. No material findings were identified.

DCDT Annual Report 2022/23
Deputy Minister’s remarks
Mr Philly Mapulane, Deputy Minister of Communications and Digital Technologies, said the DCDT  achieved 27 out of the 35 planned targets, which equates to a 77 percent overall achievement. The DCDT received an unqualified audit opinion. It spent 90 percent of the appropriated funds.
One of the key objectives was the establishment of an artificial intelligence institute. The DCDT partnered successfully with the University of Johannesburg and the Tshwane University of Technology to host two of the hubs of the institute. It hoped that more would be established through collaborating with the institutions of higher learning.
Analogue broadcasting was no longer available in five provinces. An agreement had been reached after a very lengthy period of negotiations. The first stage was to switch off analogue broadcasting above 895 MHz by 31 July, which had been done. The Minister switched off the last analogue transmitter. This agreement was important because it allowed the spectrum that was auctioned in March 2022 by the Independent Communications Authority of South Africa (ICASA) to be immediately available to mobile network operators. In this way, all the money that had been pledged in the auction of the spectrum would be received. The second stage of the agreement was to allow for dual elimination to take place until the end of 2024. After that, analogue would be a thing of the past. Briefing on DCDT entities
Mr Omega Shelembe, Deputy Director-General: ICT Enterprise and Public Entity Oversight, DCDT, took the Committee through the consolidated report.
BBI
There were 20 targets, and 14 targets were achieved. The overall performance rate was 70 percent. BBI received an unqualified audit opinion with emphasis of matter. FPB
There were 13 targets, of which 12 were achieved. The FPB achieved a clean audit for the second consecutive financial year. It developed a new fit-for-purpose organisational structure. It finalised regulatory instruments to be able to function as an online regulator pursuant to the objective of the amendment act. It appointed an enforcement committee, chaired by a retired judge, and established rules to effectively address non-compliance cases. The challenges included inadequate financial resources, a decline in materials submitted for classification and digital transformation. Non-achievement of the one target was attributed to the existing manual classification process. The FPB would develop a games matrix to reduce classification turnaround times on games.
ICASA
There were 48 targets and 42 were achieved. The overall performance rate was 87.5 percent. The highlights included publishing the eighth state of the ICT sector in South Africa report; conducting public hearings to amend the service charter regulations; evaluating applications for community sound broadcasting licences; publishing radio frequency spectrum assignment plans for public consultation; and the review of key regulations on issues such as the end user subscriber service charter. The challenges were an incomplete council and an underfunded mandate. Irregular expenditure amounted to R34.2 million, increasing the opening balance to R234.3 million at the end of the 2022/23 financial year. ICASA would develop an audit action plan to address findings, particularly in relation to payment of invoices within 30 days. It will implement consequence management, fill a financial specialist vacancy and aim for early completion of the annual financial statements to allow enough time for detailed reviews.
NEMISA
There were 20 targets of which 19 were achieved and one was not achieved. NEMISA achieved a clean audit for the first time. A digital skills colloquium and hybrid datathon were successfully held with participation by various provinces. NEMISA continued with its organisational culture change, ending the year with the production of an organisational efficiency report. During the year under review, NEMISA had irregular expenditure amounting to R483 958 and wasteful expenditure amounted to R3 000. NEMISA’s would improve management of performance information by, among others, introducing monthly reporting and verification and auditing of performance information, as well as quarterly audit readiness meetings. It would fully implement the audit action plan to ensure that all the external and internal audit findings were addressed and did not recur in the next audit.
Postbank
There were 14 targets of which only three were achieved. The overall performance rate was 21 percent. The AGSA had raised a lot of audit findings and repeat findings,  mostly relating to weaknesses in internal controls. AGSA issued a disclaimer of audit opinion, the third in a row. The net loss was due to increased expected credit loss to cover SAPO exposure and SA Social Security Agency (SASSA) project costs. Before provisions for impairment, Postbank recorded a profit of R194 million. Postbank's capital adequacy ratio decreased to 27 percent compared to 55 percent in the previous year, below the minimum required by the Prudential Authority. All the executive positions were vacant during the period under review. They were only filled after the financial year had ended. As at the end of July 2023, the Postbank had filled the positions of Chief Audit Executive (CAE), Company Secretary, CEO and CFO. During the year under review, irregular expenditure amounted to R165 million and wasteful expenditure amounted to R611 000. The majority of the audit findings were recurring. Postbank has appointed a service provider to assist in reconciling the deposit accounts in the Integrated Grant Payment System (IGPS) and the accounting system.
SAPO
There were 13 targets of which 11 were not achieved. The overall performance rate was 15 percent. The main challenges included the distressed financial position, a net loss of R2.2 billion, provisional liquidation, AGSA’s disclaimer of opinion and leadership instability. The majority of the irregular expenditure was caused by SAPO not following supply chain management processes. The majority of the fruitless and wasteful expenditure were caused by interest and penalties for late payments. There was insufficient appropriate audit evidence that disciplinary steps were taken against officials who had incurred fruitless and wasteful expenditure.
Sentech
There were 15 targets of which 12 were achieved. The overall performance rate was 87 percent. The entity had a clean audit report. For the Broadcasting Digital Migration (BDM) project, Sentech had already switched off five provinces. Sentech was in the process of acquiring 74 percent of BBI. Due diligence studies were completed, and it was anticipated the transaction would be concluded by the end of September 2023. Sentech showed positive progress with its non-financial performance. However, there is still much regression in their financial performance. Sentech was still operating analogue and digital television simultaneously pending the announcement of the analogue switch off (ASO). This created dual illumination incremental costs. For the 2022/23 financial year, there was a funding deficit of R40 million for these costs, which contributed to the loss and burdened the fiscus. The SABC was unable to pay Sentech in full due to its financial crisis. This negatively impacted Sentech’s financial sustainability and its ability to deliver services at the agreed level. The continuous power cuts had a possible impact in the delivery of services to customers. There had been increased failures in back-up generators, leading to reduced productivity, non-achievement of set targets and increased cost of operations. Sentech had made submissions for stakeholder intervention on the issues pertaining to BDM costs, the BBI acquisition and SABC debt. Remedial plans for the non-achieved targets had been put in place.
SABC
There were 25 targets of which 17 were not achieved. The overall performance rate was 36 percent. It launched an over-the-top technology (OTT) digital streaming platform called SABC+. The platform performed well operationally, with no significant downtimes recorded. The SABC continued to work to strengthen internal controls. A decline in television audiences affected advertising revenue. A failure to implement revenue improvement plans resulted in cash flow challenges. The severe liquidity challenges resulted in technical insolvency. AGSA issued a disclaimer audit opinion due to going-concern issues. Irregular expenditure amounted to R34.8 million during the year under review. The SABC board would expedite a revenue repositioning strategy. It would submit an audit action plan to the DCDT by the end of October 2023.
SITA
There were 18 targets, of which 10 were not achieved. The overall performance rate was 44.4 percent. The agency was in a good financial position. Most of the performance-related challenges are in procurement and the industry transformation programme. USAASA
All five targets were achieved and USAASA received an unqualified audit opinion. USAASA implemented 77 percent of the audit action plan activities. There were higher capacity constraints due to vacancies in executive and senior management resulting from the suspension of six senior officials and the dismissal of one additional senior staff member. During the period under review, USAASA incurred R7.895 million in irregular expenditure and R114 000 in fruitless and wasteful expenditure. USAASA closed with a balance of R87.094 million in irregular expenditure and R3.833 million in fruitless and wasteful expenditure. USAF

ZADNA

He said NEMISA’s teachers’ assistants programme had a significant impact across the country, including rural areas. It was changing the lives of people on the ground. The Committee acknowledged the clean audit of the FPB and the changes being implemented for it to become an online content regulator. This also gave assurance for the elections in 2024. The Committee acknowledged all the achievements of all the entities.
He said that the next day’s meeting would deal with the annual reports of Postbank, SAPO and the SABC. Some of these matters regarding the SABC could not be shared in public because they were commercially sensitive. Competitors might not be subjected to the same laws as the SABC. So, there had to be a  closed meeting to deal with high level information. However, if a meeting was partially closed, South Africans should still be given a summary of the discussions. The SABC was a public broadcaster. Certain things would be public such as the utilisation of the bailout funds which had nothing to do with the commercial side. The Committee had asked for guidance from the House Chair on the rules for a closed or partially closed meeting. It would be for the Committee to decide whether to close the meeting. This was nothing new. It had been done before in the case of SAPO. The SABC had requested a closed meeting.
Ms Mazzone said that she had written to the Chairperson and the House Chair regarding the closing of the meeting. She said the notice did not indicate that the Committee would decide at the time of the meeting. It indicated that a decision had already been made that there would be a partially closed meeting. It had now been explained that this had happened before with SAPO before. It was not to the benefit of the country or the Committee to ever have anything done in secret. It was the SABC’s constitutional mandate to broadcast to the public of South Africa and therefore even commercially sensitive matters could not be considered to be of a secret nature, definitely not when they were related to public funds and the South African public. She placed it on record that the DA would attend all of the meetings the next day because they had a constitutional mandate to represent those people who elected them to office. The DA placed on record its extreme concern and dismay about any part of the meeting being closed to the public.
The Chairperson said Ms Mazzone’s concerns would be placed on record. The meeting would only be partially closed. Nothing was raised when this was done for  SAPO, which is also a government-funded entity. If this discussion ensued the next day, the Committee would handle it. It was important for those following the discussions of the Committee to have this clarity. The journalists seemed to understand in this regard. There would be a summary at the end for the public. They would know what was going on.
The meeting was adjourned.
