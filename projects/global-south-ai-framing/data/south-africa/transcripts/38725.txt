Meeting Summary
Video
The Portfolio Committee held their final virtual meeting of the Sixth Parliament, at which the primary focus was on adopting the Committee's Legacy Report -- a document outlining their successes and hurdles over their five-year term.
The report highlighted the achievements in processing key legislation, reducing the DNA backlog in investigations, and conducting police station oversight visits. However, it also acknowledged challenges like infrastructure limitations within the SAPS, legislative processing delays, and limited time for in-depth report reviews. Recommendations for the incoming Parliament included strengthening oversight of civilian bodies, improving legislative drafting capabilities, addressing private security industry regulations, and continuing to monitor ongoing investigations.
While adopting the report, Members also discussed outstanding issues. These included incidents involving a lack of police response, ongoing gang violence in the Western Cape, and concerns about private security companies exceeding their authority.
The Chairperson concluded the meeting by allowing different stakeholders to share their farewell messages, as this was the last official meeting of the Committee under the Sixth Administration.
Meeting report
Chairperson's opening remarks
After recording apologies for the absence from three Members, as well as the Minister and Deputy Minister of Police, the Chairperson said the Committee would move on to item three, which involved opening remarks concerning the sixth parliamentary term. He described it as fast-paced and unprecedented, particularly due to the disruptions caused by the COVID-19 pandemic, which had led to virtual and hybrid Committee meetings. Despite challenges such as the loss of the National Assembly building in a fire, the Committee continued its duties, adapting to virtual sessions, arguably increasing public accessibility. He highlighted the unique focus of the Committee, where politics were often set aside to strengthen police oversight. He expressed humility at the professionalism, courage and decency displayed by the Committee Members.
Over the past five years, the Committee has diligently fulfilled its constitutional mandate, monitoring government department performance, processing legislation, and facilitating public participation. Achievements included adopting budget reports, conducting oversight visits, and engaging with stakeholders. Despite facing challenges, progress had been made, which the Chairperson hoped would be built upon by the next Committee.
Acknowledging the loss of three Committee Members, the Chairperson expressed gratitude for the opportunity to serve, and thanked various stakeholders for their support. He noted that the legacy report would be handed over to the Seventh Parliament, emphasising the importance of continuity in oversight work.
As the meeting marked the last of the sixth democratic Parliament, the agenda included adopting the legacy report. The Chairperson said that he would allow representatives of departments and political parties to speak briefly towards the end of the meeting, anticipating that it might be their last meeting unless there was an emergency.
He wished the Members fruitful discussions and proceeded to the next agenda item, which involved the minutes of the previous meeting and other matters outlined in the agenda.
Adoption of minutes
Minutes of 20 March
Mr A Shaik Emam (NFP) pointed out a spelling error regarding one of the attendees. The Chairperson acknowledged this, and inquired if there were any further amendments.
Ms M Gomba (ANC) proposed the adoption of the minutes with the amendments, which was seconded by Ms B Marekwa (ANC).
The Chairperson questioned whether the adopted minutes accurately reflected the discussions from the previous meeting. Moving on, he directed the discussion towards matters arising, starting with feedback on the resolutions and inviting Members to raise any additional matters. He specifically mentioned addressing matters as captured in the minutes, particularly resolutions concerning the National Commissioner.
Matters arising
The Chairperson requested a review of the resolutions, specifically focusing on the resolutions related to the National Commissioner, highlighting the resolution to provide a report on the progress made with investigations regarding a judgment mentioned by a Member. He said he had not yet received any response from the National Commissioner regarding the two cases raised in the previous meeting on 6 March, and this issue remained unresolved as of the current meeting on 27 March. He asked the National Commissioner if they were now prepared to respond to these matters.
Gen Fannie Masemola, National Commissioner, South African Police Service (SAPS), acknowledged the matters raised. He assured the Committee that another official would provide an update regarding the issue concerning Judge Daniel Thulare and the related investigation. The SAPS had presented a report to the Minister. There were issues that were pending finalisation before the report could be presented to Members. He mentioned that it was a lengthy report.
Addressing another issue concerning a security company in George, Gen Masemola said a team had been dispatched to investigate the matter. A preliminary report had been provided, and a case had been opened and investigated, although it was declined for prosecution by the public prosecutor. However, efforts were ongoing to locate individuals allegedly mistreated by the SAPS and/or the security company. He noted the need for a compliance inspection of the security company, and expressed concern over potential overreach in their operations.
Maj Gen Puleng Dimpane, Chief Financial Officer (CFO), SAPS, referred to the matter of transfers and, acknowledged its importance, and clarified that all transfers had been conducted in accordance with existing policy. However, if a written report on transfers was required, it would be provided. She mentioned that the human resources (HR) department was in the process of finalising the report, and once completed, it could be submitted in writing. She gave an assurance that all transfers and promotions were carried out within the framework of existing policies within the organisation.
Mr O Terblanche (DA) said there was another unresolved issue regarding a case reported at the Robertson police station. He said there had been a delay of about a week, during which the individual involved had not received any police assistance. The complaint revolved around the lack of police involvement and inadequate treatment of the matter. Despite previous inquiries, there had been no satisfactory response from the police, and the matter remained unresolved.
Gen Masemola responded, and indicated that a response to the mentioned issue would be provided later during the meeting, once it was confirmed whether the relevant province would be responding.
Mr Shaik Emam brought attention to the unresolved issue of the murder Ayob Mungalee, which he had previously raised without receiving a response. He mentioned that he had been informed about several community members in Eldorado Park who had allegedly been murdered by gangsters, including Mungalee, over a year ago. Despite this, there had been no information forthcoming on the matter.
The Chairperson directed the discussion towards matters arising from the previous minutes, emphasising the need to stay focused. He noted that the National Commissioner had mentioned this.
Ms N Peacock (ANC) then brought up a point from the minutes, saying that a certain item (point 11), had not been adequately captured. She elaborated on a request made by Ms Marekwa regarding the need for a report on promotions and placement, along with an examination of related policies. She emphasised the importance of addressing this request from the last meeting.
The Chairperson agreed that this should be included in the return submission, as mentioned by Gen Masemola, to ensure that all Members had a comprehensive understanding.
Mr V Gericke (EFF) sought clarification on whether he could discuss issues related to the report provided by the National Commissioner concerning the George matter, or if it would be addressed after the legacy report.
The Chairperson reassured Mr Gericke that he was free to discuss any concerns regarding that report at that moment.
Mr Gericke then addressed the National Commissioner, thanking him for the feedback provided. However, he expressed concerns about the comprehensiveness of the report, suggesting that it might not be fully updated. He highlighted the shocking revelation that the individuals involved in the case purported to be a security company but were, in fact, operating as a neighbourhood watch. He mentioned that many people had been interviewed, and statements were available, contradicting the previous assertion that no statements had been submitted.
He clarified that he himself was not interviewed, but his brother’s daughter, who was also involved in the incident, had been questioned. He stressed the seriousness of the situation, stating that these companies were being used to promote racist objectives in the area. He urged the National Commissioner to thoroughly investigate the matter to prevent its recurrence.
He also acknowledged the Commissioner's initiation of the process with the Private Security Industry Regulatory Authority (PSIRA) to inspect these security companies, but stressed the need for further exploration by the police. He appreciated the swift intervention by the Commissioner and the provincial Commissioner's actions in confronting the relevant individuals. However, he emphasised the importance of closure on the matter and awaited further developments.
SAPS's response
Gen Masemola responded by assuring the Chairperson that a report on placements and promotions would be made available. Although SAPS had not prepared presentations for that day, they would have ensured the reports were sent due to their lengthiness.
Regarding the situation in Westbury and Eldorado Park, he acknowledged the ongoing killings and mentioned sending Deputy National Commissioners and provincial Commissioners to the area to form a team. Two arrests had been made in connection with the recent murders. The SAPS was considering involving the South African Defence Force (SADF) for assistance in patrolling these areas, pending approval from the President. The SAPS would also be reviewing the team's deployment weekly and considering increasing personnel from other provinces to control the situation and apprehend the perpetrators.
He also mentioned appointing a provincial coordinator for anti-gang activities in Gauteng and in the Western Cape, and a coordinator for gang-related issues in other areas. He acknowledged the need for updated information, and agreed that the reported actions of the neighbourhood watch group were overreaching, as security companies typically did not have jurisdiction in public places unless contracted by relevant authorities. He assured the Committee that the SAPS would continue working on the issue.
The Chairperson reiterated the resolution that the National Commissioner would submit a report on Judge Thulare's judgment, and another official would provide a report on promotions, transfers and related matters. He urged that these reports should be submitted by the end of the following week, despite it being the last official meeting, as the Committee remained active until 21 May. He said that a special or emergency meeting would be convened before that date if necessary.
Committee's final draft Legacy Report
The Chairperson proposed proceeding to consideration of the Legacy Report. He welcomed input from all present, including officials from the SAPS, PSIRA, the Directorate for Priority Crime Investigation (DPCI), and the Secretariat. While priority would be given to Members, he encouraged contributions from others if they believed something was missing or needed improvement in the Legacy Report.
Ms Nicolette van Zyl-Gous, Committee Researcher, proceeded to outline the achievements and challenges encountered by the Portfolio Committee on Police during the Sixth Parliament, underscoring the need for attention in the Seventh Administration.
During the Sixth Parliament, the Committee had achieved several milestones and identified key challenges that required attention in the Sixth Administration. Significant legislation, including the Criminal Law (Forensic Procedures) Amendment Act of 2022, and the Protection of Constitutional Democracy against Terrorist and Related Activities Amendment Act of 2022, has been processed. The Committee had also focused on reducing the DNA backlog, strengthening the fight against terrorism, and overseeing the implementation of critical legislation.
She detailed the achievements of the Committee, such as the reduction of the DNA backlog and the implementation of forensic procedures. Challenges faced, including infrastructure development, the digitisation of firearm licensing, and staffing issues, were also identified.
The Committee had conducted oversight visits to areas such as Cape Town and Pretoria, making various recommendations, including personnel relocation, investigation into lease renewal corruption, and modernisation of the Central Firearm Registry (CFR). Stakeholder engagement with bodies like the Commission for Gender Equality (CGE) and the South Africa Human Rights Commission (SAHRC), was emphasised.
However, challenges included delays in legislation drafting, technical and operational issues in bill processing, and limitations in report consideration time. The Committee stressed the need for better utilisation of Budgetary Review and Recommendations Reports (BRRRs), and monitoring implementation progress.
Ms Van Zyl-Gous recommended solutions to third-party dependencies, budget allocation reviews, and disciplinary regulation strengthening. Improving civilian oversight bodies, enhancing legislative drafting capacity, and addressing private security industry challenges, were also suggested.
Looking ahead to the Seventh Administration, the Committee outlined its recommendations, including consideration of BRRRs, progress monitoring on investigations, like Lt Colonel Kinnear's death, and prioritising legislation like the SAPS Amendment Bill. Conducting inquiries into the July 2021 unrest and engaging regularly with counterpart committees, was emphasised.
Ms Van Zyl-Gous also outlined oversight visits, interventions, and legislative processes during the Sixth Parliament, concluding by stressing the importance of addressing outlined issues and implementing recommendations for effective policing and governance.
In additional notes, the document provided a comprehensive overview of the Portfolio Committee's activities and recommendations during the Sixth Parliament, covering the challenges, recommendations, and legislative processes. Challenges faced by the SAPS, including technical issues, third-party dependencies and capacity problems were highlighted, along with recommendations for improvement.
For the Seventh Parliament, the document emphasised continued monitoring of recommendations and investigations, the use of BRRRs, and regular engagement with counterpart committees.
Discussion
The Chairperson expressed gratitude to Ms Van Zyl-Gous for her presentation. He acknowledged the detailed nature of the report, albeit in a summarised form, and anticipated that it would undergo further refinement to address any repetitions or typos. However, he emphasised the importance of focusing on the content rather than minor errors. He urged the Members to direct their attention to the substance of the report, ensuring its accuracy and relevance.
Mr Shaik Emam commended Ms Van Zyl-Gous for her excellent work. He raised concerns regarding the challenges faced by the police, particularly regarding the issuance of 24-hour liquor licences by local governments, which he believed contributed to difficulties for law enforcement. He suggested revisiting the licensing regulations to address this issue.
He emphasised the need for better protection for police officers, highlighting the risks they face and the importance of providing them with adequate equipment beyond firearms. He called for the Seventh Parliament to address issues related to police resources, including human resources and medical support, as well as the heavy workload faced by detectives.
He also touched upon the issue of promotions within the police force, suggesting that qualified individuals who could not be accommodated due to rank limitations should be compensated financially, based on their experience and qualifications. He also stressed the importance of providing benefits to police officers, and reiterated the importance of addressing these concerns for the well-being and effectiveness of law enforcement personnel.
Ms Marekwa commended the office, administration, and content advisor for their work in structuring the report, emphasising its assistance in highlighting key issues raised by the Committee. The report provided clear directions for various stakeholders, including the police and different secretariats, regarding their roles and responsibilities in addressing pertinent matters.
She stressed the need for a budget emphasis, and highlighted concerns about housing schemes for police officers. Ensuring that officers had adequate housing in safe environments conducive to their work was important. She called for engagements with government entities to address housing needs and to ensure that the budget adequately covered essential items such as protective gear and vehicles for police officers.
She underscored the importance of effective programmes like Operation Shanela and others in combating crime, and said there was a need for continued funding to support these initiatives. She also raised concerns about the proliferation of guns in communities and the presence of fake or illegal security guards, calling for stricter regulation and clear directives for security companies to prevent criminal activities.
She commended the comprehensiveness of the report and expressed the hope that the next administration would consider its directives seriously. She stressed the ongoing nature of police work, and wished them success in their efforts to secure the country.
Mr B Golding (DA) expressed his gratitude for the detailed presentation. He said he would not introduce anything new, but would highlight a few key points.
He referred to a recommendation stemming from their oversight visit to Limpopo, suggesting the drafting of a national water plan or strategy. This recommendation had been repeated during a meeting with public works, where it was noted that significant funds were being allocated to water-related initiatives. Given the increasing scarcity of water resources and the growing focus on alternative energy sources such as solar power, he stressed the importance of comprehensive planning.
He also highlighted the need for proper planning around alternate services like energy and water, emphasising that this should not be done in an
ad hoc
manner, but rather integrated into broader planning efforts. He underscored the budgetary implications of such planning, reiterating the importance of the recommendation for comprehensive planning in all precincts.
Mr Golding addressed the issue of lease management and infrastructure maintenance, suggesting that the Department should explore alternative methods outside of public works. There was a need to recommend to the Seventh Parliament that agencies like public works and the State Information Technology Agency (SITA) be called more regularly to account for their progress, particularly concerning implementing the integrated case docket management system. He stressed that waiting for annual reports was insufficient, given the time-sensitive nature of these issues and the potential delays caused by external agencies.
Mr Terblanche expressed his gratitude to the staff, specifically acknowledging Ms Van Zyl-Gous for her outstanding report, which he described as comprehensive and informative. He noted the progress made since the beginning of their term in 2019, and said that while police work was ongoing, the next Parliament would need to continue building on their achievements.
Acknowledging the challenges mentioned, such as budgetary constraints, he said it was important to focus on small steps to make progress. He highlighted the need for the police to embrace new technologies and equipment to propel them further into the 21st century, including advancements in artificial intelligence.
He commended the Committee for addressing serious issues, and expressed optimism that they would leave the police in a better situation than they had found them. He stressed the importance of the collective effort in achieving this goal, and wished the police well in their endeavours. He recommended that the police work to break down silos, not only within their organisation, but also within the broader government. There was a need for an integrated approach and partnership policing with the community to effectively tackle challenges.
Mr Terblanche repeated his hope for visible progress in the country's policing efforts.
Ms Z Majozi (IFP) expressed her agreement with the points raised in the report. She emphasised the importance of ensuring that the inquiry into the July unrest transitioned smoothly to the Seventh Parliament, highlighting the need to address critical issues promptly.
Regarding the implementation of digital transformation within the SAPS, she said it should be a top priority for the Seventh Parliament, and SITA should be held accountable for delays when it comes to implementing this. She advocated a shift from manual paper-based processes to digital methods for capturing cases, urging swift action in this regard.
She also suggested continuing operations aimed at retrieving illegal firearms, noting their effectiveness in reducing firearms circulating in society. She proposed reintroducing such operations in the Seventh Parliament, to further enhance public safety.
She recalled discussions in the Sixth Parliament about benchmarking the Committee's work with other countries to learn from their practices in handling police-related matters. She recommended revisiting this initiative in the Seventh Parliament to gain insights and potentially improve the Committee's approaches.
Addressing the issue of insufficient police stations, she proposed initiating negotiations to repurpose closed facilities into sub-police stations to better serve communities, especially in remote areas. This proactive approach aimed to enhance accessibility to police services and improve response times to incidents such as accidents and hijackings.
She expressed her support for the report, noting its comprehensive coverage of the discussions held in Parliament. She expressed hope for the continuity of effective reporting and collaboration between the SAPS and Parliament, to address the country's crime challenges.
Mr Gericke expressed his appreciation for the professionalism and commitment demonstrated by the Committee Members and administrative personnel. He commended the comprehensive nature of the report, which provided valuable insights even to newcomers like himself.
However, he raised several concerns and recommendations, indicating his apprehension about the proliferation of private security companies across the country, some of which engaged in activities that blurred the lines between private security and law enforcement. He questioned the role and oversight of PSIRA in approving these companies, and suggested a need for tighter regulations to prevent potential abuses and to maintain public safety.
Secondly, he emphasised the need for a dedicated police force to combat gangsterism, particularly in areas heavily affected by gang-related violence. He called for increased visibility and the deployment of specially trained officers to address this issue effectively.
He also highlighted concerns about the delays in forensic testing due to insufficient resources and infrastructure in forensic laboratories. He urged the authorities to prioritise resolving these issues in order to expedite criminal investigations and justice delivery.
Regarding police promotions, Mr Gericke suggested streamlining the promotion process to prevent demotivation among officers waiting for advancements in their careers, stressing the importance of retaining experienced personnel within the police force.
He also advocated a significant increase in the police budget to address logistical needs, resource shortages, and operational challenges faced by various police stations. Adequately resourcing the police was crucial for effective crime prevention and law enforcement.
Lastly, he called for a holistic approach to addressing crime, involving collaboration among various government departments and stakeholders. There was a need for all relevant departments to come together to discuss and implement comprehensive strategies to combat crime effectively. He urged proactive measures and collaborative efforts to tackle the escalating crime rate in the country, pointing out the urgency of the situation and the need for innovative solutions.
The Chairperson requested a formal motion to adopt the report.
Adoption of the Legacy Report
Ms Majozi moved the adoption of the Legacy Report.
Ms Peacock seconded the proposal.
The report was adopted.
Farewell speeches
The Chairperson reflected on the significance of the day, being the Committee's final official meeting, and extended an invitation to representatives from various departments and agencies overseen by the Committee to share a few words as a farewell gesture. He emphasised that political speeches were not necessary, but rather a simple farewell message.
PSIRA
Mr Manabela Chauke, Chief Executive Officer (CEO), PSIRA, conveyed sincere appreciation to the Committee, its staff and the Members, for the robust engagements throughout the period. He said these interactions had significantly enhanced the work done by PSIRA for the country. He acknowledged the recommendations made by the Committee, and gave an assurance that PSIRA would work on strengthening them during future discussions. He concluded by thanking the Committee for their work and wishing them the best of luck in their future endeavours.
IPID
Ms Jennifer Dikeledi Ntlatseng, Executive Director, Independent Police Investigative Directorate (IPID), thanked the Committee for the significant support provided, noting that the IPID had experienced substantial growth and improvement during their tenure. She highlighted the value of the robust discussions and inputs shared during the platform's discussions, emphasising how they had greatly contributed to enhancing the Directorate's work. Despite facing challenges, she acknowledged the Committee's support and leadership, which had been instrumental in overcoming obstacles. She expressed hope that the next administration would continue to build upon the progress made, acknowledging the importance of sustaining the momentum.
Civilian Secretariat for Police Service (CSPS)
Mr Thulani Sibuyi, Secretary for Police, acknowledged that this was only his second meeting with the Portfolio Committee. He highlighted the feedback received regarding the Committee's work in strengthening the Secretariat. He stressed the importance of the Secretariat as a structure supporting various departmental functions and development efforts. He also noted the significant progress made in terms of the legal report. He underscored the Secretariat's commitment to developing a clear plan with achievable timelines to address ongoing and future work for the next administration.
The Chairperson expressed gratitude to Mr Sibuyi and noted that the timing of his entry coincided with their departure. Despite the short duration of their collaboration, he expressed confidence in the Secretary's abilities to fulfil his duties effectively.
The Chairperson then proceeded to inquire if there were any representatives from the Hawks (DPCI), but there were none. Consequently, he moved on to invite a representative from the SAPS National Commissioner's office.
SAPS
Gen Masemola expressed gratitude to the Portfolio Committee for effectively exercising its oversight role during the Sixth Administration of the government. He acknowledged the Committee's significant contribution to the effective management and progressive development of SAPS, emphasising the importance of ensuring the safety and security needs of the country's people.
He referred to the Committee's accountability measures, including direct engagements on issues such as gender-based violence and femicide (GBVF), the central firearm registry, and forensic science. Physical visits to various locations further enhanced the Committee's understanding and direction for the SAPS.
Regarding SAPS's operations, Gen Masemola mentioned Operation Shanela, which had resulted in over 536 980 arrests since its implementation in May 2023. He expressed profound gratitude to the Committee Chairperson and Members for their leadership and contributions, particularly after the untimely passing of the former Chairperson.
He assured the Committee and the people of South Africa of SAPS's readiness and preparedness for the upcoming elections, pledging to ensure peaceful and secure elections that were credible, free and fair.
The Chairperson expressed gratitude to the National Commissioner and wished him the best, particularly in handling the upcoming elections. Despite receiving threats, the Chairperson conveyed satisfaction with SAPS's response thus far, and expressed confidence in their continued efforts.
IFP
Ms Majozi expressed her gratitude to the Committee and support staff for their professionalism in enhancing the Portfolio Committee on Police. She also thanked the SAPS for their cooperation and expressed hope for future meetings. She extended well wishes for successful election campaigns without distractions, emphasising the importance of returning to serve the people of South Africa. She concluded by thanking the Chairperson for facilitating open contributions from all political parties, noting that true leadership emerged when people were given freedom.
EFF
Mr Gericke expressed his gratitude to the National Commissioner and the police for their work under challenging circumstances and with limited resources. He emphasised the importance of oversight and monitoring in ensuring the safety of communities and the country. He conveyed his belief in the divine authority bestowed upon those in positions of leadership, and urged the rebuilding of the country's "ruined walls." Drawing inspiration from the biblical story of Nehemiah, he called for collective efforts to strengthen the nation. Finally, he invoked a blessing upon all those involved in safeguarding the well-being of the people.
DA
Mr Terblanche reflected on his long history of involvement with the police, spanning nearly five decades. He acknowledged the privilege of working with the Committee and its Members, many of whom had become like family to him. He extended his well wishes to the Committee Members and thanked them for their collaboration. He also expressed appreciation for the support provided by the team and the contributions of the National Commissioner and other officials of the police departments. He said his decision not to return to the Committee in the next term was a personal choice, and he looked forward to exploring new ventures.
The Chairperson expressed gratitude for the kind words and extended best wishes to Mr Terblanche as he embarked on retirement, noting his longstanding dedication to his work. He acknowledged his decision to retire after previously postponing it, wishing him well in this new chapter of his life and encouraging him to enjoy quality time with his family.
ANC
Ms Peacock expressed her appreciation for the Chairperson's leadership, commending his guidance, coordination and humble character, which fostered unity and a focus on serving the interests of South Africans without political bias. She also acknowledged the Minister and Deputy Minister for their crucial roles in decision-making and commitment to the Committee's objectives.
She extended her appreciation to all Committee Members for their dedication to the country's interests and to SAPS officials and entities for their cooperation and implementation of Committee needs. She thanked the Committee staff for their administrative diligence and commitment to timeliness.
Lastly, she thanked all the individuals who had contributed to the Committee's success during the Sixth Administration, acknowledging the challenges faced and the learning opportunities gained. She concluded by saying it had been an honour to serve on the Committee.
NFP
Mr Shaik Emam expressed his appreciation for the Committee Members, including the former Chairperson, and acknowledged the exemplary leadership during challenging times. He thanked the staff and officials for their outstanding performance and support, highlighting their efforts during oversight visits and their attention to details such as dietary requirements.
He extended well wishes to those retiring, emphasising the importance of rest and enjoying life beyond work. He recognised the sacrifices made by politicians and the challenges they faced, and applauded the Committee's achievements.
He acknowledged the difficult job the SAPS have, and the strides made despite challenges. He emphasised the need for a holistic approach to crime prevention, and highlighted the living conditions and dangers faced by police officers, which could contribute to corruption. He stressed the importance of fair compensation and wellness programmes for officers, citing examples of trauma experienced by police personnel.
Mr Shaik Emam commended SAPS for their work, acknowledged their challenges, and expressed readiness to work collaboratively in the Seventh Parliament to create a better society. He concluded by wishing everyone well, and praying for their health and prosperity.
DA
Mr Golding expressed his gratitude to the Chairperson for his leadership and guidance, acknowledging the valuable lessons learned during his tenure. He also extended thanks to the Minister, Deputy Minister, and Committee Members for their patience and cooperation.
Chairperson's closing remarks
The Chairperson thanked everyone for their collaborative efforts, emphasising the unity and common goal of serving the citizens of South Africa. He highlighted the Committee's commitment to addressing crime, and suggested convening a crime prevention summit to engage the public in combating crime. Additionally, he proposed holding sessions with station commanders to gather grassroots perspectives on crime prevention.
He concluded by expressing appreciation to the National Commissioner, the Executive Director, Secretary and CEO, along with their staff, for their continuous engagement. He thanked the Committee Members and support staff for their dedication and positive attitude.
The meeting was adjourned.
