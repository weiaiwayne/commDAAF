Annual Reports 2024/25
The Portfolio Committee of Department of Women, Youth and Persons with Disabilities (DWYPD) to consider its 2024/25 Annual Report. The meeting provided oversight of the Department’s performance, expenditure, and policy implementation across its five programmes and the Department’s clean audit outcome for the second consecutive year.
The Minister acknowledged the Department’s limited budget and its role as a coordinating body rather than a direct service-delivery department. Despite these challenges, she highlighted key achievements including implementation of the National Strategic Plan (NSP) on Gender-Based Violence and Femicide, progress on the Youth Development and Disability Rights Bills, and the advancement of gender-responsive budgeting across government. The Director-General reinforced the Department’s focus on legislative alignment and strengthened interdepartmental coordination mechanisms.
The Department reported an 77% performance achievement rate for the year, underspending by R8.9 million mainly due to delayed recruitment and National Treasury’s control measures. The CFO explained that the Department’s reliance on consultants for research, performance reviews, translation, and specialised technical work was a result of insufficient internal capacity.
Committee members expressed concern over the contradiction between underspending and claims of underfunding, requesting a comprehensive plan to address the high vacancy rate and reduce the use of consultants. They questioned delays in finalising key legislation, particularly the Youth Development Bill, and urged DWYPD to expedite consultation and submission to Cabinet. Substantial discussion focused on the Sanitary Dignity Programme, where Members raised inconsistent provincial implementation and non-compliance with procurement guidelines. The Minister clarified that while DWYPD retained oversight responsibilities, funding allocations and transfers were managed by National Treasury.
Further scrutiny was directed toward DWYPD’s reliance on donor funding, with R23.8 million from the EU and R3.3 million from the UN Fund reported as fully utilised. Members emphasised the need for a detailed breakdown of expenditure and impact analysis. They requested progress reports on digital transformation projects, including the automation of recruitment and monitoring systems.
The Minister concluded by commending the DG for her dedicated leadership, announcing her retirement effective 31 October 2025. The Committee expressed gratitude for her service and acknowledged DWYPD’s efforts to maintain clean governance standards.
The Committee requested written timelines for outstanding legislative work and for the filling of senior vacancies.
Meeting report
Minister’s Opening Remarks
Minister Sindisiwe Chikunga confirmed that DWYPD had achieved a clean audit outcome for a second consecutive year, with the Auditor-General of South Africa (AGSA) issuing an unqualified opinion. She attributed this to sound financial management, strict internal controls, and the leadership of the Director-General.
DWYPD had 39 performance targets of which 30 were achieved (77%), reflecting a regression from the 88% achievement rate in 2023/24. The Minister explained that the decline was largely due to capacity constraints and DWYPD’s reliance on other entities to implement cross-cutting targets. Measures were being implemented to strengthen planning, coordination and monitoring functions to improve delivery in the coming year.
The Minister confirmed that DWYPD had no unauthorised or wasteful expenditure during 2024/25, underscoring its commitment to accountability and transparency in managing public funds. She highlighted a major achievement in procurement transformation, where 75% of departmental procurement spending had gone to women-owned entities, far exceeding the 40% target.
Turning to staffing, she reported that the vacancy rate stood at 0.7% above the Department of Public Service and Administration’s acceptable standard, and that urgent steps were being taken to fill critical posts and strengthen institutional capacity.
A significant milestone was the signing into law of the National Council on Gender-Based Violence and Femicide (GBVF) Act on 24 May 2024, which came into operation on 15 November 2024. The Act institutionalised a co-ordinated national response to GBVF and she expressed the goal of establishing the National Council by 1 April 2026.
The Minister reported on recent international engagements. She represented South Africa at the Global Leaders’ Meeting on Women in China, held under the theme
“Shared Future: Accelerating a New Process for Women’s All-Round Development.”
President Xi Jinping had - outlined four important global priorities:
- creating a conducive environment for women;
- building economic opportunities through innovation and technology and AI;
- ensuring access to health and sustainable, bias-free environments; and
- strengthening global cooperation under the United Nations.
China had announced a 50 000-women development skills programme and South Africa had expressed interest in participating, 10 000 women per year over five years. The Minister had met Ms Huang Xiaowei, Vice-President of the All-China Women’s Federation, who would visit South Africa to advance this collaboration.
In addition, the Minister had met with the China Disabled Persons Federation to explore support for establishing a National Empowerment Resource Facility (NERF) Centre for Disability Inclusion in South Africa as part of a G20 legacy project, to be implemented through a formal memorandum of understanding (MOU) within two years.
The Minister also signed an MOU with the International Organisation which promotes youth empowerment in artificial intelligence, coding, and robotics. Under this agreement, 600 000 opportunities for young South Africans across 57 sectors would receive free training, valued at USD 9 000 per participant, focusing on areas such as AI, cybersecurity, climate change, and the green economy. The initiative to be co-ordinated through the National Youth Development Agency (NYDA), would prioritise graduates and young professionals with technical qualifications, while ensuring data protection and information security.
Concluding her remarks, the Minister noted her admiration for China’s progress in disability inclusion, where technological innovation had enabled persons with disabilities to live more independently. She reiterated South Africa’s determination to strengthen its own inclusion framework and said DWYPD was actively mobilising international partnerships to do so.
DWYPD Annual Report
Director-General, Adv Mikateko Joyce Maluleke, stated that she would not repeat what the Minister had already outlined on performance but wished to emphasise the structural and resourcing constraints facing DWYPD.
DWYPD remained critically under-resourced and was expected to deliver on a wide national mandate with very limited staff and no provincial presence. Within the disability programme, for example, only about six staff members were functional, making it virtually impossible to meet the expectations placed on it. She described this as a “serious, serious challenge” that required urgent attention, noting that external stakeholders often failed to appreciate DWYPD’s constrained capacity when questioning its effectiveness.
The Chief Director in the Office of Director-General, Ms Val Mathobela, presented:
DWYPD’s 2024/25 Annual Report, outlining key achievements, areas of under-performance, and corrective measures across Programmes 1 to 5. The presentation provided an overview of performance information per programme, as well as financial and human resource considerations, drawing attention to areas requiring the Committee’s focus.
DWYPD had surpassed its target of 40% procurement from women-owned enterprises, exceeding the prior year’s figure, and reiterated the Minister’s emphasis on procurement as a vehicle for empowerment. However, targets for youth and persons with disabilities were not fully met.
On human resource performance, the vacancy rate stood at 10.7% at year-end, against a target of below 10%. The non-achievement was attributed to protracted recruitment processes arising from National Treasury and DPSA concurrence requirements then in force. These controls had since been lifted and the Executive Authority now held full delegated responsibility for recruitment. The Department was also developing an e-recruitment system to modernise application processing and reduce manual backlogs.
On legislative milestones, the National Council on Gender-Based Violence and Femicide Act had been assented to on 24 May 2024 and came into operation in November 2024, with work under way to establish the Council during the current year. The Youth Development Bill had not yet been tabled before Cabinet due to extended stakeholder consultations; processing would continue in 2025.
Overall achievement had declined from 88% in 2023/24 to 77% in 2024/25. Underperformance was concentrated in Programme 1 (Corporate and Financial Management) and Programme 5 (Youth Development and Empowerment), with isolated shortfalls in Programme 2 (Social Transformation and Economic Empowerment).
Under Programme 1, DWYPD achieved 50% of its targets. The 100% payment-within-30-days standard was narrowly missed at 99.88%, with 1.55% of valid invoices paid late. Delays were linked to managerial sign-off backlogs and measures to prevent overspending after National Treasury declined a request for additional funds. A control measure had since been introduced to ensure managers signed invoices promptly.
On procurement targets, DWYPD achieved 26.62% spend on youth-owned enterprises and 5.98% on enterprises owned by persons with disabilities, against targets of 30% and 7% respectively. Ms Legwale explained that suppliers’ failure to update their Central Supplier Database profiles, combined with preferential-point scoring rules, limited youth-owned firms’ success. For disability-owned entities, verification gaps in the CSD created further challenges. The Department continued to engage National Treasury to improve validation systems and to run awareness initiatives encouraging eligible suppliers to register correctly.
Under Programme 2, one target, the development of the Socio-Economic Empowerment Index for Women, Youth and Persons with Disabilities, was not achieved. The earlier model produced by HSRC was found to be non-user-friendly, leading to new research commissioned from UCT. The index was now being refined and would serve as a monitoring tool in the coming year.
Programmes 3 and 4 achieved all their planned targets.
Programme 5 performance was weakest: only 43% of targets were achieved. Implementation of the Sanitary Dignity National Service Programme could not proceed owing to lack of funding and delays by the South African National Defence Force, the intended implementing partner. Similarly, plans for five farming projects and three bakery training centres were not realised as donor and municipal co-funding did not materialise. These projects had been targeted for poverty alleviation in the poorest district municipalities.
Processing of the South African Youth Development Bill was delayed by protracted consultations with external stakeholders; internal legal review was now under way to strengthen the draft before submission to Cabinet.
The Committee was assured that DWYPD was actively addressing under-performance areas through improved recruitment, modernised systems and strengthened inter-departmental coordination.
Human Resources and Organisational Environment
Mr Mbhazima Shiviti, DWYPD Chief Director for Corporate Management, reminded the Committee that since the Department’s establishment in 2009, over 16 years, it had remained severely under-resourced and structurally constrained to fulfil its mandate to promote and monitor the empowerment of women, youth, and persons with disabilities. Despite rotating mandates and successive administrations, DWYPD’s capacity and ability to deliver on its purpose had not materially improved.
Mr Shiviti stated that DWYPD continued to face compliance burdens that limited its flexibility and delayed its ability to respond to evolving government priorities. He warned that this historic under-resourcing risked being perpetuated under the new Medium-Term Development Plan (MTDP) introduced in October 2024, which had assigned DWYPD additional responsibilities without corresponding funding allocations.
DWYPD’s organisational structure, first approved in April 2023 after concurrence with the Minister for Public Service and Administration, was amended again in July 2025 to align with the Executive Guide for Executive Authorities. However, the staffing allocation remained critically low.
As at September 2024, DWYPD was funded for only 140 posts across all programmes, far below operational needs. This limitation compelled DWYPD to channel most of its resources toward administration and compliance functions instead of its core service programmes. Due to this regression in funding, DWYPD could no longer afford to fill other critical vacancies.
Mr Shiviti outlined the distribution of posts per programme:
Programme 1 – Administration: 77 posts (over 50% of total)
Programme 2 – Advocacy and Mainstreaming for the Rights of Women: 30 posts
Programme 3 – Monitoring, Evaluation, Research and Coordination: 26 posts
Programme 4 – Rights of Persons with Disabilities: 11 posts (only six functional)
Programme 5 – Rights of Youth: 9 posts
DWYPD’s structure included:
Regulation, Advocacy and Mainstreaming for the Rights of Women;
Monitoring, Evaluation, Research and Coordination;
Regulation, Advocacy and Mainstreaming for the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (currently without a Deputy Director-General);
Corporate Support Services;
Regulation, Advocacy and Mainstreaming for the Rights of Youth; and
Operational Management unit (Office of CFO).
He then presented staffing levels:
Programme 1 had 77 posts filled, 10 vacant;
Programme 2 had 17 filled;
Programme 3 had 26 budgeted positions, none filled at that stage;
Programme 4 had 11 posts (10 filled, 1 vacant);
Programme 5 had 9 posts (8 filled, 1 vacant).
Mr Shiviti remarked that National Treasury had instructed departments to remain within their baseline headcounts, effectively preventing expansion beyond funded limits. In 2024/25 only one official was on precautionary suspension, with no dismissals recorded.
Financial Overview and Audit Outcomes
Ms Desree Legwale (CFO) confirmed that DWYPD had achieved a clean audit for 2024/25 and had spent 99.1% of its allocated budget, with no irregular, unauthorised, or wasteful expenditure recorded. The Department also surpassed its procurement target for women-owned entities, reflecting effective implementation of the preferential procurement framework and Treasury’s 40% set-aside policy.
DWYPD’s achievements were attained despite inadequate financial and human-resource capacity, noting that the limited budget continued to constrain programme implementation and staff development. The overstretched workforce had placed significant pressure on operational efficiency. She cautioned that sustaining a clean audit and ensuring continued service delivery would require improved resourcing going forward.
For procurement spend on youth- and disability-owned entities, Ms Legwale reported achievements of 20.6% and 5.9%, respectively. The challenges experienced included:
- Suppliers failing to update their profiles on the Central Supplier Database (CSD);
- Verification issues on disability status of directors listed in companies;
- The fluid nature of youth-owned procurement targets, which change between the appointment of service providers and the timing of payments.
Turning to the implementation of the Audit Action Plan, the CFO noted that capacity limitations in the Strategic Management Unit had affected timely monitoring of corrective actions. Consequently, DWYPD managed to clear 45 % of audit findings, while 27.7% were still in progress and 3% remained outstanding.
Payment of suppliers within 30 days had significantly improved, with 98.45% of invoices settled on time. Delays in the small remainder were mainly due to budget controls to avoid overspending and non-availability of managers to approve invoices during priority re-allocations.
DWYPD had been allocated R1 020 712 000, of which R1 011 019 000 was spent, resulting in underspending of R9.693 million.
Programme 1 Administration spent 94.7% of its budget.
Programme 2 Social Transformation and Economic Empowerment spent 100%.
Programme 3 Monitoring, Evaluation, Research and Coordination spent 94.4%
Programme 4 achieved 97.2% spending.
Programme 5 Youth Development utilised its full allocation.
The CFO emphasised that no irregular or unauthorised expenditure was recorded.
The Department received R 550 000 in own revenue and R12.2 million in donor funding under the Criminal Assets Recovery Account (CARA) programme, increasing the final budget to R1 072 406 000.
On the economic classification of expenditure
,
96.1 % was spent on compensation of employees, with underspending of R 8.5 million due to delays in filling vacancies caused by the Public Service and Administration Directive on Control Measures, which required Treasury concurrence before recruitment. These controls prolonged the appointment of critical staff. Additional delays arose from unfilled HR posts, the transition between the 6th and 7th Administrations, and pending inter-departmental claims for seconded staff.
Under goods and services, there was a slight overspend of R430 000, following reclassification of expenditure from capital assets during the audit process.
Under transfers and subsidies, DWYPD underspent 543 000, largely due to fluctuations in the exchange rate affecting the Commonwealth Annual Membership subscription.
For payments for capital assets, underspending resulted from delayed delivery of computer equipment, outstanding invoices for cellular devices, and the reclassification of finance leases to operating leases in line with National Treasury directives.
The CFO confirmed that two potential fruitless-and-wasteful expenditure cases were under internal audit assessment at year end but these did not affect the overall audit outcome.

Mr Tshotetsi sought clarity on funds reallocated to Programme 1 (Administration), particularly in light of existing vacancies and underspending in that programme. fruitless and wasteful expenditure under assessment
for venues paid for but not utilised. She requested clarity on the status of this.

DWYPD response
Director-General Maluleke accepted that under-spending had occurred largely under Compensation of Employees, driven by the DPSA/National Treasury control measures that, for most of the year, required prior concurrence and proof of funding before filling vacancies. Those controls had now been withdrawn (effective 1 April 2025), and DWYPD expected to accelerate recruitment. DWYPD has a coordination/oversight mandate (not service delivery), and it was strengthening collaboration with the Department of Planning, Monitoring and Evaluation (DPME) and Treasury to develop digital dashboards and automated reporting to improve data quality and timeliness across government. Consultants were used for specialised/statutory functions (e.g. competency assessments, language services, and certain research projects) and within Treasury rules.
Mr Shiviti clarified that the R 8.5 million underspending reported under Programme 1 was for primarily to compensation of employees, not programme expenditure. During 2024/25, DWYPD was subject to control measures issued by DPSA and National Treasury, which had delayed recruitment processes. These restrictions have since been discontinued, enabling DWYPD to proceed with filling vacant posts more effectively in the current financial year.
Mr Shiviti confirmed that DWYPD had made the establishment of the e-recruitment system an APP target for completion by Quarter 4 of 2025/26. Testing and configuration work was already underway with IT staff, and job descriptions were being uploaded to the system in preparation for upcoming recruitment drives. He assured the Committee that the system would be fully operational before the end of 2025/26, which would improve turnaround times, transparency, and efficiency in the recruitment process.
On job analysis, Mr Shiviti stated that for most of 2024/25, DWYPD had no job analyst or HR specialist responsible for job evaluation and organisational design. This had delayed recruitment and classification processes. A new director for organisational development had since been appointed and would commence on 1 November 2025, with the recruitment of a dedicated job analyst also scheduled for completion shortly thereafter.
He outlined the critical vacancies and their anticipated filling dates:
• Chief Director: Monitoring, Evaluation, Research and Coordination – targeted to be filled by 1 December 2025, pending ministerial scheduling for interviews;
• Chief Director: International Relations, Stakeholder Management and Capacity Building – recruitment in progress;
• Director: Governance and Compliance (Youth Rights) – the previous incumbent passed away in January 2025; post expected to be filled early 2026;
• Director: Strategic Planning and Reporting – shortlisting completed, interview to follow;
• Director: Monitoring and Evaluation – selection process underway, interviews to be conducted early November 2025;
• Director: Internal Policy – recruitment approved by DPSA and National Treasury, interview phase pending;
• Assistant Director: Organisational Job Evaluation (OJE) – appointment confirmed to start 1 November 2025;
• Assistant Director: Advocacy and Mainstreaming (Rights of Persons with Disabilities) – panel finalised, screening in progress;
• Assistant Director: Strategic Planning and Reporting – successful candidate to start 1 November 2025;
• Internal Coordinator (Co-Leader) – deployment currently underway.
Mr Shiviti explained that filling these key posts would ease the workload on remaining staff and stabilise departmental operations.
Addressing the Chairperson’s concern about consultants, Mr Shiviti clarified that the Corporate Management and HR branches did not outsource core functions to external consultants. The limited consultant expenditure under these programmes related only to competency assessments, health risk management, audit committee fees, and statutory payments required by DPSA standards. These engagements were regulatory in nature, not discretionary outsourcing.
He concluded by assuring the Committee that DWYPD was prioritising recruitment, system modernisation, and compliance improvements to address long-standing HR and capacity challenges, and that several key appointments would take effect from 1 November 2025 onwards.
Mr O.J. Fourie, representing DWYPD Youth Development Unit, addressed the non-achievement of several planned projects, notably the farming and bakery initiatives under Programme 5. He confirmed that the status remained unchanged from previous reports, as DWYPD had still been unable to secure funding from external partners to implement these projects.
He acknowledged Members’ criticism on the inclusion of targets in the APP without corresponding resources. He conceded that this had been a valuable lesson, and DWYPD had recognised the importance of avoiding over-ambitious planning. Going forward, only targets within DWYPD’s direct control and backed by confirmed resources would be included in future APPs to ensure deliverability.
Mr Fourie noted that DWYPD’s core mandate was not the direct implementation of projects, but rather policy coordination and oversight. To this end, DWYPD intended to strengthen collaborative partnerships with other implementing departments and entities. This approach would enable DWYPD to continue ensuring that youth and youth with disabilities benefited from national development initiatives, even where DWYPD was not the primary implementing authority.
Mr Fourie advised that the South African Youth Development Bill would be submitted to Cabinet once DWYPD had completed internal consultations and obtained ministerial approval for tabling.
Mr Fourie concluded by reaffirming the Department’s commitment to more realistic planning, improved inter-departmental coordination, and ensuring that youth empowerment remained central to DWYPD oversight and advocacy work.
Ms Desree Legwale addressed all finance questions, providing clarification on payment compliance, audit findings, vacancy-related underspending, consultant expenditure, and fruitless-expenditure investigations.
She explained that, to mitigate late payment of service providers beyond the prescribed 30 days, DWYPD had issued a formal financial-instruction note to all officials. This directive reinforced accountability and compliance expectations across units. An internal review had shown that most delayed invoices were photocopier rentals and monthly consumption costs. To address this, responsibility for processing those invoices had been shifted from line units to the Finance and Supply Chain Management (SCM) divisions, which had resulted in measurable improvement in turnaround times.
Within Programme 1 (Administration), the area most affected by delays and underspending, had been due to transfers and subsidies, rather than goods and services. Compensation of employees also reflected underspending at year-end due to the controls on vacancy filling.
On unresolved audit-action-plan items, Ms Legwale noted that findings within the Strategic Management Unit had remained open primarily because of severe capacity constraints. The unit was overseen by a single Chief Director, responsible for managing all operations, which limited its ability to resolve outstanding matters. These findings related mainly to performance-information audits, where the Auditor-General had identified material inconsistencies and understatement of achievements during the 2023/24 audit cycle.
Other audit findings still in progress by the end of 2024 pertained to reporting of deviations through HESA and National Treasury, non-compliance with the 30-day payment rule, and asset-register management. The latter issue had since been resolved through a comprehensive asset verification exercise conducted in preparation for the 2024/25 financial statements, and the irregularity was not repeated in the subsequent audit.
Ms Legwale elaborated on factors contributing to the underspending on Compensation of Employees. She pointed to an [October 2023] Directive on Control Measures issued by the Minister of Public Service and Administration, which required all departments to obtain National Treasury confirmation of funding and DPSA concurrence before filling any vacancy. This process created substantial delays and contributed directly to the year-end underspending. The directive was withdrawn effective 1 April 2025 after it became clear that it had caused widespread bottlenecks across government.
On the use of consultants, Ms Legwale emphasised that the expenditure reported under “consultants” covered a range of non-core but mandatory services, including:
- Competency assessments for officials undergoing recruitment processes;
-Sign-language interpretation services, essential for accessibility and inclusion;
- Human-resource-related external assessments; and
- Remuneration for Audit Committee members.
She explained that other consultant costs were linked to research projects commissioned to fill critical capacity gaps, where internal expertise or resources were insufficient to meet deadlines or technical requirements. These projects were undertaken only when specialised skills were required and where outsourcing was necessary to deliver on statutory or strategic mandates.
Ms Dineo Mmako DWYPD Chief Director of Monitoring and Evaluation, addressed fruitless and wasteful expenditure, specifically the R451 000 flagged for unutilised venues, that the matter remained under internal-audit assessment. At the close of the financial year, the Internal Audit Unit had not yet finalised its determination if the expenditure should be confirmed as fruitless or recoverable.
She concluded by reaffirming that DWYPD continued to implement corrective measures to strengthen internal financial controls, expedite payment processes, and address audit findings within available capacity.
Ms Mmako explained that the implementation and application of the Gender-Responsive Planning, Budgeting, Monitoring, Evaluation and Auditing Framework (GRPBMEA) was not confined to DWYPD, but extended to all government departments. For 2024/25, ten departments had been selected for focused monitoring and technical support, in accordance with the APP. The selection was based on two primary criteria:
Poor performance in the previous financial year, and
Non-compliance with reporting obligations under the GRPBMEA framework.
Departments meeting either of these conditions were prioritised for renewed assessment and capacity-building in the subsequent year.
Ms Mmako stated that DWYPD’s role involved both assessment and continuous support. The Department engaged directly with officials from each participating department, conducting one-on-one sessions with managers across key functional areas, planning, budgeting, monitoring, evaluation, and auditing, to provide practical guidance on how to institutionalise priorities for women, youth, and persons with disabilities in their internal systems.
She clarified that while not all departments were currently subject to full reporting, none were excluded from the overall GRPBMEA framework. The DWYPD aimed to eventually extend its reach to all government departments, offering capacity-building workshops and technical guidance to embed gender- and inclusivity-sensitive planning and budgeting processes.
In addition to periodic reviews, Ms Mmako noted that her unit maintained quarterly engagements with departments to review progress, share feedback, and resolve challenges. These sessions included participation in Executive Committee (EXCO) meetings, where DWYPD officials provided real-time direction on integrating the framework into departmental governance and reporting structures. This approach, combining oversight, technical assistance, and consistent engagement, ensured that the GRPBMEA framework was not merely a compliance exercise but a practical mechanism to advance inclusive planning and accountability across the public sector.
Ms Praveena Sukhraj-Ely, DWYPD Chief Director Governance and Compliance for Rights of Persons with Disabilities, responded about the use of consultants, particularly on the climate-change research project, and DWYPD’s monitoring role in advancing the rights of persons with disabilities. The Governance and Regulation Chief Directorate within the Disability Rights Branch was severely under-capacitated. The Directorate consisted of only three officials: herself (as Chief Director), one Director, and one Deputy Director. Together, they were responsible for a wide range of demanding outputs, including:
- Oversight of the White Paper on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (WPRPD);
- Monitoring the implementation of improvement plans and evaluation recommendations;
- Research oversight and coordination of sector-wide activities; and
- Preparation of national and international reports related to disability rights compliance.
Given this limited capacity and the highly technical nature of the climate-change research, which required both qualitative and quantitative analysis across multiple dimensions of environmental impact, the use of external consultants had been unavoidable. The scale and complexity of the research would have required her two officials to dedicate an entire year to the project, to the detriment of other statutory responsibilities. Thus, DWYPD had sought specialised external expertise to ensure delivery within reasonable timeframes and technical standards.
On the monitoring of Annual Performance Plans across government, the Disability Directorate team had reviewed draft APPs from various departments to assess the extent of disability-inclusive planning and targets. Working jointly with DPME, DWYPD had provided written feedback and recommendations for improvement. Where departments failed to incorporate these inputs, the DWYPD escalated matters through Director-General-to-Director-General (DG-to-DG) and Minister-to-Minister correspondence, urging the inclusion of disability-responsive measures in operational plans.
However, Ms Sukhraj-Ely noted a systemic challenge: the lack of enforcement mechanisms compelling departments to report comprehensively on disability inclusion. Many departments continued to struggle with disaggregated data and often submitted incomplete or non-specific reports, making comprehensive monitoring difficult. To counter this, the Disability Rights Branch compiled reports to Cabinet that visibly highlighted non-reporting departments in red, ensuring accountability and visibility at the executive level. Although DWYPD could not itself enforce sanctions, such identification enabled other oversight structures to take necessary follow-up actions.
On employment equity for persons with disabilities, Ms Sukhraj-Ely reported tangible progress. DWYPD had met with the Minister of Public Service and Administration to address widespread non-compliance with disability employment targets. The Department proposed a formal circular, drafted by DWYPD and aligned with Cabinet’s decision to increase disability employment equity representation to 3% by 2024/25, with annual incremental increases up to 2030. The draft circular was presented to the DPSA Minister, who committed to issuing a directive that would make these targets compulsory across all government departments. Importantly, the directive would also outline consequence-management provisions for departments failing to meet the required targets.
Ms Sukhraj-Ely concluded that DWYPD remained committed to advancing disability rights through coordinated oversight, collaborative monitoring with DPME and other departments, and stronger compliance mechanisms that would ensure measurable progress toward inclusivity and equity in the public service.
Ms Phuti Mabelebele, DWYPD Chief Director for Rights of Persons with Disabilities, provided a detailed update on the status and legislative progress of the Disability Rights Bill, clarifying the steps undertaken and the current stage of the drafting process.
She explained that the Disability Rights Bill originated from Discussion Paper 163, titled Project 148 of the South African Law Reform Commission (SALRC). The process had begun three years earlier, following the publication of an issue paper which was subjected to wide consultation, including engagements held after the COVID-19 lockdown period. The consultations informed the development of a draft discussion paper, which was subsequently published for public comment.
Upon the closure of the consultation period, all inputs and submissions were consolidated into a draft Disability Rights Bill, which has since undergone further internal and external review. Ms Mabelebele stated that DWYPD is now engaged in the second round of consultations on the revised draft bill, working closely with the SALRC to refine the text.
She confirmed that DWYPD had been formally appointed as part of the panel of experts assisting the SALRC to fast-track the Bill’s progression. The Department’s role includes consolidating stakeholder inputs, coordinating with advisory council members, and ensuring alignment with constitutional and policy imperatives.
Ms Mabelebele noted that the advertisement for public consultation on the second draft had already been issued by the SALRC, inviting broad public participation and written submissions. Following the conclusion of this consultative phase, DWYPD would proceed through the standard legislative processes, including obtaining a Socio-Economic Impact Assessment System (SEIAS) and the requisite executive endorsements before the Bill is submitted to Cabinet and ultimately Parliament for formal consideration and adoption.
She emphasised that DWYPD remained committed to ensuring that the Disability Rights Bill is comprehensive, inclusive, and reflective of stakeholder feedback, and that it will advance the protection and promotion of the rights of persons with disabilities in South Africa.
Director-General Maluleke added clarification on the status of the Disability Rights Bill. She noted that, once the Bill proceeds to the next phase, it will undergo public consultations, after which it must return to the State Law Advisers and other relevant authorities before being finalised. She explained that the process remained ongoing but that notable progress had been made in recent months.
Director-General Maluleke reported that DWYPD had to intervene after discovering that the South African Law Reform Commission (SALRC) had not advanced the Bill’s preparatory work due to the departure of its assigned researcher. When DWYPD inquired, the SALRC confirmed that no replacement had been appointed. The Department therefore insisted that SALRC appoint a new researcher, which was done toward the end of the previous year, allowing the process to resume and move forward.

Ms Shoki Tshabalala, Deputy Director-General: Social Transformation, indicated that some of the requested data, particularly on the number of girls reached per province, was not immediately available and would need to be verified against department records upon her return to the office.
Ms Tshabalala explained that DWYPD’s awareness and outreach sessions focus largely on menstrual-health management and education. These sessions address practical and psychosocial issues relating to puberty, helping girls understand the onset of menstruation, personal hygiene, and product use. Facilitators emphasise responsible disposal of sanitary products and encourage learners to keep an extra supply on hand to manage unexpected cycles and reduce absenteeism.
The implementation of the programme is coordinated with provincial departments, which determine preferred schools and the timing of visits. The Department relies heavily on the availability and cooperation of provincial education officials and educators, as sessions are scheduled when schools can accommodate external facilitators without disrupting classes.
In instances where concerns or complaints are raised about schools not being reached, DWYPD investigates and prioritises those institutions for inclusion in future outreach. Ms Tshabalala stressed that while national oversight is maintained, provincial authorities play a key role in directing and supporting implementation.
Statistical and logistical information, including the number of sessions conducted, schools reached, and beneficiaries, would be provided in writing once verified.
Director-General Maluleke clarified that provincial departments are responsible for administering the Sanitary Dignity Programme. The allocated funds do not pass through the national Department but are transferred directly from National Treasury to provincial governments.
She noted that this funding structure presents oversight challenges, as the national Department has limited control over distribution and implementation at provincial level. Mr Shiviti expanded on his earlier comments on vacancy management and recruitment challenges. DWYPD had received an exceptionally high volume of applications for lower-level and entry-level positions, which had placed considerable strain on administrative capacity. For example, while recruitment for a Deputy Director-General (DGG) position attracted 232 applications, each entry-level post had drawn over 4,000 applications. With seven to eight such positions advertised, this translated to tens of thousands of applications that had to be manually processed.

This manual process remained a key constraint and the introduction of the electronic recruitment system would markedly improve efficiency.
The system’s ongoing maintenance, support, and licensing would be critical to ensure its sustainability once implemented, and he thanked the Committee for allowing him to clarify these operational challenges.

The Youth Development Unit comprised approximately 11 staff posts, though in practice only five or six were fully functional, as the remainder included administrative or personal assistant positions. Given these constraints, DWYPD sought strategic partnerships with other government departments, in this case, the SANDF, because it possessed the infrastructure and logistical capacity to conduct large-scale youth training programmes.
Adv Maluleke clarified that the programme concept had been designed within DWYPD and presented by the then Minister to the Minister of Defence, who agreed to collaborate on its implementation. She emphasised that while DWYPD did not directly control such programmes or the associated funding, these partnerships were the only practical means to achieve outcomes aligned with DWYPD’s youth mandate.
She concluded by noting that “money speaks”, underscoring that without dedicated funding, DWYPD must rely on interdepartmental cooperation to deliver on its objectives.
Follow-up questions
The Chairperson appreciated the comprehensive responses.

Mr Tshotetsi stated that this practice had repeatedly led to under-achievement in previous reporting periods, as DWYPD lacked both the resources and the authority to implement such programmes independently. DWYPD response
Director-General Maluleke agreed that if a programme, such as the youth training initiative with the SANDF, was not fully within DWYPD’s operational authority, it should not be recorded in the APP but rather in the Operational Plan, which is also audited by the Auditor-General (AG). This would prevent DWYPD from being faulted for non-achievement of targets that depend on external entities.
She confirmed that the SANDF-led youth development initiative remained a valuable and high-impact programme with the potential to reduce unemployment but had been hampered by budgetary constraints and resource limitations affecting both DWYPD and the SANDF.
Mr Sipiwo Matshoba, DWYPD Chief Director: Social Empowerment and Participation, addressed the persistent challenges facing the Sanitary Dignity Programme, DWYPD’s ongoing interventions, and new measures being explored with National Treasury to improve delivery. One of the core issues remained chronic inefficiencies in provincial supply chain management (SCM), which had been reported consistently each quarter. Despite frequent engagements, provincial departments continued to provide vague or incomplete explanations for delays in procurement and distribution. While the situation was not uniform across all provinces – KwaZulu-Natal and Eastern Cape were comparatively better managed – most other provinces struggled to ensure consistent product rollout to learners.
A further concern was the institutional neglect of the programme within certain implementing departments, typically Social Development or Education provincial departments, where it was treated as a secondary or “stepchild” initiative. Mr Matshoba explained that provinces had two options for implementation: either integrate the programme within existing structures such as HIV/AIDS and social behaviour change programmes, or run it as a standalone intervention with dedicated staff and budget. However, many provinces had failed to resource it adequately, citing serious human-resource shortages. Provinces such as Mpumalanga, Northern Cape, and North West had all reported capacity constraints without tangible mitigation plans.
Mr Matshoba confirmed that the Commission for Gender Equality had raised concerns about anomalies in implementation, particularly in the North West, but said the engagement between CGE and DWYPD had been constructive rather than adversarial. A follow-up meeting was scheduled to identify joint strategies for improved oversight and coordination.
On remedial action, he reported that DWYPD, working with National Treasury and the United Nations Population Fund (UNFPA), had completed a financial-flows assessment across provinces. The assessment aimed to identify bottlenecks in how provincial budgets for sanitary dignity were being allocated and utilised. The findings revealed that in many cases, funds were under-utilised, spent late in the financial year or misaligned with implementation schedules. The Department had since engaged provincial treasuries to improve monitoring and unblock delays.
To strengthen provincial oversight capacity, DWYPD also convened monitoring and evaluation (M&E) workshops for all provinces, equipping officials to conduct on-site verification and complement national-level monitoring efforts, given DWYPD’s own limited staffing.
Mr Matshoba noted an upcoming strategic meeting with National Treasury and the Office of the Chief Procurement Officer to consider alternative procurement models under the Intergovernmental Relations Framework. The goal was to identify more effective ways to manage the procurement and distribution of sanitary products, possibly through centralised or hybrid models. He cautioned that without such reforms, the current decentralised, province-driven system would continue to produce inconsistent results and impede the intended impact of the programme to ensure uninterrupted learning and improved menstrual health and hygiene for schoolgirls across the country.
Adv Maluleke added to remarks on DWYPD’s M&E systems. She confirmed that DWYPD’s current reporting process is entirely manual, involving the collection, editing, and consolidation of information from departments and provinces, which has made accuracy and timeliness difficult to maintain.
Adv Maluleke said that DWYPD was now working with a donor partner, the Innovation Centre, to automate all M&E functions, not only the e-recruitment system previously mentioned, but also the broader M&E frameworks covering gender mainstreaming, youth development, and procurement. The Innovation Centre had already developed an automated gender-based analysis and mainstreaming training platform, which was nearing completion, pending final technical adjustments.
Once fully implemented, this digital transformation would allow departments, provinces, and municipalities to upload data directly onto a centralised system, greatly improving monitoring accuracy, efficiency, and accountability.
Adv Maluleke assured Members that DWYPD was making steady progress toward this goal and encouraged the Portfolio Committee to “hang in there,” noting that the system would soon significantly enhance DWYPD’s capacity for oversight and reporting.
Mr Mbhazima Shiviti, Chief Director: Corporate Management, expanded on staffing constraints and internal interventions to ensure continuity of operations despite persistent vacancies. Although temporary arrangements had been made by the Minister and Director-General to provide relief in certain critical positions, these measures were not sustainable, as such individuals could vacate the posts at any time before the department completed its quota-based filling of posts under the DPSA and National Treasury directives.
Strategic planning and internal audit units were among the most critical areas affected by vacancies, particularly given their importance for performance reporting, compliance with DPME requirements, and adherence to new internal audit standards. Without timely intervention, these staffing shortages would have had a direct impact on service delivery and compliance performance.
Mr Shiviti acknowledged that through the intervention and flexibility of the Minister and DG, DWYPD had been able to reallocate limited resources and prioritise posts strategically to maintain a minimum level of functionality and service delivery. However, this approach was only a temporary stopgap and underscored the urgent need to fill vacant posts permanently to ensure stability, compliance, and sustained performance going forward.
DG Maluleke stated that the Minister’s engagement with the Minister of Public Service and Administration had resulted in a firm commitment to strengthen accountability on the employment of persons with disabilities in the public service. There would soon be serious consequences for departments that fail to meet the required disability employment targets.
CFO Ms Desree Legwale confirmed that during 2024/25 DWYPD had spent R30 000 from its voted funds on catering for the G20 technical meeting, which was hosted virtually by the Department of International Relations and Cooperation (DIRCO).
On donor funding, DWYPD had spent R23.8 million from the European Union (EU) and a further R23.8 million from the Criminal Assets Recovery Account (CARA) fund. These expenditures were fully disclosed in DWYPD’s annual financial statements (Note 30) and in the annexure on page 173 of the Annual Report.
On consultant expenditure, Ms Legwale clarified that the reference made earlier did not relate to performance reviews of internal staff, as queried by Members. Instead, the consultancy expenditure pertained to competency assessments conducted for potential candidates being considered for appointment to advertised positions. These service providers were pre-approved by the Department of Public Service and Administration (DPSA) and contracted as part of the recruitment process.
Ms Legwale noted that the manager responsible for unresolved audit findings in the Strategic Management Unit, Ms Val Mathobela, was present and was best placed to provide details on the implementation challenges affecting the Audit Action Plan.
Ms Dineo Mmako expanded on the department’s evaluation of the Preferential Procurement Policy Framework and the broader monitoring landscape for gender, youth, and disability inclusion across government.
Over the past five years, the department has undertaken two formal evaluations of the Presidential Preferential Procurement Policy:
The first evaluation (2022) examined the overall implementation of the policy across departments, focusing on compliance and the consistency of application.
The second evaluation (2024) an impact study, analysed how effectively the policy translated into measurable outcomes for women, youth, and persons with disabilities (WYPD) in public procurement.
Ms Mmako noted that both studies revealed recurring systemic challenges:
• Many departments continued to treat the “set-aside” allocations (the 40/30/5 percent preferential procurement pronouncement for women, youth, and persons with disabilities) as optional or discretionary, lacking binding directives.
• Departments exercised wide latitude in determining their own percentages, resulting in inconsistent application and diluted impact.
• Persistent absence of disaggregated data across departments impeded accurate tracking of expenditure and beneficiary demographics.
• Some departments expressed reluctance or confusion, arguing that National Treasury should issue a prescriptive instruction mandating the set-aside percentages for implementation.
She added that legal disputes over the legitimacy of preferential procurement targets had further complicated enforcement, with courts reaffirming that accounting officers themselves bear ultimate responsibility for implementing and reporting on preferential procurement within their departments.
In the latest impact study, DWYPD also captured critical feedback from women-, youth-, and disability-owned businesses, who reported that they were often unaware of available tenders or excluded from procurement pipelines. One of the key recommendations from this study was to conduct proactive market-readiness and outreach programmes to raise awareness and increase participation of these groups in government procurement.
Ms Mmako confirmed that the Department is producing an Annual Monitoring Report, guided by the Country Gender Indicator Framework, which consolidates indicators for women, youth, and persons with disabilities. These indicators are now being aligned with both the Medium-Term Strategic Framework (MTSF) and the National Development Plan (NDP).
However, she acknowledged that the Department’s monitoring remains primarily desktop-based, due to severe capacity constraints, limiting its ability to perform on-site verification of reported data. The lack of disaggregated data remains a major obstacle, particularly in determining how much of each department’s spending truly benefits WYPD categories.
To address this, Ms Mmako explained that the DWYPD has engaged with DPME to strengthen frameworks that will make disaggregated reporting mandatory. The current APP and Strategic Plan templates now include a specific field for disaggregation by gender, youth, and disability, yet many departments still mark it as “not applicable”. The department, in collaboration with DPME, is working to ensure that departments that identify disaggregated targets at planning stage are obliged to report on them consistently during quarterly and annual reviews.
Ms Mmako said that the findings and recommendations from these evaluations have directly informed ongoing policy reform, including contributing to the draft Preferential Procurement Bill. The 2022 study in particular influenced the inclusion of the 40% procurement target in the APP and Strategic Plan frameworks, thereby elevating it into formal government planning instruments.
Director-General Maluleke added that public education remained an important part of improving compliance and awareness. DWYPD had previously undertaken radio talk shows in all 11 official languages to educate the public on how to trade with the state, in partnership with the Department of Trade, Industry and Competition (DTIC), National Treasury, and SARS. These engagements focused on guiding entrepreneurs and citizens on company registration, regulatory compliance, and procurement requirements.
She explained that DWYPD had initially conducted a two-month pilot of the radio initiative and had sought funding to extend it to ten months at an estimated cost of R28 million, but budget constraints prevented continuation.
On interdepartmental accountability, Adv Maluleke emphasised that DWYPD’s mandate was limited to monitoring and reporting, as it had no authority to enforce compliance by other departments. To promote transparency and recognition, DWYPD had therefore published an article in the GCIS Public Service Management magazine, highlighting departments and provinces that were compliant and reporting timeously, as well as those that were not. These were among the measures taken to encourage greater adherence to reporting and compliance obligations.
Ms Tshabalala confirmed that DWYPD had established M&E frameworks for all its strategies, specifically referencing the National Strategic Plan (NSP) as an example. This framework contains defined indicators and timelines, structured across a five-year horizon, followed by an additional five-year period, giving it a 10-year planning cycle.
Using this framework, DWYPD engages with sector departments to request progress reports on implementation activities. These reports form the basis for compiling consolidated assessments of development progress. DWYPD presents these findings to various Director-General forums, including economic and social clusters, where departments conduct peer reviews and share insights on areas of development, collaboration, and performance across specific indicators.
DWYPD recently conducted a five-year review of the NSP in partnership with the President’s Office and the Department of Planning, Monitoring and Evaluation (DPME). The review report will be submitted to the Minister for further guidance and possible additional inputs before being tabled at relevant structures and ultimately released for public dissemination.
DWYPD applies a similar M&E approach across its other strategic programmes, with on-site visits conducted primarily on the Sanitary Dignity Programme, as previously explained by Mr Matshoba.
The Director-General requested that DDG Tshabalala provide detail on DWYPD’s engagement with the Justice, Crime Prevention and Security (JCPS) Cluster, specifically how DWYPD had influenced the cluster to extend its activities to provincial level, with recent focus on the Eastern Cape and Mpumalanga.
Ms Tshabalala explained that under the JCPS Cluster and within the 90-Day Bridge Programme, DWYPD works closely with JCPS representatives, supported by the Ministers of Women and of Social Development, who co-chair the Inter-Ministerial Committee on Gender-Based Violence and Femicide (GBVF). The cluster identifies priority areas for intervention, which DWYPD then profiles in advance to determine the key issues. These visits enable coordinated planning and the reporting of progress and outcomes in the selected areas.
The JCPS approach is comprehensive, addressing GBVF and related crime through multi-departmental responses. Field engagements have revealed challenges, particularly within the Thuthuzela Care Centres, where civil-society organisations face difficulties in sustaining services due to resource constraints and the lingering effects of the COVID-19 pandemic.
To address these gaps, DWYPD has facilitated links between affected centres and the GBV Command Centre, especially to ensure continuity of psychosocial support services after hours, including weekends. Ms Tshabalala emphasised that while medico-legal services are provided on site by doctors, psychosocial care remains the critical gap. These interventions and follow-up visits have led to visible improvements as DWYPD now produces reports with findings and recommendations which are monitored jointly with the relevant departments to track implementation.
Closing remarks by Minister
In closing, Minister Chikunga thanked the Chairperson and Members for their engagement and noted that while the discussion had largely focused on unmet targets, DWYPD’s achieved targets were far greater and reflected extensive work across all branches. Each programme could expand on its progress in implementing, monitoring, evaluating, and reporting. The Minister added that some of the questions raised might have arisen because DWYPD did not present the full Annual Report during the session.
On non-implementation of certain projects, DWYPD had previously clarified that some activities listed in the Annual Performance Plan were not directly within its control. However, given the national crisis of youth unemployment, now at about 62%, DWYPD believed that extraordinary measures were warranted. Drawing a parallel to when all departments had been mobilised around education in earlier years, she said every sphere of government should contribute to addressing youth unemployment. The SANDF youth-led programme, though not implemented by DWYPD, was supported by it through a funding application to the National Skills Fund submitted jointly with the Department of Defence, which was now at the final stage of consideration.
Responding to the Committee’s suggestion that such projects be removed from the APP, the Minister said that while DWYPD accepted that implementation authority lay elsewhere, youth unemployment required an inter-departmental response, and DWYPD could not remain passive as the lead entity on youth matters.
Turning to disability inclusion, the Minister confirmed that DWYPD had engaged with the Minister of Public Service and Administration on poor compliance by departments in employing persons with disabilities. Only a few departments, including Women, Tourism, Trade and Industry, and Small Business Development, currently met or exceeded the 4% target. The DPSA Minister had therefore undertaken to issue a ministerial order, stronger than a circular, compelling departments to meet incremental disability-employment targets with associated consequences for non-compliance.
On the Sanitary Dignity Programme, the Minister reaffirmed that DWYPD’s oversight function was constrained by the fact that National Treasury transfers funds directly to provinces. While DWYPD continued to monitor and workshop provincial implementation, it had no financial lever to enforce compliance or penalise non-performance. She stressed that “money follows function”, and if Treasury retained the allocation, it effectively retained the function. Consequently, she urged that Treasury be invited to future Committee meetings on this matter to account for the provinces’ performance and spending.
Addressing the Youth Development Bill, the Minister said DWYPD intended to submit it to Cabinet but had identified technical and legislative overlaps that required refinement. Some proposed structures in the draft bill affected DWYPD’s organisational design and thus required concurrence from DPSA under the Public Service Act and the Ministerial Handbook. The bill was therefore undergoing redrafting to align with existing legislation, ensuring clarity of purpose before public consultation, Cabinet consideration, and eventual tabling in Parliament.
In conclusion, the Minister informed Members that the Director-General, Adv Joyce Maluleke, had tendered her resignation effective end-October 2025, ahead of her contract expiry in December 2025, due to her reaching retirement age and accepting another post. The Minister praised the DG’s “astute diligence and exceptional knowledge,” commending her for championing key policy and legislative initiatives, including the National Council on Gender-Based Violence and Femicide Act, the Public Procurement Bill, and the establishment of Directors-General forums for gender mainstreaming.
The Minister described the DG’s leadership as instrumental in strengthening DWYPD’s stakeholder relations, guiding the G20 Empowerment of Women Working Group, and advancing transformative policies despite severe budgetary constraints. She expressed personal gratitude for the DG’s mentorship and hoped that her expertise would continue to benefit the sector, even post-departure. The Minister confirmed that the Presidency had been informed of the resignation and an acting Director-General would be appointed from November 2025 to ensure continuity.
Closing remarks
The Chairperson noted the unexpected announcement of the Director-General’s imminent departure. She described the news as a surprise, remarking that the DG had been a driving and passionate force within DWYPD, and invited her to offer farewell comments.
In her farewell message, Director-General Maluleke expressed appreciation to the Portfolio Committee for its constructive oversight, saying that their engagement had always aimed to strengthen DWYPD rather than embarrass it. She emphasised that clean audits are achieved through a collective effort involving the Audit and Risk Committee, the Portfolio Committee, and departmental leadership. She thanked the Minister and Deputy Minister for their support and stated that she would continue contributing to South Africa’s development in her future role.
Following this exchange, the Chairperson thanked the outgoing DG for her service and reiterated the importance of ongoing cooperation between DWYPD and the Portfolio Committee. She requested that before the DG’s term concludes, DWYPD submit in writing:
• Commitments and timeframes for filling all critical vacancies;
• An action plan with deadlines for finalising the outstanding legislative bills, including amendments and explanatory notes on delays;
• The country report on the UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities; and
• Outstanding evaluation reports and published findings, particularly those under Programmes 4 and 5.
She underscored the Committee’s expectation of detailed reporting on non-compliance by other government departments on:
• Employment equity targets for women, youth, and persons with disabilities;
• Implementation of National Strategic Plan on Gender-Based Violence and Femicide;
• Compliance with the White Paper on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities.
The Chairperson affirmed the Committee’s alignment with the Minister’s call to adopt extraordinary measures for improving outcomes for women, youth, and persons with disabilities, including consideration of a single implementing partner and the ring-fencing of funding for the Sanitary Dignity Programme. She proposed that the Committee involve both the Minister and National Treasury in future deliberations to address ongoing systemic challenges.
In closing, the Chairperson thanked the DG for her years of service and dedication, wished her success in her future endeavours, and extended gratitude to all participants. She noted that although the Audit and Risk Committee could not be engaged due to time constraints, it would be accommodated at the next sitting.
The meeting was adjourned.
