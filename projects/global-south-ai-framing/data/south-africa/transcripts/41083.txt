2025/26 Annual Performance Plans
The Department of Transport (DoT) briefed the Committee on its strategic and annual performance plans, and provided details of its budget, which amounted to R287.3bn over the next three years. Its key priorities were to align with government's goals to drive inclusive economic growth and job creation, and build a capable, ethical and developmental state.
The Department's presentation indicated how it intended to improve and modernise the road and rail network infrastructure; the strengthening of oversight functions in its state-owned entities to improve governance and prevent corruption; the establishment of the Driving Licence Card Accounts entity, and how it would deal with the current licence backlog; transformation and reformation in the aviation and maritime sectors; building transport infrastructure that was sustainable, energy efficient and adapted to climate change; and creating an efficient, safe and cheaper transport network system.
Committee Members raised questions about the driving licence backlog; the digitisation of driving licence cards; the challenges facing the Road Accident Fund; the Department's ability to adapt to climate change requirements; its reliance on consultants; the taxi recapitalisation programme; and ways in which public transport could be made cheaper and more accessible.
Meeting report
Deputy Minister's overview
Mr Mkhuleko Hlengwa, Deputy Minister of Transport, began his remarks by saying that the transport sector was one of the leading components of the country’s economy, and the Department had set sustainability and growth as priorities. The Department of Planning, Monitoring & Evaluation (DPME) and the Auditor-General of South Africa (AGSA) were institutions that were used to develop the Department's documents.
The Department had aligned with the three strategic priorities of the Medium Term Development Plan (MTDP) 2024-2029:
To drive inclusive economic growth and job creation.
Reduce poverty and challenge the high cost of living.
Build a capable, ethical and developmental state.
Upgrading and modernising transport network infrastructure
The Department aimed to align and set direct lines of sight in setting goals for the Department of Transport (DoT), State Owned Entities (SOEs), provinces, and the municipalities they worked with, in the delivery of outcomes and outputs. The span of control was considered in the clarification of responsibilities of the Department against its SOEs, provinces and municipalities, to deliver its transport mandates and service delivery targets in the rail, road, civil aviation, maritime and public transport industries. It was common knowledge that the decline in freight rail infrastructure, port inefficiencies and escalating fuel costs were crippling the economy. It was important that the Department reposition its ports and reduce tariff costs to attract trans-shipment traffic.
Deputy Minister Hlengwa said the troubled nature of the rail system had, over the years, led to disruption in the freight sector and resulted in the continent and the economy losing billions of rands. The rail passenger chain statistics between 2008 and 2022 identified a decline of 97% caused by weak infrastructure and aged rolling stock. As a result, more investment was required in the transport network to open new markets in Africa and create a solid platform for intra-African trade.
The Department had been allocated R95.7bn for 2025/26, R96.4bn for 2026/27 and R95.2bn for the 2027/28 financial year. The Department’s total budget over the medium-term expenditure framework (MTEF) was projected to be R287.3bn. This included transfer subsidies to public entities, departmental agencies and provinces and municipalities made through various grants accounting for an estimated 92.1% of this amount. Expenditure was expected to increase at an average annual rate of 3.3%, from R86.6bn in 2024/25 to R95.2bn in 2027/28. During the medium term, the Department would continue to focus on investing in infrastructure that revitalises passenger rail services, freight and logistics infrastructure, maintaining the country’s road network and facilitating integrated road-based public transport networks. The Department aimed to create a conducive environment for growth and job creation through inclusive economic activities and services.
Programmes and initiatives
The Deputy Minister said the 2024 National Macro-Organisation of Government (NMOG) had resulted in the disbandment of the Department of Public Enterprises. As a result, the Department’s mandate included oversight of South African Airways (SAA) and Transnet. The Department would continue to invest in rail corridor infrastructure to revitalise passenger and rail services. It would continue to recover essential rail corridors, as witnessed in the Western Cape. The agency’s co-interventions over the MTEF period would include restoring rail services in all corridors to encourage economic activity. This would be achieved by allocating funds to the agency through the rail transport programme over the period ahead, and would be used for maintaining, recovering and renewing the Passenger Rail Agency of South Africa (PRASA) fleet of rolling stock. This would modernise rail infrastructure, rebuild the signalling system, roll out new train sets to priority corridors, and increase rail passenger trips. The total transfers to PRASA over the medium term would amount to an estimated R66.1bn, with R40.8bn allocated for capital expenditure.
Over the MTEF period, R2.8bn would be re-prioritised from the South African National Roads Agency Ltd (SANRAL) to PRASA to address funding shortfalls in rolling out the stock fleet renewal program. R3.3bn would be provisioned through the budget facility for infrastructure, and transferred to Transnet through the rail and maritime transport programmes for necessary operation advancements and infrastructure upgrades to the entities in Gauteng, the Eastern Cape High Capacity Rail Corridor, and Cape Town’s container terminal expansion.
Transfers and subsidies
The road transport program initiatives were for building and improving road infrastructure, maintaining road networks, and providing adequate access to safer roads. The program would receive R161.7bn over the MTEF, and R106.8bn (66%) would be allocated to SANRAL, the primary implementing agent responsible for these objectives (see slide no. 74 for breakdown of budget). The Department allocated public transport network grants to municipalities to carry out infrastructure projects and cover indirect costs of fast and rapid transit services in Cape Town, George, Johannesburg, Nelson Mandela Bay, Polokwane, Rustenburg and Tshwane. The allocation amount would be R22.4bn over the medium term. The activities carried out through this fund were expected to result in an increase in the number of weekday passenger trips on bus rapid transit services from a combined 245 000 in 2024-25, to 337 000 in 2027-28. The public transport operations grant subsidies were expected to increase at an average annual rate of 4.5%, from R7.7bn in 2024/25, to R8.8bn in 2027/28.

Annual Performance Plan
Mr Mathabatha said the presentation was divided into three parts: (1) the Annual Performance Plan (APP) of the Department; (2) the Driving Licence Card Account (DLCA); and (3) the DoT's budget.

The APP targets for the Department and the DLCA.
Alignment of key priorities over MTDP period
The Department made sure they were aligned with the MTDP for the next five years, which was in line with the seventh administration. It also made sure there was a line of oversight between the three spheres of government -- the national Department of Transport and its entities, the provinces and the municipalities. It ensured that when setting targets, there was a clear distinction of responsibilities between the Department and its entities, provinces and municipalities. The service delivery targets were implemented by the Department’s entities and provinces.
In the DoT's strategic plan, the APP was reported under consolidated indicators. Most of the Department’s targets were in line with its mandate responsibility as a national department, and this informed the formulation of policy, legislation, strategies, and the monitoring of the implementation of the Department's policies and legislation. It made sure that when planning and reporting, this was aligned with the prescripts from the DPME and the Auditor General (AG). The presentation slides indicated the priorities set over the next five-year period as outcomes that the Department would like to see from the interventions it creates. Most of the Department’s targets were under priority one, which was to “drive inclusive economic growth and job creation (slides 5-10).
Targets
Several targets centred around participation from the private sector in ports and rail. The DoT's targets for investment in infrastructure, particularly in port and rail infrastructure, would be realised through the National Rail Master Plan that the Department had started, and would like to have approved by 2030. It would set targets to restructure Transnet. There would be a separation of the infrastructure and operation portfolios, with independent subsidiaries, and this process was underway. The deadline would be the end of the term. Jobs would be created through the Department’s infrastructure projects and targets have been set for this. Infrastructure programmes would come from the Airports Company of South Africa (ACSA) infrastructure programme, the SA National Roads Agency Ltd (SANRAL) and provincial road infrastructure projects.
Targets would be set to increase the volume of freight that would be moved. The target would be to increase freight movement to 250 million tons by the end of the term. The Department aimed to invest in airport infrastructure and operations so that over 1.2 million tons of air freight would be moved through OR Tambo International Airport per year. It also aimed to move 40 million passengers per year across the network.
Another target was establishing a transport economic regulator and a council. This would improve economic regulation in transport. The Department would like to have this regulator established and operational by end of the term. There was also a target to establish coastal shipping agreements which would facilitate trade and improve the Department’s inter-African trade operations. To secure targets on energy security, the Department would be working with the Just Energy Transition Inter-Ministerial Committee (JET IMC), where it had set targets for the DoT's Just Transition plan, and aimed to have this approved by the end of term. The Department's goal was to increase electric vehicles (EVs) in the market by 10%, with the required charging infrastructure that would meet this increased demand.
Building a capable and ethical state
The focus was on creating skills development in the transport sector. There were plans in place to implement the Department’s sector skills master plan by the end of term. It was reviewing its organisational structure to make sure it was fit for purpose, and this would be implemented by the end of term. There were huge challenges in the form of cyber-attacks, and the Department had set targets to deal with this. It would develop an enterprise architecture and implement it and all relevant directives as prescribed by the Cybersecurity Act (2020).
The presentation did not include all the Department’s targets -- it highlighted only the ones the Department felt would be important to present to the Committee. It had eight programs it used to implement transport sector priorities (see slide no. 23). Traditionally, there were seven programmes, and recently the Department had added the eighth -- SOEs, governance assurance and performance. This was inspired by the restructuring process, where Transnet and SAA had come into the Department’s fold.
All reported allegations of fraud and corruption would be investigated within 30 days of reporting. As indicated earlier, skills development was important in the transport sector, and the Department had targeted 6 000 skills development initiatives which would be implemented by the Department and its entities. It had also targeted to get an unqualified audit opinion.
Integrated transport planning (ITP)
The integrated transport planning (ITP) speaks to the JUST Transition Plan, and the Department would want to have this approved by end of the year. The green transport strategy plan had been revised and submitted to the Minister. The process for the establishment of the Transport Economic Regulator was in progress, although the Department had encountered challenges in amending the Act. It was targeting to include PRASA in the term to start the process. This process would continue into the future.
Oversight and safety of transport networks
The Railway Safety Act (2023) had been approved, and the Department had targeted to develop regulations to ensure safety in the rail environment. It was on track in confirming the devolution strategy of rail, and had targeted to have it submitted to Cabinet by the end of the financial year. It had started the process of reviewing the road safety strategy to reduce crash fatalities, and it should also be approved by Cabinet by end of the financial year. The Department would like to have the road infrastructure funding policy approved for submission to Cabinet by the end of the year. The Department’s road infrastructure project would add to job creation through SANRAL and the provinces. It would continue to do oversight inspections for these projects, including the construction of the Mtentu and Msikaba bridges in the Eastern Cape.
The targets in civil aviation were informed by the Department of Tourism, which had asked the Department to assist them in increasing flights to key destinations. The target was ensuring safety, and as a result, the Department had established the Independent Aviation Accident and Incident Investigation Authority, and hoped to have this established by the end of the year. The Department was committed to reviewing its memorandum of understanding (MOU) on air service arrangements with five states to increase flights to key destinations. It had also targeted developing a revised air freight strategy that would be finalised by the end of the year.
There was interest in reviewing the Department’s coastal shipping and improving intra-continental and global trade. The Department had targeted developing a coastal shipping agreement with three Southern African Development Community (SADC) countries. It had also targeted to have the draft National Ports Amendment Bill submitted to Cabinet. For governance in the maritime sector, the Department had targeted to implement and address at least 30% of the International Maritime Organisation (IMO) member state audit scheme's audit findings. It had targeted to carry out oversight inspections of fishing vessels to ensure safer communities, safer compliance and increased business confidence.
In public transport, the Department had started the process of developing a public transport subsidy policy. There had been a few delays involving the National Economic Development and Labour Council (Nedlac) and other issues. It was targeting to submit this policy to the Forum of South African Directors-General (FOSAD) cluster this year and take it to Cabinet. On the taxi recapitalisation programme (TRP), the Department aimed to scrap 100% of submitted and qualifying old taxi vehicles, and implement strategies to curb violence in the taxi industry.
Integrated Public Transport Network (IPTN)
The Department would assist 11 cities in the planning, implementation and operation of Integrated Public Transport Networks (IPTNs). To improve access in public transport, it had prioritised developing new standards for accessible vehicles. The
Shova Kalula

Driving Licence Card Account (DLCA)
The Driving Licence Card Account was a trading entity of the DoT responsible for the production and delivery of the driving licence cards throughout South Africa. It was a self-funding entity reporting to the accounting officer of the DoT. It was in the process of finalising its organisational structure, which it planned to have approved by the Minister by the end of the year. When its problems with the card production machine were resolved, it would be able to produce cards within 21 days, and was expected to provide around 2.5 million cards a year. The entity had targeted an unqualified audit opinion, and to collect at least 85% of outstanding debt and ensure payment of received invoices within 30 days.
The total revenue for the 2025/26 financial year was expected to be R321 million, and the largest contributor would be the sales of cards at R313 million. Expenses incurred through the compensation of employees, goods, services and depreciation, were expected to reach R379 million -- a difference of R58 million. R99 million was required for the procurement of material for the card production machine, and R49 million for the SMART card enrolment project. The DLCA was expected to spend R334 million on the new card production machine.
There were a few vacancies, including the head of the entity, the chief financial officer (CFO), and members of the audit and risk committee. However, there were interim measures, including people in acting positions who had been seconded from the Department to head up the entity. On internal audits, requests for quotations (RFQs) had been issued to source internal audit services.
One of the challenges with the driving licence cards was that some had been produced with an “error” -- they were blank at the back, where usually one would find the driver’s fingerprint and a barcode. The process to address this and make the cards right was underway. There was an attempt for an interim solution, and a memorandum of understanding MOU) would be signed between the government, the Department and the Government Printing Works (GPW). Since 20 April 2025, the backlog number was 679 665 cards that still needed to be produced, and this number was continuing to increase because the card machine was on hold.
Mr Mathabatha continued with the presentation. The Department had awarded the tender for the new permanent driving licence card machine through the Minister, and had engaged with the Auditor-General to do a probity audit into the procurement process. The audit had unfortunately highlighted irregularities in the purchasing process, and to date, the entire process was under review by the courts. The Department hoped the court process would declare the process invalid so they could restart the process and close any loopholes that had made the initial procurement process invalid. The Department would be signing an MOU with the GPW in the next 24 hours. The MOU would provide an interim solution that had the specifications and methodology already agreed on, and would inform the interim solution that would encourage a more seamless transition to the new system. The old machine had broken down 37 days ago and had created the backlog. This matter was under control, as there was an old working machine.

DoT's response
Improving economic infrastructure
Acting Director-General Mathabatha said the presentation had highlighted that the Department’s aimed at increasing crane handling per hour from seven, to between 25 to 40 cranes per hour. It would require Transnet to improve its workload and ensure there was quality infrastructure to support this, as well as to make sure old infrastructure was replaced at the ports and ensure the logistics chain was free of red tape and backlogs.
There were structural reforms in place including, for example, a rail infrastructure manager at Transnet. The manager would be responsible for separating the network infrastructure from operations. The aim was to pull in private and independent sector participation.
The Department did acknowledge the demographic inequalities in the aviation sector, but was working to create a space that was more inclusive. It considered the demographics of applicants, and Safair was an example of where the Department insisted on a quota system.
Cheaper transport network system
PRASA and the Gautrain had plans to expand into townships where rail did not exist. Gautrain currently was a mode of transport aimed at higher end users, but when it moved into rural areas it would be made more cost effective. The Department aimed to minimise the number of connections passengers had to use to get to their destination.
It had spent the last three years recovering the lines and corridors vandalised during COVID-19. It had recovered 40, a number that exceeded the Department’s target of 28. However, this work had slowed its plan to expand into new corridors. It aimed to finalise the master plan, which would guide investment in rail into the future. The Department was looking into the feasibility of high-speed rail, where it would prioritise the following corridors: Johannesburg, Musina, Durban and Gqeberha. This system would expand the rail network into rural areas. There were several other plans in place to make transport cheaper for freight and passengers.
Road Accident Fund
The inquiry into the Road Accident Fund (RAF) was being conducted by the Standing Committee on Public Accounts (SCOPA), so details would have to be sourced from them. The inquiry had not been initiated by the DoT, it was instead notified because it was a departmental entity. The Department had no control over the proceedings, and had not been part of its considerations. Mr Mathabatha deferred the subject of RAF and the inquiry to Deputy Minister Hlengwa, who reported that the RAF board members had been re-appointed with the understanding that they had a fiduciary responsibility to perform functions as prescribed. The Department’s role was to make sure there was compliance with the above. This was one of the Department’s priorities. He and Mr Mathabatha had met with the board of RAF and had briefed the Minister of the outcome of that meeting. The outcome included:
The Department was of the opinion that the RAF should not be in court.
The RAF had 21 days to do a full-scale report on what the consequences and ramifications would be if they approached the constitutional court and continued with the legal action they had launched.
The RAF must subject itself to accounting standards that are consistent with South African accounting standards.
The DoT and the Accounting Standards Board (ASB) were developing a guide to assist the RAF so it could be audited based on recognised accounting standards.
The RAF was a concerning subject for the Department, and as a result, there would be a parliamentary process of a committee inquiry. The DoT was engaged with its own processes with the RAF to bring stability within the entity. Further announcements would be made on this.
Re-capitalisation of the taxi industry
The Department had prioritised re-capitalisation and de-risking of the financial arrangement in the taxi financing space to encourage stability. Taxi re-capitalisation was part of the broader challenges and a factor that encourages unroadworthy vehicles on our roads. This would allow the Department to undertake interventions that make this mode of transport a more affordable option for passengers. As highlighted by Mr Mathabatha, it was an ecosystem that was complex and diverse. The guiding question was, how would the Department address these issues in ways that were sustainable and viable for the industry? The Department had two main challenges in formalising the taxi industry -- empowerment programs or projects in the Department, and the work done by the World Bank and the global positioning system (GPS) to be piloted in Tshwane. There would be an exercise to confirm that vehicles on a designated route belonged on that route. This would prevent overtrading of routes and fights over routes. Should there be an excess of vehicles on a particular route, the extras would be rationalised and deployed elsewhere, creating a fairer fee structure for the passenger and the taxi operator.
Transformation in the aviation industry
The Department had an aviation policy which would set clear expectations and guidelines on transformation. The policy had been approved by Cabinet and was out for public comment. Transformation in this sector was part of the reason for the review of the policy. The Department had developed I-LIFT, a strategy that makes it easier for local airlines to enter the aviation space. At the same time, it needed to prioritise the South African Airways (SAA) and ensure it did not suffer the consequences of such opportunities being made available, and was able to compete successfully in such an environment. The aviation sector had attracted almost the same numbers as before COVID-19. Although it had fully recovered, this did not mean the space had transformed. The Department had also developed an air freight strategy, which aimed to increase freight in those areas by engaging in different initiatives and programmes.
Transformation in the maritime industry
The Department was the first to admit it had not made full strides in transformation in the maritime industry, such as port infrastructure and post management issues. The Committee would shortly engage on the Merchant Shipping Act (1951), with the aim of officially revoking it and creating a space to propose ways to transform this industry. In reviewing the Act, it aimed to improve the registry for vessels. The Department also seeks to transform the space of the ownership of vessels that visit SA's shores and, upon registration, it would insist on specific quotas. It now also has the Comprehensive Maritime Transport Policy (CMTP), which addresses marine manufacturing and issues on transformation. There would also be reformation and transformation of the carpentry and taxation sections in this field. This would prevent the restriction of trade and create opportunities for people who would not have had exposure to this market. The issues of SOEs were part of a broader strategy. In general, SOEs needed to maintain and support the mandate of the Department.
Programmes to support cities
To further support transport in the cities, the Department was working with the Automated Fare Collection (AFC) system to produce a single ticketing system. It was reviewing the learner transport policy, and from this, it would be clear which type of vehicles needed to be contracted. To strengthen the policy, the Department would need to hold the contracting authorities accountable. Sourcing the quality and type of vehicle would depend on enforcing the provisions and other regulations in the policy as well as a contract management system. Whether these contracts were given out by either the provincial department of transport or the department of education, the responsibility would be on the province. In the past, there had been loopholes, and the Department aims to establish full control over this process for better ease of management. It would develop indicators that show those in charge how to adhere to these regulations. With help from the South African Police Service (SAPS) and the regulations in place, the Department would fulfil its mandate.
DLCA
The current backlog was around 680 000 and it would take a few months to clear. It must deal with the current processing of applications, so that it was working with the present backlog whilst minimising additional backlogs. The Department aimed to increase the printing of 6 000 cards per week to eliminate the backlog in the next three months. During that period, it would start the parallel process of an internal solution with the GPW. If the Department tests the new system from the GPW and it works, then it would move new applicants into this interim arrangement. The old machine would be used only to complete the backlog. If this works, the printing of cards would be restored to its full quota, the backlog would be eliminated, and the system would go back to printing what it needs to produce per day. The Department would keep South Africans updated on the progress of the DLCAs.
The Department did want digital licence cards. It was looking into the production and issuing of digital cards, and looking at the banking industry for inspiration. The challenges included making sure the cards could not be cloned or duplicated. The important factor in the digitisation of the driving licence card was to make sure security features could not be replicated. The current card system has served the Department relatively well, and the digital card would only improve on this. The SMART identity document (ID) had minimal security issues, so the Department had confidence in the mechanisms the GPW had put in place.
Another question was how the Department would manage the expiry dates on these cards. What mechanisms would be used to put this in place? Should the cards be loaded on people’s digital, mobile or electronic devices? How could it be disabled when it no longer needed to work? What type of indicators would measure the authenticity of the cards so that they read well for law enforcement? These questions had not discouraged the Department from the possibility of embracing new technologies. As the Department moved to artificial intelligence (AI) and digital, there were those who were working hard at trying to interfere and manipulate the space. The microchip, for example, was no longer a viable option because of the security vulnerabilities it posed. The Department did not want to regress in technological advancement when producing digital licence cards.
In the MOU, one of the few lines in the plan was that the GPW would continue with its research on the digital cards and mitigate against the risks associated with them. Key stakeholders in this space were institutions tasked with security knowledge, and on a programme like this they play an important role in guiding the Department to ensure that security measures were not tampered with. This should explain why South Africa still uses paper-based driving licence cards, and why the introduction of digital licence cards has not been rolled out. The cards would have to be user-friendly and convenient, but equally be tamper-proof. The GPW was looking into all of this as part of its research.
The Department was in talks with the GPW to investigate the issue of the digital licence card as a permanent solution. One of the reasons it had been slow in committing to the digitisation of licence cards was because it did not want to edit the current specific modifications, and in so doing tamper with the court processes. As such, the interim solution would produce a similar card specification instead of changing the specifications that were intended for the tender awarded, even though that process was under review in court. To start afresh and make new modifications might open the Department to legal action.
The Department had issued a communiqué on the issue of driver’s licence cards:
Licenses that had expired could be used legally for 21 days without attracting fines.
The current arrangement was that these licence cards could be used for a period of three months without attracting fines.
People who had temporary licence cards and were still waiting for permanent ones, but because of the faulty card machine they could not get one within the usual time frame, their temporary licence cards would expire after six months.
Should there be exceptions, the Department would look at these cases on a case-by-case basis, but there should be no additional costs besides the initial R70.
Decreasing reliance on consultants
Consultants were appointed for several reasons including:
If it was a once off project that required highly skilled personnel and experts in the field.
When skills were not available in the Department.
There were financial constraints that meant it would be cheaper for the Department to employ someone for a short period.
It was never a continuous arrangement. When consultants were used, the Department factored in a built-in “skills transfer mechanism,” which was a condition in its service level agreement (SLA). This was a process where skills outsourced through consultants were gradually transferred in-house to people who worked in the Department. If the service was continuous beyond the contract of the consultant, in time there would be someone in the Department who would have the skills required to do the job. This took time, but in the long-term, employment was created even if it meant more than one person was used to complete the job, and for that role, a consultant would not be used again. The Department would provide the Select Committee with a list of where they had appointed consultants and the skills sought, and the skills that needed to be developed in the Department and were part of the skills transfer process.
The Department used accountability processes as a way of ensuring it was provided with performance indicators, and financial and non-financial information that would improve its service delivery. These accountability measures included shareholder contracts with the Minister, and reporting to the Select Committee on a quarterly basis. This allowed the Department to quickly and effectively measure challenges, including wasteful or irregular expenditure. The Minister had introduced and communicated to its own DoT audit committees. This encouraged the Department to closely inspect reports from the audit committees of its entities, with the goal of ensuring full alignment with the work it was doing, as well as assisting in raising red flags when they appear. If one looked at the comparative tables issued by the Auditor General, where the entire sector had been assessed, there were several improvements, and the Department had reduced the number of disclaimers and adverse audit opinions.
Shortage of funds
The financial allocations had not grown as much as the Department would have liked and this had affected its work. However, with the guidelines from the APP, it should be able to do its best with the resources and funds available to it. If the Department were to cover the entire scope of public transport in South Africa for road and rail, it would need close to R35bn, and the budget did not cover this amount. In general, the Department would need funds four times what they were given, to successfully invest and create a cohesive public transport system in South Africa.
The Department was doing feasibility studies with some of the projects already mentioned, but the availability of funds was always important. It was key to ensure projects were both bankable and attractive investments for the private sector. The Department needed to make sure the policy environment was right and conducive for additional funding and support from the private sector. The Department had put in a request for information from potential buyers in the industry so that it could help it to create ways to expand. It had received more than 1 000 proposals, which was encouraging. The next step would be to study these requests for information and then follow this up by approaching potential buyers who had provided meaningful ideas that speak to the needs, skills, other terms of reference and/or specifications of the industry.
Budget underspending
Another delay that resulted in an underspend of the budget was a health issue: there had been a movement in templates for the outdoor rollout bacteria and money, because it was earmarked. It was safe now. The Department was working with the Just Energy Transition (JET) inter-ministerial committee (IMC), which aligns sectors to ensure cohesive decision-making for the nation’s energy transition. This formed one of the Department’s initiatives in expanding EVs and the charging infrastructure. These were some of the projects that could attract donor funding and other forms of assistance from multinational organisations, as well as countries in the European Union (EU), which have approached the Department and want to financially assist the Department with these programmes.
Fleet management
For the past 10 years, the Department has managed its fleet system differently from one province to another. It uses hired transport -- it does not own vehicles. It therefore had no experience with stolen vehicles, but there might be problems with provinces that use government vehicles. That would be the responsibility of government to employ more prudent fleet management processes and approaches in dealing with Government Garage (GG) vehicles, including ensuring the fuel is not misused by officials. At the national level, the DoT had a transversal contract with the National Treasury -- agreements negotiated for government use -- on the procurement and maintenance of vehicles. Government officials could only use companies contracted for the maintenance of vehicles. Initially Beyond Petroleum (BP) was the contracted fuel provider the Department used, and in cases where e-fuel was used, the DoT had mechanisms in place that confirmed the vehicle that consumed fuel was the intended vehicle and there was no misuse of petrol cards. Fleet managers or transport officers were responsible for the payment of the above services. Should there be any misuse, or outliers read on the system, these would be taken up through the proper channels.
Provincial road maintenance
As a result of strong public disapproval reported by provinces, the Department chose to transfer some of their roads to SANRAL for maintenance. The plan was that the provinces would have enough resources to service their networks. This was a process the Department had had in place for the last two years, and it ended last year. The window was now closed. In general, when a network was transferred, allocations were made based on the needs and length of the network, which SANRAL, the province, and the municipalities would have provided. SANRAL had strategic data, and was therefore used to assist roads that connected major cities. To date, there has been no follow-up on these funds from any of the nine provinces. Had the Department insisted on the continuation of this project, it might not have been sustainable. However, the Department had transferred approximately 2 500km of road network to SANRAL, which was making improvements to these roads.
Addressing climate change
The Department was aware of climate change, and as such had developed a green transport strategy supply and manufacturing programme. It promotes EVs and hydrogen energy, and had recently passed on the approval for the Just Energy Transition Plan in the transport sector. The Department would insist that a certain percentage of the fleet procured by cities use Public Transport Network Grant (PTNG) money. This promoted vehicles that use less carbon fuels and creates awareness of climate change.
Deputy Minister's closing comments
Deputy Minister Hlengwa said that the RAF must be stabilised and returned to its core function to ensure efficiency in the ecosystem and discourage maladministration and corruption. He believed the process Parliament was currently engaged with would assist in bringing this about. There was also a Special Investigating Unit (SIU) probe underway. The Department would await the outcomes from all these investigations and see the recommendations given.
The APPs that the Department and its entities had submitted to Parliament were the benchmark for oversight and accountability in the work the Department did. It would be available at the Select Committe’s discretion to address RAF-related questions. It was reviewing a lot of policy positions and presenting updated versions of programmes on the understanding the ecosystem and the transport sector had changed, and the Department needed to respond correctly to these dynamics.
Producing climate resilient engineering was important, and the Department would make sure SANRAL and the process of building infrastructure spoke to this. Also important was cultivating skills development in the Department. The Department would be in a position at a later stage to address DLCA questions and confirm updates. As indicated earlier, the Department was currently in court on the printing contract because of the findings by the Auditor-General. The sequence of events listed by the Chairperson, detailing the Department’s predecessors and the subject of the DLCA, should be understood through consideration of the following factors:
When those decisions and timelines were presented, AI was not as advanced as it is now. The Department must be cautious of moving from one space to another, thinking it was avoiding a problem only to inherit other problems.
The Department needed to review the ecosystem from a technological point of view.
The digitisation of driving licence cards was a resource question insofar as validation and alignment with international standards and protocols were concerned, for the Department to comply in respect of the legitimacy and security of the cards.
The digitisation program was on the Department’s agenda, and they were working on permitting a digital program system, but security, legitimacy and authenticity were paramount.
The issue of revenue generation was secondary.
What was key was having the right driver on the road who inspired confidence.
Chairperson's closing comments
The Chairperson expressed strong support for the Department’s plan to go “back to basics.” Transport was a country’s most important economic accelerator. No department could function efficiently if it were not for the DoT. It would be impossible to source agricultural goods from farms and transport it to harbours and then overseas. One would not be able to transport patients, source medicine and medical equipment in hospitals if it were not for the Department and all its subsidiaries. One could not get school children to school without the Department -- and the list could go on. The Department of Transport was indeed the catalyst around which everything revolves in building the economy and producing jobs. The Committee would continue to monitor the Department’s progress, and looked forward to future engagements.
