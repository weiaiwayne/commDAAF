Meeting Summary
Video
The Committee held a meeting to review the Department of Women, Youth and Persons with Disabilities' (DWPD) financial and performance reports for the 2024/25 fiscal year and the first quarter of the 2025/26 fiscal year.
Members raised several concerns, including the department's failure to provide a concise presentation as requested, issues with unmet targets, overspending and underspending, and the lack of progress on key legislation like the WEGE Bill and the Disability Rights Bill. The Department's leadership, including the Director-General, apologised for not submitting the required documents and for the lengthy presentation.
The Department reported a clean audit for the 2024/25 financial year, with 99% of its adjusted budget spent. It also achieved 81% of its targets in the first quarter of 2025/26.
Despite these achievements, the department acknowledged several challenges, including:
Late Invoice Payments:The department consistently failed to pay 100% of valid invoices within 30 days due to operational and system issues.
Vacancies and Human Resources:The department had a funded vacancy rate of 10.7% as of the fourth quarter of 2024/25 and 9.3% in the first quarter of 2025/26, with key positions remaining unfilled. This was attributed to long recruitment times, a lack of an e-recruitment system, and staff attrition.
Unmet Targets:The department had repeatedly failed to meet certain targets, such as establishing farming projects, due to a lack of funding and issues with centralised procurement databases. Delays in legislative processes also affected targets.
International Travel:There was a discussion about the cost of international travel, with the department leadership defending its approach by stating they always seek the cheapest routes and adhere strictly to the ministerial handbook. The leadership also refuted a media report about an R6 million expenditure for a UN trip, calling it a false and leaked submission.
The committee members expressed frustration with the ongoing issues of vacant positions and excessive spending on travel. They requested an updated organogram, a travel forecast, and a commitment to address the recurring problems. It was also clarified that the department's role is not to hold the Commission for Gender Equality (CGE) accountable for its budget, as the CGE is a Chapter 9 institution accountable to the National Assembly.
The Committee requested that the outstanding responses from the department reach the committee by Friday.

Opening Remarks by Minister
Minister Sindisiwe Chikunga began by apologising to the Chairperson if they had not submitted the required documents as requested by the portfolio committee. She sincerely apologised for that and assured the committee that they would ensure it did not happen in the future. It was a privilege to appear before the committee. They did so in full recognition that parliamentary oversight was not only a constitutional obligation but also a vital instrument to strengthen governance and delivery.
She explained that the mandate of the department was to advance the rights, dignity, and empowerment of women, youth, and persons with disabilities. She added that, for them, the true test of their work lay not only in the documents they tabled—although those were important—but in whether their policies and programs translated into tangible improvements in the lives of these constituencies. She then continued by stating that they came before the committee not only to account for the resources spent but also to reflect on the progress achieved, the challenges they were addressing, and the steps they were taking to accelerate delivery.
She continued by asking to begin with an important development in the area of governance. The department had received a clean audit for the 2024/25 financial year, which was finalised on the 31st of July that year. This was a milestone achievement that reflected their commitment to strengthening internal controls and managing public resources with integrity. The Department had put in place an audit action plan, which had been finalised to address some of the immaterial findings made by the Auditor-General, and it would be implementing this plan during the current financial year. She added that although these findings were immaterial for the time being, if they were not corrected or attended to, they could indeed become material findings in the future. She clarified that some of these challenges were the result of a lack of resources. For example, in the department, when it came to internal auditors, there was only one person who had to do the internal auditing before the Audit and Risk Committee met, and the same person also had to provide the secretariat function for the Audit and Risk Committee. This created an immense challenge for the committee, because as much as they wanted to conduct their work as fast and as broadly as possible, they were limited by having to rely on one person who already carried heavy responsibilities while also serving as secretariat to a committee constituted by external people. She said she was raising that as an example. However, she assured the committee that they would continue to report progress as part of their accountability obligations regarding the audit action plan.
She further said that this outcome served as a reminder that sound governance was not separate from service delivery, and that strong financial management and compliance were the foundation upon which meaningful programs for women, youth, and persons with disabilities could succeed.
In reflecting on the period under review, she highlighted a few areas where the department had made important progress. She reported that for the fourth quarter of 2024/25, the department had concluded the financial year, having spent 99% of its adjusted appropriation. Out of 34 targets, the department achieved 26, which amounted to 76%, while eight were not achieved. For the first quarter of 2025/26, she said they had a total of 32 targets and achieved 26, or 81%, while six were not achieved.
She acknowledged that while there was progress to report, they also had to account for the challenges they faced. While most of the targets were met, some were not achieved, and some were recurring. She noted that these, as the chairperson had already indicated, included targets relating to youth empowerment. She explained that in this case, service providers were sourced centrally, which was not within the department’s control. This centralisation applied across all departments, and the information in the central database system sometimes indicated youth-led and youth-owned businesses. However, when the department engaged directly with the service providers following the appointment, they sometimes discovered that these businesses were no longer within the youth age bracket. She added that the database was not always continuously updated to reflect the present age of the service providers.
She said that although this was the case, as a department, they believed that the matter required their attention. Since they were leading on youth empowerment, it was in their interest to ensure that correct information was made available not only for themselves but also for other departments. Therefore, she said, this was not a matter they thought should be left only in the hands of National Treasury, but something they too should take responsibility for to ensure that accurate information was available.
She also mentioned that some targets, such as farming and bakery start-ups, were dependent on other departments for delivery. She explained that they had requested those departments to mainstream such interventions for women and persons with disabilities. She admitted that it might be easy for the department to decide to remove these targets since, for example, the farming and bakery initiatives had been included for almost two years without progress. However, she appealed to the portfolio committee to understand and exercise patience with them, saying they would prefer not to remove those targets from their Annual Performance Plan. She explained that they wanted to keep them there while continuing to seek funding, not only within government but also outside of it, through sponsorships, to ensure that those targets could eventually be delivered because they were important for ordinary people, especially the poor. She emphasised that the department itself could not fund them, and they hoped that other departments would assist, while also seeking external funders. She cautioned, however, that they could never guarantee securing such funding, and therefore appealed again for patience and understanding from the committee.
She further referred to their legislative program targets, as the chairperson had mentioned, such as the WEGE Bill, the South African Youth Development Bill, and others. She explained that these bills were required to undergo compulsory state law advisory processes, which often took longer than anticipated, and this delay affected the achievement of their targets. She said, however, that they were hopeful that they would be able to move these bills forward as soon as possible.
She continued by addressing the issue of 30-day payments, saying that they had almost missed this target. In fact, she explained that the lowest performance during Quarter 1 of both the previous year and the current one was 97%, while in other periods they had achieved 99%. She added that even on this matter, there were explanations: if one looked at the actual number of invoices that could not be paid within the 30-day requirement, they were very few—sometimes as few as two. However, she emphasised that they were aiming at achieving 100%. She noted that the issue with targets was that they were only recognised as achieved if they reached 100%; even 99% was still considered an unmet target.
On the issue of the vacancy rate, she explained that the department was not much above 10%, which was the minimum requirement. However, she acknowledged that they should, in fact, be filling some of those vacancies. She further explained that they also had work posts that were not funded. She noted that when the department advertised one post, thousands of people would apply. Ideally, clerks in the HR unit should be appointed to capture these applications as they come in, but those posts were vacant. As a result, the applications waited for senior managers to capture them manually. She said that this became a huge responsibility for senior managers, who were expected not only to do their higher-level work—such as shortlisting and other senior functions—but also to capture applications, which was time-consuming. She explained that this highlighted the challenge posed by vacancies at the lower levels, which should have been filled to ensure that such work was carried out efficiently.
She added that, as they had indicated, the senior managers were requested to do this work manually, and for each application, they had to record information manually, which took an extremely long time. She said this was a matter they were paying attention to and hoped would be addressed. She also explained that some of the vacancies had arisen due to changes in leadership. When she and the deputy minister came in as part of the new ministry, some of the people who had been in their positions resigned, and the department had to bring in new people. That process created additional vacancies immediately, and the filling of those vacancies also took some time.
She continued by explaining that some posts required them to follow a normal recruitment process because, even if individuals were in their offices, they technically belonged to departments and were seconded in terms of the ministerial handbook.
Lastly, she said, she wished to make a comment to indicate that as a department, part of their mandate included meeting international obligations. This meant attending compulsory member state meetings and conferences, including international forums such as the United Nations, the African Union, the Southern African Development Community, BRICS-related programs, and many others. She added that they also received mandate-related invitations from both the President and the Deputy President to form part of delegations where programs for the empowerment of women, youth, and persons with disabilities were advanced. In some cases, they were requested to represent the President or Deputy President at such events. In those instances, they had to attend in person, unless a hybrid option was available. Whenever such an option was offered, they always took it. However, she explained that in the SADC region, even when hybrid options were available, there were serious political reasons requiring physical attendance. She noted that those reasons could be discussed in person if necessary, but reiterated that in the SADC region, they had seen the need for physical presence despite the hybrid alternative.
She went on to explain that the ministerial handbook prescribed the size of delegations for both the Minister's and Deputy Minister’s offices, and those limits represented the bare minimum numbers that could be taken. For every other trip, they considered the value that each additional member would add and prioritised essential subject experts. For example, she stressed that an international relations person was always required to accompany either the Minister or the Deputy Minister to provide the necessary support.
She emphasised that, given the department’s budget, they always took the leanest delegations possible, even though this sometimes affected their performance in those areas. She added that they always looked for the cheapest routes, which were often long and exhausting. As an example, she said that when travelling to the United States, they sometimes had to travel via the East because it was the cheapest route available. This meant flying about eight hours to the East, followed by another sixteen or more hours westward, including additional connections. By the time they arrived, she said, they were traumatised, extremely exhausted, and yet still expected to perform their duties immediately. She noted that this was the reality for their ministry and department, unlike others that could afford direct flights.
She explained that airline tariffs varied, with some being extremely high and others more reasonable, but their department always had to choose the cheapest options, even if it meant travelling unnecessarily long distances. She said that while other departments could afford to fly directly from South Africa to the U.S., they could not, and therefore had to endure exhausting journeys. She stressed that they always minimised and reduced the cost of overseas travel.
She firmly stated that there was no R6 million to be spent by the Minister for the United Nations General Assembly. She explained that this was simply a submission that had been leaked within the department before she and the Deputy Minister had even seen it. She expressed regret that there were officials capable of leaking such information and emphasised again that there was no such expenditure. She clarified that the figure originated from the cost of a ministerial suite, which sometimes amounted to around R296,000 per day. She stressed, however, that they had made it clear that such accommodation was unnecessary because, since they would be accompanying the President, meeting venues would be secured by the Presidency.
She explained that they had opted for a standard room, which, although still expensive, was significantly cheaper. She added that they had negotiated further discounts, reducing the cost from about R58,000 per night to R44,000 per night. She emphasised that they always complied strictly with the ministerial handbook when undertaking international engagements, taking no more than what was prescribed. For the upcoming UN General Assembly, she explained, they were supposed to take one person for youth and one for women, but instead decided to take just one person from the integration branch, with the intention of calling South Africa for additional information if needed—although she admitted that time zones made this difficult.
She concluded by stressing again that there was absolutely no R6 million expenditure for the UN trip. She said it was the result of mischief by some officials who had leaked information that did not exist, and it was unfortunate that ENCA chose to broadcast it without first checking with their offices. She said they had been just as surprised to see it in the media, and that it was disappointing that a media channel would report something without verifying it. She reiterated firmly that there was no R6 million expenditure for that trip.
The Chairperson thanked the minister for her opening remarks and also for the clarity provided on international travel. She noted that it was an ongoing conversation they would continue having with the minister because, at the end of the day, as they had indicated, it was good to know that the minister had placed emphasis on cutting costs, ensuring that every cent spent was of value to the taxpayer, and not deviating from the ministerial handbook. However, she stressed that, as they had said before, it was still a lot of money when R2 million or R4 million was being spent, and they needed to be assured that such spending would actually translate into meaningful action or resolutions being implemented in communities. She repeated that it was a conversation they would continue to have. She added that she had seen the Sunday Times report that the minister had forecasted spending R2 million on going to CSW, while in response to her, the minister had indicated spending R4 million. She concluded by reiterating that this was an ongoing conversation they would continue to have.

Quarter 1 2024/25
The DWYPD achieved 18 out of 22 planned targets, resulting in an 82% overall achievement rate.

Rights of Youth: 4 out of 5 targets were achieved (80% success rate). While consultations for the National Youth Service Programme and monitoring reports were successful, a project to establish five farming projects was not achieved due to a lack of funding.

The DWYPD achieved 22 out of 28 targets, for a 79% achievement rate.

Mainstreaming Women's Rights and Advocacy (MWRA): All eight targets were achieved (100% success rate). The department implemented an intervention related to the Sanitary Dignity Programme and monitored three provincial departments for their NSP on GBVF plans.
Monitoring, Evaluation, Research and Coordination (MERC): All five targets were achieved (100% success rate). This included an inception report for research on government priorities and monitoring five national departments on their implementation of the Gender Responsive Planning, Budgeting, Monitoring, and Evaluation Framework (GRPBMEAF).
Rights of Persons with Disabilities: 2 out of 4 targets were achieved (50% success rate). The Draft Disability Advocacy and Mainstreaming Strategy was not approved, and a research project on the impact of climate change on persons with disabilities was not achieved due to delayed procurement and budget constraints.
Rights of Youth: 4 out of 5 targets were achieved (80% success rate). The department did not establish any farming projects due to a failure to secure funding.

Mainstreaming Women's Rights and Advocacy (MWRA): 5 out of 6 targets were achieved (90% success rate).
Monitoring, Evaluation, Research and Coordination (MERC): All five targets were achieved (100% success rate).
Rights of Persons with Disabilities: All five targets were achieved (100% success rate).

Rights of Youth: The "South African Youth Development Bill" was not processed to be tabled in Cabinet. The delay was attributed to a hold-up in processing the cabinet memorandum, which will now be prioritised in the next fiscal year. The department did, however, achieve its target for the number of National Youth machinery meetings convened and the number of NYDA monitoring reports produced.

DWYPD Presentation
Adv Maluleke led the committee through the second presentation, focusing on the Department’s performance for the first quarter of the 2025/26 financial year.
Overall, the DWYPD achieved 81% of its targets, hitting 26 out of 32 planned goals.
The presentation highlighted several key achievements during the quarter. The department submitted a report on women in politics, decision-making, and employment to the SADC secretariat. It also participated in the Global Disability Summit in Berlin and the 18th Conference of State Parties of the UNCRPD, where it presented South Africa's position on topics like artificial intelligence and disability inclusion. In its legislative work, the department consolidated inputs for the Draft Disability Promotion and Protection Bill and processed the South African Youth Development Bill to the Office of the Chief State Law Advisor. The department also established the "South African Association for Youth Development Practitioners," a registered non-profit organisation focused on professional development for youth workers. Additionally, it collaborated on a Youth Peace Conversation in Mahikeng, North West province, to address community violence and instability.
Despite these successes, the department faced some challenges. It failed to achieve its target of paying 100% of valid invoices within 30 days, citing operational and system issues, including a delay by an end-user, technical problems during a system transition, and a misplaced payment document. The department also missed its target for procurement spending on entities owned by youth, achieving only 24.73% against a 30% goal. This was attributed to suppliers not regularly updating their profiles on the Central Supplier Database (CSD) and contractual payments changing as directors or shareholders age and fall outside the youth demographic. Two other targets, convening a SANDF-led National Youth Service Indaba and processing the South African Youth Development Bill to Cabinet for approval as a discussion document, were also not achieved.

Overall Financial Performance
The Department had an adjusted appropriation of R1.02 billion and spent R1.011 billion, which is 99.1% of its budget. This figure includes transfers to two entities, the Commission for Gender Equality and the National Youth Development Agency, which both spent 100% of their allocated funds. At an operational level, the department spent 95.6% of its R217.8 million budget.
Budget vs. Expenditure by Programme
Administration (96.3% spent):The underspending was mainly due to compensation of employees and will be reallocated to other programmes to cover overspending.

They had been using email instead, which resulted in high volumes of applications. These had to be printed to ensure accountability for every application, which was causing delays.
He also mentioned that, going forward, each position would be linked to a specific email address. However, due to the limited number of staff in HR—only two officials and a registry clerk (three people in total)—this continued to be a challenge.
On the HR Management Plan, he said it would be finalised as a result of the seventh administration. He noted that the Director-General had correctly stated that the Medium-Term Development Plan (MTDP) had been delayed in terms of adoption. The HR Plan was derived from the MTDP. Although the MTDP had now been completed, the draft HR Plan had been submitted to DPSA along with a request for an extension, which had been granted up to a specific date. He mentioned that a meeting with DPSA officials had been scheduled for Thursday at 10 a.m. to discuss the areas that needed correction. He confirmed that it would be a virtual meeting.
He added that after that meeting, they would finalise the draft HR Plan. He said the HR committee had requested a meeting for the following week to ensure that all branches were brought on board. However, they were still waiting for confirmation of the Director-General’s calendar to align the meeting schedule and involve managers and other relevant people to assist.
Lastly, on the issue of hearings, resignations, and contracts, he stated that those details would be submitted in writing, as they required careful review to ensure that the information provided was accurate.
Adv Maluleke provided clarification regarding the appointment of the AR position. She had discussed the matter with the DG of DPSA regarding a certificate requirement that had previously prevented the department from making an appointment last year, and even in April. She explained that the qualification in question was no longer available to young persons in South Africa, as the certificate had been discontinued.
She said that after speaking with the DG, she was advised that she could deviate from the requirement, approve the appointment without the qualification, and provide reasons for the decision. She confirmed that she had approved the appointment yesterday and that the person could start either as soon as possible or might need a month to relocate. She emphasised that the approval was within her delegated authority.
Ms Ranji Reddy, Acting DDG: Policy Stakeholder Coordination and Knowledge Management, DWYPD, responded that her portfolio was that of the Chief Director of Research, but she was currently not within the research unit because she was acting in the DDG (Deputy Director-General) position. However, she went on to speak about the size of the research unit and its human resource capacity. She explained that the unit was made up of one Chief Director, two Directors, one Deputy Director, one support staff member, and one secretary. That was the full size of the research unit within the department.
She then explained the functions of the unit, stating that it focused on research and knowledge management, with four specific functional areas:
-Research
-Policy work
-International reporting (which she said included all international reports), and
-Knowledge management.
She then shifted focus to the budget of the unit, especially in terms of financial capacity. She explained that on a year-on-year basis, the unit received anything between R1.2 million and R1.8 million as the goods and services budget. She pointed out that research was a very expensive undertaking, and with such a limited budget, the unit was restricted in what kind of research it could perform.
Despite these limitations, she stated that they generally allocated around R1 million to R1.2 million specifically for research projects. She added that everything else the unit did—such as supporting the work of other units, the Minister, and the Director-General—was all done in-house, without additional funding.
She noted that with this level of funding, they could typically only do one major research project per year, and the topic of that research was generally based on the needs of the department. She gave an example, saying that the Socioeconomic Empowerment Index, which fell under Programme 2, had been supported by the research unit. That particular research project was almost complete and was in the finalisation stage for presentation purposes.
She mentioned that they had worked closely with the Economic Empowerment Unit, and that two or three years ago, they conducted a study examining how women’s economic issues had been mainstreamed into the country’s economic plan. From that research, they were able to facilitate the development of a strategy for women’s economic empowerment, which was now being institutionalised by Programme 2.
She further explained that the unit sometimes worked in partnership with other organisations and institutions, and that where the department was unable to contribute funding, the unit's name would not appear on the resulting work. Nevertheless, the support was still provided. She gave the example of working with UNIDO, through which they produced a research project on women in the green economy, covering four areas such as wind, water, and energy. From that, a second phase emerged, leading to the development of a mainstreaming framework in the green economy, which was then handed over to the Economic Empowerment Unit to implement.
She mentioned that they had conducted workshops with various departments, including the Department of Forestry, Fisheries and the Environment, and the Department of Mineral Resources and Energy, to workshop them on the green economy document.
Additionally, she said they worked with UN Women on mainstreaming and were currently running a program involving a toolkit for gender-responsive budgeting. This toolkit was being piloted with National Treasury, and last year, the unit led all the workshops with the core departments participating in the pilot. She said that this year, they planned to do the same again in November, after concluding the G20 process, with the 15 departments currently undergoing the gender-responsive budgeting process.
She also stated that they were engaging with the OECD, in collaboration with Stats SA, to carry out research for the SIGI study focused on positive masculinities. She hoped that this research could eventually become a regular periodic study conducted at the national level.
She said the unit was also working with the ILO on advancing a national policy on the care economy, and that they aimed to develop a national strategy on this issue. She reminded those present that they had previously worked with National Treasury and the IMF on that process.
She then spoke about the Knowledge Hub, saying that they had reached a certain point in its development but had to stop due to funding constraints. They had completed the first phase, which included the backend/user interface, and had placed it on the department’s intranet. However, taking the Knowledge Hub further would be a very expensive and costly exercise. She explained that in the previous year—or the year before—the cost of continuing the work was estimated at around R6 million, and the department simply did not have the funding. As a result, the project had been put on hold until adequate funding became available.
In terms of knowledge management, they had engaged with the Government of Canada, and through full sponsorship, they had developed an online training course. This course was adapted from the Canadian model of the Gender-Based Analysis online training and had been fully contextualised for the South African environment.
She stated that the course had been placed on a platform, had undergone piloting, and approximately 350 people had gone through the pilot phase. Following the pilot, the course had been distributed to a wider audience, and there had been tremendous uptake of the program.
She expressed the view that it would be good, in the future, to make the training available to the Portfolio Committee as well. She emphasised that it was free, online, and could be done at one's own time and pace. Upon completion, participants would receive a certificate.
She then moved on to the issue of surveys, acknowledging that conducting them was a very expensive undertaking. She explained that even attempting to make projections on unemployment over time would require them to rely entirely on Stats SA.
She shared that Stats SA had recently informed them that conducting one survey covering 30,000 households cost approximately R115 million, and that they were planning to expand that number to 40,000 households, which would increase the cost to R131 million.
She further recalled that when they had engaged with Stats SA to conduct a new Time Use Survey—which would allow analysis of time spent by men and women on gendered activities—the department was told that the last Time Use Survey, conducted in 2010, had cost R93 million.
She concluded that there was no way the department could afford to conduct research of that nature on its own.
Ms Shoki Tshabalala, Deputy Director General: Social Transformation, DWYPD, responded by addressing the issue of Rapid Response Team (RRT) training, stating that before any training sessions commenced, they always consulted with the relevant local municipality. In some instances, she explained, the municipalities would offer a venue, such as using their council chambers. When those chambers were unavailable, they would then proceed via supply chain management (SCM) processes to secure an alternative venue.
She emphasised, however, that trying to conduct training within the communities themselves came with many challenges. She explained that such efforts had at times made them appear unprofessional and disorganised, primarily due to a lack of proper infrastructure. She gave examples such as arriving at venues to find no functioning ablution facilities, no water, and a complete lack of networking facilities like Wi-Fi or strong internet connectivity, which was essential in cases where online access was needed.
She further stated that in cases where catering had to be provided, issues often arose during the due diligence process conducted by SCM. A day or so before the event, they would discover that some of the catering service providers did not qualify—some had used fraudulent certificates or borrowed certificates from others. When such issues were raised at the last minute, they would be left with only 2 to 3 days to initiate a new procurement process, which itself could take up to 7 days.
As a result, she said, these disruptions had at times forced them to personally purchase food using their own money to proceed with the training, after which they would submit claims for reimbursement. She acknowledged that this situation reflected poorly on them, despite their efforts.
She reiterated their commitment to continuing to use municipal facilities as far as possible and to improving the training delivery process.
On the remarks made regarding the Sanitary Dignity Programme, particularly around advocacy, she said that they took those as comments and affirmed their commitment to continuing advocacy efforts. Specifically, she stated that they would continue to advocate for the ring-fencing of funds for the Sanitary Dignity Programme with National Treasury.
Regarding the two-day workshop and whether it added value, she clarified that it was an M&E (Monitoring and Evaluation) workshop, conducted by the unit leading the Sanitary Dignity Programme. The workshop had been held because they had realised that monitoring was not happening as it should and that regular reporting was lacking.
She explained that the workshop covered training on the M&E framework and on concepts related to monitoring and evaluation. In addition, they had ensured that there were systems in place to assist the stakeholders in implementing M&E more effectively.
She continued by saying that some of the oversight systems or committees they were working with included school-based support systems, WASH (Water, Sanitation, and Hygiene) committees, and provincial sanitary dignity committees. These structures, she explained, assisted the department in strengthening oversight work, particularly in ensuring that oversight continued beyond the department’s physical presence.
In addition to the two-day M&E workshop, they had also used a third day for the WASH assessment activity, which, she noted, had been very helpful. That activity involved physical oversight visits to schools to assess whether ablution facilities met the required standards from a Menstrual Health Management (MHM) perspective and whether those facilities were girl-child friendly and responsive. She clarified that this additional day of oversight was the reason the activity extended over several days, as part of their comprehensive M&E oversight efforts.
She provided an example related to the NSP (National Strategic Plan) work, explaining that for each pillar of the NSP, there were designated committees and chairpersons, all of whom had action plans. These committees provided monthly reports to the department, which were then compiled as part of the quarterly reporting process. Additionally, she said that they ensured that the NSP M&E framework was used to engage with departments to determine whether those departments remained aligned with the monitoring and evaluation structure of the NSP. Departments, she said, reported to them quarterly, including on economic aspects of the work.
On the economic empowerment strategy, she said they had developed a matrix used to monitor departmental activities. Departments reported quarterly based on the domains set out in that matrix, and regular engagements were held.
She then addressed a question about partnerships with NGOs and the private sector, confirming that they were indeed working with various stakeholders. She mentioned partnerships with banks such as Standard Bank and ABSA, highlighting a recent trade fair held during the launch of Women’s Month, where both banks had actively participated. She described the event as very good and powerful, noting that the banks had provided training to women on de-risking, accessing funding, and business pitching.
She shared that women had pitched their business ideas during the event and that prizes were awarded to strengthen their services. In one case, a woman received R200,000 in support to begin her work. She also mentioned ongoing partnerships with UNFPA, UN Women, and the European Union (EU), emphasising that the department was bringing critical stakeholders on board to enhance and strengthen their work.
Finally, she spoke about the oversight work already done, specifically mentioning that the Minister had visited Matatiele following a series of Gender-Based Violence (GBV) incidents that had been reported there. She stated that they had gone to assess the situation and had returned for a second visit to follow up, checking in with the affected families to assess how they were coping and whether the services provided were yielding the desired outcomes.
She concluded by saying that, despite being thinly stretched, they were doing their level best in this area of work.
Adv Rosey responded that three questions had been raised to them. In relation to the assessment of deviations, she explained that during the previous financial year, they had raised concerns regarding the deviations and had recommended improved planning on the part of the department to avoid such deviations in the future.
However, she also clarified that despite those concerns, the Internal Audit unit does undertake an assessment of all deviations, thereby assuring that the deviations were handled appropriately and in accordance with National Treasury regulations.
With respect to the current financial year, she said they had been informed by management that the deviations were largely due to predetermined venues used during the G20 engagements. She explained that this situation was beyond the control of the department, as these venue selections were the result of safety and precautionary measures applied by the Department of International Relations.
She added that this rationale brought them more comfort than concern, as it reflected external constraints rather than internal mismanagement.
On the matter of the procurement spending recovery plan, she said the issue specifically related to capital assets, where spending was at 29% at one point, but by the end of the financial year, spending had increased to 83%. She further stated that they had received assurance from Internal Audit that the recovery plan was being implemented.
She noted that R5 million had already been spent out of the intended R6 million, and the remaining R1 million was due to equipment not yet delivered, which meant that invoicing had not occurred. Nevertheless, there was a clear intention to ensure that the entire recovery plan would be implemented as recommended.
Regarding the review of the ARC charter, she confirmed that both the Internal Audit Charter and the ARC Charter for the 2023/24 financial year had been reviewed and adopted for implementation in the 2024/25 financial year.
She further stated that, in the new financial year, they had approved an interim internal audit plan, which included the review of the charters during the second quarter, with a deadline set for the 30th of September. She concluded by saying that this exercise would be undertaken accordingly in the second quarter.
Closing Remarks by Minister
She thought the DM would probably want to say something, but she was unsure whether there was enough time for that. She thanked the committee for the questions and comments they had made, adding that those inputs had actually opened their eyes in certain areas. Of course, she noted, they were also able to respond to quite a number of the questions.
She stated that she had taken note of what had been said, for instance, regarding international travel and the concern for value for money. She said she believed they could always prove, beyond a reasonable doubt, the value for money for any international travel they undertook. She expressed confidence in that.
She went on to mention that the ministerial handbook was amended very frequently, and that the current version was the most lenient of them all. She recalled that, when she had served as a Deputy Minister, there had been technical staff appointed under the ministerial handbook to provide support. However, she pointed out that those provisions had now been removed. Despite this removal, she said, Deputy Ministers were still expected to seek advice and make decisions, even though they now had to do so without any support. She emphasised that this was one of the issues they faced and reiterated that the ministerial handbook was indeed updated very frequently, with the current version being the most lenient. She noted that sometimes, this situation made it impossible to properly carry out their work as executives.
She acknowledged the committee’s comment about value for money, stating that they took note of it and agreed it was something they must always be able to prove. She also said the DG had been correct in pointing out that for the CSW (Commission on the Status of Women), ministers or politicians were only present for a week, whereas officials remained there for two weeks. She added that this took place in New York, in US dollars, and emphasised that there were some aspects—like the exchange rate and the cost of living—that they had no control over. Nevertheless, she stressed that they always took into account what the portfolio committee said.
She then added that she also wanted to say something else: she had not been aware of the request for land for a person with a disability, but said that the frustration expressed was also their frustration. She confirmed that if the name of the person and the request could be provided, they would follow up. She explained that they depended on other ministers and had already had an engagement with the Minister of Land, during which they requested that, as part of their legacy projects for the G20, land be handed over to women. This would allow women to say that during the year South Africa held the G20 presidency, they had actually received land. However, she emphasised that this depended on the Minister of Land, with whom they had already had engagements, as well as with many other ministers.
She explained that the same applied to other projects they were discussing, such as farming, bakeries, and others. She said that while they were engaging with various departments, they had also made the decision to involve other institutions, including the private sector, as the DDG had indicated. This included working with banks and other stakeholders.
She stated that following the meetings they had held with these institutions, they were now working on an implementation plan for the period after Women’s Month. The goal was to collaborate with the private sector, civil society, and others to determine the way forward after having had so many engagements and events. For example, she noted that the trade fair had already received funding commitments from Standard Bank and ABSA. She mentioned that even some of their G20 projects were dependent on these institutions for funding, because the department had only been allocated around R5 million for the G20, which she said was not enough even for the very first event that took place in the North West.
She then said that they were indeed engaging with others. For that reason, they were asking the portfolio committee to be patient and to understand that while they could not implement everything immediately, they were trying their very best to secure funders and ensure that as many projects as possible could be implemented—particularly those that they could afford to carry out.
She continued by saying that they also wanted to mention the issue of the department’s resourcing, as had been raised by the chairperson of the audit and risk committee. She emphasised that this issue was having a negative impact on the department. She shared that, as a health professional, she could clearly see that some people who attended meetings were not well, yet they still came to work and then had to travel to various provinces, because the department was responsible for the entire country.
She noted that when such situations occurred, one could only cross one's fingers and hope nothing happened to those employees, because they were thinly spread, overspread, in fact. She highlighted how the same small group of people was expected to handle responsibilities related to Women’s Month, the G20 Empowerment of Women Working Group, invitations from civil society, and various other tasks.
She cited an example from the disability branch, where there were only three staff members: two chief directors and one director. She said that this was unique to their department. If someone had asked her before she joined the department whether such a staffing structure existed in the public service, she would have said it couldn’t happen—that one could not have a chief director without a span of control beneath them. Yet, she confirmed, it was indeed happening in this department.
She reiterated that the lack of resources made it very difficult to place demands on staff. Nonetheless, they still had to demand work and ask staff to take on multiple responsibilities. But she admitted that sometimes, they did so with clenched teeth, wondering just how far those employees could realistically be pushed to implement what was required.
Despite these challenges, she expressed appreciation for the questions raised by the committee and said they would be able to respond. She clarified that they definitely did not hold the Commission for Gender Equality (CGE) accountable for the money it received. She described their role as akin to a post office: they simply transferred funds to the CGE. She emphasised that the CGE was a Chapter 9 institution under the Constitution and was accountable to the National Assembly, not to the department.
She stressed that they never called the CGE to account for how the funds were spent. Their responsibility was only to transfer the allocated amount to the CGE. She noted that the CGE did not have its own vote in Parliament, which was likely why the funds were routed through their department. However, she reiterated that this routing did not make the department accountable for the CGE’s financial conduct.
In contrast, she pointed out that the National Youth Development Agency (NYDA) was a state-owned entity under their purview. As the responsible ministry and shareholder, they held the NYDA accountable for the funds transferred to it.
She concluded by thanking the committee again for the questions and acknowledged that there were some they had not been able to respond to due to time constraints. She assured the committee that they would definitely respond to those questions in writing.
Finally, she emphasised that they took such engagements very seriously. That was why she, the Deputy Minister, and the Director-General were present—to listen as members of the committee raised questions and held them accountable, which, she affirmed, was the committee’s constitutional mandate as the portfolio committee.
Closing Remarks
The Chairperson expressed her gratitude by thanking the Minister, Deputy Minister, the Director-General, their entire delegation, and all members present. She apologised for having to rush slightly towards the end of the meeting, explaining that it was due to the need to avoid being late for the President’s question-and-answer session.
Taking the opportunity to speak directly to the Minister, she expressed appreciation for the way the Minister consistently listened to their inputs and responded in a respectful manner. She emphasised that such engagement was highly valued and went a long way in strengthening their relationship.
She reiterated that the committee would always follow the "rands and cents." She acknowledged that the department did not have a large budget, but stressed that this was precisely why there had to be strict oversight. Before advocating for additional funding for the department, the committee needed to be assured that every rand and cent was being spent in a way that truly brought about positive change in the communities they were elected to serve.
She then reminded everyone of some key resolutions. She noted that the Minister and Director-General had already touched on some of them. Firstly, she requested that the outstanding responses from the department reach the committee by Friday.
She also re-emphasised the importance of the department honouring the committee’s requests regarding how documentation and presentations should be formatted and structured. She noted that such requests were made to enhance clarity and efficiency.
She mentioned specific outstanding items:
The BRRR (Budgetary Review and Recommendations Report) and the budget vote implementation plan and report were still outstanding.
The committee also needed to be furnished with the updated organogram, including staff allocations and salary levels, which she said would be helpful.
Additionally, they required an overseas travel forecast and costing plan to keep the committee informed and involved in relation to international trips.
She asked that the report the DG had wanted to present earlier also be submitted to the committee.
She noted that all committee members had echoed one specific concern: there was a general call for the department to prioritise its targets related to key legislation—the very legislation the department was mandated to drive forward.
Finally, she noted that they had discussed the need for better financial planning and cautioned against deviations from planned expenditures. While the committee had heard and understood the reasons given for the deviations in this instance, she still emphasised the need for vigilance in future planning.
She closed by thanking all colleagues and everyone present on the platform for their participation.
The meeting was adjourned.
