Western Cape Government Departments Annual Reports 2024/25
The Public Accounts Committee (SCOPA) met to receive briefings from the Western Cape Gambling and Racing Board (WCGRB) and the Western Cape Provincial Treasury on their 2024/25 Annual Reports.
Part of the engagement with the WCGRB was held in closed session due to governance matters under discussion. In the open session, the Committee focused on governance stability, internal controls, Public Finance Management Act (PFMA) compliance and the entities’ overall financial performance.
Members engaged the WCGRB on its risk assessment, the impact of board vacancies on its ability to fulfil its mandate, and the internal audit findings relating to procurement and finance capacity. They also questioned the Board on irregular expenditure, late supplier payments, and the composition of its receivables.
The WCGRB attributed earlier governance risks to vacant Board positions and staffing shortages, confirmed that these had since been addressed, and outlined the measures taken to strengthen supply chain management (SCM), including checklists, staff training and post-audit monitoring.
The briefing by the Provincial Treasury centred on municipal budget credibility, audit outcomes, organisational capacity, and compliance with PFMA requirements. Members interrogated the number of municipalities that had adopted unfunded budgets, support provided to weaker municipalities, and the ten municipalities that did not achieve clean audits. Other issues raised included staff well-being, reliance on consultants, irregular and fruitless expenditure, ICT asset losses, supplier payments, and the status of staff and municipal debt recovery.
Treasury responded that it continues to provide technical support to municipalities, listed the six municipalities with unfunded budgets, and outlined its internal capacity-building initiatives, including a structural review, flexible work arrangements and staff-wellness programmes.
It further clarified the circumstances that led to irregular, fruitless and wasteful expenditure, explained the strengthened internal controls, and reported progress in reducing consultant use and improving supplier payment systems.
Both entities received clean audit outcomes.
Meeting report
Note: The first portion of the meeting with the Auditor-General of South Africa (AGSA) was closed to the public
Chairperson’s Opening Remarks
The Chairperson welcomed Members to the meeting and acknowledged that it had been a long day. He noted that this marked the final sitting of the Public Accounts Committee (SCOPA) for the three-week period. He outlined that introductions would first be conducted for the racing and gambling entities, followed by the Department.
The Chairperson identified himself as the Chairperson of SCOPA and introduced Mr Dustin Davids, who is the Procedural Officer, Western Cape Provincial Parliament (WCPP). He then invited Members in the chamber and those joining online to introduce themselves before handing over to the ministry.
Briefing from the Western Cape Gambling and Racing Board (WCGRB)
Ms Deidré Baartman, Western Cape Minister of Finance, thanked the Chairperson and provided introductory remarks for the entities under her portfolio.
She stated that the role of the WCGRB was to control and regulate gambling within the province to ensure a stable, consistent and effective regulatory environment. This was aligned with its mission to inspire public confidence and trust in a system free from corruption and unlawful gambling, and to contribute to the provincial economy in an innovative and socially responsible manner. She invited the Board Chairperson to elaborate.
Minister Baartman noted that her priorities were value for money, innovation and trust, with a focus on supporting long-term financial sustainability. She reported that the Treasury had published three major research documents: the Provincial Economic Review and Outlook (PERO), the Medium Term Budget Policy Statement (MTBPS) and, for the first time, a Local Government Budget Performance Review.
She indicated that two budgets had been tabled: an adjustments budget focusing on disaster relief and frontline services, and a main budget focusing on growth and jobs, noting it was a difficult budget year.
The Treasury had provided interventions in three municipalities in terms of Section 154 of the Constitution:
Beaufort West, which moved from a Stage 1 to a Stage 2 intervention.
Kannaland, where a support plan was developed and adopted, assisted the municipality in completing its financial recovery plan.
Theewaterskloof, where urgent support was provided to restore financial order, and a financial recovery plan was concluded subsequent to the financial year.
Minister Baartman reported that both the WCGRB and Provincial Treasury received clean audit outcomes, with Treasury achieving its 12th consecutive clean audit.
She further noted that Treasury had successfully lobbied for updates to the provincial equitable share, with additional funding reflected in the main budget, and had supported departments in accessing national infrastructure funding through the Budget Facility for Infrastructure (BFI). She thanked the WCGRB for contributing additional revenue to the Provincial Revenue Fund and acknowledged the Treasury team for their work, expressing anticipation of further engagement with the Committee.
Ms Julinda Gantana, Head of Department (HOD), Provincial Treasury, noted that the Provincial Treasury was the smallest Treasury in South Africa, with 200 staff during the review period and 245 currently, overseeing and influencing budgets of over R200 billion. She stated that this reflected a solid governance foundation which the Department aimed to build on.
Mr Claude Bassuday, Chairperson of the WCGRB, presented the Board’s annual report for 2024/25.
He noted that the Board was governed by a fully constituted executive board of seven members and highlighted the Board’s mandate to regulate gambling, inspire public confidence, contribute to the provincial economy, and address gambling-related risks. He reported that the annual report demonstrated the Board’s alignment with government priorities, its commitment to prudent financial management, and responsible stewardship of public resources, culminating in a clean audit outcome.
Discussion
The Chairperson thanked the Minister for the briefing and reminded Members that when asking questions, they should provide the page number for reference. He indicated that the Committee would first cover governance for the Gambling and Racing Board, spanning pages 48 to 71, and asked if there were any questions on governance.
Mr B Petrus (PA) referred to pages 62 and 63, concerning strategic risks 1 and 2. He noted that the risks described the entity's inability to perform its mandate and characterised it as an ineffective and inefficient entity. He stated that this was a very strong and serious risk rating and asked what the entity had done to address it.
Ms P Lekker (ANC) referred to pages 68 and 69. She noted the mention of a clean audit with no material findings on performance or compliance, but pointed out that the internal audit had identified weaknesses, particularly in procurement and finance capacity. She asked how the Board was addressing these internal audit findings, especially given that similar issues had been raised by the Auditor-General of South Africa (AGSA's) office.
On the risk related to the inability to perform its mandate, Mr Bassuday responded and explained that a major risk had been the expiry of board member terms, which threatened the quorum required for critical decisions, such as licensing. He stated that this had been dealt with effectively and the Board was now fully constituted. The other significant challenge was staffing, with a number of vacancies. A review of staffing requirements had been completed, but filling posts was difficult due to an uncompetitive remuneration structure. The entity was implementing other controls, such as outsourcing and contract employment, and hoped to fill the vacancies as soon as possible. He invited the CEO to add to his response.
Ms Zoe Siwa, Chief Financial Officer (CFO) of the Board, referred Members to pages 62 and 63 of the report. She explained that, for example, the quorum requirement of five out of seven Board members was a control to ensure effective governance. She also cited adequate budget processes, resource planning, and staff training as measures to address risks like ineffective operations.
Regarding internal control deficiencies, Ms Siwa stated that internal audit findings were managed similarly to external audit findings. The Board would respond to each finding, agree or disagree, and then log it in an audit matrix. These findings, both internal and external, were monitored quarterly to track the implementation of recommendations.
In response to a question about resource constraints in the administration department, she clarified that this was an entity-wide issue. She explained that the Board was initially classified as a small public entity, and despite industry growth, the staff complement had not been adjusted. The Board was currently working to address these resource constraints.
The Chairperson thanked the presenters for their replies and asked if Members had any follow-up questions. Seeing none, he moved the discussion to the PFMA compliance report, covering pages 80 to 85. He complimented the clear explanation on page 84 regarding the 30-day supplier payment issue, which had been raised the previous year. As there were no questions on PFMA compliance, he moved on to the financial reports and invited Ms Lekker to speak.
Ms Lekker raised several points. On pages 80 and 81 regarding irregular expenditure totalling R704 770 for 2024, with a further R13 000 under assessment, she requested details and asked if any disciplinary processes or corrective measures had been taken. She asked what had been done to strengthen supply chain management controls to prevent recurrence. On page 84 concerning the non-payment of suppliers, she sought clarity on measures implemented to ensure suppliers, particularly small and emerging businesses, are paid within the prescribed 30 days.
Lastly, she asked if the Department monitors recurring delays to prevent late payments that negatively affect supplier cash flow and confidence in doing business with government.
Ms Siwa responded to the questions. On the irregular expenditure, she explained that the R13 000 was related to generator services for the Board's building, which the AGSA flagged as not following a competitive process. The R704 770 related to repairs and maintenance for plumbing and electrical work. This was identified as irregular because regulations require advertisements for construction services to specify a minimum Construction Industry Development Board (CIDB) rating, which was not done. She noted this issue arose as the Board, now owning its accommodation, had to procure maintenance services it did not previously require, and the Board had no supply chain officials for the full year under review.
Regarding corrective measures, Ms Siwa stated that two supply chain officials had since been appointed and compliance checklists for transactions were being implemented. Progress would be monitored quarterly by the Audit Committee and the Finance and Information Technology (IT) Committee. She added that the R704 770 was still under assessment and would be taken to the Finance and IT Committee in November, after which the Board would determine if disciplinary processes were required.
On supplier payments, Ms Siwa clarified that the Board pays upon receipt of an invoice, not after waiting 30 days. She noted that only 14 invoices in the year were paid outside the 30-day period, primarily because the Board was awaiting necessary information such as bank details or pricing clarity from the suppliers themselves.
Mr L van Wyk (DA) referred to the receivables and the AGSA’s comments on debt collection. He noted on page 96, under the financial position, receivables from non-exchange transactions of about R62 million, which appeared to be taxes not yet passed to the provincial Treasury. He also noted just under R1 million from other transactions. He asked for clarity, questioning if the R62 million represented less than two weeks' worth of taxes, given an estimated monthly tax intake of around R145 million, and enquired about the standard practice for passing these taxes through.
He also referred to other receivables on page 96, which cross-referenced note 5 on page 118. He noted small amounts outstanding for 30 days and longer and sought clarity to put the AGSA's comment into perspective.
Ms Siwa responded to the matter of taxes, explaining that bookmakers paid taxes weekly, while casinos and other operators paid monthly. She noted that the R61 million represented taxes due at the end of the financial year, which would only be paid in April. She confirmed that there were no connectivity issues, as the Board collected the funds and transferred them to the Provincial Treasury, which in turn paid them into the fiscus.
Regarding other receivables on page 118, she noted R113 000 outstanding for longer than 30 days. She explained that debtors were required to hold monies in trust funds, and the Board collected from these funds as work was performed. The outstanding amount occurred due to insufficient funds in the trust accounts. However, the Board could recover from associated security accounts or, if the licensee was associated, recover the amounts later. She confirmed that the R113 000 had been recovered subsequent to the financial statements. She acknowledged that the collection system and process could be improved but stated it was not a case of the Board being unable to collect.
The Chairperson thanked Ms Siwa for her response and asked if there were any further questions on the financials for the Gambling Board. Hearing none, he stated he would ask the board chairperson to give closing thanks, followed by the Minister.
Mr Bassuday thanked the Chairperson for the opportunity and privilege to present their report and to answer questions, noting that the Board remained available for any follow-up information.
Minister Baartman congratulated the Board and the entity on their clean audit. She emphasised that the work of the Board was done on behalf of the entire Western Cape Government, and the revenue it generated was critical for the provincial budget process, helping to address funding deficits from national allocations. She committed to continuous improvement and stated that the Provincial Treasury and the Board would continue working together to achieve even better results.
Ms B van Minnen (DA) thanked the Gambling Board for their presentation and for answering the Committee's questions. She expressed appreciation for their work and concluded by wishing them a good weekend.
Mr Petrus attempted to ask a question but was inaudible. The Chairperson noted that the question could not be heard.
Mr van Wyk intervened, suggesting that the question related to page 91, second-last bullet point, which referenced “non-credible, unresponsive and unsustainable budgets adopted and implemented by municipalities.” He clarified that the question sought to know what the Department had done to address this issue and requested the names of the municipalities mentioned in the report.
Ms Gantana responded to the risk of non-credible, unresponsive, and unsustainable municipal budgets, noting it had been identified as a key risk for the year under review. She explained that the Department provided technical support to municipalities to help adopt funded budgets and implement funding plans where unfunded budgets were unavoidable.
Budget compliance was monitored through monthly and quarterly reports, while technical and strategic engagements were used to provide feedback. Support also covered data accuracy, the Eskom Debt Relief Programme, long-term financial planning, and capacity-building grants. Evidence-based tools, including socio-economic profiles and the Municipal Economic Review and Outlook (MERO), guided this support. She invited a colleague to add further points.
Mr Steven Kenyon, Chief Director: Local Government Public Finance, Provincial Treasury, agreed that the HOD had addressed the matter comprehensively. He added that the Department's Local Government Budget Performance Review provided detailed information, referring specifically to Annexure 3, which outlines each municipality's budget funding position, comparing tabled, adopted, and adjusted budgets, and indicates whether budgets were funded.
He noted that diagnosing a credible budget was the first step before applying the support measures outlined by the HOD. He stated that future editions of the review would track progress as municipalities moved from unfunded to funded positions.
Mr Kenyon listed the municipalities with unfunded adopted budgets for 2024/25 as Matzikama, Theewaterskloof, Kannaland, Knysna, Laingsburg, and Beaufort West.
The Chairperson thanked Mr Kenyon and posed two follow-up questions. First, he referred to page 92 and the key emerging risk of population growth, asking how the Department was responding. Second, he noted that staff fatigue, highlighted by the Minister in the previous year, did not appear as a risk in the current report, a positive development, and asked the Minister to elaborate on progress in addressing staff vacancies and fatigue.
Minister Baartman responded to the questions on staff fatigue and population growth. On staff fatigue, she acknowledged it was a priority. She reported progress in filling senior vacancies since the period under review, including the permanent appointment of both Deputy Directors-General (DDGs) and the Local Government Revenue Expenditure Group head. Three more appointments had recently been approved by Cabinet, with two others in progress. She emphasised the challenge of filling vacancies while managing daily workloads, citing an example of 6000 applications for an entry-level secretarial post. She stressed the Department's commitment to expediting appointments to alleviate the strain. She invited a team member to provide more detail on capacity building and staff well-being.
On population growth, Minister Baartman expressed concern that it was outpacing GDP and job growth in the province, increasing demand for services and making gains less visible. She noted the drivers were both inter-provincial and international migration, and that the Western Cape was the second fastest-growing province in the country. She stated this was a national issue, as South Africa's overall population was growing at 14%. She highlighted ongoing lobbying at the national level for a review of the provincial equitable share formula to give heavier weighting to population and economic growth, arguing that funds should follow the people moving to economic hubs.
To address the challenge proactively, she reported that the Western Cape government had adopted an Alternative and Blended Financing Framework. This framework aimed to help departments find innovative ways to diversify revenue sources and expedite service delivery, such as infrastructure projects. She invited the team to elaborate on both staff well-being and the application of this financing framework.
Ms Gantana thanked the Minister and added to the response. She noted that while the Minister had addressed the senior management team, the capacity gap was in middle management, specifically the 28-39 age group. Staff in this category were often mobile, having gained experience through the Department's internship programme, and working for the Provincial Treasury was a powerful career marketing tool, leading to turnover. She reported that 255 positions were currently in the recruitment process, with efforts focused on filling them before the end of the financial year.
On the Alternative and Blended Financing Framework launched by the Minister in April, she noted it presented exciting opportunities and the Department was now working on practical implementation, both on and off the balance sheet. This new strategic area also required building internal capacity, and the Department was reviewing its structure accordingly. She invited Ms Naadia Ismail to add details on personnel matters.
Ms Naadia Ismail, Director: Strategic and Operational Management Support, addressed the Chairperson and outlined the Department’s three pillars for managing staff: capacity, capability, and well-being.
Capacity involved workforce planning, retirement planning, development programmes, and building a talent pipeline.
Capability focused on leveraging technology to automate systems and reduce manual work.
Well-being was the key pillar in response to the Chairperson's question on staff fatigue. The aim was to encourage a work-life rhythm and ensure staff could get home for dinner, an aspirational goal given current workload.
She acknowledged that staff fatigue was highly relevant, especially with increasing vacancy rates. The Department addressed this through its Employee Health and Wellness (EHW) programme, which offered counselling services (face-to-face or telephonic) and stress management training. Leave management practices were also emphasised to encourage staff to take annual leave. She reported a decrease in sick leave and absenteeism rates, indicating some positive impact, but stressed the ongoing importance of staff taking time to rest.
The Chairperson thanked the officials and gave the Minister an opportunity for a follow-up. He recalled the Minister's statement from the previous year about losing staff because they were doing three times the work compared to other provinces. He asked if the Department was still losing people despite filling vacancies, and if this created an ongoing imbalance. He then yielded to the Minister.
Minister Baartman confirmed the Department had embarked on a review of its current structure. While filling vacancies could not be stopped due to the general lack of capacity, the review was necessary to transform the Treasury into a different type of enabling partner for the future, particularly for alternative and blended financing. This required a structural shift towards a "banker mindset" focused on generating and saving revenue, moving beyond the traditional strength in expenditure management. Therefore, filling vacancies had to continue alongside the structural review.
To address workload and staff retention, she emphasised increasing the use of technology to automate manual "government chores," freeing staff for more strategic work. She clarified that the "home for dinner" goal was an aspirational mindset about work-life balance, not a strict time. She noted that automation required efficient systems first. Staff wellness programmes were also critical.
She shared an anecdote about a bursary holder proactively emailing suggestions for improvement, which exemplified the courageous, collaborative culture needed to find efficiencies.
Minister Baartman added a broader challenge: the Department could not compete with the higher salaries offered by municipalities. While people might naturally seek higher pay, the Treasury aimed to offer other value through its work environment and the initiatives Ms Ismail had outlined. She acknowledged the toll the situation took on people but reiterated the focus on using technology, automation, and the newly adopted provincial Artificial Intelligence (AI) framework to help expedite processes like Human Resource (HR) matters and alleviate the burden.
Ms Lekker thanked the Chairperson and asked her first question, referring to page 91 under key risks for the year, specifically the risk of non-credible, unresponsive, and unsustainable municipal budgets. She asked how the Provincial Treasury, through its enterprise risk management processes, was supporting municipalities to improve financial governance, comply with regulations, and reduce unfunded or poorly structured budgets.
She linked this to a question about the ten municipalities that did not receive a clean audit, noting that the Committee had been informed earlier that morning about four municipalities—three showing improvement and one still struggling—and asked for the names of the remaining six municipalities.
The Chairperson responded that the question on municipal budgets had already been asked and answered and would not require the Department to repeat its response. He asked if Ms Lekker had any further questions.
Ms Lekker clarified that her query was specifically about naming the other six municipalities. The Chairperson replied that these had already been mentioned. He then asked his final question on governance, recalling the Minister’s earlier example of retaining staff through non-monetary measures, such as flexible work arrangements for family responsibilities, and requested the Minister to elaborate on progress regarding staff well-being and retention initiatives before moving to the next chapter.
Minister Baartman thanked the Chairperson. She welcomed the question on tracking progress and confirmed the Department had recently done a presentation on capacity, capability, and well-being within government. She invited the team to focus specifically on the well-being initiatives.
Ms Gantana responded and outlined several measures in place:
An alternative working arrangements policy allowing staff to work from home two days per week (not as leave), with monitoring mechanisms to ensure service delivery.
A bursary programme to encourage staff development.
Regular well-being engagement sessions on everyday life topics, which she noted were well-attended, often with over 100 participants online.
Utilisation of the transversal psychosocial support offering from the Department of the Premier (DoTP), which staff or supervisors could access through self-referral or referral.
A broader focus on cultural change within the organisation to make staff feel seen and heard, emphasising that even when a solution wasn't immediately available, listening and human connection were crucial to making people feel they were not alone.
The Chairperson thanked the officials, expressing his strong personal belief in the benefits of flexible work arrangements for staff well-being and productivity. He then moved the discussion to PFMA compliance, covering pages 145 to 158, and recognised Ms Lekker to speak first, followed by Mr Petrus.
Ms Lekker referred to page 147, point 1.1, on the reconciliation of irregular expenditure. She noted an amount that was used irregularly for a contract scope expansion without approval. She asked how such an expenditure could occur without accountability processes, what condonation processes were followed, and what was being done to strengthen internal controls to prevent similar irregular expenditure.
Her second question referred to page 149, Table 1.2.1, on the reconciliation of fruitless and wasteful expenditure, which had increased. The reasons cited were late penalty fees, late cancellation of accommodation, and non-refundable travel for a candidate. She asked what steps were being taken to improve planning and compliance to avoid such unnecessary expenditure and what mitigation steps had been followed. She asked if she should wait for responses or continue with further questions.
The Chairperson suggested she wait, allowing an opportunity for Mr Petrus to ask his question first.
Mr Petrus attempted again to ask a question regarding pages 151, 153, and 197, mentioning an increase in laptop and equipment costs and asking if this was now under control. However, his connection was extremely poor.
The Chairperson noted the poor connection but observed that the Department seemed to have understood the question. He stated he would allow the Department to respond first and then return to the Members.
An official responded to the question on irregular expenditure of R43 000 (page 147). She explained it resulted from a specific official instructing service providers to continue services outside the scope of two contracts. This was only discovered after the official had left the Department, so no consequence management was possible. To prevent recurrence, the Department had an Accounting Officer system detailing supply chain rules, an online training course on it, compliance checklists, and pre- and post-audits by the internal control unit. She noted there was no financial loss as the services (training for officials) were received.
On the fruitless and wasteful expenditure (page 149), she detailed four cases. Two involved late penalty fees for membership fees to the South African Institute of Chartered Accountants (SAICA) and another body, incurred by the same official responsible for the irregular expenditure. The other two cases were a non-refundable flight for an external interview candidate who did not complete the practical assessment, and a late accommodation cancellation. She noted that all irregular and fruitless expenditure was identified internally, not by the AGSA.
Regarding the increase in laptop and equipment losses (pages 151, 153, 197), she attributed some losses to the alternative working arrangements policy, as staff travel with equipment. Each case is thoroughly investigated with legal services, and depending.
The Chairperson noted the theft of a television listed on pages 151-152, remarking that unlike headphones, a TV was a large item to steal. He asked for an explanation.
An official provided background, explaining that during a refurbishment on the third floor, a TV was mounted on a wall. The asset management team could not dismantle it due to a lack of tools, so the service provider did so and reportedly place it behind partitioning. When they returned, the TV was missing. A full investigation was conducted with the involvement of Ms Ismail, but there was no indication of how the TV was removed from the building. The amount was subsequently written off.
Ms Lekker suggested that camera surveillance could have supported the investigation and posed two questions. First, referring to page 153, she queried the rationale for engaging over 40 suppliers and whether some services could be brought in-house. Second, on page 532, she asked what measures were being taken to build internal capacity to reduce reliance on consultants for core functions such as analytics, research, and ICT projects.
The Chairperson requested clarification on page 153 regarding the ten invoices paid after 30 days, asking for details on the reasons for the delays, including technical issues and procedural complexities. With no further questions, he handed over to the HOD.
Ms Gantana responded that the Department had reduced supplier contracts where possible. She noted that the PERO, previously outsourced, was now done in-house, and the intention was to bring the MERO in-house in the 2025/26 financial year. Some specialist capacity, such as for certain ICT functions via a partnership and for specific municipal training, would continue to require external consultants. However, terms of reference now include skills transfer clauses to build internal capacity. The Department also partnered with the private sector for events like procurement conferences.
Another official added that the Department could not have all internal capacity for functions like business analysis and system development. The PERO was now done in-house, and bringing the MERO in-house was being considered and consultants were engaged with clear terms of reference mandating skills transfer.
On the 30-day payments, she reported a reduction from 38 late payments last year to 10 this year, which were monitored monthly. The delays were not related to small businesses, which were paid via a prompt system. Issues included billing disputes with suppliers like Eskom and administrative problems, such as invoices being emailed to absent individuals. A dedicated email address for all invoices has been created to improve control.
Regarding cameras, she confirmed there were none in the area where the TV was stolen. Installation was underway via the Department of Police Oversight and Community Safety (POCS) and Department of Infrastructure (DoI) but was delayed due to challenges in sourcing parts and a service provider, with completion hoped for before the end of the financial year.
The Chairperson noted that the ten invoices represented only 2.7% and proceeded to the next item.
Ms Lekker asked a follow-up question on whether systems were in place to recover debts from former employees, referring to the official involved in the irregular expenditure who had since left.
She also queried page 200 regarding municipal debt, noting an outstanding receivable of R486 000 from Kannaland and Laingsburg municipalities for unspent grants and salary-related debts from former employees. She asked what measures were being taken to recover these amounts and whether consequence management or repayment agreements had been implemented.
The Chairperson noted that staff debt was also reflected on page 200.
Minister Baartman interjected on the principle of unspent grants, stating that municipalities must repay money not spent for its intended purpose, as it could assist another municipality. She tasked the team with ensuring continuous engagement on recovery.
Ms Gantana responded to the debt recovery questions. On recovering from former employees for fruitless expenditure, she said consequence management was applied where possible. In this instance, no evidence of gross negligence was found, and the cost of legal recovery for the R10 000 involved was deemed prohibitive, so it was written off.
Another official responded to municipal debt for unspent grants. She acknowledged the challenge, as the province lacked the tool to withhold equitable shares like National Treasury. The Department was exploring future grant allocations as a lever but noted the difficulty of enforcing repayment when municipalities were under financial recovery plans and residents would suffer.
Closing Remarks
Minister Baartman thanked the Chairperson, Committee staff, and Members for their questions and acknowledged a challenging year marked by national budget uncertainty and increased workloads and expressed pride in the Department’s 12th consecutive clean audit, as well as its new strategic focus on revenue generation and alternative financing.
Ms Gantana thanked the Committee and AGSA for ensuring accountability and guidance throughout the year.
Mr van Wyk congratulated the Provincial Treasury on its clean audit, detailed annual report, and excellent work supporting provincial departments and further highlighted the Department’s initiatives in alternative financing and municipal support programmes.
He thanked all participants for the long session, acknowledged the Procedural Officer and Chairperson, and wished everyone a good weekend.
The meeting was adjourned.
