Meeting Summary
The Committee was briefed by South African Tourism (SAT) on its 2023/24 annual report. The Committee expressed concerns about its performance and highlighted issues raised in the recent audit by the Auditor-General (AG). They questioned the vacancies at SAT, progress made in filling them, as well as the targets achieved in their budget programmes. They also inquired about the strategies to address the AG's findings and improve internal controls.
Questions were raised about SAT's surplus and its request to retain a portion of it. Members questioned why the money had not been used, and why there had been a delay in receiving it. They also sought clarification on SAT's financial management practices, as well as the board's budget management and accountability for irregularities. They also wanted to know the reasons for the high level of resignations within the entity.
They questioned the composition of the new SAT board -- whether it was representative of South African society in terms of gender representation and initiatives for persons with disabilities. They urged SAT to focus on broader sports tourism, rather than elite sports such as golf, and to address internal issues affecting performance. They also asked about employment practices, strategies to deal with underperformance, and adherence to legislation.
Meeting report
Minister’s Opening Remarks
Ms Patricia de Lille, Minister of Tourism, remarked that lessons learned included the need to continue following the Auditor-General (AG) recommendations to avoid another qualified audit. She said some vacancies still needed to be filled, and there was a need to keep up with the digital and artificial intelligence (AI) movements being used in other places.
Regarding slide 22, she said the term "moratorium" was incorrect.
The Minister said that any questions not addressed that day would be answered in writing.
The newly appointed chairperson of the board of South African Tourism (SAT), Prof Gregory Davids, acknowledged the criticism of the entity, and indicated that the only change in the delivery of the board was the approach to enacting changes when they returned to the Committee. He had begun considering the opinion and notes from the AG. Although 78% of key performance indicators (KPIs) had been achieved, the AG had still expressed concerns, and this remained their focus.
SAT's 2023/24 annual Report
Ms Nombulelo Guliwe, Chief Executive Officer (CEO), SAT, said there was an upswing in international tourism for South Africa. She gave a detailed account of the five budgetary programmes SAT used to coordinate their efforts to improve management and expenditure, namely corporate support, business enablement, leisure tourism marketing, business events, and tourist experience. The entity had numerous strategies to overcome areas of under-performance, especially where they had been unable to reach their planned targets
Mr Takalani Ramovha, Acting Chief Financial Officer (CFO), SAT, elaborated on the government grant received for the 2023/24 financial year, voluntary Tourism Marketing South Africa (TOMSA) levies, the entity's sundry income, expenditure per programme, and SAT’s solvency and financial ratios.
Ms Guliwe continued by reporting on the relevant vacancies in each of their budgetary programmes, the demographics and genders of those employed by SAT, and some concerns as expressed in the AG's report on the entity

Responses
Minister De Lille said the Department needed to contextualise the situation. They had inherited "the baggage" as the executive authority, and their role was now to fix it. She acknowledged that they could not make excuses, because the Committee was right to hold them accountable, considering that this involved taxpayers' money.
The Minister noted that there had been many acting positions, such as the CEO in 2023/24, and that the acting CEO had had full powers of the board for six months. She also mentioned the acting CFO from 2023/24, who had passed away, and expressed her condolences. When she assumed her position, she had had to start stabilising those positions, which had already been mentioned in previous reports.
She had informed the Committee last week that she could not provide a full report, but could supply the proclamation request sent to the President and the Minister of Justice, due to several issues pointed out by the Committee that day. She highlighted several issues that had occurred prior to her tenure, which had been included in the proclamation, such as the irregular appointment of the chief operating officer, a corrupt relationship between SAT employees and a SAT grocery supplier, recruitment of jobs for friends, and the overlooking of qualified candidates. She also referred to a disclosure from March 2023 concerning an irregular payment of R1.7 million made to an individual who had worked for a Minister, along with issues related to the Tottenham Hotspur sponsorship and a North American tender during the same financial year. She explained that these problems had contributed to a qualified audit, and they were currently being addressed.
Additionally, she mentioned concerns regarding the management of human capital and the head of data analytics. She referred to the Proclamation, which also mentioned a financial crime advisory for the NCP investigation, the AG’s report findings from 31 March, marketing expenditures, and capital expenditures, all of which were part of the issues leading to non-compliance with Treasury regulations during the summer campaign. The Minister said that these irregularities, which had occurred before her arrival and had led to a qualified audit, were now with the Special Investigating Unit (SIU) and that they were seeking a proclamation from the President to investigate further.
The Minister expressed eagerness to return to the Committee in the first, second, and third quarters of the current financial year with updates on their progress after addressing these issues. She concurred with the sentiments expressed by Ms Linde and other Members, acknowledging that despite not being present during the preceding issues, they still had to rectify them. She appealed for the Committee's help in fixing the problems.
On the subject of Ms Ismail's concerns regarding current spending on the board, which was under review for the current financial year, she affirmed her political will to clean up the situation. She highlighted that she had a performance agreement with the President and said that with the Committee's help, they would clean up and had already begun doing so.
While she did not want to make excuses, she pointed out that this portfolio had a high turnover of ministers, which sometimes led to instability. However, she emphasised the need to move past that and focus on fixing the current issues.
Regarding the issues highlighted in the Sunday Times about the White Paper, she remarked that the journalist had not accurately reported the story, as the headline did not reflect the content of the White Paper. Nevertheless, the sector was generally pleased with the White Paper and the current board under her leadership. She said there were still two vacancies to fill, and in her meeting with the board on Friday, she suggested that a young person under 35 from the LGBTQIA+ community be brought in.
On SEFA, she indicated that she had written to the Minister of Finance, who had provided advice, and she was awaiting further guidance on potential funding. She questioned the source of funding from SEFA, noting that out of R1.2 billion, they had claimed to approve up to 14 applications totalling R220 million, but the process had been painfully slow. She emphasised the need to consider whether they were going to recapitalise the fund, but indicated they could not do so until they were able to disburse more effectively.
Minister De Lille confirmed that the chair would rotate every year, and that they planned to conduct the summer campaign in the Free State in October. She reiterated her commitment to fixing the issues at hand, and noted that she had observed the results of some remedial actions implemented in the first quarter, nearly achieving 100% performance. She concluded by expressing a plea for assistance in rectifying the inherited mess.
Ms Guliwe responded to Ms Linde's question regarding vacant positions and their current status, stating that the latest number of vacancies stood at 19%. She linked this to the recommendations made by the AG concerning the filling of senior positions, as well as the chief audit executive roles that Ms Ismail had also pointed out. They had advertised the vacant senior positions, and although the CFO's advertisement was now closed, the recruitment process for the chief audit executive position had commenced.
However, during the interview stage, discussions had arisen within the audit committee regarding whether the positions should be insourced or outsourced. Consequently, management had asked the audit and risk committee to make a decision, since the recruitment process had already begun, which was one of the key actions stemming from the AG's recommendations -- to appoint a senior individual in internal audit. She emphasised that even if certain positions were to be outsourced, a chief audit executive would still be necessary to oversee the outsourcing plan.
They advertised and shortlisted candidates for the chief operating officer (COO), CFO, and CEO positions at level F2, necessitating the presence of a board member during any of these recruitment processes. This had been identified as a priority, and the board acknowledged the urgency, especially in light of the AG's call for stabilisation.
In response to Mr Moodley's question about the surplus, she clarified that according to the PFMA, they were not permitted to budget for a surplus or a deficit. They had engaged with the Treasury on this matter, and this situation was not unique to the last financial year. Prior to making commitments for multiple years, they ensured that they had a budget aligned for the current year. After their financials had been audited, they subsequently approached Treasury to prove that they had contracts and commitments, at which point rollover funds were typically approved, as had been the case in previous years.
The CEO noted that another significant factor contributing to the surplus was the R133 million impact on cash equivalents and cash flow, as funds from the Tourism Business Council of South Africa (TBCSA) were received only post year-end. This amount affected the surplus, prompting them to explain the source of the funds -- private sector fees -- to Treasury, which had enabled them to retain those funds in the past. She also mentioned that the last financial year had included R35 million recovered by SAT for services not rendered, but they had opted not to retain that specific sum because it should revert to the National Revenue Fund (NRF) by the end of 2023.
Ms Guliwe confirmed that they did not budget for a surplus or deficit, and whenever there were commitments, they followed the appropriate process with Treasury to declare a deficit or retain a surplus, provided valid reasons existed. Addressing Mr Moodley's query about the timing, she explained that the process commences in August, a month after the audit report date, as it was based on audited financials. This process continues between August and November, and if the Treasury is not satisfied with the information provided by November, they may direct SAT, through the Department of Tourism, to return the surplus to the NRF.
Regarding Ms Ligaraba's inquiry about the tourist experience programme, she said that within SAT there existed a visitor experience business unit that undertakes such initiatives for trade. Moving forward, the programme would be referred to as the tourist experience, although the specific unit was called "visitor experience" within the framework of SAT.
Ms Guliwe acknowledged the directive to enhance the use of technology internally due to existing shortfalls, and indicated that efforts were underway to strengthen the annual performance plan (APP). She assured the Committee that their targets would remain SMART, and that feedback would be reinforced.
Although a less than 8% achievement had been reported against a 10% target, the CEO clarified that the target must remain below 10 for it to be considered an actual achievement. She took note of the vacancy numbers, indicating that there were around 20 interns, and she planned to update the Committee on this at the next meeting.
The CEO clarified that the upswing in arrivals was not solely attributable to SA tourism, as the private sector played a significant role since they own the products being marketed, making it a collaborative effort. She acknowledged the recommendations made to improve and prevent ongoing issues.
In response to Ms Ismail's question regarding the decision on insourcing and outsourcing, Ms Guliwe indicated that the CFO would address this topic further. She emphasised the importance of breaking down resignation data beyond mere percentages to include specific dates and the number of dismissals.
Regarding the slides referred to by Mr Maeco, she said that slide 14 reflected a 68% representation of SAT across various levels, while slide 19 showed that 48% of top management met the target of at least 50%. She acknowledged the need for improvement in the disability target of 2%, which she deemed unacceptable, particularly as they had struggled to meet this target during quarterly reports, achieving it only in the fourth quarter. She committed to setting more appropriate targets aligned with the SMART criteria.
Ms Guliwe agreed on the need to focus on sports tourism beyond just elite sports, noting that golf and rugby were mentioned, but should have been documented in writing regarding irregular expenditure. She also pointed out the potential to fill women's vacancies in senior management.
She said that the presentation aimed to outline achievements and non-achievements, and she would ensure future presentations reflected all the programmes comprehensively rather than focusing solely on key achievements. She accepted the need to elaborate on certain aspects in reports, and acknowledged the guidance regarding reporting unskilled employees, indicating that if the count was zero, it could be excluded.
She said that SAT conducted exit interviews, and she possessed a comprehensive report detailing the reasons provided by the CFO, which had been submitted to the board. She confirmed that a written request for remedial plans for AGSA could be provided.
Management information could be shared in writing, particularly regarding the retained R4.1 million, clarifying that the opportunity had arisen due to suppliers and multi-incumbents collaborating with SAT. The CEO explained that the R4.1 million had been deducted from SAT's overall balance, ensuring they were not in a financially detrimental position. Prepayment controls had been introduced, alongside a commitment to investigations and consequence management.
Mr Ramovha commented that they were committed to fixing issues, but did not want to make too many promises, preferring their actions to demonstrate their commitments more than their words. He highlighted that the SIU, as mentioned by the Minister, had indicated that investigations and proclamations were key to addressing the irregular expenditure. Some transactions that were part of those proclamation submissions would lead to details that other parties were not able to see.
He said there was also a question on the surplus and the period covered in August by the CEO. The Acting CFO noted that a subsequent meeting had taken place in October, during which the CEO had said it was within the period from October through November that National Treasury should take into consideration. As she had indicated, the high TOMSA levy had played a role. He explained that R133 million was paid only in August, and another payment from 2023 was made in January 2024. They had a cumulative surplus from the collection of R101 million, which was additional due to an increase in revenue collection beyond the estimated amounts and expenditures. He emphasised that this was critical, and they had agreed that the request for retention was to cover the remaining commitments and contracts in place. He also highlighted the irregular expenditure of R24 million, which they were obligated to address, like the R9 million. Therefore, their plan was to use the budget effectively and do their best in the current financial year.
He said Ms Ligaraba had discussed the SCM initiative, and in common with other organisations that had had poor audits or administrative issues leading to SIU investigations, it was important to ensure an ethical culture across the organisation. He noted that one of the key issues was the Public Finance Management Act (PFMA), where they had invited National Treasury to participate in the process at no cost. Other initiatives involved ethics in public service, managed from finance and the supply chain internally.
He also mentioned monitoring non-compliance by identifying units that had not performed well, going as far as determining blockages hindering deliverables, to ensure that supply chain perspectives could assist them in a compliant manner. He responded to a question about incorporating bidding and procurement, stating that they conduct workshops to train bid committees effectively. They had sessions with Treasury to workshop everyone on how to run effective bid committees, ensuring efficiency in tender processes.
Regarding the irregular expenditure highlighted by the AG, he said that part of the R3.8 million from the BMW/golf event had included engagement with National Treasury and assessments by internal audit. He indicated that issues arose from payments made to the past administration, including contracts with additional information beyond what was legally signed off. He emphasised that the SIU had been involved in ensuring accountability for those who tried to evade responsibility. He stated that those who left could not run away forever because the SIU would pursue them. He clarified that this situation differed from those whose contracts ended before or during investigations, where the institution decided to terminate contracts and cease operations.
He addressed points on elite sports, mentioning the R2.5 million allocated for the rugby union, which aimed to attract a larger audience due to South Africa being the defending champions at the time of the World Cup. He noted that this non-compliance was part of a concluded investigation. Regarding human capital, he said that they were working on ensuring that consequence management was fully implemented. He added that the R2.5 million was also connected to R7.4 million for rugby, which involved a pre-match with the All Blacks, and that they had not made any payments until all recommendations were addressed.
He said the SARS penalties were part of the control deficiency highlighted by the AG, which included a segregation between human capital and finance. He explained that they had moved a person on the payroll from the finance structure to improve payment processes and ensure timely payments. He acknowledged that this issue had been missed because the person responsible for approval did not see it, and they had since worked on system improvements to prevent a recurrence.
He discussed the procurement process and the committees, indicating they were addressing the R9 million situation because the procurement billing process was not followed. He noted that the National Treasury framework required an assessment of irregular expenditure to determine if the irregularities and control issues identified by the AG were accurate. During the assessment, it was identified that the procurement process had appointed an agency, which had led to the discovery of R4.1 million in material irregularities. However, the assessment's conclusion was not about the procurement or tender process being followed, but rather about the activation agency; he referenced the Dubai Expo concerning the R4.1 million.
He elaborated that when constructing a structure like the dome for the African Travel Indaba, various activities were needed as enablers. As part of their remedial action, they halted contracts with the same provider for both R9.9 million and R4.1 million, which were causing continuous damage to the organisation due to fruitless and wasteful expenditure. He concluded by stating they had the funds necessary to cover the R4.1 million owed.

The Chairperson concluded with final remarks, outlining the way forward and expressing gratitude for the attendance, presentation and responses. She acknowledged the need for a plan to be implemented by all parties. She expressed her desire to avoid the Minister and Deputy Minister being summoned before SCOPA due to irregularities within the entity, highlighting that acceptable norms should be maintained. She warned that once called before the SCOPA, they would likely face continued summons, and underperformance by the entity would reflect poorly on the Minister. She urged everyone to act on behalf of society and South African citizens, reiterating that the Minister had a contract with the President and must be held accountable.
Committee matters
Mr Sisanda Loni, Committee Researcher, read the minutes from the meeting held on 10 September, and no corrections were noted. He took the time to review the resolutions created, based on that meeting.
Ms Linde remarked that she would have appreciated receiving the minutes more than a day in advance so that she could properly review them. She also sought clarity on the process of adopting the minutes, expressing her concern about whether this was the Members' opportunity to ascertain if the minutes accurately reflected their sentiments during the meeting, and whether they could amend anything.
Mr Loni acknowledged that it had been rightly pointed out that he should send the minutes further in advance to allow for any necessary amendments.
Ms Ismail sought clarification on the use of the term “closing remarks” in relation to providing recommendations and vice versa. She also noted that a point required amending in the minutes to accurately reflect her comments from a previous meeting.
Mr Maeco commended Mr Loni for delivering good minutes despite lacking proper tools, and stated that this should be formally noted, urging the Chairperson to call for the necessary tools.
She agreed, adding that it was important to recognise the difficulties the Researcher faced due to shortcomings in Parliament's provision of tools and lack of support.
When the Chairperson attempted to close the meeting, she was interrupted by Ms Ismail, who had comments regarding the first set of minutes. She disagreed with several notes in the meeting minutes, to which Ms Linde concurred.
Ms Ligaraba expressed her confusion, believing they were re-debating issues that had already been addressed.
Ms Mchunu noted that when drafting the minutes, the Committee Secretary would consider everyone's views to ensure they were all represented. She added they would still come for a Budgetary Recommendations and Review Report (BRRR) meeting, which would provide more details on their discussions, unless they stated that the BRRR did not reflect the discussions of the day.
Ms Ismail reiterated her confusion regarding the terms “closing remarks” and “recommendations,” adding that she had pointed out various points about small, medium and micro enterprises (SMMEs) that had been omitted from the minutes.
Ms Ligaraba noted that during the presentation of the Small Enterprise Finance Agency (SEFA), there had been unanimous agreement among them.
The Secretary clarified that what was included were not recommendations, but resolutions, which were the same as the conclusions.
The Chairperson summarised that the recommendations would be debated in the House when discussing a report, while the minutes and resolutions would not be part of that debate.
She emphasised that being on the Committee meant separating from one’s political party, explaining that any debates concluded for the intentions of a party occurred during the discussion of the recommendations.
Mr Maeco asserted that other Members could not take over the Chair's role, and that the opportunity to debate what was included in the recommendations and report would occur in Parliament.
Ms Ismail reiterated her points regarding the adoption of the meeting minutes, stating that using the term “remarks” implied that all Committee Members shared that sentiment, which she disagreed with. She expressed her preference for the term “closing remarks” instead of “resolution.”
Mr Maeco responded that Ms Ismail was overruling the Chairperson's role. He insisted that she and the other Members must accept that the minutes would be consolidated into recommendations they could subsequently debate. He moved for a vote on the adoption of the minutes, suggesting that the conflicted Members accept that they did not agree with the minutes.
The Chairperson clarified that her role was merely to summarise the tone and sentiments of the discussion, stating that views not opposed by anyone would be recorded.
The Secretary confirmed that the report would be received the following week, and proposed that they summarise the meeting sentiments after each meeting, noting that this was an unprecedented event in his experience. He said that if the Chairperson closed the meeting and declared it adjourned, the meeting could not be reopened.
Ms Mchunu referred to the Rules of the National Assembly on page 116, explaining that a minority report could be submitted, and that both minority and majority views would be included. She said that in all her time with the committees, she had never encountered such a conundrum and undermining of the Chair. She moved for the adoption of the minutes, suggesting that Members could debate at the following meeting with the BRRR.
The Chairperson said that the Secretary records the meeting and reiterated that the Chairperson fulfils a necessary function. If other Members intervened to perform that role, she would no longer need to be present.
The Secretary called for objections concerning the first and second sets of the minutes.
Ms Linde commented that while the minutes were well done, she and Ms Ismail would accept them with reservations, since not everything captured reflected the Democratic Alliance's view.
The Chairperson confirmed that she was referring to the minutes and the DA’s view on them, not the BRRR. She confirmed the minutes of 10 September, and clarified how the minutes differed from their views, reiterating that the minutes were well done.
She said that what was being discussed would also be addressed in the BRRR.
Ms Ligaraba commented that they had reached a cul-de-sac and argued that there should be limits to the Committee's discussion regarding the minutes.
The Chairperson noted that the minutes would not be changed but they would be revisited in the BRRR.
She said that next week, they would receive a draft report, after which the Committee would debate as was usually done. Members, specifically in their capacity as political party representatives, could highlight their grievances, and subsequent debates would take place.
The meeting was adjourned.
