Meeting Summary
Video
The Committee met to engage on the draft 2026/27 annual performance plan (APP) for Parliament, and to consider a request from the Parliamentary Medical Aid (Parmed) scheme to hold its upcoming briefing in a closed session.
Parliament's senior legal advisor advised the Members that by default, parliamentary meetings had to be open to the public and media unless closure was justifiable under constitutional principles and Joint Rule 103. He had found no grounds in Parmed’s presentation that warranted confidentiality. Members strongly supported openness, stressing transparency, considering that the briefing concerned public funds. Some raised concerns about the process, timing, and stakeholder communication, but agreed the meeting should remain open. The Committee resolved that the forthcoming Parmed meeting would not be closed.
The APP outlined the priorities and budget proposals for the second year of the 2025–2029 five-year strategy. Key priorities included digital transformation (dashboards, online submissions, bill tracking), oversight improvements, accountability standards, and strengthening public participation. Risks highlighted included unsustainable funding, infrastructure challenges (including Parliament’s rebuild), human capital and technological capacity, cyber threats, weak oversight, and inadequate public participation. The Acting CFO explained budget changes, notably an additional R725 million linked to the Old Assembly rebuild.
Members expressed concerns about discrepancies between the draft APP figures tabled in May 2025, and current updates. Questions were raised about poor planning, contractor appointments, cost-saving measures, and documentation provided to National Treasury. They questioned the pace of Parliament’s reconstruction, the capacity of the legal drafting unit and delays in processing private members’ bills, the accessibility of digital participation for citizens in rural areas, the effective responsiveness of the Executive beyond “tick-box” accountability, automation as opposed to staffing needs, and the need for robust oversight of Parliament’s own budget.
A proposal was made to establish a multiparty budget forum to strengthen Parliament's engagement with National Treasury and ensure adequate funding. This was supported in principle by the Speaker and the National Council of Provinces (NCOP) Chairperson. Members suggested a
site visit
to monitor Parliament’s reconstruction progress. The Committee also noted and supported the draft APP, while acknowledging it was provisional until final appropriations.
Meeting report
Legal opinion on closed meeting
Chairperson Lekganyane opened the meeting, and said the Committee was due to meet with the Parliamentary Medical Aid (PARMED), but the scheme had requested that the meeting take place in a closed session. The Committee had consulted Parliament’s legal services to ensure it was procedurally correct to have a closed meeting.
Adv Frank Jenkins, Senior Parliamentary Legal Advisor, presented the legal opinion on the Parmed request for a closed meeting -- whether this would be regular in terms of the Rules and the Constitution. As Members were aware, and as was indicated in the opinion, the default position for committees of Parliament was to have open committees. This was specified in respect of the National Assembly (NA) and the National Council of Provinces (NCOP), but nothing was said about Joint Committees in the Constitution. However, considering how Parliament's compliance with constitutional obligations was analysed in the courts through jurisprudence, it was just pure logic that a Joint Committee would be subject to the same constitutional requirement, which was an open Committee to ensure transparency and accountability, which was set out in the founding values of the Constitution, and was referred to in the opinion.
Adv Jenkins said his vantage point was the two provisions that deal with the NA and the NCOP, which set out that the Committees of the Houses must be open for the public, including the media, unless it was reasonable and justifiable in a democratic society to close those meetings. There had been instances where meetings had been closed, and there were certain committees which were closed through the operation of legislation, most notably the Joint Standing Committee of Intelligence, in terms of the rules, and the mediation committees. When it came to other committees, and specifically the Joint Standing Committee on the Financial Management of Parliament, that provision applied, was it reasonable and justifiable to exclude the media, the public, from a meeting in an open and democratic society? The applicable joint rule was 103, which sets out that test, and actually had a two-pronged analysis of whether a committee could be closed.
It was useful to keep in mind that one had to justify the closing of a meeting. The default position was always an open meeting, unless it was by legislation or rules in the few mentioned examples. Joint Rule 103 indicates that if the committee must be closed in terms of legislation, such as the Joint Standing Committee of intelligence or a House resolution, the Committee could exclude the public, including the media. That was not the case in this instance. The other examples that were mentioned in joint rule 103 were whether the content of the discussion in the committee affects a particular person, whether it was confidential, and whether they could be harmed. The other consideration was if there was a matter that was prejudicial to a particular person. However, Adv Jenkins said that having gone through the two presentations to be made to the Committee on Parmed, he could not find anything which was specifically prejudicial to a particular person, and neither did he find anything that was protected under parliamentary privilege or for any other reason, such as client-attorney privilege and so forth.
Joint Rule 103 also considers where the issue was confidential in terms of legislation, but Adv Jenkins said that in this case it was not. The last point in rule 103 was whether it was of such a nature that its confidential treatment was for any reason justifiable in an open and democratic society. This was the test one must look at. He could not find compliance with this test. He was of the opinion that the meeting should not exclude the public, nor the media. The only particular person that was mentioned was a juristic person, MedScheme, which was operating this medical aid scheme in terms of the Medical Schemes Act. He did not find anything in the presentations that pertained to the protection of personal information. Even if it did deal with personal matters, one must always ask the question whether that personal matter could be overridden by the constitutional obligation to deal with them. This was seen in a recent court case of Corruption Watch and the appointment of commissioners for the Commission for Gender Equality (CGE).
Regarding procedure, joint rule 103 (2) sets out that if the Committee wanted to consider this advice and wanted to know whether to close the meeting or to exclude the public or not, there were two ways of doing it. The Chairpersons of the Committee would have to decide to close the meeting, and then the Committee could decide to open it if there were no sensitive matters. The other way would be for the Chairpersons to close the meeting from the beginning only, and for the Committee to decide whether to open or close the meeting.

Secretary to Parliament
Mr George said Members may recall that on 28 March, they had
presented
an update to the Committee on the rebuilding of Parliament on the status as at March, advising where they were on all five components of the parliamentary precinct development projects. Parliament was duty-bound, as soon as they received confirmation that a contract had been awarded, to inform National Treasury officially and submit the figures that relate to that appointment. This they had done for the December appointment in writing, as well as after the appointment in June. They had subsequently met with National Treasury on 4 August to discuss the broader budget of Parliament, not only for the rebuild, but the priority needs of the budget of Parliament for the next financial year, so that they then had a firm foundation for briefing the Executive Authority, as required by law, to engage with the Minister of Finance.
The presentation today was a scheduled engagement for the month of September, and the precursor to that had been a discussion of the draft estimates, as well as the components of this budget, by the Director-General of Treasury, the Secretary to Parliament, and related officials. As required in terms of section 15 of the Joint Financial Management of Parliament legislation, the administration was required to present this plan ten months in advance. So when they had disclosed figures, they had referred to the prevailing status at that time. Once the status was updated, section 16 and section 17 of the Financial Management of Parliament require that a draft budget, including any adjustments, must be taken through a consultative process until an appropriation letter has been confirmed by Parliament, like any other entity. It then triggers the appropriation instrument in terms of section 18. So sections 13,14, 15,16, 17 and18, were provisions that guide the entire iteration of the budget. Once the figures are known before the start of the financial year, the Executive Authority must then appropriate the budget from their own resources of Parliament, from any donor funding and any appropriation from the national budget, including any adjustment. It was therefore anticipated that when a draft is presented, there would be different stages in the next ten months where developments may necessitate changes to figures, so adjustments must be tabled until appropriation is done. Once it is done in terms of section 18, then the approval of all budgets is done at the level of Parliament, which normally takes place in July for all committees of Parliament, including the vote of Parliament. Then the figures are published, and are a confirmed and accurate appropriation.
Mr George confirmed that they were indeed talking about automation next year. It was not a substitute for what they had presented last week in respect of quarter one -- that there were a number of priority appointments that had been requested through committees to capacitate their personnel, including such expertise as interpreters, language practitioners, researchers, content advisors and committee secretariats. So when they talked the language of automation, it was not an either-or -- it was more about ensuring that the requisite support existed in Parliament. When they present the broadcasting strategy of Parliament, including any other digital strategies that they were working on, they had to assess all the governance instruments that were required, so that when they talked about AI, for example, it would have to be an approved strategy by Parliament that addresses where they start accepting that AI must do this work. It could not be that researchers or interpreters could not be recruited because of AI when the overall regulation for AI was not yet in place, or the pitfalls identified, so they needed that capacity, but the rest of the automation would be sequential in terms of the digital strategy of Parliament. At this point, it was not a substitution of either or, but rather a continuum of professional interventions to improve the work of Parliament.
Mr George responded to the operational KPIs in the APP, explaining that these were in each division for priorities, outcomes and outputs. What was contained in each division was the operational targets, so some of the elements raised were related to operational targets, which were not necessarily part of what was presented in an APP, but rather as part of the operational guidelines for a department or a division to execute against the outcomes and outputs that had been outlined. The emphasis remained at a divisional level about how to translate these outputs and quarterly targets into operational activity targets and plans.
Ms Mashika addressed the survey described in slide seven. This dealt with the targets for 2029/30, so they had defined the five-year targets that we would want to see at the end of this particular term, achieving those strategic plan targets. They would be conducting the survey for the engagement of employees possibly in 2027 and 2028. As part of the process to revise the APP, they look at sequencing some of these targets, as well as at the budget availability, and also to make sure that they can sequence them in a way that provides meaning over the years. This would therefore form part of the revision of the APP when they include the engagement survey in the human capital strategy target. This was also to ensure that they take into consideration the human capital strategy implementation and all of the improvement plans that would be implemented during the financial years.
On the public participation improvement plan in quarter two of this financial year, Members would remember that last week they had presented on the quarter one performance outcomes, as well as the results of the quarter one report, so the public participation plan would provide an improvement plan for quarter two. It was a quarter two outcome delivery in quarter two of this financial year, where all of those improvements were going to be defined. This meant that as soon as they were defined in quarter two of this year, they would be able to ensure that in the next financial year, they would be specific on what was going to be implemented, and provide for it.
The satisfaction rate of citizens was a 2029/30 target that they wanted to achieve. The citizen survey was really envisaged to be conducted either late in 2027/28 or at the beginning of the 2028/29 financial year. This should also take into account the public participation improvement plans that they would have implemented along the three financial years, so that when they do engage with citizens, they could ensure that they could provide high-impact areas for improvement.
Ms Mashika said the risk mitigation plans were being implemented, and they were using current resources and operational expenditure as much as possible. Those that were being funded included the information security risks and the improvement of the cyber resilience in the organisation. Those had specific targets, as well as specific funding that had been provided to ensure effective mitigation.
On the operational plans, the APP provides strategic initiatives, cascaded into operational plans to ensure that they can improve overall and holistically on the parliamentary service in Parliament. It was not just based on the strategic initiatives, but the operational plans, taking into consideration all the functional areas as well.
Ms Mpefumlo referred to cost-cutting measures, stating that there had been a review of the travel policy and the subsistence and travel (S&T) allowance for international engagements. They were also reviewing the length of the journey for when business class bookings were made. The human capital department was busy with its work study to assess the productivity of employees. There was an initiative to assess whether they could save, or if employees were not fully engaged, they could be redirected to other areas where they were needed instead of appointing new staff. Human capital policies were also at an advanced stage in terms of overtime management and acting allowances that were paid. Through the digital strategy, automation over time would also realise benefits and savings. She supported relooking at committees in terms of reducing the amount of printing, because in 2024/25 Parliament had spent R12 million, and R16 million in the year before. Those were areas where costs could be saved.
Feedback from the Financial and Fiscal Commission (FFC) was expected in November on the first phase of the funding model, which would assist in strengthening the conversation with National Treasury.
Adv Zuraya Adhikarie, Chief Parliamentary Legal Advisor, responded to the drafting capacity of the legal unit, saying the legal office was well capacitated and had standard operating procedures in place. On average, they allowed approximately three months to draft a bill, but that depended on whether the policy formulation was in order. They often had to go back and forth to a Member for further instructions, and while this was not insurmountable, they were aware that there had been delays in processing once the bills had been drafted, and this had been raised at the National Assembly Programming Committee (NAPC). She was sure these challenges were being addressed, but it was basically at the committee stages where there had been delays. It was a matter that the NAPC had been seized with.
Further discussion
Ms Siwisa was not convinced by the answers, and said that the more she heard, the more confused she felt. Referring to the building estimations, she said that if the office of the STP knew that it had not yet appointed a contractor for the rebuilding of the Old Assembly, or the refurbishing of the Old Assembly, it should have been part of the original estimation, so that they did not find themselves with other estimations or other proposals that they were going to present to National Treasury. It was a concern -- because they knew. They knew that the contractor had not been appointed for the Old Assembly, and it should have been part of the original drafting when it was done. She was not disputing the processes or saying the processes were flawed. Her problem was what was on the slide and what had been presented verbally. If they knew the appointment of the contractor for the Old Assembly was coming, it should have been part of the estimation. To her, this showed poor planning, and the office of the STP did not know what needed to be done, what their responsibilities were, or what the focal point was. Parliament was not functioning because the rebuilding was taking longer. National Treasury now had to be engaged in the appointment of the contractor and address the difference after the original estimation. How could Parliament not know a contractor would be appointed? They should have made provision from the outset to indicate that they were supposed to appoint a contractor. There was a person in the office of the STP appointed as a project manager - he should have given the STP the estimation for this contract, because it was his or her duty to advise the STP. This should have been a priority because of the crisis of reconstruction. She would wait to hear from National Treasury, but this was poor planning.
Ms Mpapane felt a point was being missed. Members had been informed that the reconstruction and refurbishment of Parliament was going to be done in phases, and about the delays, because of the involvement of the Heritage Council. She did not think fingers should be pointed. She cautioned the Committee to go back to square one if Members wanted to understand this rebuilding and refurbishment project. Members had been given a presentation where they had been informed of the contractor for the New Assembly, and that the contractor for the Old Assembly was still being finalised.
Mr George agreed that there might be a need for ongoing updates on these matters of the rebuild, especially as the Committee had agreed that they should make it a regular update. A budget process followed a particular regime, and that regime was guided by law, and it had specific prescriptions in terms of how they had to prepare the budget. The budget was always prepared in that way. On 28 March, the administration had briefed this Committee on where things were. The same had been done in June. In March, it had been presented that the New Assembly contractor had been appointed in December, and the Old Assembly was envisaged to be appointed in June. December was almost seven months into the financial year, but it could not be predicted in April last year what the costs of the contractors would be, as it depended on a number of factors such as inputs from quantity surveyors. Upon the appointment of contractors, they formally advise and brief the steering Committee, of which Treasury and the Department of Public Works were part. This would be part of the adjustments, given that they now had certainty, with contractors in place that were working side by side to deliver a final Parliament building by November next year. This was part of an ongoing budget development process – it remained a draft until the final figures were appropriated and approved by Parliament.
Speaker Didiza said that maybe between herself and the NCOP Chairperson, they needed to consider seriously the proposal that Mr Van Rooyen had made of a budget forum that would work with them to make sure that the budget of Parliament took care of the strategic priorities agreed to.
Chairperson Mtshweni-Tsipane said the matter raised by Mr Van Rooyen to establish such a Committee should be considered, because it was a fact that there might be insufficient resources for a number of reasons. However, at the end of the day, it must not compromise their work of overseeing the Executive and making sure that there is sufficient public participation. Therefore, it would be crucial that whatever plans they had were funded accordingly. Therefore, it was a matter which should be given due consideration as a matter of urgency to see how best such a committee could be established in a manner to influence the allocation of the budget, and possibly taking it further, to say how they could apportion that budget to the respective work of committees, and ensure the seamless execution of work, both from an administrative as well as a political component of the institution. She welcomed the proposal, which was worth interrogating further.
She thought the issue of the rebuilding should be revisited for everyone to appreciate the latest developments. There could also be a site visit for the Committee to appraise itself of the work that had been done thus far, compared to what had been presented. Members could themselves question if the timelines and budget were realistic.
Chairperson Ndhlovu agreed that it was very important to have the site visit, which would assist everyone and give Members knowledge of what was happening and how far things were with the rebuild.
Chairperson Lekganyane said the STP was leading a team of dedicated civil servants who wanted to help the Committee and help the Parliament of South Africa to succeed. He thanked the staff for their humility and for accommodating the Committee at awkward hours, still vibrant and agile enough to provide answers. He assured Members the STP would continue the robust work, and he saw the direction Parliament was being taken into the 21
st
century.
He said the draft APP was supported, and noted that there would be further discussion at the right time when allocations were made. The suggestions made in the meeting would be explored to see how they could enhance the work of Parliament and the Joint Standing Committee.
The meeting was adjourned.
