Trade, Industry and Competition
The Portfolio Committee's meeting covered two key presentations -- the audit outcomes of the Department of Trade, Industry and Competition (DTIC) for the 2023/24 financial year, and a report from the DTIC on its performance and governance. The meeting included significant discussions on the financial health, leadership challenges and operational issues within the Department and its entities, along with responses from the Department’s leadership.
The Auditor-General’s presentation provided a detailed analysis of the audit outcomes for the DTIC and its entities, and indicated a slight regression in performance compared to the previous year, with only five out of eight entities achieving clean audits. Entities such as the National Regulator for Compulsory Specifications (NRCS) and the South African Bureau of Standards (SABS) had received unqualified audits with findings, primarily due to material misstatements in financial statements and non-compliance with regulations. The audit for the National Lotteries Commission (NLC) had been delayed, pending technical discussions related to accounting standards, and its completion was expected by mid-October.
The key challenges discussed included the failure to fill key vacancies at senior levels, such as chief executive officer (CEO) and Director-General positions, which had significantly impacted governance and compliance across the DTIC’s entities. The Auditor-General stressed the need for prompt action to fill these positions and improve the Department’s internal controls. The presentation also identified issues of irregular expenditure, with the SABS contributing the most, and fruitless and wasteful expenditure, especially from the National Credit Regulator (NCR).
The second presentation focused on the Department’s overall performance for 2023/24 and the first quarter of 2024/25, and the filling of vacancies. The Department reported achieving 84% of its annual targets, successfully securing R207 billion in investments, notably through the South Africa Investment Conference (SAIC). Other key achievements included the successful hosting of the African Growth and Opportunity Act (AGOA) forum, supporting the extension of AGOA beyond 2025, and advancing the implementation of the African Continental Free Trade Area. Export growth had reached R241.4 billion, with the global business services sector creating 33 000 jobs, predominantly for youth and women.
However, the DTIC acknowledged challenges in filling key management positions, which had delayed the completion of several projects and affected the governance of its entities. The Department’s budget had been efficiently managed, with 99.5% of the allocated budget spent, but performance in certain areas, such as job creation, fell short of targets. Some programs, such as the revitalisation of industrial parks and approvals in key sectors like automotive and electro-technical, had failed to meet expectations.
During the discussion session, Members raised concerns over the governance and operational challenges within the DTIC and its entities. They stressed the importance of filling vacancies at senior levels, as these vacancies had hindered both performance and compliance. The Committee also expressed concerns about the increasing irregular and wasteful expenditure, and called for stricter oversight and governance reforms.
Members inquired about the delays in audits for certain entities, such as the NLC and ECIC, and the lack of a clear timeline for completing these audits. There was also significant discussion around the impact of fiscal austerity on the DTIC’s ability to fill key positions and fund its operations. Members questioned how the Department had reconciled its performance with budget cuts, and whether there were plans to address the ongoing fiscal constraints. They also asked how it planned to ensure that vacant positions were filled in a timely manner, and how this would affect governance moving forward.
In response, the DTIC leadership acknowledged the concerns raised by the Committee and provided assurances that efforts were underway to fill all key vacancies by the end of the year. The Minister and Deputy Ministers explained that the delays in filling senior roles were due to the need to comply with public service regulations and Cabinet approval processes. They stressed that addressing governance instability was a priority, and steps were being taken to strengthen internal controls and improve compliance across the DTIC’s entities.
The Minister also emphasised the Department’s commitment to economic transformation, noting that programs aimed at promoting inclusivity and job creation, such as the Saldanha Green Hydrogen Project and incentives for black industrialists, would continue to be prioritised. However, the Department recognised that challenges in job creation and the revitalisation of the industrial parks remained areas for improvement.
The meeting concluded with a call for improved accountability and a focus on addressing the governance gaps within the DTIC and its entities. The Committee urged the Department to expedite filling key vacancies, enhance its internal controls, and ensure transparency in its financial management and audit processes. The DTIC leadership committed to taking the necessary steps to address these issues and improve the Department’s performance in the coming year.
Meeting report
Introductory remarks
The Chairperson said the first agenda item was to receive a briefing from the Office of the Auditor-General on the audit outcome as it pertained to the Department of Trade, Industry and Competition (DTIC) and its entities for the 2023/24 financial year. This would give the Committee information regarding the accuracy and reliability of the project’s financial demands and performance, based on the annual reports. It would also highlight key areas affecting governance at the DTIC and its entities. He said that this information should help the Committee conduct oversight when assessing the performance of the entities, with the Committee's objective of producing a budgetary review and recommendation report (BRRR), which would be released soon.
AGSA overview of DTIC's performance
Ms Kumari Naicker, Deputy Business Unit Leader, and Ms Ziyanda Vitshima-Tsengwa, Senior Manager, from the Auditor-General of South Africa (AGSA), led the presentation.
The briefing provided a detailed overview of the Department's performance, challenges, and key insights into its governance and financial health. The overall audit outcomes showed a slight regression compared to the previous year. Five out of eight entities audited by AGSA, including the DTIC, the Competition Commission (CC), and the Companies and Intellectual Property Commission (CIPC), sustained their clean audit status, representing 62.5% of the portfolio. Strong internal controls and committed management contributed to these positive outcomes. However, the National Regulator for Compulsory Specifications (NRCS) had regressed from a clean audit to unqualified with findings, largely due to inadequate reviews of performance information and a key vacancy at the chief executive officer (CEO) level. The South African Bureau of Standards (SABS) had also retained its unqualified audit with findings, due to issues such as material misstatements in its financial statements and non-compliance with legislation. The delayed audit for the National Lotteries Commission (NLC) was due to technical alignment discussions, and its completion was expected by mid-October.
A key challenge highlighted across the portfolio was the vacancies in senior positions, which impacted compliance and overall performance. The report stressed that failing to fill these critical positions, particularly at the CEO and Director-General (DG) levels, posed a significant risk to governance and stability.
The briefing identified several root causes for the audit regressions, including inadequate compliance monitoring at the NRCS and SABS, and the failure to fill key vacancies in time. It was noted that the quality of financial reporting had deteriorated, with material misstatements identified in the annual financial statements of several entities, including the NRCS and SABS. The AGSA urged the Department to strengthen internal reviews to avoid future misstatements.
Performance against key targets showed some shortfalls, particularly in job creation and the revitalisation of industrial parks. For example, the DTIC's goal to create 1 459 jobs through branch interventions had resulted in only 68 jobs being created. Additionally, the target for finalising 90% of approval applications within set timeframes for sectors like automotive and electro-technical industries had been missed, with only 72% achieved.
The report also highlighted issues of irregular expenditure, with SABS being the largest contributor, at R3.8 million. Fruitless and wasteful expenditure was also a concern, with the National Consumer Regulator (NCR) incurring R23 million in such expenses, primarily due to operational system failures and penalties.
On the positive side, the AGSA praised the completion of the East London industrial development zone (IDZ), which would support job creation and manufacturing innovation. However, other projects, such as the Tshwane Automotive special economic zone (SEZ) and the Saldanha IDZ, had been delayed and required close monitoring to ensure the funds were used as intended.
The briefing concluded with key recommendations, urging the Department to fill critical vacancies promptly to maintain governance stability, to strengthen internal controls over financial reporting, and to closely monitor the performance of key projects and incentive programs. Additionally, the AGSA emphasised the need for enhanced compliance reviews to prevent further irregularities and ensure accountability across the portfolio. The importance of fast-tracking the Department’s medium-term strategic framework (MTSF) targets was also highlighted to ensure government meets its broader economic and service delivery goals.

AGSA's response
Ms Naicker began by thanking the Chairperson and the Committee Members for their questions. She proceeded to explain the process involved when the AG opts not to perform an audit directly, commonly referred to as a Section 4(3) audit. She emphasised that the decision to opt out was taken after a rigorous process, but this did not mean that the AG was entirely excluded from the audit. The AG's office was required to concur with the appointment of external auditors, ensuring that the appointment was fit for purpose. Additionally, the AG’s office participates in the audit committees alongside the external auditors, ensuring insights into the audit process, even for entities where the AG had opted out.
She said she would later hand over to her colleague, who would respond to questions about visits to industrial parks, noting that the issue of performance in those parks required closer monitoring. She added that these parks were falling behind on target achievements.
Regarding the validation of third-party information, she acknowledged that there were challenges due to the way the Department’s technical indicator descriptions were set out. She explained that their audit process ensured that any reported achievements were credible. The AG’s office obtained direct confirmations from the companies providing the data to verify information like job creation and other performance measures.
On the issue of value for money in SEZ investments, she clarified that the AG’s role was limited to validating the numbers reported in the financial statements, which follow the modified cash standard. The AG did not conduct a value-for-money assessment as part of the audit, focusing only on whether the accounting had been done according to the framework.
Regarding the use of artificial intelligence, she confirmed that the AG’s office uses various tools to improve audit efficiency. One of these tools was a "data snipper," which extracts key information from documents to speed up the process. She also mentioned a data analytics team that analyses data to provide insights.
On the increase in fruitless and wasteful expenditure, excluding the NLC, she also expressed concern about the large growth in this figure. She said that the AG’s audit process involved identifying the root causes of such findings and ensuring that the entities involved create action plans to address the control gaps. The AG made specific recommendations to prevent the recurrence of these issues.
Her colleague would provide more details on penalties and interest, as well as the overcharging by a supplier. She also acknowledged that vacancies had affected the quality of financial statements, as highlighted in their presentations. In addition, she noted that budget cuts had impacted the portfolio and that discussions were ongoing with National Treasury to address this.
Addressing the question about when audits should be completed, she said that the AG’s office aimed to finish audits by 31 July each year. Financial statements were submitted by 31 May, and the AG allowed two months for the audit process, although technical issues could sometimes cause delays.
She reiterated that the root causes of the regression in audit quality were vacancies and deficiencies in the review process. As for the minimum performance standards for meeting annual targets, she explained that if a target was set, the entity’s efforts should be aligned to meet it. Failure to meet these targets raised concerns, especially given the budget allocation intended for achieving them.
Finally, she addressed the Chairperson’s question about the AG’s involvement in the parliamentary process. She said that after reflecting on the morning’s engagement, the AG’s office would explore ways to be more involved with the Committee and formally communicate how this collaboration could be improved. She concluded by reiterating that the AG was still involved in the audit process, even in cases of Section 4(3) audits.
Ms Vitshima-Tsengwa addressed the question regarding industrial parks. She clarified that the achievement reported for the five-year period had come from the previous audit cycle, and therefore it did not form part of the current year's review. In the current financial year, they had conducted visits for work in progress, but since the industrial parks' achievements were from the previous cycle, no site inspections were done this year.
Regarding limited validation checks, she mentioned that this issue had been discussed with management, and they planned to organise a workshop to align expectations for performance reporting. Although the Department got information from third parties, the Auditor-General's office must ensure the credibility of that information. This year, they had directly engaged with third parties to verify the information reported. Moving forward, they planned to find a more efficient way for the Department to collect the information, so it was readily available for audit purposes.
On the matter of penalties, she said these penalties were charged by SARS due to the late payment of employees' taxes. The Auditor-General highlighted the need for the entity to address this issue because they continued to incur penalties. To avoid this, the entity must adhere to the payment deadlines set by SARS.
Regarding the supplier overcharge, she said it had occurred after the cancellation of a contract. Despite the cancellation, the supplier had claimed charges for work they had already done, though the work was considered unsatisfactory. She promised to provide more detailed information about the supplier and the extent of the overcharge.
On the subject of financial statements, she confirmed that the Auditor General provides recommendations to auditees regarding how they could improve their internal control environments. One key recommendation was for auditees to work closely with their internal audit teams to address and close audit findings. Since internal audit operates throughout the year, while external audits occur towards the end, it is essential that internal audit helps to ensure that controls are implemented to prevent repeat occurrences in the future.
DTIC 2023/24 annual report & Q1 2024/25 performance
The Chairperson said this presentation would assist the Committee in assessing the performance of the national Department concerning its resource allocation and compliance with Section 5 of the Money Bills Amendment Procedure, as outlined in Act 9 of 2009. He then handed over to the Minister and his team to lead the presentation.
Ms Malebo Mabitje-Thompson, Acting Director-General (ADG), DTIC, led the Committee through the DTIC's presentation for the 2023/24 fiscal year and Q1 of 2024/25, which highlighted its strong overall performance, achieving 84% of its annual targets, with 102 out of 122 targets met. Financially, the DTIC had displayed efficient management, spending 99.5% of its allocated budget, disbursing R10.66 billion out of the R10.7 billion total. Importantly, the Department had achieved a clean audit with no findings, reinforcing its strong financial controls. Nine entities under the DTIC umbrella had received clean audits, while seven others had received unqualified audits with findings. However, the audits for the Export Credit Insurance Corporation (ECIC) and National Lotteries Commission (NLC) were still pending due to changes in accounting standards.
Key highlights of the year included securing R207 billion in investments through the South Africa Investment Conference (SAIC) and successfully hosting the African Growth and Opportunity Act (AGOA) forum, which supported extending AGOA beyond 2025. The implementation of the African Continental Free Trade Agreement (AfCFTA) was also a significant achievement, as South Africa launched preferential trade under the agreement in early 2024. Moreover, a landmark R47 billion investment in the Saldanha Green Hydrogen Project solidified the country’s green energy leadership. Export growth reached R241.4 billion, with Africa contributing R117.6 billion. Additionally, the Global Business Services (GBS) sector had created 33 000 jobs, with 89% of those jobs held by youth and 67% by women.
Despite these successes, the DTIC faced challenges, particularly in filling key management positions, which had impacted certain program deliveries. Delayed audits for the ECIC and NLC, owing to changes in accounting standards, also posed difficulties in financial oversight.
Regarding programme performance, the Department has achieved significant progress across its various initiatives.
Programme 1 (Administration) had made strides in improving efficiency and promoting gender representation, with women making up 55% of senior management.
Programme 2 (Trade) achieved six out of 15 targets, focusing on international trade agreements and dispute resolution.
Programme 3 (Investment and Spatial Industrial Development) achieved seven out of 14 targets, securing critical investments, including the Green Hydrogen Project.
Programme 4 (Sectors) was a standout, achieving 14 out of 17 targets, with notable progress in the automotive and food production sectors.
Programme 5 (Regulation) focused on enhancing regulatory frameworks, achieving five out of six targets.
Programme 6 (Incentives) disbursed R1.4 billion to beneficiaries, supporting small businesses and black industrialists, though only two out of 15 targets were met.
Programme 7 (Exports) generated R62.8 billion in manufacturing exports, achieving five out of 12 targets.
Programme 8 (Transformation and Competition) focused on reducing market concentration and advancing employee share ownership, achieving 17 out of 23 targets.
Lastly, Programme 9 (Research) contributed to economic planning, and met five out of 12 targets.
Overall, the DTIC's report highlighted its success in supporting economic growth, investment, job creation, and regulatory improvements, while continuing to face and address challenges such as leadership vacancies and complex audit processes.

Response
Mr Ayabonga Cawe, Chief Commissioner: ITAC, clarified that the export duty concerning the Price Preference System (PPS) had initially been intended as a substitute to the PPS, but they had since evolved into complementary yet differentiated measures. Both aimed to ensure accessible, quality scrap materials for downstream mills and foundries. However, the PPS covered a broader range of metals, including critical minerals like manganese, molybdenum, zinc, lead, and aluminium, copper, iron, and steel. In contrast, the export duty is applied only to iron and steel, as well as aluminium and copper waste.
He explained that the PPS begins by offering scrap metals to local buyers, and if no buyer accepts, only then does the export duty kick in for the narrower set of metals (iron, steel, aluminium and copper). He emphasised that while the two measures served similar purposes, they operated differently and should not be seen as substitutes.
Lastly, he noted that, in line with earlier questions on beneficiation, the functioning of the PPS concerning critical minerals used in battery production needed alignment. Advances in recycling technology presented opportunities for South Africa to use waste materials to create battery-grade inputs for electric vehicles (EVs), offering potential benefits for the country's EV industry.

His key point was that the application of mind should be aligned with the job advertisement and the competencies of the candidate, to prevent any illegality. Mr Gigaba commented that the Committee unfortunately might not be able to leave with a clear answer at that moment, but emphasised the importance of the Department writing to them to provide an explanation regarding the timeframes. He stressed that if the board was to be rotated at the AGM, the Committee needed to know when the AGM was scheduled to take place, as it would give them a clear sense of timing. Similarly, if a post was to be re-advertised, the Committee needed to know when the re-advertisement would happen. He stressed the need for a timeline for the entire process -- from advertisement to short-listing and interviews -- and whether it would take three or four months.
His concern was to avoid a situation where, a year from now, they would still be asking the same questions without clarity on when these posts would be filled. The importance of this issue was due to the fact that they were dealing with critical posts such as CEOs, board members, and chairpersons. He urged the Department to provide a clear sense of when these appointments would occur so that the Committee understood the timeline and the end goal.
DTIC's response
Deputy Minister Godlimpi said that their intention was to fill the vacancies, and initially they had set a goal of completing this process within six months from when they started. Ideally, they aimed to have everything completed by December. Since the advertisements had already been posted, they would thoroughly review the applications. He expressed the hope that the pool of applicants would provide suitable candidates. However, if none of the applicants met the required standards, they would need to explore alternative mechanisms. He estimated that the process would take about three to five months from the time the advertisements were posted. Nevertheless, they were still aiming to complete the process by December, to ease their operations.
He referred to the example of the IDC board mentioned by the Minister, where the AGM was scheduled for May, but the AGM could occur earlier for other boards. He said the vacancies for the IDC board had already been advertised, and new members would soon join the board. The balance between institutional memory, strategy, and deciding how many current members would retire, had been emphasised as part of their approach. He also acknowledged a Member’s point about the "application of the mind," clarifying that it was not an invention of the Minister, but a legal requirement under the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act. This act mandates that decisions must be lawful, rational, and consistent with constitutional principles.
He provided an example to explain the significance of the application of the mind, noting that if someone were appointed as CEO of the IDC and another candidate challenged the decision in court, the court would inquire about the factors considered in making the appointment. The Minister must be able to demonstrate that the decision was rational, lawful, and constitutional. This process ensured accountability and transparency.
He agreed with the Chairperson on the importance of government stability, expressing appreciation for the Chairperson's insistence on these issues. He reiterated that the sooner the vacancies were filled, the better, as this would allow for more substantive discussions about board performance and accountability. He suggested that future discussions should focus on shareholder compacts and the principles underlying them, ensuring that board members understand the responsibilities and implications of their roles.
Finally, he acknowledged other suggestions made during the meeting regarding norms and corporate governance systems. He assured the Committee that they would consider these suggestions carefully and work towards establishing a corporate governance system that meets all standards and could withstand scrutiny.
Closing remarks
In his concluding remarks, the Chairperson said he appreciated the Minister's attendance and emphasised the importance of finding a dynamic way to manage the schedules and the work of the Committee, including the participation of the Deputy Ministers, to ensure proper oversight engagement.
He stressed that the role of the Committee was to hold the Department and its entities accountable. He clarified that the Committee did not view the Department as enemies or friends, but as public servants who must account for every cent of the public purse. It was their responsibility, as parliamentarians, to ask questions and ensure that the work had been carried out in accordance with the agreed mandates between the executive and the legislature. He acknowledged that some of the questions posed may be uncomfortable, but emphasised that this was the nature of the Committee's work.
He gave an assurance that while the questions may be tough, the intention was not to create bad blood, but rather to uphold the principles of democracy by holding each other accountable. He encouraged the Department's representatives not to be afraid when called to appear before the Committee, and reassured them that despite the tough questions, they could still leave in peace after the meeting. He concluded by emphasising that this process of accountability was a normal and necessary part of democratic governance.
The meeting was adjourned.
