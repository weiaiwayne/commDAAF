The Joint Standing Committee on the Financial Management of Parliament convened to assess the Seventh Parliament’s Strategic Plan (2025–2029), the Annual Performance Plan (APP) for 2025/26, and the budget.
Parliament’s 7th administration outlined five key strategic outcomes:
Strengthened oversight and executive accountability.
Improved, timely, and effective lawmaking.
Enhanced public participation, with emphasis on rural and marginalised communities.
Increased Member capacity and institutional performance.
Internal performance optimisation through administrative reform.
This strategy followed a wide consultative process, involving all political parties, and was developed in line with the Financial Management of Parliament and Provincial Legislatures Act (FMPPLA).
The APP centres on two core programmes:
Programme 1: Administration
(
R898m) – supports governance and corporate services.
Programme 2: Legislation & Oversight
(R992m) – prioritises committees, legal services, and Member support.
Despite National Treasury allocating R3.087 billion, Parliament faces a
R521 million shortfall
in its R3.608 billion request. The cumulative funding gap since 2020/21 now exceeds
R1.18 billion
.
Notably, Programme 2 received
150% of its original request
to enhance oversight and committee work, while support services (e.g. Member entitlements and medical aid for former MPs) remain underfunded.
Members expressed concern over the budget’s lack of detail, particularly the need for line-item analysis to support effective oversight. They called for detailed breakdowns—especially for senior offices—amid scrutiny of expenditure increases and perceived disparities in allocation (e.g., underfunding of the Parliamentary Budget Office).
There was widespread support for strengthening Parliament’s oversight capacity, with suggestions to amend internal rules to ensure executive accountability (e.g., ministerial responses to parliamentary questions). The Speaker endorsed this and called for more empowered committees.
The Committee reaffirmed the need to rethink and strengthen public participation frameworks, particularly in light of legal rulings (e.g., Traditional Leadership and Khoisan Act) and recurring deficiencies in public engagement on legislation. Members emphasised targeting affected communities and increasing follow-up post-engagement.
Persistent vacancies—especially in language services, research, and ICT—were highlighted as barriers to performance. Members again requested an updated organogram and raised concerns over acting appointments and alleged duplication in certain senior roles.
Parliament reported that it is developing a digital strategy aligned with national AI frameworks, aiming to enhance committee oversight through custom dashboards, big data integration, and ethical AI use. There is ongoing work to expand virtual public participation and internal performance monitoring systems.
There was concern over the alignment between executive contracts and the Strategic Plan. Members called for access to the Secretary to Parliament’s performance contract and queried if institutional outputs were being undercut by misalignment or weak performance management.
Rising Parmed costs and benefits for pre-1994 MPs were identified as areas of concern. Members questioned whether these costs should remain part of Parliament’s baseline or shift to direct charges from the National Revenue Fund. Parliament acknowledged the unsustainable nature of these costs and suggested legislative reform.
International travel logistics and cost inefficiencies were raised, with Members advocating for earlier approvals and possible in-house travel management.
the discussion also touched on
Parliament’s contribution to tackling South Africa’s structural challenges—poverty, unemployment, inequality, and transformation. Members said there should be a more transformative, impact-driven legislative and oversight agenda that aligns Parliament’s work with the lived realities of South Africans.
Members called for a follow-up meeting to address unresolved issues, particularly related to staffing.
Meeting report
Opening
The Chairperson opened the meeting and confirmed there was sufficient quorum. She said today marks an important engagement as the Committee considers Parliament’s Strategic Plan, Annual Performance Plan (APP) and the 2025/26 budget. This document reflects the institution's roadmap for delivering on its institutional mandate amidst persistent fiscal challenges. The Strategic Plan highlights Parliament's commitment to strengthening oversight, improving legislative impact, enhancing public participation and building Members' capacity. The extensive consultation process involving political parties, stakeholders and committees is noted and appreciated. However, this ambitious agenda is framed within a context of constrained resources, including a current funding shortfall of R521 million and a cumulative budget exceeding R1.1 billion since 2020/21.
The Committee’s task today is to critically assess whether the proposed resource allocations are sufficient and realistic to meet the planned performance targets. Key issues for deliberation include governance alignment with the adequacy of public participation mechanisms, capacity building programmes, Executive accountability and the viability, and of course, containment measures. The Committee must also assess the ongoing engagement with National Treasury towards developing a sustainable funding model for Parliament.

7th Parliament Strategic Plan; Parliament 2025/26 Annual Performance Plan
Speaker Didiza opened the briefing by noting the administration was present to table the Strategic Plan, as well as the Annual Performance Plan, supported with the budget. In February, when undertaking the orientation, there was a brief indication that the work started in September 2024 on the Strategic Plan. It is the norm that when the new administration is set up, a process must be undertaken by Parliament to actually look at the Strategic Plan for the next five-year period for the new administration’s term in office.
Annually, there is the Annual Performance Plan on how Parliament is performing against that Strategic Plan and how the resources that support it are actually expanded.
The process was continued after February to finalise the consultation with the parties. All the parties, both in the NCOP and the NA, participated in ensuring the views of the parties were received on the Strategic Plan. Some of them even gave detailed presentations in written form, while others participated in the forum. Only some of the presentations received dealt with issues of the Strategic Plan, but others also raised issues that will have to be taken up in other forums, such as the Rules Committee, because they related to rules as well as procedures. But the priority is that the various parties, after consultation, came up with a plan in terms of the seventh administration that will support the Strategic Plan, which is about strengthening oversight, looking at ways in which we can enhance mechanisms to monitor government responsiveness, improve data utilisation and implement accountability frameworks. Some of those issues have already been highlighted in a number of discussions, as well as issues that have been deliberated among both Houses on how we support committees actually to do their oversight work. The other issues related to strengthening oversight, mechanisms to actually follow up on the House resolutions that relate to what the Executive has to do and see to what extent there is responsiveness
The second priority that was agreed upon was enhancing legislative processes and public involvement. Members will be aware that one of the issues that has come up, even in this administration, that have come out in the courts, but also some of the matters that have been taken up by other parties, asking to what extent had there been public involvement in the development of particular legislation, such as the Traditional Leadership and Khoisan Act, which the courts turned back to say we needed to do more in terms of public engagement. There is other legislation being challenged, such as the Expropriation Act, and others being challenged legally about the extent of public consultation, provincial mandates, etc. She raised this because the Seventh Administration really needs to look at how public involvement is strengthened, not only in law-making but also in policy and oversight, as Parliament.
The other issue that related to enhancing legislative processes is the time it takes for legislation to pass which the institution must review such that there is not legislation that take longer and longer in the process, and by the time the legislation maybe gets passed, the issue that it sought to address might be irrelevant at that time.
The other issue that was a priority identified for this administration during those consultation was improving public appointment processes. there have been some concerns raised about how various appointments are made for people to serve in public institutions, particularly such as Chapter Nine institutions and others. So this is another issue where there was a view from the consultation that we need to improve that appointment process so that it's also transparent and that everybody is comfortable with how such appointments have been made.
The last issue was improving international engagements, and not only just in participating in multilateral fora, but also to what extent has Parliament examined the treaties that our countries enter into, and how those are ratified? How much time does Parliament have to engage on those issues to see whether the national interest has been covered? These were the strategic priorities that, through those consultations, were highlighted by Members.
And as part of the work of the administration, we have sought to look at how those issues can actually be operationalised in terms of the work that needs to be done, and look at how we fund those areas and issues that have been raised. In particular, enhancing oversight - have there been resources for committees where there has been limited support to committees to do their work? Has there been any transparency about the resources that committees have to undertake their task? So in the budget process, we've looked at how some of those issues that had arisen in the consultation process that produced the Strategic Plan were supported.

Strategic Context and Planning Process
The 7th Parliament's strategic direction was developed through a consultative, inclusive process guided by the
Financial Management of Parliament and Provincial Legislatures Act (FMPPLA)
. Political parties, including the EFF, RISE Mzansi, and DA, submitted inputs that influenced the priorities and performance indicators adopted.
The strategic planning process emphasises:

Executive accountability
.
Improving
lawmaking processes
.
Expanding
public participation
, especially among rural and marginalised communities.
Strengthening
committee operations
and
Member capacity
.
Improving coordination across
government spheres and state arms
.
Strategic Priorities and Outcomes (2025–2029)
The overarching impact statement is a commitment to
transform society and improve the quality of life for all citizens
.
Five strategic outcomes guide Parliament’s performance:
Strengthened oversight and Executive responsiveness.
Impactful, timely, and effective lawmaking.
Meaningful and inclusive public engagement.
Enhanced Member performance and institutional capacity.
Improved internal performance through optimised administrative processes.
Annual Performance Plan and Budget Alignment
The APP for FY2025/26 is structured around two core programmes:
Programme 1: Administration
– focused on governance, corporate services, and institutional support.
Programme 2: Legislation and Oversight
– supports parliamentary committees, legal and procedural services, and Member services.
Performance targets are tied to these programmes to ensure delivery of the strategic outcomes.
Human capital
remains the key cost driver in implementing the APP.
Budget Overview
The proposed budget for Parliament in 2025/26:
Total requested
:
608 billion (excluding rebuilding).
National Treasury allocation
: R3.087 billion.
Shortfall
: R521 million.
The
Core Business Division
—responsible for committees and public participation—was prioritised, receiving 150% of its original funding request to ensure effective delivery. However, areas such as Member entitlements, administrative infrastructure, and former Members' medical aid remain underfunded.
Key allocations:
Programme 2 (Legislation & Oversight)
:
R992 million.
Programme 1 (Administration)
: R898 million.
Associated Services
: R885 million (support for Members and political parties).
Direct Charges (Member remuneration and exit benefits)
:
R519 million.
Cost Containment and Efficiency Measures
Recognising fiscal constraints, Parliament has initiated several operational efficiency strategies:
Improved procurement and travel planning.
Workforce optimisation and voluntary retirement initiatives.
Rationalisation of ICT costs and printing.
Streamlining of international delegation sizes and allowances.
These measures aim to preserve strategic delivery despite funding gaps.
Conclusion and Way Forward
Parliament’s capacity to fulfill its constitutional mandate is under
significant financial pressure
. The funding shortfall jeopardises its ability to enhance responsiveness, oversight, and citizen engagement. In response, Parliament is engaging with the
Financial and Fiscal Commission
and
National Treasury
to develop a more sustainable funding model. Formal engagements are set to begin in
July 2025
.
