Meeting Summary
The Portfolio Committee on Tourism convened for a strategic planning workshop to consider its Draft Strategic Plan (2025–2030) and Draft Annual Performance Plan (2025/26). The presentations outlined the Committee’s mandate, long-term vision, and oversight priorities, with emphasis on strengthening accountability, transformation, and sustainable growth of the tourism sector. The five-year plan stressed tourism’s role in job creation and inclusive growth, while also highlighting challenges such as underfunding, visa and airlift barriers, safety concerns, outdated strategies, and slow transformation. Legacy issues like the incomplete Tourism Amendment Bill, poor support for SMMEs, and neglect of township and rural tourism were identified as central areas for oversight. The Annual Performance Plan detailed the programme for the coming year, including oversight visits, colloquiums, international benchmarking study tours, and cross-committee engagements to ensure a “whole of government” approach. The Committee was also briefed on its R3 million budget allocation, which would fund approximately 15 meetings, two domestic oversight visits, colloquiums, and potentially international study tours.
Members raised substantive priorities for the Committee’s work. Several called for more direct engagement with stakeholders such as the Tourism Transformation Council and South African Township and Village Tourism Association (SATOVITO), preferably without the Department present, to allow for frank discussion. The importance of reaching out beyond established industry bodies to township, rural, and small-scale operators was highlighted. Oversight of heritage sites, which often fell under other departments but were central to tourism, was also raised, with Members recommending joint oversight visits with committees like Home Affairs and Sports, Arts and Culture. Concerns about the employment of undocumented foreign nationals in the hospitality sector, job creation statistics in tourism, and late invitations to events hosted by the executive were also emphasised.
For oversight visits, Members suggested Robben Island, Limpopo, Gauteng, Mpumalanga, and the Eastern Cape. Agri-tourism, eco-tourism, and sport tourism were highlighted as growth areas needing focused oversight. Additional proposals included exploring the merger of SA Tourism and Brand SA to avoid duplication, monitoring the impact of global brand campaigns, and ensuring that SMMEs benefit from major events like the Tourism Indaba. There was broad support for international benchmarking tours, but Members differed on destinations. Some favoured BRICS countries as models for comparison, while others pointed to the US and Europe, particularly given South African Tourism’s international offices.
The Chairperson noted the importance of inclusive oversight, fairness in allocating international travel opportunities, and timely sharing of invitations with Members. She emphaised the need for digital transformation in tourism marketing and stronger oversight of community benefits from government programmes. Members were encouraged to submit written inputs to refine the Committee Strategic Plan and Annual Performance Plan (APP) ahead of their adoption.
It was noted that the Minister had not as yet shared the promised addenda concerning the SAT Board reports and records discussed the previous week but the Committee would continue to request these.

Strategic Plan
The Draft Strategic Plan sets out a comprehensive framework for guiding parliamentary oversight, legislative review, and sectoral transformation within the tourism industry during the Seventh Parliament. The plan opens with a recognition of tourism’s vital role in the South African economy as one of the leading job creators, drivers of inclusive growth, and contributors to infrastructure development. The Government of National Unity (GNU), through its Medium-Term Development Plan (2024-2029), has highlighted tourism as a priority sector under three overarching national goals: inclusive growth and job creation, reducing poverty and the high cost of living, and building a capable, ethical, and developmental state. In this context, the Portfolio Committee commits itself to strengthening its inner workings, enhancing accountability in the Department of Tourism and South African Tourism, and ensuring meaningful public participation in decision-making processes. The plan sets a long-term benchmark of achieving 15 million international arrivals by 2030, while emphasising that domestic tourism will be a crucial pillar of recovery and sustainability in the years ahead.
The strategic overview articulates the Committee’s vision of a transformative Parliament that uplifts the quality of life for all South Africans, underpinned by values of openness, integrity, responsiveness, accountability, professionalism, teamwork, and respect, all framed within the African philosophy of Ubuntu. The mission is rooted in fulfilling Parliament’s constitutional functions of passing laws, scrutinising executive action, and representing the voices of citizens. The Committee’s mandate is derived not only from the Constitution but also from key legislation such as the Powers, Privileges and Immunities of Parliament and Provincial Legislatures Act, the Financial Management of Parliament Act, and the Money Bills Amendment Procedure Act. Within this mandate, the Committee is tasked with five core objectives: legislating, oversight, facilitating public participation, fostering co-operative governance, and engaging in international relations. Tourism, being listed as a concurrent competence across national, provincial, and local government, requires coordinated intergovernmental action, a recurring theme in the Committee’s approach.
The plan aligns with national development frameworks, including the National Development Plan (NDP) 2030, the New Growth Path, the Economic Reconstruction and Recovery Plan (ERRP), the 2024 White Paper on Tourism, and the National Tourism Sector Strategy (NTSS). These policy frameworks consistently highlight tourism as a labour-intensive sector with strong potential for job creation, foreign direct investment, and foreign exchange earnings. The NDP envisages tourism as a major driver of growth capable of contributing to employment, rural development, infrastructure investment, and positioning South Africa competitively in global services trade. Similarly, the NTSS sets ambitious targets such as raising GDP contribution from R118 billion in 2015 to R302 billion in 2026 and expanding sectoral employment to one million jobs. The Committee acknowledges the importance of these strategies while also stressing the urgency of reviewing outdated sectoral policies, some dating back over a decade, that have not kept pace with evolving global trends.
The situational analysis provides a nuanced assessment of tourism’s economic contribution and challenges. According to projections by the World Travel & Tourism Council (WTTC), by 2025 the sector is expected to support 1.9 million jobs, surpassing pre-pandemic employment levels, and contribute R659.8 billion to GDP, representing 8.9% of national output. Domestic visitor spending, forecast at R445 billion, has already surpassed 2019 levels, demonstrating resilience in the face of global uncertainties. However, international visitor spending, at R128.4 billion, remains significantly below its pre-COVID peak, indicating a lag in inbound tourism recovery. While employment growth is leading the recovery, the Committee recognises that the sector still faces vulnerabilities, including global economic shocks, reputational risks related to crime and safety, and structural barriers such as limited airlift capacity and visa restrictions. Looking ahead, the WTTC forecasts sustained growth through 2035, with tourism potentially employing 2.6 million people and contributing R911.7 billion to GDP, but this depends on coordinated action to remove systemic barriers.
The Strategic Plan devotes significant attention to legacy issues carried over from the Sixth Parliament. These include the incomplete Tourism Amendment Bill, which aims to address gaps in the regulation of short-term rentals, tourist grading, and transformation through the Tourism Equity Fund. Transformation remains a stubbornly slow-moving issue in the sector, with limited progress since 1994, and will therefore be a central oversight focus. The Committee also notes the neglect of domestic tourism in Villages, Townships, Small Towns, and Dorpies (VTSDs), underfunding of the tourism vote despite its economic significance, reputational challenges linked to safety, xenophobia, and animal welfare, as well as airlift and connectivity constraints. Further challenges include insufficient skills development, poor public participation in infrastructure projects, weak internal audit functions, unimplemented forensic reports into maladministration, and a lack of a coordinated response to climate change. The Committee plans to follow up rigorously on all these issues while ensuring that transformation, skills development, destination development, and tourism SMMEs receive priority support.
The Committee’s strategic priorities for the Seventh Parliament are multifaceted.
- First, it will push for legislative review, particularly amending the Tourism Act of 2014 to regulate the sharing economy (such as Airbnb and Uber) and modernise the framework for emerging sector trends.
- Second, it will emphasise domestic tourism as a stabilising force against global volatility, promoting year-round demand, spreading economic benefits beyond major urban centres, and building patriotic pride in local travel.
- Third, it will focus on rural tourism in VTSDs, addressing barriers such as inadequate infrastructure, limited human resource capacity, policy mismatches, and financing constraints that undermine community participation in tourism development.
- Fourth, the Committee will oversee reforms in the visa regime, particularly monitoring the Trusted Tour Operator Scheme and advocating for expanded e-visa systems.
- Fifth, airlift and connectivity will be targeted through oversight of the Route Development Marketing Plan to ensure affordable, accessible, and competitive flight options.
- Sixth, the Committee will press for systematic reviews of outdated strategies and ensure that tourism infrastructure projects are implemented efficiently with strong community participation, maintenance plans, and aftercare support.
- Lastly, the Committee stresses the need to remove structural barriers to growth through intergovernmental collaboration, regulatory streamlining, financing support for SMMEs, and prioritisation of safety and sustainability.
In its oversight approach, the Committee commits to adopting a “whole of government” strategy, convening joint meetings with a wide range of departments to address tourism’s interdependencies. This includes engagements with Higher Education (for skills training and curricula), Transport (for airlift and infrastructure), Cooperative Governance and Traditional Affairs (for integration of tourism in LED and IDPs), Sports, Arts and Culture (for heritage and cultural tourism), Police (for tourist safety strategies), International Relations (for global marketing through missions abroad), Home Affairs (for visa reforms), National Treasury (for adequate sectoral funding), Environmental Affairs (for sustainability and animal welfare), and several others. This coordinated oversight model is intended to break down silos and ensure tourism is mainstreamed across government.

While members were usually only asked about their availability for trips, there were operational challenges that required attention. It was important for members to hear from the support staff about the difficulties they encountered. Staff often bore the brunt of frustrations when members misbehaved or failed to cooperate, and that this session provided an opportunity to address such matters openly. He invited staff to speak briefly on what they required from members to provide effective support during oversight activities.
Dr Khuzwayo acknowledged that this placed staff “on the spot,” but stressed that it was necessary, as staff typically did not have a voice during formal meetings. Given the strategic nature of the session, it was the appropriate moment for these concerns to be expressed, rather than leaving them unspoken.
A Support Staff member briefly outlined her responsibilities for providing administrative and logistical support for the committee, including preparing and coordinating all logistics for committee meetings, workshops, seminars, conferences, public hearings, and both local and international travel.
Dr Khuzwayo explained that staff might feel intimidated when asked to speak, but there were common challenges worth acknowledging. For example, members often confirmed attendance for an oversight visit, leading to travel and accommodation being booked, only to withdraw at the last minute. This created serious logistical complications and even instances of fruitless expenditure, since cancellations were sometimes impossible and Parliament still had to cover the costs. Some members also left midway through oversight visits, requiring staff to make costly flight changes. Such actions not only strained resources but also placed unfair responsibility on committee staff, some of whom were even charged for these costs. These challenges could undermine the financial management of committee activities. Changing travel arrangements was particularly difficult, as it required going through procurement procedures, documentation, and order forms, which turned it into a “nightmare” for staff.
Shifting the discussion to the broader context of oversight, Dr Khuzwayo stressed the importance of strengthening the committee’s role in holding the executive accountable, especially in light of the new government’s emphasis on responsiveness. He noted that the Tourism Bill was expected to be introduced in the following year and that the Department had already signalled forthcoming amendments to certain tourism strategies. He also highlighted petitions received from SMMEs about late payments and emphasised the committee’s responsibility to unblock such challenges, given that the government itself was often the worst offender in delayed payments.
He proposed that transformation within the tourism sector required greater visibility, such as holding a transformation summit annually. He suggested that the committee should aim for at least three oversight visits per year, structured to include not just site inspections but also stakeholder engagements with local tourism bodies. He pointed out the importance of ensuring that these oversight activities and engagements aligned with the committee’s strategic plans and legislative responsibilities, including consideration of the annual reports, annual performance plans, and budget reviews of the Department and South African Tourism. He mentioned ongoing and planned activities such as joint committee meetings, international benchmarking study tours, and colloquiums on specific issues like hunting, which would bring together relevant stakeholders and regulatory bodies.
Dr Khuzwayo said that the work of staff, particularly content advisors and researchers, was central to supporting the committee. He urged members to make full use of content advisor notes and research briefs, explaining that if members failed to engage with these documents, they risked entering meetings unprepared. The analyses and briefings by staff provided essential guidance to the committee.
At this point, Ms Sisanda. Loni, Committee Researcher, introduced herself and explained her role. She said her work complemented that of her colleague by providing analysis of the Department’s quarterly and annual performance, including both budgetary and non-financial aspects. She also prepared fact sheets and background papers, which provided members with a broader understanding of tourism sub-sectors and their interconnections. She noted that her research supported the committee in various ways, including oversight visits, where she could provide papers on the destination area and its tourism sector, and in comparative analyses between South Africa and other countries. It was ultimately up to members how they used this information, whether to shape the programme of work or to raise informed questions to the Minister in the House.
Dr Khuzwayo reflected on the importance of aligning research more directly with the committee’s work. He emphasised the need for monitoring if department programmes aligned with tourism sector strategies, and highlighted the importance of documenting this work in the committee’s annual performance report. He underscored the significance of effective budget utilisation and the role of staff in ensuring that the committee remained informed, strategic, and accountable in its operations.
Strategic Plan risks
Risk management considerations reveal the challenges the Committee expects to face, including persistent changes to parliamentary programmes, availability of members, non-implementation of resolutions, reprioritisation of budgets, outdated tools of trade, and lengthy approval processes. Mitigation strategies include leveraging virtual meeting technologies, escalating budget issues to the House Chairperson, intensifying follow-ups with ministries, and improving collaboration across committee secretariats. Importantly, the Committee is allocated R3 million annually, which it considers insufficient given its ambitious oversight agenda, though it will pursue strategic reprioritisation of resources.
Projected Expenditure
Mr Disang Mocumi, Committee Secretary, informed members that the Committee had a fixed budget of R3 million from to date until April 2026. This budget would be allocated to key areas of Committee activity to ensure both oversight and strategic international engagement.
He noted that not all the funds initially earmarked for committee meetings would be used, as the Committee expected to hold approximately 15 meetings between the current period and April next year. Parliamentary recess at the end of the year, as well as the State of the Nation Address, would limit the number of meetings. Nonetheless, R100 000 had been set aside to ensure the smooth functioning of regular parliamentary work.
R600,000 had been allocated to oversight visits, reflecting the Committee’s emphasis on direct engagement with tourism sites, projects, and communities. The Committee intended to conduct two oversight visits before the close of the financial year in April.
Colloquiums and summits, which provide platforms for dialogue and knowledge exchange, had been allocated R100 000. These events would facilitate roundtable discussions on relevant tourism issues.
The largest portion of the budget, R2 million, had been reserved for international benchmarking study tours. The Committee was considering two countries for these tours, potentially including New York, given the limited prior engagement reported in annual reports, or European destinations such as the Netherlands. Considerations of cost-effectiveness and logistics would guide the selection of countries, possibly allowing for visits to multiple nearby locations. An African country, such as Kenya, was also being considered due to its competitive tourism sector and relevance for regional benchmarking.
R200 000 had been allocated for stakeholder invitations, while no funds had yet been set aside for advertisements or public hearings on the Tourism Amendment Bill. Such expenditures might be considered in future years once the legislative process has advanced.
The planned expenditure reflected the Committee’s priorities of combining rigorous oversight with strategic international exposure, while remaining within the R3 million budget ceiling imposed by Parliament.
Committee Annual Performance Plan 2025/26
Dr Khuzwayo said that the draft APP would guide the Committee’s activities and priorities for the year and the focus areas were aligned with the five-year strategic plan and to the quarterly programmes.
Dr Khuzwayo highlighted the Committee’s legislative responsibilities, explaining that while the Department was not currently advancing legislation, it was in the process of developing a framework for the shared economy in the current quarter. Once this framework was finalised, the Committee would need to examine it, as it aligned with the longstanding intention to amend the Tourism Amendment Bill. He pointed to six department strategies scheduled for briefing later in the year, stressing that these were important for the Committee’s oversight despite earlier delays in their presentation.
On joint committee work, Dr Khuzwayo said that engagements were being planned with the transport sector, as well as with small business stakeholders. He suggested that the National Treasury, the Department of Small Business Development, and the Department of Planning, Monitoring and Evaluation (DPME) should also be included, given the number of petitions and complaints the Committee had received from businesses experiencing delayed payments. He explained that such joint engagements would help unblock challenges in the payment system and support transformation in the tourism sector, including small, medium, and micro enterprises (SMMEs).
Turning to oversight visits, Dr Khuzwayo proposed Gauteng as the first option, citing the range of issues and developments underway in the province. Later in the year or early in the next, the Committee could conduct an oversight in either the Eastern Cape, where there were several departmental infrastructure projects or Mpumalanga, which faced pressing sectoral challenges, particularly along the Panorama Route. He emphasised that these were proposals and that the final decision lay with members, who could also consider alternative destinations such as KwaZulu-Natal.
Dr Khuzwayo advised that the Committee should integrate stakeholder meetings into all oversight visits, engaging with provincial tourism boards and other key role players.
He referred to possible international study tours, noting that South African Tourism had offices in the Netherlands, while proximity to other European countries such as France could make logistics easier. He encouraged members to consider the value of observing international infrastructure and transport innovations such as bullet trains, as they provided lessons for strengthening domestic tourism. He reminded members that if international travel was approved, passport applications and related processes would need to be initiated immediately.
Dr Khuzwayo confirmed that engagements with SAT and SATOVITO had already been held earlier in the year, but additional stakeholder meetings would be factored into future oversight work.
Mr Mocumi spoke to logistics planning explaining that letters to the relevant countries had to be sent at least four to five months in advance and that passports for members would need to be processed without delay.
Mr Mocumi pointed out challenges relating to department invitations received at short notice, using the recent example of the G20 engagement. He explained that when invitations arrived late, it was almost impossible to process them through the required parliamentary channels, as approvals from the House Chairperson and other officials could take several days. A minimum of a week was needed to meet administrative requirements.
Turning to the focus areas for oversight, Mr Mocumi stated that these were already reflected in the APP and would be finalised in the committee programme for the upcoming week. He outlined the key accountability mechanisms that the Committee would need to address between September and April, including quarterly reports, budgetary review and recommendation reports, and annual reports with financial statements. He proposed that job creation be prioritised, given its importance within the Government of National Unity. In this regard, he suggested engagements with the Federated Hospitality Association of Southern Africa, the Tourism Business Council of South Africa, and the Department of Labour and Employment, particularly to address concerns about the employment of foreigners over locals in the hospitality sector and to discuss skills development.
The amendment of the Tourism Act should remain on the Committee’s agenda, even though the Department was unlikely to table it before the end of the financial year. He proposed that the Committee also engage the Western Cape provincial government on township and village tourism initiatives, noting that while Parliament was based in Cape Town, oversight in the Western Cape had been minimal. He suggested that the Committee look into diverse tourism offerings in smaller towns, investigate criteria used for accrediting hospitality facilities, and examine whether these criteria discriminated against poorer areas.
Mr Mocumi highlighted other areas requiring oversight. These included the Green Incentive Programme, noting that Robben Island had been a beneficiary, and the global brand campaign, where a cost-benefit analysis was required to assess if the significant spending benefited ordinary people. He stressed the need to look beyond engagements with the Development Bank of Southern Africa (DBSA) to identify subcontracted companies implementing projects on behalf of SA Tourism. He recommended deeper scrutiny of SMEs’ participation in major events such as Meetings Africa and the Tourism Indaba, where the main beneficiaries were often larger service providers rather than small enterprises.
He proposed that the Committee advocate for the merger of SA Tourism and Brand South Africa to address duplication and maximise impact. Other priorities mentioned included dual pricing in hotels to support domestic tourism, promotion of sport tourism, engagement with National Treasury on tourism’s contribution to the economy, and a comprehensive route development strategy that encompassed road and rail alongside aviation. Oversight visits were being clustered for efficiency, such as Limpopo with Mpumalanga, North West with Gauteng, and Eastern Cape with KwaZulu-Natal, while international study tours would be analysed further the following week.
Finally, Mr Mocumi referred to symposiums on funding mechanisms, transformation, and employment creation, suggesting these could be intertwined. He also flagged eco-tourism and aqua tourism as areas that could be incorporated into the Committee’s work.
Dr Khuzwayo concluded by noting that the purpose of the session was to guide members through the process and prepare the Committee for the approval of the programme in the coming week. Although there was limited time, members’ inputs would be incorporated into the final document. He encouraged members to make contributions and submissions before the next meeting so that all priorities could be reflected in the programme.
Discussion
The Chairperson thanked Dr Khuzwayo and his team noting that the document was extensive and comprehensive.
Ms A Hlongo (ANC) said she was very pleased with what had been presented by the team and commended the work that had gone into preparing the document for the committee.
She asked, firstly, whether public hearings were only held when there were bills or acts to amend, or whether the portfolio committee itself could go directly to meet stakeholders. She raised concerns about the G20, noting that while its implementation was evident in Cape Town, there was little happening in rural areas. In previous presentations, the G20 had been made to appear as if it would benefit local people and develop small tourism SMMEs, but in reality, what she was observing seemed unrelated to the growth of the township economy. She suggested that the committee needed to find ways, through oversight and stakeholder engagement, to address this. She proposed that since it was September, which is Tourism Month, the committee should consider holding meetings outside the usual venues. She suggested visits to sites such as SANParks and Robben Island so that members could better understand what was being spoken about in the sector during committee meetings.
She then raised another concern from her experience attending the Minister’s outreach in Mpumalanga. The outreach was not a true stakeholder or institutional activity, but rather an event mainly with students. She had even questioned the communications outreach officer, asking why stakeholders were not included, since they were the ones who needed to participate. She expressed concern that such exclusions were happening and reiterated her question about public hearings, when and how they were conducted, because she felt that the sector itself was being left behind. She argued that it was mainly the established stakeholders who were recognised, while those at the bottom, who were still trying to grow, were excluded.
Ms Hlongo said that the committee’s programme should prioritise going directly to such stakeholders. She noted that when stakeholders raised concerns in meetings, the department often responded by citing entities like SATOVITO. Yet, the committee had received many complaints from SATOVITO itself, claiming they were not being supported by the department. She described this as the department using SATOVITO as a shield to claim matters were being addressed, while in reality, they were not.
She proposed that the committee should consider meeting directly with entities such as the Tourism Transformation Council of South Africa (TTCSA) or SATOVITO without the department being present. She argued that when these entities met the committee in the department’s presence, they appeared reluctant to raise certain issues out of fear. She also suggested joint oversight visits with other portfolio committees, for example, Home Affairs, since land borders were critical for tourism but were currently not functioning well. She highlighted land borders with Lesotho, Swaziland, Mozambique, Zimbabwe, Zambia, and Namibia as examples.
Ms Hlongo emphasised the importance of oversight in relation to heritage sites, which often fell under the Departments of Sports, Arts and Culture or Environmental Affairs. She cautioned that when questions were raised about heritage sites, the Department of Tourism would often respond that they fell under other departments. However, from society’s perspective, heritage sites were seen as part of tourism. She argued that joint oversight visits with other portfolio committees were necessary to properly understand the role tourism played in relation to heritage sites, as well as the roles of the other departments. She admitted that before studying tourism herself, she had also assumed heritage sites fell under tourism.
She expressed her full support for international oversight visits, though she could not specify which countries the committee should visit. She would rely on input from other members and the research team, who should identify which countries would be most valuable for the committee to visit to learn how South Africa’s tourism could be strengthened.
She supported undertaking oversight visits as soon as possible.

Ms Ismail expressed disappointment that not a single Executive response to tourism-related written questions had appeared on the order paper. She did not understand why the Minister was unavailable to respond to questions. This was especially troubling given the public outcry and extensive media coverage of the South African Tourism CEO and the issues with the board. At the very least, a written response to a Member’s question on the suspension of the CEO of South African Tourism, Nombulelo Guliwe, should have been on the order paper. She pointed out that if the Minister could not attend meetings in person, she could have joined virtually via Zoom. She urged the Chairperson to highlight this matter formally so that the importance of ministerial accountability was reinforced.
Ms Ismail referred to the Minister failing to provide the necessary documents and she was uncertain how best to escalate this matter, as she had already submitted a prior application to the Department herself, but with little result. She suggested that perhaps there were ways at the parliamentary level to enforce compliance and asked the Chairperson to look into this further.
On the strategic plan, she would submit her detailed inputs in writing. However, she raised a specific query on how job creation numbers were calculated in the tourism sector, particularly how expenditure per tourist was quantified when contributions to GDP were reported. Also was there a specification of the job types that made up the reported figure of 1.9 million people employed?
Ms E. Linde (DA, Western Cape) said she had really enjoyed the proposed plan presented by Dr Khuzwayo. She would make submissions in writing, particularly on proposals on the unfunded mandates raised by municipalities. She proposed the introduction of key performance indicators (KPIs) for the committee itself, which she believed would be an important accountability tool.
Turning to Robben Island, she explained that the site was currently under construction and closed to tourists, which presented a good opportunity for the committee to conduct an oversight visit. She suggested arranging a day trip to view the ongoing work, noting that the board might be willing to grant the committee access. Such a visit would allow members to see first-hand what developments were taking place on the island.
On the issue of event invitations, she raised a concern. She recalled reading in a newspaper that the Deputy Minister had opened a women-owned hotel in Limpopo during Women’s Month. This event took place while she and some of her colleagues were in the same area, yet they had not been invited and only found out about it afterwards. This exclusion was problematic and asked if the Chairperson could formally propose to the Minister and Deputy Minister that committee members be invited to such events in the future. These were events connected to the executive’s work, and that the committee should be able to share in those successes rather than being sidelined.
On the study tour proposals, she emphasised the importance of choosing destinations that could provide valuable lessons, particularly countries that had developed successful models of recovery after COVID-19. She specifically mentioned digital tourism, cruise tourism, and the integration of airlift and cruise infrastructure as areas worth examining. Also important was rural and Agri-tourism, which was one of her personal interests. Many countries already had well-established models in these areas, pointing to examples in South Asia, the Caribbean, Spain, and Italy. She suggested that these could serve as useful case studies for the committee’s learning.
On the logistics of international oversight trips, it was clear that planning and funding would be necessary, and that often only one representative per political party was included. She asked how such decisions were made: if overseas visits were intended for the entire committee or if only selected members would attend, and what criteria were used to determine which members were chosen.
Ms R Mashabela (EFF) said that she supports the draft Annual Performance Plan, but stressed that this support was conditional upon the inclusion of critical amendments designed to strengthen accountability, transformation, and state-led development in the tourism sector. She argued that the APP should be amended to incorporate several key elements: a constitutional and regional framework, a cooperation annexe with regional partners, a localisation and transformation standard, detailed annual targets, and a completion index that would allow proper tracking of infrastructure project delivery.
She noted that there were a number of projects built by the Department in Limpopo, and she expressed a wish for the committee to conduct an oversight visit there to evaluate these projects directly.
She had many additional points to raise and she would submit the remainder of her inputs in writing to save time.
The Chairperson addressed the matter of overseas trips, noting that the committee should not only consider destinations of general interest, but also reflect on the countries where South Africa already has tourism offices responsible for marketing the country as a destination. She listed New York, Germany, United Kingdom and the Netherlands as examples and suggested that these locations could be clustered together, particularly for European visits. This was homework for the research team to assist in planning.
She reflected on her experience in a previous committee where community meetings were held during oversight visits. These engagements brought together communities and various stakeholders. She suggested that the committee could adopt a similar approach, inviting stakeholders such as NGOs, non-profit companies, and industry associations like SATOVITO to participate. This would allow the committee to better understand the type of assistance stakeholders were receiving and how tourism functions at different levels of government, from municipalities to provinces. It would also help trace how money flows from the province to communities and if communities were benefiting.
The Chairperson recalled previous successful engagements, including one held in Durban, which had seen strong exchanges. She proposed that SATOVITO be invited to future engagements and encouraged stakeholder participation at the provincial level, such as in Limpopo, where members could be brought into discussions. In addition, she suggested liaising with municipalities to identify registered local tourism organisations and inviting them, along with representatives from the hospitality sector, so that the committee could gain a full understanding of the tourism landscape in each province. She emphasised that oversight should include not only tourist attractions but also community and stakeholder engagements, potentially structured as workshops or symposiums.
Turning to employment in the hospitality industry, she said this was a conversation the committee needed to have, especially in the Western Cape, where many members had observed challenges. She raised the issue of undocumented foreign nationals working in the sector, highlighting that many did not contribute to tax revenue or the fiscus, which created additional strain. She suggested that the Department of Labour be involved in these engagements by deploying more inspectors to ensure compliance in the industry.
She also encouraged oversight in smaller towns that host important heritage sites. As an example, she cited the Sarah Baartman site in the Eastern Cape, which needed restoration. While acknowledging that responsibility might fall under Sport, Arts and Culture rather than Tourism, she stressed that such sites were vital for domestic tourism. While international tourists might visit to learn history, South Africans also had a deep personal connection to such sites, and it was important to preserve them for future generations.
On sports tourism, the Chairperson observed that large events were a major driver of travel, but even local matches, such as those between Orlando Pirates and Kaizer Chiefs, generated significant movement. Hotels should be encouraged to lower prices during such events to attract local supporters and fill rooms, which would benefit both the industry and the domestic market.
The Chairperson spoke about the need to strengthen South Africa’s digital tourism platforms. At present, people rely too heavily on Google. She suggested creating local, user-friendly platforms with algorithms that could recommend related destinations, such as linking the Kruger National Park to nearby attractions. South Africa needed to embrace innovation, artificial intelligence, and digital integration to keep pace globally. To illustrate the urgency, she shared a personal anecdote about her daughter, a computer science student, who had explained how AI was already taught to children in China from primary school level, whereas in South Africa, it was only introduced at university.
The Chairperson acknowledged the concerns raised by members about the Minister’s failure to provide documents. She suggested that the matter might have been left to staff rather than being a deliberate omission, but stressed the need to encourage the Minister to do better. She undertook to follow up with the Minister and the Department to ensure the requested documents such as the SA Tourism board reports and record were shared with the committee.
She noted that the SA Tourism board had taken the Minister to court. As a result, the matter was now sub judice and beyond the committee’s immediate influence. The committee would, however, await legal opinion on the case and share them with members once received.
On the Minister’s attendance, she said that while it was unrealistic to expect the Minister to attend every week, the Deputy Minister should be present in her absence, as was standard practice in other departments. This would ensure that questions from the committee could still be answered.
The Chairperson highlighted the importance of advocacy, particularly around the proposed alignment of Brand SA and SA Tourism. She pointed out that large amounts of money were currently being spent by Brand SA under the Presidency, and that closer collaboration with SA Tourism could lead to greater efficiency and impact. She tasked Dr Khuzwayo and his team with developing proposals on how to pursue this advocacy through engagement with the relevant stakeholders.
In conclusion, she said additional ideas would be put in writing for further consideration.
Ms N. Buthelezi (MKP) said the draft documents were well represented and very informative. She emphasised the point raised by Ms Linde, saying that as someone who lived in Durban, she often observed various events taking place. These included marathons, summits, and other gatherings that clearly had a tourism element. Yet, as a Portfolio Committee member, she was neither invited to such events nor informed about them in advance.
She had sometimes noticed that members of provincial legislatures were present at these events. They would be seen in photos participating in the activities. In future, it would be best if Portfolio Committee members were properly informed about such events, so that they could attend and familiarise themselves with what was happening in those spaces.
The Chairperson said that the point raised was valid, noting that they had in fact received an invitation the previous day. However, the invitation was for an event starting immediately, which left no time to prepare or attend. If those sending invitations truly wanted committee members to attend, they needed to give adequate notice. Late invitations made participation very difficult. When events took place in a member’s province, that member should represent the committee.
On overseas trips, she emphasised the need for fairness. Not all members could go at once, and travel would therefore be rotated across different budget cycles. This ensured that everyone eventually had an opportunity, but also that expectations were managed realistically. For domestic oversight, however, all members would participate, except where budgets became constrained.
The Chairperson highlighted that there were many tourist attractions and even “hidden gems” in the Western Cape, including projects in the Cape Flats, which members should explore as part of oversight. She reassured them that nothing untoward would happen if they visited those areas, and encouraged members to look at ways of uplifting such local tourism sites.
All Members could share their inputs to ensure that all perspectives were compiled and consolidated, after which another presentation could be convened to take the strategic plan process forward.
Dr Khuzwayo replied about how job numbers were calculated in the tourism sector. There was no universally applicable methodology but there were internationally recognised standards that were generally followed. The type of destination determined the approach. For example, Cape Town was a high-value destination. According to international benchmarking, for every ten new tourists visiting Cape Town, one permanent and sustainable job could be created. If 100 000 tourists visited the city in a year, this would translate to roughly 10 000 permanent jobs. However, tourism was a fragile industry; if visitor numbers declined, jobs could be lost, especially among seasonal workers.
Dr Khuzwayo stressed that these metrics were important not only for assessing job creation but also for understanding broader impacts such as spending and contributions to GDP. He added that public hearings and stakeholder engagements were crucial, noting that the National Tourism Sector Strategy (NTSS) provided a constitutional framework for this. The tourism system operated at three levels: national, provincial, and local, and oversight visits should draw in the appropriate stakeholders across these levels.
He suggested that when committees held engagements, they should not only invite umbrella bodies such as FGASA and TPCA but also encourage them to bring their members. That way, the committee could engage directly with hoteliers, operators, tour guides, and others in the value chain, ensuring that the conversation was not limited to representative organisations alone.
Also during oversight visits, the administration would liaise with provincial legislatures and structures to ensure alignment as tourism was also listed under provincial competences in the Constitution. He cautioned against the recurring argument that local tourism was an “unfunded mandate.” In his view, this was not accurate because funding existed under Local Economic Development (LED). The challenge was that LED was often narrowly understood, and tourism was not recognised as an equally important component of it. Dr Khuzwayo argued that this misconception created difficulties in troubleshooting tourism-related issues. NTSS structures provided the appropriate framework to engage stakeholders and strengthen accountability. There was no such thing as an unfunded mandate in this context. The NTSS already listed the relevant stakeholders, and those actors needed to be incorporated into the committee’s oversight activities.
The Chairperson encouraged Members to make their submissions via Mr Mocumi, noting that the team was available to provide any assistance required.
Mr Moodley (MKP) interrupted the Chairperson to emphasise that when Members write directly to the Minister, it bypasses the committee and undermines its role. He stated that all matters requiring challenge or debate with the Minister should be addressed in plenary sessions. By writing directly to the Minister, Members limit the committee’s capacity to act effectively. He stressed the importance of maintaining transparency and following proper channels to allow the committee to advocate on their behalf.
The Chairperson thanked Mr Moodley and addressed the situation involving Ms J Basson (PA), noting that she is new to both the committee and Parliament. Trial and error is part of learning in a new role and she praised Ms Basson for withdrawing a submission when advised, showing responsiveness.
The Chairperson highlighted the importance of following proper channels when requesting documentation from the Minister, acknowledging that sometimes ministry staff may not provide the documents, and encouraged continued engagement to ensure transparency. She added that matters relating to the SAT Board are now sub judice and outside the committee’s control.
Overall, the Chairperson praised the committee for effectively holding the Executive accountable, despite not having received all SAT documentation. She stressed that nothing would bypass the committee in the future and acknowledged the Minister for attending the meeting and “facing the music,” underscoring the importance of accountability.
Meeting adjourned.
