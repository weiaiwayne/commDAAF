The Portfolio Committee on Public Service and Administration was briefed by the National School of Governance, the Centre for Public Service Innovation and the Department of Public Service and Administration on their 2023/24 Annual Reports.
The National School of Government’s key achievements included surpassing training targets by reaching 84,443 learners (182% of the target) and generating a R29.8 million surplus. The School was aiming to reduce reliance on government funding and enhance access to training via zero-rated e-learning platforms. It would prioritise professionalising public service and fostering international partnerships.
Members asked questions about the Professionalisation Framework, funding challenges, student recruitment criteria, the inclusion of local government in NSG programs, technological readiness, suspended officials, non-completion of courses, and uptake of compulsory courses. The Chairperson praised NSG's audit results and training performance while seeking updates on financial sustainability and data access barriers in training.
The Centre for Public Service Innovation emphasised its innovation mandate in public service and achievements like the Digital Fingerprint Project and the “Let’s Talk” mobile application. Despite challenges, the Centre maintained its sixth consecutive clean audit and aimed to expand its digital transformation role.
Members asked questions about state protection of its intellectual property, the lack of a permanently appointed Executive Director, vacancies, the need to employ social workers to address bullying problems in schools, whether technological advancements would lead to job losses, the need to modernise administrative systems across government, the problem of disciplined officials reappearing in different areas of government,
The Department of Public Service and Administration emphasised the strategic priorities of professionalisation, service delivery enhancement, and public service reforms.
Members called for comprehensive reporting to improve decision-making and monitoring, and raised concerns about B-BBEE compliance monitoring, emphasised the need for professional standards in all spheres of government, the funding of senior management posts, youth programmes, the negative effect of skills gaps in local government, the misconduct case backlog, employment equity, the outsourcing of security services, and the cost of lengthy paid suspensions.

Overview by the Minister on the NSG
Minister Buthelezi expressed gratitude for presenting the NSG’s role in training public servants. It was essential for improving governance and service delivery. He urged public servants to fully utilise the programmes offered.
Performance highlights included:
- Over 84 443 learners trained and R143.2m in revenue, surpassing both targets.
- Focus on relevant, public sector-aligned training, including compulsory programmes like Ethics in the Public Service to foster accountability.
- Advocacy on the Professionalisation Framework, engagement with professional bodies, and development of the Nyukela pre-entry programme for middle management roles.
- A clean audit, reflecting strong financial management.
Deputy Minister Kekana echoed the Minister's sentiments, indicating her intention to provide further insights and engage with the Committee after the presentation.
NSG Presentation on its 2023/24 Annual Report
Professor Busani Ngcaweni, Principal, NSG, presented the institution’s achievements. It had surpassed training targets by reaching 84,443 learners against a target of 46,480. He highlighted NSG’s cost-recovery model, according to which the School generated revenue primarily from training fees. He reported a surplus of R29.8m, demonstrating strong financial management. However, non-attendance at compulsory programmes remained a challenge, with departments paying for incomplete courses. To improve access, NSG was committed to launching zero-rated e-learning platforms.
Key achievements and initiatives:
- Gender equality: More female learners than males enrolled.
- NSG utilised analytics to monitor regional learner distribution and address underserved areas.
- Professionalisation of public service remained central, with compulsory training programs like the Nyukela pre-entry programme for senior managers and ethics training gaining importance.
- NSG had expanded its training offerings to the private sector and emphasised youth participation in its programmes.
- Despite difficulties, NSG maintained a clean audit, demonstrating effective governance.
Challenges:
- The Public Service Bill, awaiting approval, was noted as crucial for supporting NSG’s professionalisation efforts.
- High vacancy rates in public sector human resources (HR) departments and challenges with financial resources were noted as ongoing issues.
Regarding global partnerships and future plans, NSG has strengthened its international collaborations, engaging in knowledge-sharing with Zambia, Kenya, and international experts. These partnerships elevated the quality of training and contributed to public service improvement.
Mr Dino Poonsamy, Chief Director: Strategy and Systems, NSG, highlighted plans for future impact evaluations to measure how training improved public service delivery. He stressed the need to expand eLearning through zero-rating platforms to eliminate data costs, and discussed efforts to diversify revenue streams, which would reduce the School’s reliance on government funding.

Responses
Professor Ngcaweni described the NSG’s funding model as a legacy system. Departments had historically paid upfront for courses, resulting in financial backlogs during the COVID-19 pandemic. The NSG accumulated over R100m in its trade account, which was not recognised as revenue until training completion. Despite monthly losses of R10m to R12m during the pandemic, the NSG had avoided requesting a bailout by transitioning to online learning and encouraging departments to use available funds for virtual courses. He noted that some departments now imposed stricter measures, such as withholding pay progression for officials who miss compulsory courses, helping to reduce financial losses from unused training funds. Although the Auditor-General recommended charging interest on unutilised funds, the Accountant-General advised against it to avoid irregular expenditure. He highlighted the challenge of chasing payments from departments, which the NSG was addressing through payment arrangements. To improve accessibility, fees for essential courses like ethics and performance management had been removed, while fees for anti-discrimination and gender equality courses had been lowered. However, premium pricing remained for programmes such as executive induction to subsidise other courses.
Professor Ngcaweni explained that the NSG operated similarly to a business, issuing around 21,000 invoices annually with a consistent staff size. It ranked highly in timely payments to service providers and was focused on maximising its assets. The Chief Financial Officer (CFO) actively pursued payments, ensuring over R100m was received. The NSG was developing a proposal to change its funding model, aiming for state support for specific compulsory courses. He noted that the Skills Development Act mandated a 1% payroll contribution for training, which could enhance the NSG's sustainability, especially for members of Parliament. He emphasised the importance of reserving a percentage of the budget for training costs. He expressed a desire to increase the NSG's share of the estimated R4bn training budget, which was currently distributed among various private providers and universities. The aim was not to redesign the funding system but to enhance the NSG's portion of the existing budget.
Professor Ngcaweni highlighted the upcoming implementation of the Public Procurement Act, which was aimed at eliminating obstacles for departments purchasing training services. He noted that the current procurement process was cumbersome and required excessive paperwork. Under the new Act, the NSG would compete like other training providers in the tender process, streamlining procedures for better engagement while maintaining competitiveness. This change would help address the challenge of selecting reputable training options amidst competing institutions that may appear credible but are untrustworthy.
Professor Ngcaweni promised a detailed written report breaking down participation by government sphere, province, and department. He emphasised that most local government officials participated in NSG courses voluntarily. He said that the NSG was actively working to fill vacancies and had received approval from the Department of Public Service and Administration (DPSA) to advertise openings. It was aiming to fill over half of its current vacancies by the end of the year.
Professor Ngcaweni said that some individuals chose not to disclose their gender. This was a personal decision that should be respected, so making gender disclosure compulsory could be problematic. He noted that gender non-disclosures were low, with individuals still indicating other equity targets.
Professor Ngcaweni stated NSG would provide a detailed breakdown of participants in the Nyukela programme, including those from the private sector and current government roles. He emphasised that Nyukela attracted individuals seeking career advancement, indicating confidence among participants. He clarified that Nyukela was not a "keep busy" program. It had a cost of R400 and a completion time of approximately three months. It could be seen as an indicator of ambition. A report would be provided to assess Nyukela's importance from both government and private sector perspectives.
Professor Ngcaweni confirmed that the NSG had submitted the application for zero-rating to the Minister of Communications and Digital Technologies. Zero-rating of its online courses would significantly reduce costs if approved. He expressed hope for a swift response to improve course accessibility and address hardware limitations for participants. NSG was also exploring partnerships with professional organisations to offer Continuing Professional Development (CPD) points for their courses, enhancing the value of the training.
Regarding future skills, Professor Ngcaweni discussed the introduction of scenario planning while acknowledging the Department's internal capacity gaps. To address this, NSG opted to collaborate with Stellenbosch University, which had the required expertise, to launch a three-month course titled "Introduction to Scenario Planning" next month. The course was aimed at integrating future thinking into the public sector. The NSG has also developed a Design Thinking course in partnership with the Centre for Public Service Innovation (CPSI), the Department of Science and Innovation, the Innovation Hub, and others, focusing on equipping public sector employees with future-ready skills. Additionally, NSG was working with the Durban University of Technology (DUT) on a cybersecurity course and collaborating with various institutions to introduce a range of future-oriented skills. Two critical areas of focus for the NSG in preparing public servants for a digital future include the course on Leading Digital Transformation in the Public Sector, which is currently undergoing quality assurance. This course, shaped by leaders in digital transformation like Professor Tshilidzi Marwala, aimed to equip public servants with the necessary skills for digital initiatives.
Additionally, through a partnership with the French School of Government, the NSG was developing a course on leading digital transformation. It was also finalising arrangements with sister departments to implement a digital literacy course led by Minister Malatsi. These initiatives reflected the NSG's proactive approach to ensuring public servants were equipped with both current technological skills and the ability to lead future digital initiatives.
Professor Ngcaweni outlined the NSG's efforts to ensure its programs meet necessary accreditation standards. Many courses were accredited by various bodies, with a clear strategy in place: for example, project management courses underwent a rigorous assessment process verified by the relevant accreditation councils. He acknowledged that compiling portfolios of evidence for accreditation could be labour-intensive, but emphasised the team's commitment to this process. However, if accreditation became compulsory, the NSG would be unable to waive fees, due to the substantial costs associated with assessment and moderation, which involved engaging independent assessors. Accredited courses would incur fees, while non-accredited courses might be offered at no cost.
Professor Ngcaweni highlighted the NSG's collaboration with professional organisations to provide Continuing Professional Development (CPD) points for participants in week-long courses, regardless of accreditation status. This initiative was significant for professionals seeking to demonstrate their commitment to ongoing learning, and he noted a growing trend of elected officials choosing the NSG for skills acquisition. The NSG was also working to offer more full qualifications. Currently, it offers one full qualification and it is developing three more, according to the requirements of the Department of Higher Education Transformation (DHET). One key requirement was appointing someone to oversee academic affairs and distinguishing between full qualifications and short- to medium-term programmes.
Professor Ngcaweni noted that Minister Buthelezi had recently attended a significant event at which a Memorandum of Understanding (MOU) was signed by nine universities. The MOU aimed to implement intermediate courses, including the Executive Development Programme (EDP), which would be accessible to all, including Members of Parliament, to further their education and pursue full qualifications. In closing, he stated that the NSG courses were dedicated to a practice-oriented approach to learning, prioritising training that would enable participants to apply theoretical knowledge effectively in their roles.
Mr Kubele Hlalethwa, CFO, NSG, provided an overview of the organisation’s supply chain management (SCM). He explained that the NSG adhered to National Treasury guidelines for appointing service providers through a competitive bidding process. He explained that training costs accounted for 50-60% of the NSG's total budget, with any overspending being due to these expenses. He outlined the NSG's financial model, drawing attention to the pre-payment structure for training courses, which required institutions to pay in advance. He mentioned that the current debtors’ book ranged from R30m to R40m, with collection periods of 25 to 30 days, which aligned with standard departmental payment practices.
Minister Buthelezi said that some vacancies resulted from retirements and resignations but acknowledged that there was a broader issue affecting performance and service delivery. He suggested exploring global mechanisms for reporting vacancies and clarified that vacancies were not solely due to departmental decisions; they often stemmed from Treasury interventions and budget constraints, which limited the filling of positions to critical roles. He questioned the criteria for defining “critical” positions, using the medical field as an example, where while a doctor's role was critical, other positions were also vital to the organisation’s effectiveness. The Minister highlighted the need for a clear mechanism to report vacancies to prevent negative perceptions of the Department.
Deputy Minister Kekana expressed gratitude to the Minister and the Portfolio Committee, affirming the importance of professionalising the public service. She referenced a recent discussion with the Auditor-General and noted that this issue has been addressed with the President and Cabinet. She stressed that all Annual Performance Plans (APPs) should incorporate a framework for professionalisation across departments and that this inclusion was vital for all Directors-General (DGs). The NSG was attentive to these recommendations and the Public Service Commission was considering them. According to the Auditor-General, the next four years would focus on implementing the professionalisation framework, which was a key priority for the ministry. She discussed plans to fast-track modernisation through e-government as a vital enabler for improved service delivery and public service professionalisation. She mentioned an upcoming meeting between the Minister and the Minister of Communications and Digital Technologies to address digital challenges across government institutions.
Acknowledging Mr Sithole's comments about 5IR, Deputy Minister Kekana highlighted government's progress in this area, particularly through the Presidential Commission on the Fourth Industrial Revolution. Efforts were underway to integrate technology institutions within the government to prepare for advancements like 5G. She noted positive collaboration with the Department of Communications and Digital Technologies (DCDT), the Independent Communications Authority of South Africa (Icasa) and institutions like Telkom, particularly in zero-rating government programmes. She reassured members that the Department was working with others to support modernisation and professionalisation initiatives. She agreed on the need for improvements in administering the GEPF, suggesting early engagement with retiring public servants to streamline processes and reduce long queues. She concluded by assuring the Committee that the Minister would follow up on these matters and provide feedback later.
The Chairperson thanked Professor Ngcaweni, the Minister, and the Deputy Minister for their feedback and comments. He agreed with the Minister on the issue of reporting vacancies, noting that if vacancies were unfunded, it seemed unfair for them to be reported as such. He noted that two other members had also raised concerns with him about the GEPF. An oversight visit conducted the previous week had revealed a persistent misunderstanding in the public sector about the two-pot retirement system, according to which it robbed individuals of their money. The issue is one of education, as the system was actually designed to help South Africans secure a proper retirement savings plan. He suggested that there could be an opportunity for the NSG to collaborate with the GEPF in developing training and educational courses for public servants. A simple, half-day online course could explain the savings and retirement benefits and how the government worked with the legislative framework to ensure that it is to the advantage of the individual.
In conclusion, the Chairperson addressed the financial implications of lending money without charging interest. He suggested that this practice inadvertently positioned the NSG as a financial service provider, which was not its intended role. He asserted that the institution should not be helping others manage their cash flow, and stressed the importance of maintaining financial discipline. He explained that charging interest on outstanding debts was necessary and aligned with government legislation that mandated payment within 30 days.

Ms Xaba-Ntshaba raised a question about the discussion of the recruitment of youth from various schools and provinces for 4IR training. She noted that while the report mentioned some provinces, others were not referenced, and she requested information on the missing provinces and their inclusion in the youth recruitment efforts. She stressed the need for detailed information about the specific schools being targeted for youth recruitment so that the Committee could monitor progress during oversight visits. She asked about the key changes proposed in the remuneration policy. How would these changes help the state attract and retain the required skills? In addition, she recommended that audits within the Department be made mandatory to ensure continuous skills development and recruitment, thereby enhancing the state's capability to implement its programs effectively. She shared other Members’ concerns about vacancies within the CPSI, noting that while three had been filled, two positions remained vacant. She also asked what outcomes the CPSI could highlight as a result of implementing the Public Service Professionalisation Framework.
The Chairperson began by congratulating the CPSI on achieving an eighth consecutive clean audit and it 96% expenditure. He drew special attention to the department's swift service provider payment culture. This was an example for all government departments to follow. He asked for an update and a timeline for filling the Executive Director position. He asked whether there was any collaboration between the CPSI and the Department of Home Affairs (DHA) on the DHA digitisation project. The Committee welcomed the project, which would reduce queues and provide better access to government services through computers and smartphones and it should serve as a model for other departments. He noted that the Minister had publicly addressed the issue of state employees undergoing disciplinary processes, yet remaining at home on full pay, and often moving on to other government departments without any proper HR cross-checks between entities. The Chairperson asked if there was collaboration with the DPSA on the potential rollout of a single government-wide HR system. Had there been any research into international best practices for implementing such a system? Lastly, the Chairperson inquired whether it might be possible for the CPSI to take equity or invest in some of the innovations it encountered, similar to how universities could create separate investment funds to financially support innovative ideas while holding equity stakes. Such an approach could enable the CPSI to work toward a model similar to the NSG, where it could fund its operations through investment returns. He acknowledged that this might fall outside the CPSI's current mandate and left the suggestion as a comment for future consideration.
Responses
Minister Buthelezi noted the shock on Mr Sithole's face when he heard that CPSI paid service providers within four days. This reaction highlighted a significant issue within the government; what should be normal had become so abnormal that it was no longer recognised as correct. He said that the issue of unfilled posts remained a top priority for him. He acknowledged that it was a recurring concern that required immediate attention and he committed to filling these positions within the next six months. He addressed the ongoing discussions concerning collaboration among departments and entities including SITA, the CPSI, the Innovation Hub, and the Department of Science and Technology (DST), all of which related to “e-government.” He planned to convene a meeting with the Minister of Communications and Digital Technologies. The gathering aimed to thoroughly discuss all concerns and recommendations, adopting a holistic approach to effectively integrate all aspects of technology. He acknowledged that while the state had the capacity to address these challenges, there was a critical need to streamline solutions across various departments. He remained optimistic that, through cooperation and a focused approach, the barriers hindering progress could be overcome. He assured the Committee that he would provide feedback and progress updates moving forward.
Deputy Minister Kekana addressed the issue of bullying in schools and the actions necessary to combat it. She emphasised the importance of collaboration across various departments, including the Department of Basic Education, the Department of Social Development, and the DCDT. The DCDT, for instance, had an entity known as the Film and Publications Board, which was responsible for educating learners about appropriate behaviour in the digital space. It taught students about the dangers of sharing personal information and photos with strangers online via their phones. Distributing someone’s image without consent could lead to severe legal consequences, including imprisonment, in terms of the Protection of Personal Information Act (POPIA). This education aimed to inform learners that harmful digital actions could be traced back to them, emphasising personal accountability. She highlighted the importance of these collaborations in raising awareness about digital footprints from an early age. Organisations like Facebook and Microsoft have joined the initiative to teach learners how to navigate the technology space responsibly. She encouraged all stakeholders to actively participate in promoting responsible digital behaviour among learners. She urged members in other portfolios to monitor and evaluate how aggressively the DCDT and the Department of Basic Education were addressing these issues.
Ms Sebokedi replied that concerns about the private sector taking advantage of public sector innovations had prompted CPSI to conduct a research project focused on IP management within the public sector. The research revealed that the National IP Management Office (NIPO) predominantly focused on commercialising products in the private sector. The CPSI has shared the report with NIPO and would engage with them to ensure their mandate extended to protecting intellectual property produced by public servants. The goal was to establish systems that prevented the misappropriation of public sector innovations and protected against potential exploitation by the private sector. She acknowledged the suggestion to link their efforts with the security cluster. This was an avenue worth exploring to enhance protective measures further.
Regarding concerns about 4IR and whether it is contributing to job losses, Ms Sebokedi replied that the challenge lay in reskilling public servants to adapt to technological advancements. Chief Information Officers (CIOs) should be transformed into Chief Innovation Officers to expand their role in fostering innovation across departments. She highlighted past experiences, such as the disappearance of typist pools in government departments, to illustrate the need to repurpose skills for the future. CPSI was engaging with the NSG to introduce new digital and IT-related courses aimed at upskilling public servants. She stressed the urgency of preparing for AI, mentioning how countries like the United Kingdom are regulating the use of their data for generative AI. South Africa was also taking steps to protect its data from being exploited for AI development.
Ms Sebokedi shared details about CPSI’s Youth Digital Skills Development Program, noting that it began in rural areas, including St. John's College Mthatha, and continued to spread to other areas. Hackathons were held in both urban and rural regions, with a focus on creating excitement around technologies like drones, which were opening new career paths for students. She agreed that digital initiatives needed to be consolidated under one plan and added that CPSI was working closely with various stakeholders, including teachers, to improve the implementation of interactive systems.
Ms Annette Snyman, CFO, CPSI, explained that CPSI was able to pay suppliers timeously through a reminder system that notified employees of pending tasks. It ensured that any received invoices were returned to the finance team within one day. The implementation of an internal electronic spending system facilitated quicker processing, as invoices could be electronically signed and returned to the finance team more efficiently. She highlighted the importance of adopting standard operating procedures (SOPs) to ensure all employees were well-informed and aligned with the payment timelines. Establishing these SOPs was crucial for maintaining consistency and accountability within an organisation. She reiterated the integral nature of these processes in enhancing the overall efficiency of financial operations.
Ms Sebokedi said written feedback would be provided for any questions that remained unanswered or required further elaboration.
DPSA Presentation on its 2023/24 Annual Report
Ms Yoliswa Makhasi, Director-General (DG), DPSA, proudly announced the Department's clean audit for the 2023/24 financial year, which marked an improvement from the previous year. She credited fiscal discipline and strong governance for this achievement.
The Department has made significant progress on several initiatives:
- Skills Audit: in collaboration with provinces, a framework was implemented to assess public servants’ skills.
- Government Employee Housing Scheme: 812,000 of 1.18m eligible employees were making use of housing allowances.
- Human Resources: the vacancy rate stood at 7.53% and efforts were underway to remove unfunded positions and manage vacancies efficiently.
Ms Makhasi emphasised SITA's role in planning, as process modernisation faced resource constraints. Ethics management had improved through the introduction of a discipline management strategy, reducing long-term suspensions by 53%. Key achievements included surpassing targets for female senior management (51%) and persons with disabilities (2.23%). However, due to budget cuts, the goal of reducing fruitless expenditure by 70% and meeting the 30% youth employment target was not met. She also highlighted international collaborations and initiatives to enhance transparency, including an Integrated Service Delivery Improvement Policy.
Mr Masilo Makhura, CFO, DPSA, reported that the Department had spent 93.7% of its R507m budget, and savings had been realised through cost containment measures. There had been no unauthorised or irregular expenditure, but three cases of fruitless expenditure were under investigation.
Ms Linda Dludla, Deputy Director-General (DDG): Administration, DPSA, discussed the Department’s progress on recommendations, including efforts to enforce public service regulations, conduct lifestyle audits, and address vacancy rates and wage bill concerns. She said that the challenges involved in implementing the Integrated Financial Management System (IFMS), as per the Committee’s recommendation, were well-known. DPSA had approached National Treasury for a deviation to develop an integrated human resources system that would enable people to apply for jobs online. The Minister of Finance had now granted the request and development of the system would start soon.
The Minister noted that the DPSA was central to all government activities. Without a robust public service, he cautioned, any initiatives aimed at recovery would likely falter. He acknowledged the challenges the Department had faced over the past year, particularly regarding resource allocation. He specifically pointed out the inability to integrate young people into the system and noted the lack of adequate representation of women within the Department. He stressed the importance of adequate resources, stating that the Department's efforts would be meaningless without them. He likened the Department's responsibility to the essential functions of the human body, asserting that just as a body cannot function without its vital organs, government cannot thrive without a well-resourced public service. He further highlighted the importance of addressing corruption and promoting integrity through lifestyle audits. He pointed out that limited resources hindered efforts to combat these issues.

Responses from DPSA
Minister Buthelezi acknowledged that there had been repeated inquiries about the employment of women and people with disabilities. There was a need for clearer reporting formats that would not paint the Department in an unduly negative light. Regarding the central register for public service offenders, he confirmed that it was up and running. He noted the need for better engagement with local government and emphasised the importance of accountability for wrongful suspensions, urging political leaders to take responsibility for appointments and actions. With regard to the backlog of precautionary suspensions, he noted that that case complexity often led to delays, especially when law enforcement was involved.
Regarding employment equity, the Department acknowledged that while current compliance levels may appear satisfactory, individual circumstances could impact compliance reporting. There was a need for a clearer reporting format to distinguish between genuine compliance issues and those arising from personal decisions, to ensure a genuine representation of compliance ratios. Monitoring strategies must be implemented to prevent misuse, especially in politically sensitive contexts, and fair disciplinary actions are essential, particularly concerning unlawful instructions.
The Department addressed the disciplinary case backlog, highlighting that the complexity of certain cases, particularly those involving fraud and corruption, could lead to delays. Collaboration with external law enforcement agencies was often necessary, and adequate resources and training were required for staff managing these cases. Precautionary transfers were noted as effective in maintaining workplace engagement while issues were being resolved.
The Department addressed the issue of local government accountability, emphasising that it lay not only with the DPSA but also with the political parties responsible for appointments. Systemic issues would persist until these parties took responsibility. The Department noted progress on the disciplinary code. While the draft had not been submitted by the end of the previous financial year, work was ongoing and would continue to be prioritised.
The Department reiterated its adherence to the national definition of “youth” and detailed steps taken to monitor and support the rollout of the professionalisation framework. Integrative to this process, mandatory training was reported separately, with directives issued for implementation and budget allocations made. However, challenges with data delays from the National School of Government affected comprehensive analysis and reporting.
The Department highlighted that SAPS had the largest backlog of cases, with efforts underway to address the issues through ongoing monitoring and interventions, leading to resolutions for half of the cases in the system.
Regarding communication with the Minister, the Department confirmed a standing arrangement for weekly briefings on various matters. While some initiatives were not launched in the last financial year, a new system was successfully implemented in May of the current financial year, with training provided to departments for effective job evaluation management.
The Department explained that it had no authority to discipline staff in other departments, as each department has its own DG responsible for such matters. Issues were escalated to the relevant DG and, if necessary, Minister.
The Department acknowledged recent budget cuts from National Treasury, which resulted in a uniform 10% reduction across departments. Internal reviews and adjustments were necessary to manage the implications of these cuts effectively. The importance of aligning vacancy reviews with organisational restructuring was underscored, with a commitment to supporting affected employees in transitioning to new roles.
The Department reiterated its support for precautionary transfers during investigations. Such transfers were aimed at safeguarding departmental integrity. It recognised the need for streamlined processes to address delays in suspension cases and ensure timely resolutions, and committed to enhancing reporting mechanisms.
Adoption of minutes and meeting conclusion
Minutes of meetings on 28 August, 4 September, 11 September and 18 September 2024 were adopted.
Some Members raised concerns and made suggestions regarding key issues, such as the two-pot retirement system and the need for better education around it. They also highlighted the need for increased oversight in sectors such as police and public works, and made specific suggestions for inviting relevant ministers to brief the Committee. Members also highlighted the importance of addressing unauthorised building usage and conducting proper building audits.
One Member noted the importance of aligning Committee discussions with political expectations from their respective parties and ensuring that individual contributions were reflected in the minutes. They called for a more structured approach to oversight, with suggestions for a proper plan to cover various departments.
The Chairperson agreed with the need for oversight and emphasised the importance of monitoring resolutions to ensure departmental accountability. He also suggested holding a strategic planning session at the beginning of the following year to plan oversight activities, given the already full Committee schedule for the remainder of the current year.
The meeting was adjourned.
