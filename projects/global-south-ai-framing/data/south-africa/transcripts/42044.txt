Meeting Summary
Video
The Portfolio Committee on Higher Education met physically with Sol Plaatje University (SPU) to engage on matters relating to governance, administration, student welfare, and institutional effectiveness. The meeting took place within the broader context of systemic challenges facing the Post-School Education and Training (PSET) sector, including governance deficiencies, gender-based violence (GBV), and financial instability associated with the National Student Financial Aid Scheme (NSFAS).
The Committee sought clarity on SPU’s institutional culture, student accommodation environment, and disciplinary processes. In line with its oversight mandate, the Committee examined SPU’s governance practices, financial management, and preparedness for the 2026 academic year. Members also expressed concern over the Department of Higher Education and Training’s (DHET) repeated failures to respond to correspondence and attend scheduled meetings, describing this as a breakdown in accountability.
SPU reported several achievements, including consecutive unqualified audits, infrastructure investments exceeding R3 billion, and an increase in enrolment to 7 182 students. The University highlighted strategic growth areas, including health sciences, mining, information and communication technology, and expanded research capacity.
Members raised a number of serious concerns. The Student Representative Council (SRC) highlighted unsafe and exploitative accommodation conditions, delayed NSFAS disbursements, and debt-related exclusion practices. Organised labour drew attention to inadequate employee benefits, continued outsourcing despite previous insourcing commitments, and governance gaps such as the absence of a Legal Directorate. The Institutional Forum (IF) reported persistent gender disparities in senior academic positions and weaknesses in the university’s policy review cycles.
Concerns were also raised about SPU’s handling of GBV matters. Members noted cases in which victims appeared to be marginalised while alleged perpetrators were reinstated or promoted. The use of non-disclosure agreements (NDAs) in sexual misconduct cases was viewed as contributing to perceptions of impunity and undermining trust in institutional processes.
Financial and operational issues received further scrutiny. Members questioned the implementation of the NSFAS accommodation pilot, which had resulted in eviction threats and service interruptions due to unpaid landlords. Residence fees, reportedly exceeding R67 000 per annum despite government-funded infrastructure, were described as exclusionary. Legal expenses related to staff suspensions, totalling nearly R2 million, were also examined.
Members expressed dissatisfaction with both departmental absenteeism and what they perceived as limited transparency from SPU. The Committee called for urgent action to protect students from exploitative practices and to rebuild confidence in GBV-related policies and procedures. Strong emphasis was placed on the need for systemic reform of NSFAS processes, including the removal of intermediaries that contribute to payment delays and corruption risks.
The Chairperson underscored the seriousness of misleading Parliament, highlighting the consequences set out in Section 17 of the Powers, Privileges and Immunities Act. SPU was directed to submit detailed reports on GBV cases, NSFAS accommodation payments, legal expenditures, and suspension records by 26 November. GBV was described as a critical concern requiring urgent cultural and institutional change. The Chairperson also called for a review of residence fee structures, warning that exclusionary practices could fuel student unrest.
The Committee reaffirmed its commitment to holding the Department and its entities accountable, emphasising that systemic failures in governance, funding, and institutional responsiveness cannot continue.

Council Briefing on Sol Plaatje University (SPU)
Prof Randall Carolissen, Chairperson, SPU Council, conveyed the Vice-Chancellor’s apology, noting that she was recovering from major surgery. He invited Prof Meyer to support the briefing and highlighted five overarching themes which, in Council’s view, best reflected the current state, performance trajectory and long-term strategic positioning of Sol Plaatje University (SPU).
Governance and institutional credibility
Prof Carolissen reported that SPU had maintained an unbroken record of unqualified audits throughout its 10-year existence. According to the Council on Higher Education (CHE), the institution had established a strong governance foundation with the potential to become a leading university nationally and internationally. During their respective visits in 2025, both the President of the Republic and the Minister of Education commended SPU for its achievements and governance culture.
He added that the Council comprised members with diverse professional backgrounds who demonstrated a strong commitment to institutional governance, evidenced by an 82% attendance rate and active engagement in Council proceedings.
Future-fit academic positioning
Prof Carolissen stated that SPU was intentionally positioning itself to respond to rapidly changing skills demands driven by technological disruption and artificial intelligence. He referenced the World Economic Forum’s projection that approximately 40% of current skills would become obsolete between 2025 and 2030, underscoring the urgency of developing programmes that address graduate unemployment and evolving labour-market needs.
He outlined the University’s strategic academic focus areas:
Education
Data Sciences
Heritage Studies
Arid Desert Studies
Geology and Mining (with a new geology programme launched in July 2025)
A new Faculty of Health Sciences, currently in the commissioning phase
These programmes, he explained, were aligned to regional development priorities and sought to address acute skills shortages in the Northern Cape, particularly in mining and health sciences.
Academic performance and infrastructure development
Prof Carolissen reported that SPU’s student performance indicators—including throughput and retention rates—exceeded national sector averages. He emphasised that 72% of SPU’s academic staff held doctoral qualifications, significantly above the national average of approximately 50%. This academic strength, he argued, contributed to the quality of teaching and research development, while supporting the broader economic trajectory of the Northern Cape.
Turning to infrastructure, he noted that more than R3 billion had been invested over the past decade to develop the University’s physical footprint in Kimberley. SPU had constructed 29 new buildings, refurbished 10 more, and now managed a 40-building property portfolio. All major developments were delivered on time and within budget. He further highlighted the recent donation of the historic De Beers building, which would house significant administrative functions. SPU’s infrastructural growth had catalysed new economic activity in Kimberley, particularly in housing, transport and retail sectors.
Management report
SPU management reported sustained institutional growth, with enrolment increasing from 124 students in 2014 to 7 182 in 2025. This growth was driven largely by the expansion of the Programme Qualification Mix, particularly in Science, Technology, Engineering and Mathematics (STEM) and Education. The University confirmed the establishment of a fifth faculty, Health Sciences, with Nursing already endorsed by the South African Nursing Council (SANC). Across its four current faculties, SPU now offers 42 accredited qualifications, including its first Doctor of Philosophy (PhD) in Science scheduled for implementation in 2026, alongside 44 short learning programmes aimed at flexible and accessible learning pathways.
The University highlighted strong academic capability, noting that 72% of academic staff hold doctoral qualifications, well above the national average. Academic performance remains consistently high, with undergraduate pass rates of 87–91%, top national throughput rates across years one to three, and a 95% student retention rate. SPU has adopted a digitally enabled model of delivery, issuing laptops to all first-year students and integrating Artificial Intelligence (AI), Virtual Reality (VR) and blended learning into teaching. As the only university in the Northern Cape, SPU aims to grow its provincial footprint, with a centre operating in Upington and another planned for Postmasburg.
Research output has expanded rapidly since 2019, growing from 49 DHET-accredited publications in 2022 to more than 100 by September 2025. The University identified core areas of research strength aligned to regional needs: geology, indigenous language studies, water security (supported by a R25 million Water Research Commission (WRC) grant, and renewable energy, with a new research centre expected to launch before year-end. SPU has also established a Technology Innovation and Transfer Office and submitted six intellectual property disclosures between 2024 and 2025, signalling early progress toward commercialisation.
Financially, the University reported stable growth between 2016 and 2024, achieving a surplus in 2024 despite ring-fenced capital and donation funds. However, SPU raised concerns about significant financial strain in 2025 stemming from challenges with the National Student Financial Aid Scheme (NSFAS), including blocked registration data, delayed disbursements and the University’s need to cover two months of student accommodation costs to prevent evictions.
With approximately 80% of undergraduate students dependent on NSFAS, the institution remains highly vulnerable to funding delays and continues to engage the Scheme to secure more predictable payment cycles.

Briefing by Institutional Forum (IF) on Governance and Equity
The Institutional Forum (IF) outlined its statutory mandate as an advisory body to the Council on equity, policy implementation, senior appointments, institutional culture and dispute-resolution mechanisms. The IF is broadly representative, comprising management, Council, Senate, academic and support staff, unions, female and differently-abled staff, and Student Representative Council (SRC) representatives, although some vacancies remain. Regular reporting to the IF includes staff complement data, human resources (HR) matters, employment equity progress, performance management and policy development, alongside updates from the SRC and Student Affairs.
Key challenges highlighted include persistent concerns regarding gender representation at senior academic levels, particularly the low number of female Associate Professors and Professors. The Forum also flagged staff wellness, equity transformation and employment-relations tensions as recurring issues requiring close monitoring. Another concern is ensuring effective policy review cycles and improving communication flows between governance structures.
The IF recommended strengthening diversity-oriented recruitment strategies, accelerating policy reviews and ensuring a stable institutional culture through transparent governance. The Chairperson of the IF plays a critical role in providing the Council with advisory input, participating in executive appointment processes and ensuring alignment between governance structures. While financial issues were not the primary focus of the presentation, implications arise in areas such as staff development, transformation initiatives and HR planning, which depend on consistent institutional funding and oversight.
Briefing by Student Representative Council (SRC) on Student Experience and Accommodation
The SRC presented a comprehensive assessment of student life at SPU, balancing acknowledgement of institutional progress with deep concern over systemic shortcomings. Students recognised improvements in academic support, blended learning and expanding infrastructure. However, they noted persistent inequalities in access to study spaces, inadequate Wi-Fi and insufficient academic support structures such as peer advisors and tutoring capacity. The SRC stressed that the student experience is shaped by both institutional growth and unresolved structural problems.
The most critical challenges relate to accommodation, both on-campus and off-campus. Students pay for 10 months of residence yet are required to vacate during the June recess. Moroka Residence continues to experience cold-water problems, and closed-circuit television (CCTV) coverage remains insufficient for safety. Off-campus, students face unsafe living conditions, poor maintenance after accreditation, landlords charging above NSFAS caps and a R500 non-refundable administration fee that financially burdens poor students. Compounding this is the failure of the NSFAS accommodation pilot, which has resulted in non-payment of landlords, eviction threats during exams and overcrowded NSFAS-accredited housing.
The SRC recommended eliminating exploitative fees, enforcing quality monitoring of off-campus residences, expanding student health services to a 24/7 university clinic, increasing counsellor capacity, ensuring 24/7 security visibility and urgently addressing water-quality concerns that previously led to hospitalisations. On finances, the SRC argued that SPU’s registration guidelines—such as requiring students to reduce debt to R25 000 before registering—are unrealistic and exclusionary. Overall, the SRC called for a more humane, student-centred approach to governance, grounded in dignity, accountability and transformation.
Briefing by Organised Labour Union on Employee Benefits and Governance Concerns
The Health and Other Service Personnel Trade Union of South Africa (HOSPERSA) briefed the Committee on long-standing employee concerns, noting that although the union only gained formal recognition at SPU in 2024, it maintains institutional memory of historical challenges. A central grievance is that SPU employees do not enjoy the same benefits as counterparts at comparable universities. The union reported the absence of clear, structured benefits—such as housing allowances and pension contributions—due to inconsistent benchmarking practices. This has resulted in employee packages that lag significantly behind sector norms.
The Union highlighted several instances of alleged financial mismanagement. Despite insourcing cleaning and security personnel in 2018, the University continued to contract external service providers for the same functions, even deploying SPU security officers to patrol city streets while private contractors carried out campus duties. Additional concerns included outsourcing the cleaning of student rooms and purchasing a stadium that students cannot access, calling into question decisions that divert resources away from employee welfare.
HOSPERSA further emphasised structural governance weaknesses, including the absence of a Legal Directorate despite money spent on job-grading consultancy. The gap has led to inconsistent handling of disciplinary matters and growing legal costs. The Union also criticised the practice of senior managers chairing tender committees, cautioning that it creates an environment ripe for conflicts of interest. The Union recommended an independent Council member chair supply-chain structures and called for mandatory audits at the end of each Vice-Chancellor’s term to detect potential abuse of public resources.

Discussion
Dr Christians directed her questions to the Vice-Chancellor, Council and the Registrar. She reminded the Committee that, although SPU was a relatively new institution, it had repeatedly been associated with allegations of governance weaknesses. She referred to claims that consultation processes, disciplinary matters, appointments and student affairs appeared to be centrally controlled. She asked whether these functions had been appropriately delegated, which responsibilities remained with Council, and which had been delegated to the Registrar. She also asked whether student-related functions had been formally assigned to the Registrar and whether such delegation had been recorded in writing.
Prof Debra Meyer, Deputy Vice-Chancellor: Research, Innovation and Postgraduate Affairs and Acting Vice-Chancellor, stated that no centralisation existed at the University and explained that functions were delegated within their respective environments. She noted that challenges in student affairs had arisen because the Dean of Student Affairs had left the institution, which resulted in the delegation of oversight of student affairs to the Registrar.
Prof Meyer confirmed that the delegation had been done in writing.
Dr Christians referred to a disciplinary extension granted by the Registrar on 21 October. She stated that she had seen a letter signed by the Registrar and asked whether the action complied with university policy, specifically clause 6.3.7 of the disciplinary code.
Dr Jody Cedras, University Registrar, SPU, replied that the disciplinary code had been followed in relation to the suspension and stated that the reasons for suspension aligned with the management framework.
Dr Christians raised concerns about recent student suspensions. She noted that although institutional performance indicators appeared positive, she questioned whether this was sustainable given developments in institutional culture, gender-based violence and ethical conduct. She referred to allegations that a former student leader implicated in a 2023 gender-based violence and femicide-related assault case had been employed in the Registrar’s Office without a formal recruitment process. She stated that the post had not been advertised and that the alleged victim had been excluded from the institution.
She asked whether the former SRC president had been employed outside proper procedures, how the victim’s case had been handled, her current circumstances, why she had not completed her studies, what support had been provided, and on what basis she had been excluded or suspended. She further asked whether Council had been informed, whether an independent review of gender-based violence and misconduct cases had taken place, and whether the victim would be allowed to return to the institution.
Dr Christians also asked about the implementation of the University’s Gender-Based Violence Policy Framework, approved in 2020, the functionality of the gender-based violence committee and gender-based violence desk, and the number of reported cases.
Prof Meyer stated that the University did not exclude students on the basis of circumstances such as those described, as this would be inhumane. She said management would need to verify whether the student had left voluntarily or due to academic exclusion. She noted that students were not excluded for other reasons, although financial exclusion occurred at times and was handled through counselling and engagement.
She confirmed that the Gender-Based Violence Policy Framework could be provided and said the Chief Operations Officer had indicated that the policy was being reviewed to include broader communities, including LGBTQ+ individuals. She added that a gender desk was in place. She stated that the Registrar would respond to the question on the employment of the former SRC president.
The Chairperson asked for clarity on what the University’s gender-based violence and femicide policy required and what actions management had taken.
Prof Meyer replied that she was not aware of the specific case, as she had joined the University in 2023, and could not speak to the details. She stated that investigations took place in line with policy and that disciplinary action followed if wrongdoing was confirmed. She said an independent chairperson presided over such processes.
The Chairperson expressed dissatisfaction and stated that it was illogical for policy to allow an alleged perpetrator to attend the same classes as a victim. He referred to a recent incident at Nelson Mandela University (NMU) in which a student had died by suicide and emphasised that institutions should never place victims and alleged perpetrators in the same environment. He stressed that policy must protect victims.
Prof Meyer clarified that the alleged perpetrator would be suspended pending investigation and said she had omitted this earlier. She confirmed that suspension upon a gender-based violence complaint was provided for in policy.
Dr Christians expressed deep concern and stated that the responses suggested that policy might not have been implemented. She referred to a letter from the victim dated 23 April 2024 at 11:34, in which the student wrote that she did not feel safe and requested that the University remove the alleged perpetrator because he was housed nearby. She stated that the young woman had not been excluded academically but through disciplinary action and asked on what grounds this had occurred.
Dr Cedras stated that an independent chairperson had overseen the disciplinary proceedings and had found that both students were simultaneously perpetrators and victims. He indicated that this context might have been omitted from the information received by Dr Christians.
The Chairperson referred to the procedural requirements in the gender-based violence and femicide policy and asked whether the institution had placed the alleged perpetrator on suspension immediately, as required.
Dr Cedras confirmed that suspension had occurred but said he could not recall whether it had been immediate. He advised that the report could be provided.
The Chairperson rejected the response and stated that if the process had been followed correctly, Dr Cedras would not be unable to recall. He said that the uncertainty indicated that discretion had been applied, contrary to policy.
Dr Cedras explained that both students had lodged formal complaints concurrently and stated that he could provide the relevant documentation.
The Chairperson asked what actions had been taken after the student wrote to the University about her safety.
Dr Cedras stated that this was the first time he had become aware of the letter. He added that the alleged perpetrator had not been an employee but a student assistant receiving a stipend, although the individual had worked within the institution.
Dr Christians stated that she did not believe Dr Cedras was providing truthful responses. She argued that the alleged perpetrator had been employed and receiving a salary and said the institution could not account for the whereabouts or status of the victim, which, in her view, suggested that she had indeed been excluded.
Dr Cedras replied that the young woman had not been excluded and said that counselling support had been offered, though she had chosen not to use it. He stated that nothing prevented her from returning to Sol Plaatje University and said she had relocated to Gauteng. He added that the former SRC member had received a one-year suspension, but he could not recall the disciplinary outcome for the young woman.
The Chairperson pressed for clarity.
Dr Cedras stated that he would revisit the disciplinary sanctions and provide the Committee with details.
The Chairperson stated that the matter was serious and should not have been treated as minor. He noted that Members had access to the full case record and were aware that the alleged perpetrator had been found guilty. He said the University had subsequently employed him, creating an impression that individuals close to management could commit serious misconduct, including gender-based violence, and still be protected and rewarded. He criticised Dr Cedras for failing to conduct due diligence to ensure that staff working in his office would be safe.
Mr David Kanguwe, Chief Operating Officer (COO), stated that an external case had existed between the two students. He said charges had been withdrawn because the alleged victim had stopped attending court proceedings. He added that the alleged perpetrator had been hired a year after completing his disciplinary sanction. He emphasised that the individual had been a student assistant, not a permanent employee.
The Chairperson asked what process had been followed when hiring him.
Mr Kanguwe stated that student assistants were typically recommended and that the process involved an element of discretion.
The Chairperson criticised the explanation and stated that management had failed to apply discretion responsibly. He said the matter reflected patronage, particularly given the individual’s factional associations.
Mr Saul asked whether the alleged perpetrator’s role was located within the Registrar’s Office.
Dr Cedras confirmed that the individual worked within his operational environment, although not directly under him.
Mr Kgabo asked whether the discretion involved in appointing student assistants was subject to any formalised process.
Dr Cedras stated that recommendations were processed through the relevant levels, including human resources, finance and academic administration.
Dr Christians stated that the matter had escalated externally because the victim had not received assistance from the University. She said the student had written repeatedly seeking intervention, had not received support, and had eventually stopped attending court. She added that the student had been suspended for posting about the matter on social media.
Prof Carolissen assured Members that Council treated gender-based violence with utmost seriousness. He indicated that Council would review and strengthen policies, even though the events had occurred before its tenure.
Dr Christians raised concerns regarding the external accreditation of student accommodation by the NSFAS. She said some landlords had not been paid and were threatening to evict students. She asked how external accommodation providers were vetted and why providers accredited by the NSFAS appeared on its lists but not on the University’s. She questioned why the University had not escalated the matter more urgently.
Prof Meyer stated that a committee, which included student representatives, evaluated accommodation options using policy guidelines such as distance, transport availability and Wi-Fi access. She emphasised that external accommodation was required to meet standards comparable to university-managed facilities.
Regarding the NSFAS list, she said the University had no control over the national accreditation process and that responses from the Scheme were often delayed. She added that the University had communicated its position that it should serve as the sole accreditor of student accommodation to ensure consistent standards.
Ms Annalene Marais, Chief Financial Officer (CFO), stated that delays in payments were caused by blocked registration data and prolonged portal closures. She said she had alerted the Chief Executive Officer (CEO) of the NSFAS that Training Young Minds, the Scheme’s agent, was a root cause due to late and inaccurate data submissions. She added that the University had received a list of students residing in NSFAS off-campus accommodation who were not registered at SPU.
Dr Christians asked how many service providers remained unpaid and what steps had been taken to protect students facing eviction.
Ms Marais stated that R8.432 million had been paid a week earlier across the accredited providers. She said she could not provide the total outstanding amount because she did not have access to the contract values between landlords and students.
Returning to the GBVF matter, the Chairperson asked Prof Meyer to confirm whether the young woman had been excluded.
Prof Meyer stated that she was unaware of the details of the case but said the University did not suspend students arbitrarily, as suspensions normally arose from academic grounds and followed due process.
The Chairperson then asked Dr Cedras whether the student had been excluded.
Dr Cedras stated that he could not recall. When asked what he would say if presented with an expulsion letter, he replied that he would accept it as fact but noted the volume of cases processed by his office.
The Chairperson rejected this explanation and said that this matter was not an ordinary case, as it involved an individual whom Dr Cedras had recommended for a student assistant position. He stated that he did not believe the claim of poor recollection and reminded management that Members already knew the answers and required truthful responses.
Mr Ngubane stated that South Africa faced a problem of officials drawing monthly salaries and perks while failing to perform their duties. He argued that many did not care about the institutions they were managing and questioned whether they understood the responsibilities attached to their positions. He remarked that officials were presiding over institutions without a clear understanding of their purpose or responsibilities.
Mr Ngubane referred to reports from Moroka Residence, where students had stated that they were bathing with cold water, and asked why this had occurred.
Mr David Kanguwe, Chief Operating Officer, said there had been cold water in June but stated that the matter had been resolved. He indicated that he had evidence on his phone showing the temperature settings on the water-heating system and that, as of the previous day, each boiler had been set at 56 °C.
He explained that the University experienced strain during winter because Kimberley became extremely cold or hot. In June, external temperatures had been between 35 °C and 45 °C, which had caused the water to be lukewarm. He said the temperature needed to be around 50 °C for proper hot water and therefore refuted the claim that the residence had no hot water.
Mr Ngubane asked the student representatives why the student had “lied” about the water.
Mr Gershwin van Niekerk, SRC President, replied that they had conveyed what students had reported to them.
Mr Ngubane raised the issue of off-campus accommodation and the R500 non-refundable fee that students wanted removed because they could not afford it.
Ms Marais said the R500 fee was charged to landlords as an administrative levy for the University’s management of funds. She said invoices were issued to landlords at the end of the year for Value Added Tax (VAT) purposes and emphasised that the fee was imposed on landlords, not on students.

Many of these providers were ordinary community members who had shared their experiences, including in church settings.
He noted that around R14 billion of the National Student Financial Aid Scheme’s budget was flowing into student accommodation at the time, creating opportunities for corrupt officials to enrich themselves and destabilise small businesses. As a result, the Department had decided that the scheme and universities should jointly handle accreditation, with the scheme playing an oversight role. He acknowledged, however, that the scheme had erred by appointing unethical individuals, creating new problems.
He referred to an oversight visit to Umfolozi Technical and Vocational Education and Training College (TVET) in eNkandla, where an NSFAS-accredited facility had been found in such poor condition that students were sleeping on sponges. He said that he had told the principal that, had he not had other commitments, he would have insisted that they both spend the night there. He argued that this demonstrated how some officials within NSFAS had replicated the very misconduct they were meant to prevent.
Concerns about outstanding payments and accommodation providers
The Chairperson stressed that many compliant accommodation providers met norms and standards but were not being paid because they could not afford to pay bribes. He stated that this would be fully interrogated in the Committee’s upcoming engagement with the National Student Financial Aid Scheme. He reported that the Committee had already requested a full breakdown of Off-Budget Allowance allocations, including amounts allocated, expected inflows, provincial distribution and provider-level payment details.
He noted particular concern regarding Training Young Minds, which appeared in accommodation arrangements across the country—in Upington, Kimberley, Pretoria and Bloemfontein—and continued to attract numerous complaints. He stated that this would form a central part of the next meeting with the National Student Financial Aid Scheme. He emphasised that he had already informed the scheme that someone must be held accountable for every small accommodation business that had collapsed due to non-payment.
He explained that, in some cases, corrupt actors manipulated payments by crediting an accommodation provider with R4 000 while charging the state R6 000, and then sharing the difference. In other cases, providers were accredited for “ghost” facilities, with students actually living at home while being recorded as residing in non-existent accommodation. Officials then pocketed the funds. He stated that he could substantiate these claims with evidence.
The Chairperson argued that the National Student Financial Aid Scheme could not simply “pour money” into the system without control and that a broader conversation was required with Universities South Africa, the Council on Higher Education and the South African Qualifications Authority about the structuring of university fees. He rejected the notion that the state could be told to “pay but not participate” in fee determination, describing this as regressive. He questioned why the same qualification could cost four times more at one institution than at another.
Sexual harassment case in the sports department
Turning to the Chief Operating Officer, the Chairperson raised a 2023 sexual harassment case involving an employee in the sports department and asked what had happened in that matter.
The Registrar replied that he did not have information on a sexual harassment case in the sports environment. He explained that Student Affairs had only been added to his portfolio in August 2024 and undertook to obtain and submit the details.
The Chairperson then recounted the case. He stated that a male staff member had committed a serious sexual assault against a young woman who had shown great courage in reporting it. He highlighted the power dynamics of a lecturer–student relationship and the difficulty for a student in reporting rape against an institutional employee.
He said that the alleged perpetrator had initially been suspended for only two days. According to information received, officials had questioned the perpetrator in the presence of the victim, and he had admitted to the assault. Instead of ensuring proper disciplinary action, they had coerced the victim into accepting an apology after the perpetrator claimed he would commit suicide. He stated that this signalled that there was no justice and that bravery in reporting such crimes could be turned into “stupidity” because no consequences followed. He said the victim had been manipulated and blackmailed.
The Chairperson instructed the institution to submit all required documentation by 19 November, noting that while he was protecting the identity of the victim, he was naming the perpetrator as a sports officer, Hendrik Mpho (or Mpho Hendricks).
He then asked the Deputy Vice-Chancellor: Teaching and Learning, whether any allegations of “sex for marks” had been brought to his attention.
Prof Paul Green, Deputy Vice-Chancellor: Teaching and Learning, replied that he had not received any formal complaints, but had raised the matter in the Deans and Directors Forum. He stated that the institution would not tolerate such conduct and that deans had been instructed to ensure transparent mark capturing.
When asked whether he had heard of such practices in the sector, Prof Green replied that, while no formal cases had been reported at Sol Plaatje University, his 30 years’ experience in the sector meant that he could not be naïve about the existence of such behaviour.
The Chairperson noted that students often did not report such cases because they had lost faith in the institution’s ability to protect victims. He explained that “sex for marks” involved sex under duress and was non-consensual, and that Members received such information directly from students during oversight visits. He urged the institution to investigate the matter proactively.
He then turned back to the Chief Operating Officer and asked why the new buildings shown in the presentation had defects and why this had not been disclosed.
Mr Kanguwe replied that there were two buildings with minor defects—the new Agriculture building and the Great Hall—but that these would be fixed within 12 months at no cost to the University. He stated that municipal inspections had identified no structural defects.
The Chairperson asked who had built the two buildings.
Mr Kanguwe replied that Ruwacon (Pty) Ltd had constructed both the new Agriculture building and the Great Hall. When asked whether the contractor had undertaken other projects at the institution, he stated that he could only recall the two. Regarding handover, he explained that the contractor was on site addressing minor defects.
Consideration of Committee reports
The Committee considered the Draft Reports on its Oversight Visits to KwaZulu-Natal and the University of Fort Hare. It also considered its Report on the Manufacturing, Engineering and Related Services Education and Training Authority (MERSETA) and Construction Annual Report.
All reports were adopted without amendment.

Closing remarks by the Chairperson
The Chairperson noted that the original submission deadline of Wednesday was likely too soon, given the volume of information requested, and extended it to 26 November. He emphasised that gender-based violence and femicide (GBVF) remained highly sensitive in South Africa and that SPU had not demonstrated adequate measures in handling such matters.
He stated that further engagement with the Department would be necessary, particularly regarding NDAs concluded by SPU, which appeared suspicious. He highlighted a perception that a “clique” led by the Acting Vice-Chancellor, including the Registrar and COO, dominated the institution, with those opposing this group facing negative consequences. Cases involving Prof Luvuyo, the Dean of Students, the CIO, and the current Vice-Chancellor’s extended period at home were cited as examples.
The Chairperson reported that Members had received multiple documents during the meeting that contradicted statements by the COO, CFO, Acting Vice-Chancellor, and Registrar. He reminded the delegation that providing false information to Parliament constituted perjury. He warned that should the Committee find any delegate had misled Parliament, the matter would be referred to the Powers, Privileges and Immunities Committee in terms of Section 17, with a request that imprisonment of up to three years be considered.
The Chairperson observed that Members had not covered all issues, noting the absence of the Legal Director despite rising legal costs. He reiterated his request for a full breakdown of all legal cases and associated costs and emphasised the need for complete transparency. He confirmed that the Committee would engage with NSFAS the following week, stressing that the scheme could not be allowed to compromise accommodation providers by failing to make payments, as this undermined basic services such as Wi-Fi, security, and maintenance.
He noted that unethical practices by some university and NSFAS officials appeared to deliberately disadvantage accommodation providers not formally linked to universities, with payments favouring providers connected to institutional structures. Members had observed these patterns in correspondence, including at SPU. The Chairperson warned that if providers were not paid, students would face severe hardship during exams, including a lack of food, bathing facilities, and hot water. He said the Committee would raise broader concerns about accommodation costs with the Department, highlighting that universities charged high rents for facilities funded by the state, yet expected the government to bear the burden. He stressed that institutions needed to remain solvent without punishing students.
The Chairperson appealed to Prof Carolissen to treat GBVF as a matter of urgency, describing cases at SPU as a “sore point” for Members. He argued that the Council needed to take ownership of cultural change, noting that the proliferation of NDAs in sexual misconduct cases created a perception that alleged perpetrators were favoured. He warned that such practices could deter young women from applying to SPU. He emphasised that government funding demonstrated a serious commitment to building SPU as a strong institution, and the University’s handling of GBVF needed to reflect that seriousness.
In closing, the Chairperson stressed the importance of constructive engagement with students and staff, noting that ignoring concerns worsened issues. He cautioned that the proposed R25 000 fee risked serious conflict, recalling similar incidents at the University of the Free State (UFS) where failure to heed warnings led to infrastructure damage. He urged proactive engagement with stakeholders, including discussions with the CFO about alternatives to the fee requirement. He warned that even partial registration blocks could trigger mobilisation and student strikes, which external actors could exploit for profit.
He called on management to meet with students and stakeholders to discuss concessions openly, emphasising that students had already escalated concerns to Parliament. He cautioned against a top-down approach, advocating for collaborative leadership to find mutually acceptable solutions while protecting the institution from internal and external risks.
The Chairperson reiterated that GBVF at SPU remained deeply troubling and that the Council needed to change the institutional culture urgently.
The meeting was adjourned.
