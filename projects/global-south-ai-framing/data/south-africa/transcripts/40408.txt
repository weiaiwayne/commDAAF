Meeting Summary
The Department of Tourism briefed the Committee on the Route Development Marketing Plan (RDMP), which had been developed to maximise South Africa’s domestic and international route development through marketing initiatives. This would have the effect of stimulating the economy and, in the process, create jobs and inclusive growth in the small business sector.
The Minister of Tourism said South African Tourism (SAT) had been allocated R6.5 million for marketing, and the Cabinet approved the RDMP in December last year. The presentation provided an update on the work done since then.
SAT said the plan aimed to maximise air connectivity for tourism by increasing market volumes, and to align with key policies -- the National Development Plan (NDP), Economic Reconstruction & Recovery Plan (ERRP), the National Tourism Sector Strategy, and the Tourism Master Plan. Before 2024, South Africa had lacked a coordinated route development strategy. The plan aimed to improve competitiveness, create a national structure, and offer business cases for airline profitability, marketing incentives for airlines, coordination with the Airports Company SA (ACSA), the Department of Transport, tourism stakeholders and public-private partnerships.
Key concerns raised by Members included the high price of air tickets for tourists to South Africa, as well as their impact on domestic tourism; the long passenger queues at the country's main arrival airports; the risk of business partnerships resulting in a complete handover to private entities to do everything; the lack of emphasis on a transformation framework for black-owned businesses or tourism operators; and the involvement of too many committees in the process leaving room for corruption.

Implementation of the Route Development Marketing Programme
Ms Nombulelo Guliwe, CEO of SAT, presented an overview of the Route Development Marketing Programme, and provided an update on work done since Cabinet's approval on 4 December 2024.
Ms Guliwe said the strategic focus was to supports air service development for tourism growth. This would require government-private sector collaboration, and would align with the Government of National Unity’s goal of inclusive growth and job creation.
The Tourism Route Development marketing plan aims to maximise air connectivity for tourism by increasing market volumes, and to align with key policies -- the National Development Plan (NDP), Economic Reconstruction & Recovery Plan (ERRP), National Tourism Sector Strategy, and Tourism Master Plan.
She said that before 2024, South Africa had lacked a coordinated route development strategy.
The plan aimed to improve competitiveness, create a national structure, and offer business cases for airline profitability, marketing incentives for airlines, coordination with the Airports Company SA (ACSA), the Department of Transport, tourism stakeholders and public-private partnerships.
Benchmarking with other countries had been led by the Minister of Tourism (Saudi Arabia), the civil aviation authority, but supported by SAT (Brazil), and through different tourism-led or aviation-led approaches (Canary Islands, Malaysia, and Scotland).
South Africa’s competitors were Australia, India, Thailand, the United Arab Emirates (UAE), Kenya and Brazil. The focus was on increasing international arrivals, the length of stays, and the spending per tourist.
Key route development insights
103 international routes to 64 cities worldwide via 49 airlines.
5 million seats scheduled for 2025 (92% of 2019 levels).
South Africa lacks direct flights to key markets like India, Japan, Spain, Italy, Ireland and Canada.
Implementation plan
SA Tourism to lead for 18 months.
5 million budget (2024/25) for marketing.
Focus on China, India, and Africa’s connectivity.
Goal: 15 million tourist arrivals by 2030.
Collaboration with provincial air access programmes (Cape Town, Durban and Gauteng).
The next steps were to appoint a reputable company by 29 March to lead the marketing programme, and to develop a national air access structure with provincial committees.
The SATS document outlined South Africa’s strategy for growing its tourism sector through improved air connectivity, marketing incentives, and international collaboration.

Minister's remarks after the presentation
Minister De Lille raised an issue regarding the structure at a political level, called the single air transport market. This was something that was being adopted by the African Union (AU), having a long history going back to 1988, and 54 countries in Africa had signed up to it. It was public knowledge that it was difficult to fly to destinations in Africa, sometimes having to fly out of Africa and then back. There had been a failure of the AU member countries to implement the single air traffic market, with very poor coordination and poor planning. In the search that the Minister had done in 2018, it had been launched in Rwanda. The South African President at the time, Jacob Zuma, had attended but by the time had been launched in 2018, the number of countries had reduced to 34 signatories.
There had then been terrible competition amongst them, wanting to cash in on the single air transport market, and there had been resistance to opening the skies so that Africa could get more airlines to fly in. This was one issue that SAT would take up on the agenda of the AU. It was in the AU’s plan, but had not been implemented -- it was just lying somewhere and had been filed. Another point they were going to take up through the structure, in order to improve flying within the region of Africa, was that it was very difficult and very expensive.
One currently had airlines like Emirates and Ethiopian Airlines that were exploiting the route. South African Airways (SAA) had gained back some of the regional markets, but again, not many airlines were flying into Africa. SAT would take the initiative, and a Cabinet memo would be sent to Minister of International Relations and Cooperation, Mr Ronald Lamola, and to the Cabinet in order to activate and implement the single air transport market.

Minister
De Lille thanked the Committee for the questions and advice, and commented that the constitution was clear as to who was accountable, saying the buck stops here. She highlighted the importance of growth for the tourism sector which was subject to two key critical areas, the one being business and the other being air access. Neither of them was in the mandate of the Department of Tourism, although both of them were in the performance agreement of the Minister of Tourism, which was signed with the President. This ultimately meant that there was a need to coordinate the mandates of various departments, and this was done via the Intergovernmental Relations Act. This Act states the terms of how to coordinate with other departments, how to consult, and, when there is an overlap, how to deal with it. It was also dealt with in an inter-ministerial committee that was a sub-committee of Cabinet, and was dealt with by Cabinet. This decision was approved by all Ministers in Cabinet, thus being the mandate of transport and tourism, as the mandate of the Department of Transport was to regulate the airways, as well as to have the civil aviation authority, which decides on landing routes.
The Minister maintained that SAT’s role was route development, which meant it had to market South Africa as a destination for airlines to fly here. Ultimately marketing was the role, hence the role being called the Route Development Marketing Plan. She strongly asserted that the Department of Transport must not get confused with the idea that SAT was going into airways and play a role there, because that was not the case.
She reiterated that SAT was marketing South Africa as a destination, and the Department of Tourism had dealt with the fragmentation of marketing Mzansi (a colloquial name for South Africa) as one country. Each province had a plan, at a national level there was a plan, local government had a plan, and the cities also had plans. With that being said, there was a need to pull everyone together keep to the budget and do what was best, and at least have one marketing message, which was South Africa.
She made it clear that the role of the Route Development Marketing Plan that was being established was not to ‘control’ anyone, but to facilitate a national response to issues, with all ministers -- whether transport or tourism -- working hand-in-hand. The Departments were bound by the three key priorities of the Government of National Unity (GNU), and the number one was to build an inclusive economy and to create jobs. The Minister would not be able to do this on her own; thus, there is a need for the inter-ministerial committee and Cabinet. Everyone was in agreement that this would add to the growth of the economy.
Minister De Lille said that the existing air access organisations in the provinces had been consulted on this strategy. The Department of Defence, SAA, State Security, and the Department of Transport had also been consulted to explain what the Route Development Marketing Strategy was, and to give an assurance that SAT was not getting in the way of transport.

All in all, the agreement signed brought accountability back to the Minister.

She expected an increase in the number of people coming to South Africa with a relaxed visa system, and there was therefore a need to plan now to get more airlines to the country.
Minister De Lille said she had liaised with the Chairperson of the Banking Association South Africa (BASA) who was on the board of SAA, and was addressing the long queues in Cape Town, Durban and O.R. Tambo. They did not want to divulge too much information about their plans due commercial sensitivity. Taking this into account, they were looking at fixing the routes, because the demand was high between Johannesburg, Cape Town and Durban.
The Minister’s last point was that she had convened a meeting between the marketing company, the marketing committee of the Tourism Business Council, the private sector and the marketing committee of South African Tourism. The objective was to pull the resources together and work together, as the government put in R1 billion and the private sector put in R30 billion. Taking this into account, there could not be competition between SAT and the Tourism Business Council, and a complete action plan was received from this meeting in the last week. This plan gave her hope and confidence to work with the private sector within the framework of a public-private partnership (PPP) and a private sector partner (PSP), with all matters being regulated.
She said that once the annual performance plan (APP) was finalised, the budget would be finalised. The Exempt Micro Enterprises (EME) had been approved by National Treasury, but the budget was still to be approved by Parliament. This decision should be made during April.
Chairperson's concluding remarks
The Chairperson referred to the issue the Minister had brought up regarding allegations of corruption, saying that it was in fact the third time this had been raised. She urged Committee Members that if they had any evidence to support the allegations, there were legal processes that had been developed by Parliament to deal with criminal activity. She hoped that would be the third and final time that this issue would be raised.
On 11 March, a presentation about the International Marketing Hub had been sent back, and the Chairperson asked when it was going to be presented, adding that it should be forwarded ahead to Members to peruse.
The Chairperson addressed the Minister and Members, saying that the Portfolio Committee acknowledges that the mandate for air transport falls outside of the Department of Tourism. The Committee welcomed the Route Development Marketing Plan initiative to ensure coordination of air access and tourism at the national level.
The Committee looked forward to seeing the implementation of this plan, especially the coordination at a provincial level to ensure that the aviation benefits were optimised by the provinces. It was imperative that the route plan and its structures were understood as not replacing existing initiatives, and that it was meant to serve a unifying platform of these programmes and other future initiatives by the provinces.
She said route development was critical for achieving the target of 15 million arrivals by 2030. Therefore partnerships around this were important to optimise.
The Committee was looking forward to seeing how the route plan would improve connectivity in the rest of the continent through the air transport market of the African Union. A meeting would be held later in the year for SAT to present the operational plan of these routes, including the budget allocation and spending, which should be approved in April.
Committee minutes
The minutes of the previous meeting were presented. The Chairperson asked whether any corrections or amendments were needed. After a minor correction, Ms Linde proposed their adoption and Mr Maeco seconded it.
The minutes were adopted.
The meeting was adjourned.
