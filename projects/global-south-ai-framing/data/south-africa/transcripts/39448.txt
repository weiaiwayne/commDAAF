Meeting Summary

2019 White Paper on Science, Technology and Innovation
2022-2032 Science, Technology and Innovation Decadal Plan
In a virtual meeting, the Portfolio Committee received a briefing from the Department of Science and Innovation (DSI) on the 2019 White Paper on Science, Technology and Innovation (STI), and the 2022-2032 Science, Technology and Innovation Decadal Plan.
The presentation focused on four main areas:
The South African National System of Innovation (NSI);
The policy context and performance of the NSI;
The 2019 White Paper on Science Technology and Innovation; and
Implementing the White Paper: the 2022 Decadal Plan on Science Technology and Innovation.
The Department said the NSI was a system of interconnected institutions, formal and informal, in the public and private spheres, whose activities and interactions initiate, import, modify and diffuse new technologies. The actors and stakeholders include business, government, academia and civil society. The policy context covers the overarching trends and places the South African economic environment on a global stage in a local context.
The South African Economic Reconstruction and Recovery Plan (ERRP) of 2021 was presented, along with corresponding thematic interventions.
The discussion also touched on e-government development trends and internet accessibility per province. According to the Department, the projected gross expenditure on research and development (GERD)/gross domestic product (GDP) targets of 1.1% by 2024 and 1.5% by 2030 would fall short if no changes were made, with spending currently below the 1.1% target for 2024. In terms of GERD over GDP, the country lagged far behind the world's leading knowledge-based economies. Over the last ten years, there has been a steady decrease in the private sector's contribution to research output, reflecting a decrease in the sector's investment in research and development, while universities continued to be the primary sources of research.
The White Paper aimed to establish a supportive and all-encompassing NSI governance setting, focusing on planning and monitoring, evaluation and learning, as well as fostering innovation and enhancing the human resource capacity within the NSI.
The meeting's discussion covered a number of issues, including the need for strong mechanisms to ensure effective implementation of the Decadal Plan, which the Department assured the Committee of. Members also put forward questions about the Department’s concerns on the state of intellectual property (IP) in the country. The Department conceded that a lot more work needed to be done in this area, including around registrations, the protection of IP, and the provision of more support and guidance through the National Intellectual Property Management Office. Patenting was quite expensive, and the support given to the relevant patenting authorities was there, but was not enough. As a result, R&D activity needed to be increased substantially so as to register more patents.
The meeting also discussed the need to ensure that there were public education engagements and awareness of the importance and impact of science on societal life. The Department outlined current plans and committed to ensuring these were improved to engage better with citizens.
Meeting report
Department of Science and Innovation briefing
Mr Daan du Toit, Acting Director-General, Department of Science and Innovation (DSI), said that the national system of innovation (NSI) concept arose from studies on the importance of innovation for economic development around the mid-1980s and early 1990s. The NSI concept was adopted in South Africa in the 1996 White Paper on Science and Technology. South Africa’s 2019 White Paper on Science, Technology and Innovation (STI) adopted the NSI as the organising framework, but infused it with a focus on transformative policy approaches -- for example, to protect the environment and drive inclusive economic growth.
The structure of the NSI and research in South Africa included the involvement of various stakeholders, with Parliament and Cabinet at the policy level, and a myriad of others at the advisory level, such as government departments, eight science councils, 23 institutions of higher learning, public research funding agencies and the private sector, to fund research and development (R&D). Overarching trends included rapid technological change that required new policy responses on the global stage and structural economic constraints like low economic growth, rising unemployment, and inequality in a local context.
The South African Economic Reconstruction and Recovery Plan (ERRP) of 2021 was outlined, together with corresponding thematic interventions. The presentation addressed trends in e-government development, and access to the internet per province. The Department reported that the gross expenditure on research and development (GERD)/gross domestic product (GDP) targets of 1.1% by 2024 and 1.5% by 2030, would not be met if business continued as usual, and that the current spending was significantly lower than the target of 1.1% by 2024. The country was still quite far behind the leading knowledge economies of the world in terms of GERD over GDP.
The most important resource for the NSI was the number of researchers employed, and the system had seen a decline in these after a sustained period of growth over the years. The Decadal Plan also had a clear transformation focus on the researcher profiles, and current data showed that there was still a lot of work and improvements to be done in this area. Universities remained the main sources of research output, but there were concerns at the decline in the percentage share of the private sector role, which had been declining consistently over the past decade, which mirrored the sector’s investment in R&D. South Africa’s world share of publication of internationally peer-reviewed journals should, however, be celebrated, as it had seen growth from 0.4% a decade ago, to 1% currently.
The National Advisory Council on Innovation (NACI) had confirmed that South Africa was experiencing an improvement in its STI human resources, and that research excellence was increasing, amongst other notable achievements.
2019 White Paper on Science, Technology and Innovation (STI)
Speaking on the motivation for the White Paper, Mr Du Toit reported that reviews showed that the DST and STI-intensive departments had made good progress on the 1996 White Paper, but that challenges remained. These included a lack of coherence, the size and transformation of the research system, insufficient human resources, sub-optimal and poor innovation performance, and underfunding of the National System of Innovation (NSI). Despite good progress, the NSI has yet to realise its potential to contribute to solving South Africa’s development challenges. He also spoke to the analysis and processes that linked the White Paper and the Decadal Plan.
The objectives of the White Paper were to create an enabling and inclusive NSI governance environment of planning and monitoring, evaluation and learning, facilitating innovation, and increasing and transforming the human resource base of the NSI, amongst others. There needed to be coherence and coordination of governance and monitoring and evaluation (M&E). He elaborated on the shifts in the 2019 White Paper, which included a stronger focus on innovation, therefore the use of STI and partnerships were mainstreamed through the document, and institutionalised so that STI initiatives would have the cooperation and support of business and civil society.
Referring to the 2022 Decadal Plan on STI -- the implementation plan of the White Paper -- the Acting DG outlined the philosophy that underlined the plan and the enablers for its implementation. The presentation also looked at the progress highlights, with a focus on areas such as governance and funding, the CoalCO2-X Programme and vaccine development.

Discussion
Ms A Hlongo (ANC) said she wished the meeting had been afforded more time to engage the Department on these two papers, especially since they were already being implemented, and had passed through the Sixth Administration. However, Members of the Committee had a better understanding of what the papers were on, and how they were being implemented. She wished to know the monitoring and evaluation mechanisms the Department had to ensure the implementation of the Decadal Plan.
Ms N Mazzone (DA) wanted to know how often the Committee should expect to receive updates on variations and amendments to the programme from the Department that would be in line with the changes and advancements made in technology and innovation in the country. A lot of emphasis had been put on the intellectual property (IP) aspect, and it was noted that the Department had concerns in this area. Why was this the case, and how could it be rectified? It also seemed there was a lot of cross-pollination between the work of the DSI and the Departments of Energy, as well as Communication, in terms of artificial intelligence (AI) regulation and hydrogen innovation etc. Were joint briefings and oversight by these portfolios on the cards?
Dr W Boshoff (FF+) asked the Acting DG to elaborate on issues around patenting, and the causes of the challenges he had spoken about. He believed it was important to reflect on this, as this was where the whole system of innovation should translate into job creation and increased economic growth.
Mr T Ramongalo (DA) asked if the Acting DG was happy with the current NSI legislation, if it responded to South Africa's current needs, or if legislative amendments were recommended. He wanted to understand how the translation of scientific research had impacted economic growth in the country. Could the Department assure the Committee that the upcoming science and innovation plans would address key strategic areas like energy, agriculture and health? Would these plans be sustainable and contribute to the economic growth and development of the country?
Mr V Nkosi (ANC) enquired about the R&D investments by both the public and private sectors. How would the funding be allocated to ensure equitable distribution amongst the different fields of study and sectors of research and development? What metrics would be used to measure the success of the initiatives undertaken through the programmes? The Department needed to improve its communication strategies, especially public education programmes, to ensure citizens understand these initiatives and thus gain their support.
DSI's response
Responding to the questions, Mr Du Toit assured the Committee that monitoring and evaluation were at the very heart of the Decadal Plan. It was done in a systematic way to track progress, because it was information from the tracking which informed the revisions and strategy of the ‘living’ document, as referred to by some of the Members.
Ms Gugulethu Zwane, DDG: Institutional Planning and Support, DSI, said the Decadal Plan was quite explicit in Chapter 10 in terms of proper monitoring and evaluation through reflections, to look at learning and evidence-based policy and decision-making. The Department was finalising the NSI M&E framework internally. It helped that the Decadal Plan already had a number of indicators that would assist with the process. There were also baselines that had been taken from other areas, documents and studies within the NSI nationally. Some were new and the baseline would then be determined after the first measurements had been done. These included economic indicators, patents, skills and qualifications.
Mr Imraan Patel, DDG: Research and Development Support, DSI, responded that the improvement of public communication had been identified as a priority from the first White Paper. This was through a programme called Public Engagement with Science, which involved getting into genuine engagements with citizens across all sectors on the impact of science on society. The strategy was finalised a number of years ago, and improvements have been put in place. This placed the promotion of science and engagement with the National Research Foundation (NRF). Institutionally, they had strengthened the capacity of an agency that existed under the NRF, the South African Advancement for Science and Technology, which played an immense role in terms of the National Science Week activities, for example, and in supporting science centres in a whole range of public understanding programmes. There were plans to set aside a budget for the promotion of science in a coordinated way with entities. The Committee would be updated in the future on progress in this area.
Mr Patel said R&D funding, particularly for the NRF and its investment decisions, had been identified as another area that needed more work. The entity had improved its information systems, and there was now a centre of excellence that assisted with scientometric modelling and human capital development, etc. These were being interrogated because opportunities for co-funding would have to be identified in the case of a constrained budget. Using available evidence, he was confident that current funding strategies were effective, especially with the view to enhancing transformation outcomes, creating opportunities for particular demographic groups, and improving the support of historically disadvantaged institutions through a much more consolidated programme of work.
Dr Mmboneni Muofhe, DDG: Socio-Economic Innovation Partnerships (DSI), said that productivity, whether in terms of publications or patents, would follow the kind of trajectory in investment in research and development, so the more activities published, the more intellectual property would be generated. Investment in R&D had gone down to 0.61% of GDP, so that would reflect the productivity of the system. The metrics that were used generally looked at patents, although there were other forms of intellectual properties that would not always be reported on. Patenting was quite expensive, and the support given to the relevant patenting authorities was there, but not enough. As a result. R&D activity needed to be increased substantially so as to register more patents.
Locally, there were challenges around IP registrations because of the absence of a policy. Work was being done to rectify this, although the processes were cumbersome and costly, and might take time. There were concerns, and the DSI would look at providing more support and guidance on handling IP registrations, most likely through the National Intellectual Property Management Office (NIPMO). This would assist with avoiding challenges around intellectual property leakages.
Dr Rakeshnie Ramoutar-Prieschl, Acting DDG: Technology Innovation, said that the Intellectual Property Rights (IPR) Act did call for the regulatory framework for the protection of IP within the country, as well as the establishment of the NIPMO. There were a number of activities led by the latter, in terms of addressing the challenges across the sector, including awareness campaigns and partnering with relevant stakeholders. There remained challenges with IP owners who were happy to license to big corporates or industry partners, instead of exploring avenues to create businesses that would contribute to the country’s economy. The DSI was also looking into forging stronger partnerships between universities and industries and exploring how the IPR Act could be amended to support innovation efforts in the country.
Mr Du Toit agreed that the Decadal Plan was a ‘living’ document and an enabling framework. The implementation of the plan would be sensitive to developments, and subject to constant revisions and enhancement.
The Department looked forward to further engagements with the Committee for updates and to receive its much-valued guidance. He agreed that it would be important to work with sister departments in government to unlock the country’s science and innovation potential.
Consideration of committee minutes
The Committee considered its minutes of the meetings of 10, 17 and 18 July; and 20, 23 and 27 August. The minutes were adopted.
The meeting was adjourned.
