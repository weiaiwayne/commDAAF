Ad hoc Committee on Lt Gen Mkhwanazi’s allegations
The Ad Hoc Committee, led by the evidence leader, engaged suspended Lieutenant General Shadrack Sibiya, SAPS Deputy National Commissioner for Crime Detection, in a detailed examination of his sworn testimony, focusing on the strained relationship between him and Lieutenant General Nhlanhla Mkhwanazi, as well as the controversial disbandment of the Political Killings Task Team (PKTT). General Sibiya strongly disputed Lt Gen Mkhwanazi’s public allegations, including claims that he procured spy equipment and mishandled 121 investigative dockets related to politically motivated murders. He characterised much of Lt Gen Mkhwanazi’s narrative as misleading and motivated by internal SAPS power struggles. General Sibiya emphasised that he acted under the authority of the National Commissioner’s office and denied any role in obstructing investigations or shielding criminal figures. The Committee scrutinised procedural inconsistencies in the disbandment process and explored the operational and political tensions impacting SAPS leadership.
The Committee also probed allegations of political interference, possible conflicts of interest among its members, and General Sibiya’s associations with controversial figures implicated in investigations. General Sibiya explained that the PKTT was intended as a temporary unit, with its mandate fulfilled and its expertise being integrated into other national units. The Committee probed allegations of political interference, corruption, and conflicts of interest, particularly concerning relationships with controversial figures implicated in investigations.  The Committee also addressed Gen Sibiya’s concerns about a Member of Parliament asking the witness questions when that Member’s party had opened a criminal case against him.

Chairperson: With the 37 years of service and your position as Deputy National Commissioner, second in command, do you think the things that have been happening reflect well on an institution like SAPS?
General Sibiya: From my perspective, I have always been defending myself, my name, and my reputation. I have not started conflicts. For example, the press briefing by Lt Gen Mkhwanazi. If I had done it, I would have been fired long ago. No one sees anything wrong with a General wearing camouflage and doing what he did while others are treated differently. We are not equal. I do not suggest he should be fired. Even if one is unhappy, there are other ways to voice concerns without misleading the public.
Chairperson: Members asked you about Lt Gen Mkhwanazi. If you were brought together and placed side by side, would you say the things you stated about each other as senior officers are correct?
General Sibiya: I would not justify anything wrong. If I was wrong, I would accept it. I do not insist I was right where I was not.
Chairperson: You said your position is senior to that of the Provincial Police Commissioner. Given your seniority, South Africans expect a higher standard. Do you have any regrets about things you might have said that could have been uncomfortable for the public?
General Sibiya: I do, and I have expressed it. I apologize to the people of South Africa for the manner in which we, as police management, carried ourselves. I said this openly on television.
Senior Counsel Questions:
Adv Arendse: There was no mention of any November meeting before the directive issued on 31 December. It was not mentioned by General Masemola either. You can reference his affidavit, which shows he was caught by surprise by the Minister's decision on 31 December. Nothing was raised at the event on 28 December in KZN. If a meeting occurred, you could only say there was a meeting without the minute. On your affidavit, pages 20 and 21, paragraph 57, you said you learned in November 2024 of a complaint lodged by Professor Mary De Haas. On 4 November, Professor De Haas addressed a complaint to the Minister regarding the immediate disbandment of PKTT. The complaint was sent to the portfolio committee. You received a copy via the Office of the National Commissioner on 6 November. At paragraph 63, you said, "I was not aware of any high-level discussions in relation to the disbandment of PKTT prior to the Minister's directive." It was never mentioned, and no such meeting occurred in November. Do you accept this?
General Sibiya: The paragraph I read came from the minutes of a meeting. What I noted myself from that meeting may have been an oversight.
Mr Ntuli: I apologise for disrupting, Senior Counsel. I am concerned. The General’s affidavit, paragraph 63, states he was not aware of high-level discussions prior to the Minister's directive. Earlier, he read personal notes referencing a November meeting. Which should we accept, his written notes or sworn statement?
General Sibiya: I acknowledge and accept that paragraph 63 of my statement is correct. I was not aware of high-level discussions regarding the disbandment of PKTT prior to the Minister’s directive. I have been requested to expedite the establishment of a murder and robbery unit. For example, page 167 notes the project’s dual purpose: determining the implementation status of specialized units in the Detective Service since 2016. Page 76 highlights provincial murder and robbery unit capacity at 18% against a target of 25%. The unit was established to conduct provincially directed investigations and replace multiple task teams. It is necessary to assess provincial investigation unit capacities before deciding whether to close units due to budget constraints. The task teams should be placed within the murder and robbery unit. I may need to verify the date of the meeting, possibly 27 November.
Adv Arendse: Let us move on. There was clearly no meeting in November. If the General wants to find a minute of that meeting tonight or tomorrow, he can make it available. The evidence is clear. His affidavit, which he signed under oath, aligns with what General Masemola stated.
Chairperson: Mr Ntuli, are you satisfied?
Mr Ntuli: I can wait, as there is a chain to follow.
Adv Arendse: This affidavit was signed by the General on 8 October, likely in Sandton. Can we agree that the proposed closure of PKTT never happened, so the objective was not achieved?
General Sibiya: Yes.
Adv Arendse: Can we also agree that the purpose of closing PKTT was never achieved? You were never involved with PKTT.
General Sibiya: Yes.
Adv Arendse: We know, based on the facts, that you ordered the dockets to be brought to head office. You did not receive them physically, but you were given a schedule of what was in the dockets. You spent quite some time going through various cases. You mentioned that some of these cases were never investigated, going back to 2014 and 2016, and it appeared that these dockets were being neglected, according to what you tried to demonstrate.
General Sibiya: I never ordered the dockets to be brought to head office. I instructed that they be exchanged with the divisional commissioner and that a repository be decided upon where they could be kept, stored, evaluated, inspected, and returned to where they belong.
Adv Arendse: Yes, you ordered that. You instructed General Khumalo to do that, and that is what he did. The first batch of 118 dockets was brought on 8 March, and the remaining three Eastern Cape dockets were brought in April.
General Sibiya: Yes.
Adv Arendse: Those dockets were evaluated by analysts. Some appeared to be cold cases, while others seemed not to have been worked on, and arrests had not been made. Why would you allow that when the dockets were sitting in the office for three to four months, neglected? You are the head of detectives. Why did you not order the dockets to be returned so that arrests could be effected and the cases either closed or activated and brought to court?
General Sibiya: I find this question unfair. The process was that if we were deactivating a docket, it was to be taken to the murder and robbery unit. When the dockets were taken there, the provincial commissioner rejected them. I never said they must be brought to head office.
Adv Arendse: That was exactly my point. They were not sent to the murder and robbery unit either. They simply sat for three months.
General Sibiya: For three months, yes.
Adv Arendse: The victims of these crimes want to see justice. They do not want delays, especially where killings are involved and families are affected.
General Sibiya: May I ask a question?
Chairperson: Yes, you may ask the Senior Counsel.
General Sibiya: Has the counsel accepted that the dockets were supposed to go to KZN, and that KZN rejected them?
Adv Arendse: No. The counsel stated he was not the custodian of the dockets. The documents should have been returned to General Senthumule and General Khumalo. General Khumalo is the project leader of PKTT. Why were the dockets not restored to them?
General Sibiya: Eventually, they were taken.
Adv Arendse: Why would they go to Lt Gen Mkhwanazi? He refused to accept them. The point I am making is that these dockets represent neglect. These are active cases, 121 in total. Some of your best detectives, the PKTT, have done good work. Now, the head of detectives issues a circular instructing Khumalo to bring the dockets. They were evaluated, sat for three months, then sent to Lt Gen Mkhwanazi, who never had the dockets in the first place. They then lay there for a further couple of months, neglected again.
General Sibiya: When the transaction occurred between General Khumalo and General Senthumule, they exchanged the dockets. General Senthumule attempted to take them to KZN, but the provincial commissioner rejected them. Nobody sees a mistake there. The only mistake some people perceive is that the dockets were taken to head office, which I did not instruct.
Adv Arendse: So, you followed orders. You did not investigate what PKTT was doing or how they were operating. It was only on 7 March that you sent a directive to General Khumalo to bring you the dockets and prepare a closing report. You did not wait for the closing report. On 15 March, you went to General Masemola, who told you to go to General Rabi in the office to draft the document. You returned with the first document, which had space for the National Commissioner’s signature. He refused to sign. You then went back to General Rabi, returned with the same document without his signature, and you signed it yourself. That is why you were charged in a disciplinary inquiry. Provincial Commissioner Patekile was the investigating officer. You were charged for that, correct?
General Sibiya: Chair, I do not agree with the Counsel’s statement. The National Commissioner instructed me to proceed and take precedence. Everything that occurred thereafter followed his instructions. Even the meeting on 6 and 7 March outlined the plan approved by the National Commissioner.
Adv Arendse: General Sibiya, it is late. We have spent hours on this. The meeting on 5 and 6 March did not resolve the issue. On 6 March, there was dissatisfaction, and the meeting was adjourned until 27 March. You refer to 6 March repeatedly. You wrote another letter stating that you wanted closure by 24 April. That did not happen. On 24 April, General Khumalo brought you the three remaining Eastern Cape dockets. The PKTT was not closed. You submitted a budget on 12 July, which was rejected. On 27 July, Lt Gen Mkhwanazi submitted a budget proposal, which was approved. It is now common cause that the PKTT will operate until 26 March next year. We agreed on these facts.
General Sibiya: We do not agree, Counsel. I feel you are oppressing me. We went step by step and showed that everything occurred according to the plan approved by the National Commissioner. Even the disbandment letter I activated came from the National Commissioner’s office through General Sebola.
Adv Arendse: Can we agree that the PKTT has not been disbanded? You followed instructions to disband it, but it has not been disbanded. Does that mean you are now in trouble for not following instructions?
General Sibiya: It was never disbanded. It is still operating.
Adv Arendse: No, it was not disbanded. The only delay occurred when the dockets were sent to head office in Pretoria and lay there for three months, preventing the unit from operating.
Chairperson: Senior Counsel, do you still have questions?
Mr Sauls: I want clarity.
Chairperson: You are now debating the issues with the witness.
Mr Sauls: I just want clarity on the dockets. Where did the dockets come from? Secondly, the dockets that were sent back, are they KZN cases? If they are sent back, they should go to KZN, because that is where the cases must be handled. I want to know where the dockets were before being removed and where they must be.
Chairperson: I want us to conclude the proceedings. When the Senior Counsel requested a minute, it was to clarify matters that would not have been clarified by the witness.
Mr Sauls: Can I get clarity, please?
Chairperson: Yes, or if you wish, I can note a further set of hands.
Mr Sauls: Can the Senior Counsel clarify for me? I want to understand.
Adv Arendse: I will answer quickly. The answer is simple. The dockets came from KZN and they were returned to KZN.
Mr Sauls: So why is the witness being interrogated about why the dockets were sent back to KZN?
Adv Arendse: Absolutely.
Mr Sauls: Absolutely?
Adv Arendse: Yes. Lieutenant General Khumalo, the head of the task team, says that the purpose of requesting those dockets was not related to PKTT. The dockets were being analysed to determine whether any Gauteng cartel drug-related cases were being investigated by PKTT. Would you like to comment on that?
General Sibiya: Just to ask a question, did General Khumalo testify before this committee?
Adv Arendse: No, he has not testified yet. I am putting this to you to give you an opportunity to respond.
General Sibiya: I hear the evidence leader comment that General Khumalo “says”.
Adv Arendse: Yes.
General Sibiya: Are you communicating directly with General Khumalo?
Adv Arendse: No. We are listening to what he previously stated. He will testify officially before this committee at a later stage. He was the project leader of the team.
General Sibiya: I do not want to get the impression that the evidence here keeps generating ongoing questions and messages.
Adv Arendse: Were you aware of the arrest of Mr Katiso Molefe on 6 December?
General Sibiya: I did not know.
Adv Arendse: Why not? The arrest took place in Gauteng, under one of your units.
General Sibiya: I heard about it when it happened, but I was not aware beforehand.
Mr Sauls: Point of order, Chair. It is after midnight. We are starting the process all over again, and we are being made to sit here and listen. Please, Chair, make a ruling. We have already heard this question.
Chairperson: I have made a ruling, Mr Sauls. I stated that the Senior Counsel must clarify issues that need clarity.
Adv Arendse: Do you know Mr Cedric Nkabinde?
General Sibiya: Yes, I know Mr Nkabinde. He is the Chief of Staff of the Minister.
Adv Arendse: Do you know Major General Lesetja Senona, who is the head of the Hawks in KZN?
General Sibiya: Yes, I know him.
Adv Arendse: Do you know Brigadier Julius Mkhwanazi, the Ekurhuleni Metro Police Chief?
General Sibiya: No, I do not.
Adv Arendse: Do you know Mr Calvin Rafadi?
General Sibiya: Yes, I know him.
Adv Arendse: Do you know Mr Matlala?
General Sibiya: Yes.
Adv Arendse: Do you know Mr Mogotsi?
General Sibiya: We have been talking about Mr Mogotsi and Mr Matlala all day.
Adv Arendse: Do you know Mr Suleiman Carrim?
General Sibiya: No.
Adv Arendse: I am asking because these people are mentioned in WhatsApp messages. They are all linked to Gauteng drug cartels. Several Members have observed that we are often known by the company we keep. You know all of these people. Since it is late, I will not ask whether you have all their numbers on your cell phone.
General Sibiya: No, I do not.
Adv Arendse: Finally, one observation for your comment. It is disturbing that, as part of this committee's terms of reference, we are asked how to restore trust and confidence in the police. It is concerning to hear about phone tapping and data tampering at high levels within the police. You say this is being done, or is perceived to be done, by some of your opponents. How do you expect the public listening or watching this to trust the police? How should this trust be restored?
General Sibiya: I believe the recommendations from this ad hoc committee will contribute to how this matter should be handled going forward.
Mr Ntuli: I have one question. I did not finish earlier, but since this matter has arisen now, and we will not have another session with General Sibiya according to our schedule, I want to clarify what we should take home. Between paragraph 63 and the private note the General presented to our meeting, which should we rely on and which should we disregard?
General Sibiya: If I am allowed to confirm the date of 27 November, I can submit a written explanation in relation to that.
Chairperson: Is that acceptable, Mr Ntuli?
Mr Ntuli: I am not completely satisfied, Chair, but I can accept it.
Chairperson: He is requesting permission to make a written submission.
Mr Ntuli: For now, we will regard the contradiction as unresolved until that written submission is received.
Chairperson: That is correct.
Mr Skosana: General, you mentioned the murder and robbery unit. Do we have such units in KZN, Mpumalanga, North West, and all other provinces?
General Sibiya: I believe there are, but I cannot confirm how many at present, as I have been out of the system for some time. The document says the establishment must be expedited by the Deputy National Commissioner, so I am not aware of the progress made so far.
Chairperson’s Closing Remarks
Chairperson: Members, let me start by thanking the staff of Parliament for their support. It is very late, and you are still with us. Your commitment has not gone unnoticed. Thank you, Members, and thank you Senior Counsel and General Sibiya. Tomorrow, or today, we have a day off. We will return on Thursday, when Minister Mchunu will be here. The Senior Counsel has requested that we allocate him a day and a half for evidence, so Thursday and Friday will be dedicated to that. On Friday, we may finish around one or two o’clock, depending on progress. We will then adjourn and return on Tuesday next week for Members to engage with Minister Mchunu. We start at 11 o’clock on Thursday. Our meeting today, 15 October, began at 19:00. Thank you to the two gentlemen here. They do not speak, but they are always present. I must thank them for their support.
The proceedings were adjourned at 00:40.
