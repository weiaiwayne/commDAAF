Meeting Summary
Annual Reports 2024/25
The Portfolio Committee on Basic Education met to receive briefings from the Department of Basic Education (DBE), the South African Council for Educators (SACE), and Umalusi on their 2024/25 Annual Reports as part of the Budgetary Review and Recommendations Report (BRRR) process. The purpose was to assess institutional performance against planned targets, evaluate audit outcomes, and strengthen parliamentary oversight of the basic education sector.
The meeting opened with serious concern regarding the absence of the Minister of Basic Education, who was attending a Cabinet committee meeting. Members expressed frustration at the Minister’s continued non-attendance, stressing that accountability to Parliament required her direct participation.
After extensive discussion, the Committee resolved that the Department of Basic Education’s briefing would be deferred until the Minister could attend but agreed to proceed with the briefings by the SACE and Umalusi.
Deputy Minister Reginah Mhaule explained that although she did not hold executive authority, she and the Minister worked jointly on all departmental matters. She addressed the ongoing delays in payments for Education Assistants, attributing them to administrative and Information and Communications Technology (ICT) challenges, particularly in rural provinces. She confirmed that all outstanding payments would be processed by the following week. The Committee welcomed this assurance but requested a comprehensive written report detailing the reasons for the delays, accountability steps taken, and corrective measures to ensure consistent and timely payments in future.
The SACE reported that it achieved 81% of its annual targets and maintained a surplus of R8 million. It received an unqualified audit opinion, supported by improved membership fee compliance. Members, however, expressed concern about the backlog of more than 600 misconduct cases, including sexual offences, and the continued employment of unregistered or unqualified teachers, particularly in independent schools. Members called for stronger collaboration between SACE, the Department of Justice and Constitutional Development (DoJ&CD), and the South African Police Service (SAPS) to ensure that offenders were struck off the register and recorded on the Sexual Offenders Register. Members also raised concerns about the growing number of foreign national teachers, the lack of baseline data for key performance indicators, and the use of attendance figures rather than competence as a measure of teacher development. SACE committed to strengthening its monitoring systems, enhancing ethical conduct management, and advancing the professionalisation of Early Childhood Development (ECD) practitioners.
Umalusi reported achieving 93% of its annual targets and received a clean audit outcome. It highlighted progress in governance, ICT modernisation, and research outputs, but noted budgetary constraints that limited its monitoring of examination and marking centres. Members commended Umalusi’s performance and governance but raised concern about the limited oversight of unregulated online schools and the need to extend quality assurance coverage. Umalusi assured the Committee that measures were being implemented to strengthen compliance, financial sustainability, and monitoring capacity.
The Committee resolved to reschedule the Department of Basic Education’s presentation to a date when the Minister would be available. It also requested detailed reports on Education Assistant payments, SACE’s backlog of misconduct cases, and measures to strengthen oversight of independent schools and the ECD sector. The Chairperson emphasised the urgent need for a coordinated approach to address gender-based violence in schools and proposed a joint engagement with relevant departments to address the issue comprehensively.

Highlights on departmental challenges and oversight: DM Overview
Deputy Minister Mhaule provided highlights regarding basic education, explaining that the Department had not fully adhered to the Memorandum of Understanding (MoU) as funds for the first three months had been sent without issue. Challenges arose when attendance registers were required from every province, district, circuit, and school. She noted that an ICT system had been established for electronic submission, but some rural provinces lacked connectivity, causing delays. She added that the Department was working with the Department of Employment and Labour (DEL), the Unemployment Insurance Fund (UIF), and other entities to address these challenges. She indicated that payments would be processed by the following Friday and that the system would be updated monthly to account for resignations or postponed submissions.
She also highlighted that October was a month to celebrate teachers, noting that the National Teaching Awards were scheduled, and emphasised that all teachers should be recognised, not only those involved in specific programmes. Deputy Minister Mhaule stressed the importance of presenting quarterly reports, preparing for examinations, and ensuring the readiness of learners and subjects. She indicated that, historically, the Auditor-General South Africa (AGSA) and other relevant sectors would attend the presentation of the Department’s APP to ensure accountability and encouraged Committee Secretary to verify that this process was followed. She further noted that the Committee would need to consider preparations for 2026.
Deputy Minister Mhaule concluded by acknowledging the Committee’s time constraints, emphasising that the Department faced challenges while remaining committed to the oversight process, before giving way for SACE to make their presentation.

Overview and strategic-plan conclusion
Ms Ella Mokgalane, Chief Executive Officer (CEO), SACE, reported that the SACE Annual Report for 2024/25 marked the conclusion of the Council’s five-year strategic plan (2020-25). She noted that SACE had achieved 81% of its planned targets, meeting 17 of 21 performance indicators, and said the entity’s focus continued to be on advancing teacher professionalism through registration, ethics enforcement, continuous professional development and digital transformation.

She reported that over 66 000 educators had been registered and that 31 808 educators had participated in professional development programmes. She reported that SACE had recorded a surplus of R8 million for the year, marginally lower than the prior year’s R9 million, and noted an 11% increase in net assets to R199 million. Strategic priorities and way forward
In conclusion, she said the report reflected a clear commitment to teacher professionalism, ethical conduct and accountability despite structural and resource constraints. She stated that SACE’s strategic focus going forward would be to improve financial sustainability through a revised funding model, strengthen digital systems and enhance organisational capacity to secure stability and effectiveness in delivering its mandate.

Ms Mokgalane explained that the SACE derived its authority from the SACE Act No. 31 of 2000, which empowered the Council to register all individuals teaching in front of learners in both public and independent schools. Non-compliance and employer responsibilities
She pointed out challenges with non-compliance under Section 21 of the SACE Act, which applied to both individuals and employers, including provincial education departments, the DBE and independent schools, and she clarified that associations were not considered employers.
Data systems and educator mobility
Ms Mokgalane explained that public schools benefited from a central database to monitor teachers while independent schools lacked comparable systems, resulting in instability as educators frequently moved between institutions. She emphasised that SACE registered both fully qualified and conditionally qualified educators and that the Council had prioritised professionalising the ECD sector in alignment with departmental goals.

She reported that SACE mainly dealt with cases of corporal punishment, sexual misconduct and teacher-on-teacher disputes, and that many of these matters were minor and should have been resolved at school or district level but were often escalated to SACE owing to fear of unions or lack of local intervention.

Under the Quality Assurance and Monitoring programme it reported moderation of question papers, verification of marking processes, monitoring of assessments and accreditation or monitoring of a large number of independent schools and private colleges; accreditation decisions were finalised within twelve months in nearly all cases, exceeding performance targets. The Council said it had one hundred and fifty-one approved posts and maintained a stable workforce, with personnel costs accounting for almost half of total expenditure.
Operational constraints and quality assurance coverage
The Council acknowledged constraints, noting that budget limitations restricted the extent of monitoring of examination and marking centres and that only a small percentage of sites could be visited for quality assurance. It reported ongoing difficulties in recruiting moderators in specialised subjects and delays in the implementation of the National Senior Certificate (NSC) for Adults by the Department of Higher Education and Training (DHET). Umalusi raised concern about an increase in unregistered online schools offering uncertified qualifications and said it had issued compliance guidelines to address the matter.
Compliance, irregularities and audit follow-up
Umalusi reported that irregular expenditure of over R500 000 and fruitless and wasteful expenditure of about R 7.8 million from previous years had been condoned and that only R6 000 in fruitless expenditure was incurred in the 2024/2025 financial year; the latter was under investigation. The Council said measures were in place to prevent recurrence.
Conclusion and acknowledgements
The Council concluded that, notwithstanding financial pressure and capacity challenges, it continued to carry out its mandate effectively and to uphold the integrity of its quality assurance processes. It expressed confidence that the clean audit outcome, sound governance and high level of performance evidenced Umalusi’s credibility and commitment to maintaining high education standards. Prof Ballim expressed gratitude to the Minister and Deputy Minister of Basic Education, the Director-General and his colleagues on the Umalusi Council for their support and then handed over to the CEO and management to lead the presentation.

Quality of marking
Addressing concerns about marking quality, Dr Poliah observed that the CEO of Umalusi had demonstrated a sound understanding of departmental marking processes but provided further clarity. He explained that although markers were appointed through a formal selection process, they were required to be authorised before marking. Authorisation occurred at two levels: at national level, chief markers and internal moderators were authorised at the marking standardisation meeting held in Pretoria; and at provincial level, each marker was authorised for the specific questions they would mark. He described the use of a tolerance-range system for open-ended questions—typically two to three marks—which set the acceptable difference between marks awarded by different markers for the same question; where a senior marker’s moderation showed the tolerance limit exceeded, the entire batch was re-evaluated. He added that extensive training used live learner scripts (dummy scripts) to test consistency among markers, enabling coordinators to identify those who were too lenient or too strict, and that Umalusi performed final ratification to confirm marking quality.
Regulation of online schools
Dr Poliah’s final point concerned the regulation of online schools. He described the rapid proliferation of online institutions as a growing concern and reported that both the Department and Umalusi were working to fast-track regulation. He said that, about two to three weeks earlier, the Department had developed draft regulations and had engaged the Office: Chief State Law Advisor, which had advised that an amendment to the South African Schools Act (SASA) would be required. He explained that while the Department had initially assumed online schools could be treated as schools with a different mode of delivery, current legislation made no provision for online schooling as an educational institution and that the required legislative amendment would take several years to finalise.
Dr Poliah concluded by explaining that, in response, the Minister had directed the Department to pursue a simultaneous process—developing the new regulations while also amending the South African Schools Act. At the same time, Umalusi would continue to set minimum operational standards to ensure that currently active online schools could continue functioning under controlled and regulated conditions. He said this parallel approach would allow the Department to manage implementation effectively while the broader legislative amendments were being processed.
Further discussion
Marker qualifications and appointment procedure
Mr Shikwambana sought clarification on the qualifications and quality standards required of examination markers. He referred to the structure in which five markers were supervised by a senior moderator and asked what the current standards were for those five markers. He explained that, in about 2010 when he had been involved in marking, the only apparent requirement to become a marker had been possession of a Matric certificate. He described his own experience of applying and then participating in marking with only three markers present, and he requested confirmation from the CEO and the Chairperson as to whether the procedure and requirements for appointing markers had changed since that time.
Concerns about regulatory grey areas
Mr Mkhize warned that a “hands-off, eyes-on” approach created a grey area that could be exploited by criminals. He cited an academy in Durban as an example of an institution operating without proper registration or approval that had been shut down following intervention by the DBE. He stressed the need for Umalusi to enforce consequences for institutions operating without accreditation, warning that the absence of consequence management left the public vulnerable—particularly where paid schooling was involved—and urged that such unregistered schools be reported, noting that the matter fell within the academic regulatory space.
Departmental assurance on marker requirements
Dr Poliah assured the Chairperson and Members that a Matric certificate alone was not sufficient to qualify an individual as a marker. He explained that markers must first have extensive teaching experience, including at least two years of Matric teaching experience within the last five years. In addition, markers must demonstrate relevant subject knowledge at a second-year tertiary level, either through a teacher’s diploma or a bachelor’s degree. He emphasised that the selection process was stringent and that candidates were also evaluated on previous marking performance; those who failed to meet a minimum score were not reappointed in subsequent years.
Closing remarks
The Chairperson expressed gratitude to attendees and noted that the meeting had taken considerable time because Members had sought to engage in strategic discussion. She said the extended deliberation reflected the necessity of addressing substantive issues rather than merely hearing presentations.
She indicated that there would be further opportunities to engage on three priority issues. First, on online schools she highlighted the seriousness of the matter and emphasised the need for continued engagement with the DBE, specifically on the required amendments to the South African Schools Act to provide appropriate legal recognition and regulation of online provision.
Second, on the National Certificate Vocational (NCV), she stressed the importance of establishing a coherent development pathway for young people from ECD through to the labour market. She observed that Basic Education, Higher Education and other stakeholders were not presently coordinating effectively to secure that pathway and said this coordination must be improved.
Third, she raised the topic of automation and artificial intelligence, acknowledging that the learning process was changing with technological advances such as Artificial Intelligence (AI), and emphasised the need for focused discussion on the implications for education and market readiness.
The Chairperson thanked Members for their participation throughout the day, noted that the agenda remained incomplete and indicated that the meeting would continue, likely on Thursday of the following week, at which time the minutes would be adopted during oversight.
Meeting adjourned.
