Meeting Summary
‘Addressing Contemporary Challenges in the SANDF’ by Dr Moses B. Khanyile, Director: Centre for Military Science (CEMIS) and Director of Defence, Artificial Intelligence Research Unit, which is part of the Faculty of Military Science at Stellenbosch University (awaited document)
The Portfolio Committee on Defence and Military Veterans met for a round-table discussion with academic experts including the University of Stellenbosch’s Faculty of Military Science, including the Security Institute for Governance and Leadership in Africa and the Centre for Military Studies.
The presentations from academia focused on defence policy development after 1994, measures to improve the critical state of decline of the South African National Defence Force (SANDF), the need for military discipline, and the maintenance of sound democratic civil-military relations. The submissions also provided an overview of post-1994 parliamentary defence oversight, a vital contributor to strong civil-military relations. They also highlighted several pitfalls to be avoided during the 7th Parliament.
The Committee expressed alarm that some previous Parliaments had allowed ministers to avoid answering questions posed by Members of Parliament, with defence committees often permitting the Department to ignore questions during committee meetings.
The Committee voiced serious concerns about the deteriorating state of the SANDF, noting that the Defence Force’s mandate was misaligned with its budget, leading to its potential collapse. The Committee also noted observations by the academics around the increasing domestic use of the SANDF, stating that while it can be used for good effect, prolonged deployments may become problematic in some instances.
The Committee said, “For defence committees to be effective, they must clearly define their expectations from the department and ensure that those are met.” The Committee also noted that while the shrinking budget contributes to a declining force, further interventions and corrective measures in the SANDF were also required. Another concern was that the Secretary for Defence has no operational authority over SANDF yet remains accountable for its assets under the Public Finance Management Act. This has implications for consequence management.
The Committee resolved that new legislation must be introduced to strengthen and empower the Office of the Military Ombud and cement its authority and capabilities. The Committee concluded that it envisages regular engagements with sector specialists from academia to inform oversight in the 7th Parliament.

Looking back: Pitfalls in post-94 Defence Oversight
He then turned the floor over to Dr Evert Jordaan, a lecturer in the Department of Strategic Studies at the Faculty of Military Science Stellenbosch University, to present his findings titled: Looking back: Pitfalls in post-94 defence oversight.
The aim of the presentation was to provide an overview of defence oversight in South Africa since 1994.
Key points made established within the presentation were that political oversight is at the heart of civil-military relations in a democracy; military change cannot occur without political direction, tough decisions and interventions and bureaucracies/militaries do not change themselves therefore, politicians must apply deliberate and consistent pressure. Without political effectiveness, all other types of effectiveness are endangered. As such, specific questions underpinning the political effectiveness of military organisations include:
1. To what extent can military organisations assure themselves a regular share of the national budget sufficient to meet their basic needs?
2. To what extent do military organisations have access to the industrial and technological resources necessary to produce the equipment needed?
3. To what extent do military organisations have access to manpower in the required quantity and quality?
Vision and Culture of the SANDF
The presentation showcased that there is no vision for how an affordable SANDF should look into supporting foreign policy, fulfilling its constitutional mandate, force design and capability trade-offs. From policy to budget, strategy and structure – there is no affordable SANDF military strategy and structure since democratisation. In addition, it highlighted that there is a “culture of distrust” within the SANDF: people are not trusted - thus over-reliance on bureaucratic processes to prevent irregularities, which creates disabling red tape to perform core functions such as procurement for operations.
Continuous civil-military and oversight pitfalls include:
- Ministers shielding generals from scrutiny in Parliament, especially on spending resource
- Insufficient level of transparency from the DOD into oversight
- PCD allowing the DOD to ignore questions at committee meetings
- Reluctance of the PCD to hold the Minister accountable
The presentation highlighted that (1) the SANDF/DOD conventional paradigm is outdated, (2) a new defence paradigm is only possible through open debate and civil-military interaction and (3) political direction and intervention are needed to align defence (structures, personnel & equipment) with budget realities.

1994-2015 – First Defence Review (1998), Strategic defence acquisition packages and the National Development Plan (NDP) adopted.
2015-2030 – Defence Review 2015 adopted by Parliament, Defence Review not implemented due to budget constraints and Shortfall in Compensation of Employees (CoE).
Post-2030 – Government has had difficulty achieving NDP objectives, which will mark the renewed focus on achieving objectives, which will not necessarily include defence.
Potential drivers of defence reforms
Internal drivers include - The Ministry, Secretary of Defence & Chief of SANDF taking responsibility, the SANDF engaging in a systematic process of self-correction, Strong leadership at all levels, and rejuvenation across structures. External drivers include: Defence matters forming part of the proposed National Dialogue and defence Industry Lekgotla, Defence-backed foreign policy needs to be revisited, critical decisions need to be made on the future of Armscor and Denel

The Military Legal System and the Military Ombud – Stellenbosch University
ProfMichelle Nel, lecturer in Criminal and Military law and the Law of Armed Conflict at the Faculty of Military Science, Stellenbosch University, then proceeded to report on the military legal system and the Military Ombud.
The Office of the Military Ombuds (MO) was established in 2012 by means of the Military Ombud Act 4 of 2012. In a media statement the day after the launch of the MO, SANDF indicated that MO would serve as “an oversight mechanism independent from the military command structures”, providing oversight and ensuring the military adheres to principles of good governance. With the aim of making the military accountable & responsive as “an instrument of civilian authority as embodied in the country’s Constitution.” The presentation highlighted that when reporting to Parliament, the Military Ombuds Act does not require the MO to report to Parliament. They must report to the Minister who must then table the report to Parliament. Yet, the Office of MO sees Committee as integral in supporting their oversight role due to the gaps left by legislation and court decisions.
Limited mandate in terms of section 4 of the Act:
- Office of the MO may investigate any written complaint lodged by:
- Any member of the SANDF pertaining to his/her conditions of service
- Any former member of SANDF regarding his/her conditions of service
- Any member of the public regarding any official conduct of a member of the SANDF
- Any person on behalf of a member
On the issue of receiving and investigating complaints, complaints by members of the SANDF, there was a narrow focus on matters pertaining to service conditions, relegating them to an extension of the DOD grievance process. In Financial year 2023/24 – conditions of service made up 89% of complaints and covered mostly service benefits, service termination, and placement/utilisation. The presentation highlighted that legislation does not explicitly provide for external jurisdiction of MO in this context. Currently, the interpretation of their mandate during external deployments is restrictive.
The most important aspects that require attention include:
- Institutional independence of the MO
- Clarity regarding their mandate, especially regarding extraterritorial jurisdiction
- Providing them with the authority to initiate their own investigations, thereby identifying systemic problems in the military and allowing them to address these concerns timeously.
- Providing the MO with powers to oversee the finalisation of their investigations, especially regarding implementation of recommendations.

Responses
Dr Jordaan started off by noting that it is important to look at the broad tendency of what tends to happen in young democratic states, in terms of defence oversight. Usually, politicians focus more on attaining civil control, but never spend political capital to find out to what extent the military is conforming and aligning itself with democratic principles and values. This can be classified as a general problem. He added that they had not seen the continuous pressure on the military to reform themselves in the inner workings of how it strategises and uses its resources effectively and sufficiently. Tackling the question of a continuation of the problems of the Parliamentary Defence Committee after 2014, he referred to the study done by Dr Janse van Rensburg, whereby he quantitatively looked at the number of questions that were answered, the number of Committee engagements and further issues tackled. His research indicated that there was a down-curve in engagement by the Committee.
Concerning the extent to which the DOD was allowed to ignore questions, he contended that during one particular engagement session, the opposition asked a question concerning finances. Neither the defence institution in question nor the Minister had the documents or knowledge required around the topic. On the issue of a reluctance to hold Ministers accountable, he referred to the 2013 Battle of Bangui. Concerning this matter, the Minister at the time, had no response to give. With this in mind, he then noted that neither the Minister or Deputy Minister during that period, contacted the DOD to go through outcomes and objectives envisaged. This is something the new administration should tackle so that unrealistic targets are not set, with all relevant role players being made fully aware of the limits to that which the Department can afford to spend.
In covering the question of what the Portfolio Committee on Defence and Military Veterans can do to fulfil its mandate, he noted that there is a tendency by both Committees attached to Defence, to not initiate policy. It is assumed that the executive and the Department are to initiate policy. He stressed that this can be rectified as nothing restricts any Member of this Committee from initiating with Parliament. He suggested that though it might seem a minor issue for MPs, it could make a huge difference in tackling military affairs and challenges. He noted that there had been a scenario where the DOD was involved in a policy-making strategy. The Department sourced MPs to deliberate on matters in Parliament tackling a defence review. This session included relevant role-players and experts in respective fields.
The outcome of this was the DOD developing an accurate costing model attached to cost policy. He concluded by suggesting that the responsibility of this Committee is to correctly utilise this model and to familiarise themselves with the extent to which policy can create change within the defence domain.
Dr Khanyile contended that it is never an easy exercise to deliberate defence programmes, with discussions around oversight being harder. He noted that there is a danger in the contextualisation of oversight in terms of how deep you venture into the bureaucracy. He stressed that the Committee has the right to exercise oversight up to a particular level. He argued that though we are in a democratic dispensation, it is important for the Committee to be cognisant of the level below which it would not like to venture. This is of importance as not knowing your limitations can confuse command and control and add multiple and conflicting messaging, in the up and down flow of information. He added that it is critical for this Committee to understand which information – and its level of detail – is needed, and from which role-players. He then alluded to the Minister of Defence and Military Veterans, the Accounting Officer of the Department and the Chief of the SANDF.
Around the question on the Military Ombuds, he noted that Prof Nel would deliberate more on the issue, but stated that due to the South African Defence force being reconfigured in so many ways, seen as an equivalent of a Public Protector, the Military Ombuds monitors the benefits, service conditions and the rights of troupes. It is evident that from an institutional perspective, the military has been supported. The concern, rightfully pointed out, is that the tasks assigned to the Defence Force, far outweigh its budget. Planned and unplanned operation costs do not align with the allocation given and as a result, the Defence Force has been crippled to a point where minor aspects such as uniform, state of buildings and sufficiency of ammunition for training have become issues. He argued that the Committee should look into this area, as the nation cannot expect operational effectiveness when soldiers are ill-equipped and not provided for. Around the notion of the SANDF being in a state of collapse, he stressed that the situation is indeed dire but insisted that it becomes very tricky to put together a viable and balanced Defence Force that is combat-ready because if they do not have the equipment to do operations, it means you have a challenge with the Reserve personnel. He concluded by suggesting that if South Africans were to engage in war in multiple theatres of operation, the country would not have enough equipment, material or personnel in reserve, ready to tackle the challenges faced.
Prof Nel then stated that she did not wish to leave today’s deliberations with the impression that the Military Ombuds office is reluctant to make the critical changes they desperately need. In conducting research on the issue, her results showcased that the office is adamant they require rigorous change in mandate. She then reiterated that the Military Ombuds is very reliant on the Committee to ask the questions they cannot, in order to support them in their oversight role. She then referred to her study surrounding the Military Ombuds and noted that on one border control visit, troupes did not have proper food allocation, uniform or accommodation. These troupes were too afraid to create a written complaint, fearing how the Defence Force would treat them. It becomes an issue as the Military Ombuds does not have the legal ability to perform the investigation.
She asserted that the Military Ombuds has a good relationship with the Chief of the SANDF, with frequent discussions taking place to make him aware and mitigate the challenges. However, these processes can become more efficient if legislation allows for it. In light of answering the Chairperson's question about ways in which to stop the 60 000 deaths through crime, she stated that ultimately, the SANDF on its own is not the solution. It is going to be critical to get all role-players on board, such as SAPS, Border Management and particularly the Criminal Justice System, as criminals are not fearful of the consequences, or lack of, for these crimes committed. She concluded by suggesting that what she found in the literature, is that Departments and various role-players work in silence and do not share intelligence. This mindset needs to change to establish the type of Defence Force needed in the country and to facilitate more roles and objectives for the SANDF to accomplish.
Further Discussions
Mr C Hattingh (DA) then made the point that opinions should be kept aside when it is time for questioning, as that restricts the time allocated for other Members to pose their respective questions.
Mr M Hala (ANC) had no question to pose, but commented that as government, the ball has been dropped. This is due to the fact that government has civilians in Ministerial positions in Defence. He declared that today’s presentations confirmed that the sustainability issues our military is challenged with, undoubtedly highlight that the Defence Force is in a crisis.
Ms T Sokanyile (ANC) started by dissecting an issue in Dr Khanyile’s presentation. Around the aspect of SANDF Chiefs always coming from the Army and being male, she argued that there are females who have been trained to do this job effectively, and that the SANDF should consider females for roles as esteemed as this in the future. She noted that a report will assist in oversight of the DOD. She suggested that Ministers and Departments need to come together to discuss the recommendations given in the 2015 Defence Force review. She concluded by questioning if there is a report dealing with research and intelligence, from the military itself. If not, are oversight Committees allowed to ask for these reports?
Mr W Plaatjies (ANC) questioned what the implications are, on the military, when the Secretary of Defence is a civilian and the Chief of Defence being a military operative. He noted that this has become an issue when you have cases of Secretaries and HOD’s having less authority than those working under them. He requested clarity regarding the mandate around the military, as it is his understanding that it derives from the Constitution.
Mr Hattingh referred to Dr Jordaan’s presentation, which showcased the decline of the efficiency of the Portfolio Committee in keeping the defence force accountable. As he stopped at the period of 2014, he would like to understand if the decline continued, stabilised or improved? In referring to Dr Khanyile’s repeated usage of the phrase “total collapse”, he noted that with the loss of the country's maritime recognisances and surveillance capacity and capabilities and air logistics capabilities, should MPs now go as far as to say that the Defence Force is in fact in a state of “total collapse”?
Mr M Ntshingila (MK) highlighted that if the mandate is not being met, then that means the SANDF is in a state or process of collapse. He noted that the structure itself, needs a review to check if sufficient resources are required to implement the defence strategy and assist in meeting targets. He suggested that there needs to be certainty in hiring the right personnel. From a stakeholder relation, research and development perspective, he wished for continuous strengthening of the relationship with academics as the Defence Force will require monitoring and evaluation from all parties. An example of this, could be the Defence Technology Programme, which will require academic insight to look at the advancement of weaponry within the SA Army and Marine Corps. He suggested that further engagement with academics will be essential for the Committee to do justice with today’s key points.
Mr M Shelembe (DA) highlighted the issue of appointing members with no military background into key positions within the SANDF. He noted that these positions should be filled based on merit, and if the individual is fit to do the role and responsibly required.
On the question of whether the Portfolio Committee declined after 2014, Dr Jordaan referred to the research done by Dr Janse van Rensburg, showcasing that there was a definite decline. Around the issue of the impact of having a civilian in the role of Defence Secretary, he noted that one of the biggest concerns is when the Secretary for Defence is the HOD. He or she is then mainly responsible for being an accounting officer and has to report on activities, expenditure and personnel audits. This person does not have command or control over the troupes. He then made an example of a recent Secretary of Defence having to account as to why the DOD does not have an asset register, but cannot ask military personnel to make changes in order to close communication gaps. As it has to be reported to the Chief of the SANDF, this shows that the SANDF is needed to rectify challenges. He then concluded by stating that there is a tendency amongst politicians that all mandates, all goals of foreign policy, and diplomatic recognition must be met, but these steps do not align with the finances and resources allocated.
Dr Khanyile looked at the issue of separation of powers between the Secretary of Defence and the Chief of the SANDF, and noted that during the defence review process, there was a lot of discussion around who is to be the head of the Department as required by the Public Finance Management Act. Different models were developed. However, there was an issue of civilian oversight, particularly regarding whether oversight starts with the HOD or with the Minister of Defence. This matter was resolved, with the Secretary of Defence occupying this role. He reiterated that the Secretary has all the powers, in terms of the PFMA, those powers are delegated in a formal manner to the Chief of the SANDF.
When the Chief of the SANDF has to account on certain matters, there is no dispute, as he can then delegate roles to his respective members. He did however note that the response process is quite tricky and long. Regarding the issue of the mandate, he noted that there are two types of mandates that the SANDF has. The primary tasks are articulated in the Constitution (defending the country and its territories), and then the secondary tasks deal with instances of national disasters and supporting other Departments and SAPS. On supporting SAPS, he highlighted that the Committee take into consideration that the SANDF does not just get involved, it gets invited. SAPS has to declare that a situation is beyond its control and, therefore, needs additional capacity, which has to be processed by the Commander in Chief.
Also, the SANDF cannot operate on its own without the presence of the police. However, if there is an officially declared state of national disaster, or state of emergency as stated in the Constitution, the SANDF acquires ‘peace officer’ status, meaning they can also effect arrests in the same manner in which police do. The SAPS, SANDF and all other security structures are part of the Justice, Crime Prevention and Security cluster. If there are instances of policy clashes, Directors-General will discuss matters which are then taken to the JCPS cluster at ministerial levels. He spoke on the collapse of the SANDF. He noted that the defence review, which was approved by Parliament in 2015, already spoke about the decline, which is a critical element in the SANDF strategy. This was highlighted due to equipment not being renewed and replaced at that time.
He then asserted that it was assumed that the budget allocation within the defence review, would be closer to 2% of the GDP, which is a national norm for a sustainable defence force. He noted that if you stripped away the Arms Deal and various packages, the budget allocation stands at less than 0.5 % of the GDP. In reality, this means that the SANDF has been going backwards instead of forward, as it cannot renew, replace or rejuvenate equipment and structures. He highlighted that the Military Skills Development Initiatives, now have to be conducted every two years, as opposed to every year, as there is not enough funding. He said the issue of collapse is very much real. He added that it is not an absolute requirement for a Minister or DM of Defence to have a military background, but that the person fulfils more important requirements. He argued that one can only improvise up to a point. He expressed his concern that the SANDF has gone beyond its threshold, with its resources and budget allocation already at a bare minimum. To conclude, on the Defence Artificial Intelligence Research Unit, and if such a report would be coming to MPs, he stressed that his delegation is ready, with a conference scheduled in the upcoming months. Engagement has already occurred with international members within the AI space concerning military affairs.
Mr R Cebekhulu (IFP) noted a point on the use of external foreign companies when doing research and development. He questioned if we, as a young democracy, should allow this to transpire within our nation. The other point he made concerned the issue of lawlessness within the country. In instances of police getting attacked, should the SANDF use alternative measures and procedures to combat this, in line with the mandate?
Prof Francois Vrey, Emeritus Professor of Military Science at Stellenbosch University and current research co-ordinator of SIGLA, expressed his belief that the South African society deserves proper defence service delivery. Primarily from the SANDF and from leadership from the political level, the DOD supported by this Committee and by academia, will have the necessary tools to impact this delivery. He noted that academic interaction is one way in which research can be communicated professionally and acceptably to Parliament. He reiterated that the first few presentations were very much indicative, raising the perceptions of a downward curve in terms of the SANDF and quality of oversight. Prof Nel’s presentation showcased the constitutional imperative, legal environment, and role of the Military Ombud, while Prof Heinecken looked at the matters that might interest this Committee.
Prof Vrey then stressed that this Committee has a critical role to play in the advancement of Civil-Military relations within the country and expressed his belief that this Committee must be the bastion to ensuring democratic values are met and the country heads in the right direction in terms of how oversight of the military should be executed. On the question that Mr Cebekhulu asked, he noted that in the mandate of the SANDF and the military, one would see that there are priorities. What he picked up on within the presentations, is that secondary roles/ priorities have begun to climb the agenda list. However, he argued that other departments, who play roles in intelligence, looking at social fabric of the country, for policing and driving back poverty, have stepped away from hostile situations, leaving the Defence Force to step in an attempt to fill the void.
Prof Solomon thanked all Members for allowing academia to present, and requested that future engagements certainly take place.
The Chairperson proclaimed that today’s deliberation was critical as it dealt with national interests and that the collaboration with academia has assisted Members of Parliament tremendously. He noted that the DOD is generally looked down upon, but, in fact, is a primary department within the nation. He mentioned that if a government is not able to correctly and efficiently defend its people, this becomes a huge concern and somewhat of a crisis. He therefore suggested that academia continuously assist in matters pertaining to the JCPS, to improve the manner in which troupes are able to operate. The issue of intelligence must also be further looked at as it becomes concerning to the local police force.
On Artificial Intelligence, the Chairperson highlighted that all South Africans are vulnerable to cyber-security crimes. He then suggested that it is important to research and develop collaboration on this issue. Noting that there is no proper legislation that can equip South Africans to combat safety and security, the Chairperson reiterated that in terms of Section 200 Subsection (2) of the Constitution, as a Parliament and as a government, they need to ensure the protection and defence of citizens.
In the last national dialogue, the Chairperson noted that it was fundamental to respective entities that the country cannot continue to rot in the manner it currently is. He stressed that it is upon this generation to prevent further decline within the military and to ensure that future generations experience a safer South Africa, with sufficient resources being granted to all citizens. The Chairperson thanked all MPs and members from academia, both present and virtually.
Dr Janse van Rensburg added that a short survey will be presented to MPs concerning today’s engagement and the induction period the Committee has had, to correctly and efficiently schedule future engagements.
The consideration for outstanding minutes was postponed until further notice.
The meeting was adjourned.
