Meeting Summary
Video
Department of Transport and the Road Traffic Management Corporation briefed the Committee on the Festive Season Road Safety Plan, and the Department’s 2024/25 Second Quarter Expenditure Report.
The Committee welcomed the Festive Season Plan on road safety. It was data-driven and comprehensive in ways previous festive season plans had not been. The Committee was pleased with the 24/7 deployment of traffic officers, especially in hotspots, and all efforts to curb road accidents and fatalities.
The Committee pledged its support for the Festive Season Plan and indicated that they would issue a public statement to express their support and encourage citizens to adhere to road rules and drive safely. It was emphasised that road safety was a societal issue.
The Committee asked how Department planned to use the experience and lessons learned during the festive season to inform the National Road Safety Plan. The Committee urged the Department and the Corporation to bolster their technological capabilities to use real-time data and statistics, diminish the corruption and bribery of traffic officials, and tackle unsafe driving behaviours such as speeding and illegal lane changes.
The Committee wished the Department and the Corporation luck in implementing the Festive Season Plan.
Members were concerned that the Department had failed to meet some critical performance targets specified for the Second Quarter, particularly in terms of Integrated Transport Planning.
The Committee was pleased that the Department had resolved 97% of reported corruption cases. However, Members asked what was meant by ‘resolved’ and what the outcomes of the cases were.
Why were challenges still experienced in payment of invoices within 30-days? The Committee had previously raised this issue, and while the Department had put mechanisms in place to address it, the issue persisted.
The Committee asked about the lack of progress in the taxi recapitalisation programme. The Committee urged the Department to relook at the cost of scrapping and potentially increase subsidies to encourage taxi owners to participate.

Deputy Minister’s Remarks
Mr
Mkhuleko
Hlengwa, Deputy Minister of Transport, said Department and RTMC had come before the Committee to present the 2024 Festive Season Road Safety Plan. The Department and RTMC had undertaken a process of consultations with the various provinces and other stakeholders to consolidate the plan.
This was not the National Road Safety Plan; it was a focused area for the duration of the festive season. The Department felt it was important to have a dedicated effort in this regard, considering that 10% of annual road fatalities occurred during the festive season. There was an ‘urban-rural migration’ that took place during this time.
The festive season was marked by a sharp increase in road usage and tragically, a corresponding rise in accidents and fatalities. This dual reality demanded a targeted, data-driven and collaborative approach to road safety. The Department was happy with the collaboration with the RTMC and other stakeholders. The Department was ready to roll out the Safety Plan.
One of the most pressing threats to road safety in South Africa was the dangerous intersection of alcohol consumption and road use. According to the RTMC’s studies, alcohol-related accidents contributed to 27% of fatalities annually. 58% of drivers in fatal accidents tested positive for alcohol. These alarming statistics highlighted the urgent need for strict enforcement and a robust public education campaign to address this crisis directly. According to the World Health Organisation (WHO), South Africa was fifth in the world for alcohol consumption. This required particular action. Another growing and concerning trend was the use of cell phones while driving, particularly amongst younger drivers and ‘influencers’. This included texting, engaging on social media and recording content for online platforms. These behaviours significantly impair a driver’s attention, reaction time and decision-making, all in a bid to trend or gain likes and views. This was a dangerous and reckless reality. Studies showed that texting while driving increased the risk of accidents by 23 times, because drivers became blind to the road at an average of five seconds per text. Recording content while driving, such as livestreaming or creating videos, added additional layers of distraction and diverted mental and physical focus from the road. The dangers posed by such practices cannot be overstated. Distracted driving was quickly becoming a leading contributor to road accidents in South Africa. Addressing this issue was one of the key areas of this campaign.
The presentation would indicate that RTMC was focusing on high priority roads. It would provide a detailed overview of how the strategy aligned with the national priorities and international commitments. The presentation would also address the critical risk factors, such as speeding, unroadworthy vehicles and distracted driving, while emphasising the need for a culture of responsibility and accountability amongst road users.
Road safety was not solely a responsibility of government – it was a shared societal mandate. By uniting all sectors, government, civil society, faith-based organisations, the transport public sector, and law enforcement, it could be ensured that the festive season was both joyful and safe.
RTMC Festive Season Road Safety Plan
Ms Nomusa Mufamadi, Chairperson, RTMC, said that during the approach to the festive season it was important to always remember the attendant problems which were experienced on the road. The higher traffic volumes on the roads often led to unhappy incidents and accidents. This could and should be avoided by effectively enforcing road safety laws, while encouraging responsible behaviour on the roads.
RTMC would provide an overview of the plan which intended to keep the lives of road users safe during the festive season.
Adv Makhosini Msibi, Chief Executive Officer (CEO), RTMC, presented the ‘reimagined’ Festive Road Safety Plan. [Please see the electronic slides for full details]. This was the first time this plan had been underpinned by a number of pillars founded in a scientific, outcome-based approach. This approach was data-driven. Indicators were set to ensure that the impact and success of the plan were evaluated post-festive season. RTMC engaged with research in terms of predictive and intelligent policing deployment. This meant that police would not be deployed sporadically, but rather in targeted areas. This was a collaborative effort with provinces, municipalities, stakeholders, and other public entities. RTMC said the priority deployment was the beginning of a shared mobilisation and vision with the provinces and municipalities.
RTMC adopted project EDWARD – Every Day Without A Road Death. This would play a critical role in the Festive Season Road Safety Plan. The objectives of this project were to reduce road fatalities and injuries, increase public awareness of road safety, foster stakeholder collaboration, and use data to guide strategies.
The presentation highlighted critical dates and events during the festive season, including public holidays, long weekends and events taking place. These dates coincided with the days when road usage was expected to spike and would be prioritised.
RTMC outlined the deployment methodology for the following periods: pre-festive (1-31 November), during festive (1-13 December), and during and post-festive (13 December 2024 – 15 January 2025). They covered the activities and strategy for each period. RTMC would conduct weekly monitoring of alcohol sales figures and drunk driving arrests, documenting all crashes where alcohol was a contributing factor, and documenting crashes during rainy days and the impact thereof.
The presentation highlighted the top 20 critical routes, as well as provincial deployments. “Provinces have raised the challenge of non-deployment’s post 22:00 with labour unions”.
Deployment plans would be monitored by the provinces and municipalities. The Chief Directors and Law Enforcement Operational Directors would form part of the 24/7 deployment monitoring. The chairperson of the Corridors would also conduct daily review meetings, and there would be morning meetings to monitor progress. The RTMC Inspectorate Unit would play an oversight role.

Response
The Deputy Minister responded to the question of why the Festive Season Plan had an RTMC letterhead. The RTMC was an entity of the Department. In its shareholder representation, it was inclusive of provinces. The RTMC had the necessary skills and expertise to drive the process. The Minister had directed that the RTMC lead the Festive Season Plan to ensure that from an expertise and practicality point of view that the plan could be consolidated. The Road Branch of the Department was in constant engagement with the RTMC. When the Department met with RTMC, RTMC had reported on behalf of the provinces. There was no disjuncture between the two. RTMC was an entity within the Department.
The Deputy Minister said there had been a marginal positive outcome [from past road safety campaigns]. Last year, there had been a 4.4% decrease compared to the previous year. The goal was to have zero deaths. It was necessary to look at the outer reality, which was what was the current worst-case scenario. The project was a work in progress. The Department and government had to confront the reality that the roads were fatality-prone. What intervention could be done to deal with this? The Festive Season Plan sought to do this, to shift from the worst-case scenario to the desired reality – zero deaths. Slide 4 on the EDWARD project spoke to this desired reality. Whilst there were targets to reduce fatalities, the Department did not think it was good that any life be lost. There were gaps. The Department would be reviewing the National Road Safety Strategy. Many of the issues raised by Members would guide the Department in the review process. It was clear that more had to be done during the Festive Season. The experiences that would emerge from the reimagined Festive Season Plan would inform reviewing the National Road Safety Plan.
Mobile phone apps have pros and cons. There was an app called Waze that allowed drivers to plot roadblocks for other users to see and then divert away from the roadblock. This was counterproductive. He felt it bordered on illegality. Apps like these were a contributing factor to people diverting away from consequences for their actions. While apps could also assist in dealing with congestion, the ability to plot roadblocks, cameras and traffic officers was problematic. This was the same as when road users flicked lights at each other in a warning. The Department and RTMC would not divulge all the details of the plan because this would inform road users of roadblocks and other strategies and could render the efforts ineffective.
Prosecutors could not be on the scene [of traffic offences]. There could not be expedited prosecution for road offenders. It was not a special crime. It would be a waste of resources. Offenders would remain behind bars until the next available opportunity to appear before the court.
The issues around basic education were receiving attention. The Department had met with the Minister of Basic Education. The review of the National Road Safety Plan would reveal that there were 200 days of heightened traffic on the roads because of the education ecosystem, particularly during the peak times in the morning and afternoon. This was linked to the scholar transport issue. The Department would appear before the Committee to report on this at a later date. Holidays were linked to education as well because this was the time when families travelled. He had asked the Minister of Basic Education to consider including road safety education in the life orientation curriculum. This would increase the scope of the awareness campaign as all learners would be exposed to the issues of road safety. When a driver flicked their lights on the road, this was not something the Department could control – it was citizen irresponsibility. Heightened messaging had to be done.
On the issue of ‘cold drinks’, there had been a review of the curriculum used in law enforcement, from National Qualifications Framework (NQF) 4 to NQF 6. Trainee law enforcement officers would be exposed to the ethical realities of their work. Higher NQF levels would increase renumeration, which could deter the officers from accepting products in exchange for no fines or arrests. It had to be understood that it was corruption that was citizenry-based, without absolving the law enforcement officers who were recipients of ‘cold drinks.’ It was clear that when citizens flicked their lights on the roads, shared roadblocks on apps or social media, and gave officers ‘cold drinks’ they were contributing to the issue. These issues had to be discussed as part of the awareness campaign. Citizens should be encouraged not to participate in such activities.
The technological issues highlighted by Members would inform the work that needed to be done. There needed to be a move toward body cameras for law enforcement officers. The RTMC had a project to roll out digitisation of testing centres for roadworthiness, as part of building technological capability. Moving forward, cameras should be linked to servers, which would allow the Department to question why officers have not stopped offending drivers. The technological thinking was there. He understood the comments of the Members. The aim for the festive season was to reduce road accidents and fatalities and promote safe driving behaviours, as well as gain experience for the continued road safety agenda.
Ms Mufamadi said that the questions raised went back to the issue of collaboration with stakeholders. For instance, there was an issue about liquor outlets. This was not something that could be done alone; RTMC would have to work with the liquor boards that had power over the outlets. Working together would aid success. When RTMC was able to begin implementing body cams, it would assist in reducing the ‘cold drink’ issues. RTMC was trying to alleviate these issues, which negatively affected its efforts.

RTMC has resuscitated the technical committee between the Director of Public Prosecutions (DPP) and the Department of Justice to place the reclassification of offences back on the agenda. It was tabled to reclassify reckless and negligent driving and drunk driving as applicable in terms of Schedule 5 of the Criminal Procedure Act. People were of the view that they could drive over the speed limit and immediately qualify for bail. Other offences above Schedule 5 had a waiting period of seven days. RTMC had been assisted by the Department in this regard. The collapse of the technical team was simply because the senior public prosecutors were assigned very difficult cases. Research has shown that other countries have reclassified spiking.
There had been questions on the liquor boards and liquor industry. RTMC had engaged with South African Breweries (SAB), which had come on board voluntarily in terms of messaging and funding road safety initiatives. Progress was being made with other institutions. The tourism industry has also come on board. There was willingness from these industries. RTMC was not tampering with the businesses of these industries, but rather [asserting] that these industries had to take responsibility. When their patrons imbibed (and were drunk), they should not be allowed to drive. There are alternatives to driving drunk. The taxi industry was willing to be on-call in the event that people drank and would need rides. There continued to be issues with events managers. It seemed that everyone had become an event manager. This gap would be addressed following the festive season. Before municipalities approve events, there should be an undertaking in terms of road safety. In terms of the Chris Brown concert, RTMC was engaging with the city.
Adv Msibi said that deployment would include undercover officers. This would enable RTMC and the Department to address officers who were involved in corrupt activities.
Mr B Hlabisa, Deputy Director-General (DDG): Road Transport, DoT, responded to the concerns regarding safety and street lighting, particularly on the R71. SANRAL’s engineering unit had informed him that there were plans to address the lighting issues experienced on the R71 and R81 to make it safer for road users. Budgeting for this was scheduled for 2026/27. This was later than people would have liked, however it was a result of the stretched budget.
Adv Msibi responded to the question regarding trucks on the road during the festive season. The Minister introduced a limitation on the timing of the trucks, and there was a massive rejection from the business sector. This necessitated further engagement. The Minister proposed that from 6pm-6am, there should be no trucks on the roads. The business sector has requested further engagement.
The Deputy Minister said correspondence on the 30% exemption regarding overtime had been sent to the DPSA the previous week. The Department was confident of a favourable response to allow for 24/7 deployment. There was a problem beyond the perception of Polo drivers. This problem was the entertainment-heavy economy that South Africa had. By consequence, pre- and post-these events were the behaviours and conducts which took place on the roads. When engaging the taxi industry, the Committee would become aware of ‘Ingezo’ which sought to showcase pimped up Quantum’s. This was to show that these vehicles could be taken care of and taken to a certain standard. The fundamental issue was the pre- and post-activities of drivers in terms of entertainment. There should be a condition that these events should have a transportation plan. He did not want the government to be a ‘nanny state.’ Government had to insist on driver responsibility and accountability. This was non-negotiable. The consequence management around this is what the Department and government should focus on. The ideas and issues that had been raised were important. The Polo issue was not just about driving, but also about the criminality linked to it, such as the high levels of hijacking and theft linked to Polos. The Department was not the driver or the passengers. The Department’s role was to create a conducive and enabling environment for drivers and passengers, interact with law enforcement when there were challenges, and ensure responsibility and accountability for all road users.
Adv Msibi responded to the question regarding engagement with metros and municipalities on 24/7 deployment. RTMC had engaged and consulted with all chiefs of the Metro Police and the metros. The only limitation with the metros, except for eThekwini, was that most of the metros’ deployment would end at 1am and there would be a gap between 1am and 4am. RTMC continued to try and pursue the metros to extend this. However, where it was critical, there would be officials available to deal with anything that arose. This was again due to the stringent budget constraints being experienced. All metros participated in the process of rolling out the Festive Season Plan.
RTMC was improving on the matter of AI. There were huge limitations due to the financial aspect. Even aspects such as body cams necessitated a stretch in the budget.
There had been a comment on the NTP. The shareholders, led by the Minister and Deputy Minister, have made the decision that provinces should collaborate with RTMC in the piloting process. The Road Traffic Act and Schedules Four and Five had concurrent functions, thus there had to be collaboration with provinces. Some provinces were not ready, however others were indicating that they were ready to begin the pilot process. For now, it was on an
ad hoc
basis, but ultimately, it would be rolled out to provinces. In the engagements, RTMC had been supported by labour formations.
The Chairperson thanked RTMC and the Department for their engagement and responses. The Deputy Minister, DDG, CEO and Chairperson of RTMC would leave the meeting knowing they had presented before Parliament and the public. A comprehensive strategy had been presented. It may be the best Festive Season Plan yet. The plan was comprehensive and touched on all aspects, including law enforcement, and collaborations with various institutions. Road safety was a societal issue and had to be prioritised by all. The Committee welcomed the interventions that were being made, including 24/7 deployment. He extended gratitude to the officers that would be deployed to the roads. In terms of lighting on the roads, this was a very important issue and many of the roads highlighted as hotspots had lighting issues. There was a high number of pedestrian deaths where roads passed through human settlements. Along the R37, where it passed through villages, was where most people were dying. Lighting on these roads was very important. The Committee and Parliament would extend support to the Department and RTMC regarding the Festive Season Plan. The Committee supported the strategy. He called upon the people of South Africa to rally and support the strategy. The Committee would formally issue a statement to convey that the Committee was happy with the Festive Season Plan and that the public should support the strategy. This would also be conveyed in the Chair of Chair meetings, and it would be requested that all chairpersons request their members’ support for the plan. The festive season was a period where many lives were lost.
There were two important issues that the Committee would discuss. One was the issue of the taxi and bus operators. When it was indicated that the Department would present the Festive Season Road Safety Plan, there was a call expressing concern about taxi operators. The Committee had asked for a formal written request. This has not been received. Should it warrant it, the Committee would call the Department to engage on the matter virtually. The issues that could be raised may have a bearing on the strategy. The Committee would support the Department and RTMC in the implementation of the Road Safety Plan. It was also possible that members of the Committee would join the Department and RTMC on some campaigns during the festive season, when there was availability. This Festive Season Plan was not only the responsibility of the Department and RTMC, it was the priority of Parliament and all citizens.
DoT 2024/25 Second Quarter Expenditure Report
The Chairperson invited the Department to present its report on 2024/25 Second Quarter Expenditure. He excused RTMC from the meeting but indicated they could stay, if they wished.
Ms Hlengiwe Ngwenya, Director: Strategic Planning and Monitoring, DoT, said that during quarter two, the Department achieved 40 out of 57 targets – 70%. This was down 1% from quarter one. [Please see the electronic slides for full details].
Programme 1: Administration. The Department resolved 97% of reported incidents of corruption. Additionally, the Department managed 179 bursaries at the end of quarter one including 14 officials who completed their qualifications. The Department achieved a 100% reduction in new cases of irregular and unauthorised expenditure.
Programme 2: Integrated Transport Planning. The Department achieved 30% of its performance targets.
Programme 3: Rail Transport. The Department achieved 80% of its performance targets.
Programme 4: Road Transport. The Department achieved 70% of its performance targets.
The Department spent R39.923 billion (50% of its current budget). Less spending than anticipated had been realised on compensation of employees, goods and services, and transfers and subsidies, however spending on payments for capital assets and financial assets was above estimates.

Discussion
The Chairperson thanked the Department for the presentation and invited members to share questions and comments.
Ms Nkopane asked what the root causes were of the Department not meeting its quarter two targets. The Department was unable to perform on almost one third of its targets. Quarter two targets had been brought down by the performance of the civil aviation programme and the integrated transport programme. This was also an issue in quarter one. What was the Department doing to correct the shortcomings of these programmes?
She appreciated the 97% performance on corruption cases resolved. By when could the Committee expect the outstanding cases to be resolved? How did the bursary scheme work? Was the Department able to recruit from the pool of bursary recipients as interns or in permanent positions? She understood the non-employee beneficiaries of bursaries, but what was the case internally? To what extent had the Department's vacancies negatively impacted the administration programme to enable the Department to improve its performance? How would the Department fix this issue? What was affecting the [poor, 30%] performance of integrated transport planning? This programme was not able to meet its performance target. This was extremely worrying.
Mr Gana referred to the three adjustments to the programme – in slide 67. Money had been allocated to the taxi empowerment project for a total of R80 million. There was a once-off taxi gratuity of R300 million. There was money that has moved from SANRAL to the Driving Licence Account to the value of R133 million. He requested an explanation of this. The response could be provided in writing. These movements of money had to be explained. He felt that sufficient details had not been provided.
Mr Mabhena referred to the 30-day invoice settlement [requirement]. There had been an issue like this previously that had been condemned by the Committee. There was a lot of clumsiness in the response. He congratulated the DG regarding consequence management in terms of settling invoices within 30 days. Yet the same issue had arisen again. What happened to the consequence management? Who were the culprits? He requested that the Department provide a report on who the culprits were. This issue was causing tremendous problems for small businesses. This was a major issue because small businesses were a great source of employment, and if invoices were not paid, they would add to the high level of unemployment. The Department should work to ensure that actions put in place were consistent and ensure that it did not default on prior commitments. He had previously asked for the Department’s definition of consequence management – what actions were being taken? What were the consequences? The report stated that 97% of all corruption incidents reported had been resolved. What did this mean? Were people fired or arrested? What were the outcomes of these incidents? 3% was not resolved. It was indicated that outside assistance had been used in terms of investigations. What forensic companies had done the investigations? What were the findings?
He raised the issue of drivers’ licenses being received within 25 days. This issue had been discussed in the previous meeting. It was concluded that this would not be possible at present. The Chief Financial Officer (CFO) had indicated that the taxi recapitalisation programme was demand driven and therefore movement of money should be expected. Would movement of money from taxi empowerment to other programmes be seen in the next report? The demand from taxi recapitalisation was low and money was moved to taxi empowerment. Was progress being made? Some taxis were not in the position to be scrapped and there was a cut-off date for which taxis could participate. Could the criteria be adjusted to encourage uptake of the programme? Increasing the amount of money per taxi could entice uptake.
Mr Malematja said there were delays in many important policies and legislation which required monitoring to ensure the second quarter targets were met. The public transport subsidy was critical for easing the burden of public transport costs for working-class and poor families. The issue of recurring invoices was a serious concern. This issue had been discussed by the Committee previously and it could not be tolerated. What were the reasons for this?
Response
Ms Ngwenya discussed the root cause of challenges in performance. When it came to finishing targets, particularly in terms of policy and legislation, a big challenge was dependency [on other processes] outside of the Department’s control. The presentation indicated that seven targets had not been achieved in Integrated Transport Planning. These targets were linked to the establishment of the Transport Economic Regulator. This was dependent on the President signing the Economic Regulation of Transport Act into law [and its commencement date being set]. In terms of the civil aviation targets, the policy target had not been achieved. Written responses would be provided to the questions about the bursary scheme and the resolution of reported corruption cases.
The Department had amended its Annual Performance Plan (APP) because of the delays of policy and legislation. This was due to the change in administration. The Department had taken the opportunity to address the areas where there were dependencies outside of the Department’s control. The Department had set realistic targets for the remainder of the year. The Department was putting measures in place to try to fast-track the consultation processes internally and externally.
Mr Mokoto Matlala, CFO, DoT, responded to the question regarding the 30-day payments. It was fortunate that only one company had been affected, which was the company that offered travel services. This company contributed to the problem because of the processes they needed to follow. After-hours bookings were done telephonically, and when the invoice was submitted there was no evidence of approval of the travelling. This caused delays in payment. 99% of the invoices belonged to one company. The Department was trying to ensure the company was paid on time and engaging with the service provider on the issue.
Slide 56 provided a breakdown of the underspending. The first item was the compensation of employees and the underspending on this item was because of the vacant posts. When considering the amount of this particular underspending, it was only R1.2 million. Salaries were cumulative and some people received bonuses. The Department was very cautious about the item in terms of overspending. The Department had requested a budget reprioritisation of R10 million to ensure that the Department remained in budget. There was also underspending in terms of goods and services. There was a certain project where appointments were only made after the second quarter. Regarding the transfers and subsidies, the Department was awaiting an invoice from the entity that would align with the condition for which the allocation was made. The taxi recapitalisation project was demand-driven, if only a certain amount of requests were submitted, the Department would only deal with those requests.
Ms Tshitshi Phewa, DDG: Aviation, DoT, said that the one target that had been missed under the civil aviation programme spoke to the interdependencies. The processing of phase two of the Socio Economic Impact Assessment System (SEIAS) depended on the Department obtaining Cabinet approval, and publishing the policy. The Department had finished all of the Cabinet processes, however, when it got to the Cabinet Ministers’ Committee the item was withdrawn from the agenda. It had been felt that the policy had too many policy statements, it was requested that the Department repackage the policy statements. The Department has repackaged the policy, resubmitted it, and is awaiting a response.
The Chairperson asked if the Department intended to make up for this in the third quarter.
Ms Phewa said that this would depend on whether the repackaged Civil Aviation Policy was accepted by the Cabinet Ministers’ Committee.
Mr Mokonyama Mathabatha, DDG: Public Transport, DoT, said that two weeks before, the Department had presented the taxi recapitalisation project, the challenges and the way forward. The Department was being assisted by the World Bank. An important part of this was how the Department would make this a valuable capital subsidy to mitigate against the high vehicle prices, which was the deterrent [to participation by taxi owners]. This may include improving the scrapping allowance. The Revised Taxi Recapitalisation Programme (RTRP) included empowerment programmes and the Department has struggled to get the item classified on the budget. This has now been successful and the R80 million would be spent before the end of the financial year. The R300 million was part of the R700 million returned to government. The R300 million was budgeted for this year and the remaining R400 million was budgeted for the next financial year. In terms of the subsidy policy, one of the key stakeholders had legislated processes that had to be completed. The Department had hoped that it would have been completed in December, however, it may have to be extended by three months.
The 97% resolution of reported corruption cases did not necessarily mean that 97% of people were corrupt. If there were five cases, it would be verified, investigated, etc. 97% of reported cases had been attended to.
On bursaries, it was in the Department’s policy that the people who received bursaries would be able to become employed by the Department. Unfortunately, this also depended on the availability of positions. This measure has been relaxed and recipients could work elsewhere in the sector. The process of interns was a different process. The Department also had to advertise openly, so that people not part of the bursary scheme could become interns.
The Deputy Minister said the Department would make written submissions on the outstanding responses. Other matters would be expanded on when the Department met with the Committee again. He confirmed that the APP had been reviewed, consistent with the Medium-Term Development Plan, the 7
th
Administration, and the key priority areas announced by the President during the opening of Parliament. He did not think that the Minister had made a commitment on the issues raised by Mr Mabhena. What the Minister did commit to, was to look at the taxi recapitalisation programme. The critical area would be around de-risking, as the financial cost was problematic to owners. The Department was prepared and was looking at the necessary issues along the issue of re-risking. The Department would report back to the Committee once it had consolidated the issue.
Mr Mabhena referred to the 97% resolution of reported corruption cases. The DDG said that people had been attended to. What did this mean? Could a report be provided on who had been attended to?
The Deputy Minister said this would be responded to in writing.
The Chairperson noted this. He reminded the Deputy Minister that the Committee was still awaiting the progress report on the Driving License Card Account (DLCA).
The Deputy Minister said the matter was still with the Auditor-General (AG). The Minister would brief the Committee on the outcome as soon as the investigation was concluded.
The Chairperson said it had a bearing on one of the Department’s indicators.
