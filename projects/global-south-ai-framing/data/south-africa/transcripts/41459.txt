Meeting Summary
Video
The Standing Committee met with the Western Cape Provincial Treasury to consider its 2025 annual performance plan (APP) and its third and fourth quarter 2024/25 performance reports.
Treasury's presentation covered the Department’s strategic vision, which was framed around fairness in fiscal allocations, building trust through transparency and evidence-based reporting, ensuring value for money in procurement, and promoting innovation through alternative and blended financing mechanisms. The APP also set out statutory obligations such as publishing cash management reports, revenue management reports, and assessments of the provincial and local fiscal system, as well as initiatives to strengthen supply chain management, provide financial management support to municipalities, and fill critical staff vacancies.
In its quarterly performance reports, Treasury highlighted progress in areas such as capacity support to municipalities, supply chain management compliance monitoring, and the publication of required reports. However, challenges remained around resource constraints, delayed delivery of some support due to service provider availability, and persistent staffing shortages. The Head of Department and the Minister both stressed ongoing efforts to fill vacancies, although the volume of applications received had slowed recruitment processes.
Members raised concerns that gender-responsive budgeting was not reflected in targets or indicators, arguing that unmeasured activities could not be effectively monitored or resourced. Treasury was pressed on interventions in municipalities in financial distress, particularly Beaufort West, with Members pointing out that the issue was not visible in the APP or reports. The Committee also interrogated the impact of the new Preferential Procurement Act, with Treasury officials warning that its “one size fits all” approach would raise costs, undermine value for money, and disregard sector-specific transformation already achieved in industries such as learner transport. The disjuncture between the Act and the Public Finance Management Act was flagged as a serious concern that could weaken fiscal oversight.
Further questioning probed the use of external service providers, with Members expressing unease about reliance on individuals, and the absence of clear contingency plans when contractors were unable to deliver on time. Questions were also asked about how service providers were selected -- whether through panels or transversal contracts -- and what mechanisms existed to safeguard municipal support functions when delays occurred.
On fiscal policy, Members requested clarity on the five indicators linked to Programme Two, covering provincial and local fiscal research outputs, revenue management, cash management reports, monitoring of the Western Cape Gambling and Racing Board, and consolidated revenue retention reports. They emphasised the need for more qualitative reporting, not just quantitative indicators, to show whether outputs were contributing to improved municipal and provincial financial performance.
The Minister acknowledged that gender-responsive budgeting reporting could be strengthened, and undertook to improve the inclusion of qualitative elements in Treasury’s reporting. She explained the statutory framework for financial recovery plans, noting that while the Provincial Treasury could advise on targets, responsibility for outcomes lay with both national and provincial treasuries and the municipalities concerned. On procurement reforms, she cautioned that every rand spent on new compliance systems would reduce the budget available for service delivery.
The Chairperson concluded that the documentation presented was too thin, particularly in terms of financial detail, and fell short of the professional standard expected of APPs and quarterly reports. Members agreed that more comprehensive reporting was needed to avoid protracted questioning in meetings. Recommendations included requesting further briefings on financial interventions in municipalities, as well as a detailed presentation on the service providers used to support municipal compliance.
Meeting report
Minister’s opening remarks
Ms Deidré Baartman, Western Cape Minister of Finance, began by reflecting on the theme of "magic." She had recently had a conversation in which she was asked whether government could save everyone. In response, she recounted the story of a boy throwing stranded starfish back into the ocean one by one. Although the boy could not save them all, he could make a difference to each one he returned to the water. This story illustrated how the Western Cape Provincial Treasury approached its work. Although the challenges were vast, the Department sought to do the best it could with the resources and team available, and believed in the capacity of its people.
She said Treasury's work was underpinned by four values, three of which were drawn from the Constitution and law, with a fourth added by the Department itself.
The first was fairness, ensuring that legislation, revenue proposals, and fiscal frameworks were just and equitable, including fighting for the Western Cape’s fair share in the vertical and horizontal division of revenue in terms of section 214 of the Constitution.
The second was trust, which was fostered through the publication of credible and transparent data. She cited as examples the Provincial Economic Review and Outlook (PERO), the Municipal Economic Review and Outlook (MERO), and the first-ever Local Government Budget Performance Review published the previous year. These documents tracked operational and capital expenditure and supported municipalities in developing cost-reflective tariffs. She said trust was also built by capacitating municipalities through the Financial Management Grant to strengthen long-term financial management strategies.
The third value was value for money, which was achieved by making it easier to do business with government. Minister Baartman highlighted the work of the Procurement Client Centre, which provided training, webinars, query resolution, and the publication of procurement disclosure reports.
The fourth value was innovation, which she described as essential, given the current economic environment. She stressed that Treasury worked on the assumption that additional funds from National Treasury were unlikely, and therefore had to explore alternative and blended financing mechanisms. These included partnerships with the private sector, development banks, donor funding, philanthropists, hybrid capital, and bonds. This was why the Provincial Treasury had launched the alternative and blended financing framework, which sought to encourage innovative financing approaches across the provincial government.
The Minister added that in addition to these values, Treasury fulfilled statutory obligations through its financial reporting, cash management, and budget documentation, all of which supported service delivery indirectly by enabling departments and municipalities to function effectively. She explained that although Treasury was not always perceived as a service delivery Department, its role in capacitating and supporting departments was critical to achieving the broader provincial strategic plan goals of economic growth and job creation. She concluded by stating that she would hand over to her team to present the technical details of the annual performance plan (APP) and quarterly performance report. She invited Members to raise any items that required further clarification.
Provincial Treasury on its 2025/26 annual performance plan
Ms Naadia Ismail, Director: Strategic and Operational Management Support, Provincial Treasury, briefed the Committee on the Department’s 2025/26 annual performance plan (APP) and the third and fourth quarter performance reports for 2024/25. She said the APP was developed through a series of strategic planning engagements and was aligned with the provincial strategic plan (PSP) 2025–2030, which emphasised economic growth, job creation, resident-centred governance, and sustainable service delivery. The vision of the Treasury was to remain a responsive and inclusive institution that enabled positive change in the lives of residents of the Western Cape. Key strategic priorities for 2025–2030 included integrated provincial governance, effective local government, strategic supply chain management (SCM), and efficient infrastructure investment.
Ms Ismail outlined flagship initiatives under the integrated provincial governance framework, including the alternative and blended finance programme to diversify revenue sources, the sustainable infrastructure development and financial facility to accelerate municipal water and wastewater projects, the collaborative integrated planning and budgeting framework to align provincial and municipal budgets, and the roll-out of an e-procurement system to modernise supply chain management.
The 2025/26 APP was structured into four programmes.
Programme 1: Administration, focused on strengthening financial and corporate governance, with initiatives such as artificial intelligence (AI)-driven human resources (HR) process automation and Power BI dashboards.
Programme 2: Sustainable Resource Management, provided for provincial and municipal budget preparation, fiscal policy development, cash-flow management, and socio-economic research, and constituted the largest share of the budget at R170.4 million (47.4%).
Programme 3: Asset Management, centred on governance and oversight of financial systems, supply chain management, and asset oversight, with reforms including the upgrade of the evergreen legacy systems and the establishment of a business intelligence hub.
Programme 4: Governance and accounting, supported governance transformation in departments and municipalities through capacity building, combined assurance frameworks, financial statement automation, and implementation of the municipal Standard Chart of Accounts (mSCOA).
Ms Ismail said that in total, Treasury's budget for 2025/26 amounted to R359 million, with 63 performance indicators across the four programmes. She emphasised that the APP aimed to strengthen fiscal sustainability, improve oversight of financial management, and ensure value for money in service delivery.

Ms Julinda Gantana, Head of Department (HOD), Provincial Treasury, said that the APP reflected the goals that Treasury had set for itself in fulfilling its role as an enabler and a trusted partner. This required the Department to think differently about how it pursued its mandate, with a particular emphasis on innovation, partnership building, and adaptive leadership. She reminded the Committee that in the previous financial year, the Department had carried a significant number of vacancies, and it had committed to addressing this. Work was underway to fill the vacant posts. She noted that the innovative, culture and governance (ICG) framework strategy, which focused on capacity, capability and well-being, underpinned Treasury’s people management approach and was essential to giving effect to the commitments captured in the APP presentation. She added that this strategy required alignment and collaboration with the Department of the Premier and the Department of Local Government, so that systems could be advanced together in a cohesive manner.
Minister Baartman added that Members of the Committee had previously raised concerns about human resources and Treasury’s vacancy rate. She confirmed that filling vacancies was a strong focus for the current financial year, but it was proving to be a difficult and time-consuming process. By way of example, she said that one advertised post had received more than 7 000 applications. This meant that staff were burdened with reviewing applications on top of their normal workload, creating a “chicken and egg” situation where the workload could not decrease until posts were filled, but filling the posts required additional capacity. She said Treasury was working very hard to resolve this, and expressed the hope that progress would be visible in the next annual report. She assured the Committee that Treasury remained committed to providing regular feedback on progress in filling vacancies.
Performance report for third and fourth quarters of 2024/25
Ms Ismail presented the Department’s performance for the third and fourth quarters of the 2024/25 financial year. She reported that overall performance remained strong, with the Department achieving 91% of its indicators in Quarter 3 (39 of 43) and 93% in Quarter 4 (55 of 59). This reflected sustained delivery above 90%, with proactive measures taken to address delays and mitigate risks.
For Quarter 3, Programmes 1 (Administration), 2 (Sustainable Resource Management), and 3 (Asset Management) had achieved 100% of their performance targets, while Programme 4 (Governance and Accounting) had achieved 50% of its indicators. For Quarter 4, Programmes 1 and 2 recorded achievement rates of 100% and 96% respectively, while Programme 3 achieved 82% and Programme 4 achieved 92%.
Ms Ismail highlighted that most commitments were on track across both quarters, with underperformance confined to a small number of indicators. She concluded that the Provincial Treasury had demonstrated resilience and consistency in delivery, with continuous improvements in governance, financial oversight, and accountability across its core programmes.

Minister Baartman added to the presentation by explaining that certain performance indicators listed as “partial” or “not achieved” required context. She pointed out that in some cases the work had been completed, but because of reporting rules, the output could be recorded only in the following quarter. As an example, she referred to an audit at a municipality which had been delayed due to the illness of a service provider. The work had nonetheless been finalised, but because it was not concluded within the specified reporting period, it had been marked as underperformance. She gave another example relating to the provincial revenue audit, which had been underway at the time that the annual consolidated financial statements (ACFS) were due. The Provincial Treasury had written to the Speaker to request a later tabling, and while the documents had been in order, the delay had caused the target to appear as not achieved. She emphasised that these nuances were important for Members to appreciate when reviewing the figures in the Quarterly Performance Report.
Ms Ismail then unpacked the detail of underperforming indicators from Quarters 3 and 4. She said the number of governance performance engagement reports had been completed ahead of schedule to manage staffing constraints, but this had caused them to fall outside of the reporting window. On the publication and tabling of the ACFS, she confirmed that the delay had been due to the provincial revenue fund audit process, which was still ongoing and directly dependent on the ACFS submission.
She reported that planned interventions to support departments and entities in applying accounting standards had been delayed due to external factors, including a National Treasury working group in November. While the target was not achieved in that quarter, the intervention had been carried out in the subsequent quarter and the annual target was ultimately achieved.
She also highlighted challenges in delivering internal audit and risk management support at municipalities. In Laingsburg, the illness of a service provider had delayed risk management work, while in Stellenbosch, the late appointment of a service provider for an agile auditing pilot had forced the project to be pushed into the next quarter. Ms Ismail stressed that most of the underperformance was due to factors beyond the Department’s control, and that the targets had ultimately been met by year-end.

Ms N Nkondlo (ANC) asked whether the quarterly report of the Gambling Board had been submitted, noting that she could not find it in the documentation provided. She said she would leave it to the Chairperson or the Minister to clarify this.

Referring to the APP, she asked how Provincial Treasury defined “citizens” within its planning documents. She wanted to know who counted as a citizen from Treasury’s perspective, and how Treasury interacted with citizens in its budgetary and planning processes. She also asked about the flagship initiatives listed in the APP, wanting to know whether these were ordinary projects that had simply been labelled as flagships, what their intended timeframes were, and whether they had end-dates linked to their outcomes.
On human resources, she said that the Minister had reassured the Committee that staffing challenges would be attended to. This had long been a sore point within Provincial Treasury. She asked how the internal human resources (HR) environment in Treasury was being managed across Programmes 1 and 4 to ensure capacity and prevent staff exhaustion or mass resignations. This required practical implementation of facilities like SIDAFF, rather than indefinite discussions. This was particularly urgent, given the national water and wastewater crisis, which was also affecting municipalities in the Western Cape.
The Chairperson said that SIDAFF and the blended finance programme should be bankable facilities that municipalities could rely on, with financiers and project backers ready to support projects within a few months. He asked for comments from Provincial Treasury on how these programmes would be practically rolled out and implemented.
Department's response
Minister Baartman addressed several of the questions raised. She said that from a legal standpoint, the term “citizen” was defined by the South African Citizenship Act, noting that residency and citizenship were distinct concepts -- residency may vary depending on context (such as tax law), whereas citizenship was governed by statute.
On the request for further presentations on strengthening financial management in municipalities, the Minister confirmed that Treasury was ready to accommodate this, and awaited specific direction from the Committee.
Regarding delays in reporting and audits, the Minister explained that the Auditor-General of South Africa (AGSA) must complete audits, such as of the Provincial Revenue Fund, before documents could be submitted. If AGSA delayed, Treasury must formally notify the Speaker of Parliament and submit once the process concludes. For this reason, a target may appear as missed in one quarter but be recorded later in the annual performance. The Department would ask the team to outline mitigation strategies for future improvements.
On the amended Procurement Act, Minister Baartman confirmed that the Western Cape Government had launched a Constitutional Court challenge, arguing that the Act was unconstitutional. She explained that the Act’s regulations would also be unconstitutional if the Act itself was invalid. She added that the AmaBhungane Investigative Unit had applied to join the case, indicating broader support for the challenge.
Regarding the missing Gambling Board report, the Minister said she had received notice to present only on the APP and quarterly performance, but was willing to provide a gambling and racing briefing if guided to do so.
On performance targets outside of the Department’s control, she said that although delays happened, they could not simply remove legally mandated targets. It had to maintain discipline and continue to strive for timely compliance.
Concerning the flagship initiatives, she explained that these were projects aligned with the provincial strategic plan (PSP), particularly those that supported innovation, culture, and governance. While not all Treasury support projects were flagged this way, the deemed flagship projects reflected outstanding contributions in capacitation and HR.
Regarding the mSCOA reforms, the Minister confirmed these fell under Programme 4, which assists municipalities with fiscal governance. By contrast, work on the equitable share formula fell under Programme 2, which covers fiscal and economic services. She said Treasury was engaged in both horizontal (inter-provincial fairness) and vertical (province-municipality budget splits) fiscal discussions, aiming to secure a greater budget share for municipalities and provinces, considering their service delivery mandates.
On alternative and blended financing, she clarified that municipalities had been using such mechanisms longer than provinces due to enabling legislation. Treasury would assist municipalities with capacity for applications, including to the budget facility for infrastructure (BFI). Successful BFI funding flowed directly to municipal budgets, not the provincial one.
The Minister concluded by inviting the HOD and team to provide further technical detail and clarification on the outstanding items raised.
Ms Gantana said that while questions may have been directed to specific programmes, Treasury’s work with municipalities was undertaken through an integrated team approach that cut across all programmes. Treasury conducted regular oversight and support engagements with municipalities, and should the Committee resolve, they could return to provide a more detailed breakdown of the specific areas of support rendered.
On the matter of local government reforms and the White Paper process, she confirmed that Treasury was providing inputs and that the issue was “live” and ongoing. Further unpacking would occur during the Technical Committee on Finance (TCF)
lekgotla
starting the following day, where the reforms would be dealt with in more detail. These reforms were significant, because they touched directly on the financial sustainability of municipalities and what a reconfigured local government landscape might look like in future.
Turning to the question of performance indicators, Ms Gantana stressed that she was satisfied that Treasury’s indicators were “SMART” (specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound). She placed this on record, noting that the indicators were reviewed in collaboration with the Department of the Premier (DotP). The targets were largely informed by Treasury’s legislative mandates, primarily the Public Finance Management Act (PFMA) and the Municipal Finance Management Act (MFMA). She reminded Members that the Provincial Revenue Fund (PRF) was audited separately from Departmental votes, which created sequencing issues, as the same AGSA teams moved from municipal audits (MFMA) to departmental audits (PFMA), and finally to the PRF. Delays in one part of the cycle inevitably affected others. She said that Treasury would not remove such targets, as they were required by law, specifically section 55 of the PFMA. Where delays arose, the legislation prescribed the steps Treasury must follow, such as formally notifying the Speaker.
Regarding the question of service providers, she explained that procurement rules made it difficult to switch between service providers when unforeseen circumstances, such as illness, arose. In such cases, Treasury had to remain within the regulatory framework and manage the situation as best as possible.
On the flagship initiatives, Ms Gantana noted that the Minister had already outlined Treasury’s approach, but added that there was an ongoing process to ensure that such projects “landed” effectively. Treasury was currently reviewing how to reconfigure itself as a “future-fit” institution capable of meeting the demands of its ambitious APP. This organisational review was part of Treasury’s ongoing internal transformation work.
Mr Steven Kenyon, Chief Director: Local Government Public Finance, responded on the SIDAFF. He said that it was run by the Department of Local Government (DLG), but acknowledged the frustrations around its slow pace. He stressed that SIDAFF was a highly ambitious programme attempting to pioneer new approaches to municipal borrowing by blending donor finance with longer-term and more affordable loans.
In the meantime, municipalities were not waiting for SIDAFF. Many were continuing to borrow independently or apply successfully for BFI funding, including for projects listed under SIDAFF. These projects were therefore not stalled, although the added benefits that SIDAFF sought to achieve were still being worked on. Treasury was continuing to partner with the DLG on this.
On the broader reforms and the White Paper process, Mr Kenyon said it was an “exciting time” in the local government policy space, as there was now openness to change. The Western Cape was proud to have a relatively strong cohort of municipalities that could demonstrate both successes and challenges. Treasury’s role was to ensure that national reforms were informed not only by the provincial Treasury’s views, but by the lived experiences of municipalities. To this end, Treasury had engaged extensively with municipalities and even facilitated workshops where national experts came to the Western Cape to hear directly from local municipalities. In preparation for the upcoming Technical Committee on Finance (TCF) lekgotla, it was evident that many of the challenges raised by Western Cape municipalities were common across the country. The key difficulty now was moving from shared problem identification to practical solutions.
He added that local government reform was complex, because issues were deeply interlinked. Challenges were not only about revenue collection or the fiscal framework, but also about governance failures, political interference, and inefficient expenditure. These structural weaknesses meant that reforms had to tackle multiple aspects simultaneously, which was difficult. However, Provincial Treasury remained committed to actively participating in the national reform processes, while ensuring that the voices of Western Cape municipalities were properly represented.
Mr Jason Facoline, Chief Financial Analyst, Provincial Treasury, responded to the questions raised on the mSCOA reforms. He explained that although the reforms were being coordinated under Programme 4, they were in fact a business-wide reform cutting across the Provincial Treasury. Programme 4 served as a mechanism to coordinate the functions, because the mSCOA was such a large project involving budgeting, in-year reporting, and end-of-year reporting. However, this did not remove the responsibilities of the various other components of Treasury, which also carried aspects of the reform.
Mr Facoline noted the Member’s concerns on Programme 4 indicators, but explained that some of the confusion arose because quarterly reporting could appear to understate performance. For example, if an indicator was not achieved in Quarter 3 but achieved in Quarter 4, the annual target would still be met, but the quarterly report could give the impression of underperformance. He reiterated that some delays were caused by procurement extensions, or by the fact that service providers appointed to assist municipalities were individuals with highly specialised expertise, making them difficult to replace at short notice.
Ms Ismail provided further detail on the court challenge against the Public Procurement Act. She said the current challenge was confined to procedural issues, particularly the constitutionality of the process followed in passing the legislation. These issues related to public consultation and, more specifically, the inclusion of Chapter 4 on preferential procurement, which had not been part of the original commentary but was introduced only when the Bill had been assented to. The challenge did not yet cover substantive issues, as those would first require review through the High Court before they could be challenged constitutionally.
She explained that if Treasury succeeded in the procedural challenge, it would not be necessary to challenge the Act on substantive grounds. A key concern was Chapter 4, particularly how it set targets, quotas, and set-asides. Many provisions from the 2017 regulations, which had been subject to litigation in the case of
Sakeliga v Minister of Finance
, now appeared in the Act. This raised questions about their constitutional compatibility, especially in relation to how organs of state were required to implement preferential procurement.
She elaborated that the Act created a siloed approach to procurement that was at odds with the Western Cape’s integrated approach, where financial processes, budgeting, planning and procurement were aligned. Implementing the Act as it stood could undermine progress already made in the province. Further complications arose from conflicts with sector-specific legislation, particularly in relation to immovable asset management and infrastructure planning. She warned that centralisation of procurement at the national or provincial level risked undermining the mandates of specific portfolios.
Ms Ismail said there were positive aspects of the Act, such as provisions on capacity building and technology requirements, which Treasury supported. However, the negative aspects were too significant to ignore, as they could disrupt Treasury’s existing systems. She reminded the Committee that although the Bill had been assented to, the regulations had not yet been issued. As such, the Act was not yet being implemented, and the PFMA, together with the Preferential Procurement Policy Framework Act and their existing regulations, still applied.
She informed the Committee that Cabinet had adopted resolution 544 of 2022, reinforcing an earlier decision on how the province would manage preferential procurement in a stable manner. In practice, this meant that the Western Cape had consistently applied the compulsory requirements of the 2017 regulations, avoiding the discretionary elements that had later been constitutionally challenged. The only significant change was the exclusion of local content prescriptions, which now impacted implementation, but Treasury had developed workarounds within existing supply chain systems to maintain local empowerment objectives.
She added that Treasury departments were encouraged to define policies that supported empowerment of local small and medium enterprises (SMEs), and that research work was underway to strengthen this. Through compliance with the existing rules, the province had already exceeded national targets on preferential procurement. However, for larger procurement items, Treasury was now exploring economic viability and empowerment strategies in greater detail. These efforts were being incorporated into Treasury’s operational plans, including research, departmental consultations, and use of the “Problem-Driven Iterative Adaptation” (PDIA) model under the Director-General. Within this framework, a strategic procurement working group had been established across all departments to develop practical solutions for the province.
Further discussion
Mr Bryant said that one of the challenges in engaging with the Department’s performance indicators was that the presentation was thin, without all the detailed key performance indicators (KPIs). It would be more beneficial for the Committee to receive the specific sections with individual KPIs, as this would enable Members to see where challenges lie and the reasons for underperformance.
He referred to the issue of the service provider who had fallen ill and delayed delivery. He clarified that he was not requesting any deviation from the SCM processes, but he was concerned about the due diligence when selecting service providers. It seemed problematic that the appointment of a company could rest so heavily on the capacity of a single individual, such that if that individual became incapacitated, the process could not continue. This appeared to him to be a gap in the system. He therefore requested more information on exactly what the role of that individual was, noting that it was an enormous amount of responsibility for one person to bear, given the consequences if they became unavailable.
Ms Nkondlo said that Mr Bryant had already touched on some of the Committee’s concerns regarding targets, and she wanted to add further reflections. She suggested that the Committee might need to rethink its approach to engaging with the APP. When annual reports were considered, the Committee went through the full document in detail, enabling meaningful engagement. In the case of the APP, however, Members were expected to review the APP itself, as well as quarterly reports and ultimately the annual report, yet the documents presented at the meeting were shared with Members only the previous Friday, and contained limited information. This made it difficult to interrogate the APP properly and do justice to the process.
On the substance of the APP, she questioned why most of Treasury’s targets were framed in purely quantitative terms, such as the number of reports or the number of assessments. She wanted to understand the rationale behind this approach. She asked how such quantitative targets reflected real change in distressed municipalities like Beaufort West. If the targets measured only the number of reports produced, how would the Committee know whether municipalities were improving or whether substantive outcomes were being achieved? In her view, such targets gave the impression that Treasury’s work was routine and repetitive, rather than transformative.
She then turned to the baselines used in setting the targets. She gave the example of page 54 of the APP, where Treasury had reported 60 municipal budget assessments in 2024/25, with a target of 58 in 2025/26. There was no clear explanation of why the baseline was set at 60 and why it would decline to 58. Similarly, she pointed to page 50, where municipal engagement reports had initially been 30, but were later revised down to 28. She questioned the logic of reducing targets when an indicator had already been achieved at a higher level, saying that this was not empowering for the Committee in its oversight role.
She gave further examples on pages 56 and 66, where indicators shifted dramatically -- for instance, moving from five in the previous year to 32 in the new year. She asked the Department to explain the arithmetic and logic behind such fluctuations.
Ms Nkondlo then raised the issue of emerging risks. The APP was being tabled in a period leading up to local government elections, at a time when coalition politics were becoming increasingly prevalent. She said coalitions had had a direct impact on municipal governance, with some councils failing to meet or approve budgets because of political instability. She asked where in the APP such risks were recognised and addressed, as the issue had been raised before by the Committee but did not appear clearly in the Department’s planning.
On gender-responsive budgeting, she noted that the APP referred to this intervention, but she could not find any specific indicator in the performance plan that reflected it. She asked the Department to show her which indicator corresponded to gender-based budgeting, how it was in the APP, and what the planned outcomes were.
Finally, she raised the issue of municipal borrowing. She said that this had been prominent in the public domain recently. She asked what role the Provincial Treasury played when municipalities went to the market to borrow, particularly given that many municipalities were already financially distressed. While she acknowledged that municipalities were independent spheres of government, she was concerned about the risks to future generations of citizens who would have to service these loans. She wanted to know who was responsible for assessing whether municipalities were overexposing themselves financially, and whether Treasury had a role in such oversight. She also asked how public participation was ensured when municipalities took out loans, given that such borrowing decisions had long-term implications for municipal residents and for future administrations that would inherit these liabilities.
Mr Masipa said his earlier question had largely been answered, but he wished to follow up on the issue of the court case concerning the new Procurement Act. He asked what the impact would be if the Western Cape’s court challenge failed. Specifically, he wanted to know what the implications would be for service delivery if the province was compelled to implement the new legislation, and what the cost implications would be should the Act come into force.
Minister Baartman responded that she would address some of the broader issues before handing over to the technical team.
On the performance indicators, she said she supported the suggestion that the Department should align its annual targets with its quarterly performance reporting. Often, a target that was missed in one quarter could still be achieved in the following quarter, meaning that the annual target was met. Aligning the annual and quarterly reporting would give a more accurate overall picture of progress.
She addressed the question of service providers, noting that the Provincial Treasury was constrained by SCM legislation. Nonetheless, she agreed that mitigation measures needed to be considered in cases where the work depended heavily on one individual. She also clarified the reason for the reduction of certain targets, explaining that the Metro municipality reported directly to National Treasury. As a result, while the Provincial Treasury still conducted assessments, the reporting line differed, and this explained why municipal budget assessments had been revised down from 60 to 58.
The Minister emphasised the unique role that the Provincial Treasury played in relation to municipalities. While Treasury could provide guidance, support and capacity building, it could not govern municipalities. Financial recovery plans were different in that case, as they were written by National Treasury but imposed by the Provincial Treasury. These plans contained specific targets for each phase of recovery, and municipalities could move through the phases only if they met those targets. This required a collaborative approach, involving National Treasury, the Provincial Treasury and the municipalities themselves, to ensure that once municipalities exited financial recovery plans, they were sustainable on their own.
She explained that some of the Department’s reporting obligations were routine in nature, such as tabling the PERO and MERO. These were statutory reports, and while Treasury sought to innovate in their content, the targets for them were inherently quantitative. Similarly, two budgets were tabled each year, though extraordinary events such as the COVID-19 pandemic had required three budgets. Other reporting, such as procurement disclosure reports, was based on departmental submissions, and the Provincial Treasury could only publish data as provided. She highlighted that while quantitative reports could appear routine, nothing prevented the Committee from engaging Treasury on the qualitative aspects behind those reports.
On the issue of risks related to local government and coalition politics, the Minister said that Treasury worked closely with the Department of Local Government, because risks in one area inevitably created risks in the other. Coalition instability posed risks to governance, financial management and service delivery. She undertook to provide more detail on how such risks were reflected in the APP.
On gender-based budgeting, she said that references were contained in the APP, but the team would provide specifics on where and how it was practically implemented. She added that Treasury would also expand on the processes around municipal borrowing, including how these were integrated into the alternative and blended finance (ABF) framework and municipal loan committees, as well as the public participation requirements that accompanied such decisions.
Turning to the court case, the Minister explained that the province had deliberately separated its challenge into two parts. The current legal action was limited to procedural issues, including the adequacy of public consultation and the late inclusion of Chapter 4 on preferential procurement. If the province succeeded, there would be no need to challenge the substantive provisions. However, the option of a substantive challenge was not off the table.
In terms of immediate impact, she stressed that the new Act did not yet apply, since the regulations had not been issued. The PFMA and the existing preferential procurement framework remained in place. However, if the Act was implemented in its current form, there would be significant risks. The most concerning aspect was the centralisation of procurement powers in the hands of the national Minister of Finance or the Office of the Public Procurement Officer. She referred to the earlier AfriBusiness case, in which the Constitutional Court had held that while National Treasury could provide a framework, it could not prescribe detailed procurement policy for other organs of state.
The Minister said she was proud that the Western Cape had exceeded national targets for procurement with black-owned businesses without relying on set-asides or mandatory subcontracting. This showed that transformation, job creation, growth and value for money could all be achieved without centralised, prescriptive rules.
On the cost implications, the Minister cautioned that the Act would require expensive new systems to be implemented, which municipalities could not afford. This would create compliance burdens that many municipalities would struggle to meet, and some would not even have the resources to legally challenge the Act. She said the Western Cape had consulted its municipalities through the Provincial Budget and Governance Forum, where municipalities had unanimously supported the province’s decision to challenge the Act.
Ms Gatana responded first to the discussion on performance indicators. She reminded Members that the APP contained technical indicator descriptions at the back of the document, which explained the basis for the numbers and their movement. For example, some indicators were linked to specific municipalities and entities, which accounted for changes in targets. This was the straightforward part of the question.
The more complex issue was the one raised by Ms Nkondlo, namely, whether the indicators demonstrated the actual impact on the ground. She explained that Treasury had begun developing a budget reform agenda to better link performance information to policy decisions and the impact on residents. The challenge was moving beyond outputs to demonstrate the “lived reality” of citizens, which was a key focus of the provincial strategic plan. Treasury had already acknowledged in the 2024 medium term budget policy statement (MTBPS) that the system needed to evolve to reflect this. She said the Finance Department was partnering with the Department of the Premier to build this capacity, and was putting building blocks in place to move in this direction. While the current indicators remained quantitative, the aim was to show more clearly the impact of spending and reforms.
Ms Gatana said that while gender-responsive budgeting was referred to in the narrative of the APP, it was not always reflected in a standalone indicator. Much of the work happened at sub-programme level and was embedded in Treasury’s broader fiscal strategy. Gender-responsive budgeting involved tagging and analysing expenditure across departments to determine how it advanced equality for women and girls. Some of this work had begun, including budget tagging initiatives, but Treasury still needed to strengthen its systems and instruments to integrate gender considerations more explicitly. She said the Department could demonstrate its spending on gender-focused initiatives, such as bursaries, even if these were not reflected as APP targets. She added that National Treasury had also started asking provinces to tag information communication technology (ICT) expenditure, showing how tagging could be expanded to gender budgeting in the future.
On municipal borrowing, she confirmed that Treasury had an integrated team and an internal committee to consider such matters.
Mr Malcolm Booysen, Senior Manager: Local Government Budget Management, Provincial Treasury, then elaborated on the borrowing process. He said municipal borrowing was regulated under Section 46 of the MFMA, which required consultation with both National Treasury, Provincial Treasury and the public. The Provincial Treasury conducted its own assessment, which included compliance with laws and regulations, as well as a credibility test using ratio analysis. This assessment covered the two most recent financial years, as well as an in-year financial review.
He explained that municipalities were required to consult the public at two stages: first, when tabling their budgets, and second, when they approached the market to raise the loan. Provincial Treasury also took account of National Treasury’s comments before forming its view. Importantly, the Department was not afraid to withhold approval when it found that a municipality had exceeded prudent borrowing limits. In some cases, Treasury had instructed councils to reconsider either the size or the necessity of loans. However, there were also cases where borrowing had been beneficial in unlocking economic growth.
Mr Isaac Tsie, Director: Local Government Revenue and Expenditure, Provincial Treasury, addressed the Committee on the implications of the new Public Procurement Act. He said that many of the challenges now found in the Act had already been flagged in 2017 when Treasury had engaged with National Treasury during the drafting of procurement regulations. At that stage, Provincial Treasury had cautioned that the way targeting was designed would significantly increase costs. The Western Cape had argued that instead of rigid targets, the focus should be on creating an enabling environment for businesses.
He provided evidence that the province had achieved empowerment outcomes without rigid targets. Of the provincial supplier database, 83% of suppliers were small businesses, and 74% were black-owned. For the 2024/25 financial year, 53% of the value of contracts had already been allocated to black-owned businesses. This showed that transformation was possible without the strict measures imposed in the national policy. For example, in 2017, certain national requirements had not been implemented in the province, but alternative mechanisms had been used to achieve the same outcomes.
He cautioned that the new Act was even more prescriptive, applying a “one size fits all” model across industries. This was problematic, because sectors in the Western Cape were at different stages of transformation. For instance, learner transport was already 95% transformed, making additional requirements unnecessary and costly. The Act was divorced from the PFMA, which had long been the guiding framework for fiscal discipline, and this separation would create misalignment between procurement and fiscal management. He stressed that the province was not opposed to empowerment or preferential procurement, as its record demonstrated. However, the concern was that some provisions of the Act did not deliver value for money, and would increase costs unnecessarily. He also gave the example that while national policy required three quotations for procurements under R1 million, the provincial approach was to use open market processes, which had broadened access and improved service delivery.
Mr Bryant interjected, noting that a specific question he had raised earlier had not yet been answered. He repeated his query regarding the service provider whose illness had delayed a project. He asked how that service provider had been chosen, what process had been followed, and what the individual’s exact role was. He stressed that it seemed unusual for an entire project to be dependent on one person, and he sought clarity on whether contingency arrangements existed.
Mr Tsie responded by providing context on the service provider arrangements. He explained that at the beginning of each calendar year, around February, Provincial Treasury conducted technical assessments with municipalities. Based on these assessments, Treasury designed support plans for municipalities requiring assistance. Because Treasury had a limited in-house audit and risk compliance unit, it relied on a transversal contract with a panel of service providers who could step in to support municipalities. In this case, a service provider had been contracted to deliver work in a specific quarter but had fallen ill, which had delayed the work. Mr Tsie emphasised that the delay was due to timing and not quality, since the work was eventually completed to standard. He explained that there was not only one service provider on the panel but several. However, because many municipalities required assistance, resources were stretched, and in this instance, the Department had chosen to defer the work into the next quarter rather than declare an emergency. He said Treasury took note of Mr Bryant’s concern about contingency planning, but assured the Committee that the quality of work had not been compromised. He added that many municipalities lacked the capacity to perform these functions themselves, which meant that reliance on Provincial Treasury and contracted service providers would remain necessary.
Ms Nkondlo said she was concerned when officials began "speaking like politicians", noting that this blurred accountability. She reminded Provincial Treasury that its APP was the official oversight instrument, and therefore had to reflect the Department’s actual work. She pointed out that some important matters raised in presentations and strategy documents, such as gender-responsive budgeting, were not captured as measurable targets in the APP. She stressed that if something could not be measured, “it does not exist” in the oversight framework.
She asked how activities not included in the APP’s target setting were resourced. She argued that this created a lack of clarity on accountability, because work was being carried out but was not reflected in the indicators. She referred specifically to the situation in Beaufort West, which she said the Minister had personally highlighted during an oversight visit. She asked where this intervention could be found in the APP, since it was not visible as a target or indicator. She questioned whether it was hidden in the number of reports that the Department claimed it would produce.
Ms Nkondlo further argued that the Provincial Treasury’s indicators were overwhelmingly quantitative and lacked any qualitative measures. She said the Committee’s questions were focused on the qualitative dimension, whether the reports and assessments led to actual change in municipalities, yet the APP allowed only for counting outputs. She challenged the Department to point to even one indicator that was qualitative in nature. She concluded that the Department was still at the stage of discussing gender-based budgeting “as a concept,” rather than implementing it. Without measurable indicators, oversight could not confirm progress, and she could not accept assurances that the work was being done “somewhere.”
Mr Masipa said that while his questions on the cost implications of the new Public Procurement Act had been answered, he still had concerns about its impact on service delivery. He asked specifically whether the centralisation model being introduced by National Treasury would delay the pace of service delivery. He stressed that his concern was not financial costs, but whether turnaround times in contracting and procurement would worsen, thereby affecting delivery to communities.
Dr N Mbombo (DA) asked for clarity on the indicators linked to fiscal policy. She said her understanding from the presentation was that there were five indicators listed under Programme Two. She asked the Department to confirm what those indicators were, and whether her recollection was correct.
Minister Baartman responded to the issue of gender-responsive budgeting. She acknowledged that the Provincial Treasury could improve its reporting in this area, and committed to finding ways to collate and present the data more effectively. She noted that some reporting already took place through procurement publications, but the Department would consider how to integrate this work more clearly into its APP, and come back to the Committee with improvements.
Minister Baartman explained the legal framework regarding Beaufort West and other municipalities under financial recovery plans. In terms of the law, National Treasury must draft financial recovery plans, after which the Provincial Treasury and the public could provide input. National Treasury would then decide whether to include or exclude suggested targets. Once finalised, she, as Provincial Minister, would sign off the plan, and it had to be imposed by law. She emphasised that responsibility for meeting targets in a financial recovery plan was shared between National Treasury, Provincial Treasury, and the affected municipality. Some municipalities moved faster than others, depending on capacity. She pointed out the dilemma that while some targets depended on external stakeholders, the Provincial Treasury still had to include them and work collectively to ensure municipalities exited financial recovery plans sustainably, without relapsing.
Minister Baartman said certain documents, such as cash management reports, budgets, and research outputs, were mandatory, while others involved assessments and support processes that could not always be reflected as quantitative targets. She acknowledged the Committee’s request to strengthen qualitative reporting, and said the Department would review how to better capture the impact of its work rather than just its outputs. She gave the example of SCM assessments, which Treasury had to conduct, but said the actual procurement choices remained the responsibility of departments. Treasury’s role was to enable and support, rather than dictate those decisions.
Responding to Dr Mbombo's question on fiscal policy indicators, Minister Baartman confirmed that the five indicators under Programme Two were linked to:
research reports on provincial and local government fiscal systems;
the number of provincial revenue management reports;
cash management reports for the provincial government;
reports on the performance of the Western Cape Gambling and Racing Board; and
consolidated revenue retention reports.
Turning to Mr Masipa’s concern about service delivery, she said that while the impact of the Public Procurement Act would not necessarily be visible as “one less school” or “ten fewer pens,” the reality was that any money spent on compliance systems would reduce the funds available for service delivery. She stressed that every rand diverted from services to administrative systems represented an indirect impact on service delivery.
Committee matters
Actions and resolutions
The Chairperson concluded the session by noting that the Committee now needed to consider its resolutions and recommendations before moving to draft minutes. He expressed concern that the documentation provided by the Department had been thin and sparse. The APP presented had not met the expected standard, particularly in its professional quality. Similarly, the quarterly reports had not included financials, and the performance indicators lacked the rigour expected. He observed that the Committee had spent almost two and a half hours listening to oral presentations without adequate documents to interrogate, which raised concerns about the effectiveness of the oversight process.
Mr Bryant agreed with the Chairperson, and said more information upfront would make the Committee’s work easier and reduce the need for prolonged questioning. He pointed to the example of the service provider issue, which had not been detailed in the minutes. If the documentation had been clear, he would not have needed to raise questions. However, he remained concerned that a provincial contract could be jeopardised by reliance on the health of a single individual. He asked what would have happened if the individual had passed away, and stressed that this was a serious risk.
Mr Bryant proposed two recommendations. First, the Committee should request a presentation on the financial interventions currently taking place in municipalities and their status. Second, it should be given a presentation on the existing agreements with service providers supporting municipalities, including who those service providers were, and the processes governing their contracts. He said the Committee's oversight role required transparency in this area, and there was no justification for withholding such information.
Dr Mbombo asked about the APP book. She noted that in previous years, the Committee had received the full APP document, which provided the comprehensive information, including programmes, budget policies, and indicators. She could not recall whether the current APP was distributed electronically, and assumed the presentation provided was only a summary. She requested clarity on why the full APP book had not been provided to Members in the usual format.
The Chairperson responded that the APPs had been handed out to Members earlier in the year, and he still had a copy on his shelf. He acknowledged, however, that he had not brought it to the meeting the previous week.
Dr Mbombo clarified that she was not referring to the annual performance report (APR), but the APP. She recalled that last year, the Auditor-General had suggested that Parliament should engage with draft APPs before Departments finalised them, but this had not been possible because of legal processes. Departments had to submit APPs to the Premier, and only once the Premier had finalised them could they be tabled with the Speaker. For this reason, APPs were not available to committees before March. She speculated that this could explain the delays in Members receiving the full APP book this year.
The Chairperson noted that the presentation contained a timetable for when APPs and related documents should be prepared. He expressed concern that it was already August, and the Committee was dealing with the APP only now, meaning it was already well into the second quarter of the year. This was not unique to the Provincial Treasury, as other committees had experienced similar delays. He suggested that Members could review the APP in more detail and submit issues they wished to be elaborated on to the procedural officer by Friday. This could then feed into the Committee’s resolutions and recommendations. He reminded Members that the APP contained not only indicators but also technical descriptions, risks, and other aspects which the Committee should have been able to interrogate if presented timeously.
Ms Nkondlo supported the Chairperson’s proposal. She confirmed that she had received an electronic version of the APP the previous Friday from Committee staff. She supported the idea of making submissions by Friday on the current APP, but stressed that a broader conversation was needed about how the legislature engaged the APP process. She argued that committees needed to be involved at the stage when departments were drafting APPs, rather than only after they were finalised. This would allow Members to influence the content and ensure that their oversight role was meaningful. She warned that when oversight occurred only after the APP was finalised, Members’ questions were often dismissed on the basis that the APP had already been approved. She therefore urged that committees should see APPs before they were concluded, so they could provide input that could shape the final product. She would also make her own submissions by Friday, as proposed.
Mr Bryant said he was in full agreement. He stressed that while the APP itself was a useful reference document, the real issue was that quarterly performance presentations needed to be more detailed. It was not reasonable to expect Members to have mastered every page of the APP, and the purpose of presentations was to highlight and explain performance information. If presentations were more comprehensive, Members would not need to interrogate every target during meetings.
The Chairperson added that quarterly performance reports should be tabled with standing committees as a matter of course. In his previous experience on the Education Committee, he had requested a quarterly report and was told that it had never been done before. However, he had insisted that in municipalities, councils automatically received quarterly reports by law, and he believed the same practice should apply in the provincial legislature. This was an area that needed attention, and the Committee should take it up further.
The Chairperson asked if there were any further issues to deal with, or whether Members agreed that documentation and questions should be submitted by Friday. Members expressed general agreement.
Committee minutes
The Committee then turned to its draft minutes. The minutes of 12 August, dealing with the blended finance presentation, and 19 August were considered. Members noted that the 12 August minutes would be quick to process, while the 19 August minutes would take longer, as it had been a lengthy meeting.
Both sets of minutes were adopted and seconded.
The meeting was adjourned.
