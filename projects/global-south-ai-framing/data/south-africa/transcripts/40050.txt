Meeting Summary
Video
The Portfolio Committee met on a virtual platform for its last meeting of the year to consider and adopt its oversight report to the Eastern Cape, as well as the report on the appointment of suitable candidates for the Independent Communications Authority of South Africa (ICASA) Council.
During the Committee’s deliberation on the ICASA Council report, Members engaged in a detailed discussion on the selection of the six shortlisted candidates, considering their qualifications, expertise, and alignment with demographic and policy priorities. Emphasis was placed on promoting diversity, including youth representation, gender balance, and representation of national interest groups.
The Committee emphasised that the list of candidates was not ranked in order of preference, and acknowledged the high calibre of all nominees. It also proposed exploring other ICASA roles, such as positions on the complaints and compliance committee, for candidates not selected for council positions. It approved the report for submission to the National Assembly. Once the Assembly adopted the report, it would be sent to the Minister, who would select four of the six candidates for appointment to the Council. These selections would return to the National Assembly for formal ratification.
The Committee discussed the need to conduct an oversight visit to the State Information Technology Agency (SITA) to probe governance issues involving its board. These included the suspension of its acting managing director and the organisation’s leadership challenges. Members proposed an oversight visit to address these issues before year-end, particularly as the board's term of office was due to expire in January 2025. A potential joint meeting with the Portfolio Committee on Basic Education was also discussed to investigate irregularities in a Western Cape Education Department’s tender award, which SITA had flagged.
The Committee indicated that it had noted Cabinet’s refusal to extend the analogue switch-off deadline beyond 31 December 2024. It had motivated Cabinet to extend the deadline due to concerns over the lack of sufficient satellite boxes for 4.4 million households, and its impact on citizens’ constitutional right to access free public broadcasting.
The status of the South African Broadcasting Corporation (SABC) Bill was clarified, with confirmation that the bill remained active despite earlier indications of its potential withdrawal.
The Committee adopted its oversight report to the Eastern Cape, integrating the key findings and amendments. Its main highlights included challenges in inter-departmental communication, the SABC's debt to Sentech, and the need for robust post-implementation follow-ups.
Meeting report
The Chairperson said the primary purpose of the meeting was to deliberate on the appointment of suitable candidates for the Independent Communications Authority of South Africa (ICASA) Council, and to make its recommendation for concurrence.
Committee matters
Adoption of minutes
The draft Committee minutes for 15 November 2024 were adopted.
Matters arising: Western Cape-SITA contract issue
Mr S Subrathie (ANC) said he had attended the
meeting
yesterday as a Member who had an interest in the State Information Technology Agency (SITA). He encouraged the Committee to take charge of it and commended the Portfolio Committee on Basic Education for convening this meeting with SITA. The meeting had started with the Western Cape Education Department (WCED) presenting, during which it asserted that no wrongdoing had occurred in the process. There were a lot of concerns that had been raised, such as the WCED ignoring the warnings despite the tender having been flagged as irregular. There was also the legal opinion that the WCED had sought, and the legal opinion had stated there was no need for it.
His impression was that the Auditor-General of South Africa (AGSA) had red-flagged the SITA contract and raised ten findings. The WCED was criticised because it could have prevented the contract two months into the contract, but now a lot of money has been paid to the contractor. He said that the engagement had been very useful.
The SITA then made a presentation. He clarified that the current SITA board would complete its term on 25 January 2025. The input was that they needed to strengthen the SITA board since leadership was currently lacking at SITA.
The last presentation was done by Cliffe Dekker Hofmeyr (CDH). Since it was a reputable organisation, it was unlikely that CDH would misrepresent anything.
He had found the entire engagement robust, and encouraged Members to watch the
recording
on YouTube. He suggested that the Committee hold a joint meeting with the Committee on Basic Education.
Ms T Bodlani (DA) indicated that the Committee did have two Members who served on the  Basic Education Committee, and provided them with information on how the meeting went. They had also indicated the need for a joint meeting, so she supported the suggestion as well.
She suggested that the Committee needed to pay SITA a visit. She had also heard that SITA’s Acting managing director (MD) had been suspended. It was something that the Committee needed to ascertain -- was he being victimised, who was acting in that position, had the Department been made aware, had the Portfolio Committee been made aware?
The Chairperson confirmed that she had also heard rumours about the suspension, but given that the Acting MD had been in the meeting, she did not understand what was going on. She thus asked if Members would be available to do an oversight at SITA during the week before 17 December.
Mr S Vilakazi (DA) supported the Chairperson’s suggestion. It was important to pay close attention to the development of events at SITA now, rather than waiting for January next year when a lot may have transpired within the entity. Also, certain departments such as the security cluster and Home Affairs, would be busier than usual during the festive season, and the Committee had to be certain that SITA was in good shape to provide support.
Mr Subrathie suggested the Chairperson should seek advice from the whips of political parties, since next week was constituency week, not oversight week. He seconded her suggestion, nevertheless.
He also highlighted that one member of the SITA board had said the public should not pay the board a salary over the next two months because the board was dysfunctional. He also expressed concern over the continuity of the board, because it was unlikely it would be reappointed, given the circumstances.
The Chairperson noted all the inputs and suggested that the Committee put in an urgent application for an emergency oversight visit.
Mr Mbombo Maleka, Committee Content Advisor, confirmed that he would work on that and get back to the Chairperson.
Adoption of minutes
The draft Committee minutes for 19 November were adopted.
Matters arising: Analogue switch-off
The Chairperson reported that the Cabinet denied the Minister permission to extend the deadline for the analogue switch-off to beyond 31 December 2024. The Committee had engaged with the SABC, eMedia, and the Media Development and Diversity Agency (MDDA) to get their feedback and submissions. It subsequently sent a motivation to the President, copying the Leader of Government Business as well as the Minister, supporting the decision for an extension of the analogue switch-off deadline. The Committee hoped that the Cabinet would consider the Committee’s support for the extension. The Committee’s submission was that people’s constitutional right to free public broadcasting services was being tampered with. South Africans should have the option of having a free public broadcaster. In addition, the country did not have enough satellite boxes to cover more than 4.4 million people who would need them after the switch off. The Committee also highlighted the constitutional ruling and indicated that those rights had not been fulfilled.
Matters arising: SABC Bill
On the SABC bill, the Chairperson said the Speaker had indicated to her that the Minister had earlier expressed the intention for the bill to be withdrawn. Subsequently, however, the Deputy President stated that all of the bills re-introduced in Parliament had been done through his office as the leader of government business. The Speaker in future would only receive notifications, such as the withdrawal of bills like the SABC bill, from his office. It therefore meant that the SABC bill was not withdrawn.
Adoption of minutes
The draft committee minutes for 20 and 26 November were duly adopted.
Report on oversight visit to Eastern Cape
Dr Jacob Medupe, Committee Content Advisor, took the Committee through the report on its oversight visit to the Eastern Cape.
The Chairperson asked if the report had included the Committee’s visits to Umtata.
Mr Subrathie suggested having a short executive summary of the report that encapsulates the key concerns from those oversight visits, such as the serious lack of inter-departmental communication and collaboration, and follow-ups on post-implementation.
Ms Bodlani commended the accurate report. She agreed with her colleague on how the Committee could track the recommendations that it had made.
Mr K Sithole (IFP) was uncertain how the Committee should deal with the Sentech situation. He highlighted the outstanding debt that the SABC owed to Sentech.
Mr Vilakazi supported the suggestion to insert an executive summary.
The draft report on the oversight visit to the Eastern Cape was duly adopted by the Committee, with amendments made.
Report of the Portfolio Committee on Communications and Digital Technologies on the filling of four (4) vacancies in the Independent Communications Authority of South Africa (ICASA) Council
Mr Thembinkosi Ngoma, Committee Secretary, took the Committee through the report on the deliberations and consideration of the recommendations of the sub-committee on the appointment of suitable candidates for the ICASA Council, and the report recommending the candidates for appointment.
The Chairperson reminded Mr Ngoma to add to the report that the EFF had objected to the nomination of Dr Charlie Lewis as a returning candidate.
She then explained the next step. If the Portfolio Committee accepted the report, it would be presented to the National Assembly. It would then be processed to the Minister, who would select four out of the six candidates to fill the ICASA council. Those four names would then revert to the National Assembly for ratification.
She was of the view that the Committee had selected the best of the qualified candidates, and any of them would be suitable to fill the vacancies.
Ms T Chauke-Adonis (ANC) sought assurance that different demographic groups were represented such as youth, persons with disabilities, gender balance, national interest groups, etc. She sought clarity on who they were.
She asked what the Committee could do to ensure the various demographic groups were represented, and emphasised the need for youth representation on the ICASA Council.
Ms Bodlani added that it should be highlighted that the list was not presented in sequence of preference.
Mr Subrathie expressed his appreciation, and commended the Chairperson’s leadership. He was impressed with the level of consensus that the Committee had been able to reach. He had been impressed by the skill sets of the 17 candidates. While the Committee could accommodate only four, a recommendation should be made to see if there were other positions at ICASA that could be made available.
The Chairperson requested Mr Ngoma to clarify the input of Ms Bodlani in the report, as well as to factor in the demographic matter. In addition, those who did not make the shortlist of candidates should also be considered for other positions at ICASA, such as the Complaints and Compliance Committee (CCC).
Mr Ngoma informed the Committee that the ICASA Council currently consists of four female councillors and one male, who is the Chairperson. The shortlisted candidates were:
Dr Charles Alastair Lewis - a specialist in the ICT sector who specialises in information systems.
Dr Tshifhiwa Joshua Maumela - an engineer and economist.
Mr Melusi Mthethwa was in business administration.
Ms Cassandra Cordelia Gabriel was a telecommunication specialist.
Ms Karabo Mohale was a 33-year-old who specialises in finance.
Mr Andrew Dibi Matseke was a business administrator.
Mr Maleka indicated that they did not have sufficient profile information at the moment, but assured the Committee that the sub-committee had done its due diligence in broadening the representation of current board members. The list would be sent to the Committee.
On demographics, the Chairperson commented that the Committee had taken a deliberate approach to promoting women. It had identified that there were already four women on the ICASA board, so only one was needed to make the 50+ percent balance on the Council. Four of the shortlisted candidates represented other national groups, beyond just African candidates. The Committee had also shortlisted candidates who had disabilities, and had one candidate who was visually impaired.
Dr Lewis was a white male who had extensive experience in the field, and was a current serving councillor.
Ms Mohale was a 33-year-old African female who had two Masters degrees. The Committee was of the view that the board should have her experience in economic policy.
Mr Matseke was an engineer and had experience of broadband.
Mr Mthethwa was an executive at Telkom and had extensive experience working for different organisations.
Ms Gabriel was an Indian female who was a former member of staff at ICASA and had a lot of broadcasting experience.
Dr Maumela was a 33-year-old male, whose doctorate thesis was on artificial intelligence. The Committee was of the view that he would be useful in research that would be done around emerging technology, such as issues of blockchain and artificial intelligence.
Mr Sithole asked what would happen next, after the report was adopted.
Mr Ngoma responded that once the report was adopted, it would follow the announcements, tablings and committees (ATC) process. He hoped this report would form part of the discussion in the House tomorrow. Once the House adopted it, the Speaker would inform the Minister of the House’s resolutions. Once the Minister made the appointment, the Minister would write back to the Speaker on the four selected candidates. It would then come before the Committee, which could either accept, reject or amend. Once the Committee adopted the report, the matter would go to the House and be communicated to the Minister, and the Minister would then make the appointments.
The Chairperson sought more clarity on the expectations of the Committee as to whether this report would form part of the discussion tomorrow.
Mr Ngoma indicated that he had communicated with the NA table in the hope that it would be on the agenda of the House tomorrow. He would provide an update on the Committee's WhatsApp group.
