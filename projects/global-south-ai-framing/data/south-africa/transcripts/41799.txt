Annual Reports 2024/25
The Portfolio Committee on Science, Technology and Innovation met to receive briefings from the Technology Innovation Agency (TIA) and the South African National Space Agency (SANSA) on their 2024/25 Annual Reports.
Apologies were received from the Minister, Deputy Minister, and Director-General. Members expressed concern about the absence of accounting officers, viewing it as a sign of disregard for the Committee’s oversight role.
TIA reported that it had achieved 79% of its annual performance targets (15 out of 19), with 12 of 13 core mandate targets met. The Agency highlighted overachievement in several areas, including product launches, revenue from commercialised innovations, and bio-based technology development.
TIA received an unqualified audit with findings, mainly relating to material adjustments in financial statements and inadequate evidence of consequence management.
Members raised strong concerns about repeat findings, poor consequence management, and weak internal controls.
The Committee questioned the lack of disciplinary action for irregular expenditure and called for quarterly progress reports to monitor corrective action. Members also raised concerns about duplication of innovation programmes, weak commercialisation pipelines, and limited support for persons with disabilities.
SANSA achieved 84% of its annual performance targets (16 out of 19) and received a clean audit (unqualified without findings) for the fourth consecutive year. Members commended SANSA’s audit performance but raised concerns about project delays, vacancies, and financial sustainability.
SANSA reported total revenue of R436 million and a R26 million deficit, largely due to underfunding of the Parliamentary Grant, which was insufficient to cover personnel costs and the Board explained that SANSA’s operational grant of R137 million was far below the required R375 million needed to sustain its mandate.
Members questioned the persistent delays in the Matjiesfontein Deep Space Network and EOSat-1 satellite projects, as well as SANSA’s partnership with Denel, which was described as high-risk given Denel’s financial instability. The Committee requested a written response from SANSA on the Denel partnership and compliance with the PFMA in the procurement process.
Members were particularly concerned about TIA’s repeated governance issues and the need for stronger oversight by the Department. The Committee resolved to monitor TIA quarterly and expected measurable improvement in audit outcomes and executive stability.
On SANSA, the Committee welcomed the clean audit but urged greater attention to financial sustainability, infrastructure delays, and the inclusion of rural areas in satellite connectivity programmes. The Chairperson commended both entities for their engagements but emphasised the need for improved accountability and strategic alignment within the national innovation system. SANSA would be invited back for a focused discussion on the Denel partnership.
Meeting report
Chairperson’s Opening Remarks
The Chairperson welcomed Members of the Committee, representatives from the Technology Innovation Agency (TIA), the South African National Space Agency (SANSA), and officials from the Department of Science, Technology and Innovation (DSTI) to the meeting.
She noted that the Committee had been engaging with the Department and its entities since the previous day to ensure a comprehensive understanding of their performance and strategic priorities. The purpose of the meeting was to receive briefings from TIA and SANSA on their 2024/25 Annual Reports.
Apologies were received from the Minister, Deputy Minister, and Director-General.
The Chairperson outlined the programme for the session, indicating that both presentations would be delivered consecutively, followed by a joint discussion and deliberation.
During the preliminary proceedings, Ms S Thembekwayo (EFF) expressed concern regarding the absence of the Department’s accounting officers, stating that their non-attendance could create the perception that the meeting was of limited importance.
The Chairperson acknowledged the concern, noting that similar issues had been raised in a meeting held on 14 October. She expressed confidence that the Deputy Director-General (DDG) would communicate the Committee’s dissatisfaction to the Department.
Ms Gugulethu Zwane, DDG: Institutional Planning and Support, DSTI, confirmed that she would convey the Committee’s concerns regarding non-attendance to the Department.
The Chairperson then invited TIA and SANSA to proceed with their presentations.
TIA 2024/25 Annual Report
Mr Ismail Abdoola, Acting CEO, briefed the Committee on its 2024/25 Annual Report and the progress made under its 2020–2025 Strategic Plan. The Agency reported strong performance outcomes, meeting 79% of its Annual Performance Plan (APP) targets, with notable overachievement in key innovation indicators such as technology diffusion, product launches, and revenue from commercialised innovations.
TIA continued its transition to “TIA 2.0”, repositioning itself as a dynamic, intelligence-driven national innovation agency. The presentation highlighted that since its inception, TIA has catalysed R3.9 billion in co-investment for technology development and commercialisation and diffused 91 technologies for inclusive community development.
Under the three strategic outcomes, Commercialised Innovations, Bio-economy Strategy, and Innovation Enabling, TIA showcased achievements in promoting locally developed technologies, advancing bio-based innovations, and supporting SMMEs and community-based innovators across provinces.
Significant achievements included:
15 of 19 APP targets met, with 12 of 13 core mandate targets achieved.
Overachievement in revenue from commercialised innovations (207%), bio-based technologies developed (162%), and human capital development (199%).
Support to underserved provinces and designated groups, including women, youth, and persons with disabilities, through targeted innovation programmes.
Under Outcome 1, TIA reported the commercialisation of technologies such as NWU’s Supercritical Fluid Extraction plant and the Lodox Photon Counting Detection X-ray Scanner. Under Outcome 2, initiatives under the Bio-economy Strategy led to new bio-based products, including the Open Genome Project and biosurfactant innovations.
Under Outcome 3, TIA strengthened its innovation ecosystem through Innovation Centres, Technology Stations, and the Grassroots Innovation Programme, which supported 292 projects and launched the 2024 Grassroots Innovation Awards.
Transformation initiatives included the Women, Youth, and Disability Technology Innovation Programmes, each targeting economic participation among underrepresented groups.
On funding, the Innovation Fund disbursed R115 million in 2024/25, with cumulative disbursements of R241.1 million. The launch of a R300 million High Impact Seed Fund was recognised in the 2025 State of the Nation Address (SONA).
Financially, TIA reported a 6.3% increase in revenue and improved efficiency ratios. No fruitless or wasteful expenditure was incurred, though R0.2 million in irregular expenditure was identified (down from R3.5 million in 2024).
The Auditor-General issued an unqualified audit opinion with no findings for the 14th consecutive year.

SANSA 2024/25 Annual Report
Mr Humbulani Mudai, CEO, SANSA, presented SANSA’s 2024/25 Annual Report to the Committee.
SANSA achieved 84% of its targets – 16 out of 19. The presentation highlighted SANSA’s various programmes and projects, focusing on the current level of achievement and plans for the next strategic cycle.
SANSA’s audit outcome was unqualified without findings. This was SANSA’s fourth consecutive year of achieving a clean audit.
Total revenue amounted to R436 million, with a R 77 million variance against the revised budget. Operating expenditure amounted to R463 million which was 3% below the revised budget. Employment costs and other operating expenses were below budget largely due to vacancies and cost-cutting measures.

Discussion
The Chairperson thanked both TIA and SANSA for the presentations.
Dr S Thembekwayo (EFF) expressed serious concern regarding the Technology Innovation Agency’s (TIA) recurring audit findings and weak financial controls. She noted that while all six other entities reporting to the Department had maintained unqualified audit opinions with no findings, TIA continued to record repeat findings on material non-compliance, particularly related to material adjustments in its annual financial statements. This, she said, reflected poorly on the Agency’s commitment to effective governance and performance management.
She added that the emergence of a new material finding on consequence management, where the Auditor-General could not obtain sufficient evidence that disciplinary action had been taken against responsible officials, was particularly troubling. Repeat findings indicated that previous corrective action plans had either been ineffective or not implemented.
Dr Thembekwayo questioned what new and concrete measures TIA would introduce to address the root causes of these deficiencies. She emphasised that TIA’s stated value of “excellence” was inconsistent with its repeated audit failures. She urged the Agency to develop and present a revised corrective action plan, rather than relying on previous, unproductive approaches.
She also raised concerns about whether TIA possessed adequate capacity and resources to implement corrective actions. The persistent shortcomings suggested a lack of internal capability and possible training gaps among staff. She asked what capacity-building, or professional development initiatives would be introduced to ensure that similar findings did not recur in future audit cycles.
Turning to the South African National Space Agency (SANSA), Dr Thembekwayo asked how the Agency planned to leverage emerging technologies, such as artificial intelligence and blockchain, to enhance service delivery and operational efficiency. She further enquired how SANSA utilised its technology and data capabilities to support sectors such as agriculture, disaster management, and transport.
She requested clarity on what sustainability measures SANSA had implemented to promote environmental conservation within its operations. Finally, she expressed concern that only eight small and medium enterprises (SMEs) had been supported in 2024/25, describing this figure as unacceptably low given SANSA’s national mandate and potential to stimulate economic participation.
Mr T Ramongalo (DA) commended SANSA for achieving a clean audit outcome, noting that such results inspired confidence among legislators and demonstrated sound governance and accountability. He said this was precisely the kind of performance Parliament expected from public entities.
However, he expressed concern that three of SANSA’s targets had not been achieved, largely due to delays in infrastructure projects and underspending on capital expenditure. He asked what measures SANSA had put in place to address these outstanding targets and to improve implementation efficiency going forward.
Referring specifically to the Matjiesfontein Deep Space Network project, which had achieved only 52% of its target against the planned 75%, Mr Ramongalo described the situation as regrettable. The project delays, reportedly caused by the cancellation of tenders for power and fencing due to unaffordability and supplier withdrawal, were a setback. He asked whether SANSA believed its revised project plan was now viable, and what risk mitigation strategies were in place to prevent further disruptions. He also sought clarity on whether these delays had financial implications for the Agency.
Turning to SANSA’s financial position, Mr Ramongalo raised concern over the reported R26.4 million deficit, which he attributed to an over-reliance on government grants. He asked how SANSA planned to diversify its revenue streams and reduce dependence on state funding, particularly through partnerships and commercial initiatives.
He also expressed alarm over SANSA’s high vacancy rate, noting that a 45% overall vacancy rate and 74% in technical roles were unacceptable. He cautioned that such levels of understaffing could jeopardise critical projects and undermine operational effectiveness. He urged SANSA to prioritise filling these posts as a matter of urgency, especially considering South Africa’s high unemployment rate.
Addressing the Technology Innovation Agency (TIA), Mr Ramongalo acknowledged that the Agency faced several challenges but commended its efforts in turning innovative ideas into commercial successes. He noted that the achievement of several performance targets was encouraging, but certain issues in the report remained worrying. He raised questions regarding the Technology Stations Programme, which played a key role in supporting SMMEs. He asked how TIA intended to address the challenges affecting this programme and improve the turnaround time for investment decisions, which had fallen below expectations. He also enquired about the steps being taken to build institutional capacity and operational efficiency.
Lastly, Mr Ramongalo highlighted concerns around irregular expenditure at TIA. He noted that the Auditor-General had previously cautioned the Agency about procurement and sourcing processes that were not fully compliant with the Public Finance Management Act (PFMA). He asked whether TIA could confidently assure the Committee that such issues would not recur, and why no consequence management had been implemented against officials found responsible.
Mr T Mjadu (MKP) expressed serious concern that the report entirely overlooked the global air pollution crisis, which claims over 7 million lives annually. He highlighted the critical issue of children breathing toxic air, describing this omission as a failure of prioritisation and a disconnect from the real dangers facing the population. He questioned how many of TIA’s projects were designed to monitor air pollution directly, and why there was no dedicated budget for life-saving air quality technologies.
He also raised concerns about TIA’s partnerships with black-owned SMMEs, noting a lack of support for local innovators developing air quality solutions. Mr Mjadu argued that this exposed TIA’s BEE commitments as superficial. He asked whether TIA could identify even a single black-owned SMME that it had supported to develop air quality technology, and how much of TIA’s budget was specifically reserved for black-owned small businesses in the green technology sector.
Regarding financial governance, Mr Mjadu highlighted that AGSA had flagged irregular expenditure at TIA for 14 years. He questioned how the Agency could be trusted with public funds given its consistent failure to adhere to basic financial rules. He asked for the names of officials responsible for irregular expenditure, the sanctions imposed, and why Parliament should approve TIA’s budget while it repeatedly failed to secure a clean audit.
He criticised TIA for emphasising "high-impact innovations” while ignoring urgent public health crises. He questioned the relevance of projects such as cosmetic body creams in addressing air pollution that kills millions annually and sought a comparison of the budget allocation for air quality projects versus less critical initiatives such as herbal teas.
Turning to SANSA, Mr Mjadu noted a “shocking failure” in the Agency’s operations. While SANSA promotes values of customer-centricity, he argued that it was unresponsive to South African SMMEs, forcing them to rely on international sources for critical weather data. He requested figures on formal partnership requests from local SMMEs over the past year, including how many were ignored or rejected, and asked for evidence of successful, transformative partnerships that created value for local businesses.
Mr Mjadu described it as a major failure that, after a year of operation, SANSA was still not the primary source of space-based data for South Africa. He asked whether SANSA would commit to a public inquiry into the breakdown in engagement with the SMME sector.
On financial performance, he stated that SANSA’s results were a “disaster hidden behind a clean audit,” citing a R226 million deficit and revenue falling R77 million short of the budget. He asked for explanations for the deficit, the names and positions of key technical staff lost, and measures taken to address these losses. He also sought information on cost-cutting measures that did not compromise vital projects and asked who was accountable for recruitment failures in hiring qualified engineers.
Finally, Mr Mjadu questioned how the public could have confidence in management that had allowed decades of unachieved projects. He urged the Minister to consider replacing the entire senior management responsible for these failures.
Mr V Nkosi (ANC) raised concerns about persistent project delays and capital underspending, which he said indicated systemic weaknesses in managing large and complex procurement projects. He emphasised the need for SANSA to address these issues.
He highlighted the Matjiesfontein project and the Space Infrastructure Hub (SIH) Satellite 1 project, noting that both had stalled after unresponsive RFP processes, which he described as a major concern.
Mr Nkosi asked what progress had been made in securing commercial partners beyond NASA to host equipment at Matjiesfontein, generate revenue, and ensure the site’s long-term sustainability. He also inquired whether delays in bilateral agreements and coordination with NASA had affected the operational readiness date for the site and future transmission plans.
He questioned what steps were being taken to overhaul project and procurement management considering significant capital underspending and the failure to achieve Strategic Outcome 5, to prevent recurring tender cancellations and project delays, as seen with Matjiesfontein and SIH.
Regarding the Earth Observation Satellite 1 (EOSat-1), Mr Nkosi asked whether the strategic benefits of supporting the SOE Denel outweighed potential risks of further delays and financial losses, given Denel’s instability. He also sought updates on progress toward securing private and international investment to accelerate the SIH project and reduce reliance on limited government funds.
He highlighted high staff vacancy rates, noting that shortages directly affected the capacity to execute complex engineering projects such as EOSat-1. He asked how SANSA was addressing the retention of scarce, specialised skills in a competitive global market. Regarding TIA, Mr Nkosi raised concerns about high administrative costs identified in the 2022 spending review and asked how TIA planned to address these, along with a weak commercialisation pipeline.
He highlighted intellectual property (IP) leakage from publicly funded research and asked for the primary causes and the concrete remedial actions TIA had implemented to safeguard socio-economic returns.
Mr Nkosi also questioned the absence of disciplinary measures for irregular expenditure. He requested a detailed account of why consequence management had not been implemented, as well as measures now in place to ensure compliance with Section 51 of the PFMA.
Finally, he addressed TIA’s performance in supporting persons with disabilities, noting that 2024/25 targets had not been met. He asked what specific interventions would be implemented to address systemic barriers preventing a greater number of innovators with disabilities from receiving support.
Ms N Mazzone (DA) highlighted that the primary challenge facing TIA was duplicated efforts across multiple entities under DSTI, which limited funding and prevented each entity from fully executing its strategic plan. She noted that this duplication created inefficiencies in the innovation ecosystem and hindered support for entrepreneurs. Funding constraints were a critical problem, particularly given South Africa’s limited gross domestic expenditure on R&D.
She emphasised that TIA had structural issues that required attention and urged the Board to indicate to the Committee how it could assist in addressing these challenges. She stressed that the Committee did not want to repeatedly highlight TIA’s problems but instead sought solutions and actionable requests from the Board to fix the Agency’s persistent issues.
Regarding SANSA, Ms Mazzone acknowledged the valuable work being done by the science and space community and expressed pride in the achievements of South African space initiatives. However, she raised serious concerns regarding SANSA’s partnership with Denel, noting that Denel had been omitted from presentations despite owing SANSA significant amounts of money. She argued that SANSA appeared to be sustaining Denel financially, essentially acting as a lifeline for the struggling SOE. She cited media, technical reports, and professional commentary that described the SANSA–Denel partnership using terms such as “lifeline” and “saviour,” highlighting the risks involved.
She questioned the rationale behind reviving the partnership with Denel, emphasising its financial instability, reputational risks, and logistical challenges. Ms Mazzone stressed that the EOSat-1 project was a critical strategic asset for South Africa, essential for national sovereignty, and expressed concern over the reliance on Denel for its development. She challenged SANSA for referring to private sector criticism rather than addressing internal project issues.
Ms Mazzone noted that South African technical expertise already existed domestically. Engineers, scientists, and technicians had remained in-country, with some working at firms like Dragonfly, which had successfully launched its own EO Satellite. She questioned why SANSA was allocating millions of rands to a project largely achievable by other South African firms and why existing, more up-to-date IP was not being leveraged. She called into question the logic of prioritising Denel over SMEs when funding was scarce and value for money was critical.
She requested clarity on the status and progress of the EOSat-1 project with Denel and asked whether SANSA had absolute assurance of Denel’s ability to deliver, given its history of failures. She emphasised compliance with the PFMA, noting that proper market analysis must precede awarding government tenders. Ms Mazzone requested written confirmation from SANSA’s CEO that Denel had not been forced upon SANSA and that the SOE status had not influenced the re-awarding of contracts.
Finally, she raised concerns about cancelled Denel tenders to private entities for satellite parts and IP, requesting an explanation for these cancellations. While she supported SANSA’s exploration of newer, low-cost satellite options, she urged the Agency to prioritise investing in local SMEs and making use of domestic capabilities.
Ms T Mchunu (ANC) expressed concern over TIA’s governance performance, noting that internal control weaknesses were a significant issue. She asked how TIA planned to strengthen and regularly monitor its internal control environment to address these weaknesses and ensure full compliance with governance policies and regulations, particularly considering increasing cybersecurity threats and operational inefficiencies highlighted in the report.
She raised questions regarding the functioning of TIA’s audit committee at the board level, emphasising its critical role in maintaining effective internal controls.
Ms Mchunu proposed that the Department implement closer oversight to address recurring issues identified by AGSA, noting that TIA’s annual financial statements had been flagged for three consecutive years. She suggested quarterly monitoring by the Department, including presentations to the Committee on TIA’s progress in areas such as consequence management, expenditure management, and procurement management. She asked for an update on TIA’s progress in addressing these issues and requested clarification on any delays or stagnation.
She stressed that the Department’s audit committee needed to collaborate closely with TIA’s internal auditors and audit committee. This coordination, she argued, would enable TIA, the Department, and the Committee to identify challenges proactively and implement corrective measures before AGSA’s audits.
Ms Mchunu noted that SANSA had achieved a clean audit but cautioned that the entity’s financial position still raised concerns. She requested that both TIA and SANSA provide the Committee with their liquidity ratios to assess financial stability. She further asked whether there were any challenges related to financial sustainability, and how SANSA planned to address the shortfall in capital expenditure, resource constraints, and workload pressures.
Responses from TIA
Mr Loyiso Tyira, Chairperson of the TIA Board, thanked the Committee for their engagement and questions, noting that they reflected genuine concern for TIA’s performance and the organisation’s ability to function optimally.
He explained that the internal reviews conducted at TIA were necessary to ensure the Agency was fit for purpose and had informed the development of the current strategic plan. These reviews had significant implications, including the exit of several employees at executive and senior management levels. At present, TIA has only two executives, both of whom have served in acting capacities as CEO. The CFO was currently acting as CEO, and an acting CFO had been appointed prior to the audit to ensure timely completion.
Mr Tyira highlighted the pressures on leadership, recounting that an acting executive had indicated the toll of managing multiple roles simultaneously. He emphasised that TIA had historically achieved a great deal despite very limited senior management capacity. He warned that the instability at executive and senior levels had serious implications for performance and project delivery.
He reported that the moratorium on appointments had been lifted, and TIA had been authorised to appoint a permanent CEO. This process was nearing completion, with the expectation that a permanent CEO would be in place by 1 November. Recruitment of additional executives was underway to fully capacitate the executive layer, enabling TIA to implement corrective measures effectively. Mr Tyira expressed confidence that, with the approval of the five-year strategic plan, performance would improve significantly.
He stated that next year, with a fully functioning executive and senior management team, TIA would be able to demonstrate better outcomes. He noted that TIA had recently been recognised by the Department of Planning, Monitoring and Evaluation (DPME) for excellence in its control environment for fund disbursement, highlighting that proper staffing was critical to achieving the organisation’s objectives.
Regarding turnaround and operational efficiency, Mr Tyira explained that the leadership challenges previously mentioned had contributed to delays. To address this, TIA had initiated a business process mapping exercise, followed by business process engineering, which would be the foundation of the organisation’s digital transformation programme. This initiative aimed to automate key processes, ensure adequate resourcing, and improve overall organisational efficiency.
In response to questions on TIA’s transformation agenda, Mr Tyira explained that the Board had ensured a clear directive for transformation in the 2025/26 Annual Performance Plan (APP), incorporating a sliding scale to track progress.
Regarding efforts to increase participation of people with disabilities, TIA had adopted the Quadruple Helix model of innovation. This engagement model enabled TIA to work closely with civil society, including associations representing people with disabilities, to receive guidance and ensure inclusive innovation initiatives.
Mr Tyira expressed agreement with Ms Mazzone’s remarks, emphasising that TIA envisaged itself as a curator within the National System of Innovation (NSI). TIA aimed to improve coordination not only within the Department but across the country, engaging with other government entities and the private sector. He requested the Committee’s support for TIA’s corporate strategy, which would be presented to the Committee, to enable better coordination of innovation efforts regardless of the source of funding.
On the perception of TIA as the “problem child,” Mr Tyira stated that strategic-level challenges were being addressed with the Minister’s support. He highlighted that the moratorium on appointments had been lifted, allowing TIA to fill critical positions, which had been a key constraint. He acknowledged the importance of receiving a budget aligned with TIA’s mandate and noted that efforts were underway to leverage additional funding from partners to ensure coordinated innovation efforts across government and private sector resources. He concluded that the greatest challenge remained the lack of coordination of funds to achieve shared outcomes.
Regarding the Audit and Risks, Mr Thabiso Ramasike, member of the TIA Board and Chair of Audit and Risk Committee, provided further context regarding the issues raised by Committee Members, noting that he was an outgoing member of the TIA Board. He highlighted that over the past eight years, TIA had experienced significant leadership instability, with four CEOs and five chairpersons. The CEO position had remained vacant for more than seven years. During the last four years, TIA had undergone three internal reviews.
Drawing on his experience as Chair of the Audit and Risk Committee at both UNISA, where the university achieved a clean audit for the first time in 2016, and at the SA Revenue Service (SARS) and emphasised that the core challenges at TIA were not complex but boiled down to two key factors: instability and insufficient capacity. He stressed that an organisation could not function effectively without a CEO or adequate senior management.
He noted that TIA had transitioned to “TIA 2.0,” shifting from an operational organisation to a curator within the National System of Innovation (NSI). In this new role, TIA would move away from high-volume transactional operations, which require substantial capacity, and instead focus on strategic coordination. He added that the Board had operated with fewer than three members over the past six years, which compounded operational challenges. High vacancy rates, large volumes of low-value transactions, and post-investment monitoring gaps had strained the organisation. These gaps, she noted, were exacerbated by manual processes and underscored the need for digitisation. She also highlighted that TIA had been consistently underfunded over the past eight years.
Regarding audit findings, Mr Ramasike identified four key issues. He noted that TIA had changed auditors, and some findings related to property and equipment arose during this transition. Defunding and operational changes contributed to material findings being raised. He emphasised that for effective oversight, the Committee needed to see two critical elements: management stability and sufficient capacity. Once these were in place, meaningful improvement would be possible.
On consequence management, Mr Ramasike highlighted that TIA had taken decisive action, including the dismissal of an executive responsible for irregular expenditure. He noted that while consequence management had sometimes been delayed, it was being implemented and would continue. Quarterly progress updates, as suggested by the Committee, were being considered to strengthen oversight and stressed that the Board would not fail to act against executives or management who did not comply with TIA policies and regulations.
Mr Ramasike further stated that TIA would provide the Committee with clear reporting on steps taken regarding irregular expenditure and the implementation of consequence management. While AGSA had expressed concerns about the scale of issues, it was important to note that corrective measures were underway.
He also highlighted TIA’s efficient use of funds, noting that in the previous strategic planning period, administrative costs had been planned at less than 30 cents of every rand, with the remainder directed toward investments. Mr Ramasike also addressed strategic risks, noting that one of TIA’s top risks was disintermediation, which aligned with concerns raised by Ms Mazzone regarding duplicated efforts across the innovation ecosystem. This had been formally recognised as a strategic risk by the Board.
Mr Ramasike confirmed that TIA had a plan in place for strengthening internal controls, emphasising that the effectiveness of any plan depended on its implementation. He noted that TIA was already submitting quarterly performance reports to the Department, and going forward, these reports could include updates specifically on internal controls.
He assured the Committee that the Audit and Risk Committee actively monitored cybersecurity threats and that TIA employed robust technology and systems to manage these risks effectively.
Regarding the role of the Audit and Risk Committee, Mr Ramasike stated that it functioned effectively within its mandate. However, he noted that even a strong committee could not fully compensate for instability in leadership or insufficient human capacity. TIA’s challenges were largely the result of underinvestment in personnel, particularly at the executive level, which had contributed to the unique difficulties the organisation faced compared to other DSTI entities. He emphasised that TIA had the strongest internal audit function, which was relied upon by external auditors. The recent change in auditors was also positive, as a new auditor may identify issues previously deemed compliant.
On financial matters, he confirmed that TIA’s liquidity ratio remained positive, indicating that the Agency did not require borrowing and operated within the funds allocated to it.
Mr Ramasike thanked the Committee members for their engagement and input. As the outgoing Chair of the Audit and Risk Committee, he expressed his hope to see a fully capacitated TIA with a permanent CEO in place. He stressed that the issues facing TIA were not complex and, with the planned interventions, the likelihood of these challenges recurring in the future was minimal.
Mr Tyira added that TIA was actively addressing capacitation challenges and that significant progress was being made in this regard.
Regarding the IP Leakages, Ms Anati Canca, Board Member and Chair of the Board Technical Committee addressed the issue of intellectual property (IP) leakages, noting that it had been a long-standing concern for TIA. She explained that while TIA had initially begun working on the problem with the aim of finding an internal solution, early findings revealed that the issue was far broader than anticipated and extended beyond TIA’s mandate.
To address this, TIA was leading a multi-institutional task team to examine the problem comprehensively. She emphasised the importance of first understanding the nature and extent of IP leakages, including which forms of IP were affected and where leakages were occurring. The Intellectual Property Rights (IPR) Act governs publicly funded IP, and relevant institutions include higher education institutions, science councils, and other public research and technology organisations. Determining whether leakages were systemic or concentrated in specific institutions was critical to formulating effective solutions.
Under the TIA 2.0 strategy, IP management was prioritised in TIA’s curatorship role. Although TIA did not produce the IP, it was committed to overseeing and understanding the issue. Once the problem was fully mapped, solutions would require coordinated, multi-institutional action. Ms Canca highlighted that the analysis would not only focus on the causes of IP leakage but also assess its impact on achieving national goals. Where necessary, the initiative could inform potential legislative and policy amendments to better safeguard publicly funded IP.
Ms Sebenzile Matsebula, Board Member and Chair of HR & Remuneration Committee highlighted TIA’s initiatives targeting persons with disabilities. She noted that within a short period, TIA had launched a Disability Technology and Innovation Programme aimed at creating meaningful impact on the lives of people with disabilities.
While past funding for disability-related programmes had been relatively low, TIA had conducted a targeted process to establish a programme that addressed these gaps. Early results showed that individuals with disabilities had already developed innovations making tangible contributions, demonstrating excellence in the initiative. A call for proposals had also been issued to ensure the programme’s continued productivity, and it had been integrated into TIA’s key performance areas to allow for proper monitoring of implementation.
Mr Patrick Krappie, Executive Innovation Enabling, addressed the Committee on the Technology Stations Programme, highlighting its significance as one of the country’s key systemic capabilities. He noted that few other organisations provided SMMEs with access to the necessary infrastructure to develop prototypes, gain technological support, and meet corporate standards. Technology stations played a critical role in delivering these services.
He acknowledged that funding remained a challenge, with technology stations relying heavily on the Department and TIA for support. The total budget across all stations was approximately R100 million. This financial dependency posed a risk, as contracted staff could be lost if funding was insufficient, reducing overall capability. To address this, TIA was exploring ways to enhance the financial sustainability of technology stations, including allowing stations to propose revenue development strategies and improve connections with companies requiring their services.
Mr Krappie explained that TIA had a steering committee with the Department to brainstorm solutions, with technology stations aligned to thematic focus areas. TIA was submitting proposals to secure additional funding, and stations were actively contributing input on the type of support needed and the skills being developed. He emphasised that the services provided by technology stations were unique on the continent, positioning them to assist entrepreneurs regionally. Given fiscal constraints, creative funding approaches were necessary, and TIA was working to raise awareness about the programme’s capabilities.
On green technologies, Mr Krappie noted that TIA had a programme with the United Nations Industrial Development Organisation (UNIDO) focusing on clean technologies and various renewable solutions.
Regarding air quality, TIA allocated approximately R11 million annually toward green technology initiatives addressing climate change. TIA also hosted an annual gala to recognise successful entrepreneurs in the green technology space, and including the Committee in this event could help provide insights into technologies relevant to air quality. He added that at least one technology station had undertaken a project specifically to monitor air quality, demonstrating TIA’s active investment in this area.
Responses from SANSA
Mr Patrick Ndlovu, Chairperson of the SANSA Board, thanked the Committee for its engagement and questions. He acknowledged that SANSA faced a significant operational funding challenge. The Parliamentary Grant allocated to SANSA was consistently below the personnel costs required annually. This gap had widened over the past two years; for example, in the previous financial year, SANSA received a Parliamentary Grant of R137 million, while personnel costs amounted to R200 million. SANSA had been able to manage only because of additional revenue generated by its divisions. This funding shortfall was not a new issue, and the organisation remained under constant pressure to cover the gap.
Regarding project implementation, Mr Ndlovu confirmed that SANSA recognised the challenges highlighted by the Committee. The Board shared the concern and had established a programme to address these issues. SANSA historically lacked programme-level project management, meaning resources were not always optimally allocated. While programme-level management structures were being implemented, the rollout had been slow.
On capacity, Mr Ndlovu explained that the space engineering function had been severely under-resourced before the current Board’s tenure, with only three staff members responsible for managing projects. Over the past few years, SANSA had been building this capacity, and the team had now grown to seven members, with further expansion planned. The pace of growth had been constrained by financial limitations.
To address financial sustainability and the deficit, SANSA recognised that reliance solely on state funding was not viable. Mr Ndlovu referred to the space investment strategy presented to the Committee last year, which aimed to identify investment opportunities and strengthen partnerships, including with SMMEs. The strategy was being finalised, with management expected to present the first version by the end of the month. The Board anticipated that this strategy would enable SANSA to leverage upstream and downstream partners to deliver on its mandate more effectively. He expressed optimism that, by the next Committee engagement, SANSA would be able to demonstrate that the space investment strategy had been approved, and implementation had commenced.
Mr Patrick Ndlovu, Chairperson of the SANSA Board, thanked the Committee for its questions and engagement. He noted that there was a clear funding challenge: the Parliamentary Grant allocated to SANSA was significantly below the personnel costs required annually. Over the past two years, the grant had decreased, with R137 million allocated in the previous financial year, while personnel costs amounted to R200 million. SANSA was able to operate only because divisions within the organisation generated revenue. This funding gap was a persistent challenge, and SANSA was doing its best to generate additional revenue to bridge it.
Regarding project implementation challenges, Mr Ndlovu acknowledged the Committee’s concerns and confirmed that SANSA shared them. The Board had established a programme to address these issues, noting that SANSA had previously lacked programme-level project management. Planning at a programme level was being implemented to ensure resources were appropriately allocated, though the process was slow.
On capacity, Mr Ndlovu explained that prior to the current Board, the space engineering function at SANSA had almost no capacity, with only three people responsible for major projects. Over the past few years, this team has been expanded to seven members, with further growth planned. The slow pace was largely due to financial constraints.
In addressing financial sustainability and the budget deficit, SANSA recognised that reliance on state funding alone was not sustainable. The space investment strategy, presented to the Committee last year, was being finalised, with management expected to present the first version by the end of the month. The strategy would also address partnerships with SMMEs, recognising that SANSA could not fulfil its mandate alone and needed upstream and downstream partners. The Board was hopeful that by the next engagement, the Committee would be able to see progress on the strategy’s approval and implementation.
On Denel, Mr Ndlovu noted that SANSA had previously discussed related issues with the Committee. He acknowledged ongoing questions regarding corporate governance and compliance with the PFMA, and requested that the Committee allow SANSA to provide a detailed written response.
Mr Humbulani Mudau, CEO of SANSA, emphasised the need for the Committee’s support regarding the parliamentary grant, which was currently insufficient to cover compensation costs. Personnel costs were nearly R200 million, with total expenditure around R536 million. For SANSA to operate efficiently and deliver on its mandate to build strategic and sovereign capabilities, it required a grant of approximately R375 million. He highlighted that Africa’s space sector had historically lagged due to underfunding, which was unsustainable. Over the years, SANSA and DSTI have invested in globally competitive intellectual property and technologies.
Mr Mudau warned that reliance on international funding posed a risk due to shifting geopolitical conditions. Over the past five years, SANSA has generated over R440 million from international partners, but this could change. SANSA was committed to developing the Deep Space Network Grant Station to strengthen national and indigenous capabilities, not only hosting international partners.
SANSA was also leveraging Fourth Industrial Revolution technologies to build operational systems that respond to service delivery needs while supporting environmental sustainability and governance. The organisation had funded eight start-ups with DSTI funding, deliberately supporting 50% youth-led and 50% women-led enterprises. Over the past five years, SANSA has allocated about 42% of its funding to SMMEs, with 49% being black owned in the last financial year.
The Board highlighted SANSA’s efforts over the past four years to achieve clean audits. On capital spending, the deviation in 2024/25 was only R2 million, with R105 million spent out of a R107 million allocation. Satellite projects and the Deep Space Network Grant Station were multi-year initiatives requiring phased disbursement. Delays were also influenced by factors outside SANSA’s control, such as Eskom power supply costs, which required SANSA to explore alternatives.
Regarding the vacancy rate, the 74% figure relates specifically to the engineering department. Eleven engineers had now been appointed, which had already resulted in visible progress on the upgrade of the Assembly Integration Testing Facility and the development of EOSat-1.
Mr Mudau stated that he would investigate allegations that certain SMMEs had been ignored or denied access to data, noting that this had never been brought to his attention. SANSA worked inclusively with all stakeholders. The organisation had recently launched a Natural Resource Management Programme involving traditional councils, local universities, and other partners. The programme aimed to empower communities by demonstrating the benefits of space applications, creating local SMMEs capable of providing services to communities and partnering with municipalities.
With strengthened capacity in the engineering department, Mr Mudau indicated that delays were now less of an issue. He emphasised that while some underspending and delays may have appeared, SANSA was executing its plan and making progress. The organisation remained on track to deliver the Matjiesfontein project and launch EOSat-1 by 2027.
On international partnerships, he highlighted the importance of maintaining a balance. A national space programme should be fully funded domestically, while SANSA would continue to leverage South Africa’s geographic advantages and space diplomacy to attract additional funding. The deep space programme targeted advanced and commercial entities seeking capabilities beyond Mars. SANSA was also exploring potential BRICS partnerships, including the construction of an 18-metre dish, and evaluating the sustainability of the Matjiesfontein project.
Mr Mudau confirmed that there had been no financial loss related to EOSat-1 in collaboration with Denel. SANSA was reviewing its growth and investment strategy to leverage emerging opportunities across Africa. He noted that staffing challenges were a consequence of cost-cutting measures and that further appointments would be constrained until the Parliamentary Grant increased, which would also support greater operational efficiency.
He acknowledged that SANSA’s brand equity domestically was not where it should be and that the organisation needed to better highlight its successes. SANSA recognised the significant contributions of South African companies, which were now participating internationally as satellite builders and component suppliers. The strategy for EOSat-1 aimed to ensure that all local players could participate, thereby growing the domestic space industry. A detailed submission on EOSat-1 procurement had been provided to DSTI and could be shared with the Committee.
Regarding financial performance, Mr Brighton Jena, CFO, noted a reported deficit of R26 million, which was after accounting for depreciation. On a cash basis, SANSA had a surplus of R27 million. The rising deficit was an undesired trend linked primarily to the low Parliamentary Grant, which was insufficient to meet operational needs. Despite efforts to reduce costs in areas such as travel and conferences, the organisation was constrained, limiting its ability to fill vacancies and maintain full liquidity.
SANSA’s liquidity ratio at the end of March 2025 stood at 1.14, slightly lower than the 1.18 recorded in the previous year. This indicated that SANSA’s current assets were 1.14 times its current liabilities, reflecting ongoing liquidity pressures.
Mr Ndlovu assured the Committee that SANSA would provide written responses to any outstanding questions.
Chairperson’s Closing Remarks
The Chairperson thanked TIA and SANSA for their responses and engagement, noting that the Committee appreciated the detailed and comprehensive information provided.
Regarding TIA, the Chairperson emphasised the Committee’s commitment to closely monitor the entity, as recommended by AGSA. Regular updates were requested, and the Committee expressed its expectation that vacant and acting positions would be filled and that the Board would be fully capacitated. Continuous engagement would be maintained to ensure measurable improvements in TIA’s performance.
With respect to SANSA, the Chairperson highlighted the need to improve connectivity in rural areas and urged SANSA to ensure that satellite programmes address this disparity. While collaboration with international partners was acknowledged, SANSA was reminded to be clear on the terms and conditions of any external funding. The Committee confirmed that there would be a further engagement with SANSA to discuss the issues concerning Denel.
The Chairperson concluded by thanking all members and officials from TIA and SANSA for their attendance and constructive participation.
Meeting was adjourned.
