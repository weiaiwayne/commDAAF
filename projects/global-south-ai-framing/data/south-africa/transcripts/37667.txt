Meeting Summary
Air Traffic and Navigation Services (ATNS)
Airports Company SA (ACSA)
Three entities of the Department of Transport—Air Traffic & Navigation Services; the South African Civil Aviation Authority; and Airports Company South Africa—briefed the Committee on their 2022/23 Annual Reports. The meeting took place in person in Parliament.
The meeting was a continuation of the
previous day
, where the Committee met with the Minister of Transport, together with the respective senior officials. In the previous day’s meeting, the Auditor-General took the Committee through the performance reports of all the entities within the Department.
Air Traffic Navigation Services
Revenue: R1.4-billion; Loss R42 million: Audit outcome: unqualified with findings
50% of the staff are women employees. The entity has had stable leadership over the years. There have been no fatalities from scheduled air travel in South Africa during the 30 years of its existence.
South African Civil Aviation Authority
Revenue: R0.9-billion; Surplus R42 million: Audit outcome: clean audit
The Authority received more than 91% in the recent audit by the International Civil Aviation Organisation. South Africa is the first country to go beyond 90% on the African continent.
Airports Company South Africa
Revenue: R6-billion; Loss: R143 million: Audit outcome: unqualified
The 2023 financial year proved to be more challenging than anticipated due to the ongoing effects of the COVID-19 pandemic, and the far-reaching impact of the war in Ukraine.
The Committee Members engaged with all three reports and the performance of the entities in detail.
Members asked for comment on the unfair and non-competitive procurement practices within the Air Traffic Navigation Services that the Auditor General had flagged. They raised specific problems on budget limitations which could lead to a lack of maintenance of the air navigation systems and efforts to address staff shortages. They pointed to occasions where there have not been any air traffic navigation services in Polokwane, Cape Town and Johannesburg when planes were unable to land. There are cases where the ground services are said to be closed due to staff shortages. Members suggested that the entities involved should find innovative ways to find people to employ. The Air Traffic Navigation Services (and the two other entities) should go into the schools to get young people into the air transport sector. Why were the outreach efforts at schools restricted to just four provinces? Does the entity adhere to the 30-day payment policy? The presentation did not make any reference to transformation within the entity.
Members also raised the transformation issue when questioning the Civil Aviation Authority. The gap between the number of black pilots in comparison to white pilots is huge. How does the Authority intend to address the organisation's risk-related issues raised in the report? Does the Authority have a specific target to attract more workforce to join the organisation. Were the learners being sent to France provided bursaries, or did they have to cover their expenses? How many learners were provided with a bursary? Members commended the Authority for its consistent, clean audit outcome, its high ratings and international accreditation. They asked for a final report on what happened on flight SA9053, where the aircraft had water in its fuel.
Members questioned the Airports Company on their procurement practices. Airports are still stuck with transfer buses that “should not even be in a scrap yard”. And these are the buses which transport international passengers. Is there a plan of action in place for the provision of jet fuel which will become a scarce commodity, because of the climate in the Middle East? The closure of taxiways creates a huge inconvenience for operators. ACSA needs to work together with Home Affairs on its queuing system and work at implementing better systems. Airports form part of the tourist attraction and therefore they must be well taken care of. What is the payment process with the transfer buses, and why could not there be a bridge connecting the passengers between the flight and the airport?
Both the Air Traffic Navigation Services and the Authority were given seven days to provide written answers to the concerns raised by Members that had gone unanswered due to time constraints. The Chairperson of the Airports Company suggested to the Committee that his response to questions on management of the R4,8 million tender for grass-cutting services should be submitted in writing and not for public consumption.

Air Traffic and Navigation Services (ATNS) Annual Report 2022/23
The Chairperson said that the Air Traffic and Navigation Services (ATNS) received an unqualified audit outcome with findings, and the entity would take the Committee through the areas that the AG has flagged. The Chairperson expressed her appreciation for the entity’s improved submission of its financials. The AG indicated that while the targets set out by the entity are achievable, they lack yardstick measurements. The entity has opted for the low-hanging fruits. The Chairperson highlighted that the entity needs to improve its debt collection strategy. Presentation by ATNS
Mr Zola Majavu, Chairperson of ATNS, conveyed an apology on behalf of the Chief Executive Officer, Ms Nozipho Mdawe. He was appearing before the Committee in his capacity as a Chairperson for the first time since his recent appointment. He introduced the team members accompanying him from ATNS.
Mr Majavu agreed that the concerns raised by the AG are true and there is indeed room for improvement. He said that the entity will not even begin to make any excuses, but instead, it needs to work on improving. The entity subscribes to the notion of openness and accountability and appearing before the Committee is part of those efforts to inform the Committee what has been done thus far and receive guidance on its blind spots. The comments in these engagements are not taken lightly.
As soon as the audit report was finalised the entity proceeded with engagements with the AG to find ways in which they could close the gaps. The entity, together with the AG, has devised a plan which they have been able to agree upon, which regards every month as an audit month. One of the issues that have been flagged is the issue of the entity’s vacancies which have not been filled. He confirmed that tier one has since been filled, which may not have been the case in the previous year. The entity is currently in the process of capacitating tier two, as an indication that the gaps that have been identified are being taken seriously.
The Chairperson had touched on the issue of the experience of the end-users. He agreed there has been a time when the stakeholders have not been managed and taken care of as best as they should, and there is no reason for that. The entity is currently embarking on a project to reach out to the consumers of its services because it would appear that the functions of the ATNS are not as commonly known as the entity would like. The entity has a mandate to increase its reach. He stated that in the coming year, when the entity accounts, there will be less criticism in this regard because the matter would have been addressed.
Mr Majavu said leading a team of talented and competent officials at the ATNS was an honour and a privilege. The entity is celebrating 30 years since its inception. He outlined the structure of the entity. The non-executive and executive board of directors are appointed for three years, by the shareholder. The current board started on 13 January 2023, with ten non-executives and two executives. The entity is currently undergoing an organisational redesign due to the recognition of its shortcomings. Tier one, traditionally known as the exco-level, was approved by the board and it was reduced from 15 to ten officials.
He stated that, like many businesses, it is true that the entity is still recovering from the remnants of COVID-19, however, everything is going accordingly in terms of governance and operations. The presentation will be an overview of the concerted effort of the organisation to take the organisation to a better level. The vision of the entity is to be the leading service provider of Air Traffic Management (ATM) Solutions and Associated Services in Africa. Celebrating 30 years of service is a massive milestone not only for the entity, but for the Shareholders, the Department of Transport, and the South African government. Over the past couple of years, there has been a downward trend in the entity’s non-financial performance, however, the organisation has seen an upward trajectory towards the closure of the 2022/23 financial year and it is an indication of improvements in the ATNS. He expressed his confidence that moving forward the organisation’s performance will show an upward trajectory and it will indicate the strengthened leadership.
The first quarter results for 2023/24 at the end of June were already sitting at 79% and it has been signed off and sent to the Department. The entity’s resolve is to close the current financial year with better performance outcomes.
The Chairperson handed over to the senior officials to take the Committee through the presentation.
Mr Josia Manyakoana, Chief Operating Officer (COO), took the Committee through the overview of the performance of the organisation. The mandate of the ATNS is the acquisition, establishment, provision, control, and maintenance of air traffic services, and air navigation infrastructure. The mission of the organisation is to provide safe and efficient Air Traffic Management solutions. [Please see the electronic presentation slides. ATNS: Revenue: R1.4-billion; Loss R42 million: Audit outcome: unqualified with findings]
The COO stated that the operating environment of the entity is driven by five areas namely, Society; Technology; Economy; Politics; and the Environment. The main factors influencing the operating environment are population growth, youth unemployment, and access to education and training. In terms of technology, cyber-security remains the main consideration in the aviation space at large. In terms of the economy, fuel pricing and demand and supply imbalances are highly considered. There have been a few supply chain issues relating to the procurement of services, and from the ATM perspective, the importing of services [is critical] because the entity operates in a unique and highly technical environment. The entity is working towards an area of localisation and ensuring that services and products consider the local aspect. Regarding the political environment, some of the key issues under consideration include Geopolitics; governance; and fraud and corruption. The entity is very conscious of the impact which its services have on the environment.
The strategy and the key performance indicators are based on three pillars: Service excellence; Sustainability; and Innovation. The main objective in terms of the entity’s sustainability is to maintain its financial sustainability.
Air Traffic Service Units are scattered across the country, both the statutory service provisions and the contractual service provisions. There are nine statutory airports and more than ten contractual airports.
The entity is happy to announce that it has maintained liquidity in the current financial year. 50% of the staff are women employees. The entity has had stable leadership over the years. He also highlighted that the entity has had zero accidents in the 30 years of operation.
The Aviation Training Academy has remained the flagship of the entity not only in the country but also throughout the continent. More information is available in the report.
The key risks and opportunities faced by the entity provide context to the challenges faced by the entity. The major risk was the financial sustainability risk. The risk of not being financially sustainable in the short and medium term in the aviation environment is a huge concern. The second highest risk was human resource skills and retention. A great majority of the talent from the entity is being hired by external companies. The entity continuously manages the risk of not having enough skills and not being able to retain the already existing skills, however, the entity is managing the risks through pipeline training and career development. More details on the various risks are found in the report.
The entity has five key performance areas and it only managed to achieve 72%. The ATNS did not perform well on the second Key Performance Indicator (KPA), which was related to innovation. The entity set out to achieve a total of five performance indicators and only achieved four. In the Infrastructure KPA, the entity only achieved ten out of the sixteen set out.
The Chief Financial Officer (CFO), Mr Matome Moholola, reviewed the financial performance of the entity. ATNS received an unqualified audit opinion for the financial year under review. The findings raised in the current year as compared to the previous year, have been minor and the entity is well on its way to obtaining a clean audit in the current year.
ATNS revenues are currently sitting at R1.4 billion, which is an increase of 40% compared to the previous financial year. The entity is still recovering from the effects of COVID-19, however, the strides have been made have been impactful.
The entity reported a loss of only R42 million compared to R378 million in the previous financial year. It continues to maintain a strong balance sheet with assets worth close to R2.5 billion. The entity continues to invest in long-term growth looking at the opportunities that exist both locally and internationally.
The entity has seen an upward improvement in its current ratio from 2.3 in 2022 to 3.1 in the current year. The Return on Capital Employed has also improved positively from -3.4% in 2022 to 7.8% in the current year.
The CFO touched on ATNS’s impact on the community. It has supported both primary and high schools with learning equipment and infrastructure as stipulated in the Corporate Social Investment Policy and as required by its flagship area, education. The project has reached 10,658 beneficiaries from 15 schools across Gauteng, Limpopo, Northwest and Mpumalanga.

Presentation by the South African Civil Aviation Authority
Mr Ernest Khosa, Chairperson of SACAA, introduced the team of delegates from SACAA, accompanying him. SACAA is celebrating its 25th year of existence in October. The achievements presented by the team will not be outside of the vibrant inputs made over the years by the Committee members. The entity always aims to take full accountability.
Mr Khosa said that the transformation state of the entity is represented by the delegation itself. The entity has attained a clean audit, with zero findings. He also indicated that the entity received more than 91% in the recent audit by the International Civil Aviation Organisation (ICAO), which is the first to go beyond 90% on the African continent.
In 2022, the Director of Civil Aviation, Ms Poppy Khoza, was appointed the first woman to be the president of the ICAO General Assembly, in Montreal, Canada. This is a representation of the global confidence of the entity and the overall key players in the aviation space. She has also received awards in recognition for her role in championing transformation within the aviation space. He added that the board believes that running an organisation effectively and efficiently knows no race, but it is great to watch a black woman leading an organisation predominantly known as a male-dominated industry to greater heights. Her journey remains a beacon of hope for the average girl child in rural areas.
Ms Poppy Khoza, Director: Civil Aviation, outlined the mandate of the entity. SACAA was created in terms of the Civil Aviation Act of 1998, which was amended in 2009 when all the Acts were consolidated. The Civil Aviation Act consists of six objectives. The mandate of Civil Aviation is aimed at regulating the civil aviation industry to ensure security and safety by complying with the International Civil Aviation Organization (ICAO) Standards and Recommended Practices (SARPs), taking into consideration the local context. [For full details, please see the presentation. SACAA: Revenue: R0.9-billion; Surplus R42 million: Audit outcome: clean audit]
The Civil Aviation Act is underpinned by the international obligations of which South Africa is a signatory. The ICAO, a United Nations agency, prescribes SARPs that must be complied with by all the contracting states. The obligations that need to be met by South Africa, include Aviation security; and the Aviation safety system. South Africa made a conscious decision to ensure that it has a robust system compliant with International obligations.
The organisation's core ideology is to be a world-class civil aviation regulator. The mission of the entity is to regulate civil aviation safety and security in support of the sustainable development of the aviation industry. Some of the values of the organisation include service excellence, teamwork, integrity and collaboration. More information is available in the report.
SACAA adopted a set of strategic outcomes, as signed by the Board and the Ministry. The first strategic outcome is related to the organisation's core mandate, which is a strengthened safety and security oversight system. The second strategic outcome is related to financial sustainability. More information on the strategic outcomes is available in the report.
The structure at SACAA is reviewed from time to time to ensure that there will always be a shift in terms of new requirements. The organisation also has an opportunity to always review whether the structure has adequate coverage following International standards as per the Act.
The aircraft register shows that there were a total of 15 645 aircraft as of the end of the 2022/23 financial year. The numbers fluctuate every so often when more aircraft are occupied. In April 2022, 6 523 (42.93%) Type Certified Aircraft followed a particular standard. There were 6 450 of these aircraft at the end of the financial year, 41.48% of the total. The fluctuation of the aircraft numbers is tied to the economic activity of the country; when the economy is performing well, there are typically more aircraft and vice versa.
SACAA has made strides in the transformation of aviation personnel. Under the category of Pilots, the entity has not made any significant strides that would mirror the demographics of the country. She referred to some of the initiatives from the Department of Transport, as mentioned by Mr Khosa in his remarks. She touched on issues of accessibility and affordability in terms of the courses related to Pilot training which hinder certain demographics from applying.
South Africa is sitting at a very comfortable position in terms of its incident rates compared to other countries. In more than 36 years, South Africa has recorded zero fatal accidents on scheduled airline flights. The organisation had a five-year target of maintaining a 0% accident rate and has managed to maintain the target. This speaks to the robust compliance regime adopted by the country.
On the other hand, SACAA has been experiencing challenges within the general aviation sector. Several incidents have occurred, particularly in the 2021/22 financial year. Although there has been somewhat of a reduction, the organisation is not yet where it wants to be. She highlighted some aircraft accidents recorded over the various financial years (2021/2022; 2022/2023). The Minister of Transport has tasked the entity with reducing the number of fatalities [which have occurred on non-scheduled flights] by 50% by the year 2025. The entity believes that with the current decrease in fatalities visible in the past year, it is optimistic to reach the target set out.
She highlighted the performance overview of the entity over ten years.
Mr Asruf Seedat, Chief Financial Officer, SACAA, presented a high-level overview of SACAA's financial performance. He began by highlighting the passenger numbers to paint a picture for the Committee of where the organisation came from pre-COVID. Passenger numbers are the biggest contributors to the revenue of the organisation. Passengers numbered 22.2 million at the beginning of the pandemic in 2019/20 and the decline was highly visible in the year 2020/21. There has been a steady increase in passenger numbers over the past two years.
The total revenue for the 2022/23 financial year was R885,297,799 which was an increase of 14.2% from the previous year. The biggest contributors to revenue are the safety charges, which were R455,811,640; followed by the user fees which were R102,631,608. The revenue for the 2022/23 financial year was 2.5% higher than what was budgeted for.
The CFO said the organisation's financial position has improved significantly over the last year. The total assets for the 2023 financial year, increased by 27.2%. The organisation has also indicated a healthy liquidity position. The debtors base has improved over the last year, and it has gone from 25.7 days to 22.6 days. During the time that COMAIR [an airline] went into provisional liquidation, it owed the organisation R7.2 million rand and the entity had to make provisions. He stated that the entity is working according to the amended act to have SACAA treated as a preferential creditor when it comes to the amount owing to the regulator. He said that this option would go a long way in terms of retrieving the debt owed to SACAA.
Ms Nivashnee Naraindath, Company Secretary, SACAA, gave a brief overview of the human resources within the organisation. SACAA has implemented quite a few interventions, as it is a human capital-intensive organisation. SACAA relies on its talented and skilled workforce to fulfil its mandate. The organisation has undertaken several projects in its efforts to enforce talent development; and transformation. The projects include skills development, which has seen SACAA complete a successful skills audit which assisted the organisation to identify areas of improvement and areas of strength among its workforce. SACAA has implemented a women's mentorship programme, to drive the transformation agenda forward. The SACAA organisational culture not only creates a sense of belonging for its employees but is also at the core of supporting organisational effectiveness. She said that sound employee-employer relations are at the heart of SACAA's Human Capital strategy. More information is available in the report.
Ms Phindi Gwebu, Executive for Corporate Services, SACAA, gave a brief overview of the risk overview of the organisation. The organisation is constantly aware of the risks that may hinder the regulator from achieving its strategic objectives. The report indicated the top ten risks, which include the financial sustainability of the regulator and the ability of SACAA to deliver on its mandate, which is highly reliant on having critical staff. The buy-in of the major stakeholders in important for the entity to fulfil its mandate. As an organisation that has plans to be fully digitised, she indicated the importance of innovation and technology management. More information is available in the report. SACAA launched a new contact centre on 1 November 2022, which provides a single primary point of contact for clients who require information or services. SACAA has standardised its client experience in all interactions with the organisation. The entity has been able to assist a few Southern African Countries to prepare for the possible outbreak of communicable diseases.
Ms Khoza concluded by highlighting that the South African Pilot license is held in high regard globally. There has been a migration of license holders across the globe, however. This is beginning to be a problem for South Africa because of the loss of skills. The main reason why SACAA chose to move to card licensing was to uphold the integrity - and to avoid any undermining - of the licenses.

He was a bit concerned about the issue of transformation. The gap between the number of black pilots in comparison to white pilots was quite huge. He added that the company has existed for too long to still address these gaps and it was unclear how SACAA plans to address the matter. The issue of transformation was a disaster. He asked for further clarity on the matter. Mr Sithole asked SACAA to provide more information on the statistics regarding the schools visited. He asked for the numbers in terms of schools per province, and area. He asked for clarity on how the organisation plans to address the risk-related issues. He also asked for more information on the strategy to overcome the issues related to stakeholder-related matters.
Ms Khumalo welcomed the presentation from SACAA. She indicated her appreciation for the work done by the organisation in its years of existence, particularly concerning transformation. She asked for more information on the target which was not met and the reasons behind the entity not being able to follow through with the target. She was unaware that cabin crew members had to have licenses. Some of the schools which the entity visited should have shown an interest. Had any of the schools given feedback? Did the organisation have a specific target to attract more workforce to join the organisation?
Ms Ramadwa welcomed the presentation. She commended SACAA for a consistent, clean audit outcome. She said that the good work being done by the organisation needs to correlate with the output and not just with a clean audit. She also commended the entity for the record of 36 years with zero fatalities. SACC failed to mention any specific challenges on the issue of transformation concerning people living with disabilities. She asked for further details on the issue. SACAA only mentioned the National Union of Metalworkers of South Africa (NUMSA) as a collective bargaining partner. She asked whether this was the only available union. She suggested that the organisation should also focus on raising awareness to alert people about the existence of SACAA as an entity. Were the learners being sent to France provided bursaries, or did they have to cover their expenses? How many learners were provided with a bursary?
Mr McDonald thanked the organisation for the presentation. He congratulated the entity on its ratings and international accreditation. SACAA continues to make South Africans proud. He asked for an update on the discussions between the ATNS and the ACSA on the downgrade of the fibre procedures in the previous year, to have them up and running again. He said that the process has greatly impacted civil aviation at the smaller airports. Flying into these airports during adverse weather conditions could become dangerous due to the issues with ATNS or perhaps ACSA. Could SACAA provide the Committee with a final answer on what happened on flight SA9053, where the aircraft had water in its fuel? The South African Police Services (SAPS) have continued to fail the aircraft services, due to not performing their due diligence. He said that some of these aircraft could potentially kill more than 300 people due to aircraft which are not serviceable. He said that the entities need to be brought closer to one another to manage the safety of the air navigation space. There seem to not be any consequences for pilots who are in contravention of the law. SACAA needs to think about implementing random drug testing on commercial pilots. He agreed that there needs to be transformation in the aviation space, however, training an individual from ground level up costs close to a million rands. He said that SACAA would need to consider getting private partnerships involved, as [funding] continues to constrain the number of black pilots the entity could train. He said that the issue is accessibility and it needs to be addressed to open up the air space.
Mr Zwane stated that some of these issues have persisted, however, he commended the entity on the good work that they have been doing. He referred to the placement of trained pilots currently struggling to find employment. He asked whether the government entities could push forward an initiative that would assist the trained young pilots sitting at home with their qualifications due to the lack of availability of jobs.
The Chairperson asked Mr Khosa to elaborate on the contingent liability following the aeroplane crash. Members’ comments on transformation are mindful of the journey one has to undertake to become a pilot. She said that popularising aviation space is a collective effort and responsibility. She suggested that the entity return within seven days with a detailed report for the Committee.
Response from SACAA
Mr Khosa responded to the concerns raised about transformation within the entity. In his last engagement with the Committee, similar concerns were raised. He agreed with Mr McDonald that the transformation problem was largely attributed to matters of inaccessibility and these are beyond SACAA as an entity. He agreed that it was a sectoral matter. He said that the mastery of the skies cannot be left to one race group, taking into consideration the context of South African history. SACAA is a regulator and the best it can do is regulate how the industry operates. Transformation occurs at various levels and at the level of addressing the pilots, the entity will continue to look into the matter as a sectoral project together with the Shareholder, and the Department. The Department is currently working on a National Action Transformation Strategy which will have a galactic response to the matter. SACAA has found it much more effective to take young pilots abroad to complete their studies and come back with better skills, as they end up spending more time in training which costs close to R1.8 million. The entity is doing its best to assist the young pilots.
The Chairperson said that the issue of the aircraft that crashed is in the hands of the Ministry and the Department.
Ms Khoza echoed the sentiments shared by the Chairperson on the issues of transformation. She added that there have been talks around establishing a government-owned aviation school to fast-track transformation issues. She responded to the questions raised about the risk-related issues. She indicated that where the rating is 25, it is based on an inherent risk rating before any controls. However, the residual has drastically reduced based on all the risk mitigations put in place.
She referred to the question on the unmet target. She stated that because SACAA had not had any wasteful expenditure for a long time, it took a directive from the Ministry to take some of the targets from the Minister's contract. This indicated that there needs to be a reduction of irregular expenditure by 75% which was a challenge for SACAA, considering that it has not had any irregular expenditure over 4 financial years. The discussion with the Department was that the entity cannot have a lower target than its performance. The irregular expenditure which SACAA had was immaterial in terms of the threshold, and it was a lesser amount. AG took into account that all processes were followed except for one, where there needed to be approval. There was a motivation for the irregular expenditure.
The Chairperson of the Committee suggested that the entity respond to the remaining questions in writing. The
Committee Members agreed with the suggestion from the Chairperson.
(30-minute lunch break.)
Airports Company South Africa (ACSA) Annual Report 2022/23
The meeting with ACSA was a continuation of the previous day's meeting. The Chairperson recapped some of the takeaways from the previous meeting to provide context for the report to be presented by ACSA.
Opening remarks from the ACSA Chairperson
Adv Sandile Nogxina, Chairperson of ACSA, introduced the team representing ACSA. He echoed the sentiments shared by Ms Lesoma, that the issue is not about the quantity but rather an issue of compliance. The 2023 financial year proved to be more challenging than the company had anticipated due to the ongoing impact of the COVID-19 pandemic, and the far-reaching impact of the war in Ukraine. He attributed the impact at a local level to the severe flooding which occurred in Kwa-Zulu Natal in April. ACSA also experienced difficulties with its supply chain throughout the year, and the increase in jet fuel prices amplified the situation. The closure of two major airlines reduced sitting capacity by nearly half the capacity. The entity has displayed remarkable adaptability in navigating these challenges and reconciling the issues.
The company managed to obtain a secure recovery position before the end of the year, and it looks forward to a brighter future. Implementing the recovery strategy and the revised corporate plan has placed the entity in a position to remain sustainable in the short term and develop in the medium-term and long-term. The Chairperson assured the Committee of the strong leadership capabilities possessed by the company. ACSA is beginning to implement the growth strategy which was approved in the previous financial period. The strategy will be used as a bridge between the recovery phase and the growth which is envisioned. Shareholders have been unfailing in their support of the efforts being made by the company on its road to recovery. ACSA has embedded a culture of agility and decisive leadership.
Adv Nogxina said ACSA is committed to the transformation project. ACSA’s focus on sustainability and growth will always be supported by the focus on economic transformation. The success of the business depends mainly on the ability to deliver value to the end-user. ACSA continues to play a critical role in the transformation within the aviation industry through maximising commercial opportunities in the short and medium term.
He said that the report would deal with the performance of the organisation and answer some of the areas raised by the AG.
Report from ACSA
Ms Mpumi Mpofu, CEO, ACSA, briefly highlighted the inner workings of ACSA as a business. She stated that there are matters that the entity had previously discussed with the Portfolio Committee, and may not be discussed in great detail during the presentation. [See full details in the electronic documentation. ACSA: Revenue: R6-billion; Loss R143 million: Audit outcome: unqualified]
While ACSA has faced a slow recovery, it has been able to recover in a very volatile geopolitical climate. The entity has shown resilience in the challenges which were faced.
ACSA highlighted an issue which resulted in irregular expenditure which had been discussed in the Committee in the previous year. The issue translated into legal action taken against ACSA by an unsuccessful bidder for a grass-cutting tender. The issue brought forth issues related to the replacement contract, which resulted in irregular expenditure, but when it was examined, ACSA was able to determine the problem with the awarded contract. There was a legal section that referred to interdicting ACSA from appealing. There was also a matter of an investigation done by the ACSA to get to the bottom of the problem. The issue was related to the grass-cutting contract, and the investigation had been concluded. All the employees involved in this matter have been placed on suspension pending the finalisation of the enquiry. ACSA has laid criminal charges in conjunction with the disciplinary proceedings. ACSA has appointed an external individual to deal with the disciplinary proceedings. The AG was happy to see that even when dealing with historical cases, the company does not take consequence management lightly.
The Chairperson concluded by indicating that ACSA is closely watching the geopolitical climate which is unfolding in the Middle East. ACSA relies heavily on the resources found in the Middle East, and the tension may also impact the travelling passengers.
Discussion
Mr McDonald asked for comment on the fact that the AG flagged ACSA for uncompetitive and unfair procurement processes. He said there is still no transformation on the grounds of the aviation industry. He highlighted that airports are still stuck with Irish companies, Menzies Aviation, and Swissport, just to mention a few. He raised concerns concerning the poor condition of the Menzies transfer buses. He stated that they should not even be in a scrap yard; these buses transport international passengers. He said that the buses are not only associated with Menzies but with South African airports. He indicated that small things can elevate an airport to the best in the world.
He said that there needs to be a plan of action in place for the provision of jet fuel because South Africa will not be producing any jet fuel in the near future. With the geopolitical climate in the Middle East, jet fuel will slowly start becoming a scarce commodity. He suggested that the budget of ACSA should start considering the expansion of the availability of jet fuel, as well as maintenance.
Several airports are experiencing closure of the taxiways, specifically concerning the OR Tambo International Airport. The closure creates a huge inconvenience for big aeroplane companies such as Emirates and is a high risk. ACSA needs to work together with the Department of Home Affairs on its queuing system and work at implementing better systems. He said that the airport is the first point of entry into the country and is not a good site for international visitors when long queues go unattended for long periods.
Ms Khumalo commended ACSA for maintaining the titles of “best airports”, after the concerns raised by the Committee members on the conditions of airports around the country. Airports form part of the tourist attraction for many countries and therefore they must be well taken care of.
Ms Ramadwa welcomed the presentation from ACSA. She asked ACSA to elaborate on the community project that it has undertaken and share details on the province it is working with and which Early Childhood Development (ECD) centres it is working with. She was happy that the grass-cutting issue was concluding eventually. [This concerned the management of a R4.8 million ACSA tender for grass-cutting services.]
What is the payment process with the transfer buses and why could not there be a bridge connecting the passengers between the flight and the airport?
Response from ACSA
Adv Nogxina stated that the issues raised by the Committee members are issues that have been a problem for a long time, specifically the inherited ever-green projects. He said that some of the contracts date back to the times when OR Tambo was called Jan Smuts Airport. He indicated that there are several legal constraints that the entity has to deal with. The entity has made creative efforts in dealing with these issues, and he suggested to the Committee that his elaborate response on the matter should be submitted in writing and not for public consumption.
The CEO said the issues related to the unfair procurement processes and the uncompetitiveness have been investigated and it was found that there was bias in the bid-adjudication committees where the matters were decided. The investigations lead the entity to a clear path of consequence management. This challenge emanates from a process in State Owned Entities that combine bid specifications with bid evaluation—this is not allowed in government.
ACSA licenses all ground-handling activities. The agreement was that the mentioned companies would be the ground-handling companies for the last decade. The CEO indicated that the Swissport is no longer under ACSA, and other companies have occupied the space. The challenges the ground handling services face reflect its financial standing due to the effects of COVID-19.
ACSA has been able to implement border management which is great progress on its part. The Department of Home Affairs operations are now under the Border Management Authority (BMA). While the transition to BMA is currently underway, ACSA has found that the BMA is considerably short-staffed. BMA has been engaging with ACSA on ways to assist and these engagements will yield positive results.
There are several ECD projects, namely in Upington, and in KZN. The airports are making considerable efforts to bring positive change in the communities nearest to them. The project is aimed at interventions, assisting, and providing funding for these facilities.
The CFO referred to a question related to the parking charges for an aircraft. She said that these charges are regulated and are charged per 24 hours. The charge is based on the maximum take-off weight.
Ms Gwebu indicated that it is much better when an airline has a contact stand, as it creates efficiency in the entire process. The availability of contact stands is also dependent on the demand thereof. There are general agreements in the airport space for the notification of a connecting airline and the process is fast-tracked.
Concerning the taxiway which has been closed - there was a non-performing contractor and ACSA had to cancel the contract. The work currently being done will be concluded in October.
The meeting was adjourned.
