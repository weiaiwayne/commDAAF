Annual Reports 2024/25
The Portfolio Committee convened virtually to receive briefings on the annual performance reports of the Department of Home Affairs (DHA) and the Border Management Authority (BMA) for the 2024/25 financial year, and to receive clarification on legislative matters pertaining to the One-Stop Border Post (OSBP) Bill.
National Treasury clarified the revised clause six of the OSBP Bill, confirming that the movement of goods or money by officials for official purposes within the common control zone was not subject to the import or export laws of South Africa or the adjoining state. Treasury submitted that there was no revenue implication that required a money bill in terms of section 77 of the Constitution. The matter was settled, and the item was deferred for deliberation and A-list consideration at the next meeting.
The DHA reported achieving 83% of its targets, representing a 12% increase in performance from the previous financial year. Significant achievements included the over-issuance of Smart ID Cards (3.6 million issued against a 2.5 million target) and a dramatic improvement in immigration services, such as general work visas processed on time rising to 98%. Key unmet targets included automated birth functionality, e-Visa prototype development -- which was now shifting to Electronic Travel Authorisation (ETA) -- and digitisation volume, which had fallen short of the 14.8 million target.
The Department had received a qualified audit opinion, with material findings on compliance. Qualification areas were related to understated contingent liabilities and immovable assets being overstated by R101 million. The Department committed to achieving an unqualified audit outcome for 2025/26, and had established an audit oversight committee chaired by the Director-General.
The DHA described a strategic shift away from the "brick and mortar approach" towards digital transformation. Priority was being given to integration with the banking platform over kiosk integration. R20.63 million had been spent on 91 kiosks before the integration partner was terminated due to underperformance. The new strategy, secured by master agreements with seven banks, aimed to expand the footprint into hundreds of locations at no cost to the DHA. This expansion was designed to accelerate the phase-out of the 16 million remaining green ID books. The Department affirmed that data security was paramount, and the model ensured biometric data remained under state control via secure application programming interface integration, protecting Home Affairs data from third-party misuse.
The BMA reported achieving 81% of its 16 annual targets, improving from 60% in the previous year. Achievements included increased joint law enforcement operations, and 100% refusal of entry/deportation of illegal foreigners detected (24 299 persons). Targets not met included full installation of network connectivity due to challenging port locations and verifiable processing of time data due to movement control system instability. The Authority had incurred a R90.5 million deficit, resulting in a qualified audit opinion. The Commissioner stated that the BMA remained grossly underfunded, with 85% of the budget allocated to compensation of employees. The transfer of South African Police Service policing functions had remained stalled due to administrative resistance, despite legal clarity and a decision by the Justice, Crime Prevention and Security cluster. The Minister confirmed there had been direct engagement with the Minister of Finance, and asserted that Parliament must use its power to appropriate funds adequately for the BMA, given its critical mandate.
The Committee adopted the fourth term programme for 2025 with amendments, and concluded the meeting by adopting outstanding minutes.

National Treasury briefing on the One-Stop Border Post
Bill
– clause six
Adv Empie van Schoor, Chief Director: Legislation, NT, presented the clarification on Clause six of the One-Stop Border Post (OSBP) Bill [B12-2024], which had been introduced by the Minister of Home Affairs.
The purpose of the Bill was to regulate the establishment of one-stop border posts between South Africa and its adjoining states and common control zones. The proposed revised clause six stipulates that the movement of goods and money by officials for official purposes within the common control zone is not subject to entry or exit import or export laws of the Republic or the adjoining state.
Adv Van Schoor explained that:
The application of Clause six was limited in scope to movement "within the common control zone," and only "for official purpose".
The common control zone was effectively regarded as being in South Africa, even if it may be partially or fully located in the adjoining state (for example, Botswana or Zimbabwe).
If officials move goods or money for official purposes between South Africa and a neighbouring country within a shared border zone, those items are not subject to imports or exports in the Republic or the adjoining state.
Officials may freely use and transfer necessary equipment within the zone for official purposes, provided that proper records and declarations are made.
NT submitted that there was no revenue implication on the revised formulation of clause six that required a money Bill in terms of section 77 of the Constitution.

DHA briefing on its annual report and ICT footprint
Minister’s overview
Minister Schreiber acknowledged the importance of the discussion, particularly the focus on the audit and performance outcomes.
He provided context for the 2024/2025 annual performance report (APR), stating that it had been a challenging year due to the scale of transitions. He noted the first four months of the reporting period were under the sixth administration, and the last eight were under the seventh, which had impacted the acceleration of modernisation efforts and the focus on digital transformation. He highlighted that Cabinet-adopted digital transformation roadmap placed a big and exciting emphasis on technology, which required adjusting plans and fundamentally looking at strategies for both the DHA and the BMA.
Despite the transition, there were good signs and progress had been built upon previous performance. Minister Schreiber acknowledged, however, that the audit outcome was not good enough, and opportunities were missed, particularly regarding low-hanging fruit that should have been addressed. He stressed that the capability and commitment were present, and they must now focus on enforcing changes, addressing repeat findings, and building on the foundation provided by strategic planning revisions completed during the period.
The Minister addressed the two agenda items relating to digital services:
Kiosk project
This project required reassessment because the strategy was shifting from the traditional "brick and mortar approach" to digital service channels. Minister Schreiber explained that kiosks were designed for the period which focused on physical infrastructure investment. With the whole-of-government digital roadmap promoting digital and virtual services -- such as drivers’ licences, digital identification documents (IDs), and banking apps -- they needed to reassess where kiosks fit into to avoid overinvesting in infrastructure that might quickly become redundant.
Bank expansion/partnership
The uptake on the digital partnership had been "encouraging". The biggest public complaint was the limited number of booking slots on the BAP system, caused by the constrained footprint of only 30 pilot bank branches. The new approach seeks to remove constraints, allowing them to expand presence dramatically into hundreds of locations, including rural communities, by moving processes onto digital channels, avoiding duplication of infrastructure and costs.
Verification Service
This existing integration with banks and insurers allowed them to check a person’s identity (e.g., via fingerprints when opening an account) to prevent identity theft. The fee structure had been corrected because these institutions were not previously paying enough for the valuable state service. This correction had allowed the DHA to invest in maintenance, reducing turnaround times from 24 hours to milliseconds, and lowering the error rate to below one per cent. This improved and secure verification pipeline was crucial for integrating Smart ID and passport services into other channels.

Financial information and audit outcomes
Mr Gordon Hollamby, Chief Financial Officer (CFO), DHA, presented the financial performance and audit outcomes.
Expenditure
R11.831 billion was spent, equating to 97.8% of the R12.095 billion final budget. The underspending of R263 million was attributed to the Department's inability to collect the full revenue target. Transfers and subsidies to the Independent Electoral Commission (IEC), the BMA and political parties, amounted to R4.052 billion. The surplus for the year was R1.583 billion.
Financial position
Total assets were R629.7 million and total liabilities R610.6 million, leaving a net asset value of R19.066 million. The decrease in the financial position was primarily due to the voted fund of R263 million surrendered, relating to the previous financial year.
Audit outcome
The DHA had received a qualified audit opinion, with material findings on compliance with laws and regulations.
Qualification areas
Immovable tangible capital assets were overstated by R101 million because assets no longer under the Department’s control were not derecognised. This was a repeat finding. Contingent liabilities were understated due to inadequate controls to identify and account for all legal cases. The CFO noted that three prior-year qualification areas had been resolved.
Matter of emphasis
R244.7 million of accrued departmental revenue (fines and penalties) had been impaired due to non-recovery. This finding was linked to the material irregularity.
Non-compliance
Findings were related to:
material misstatements in financial statements/APR;
not paying all invoices within 30 days (97% achieved, missing 100% compliance);
non-collection of accrued departmental revenue;
unfinalised Section 42 of the Public Finance Management Act (PFMA) transfers (BMA); and
irregular, fruitless, and wasteful expenditure.
Irregular expenditure
The DHA’s irregular expenditure balance amounted to R202.3 million. This consisted mainly of the old security tender (R199.971 million, 2014/15 to 2017/18) and R2.671 million due to a compensation of employees (COE) budget increase condoned in May 2025.
Performance information
Material misstatements were noted on performance information in Programme 1 (e.g., e-visa prototype, fraud cases, online birth registrations); Programme 2 (Smart ID cards issued); and Programme 3 (Critical skills, law enforcement operations, deportations).
Audit remedial actions and way forward
Mr Hollamby said that management had developed an eight-point plan. Following a meeting with the Minister on 7 August, the Minister had expressed "utter dissatisfaction" with the audit outcomes and put management on terms.
Financial strategy
The DHA had agreed to increase the self-financing revenue target to R2.5 billion for 2025/26, earmarking R1 billion for digital transformation.
Audit oversight committee (AOC):
An AOC had been established as a specialised audit monitoring team, chaired by the DG, with outside experts, to monitor the audit action plan (AAP). This structure was advised by the IEC as a best practice. The AAP was verified by internal audit against SMART (specific, measurable, achievable, relevant and time-bound) criteria.
Ministerial commitment
Minister Schreiber committed to an unqualified audit outcome for the 2025/26 financial year.
DG Mokhode outlined the strategic way forward:
Finalising the digital identity policy and the National Identification Registration Bill.
Finalising the revised White Paper on Citizenship, Immigration and Refugee Protection.
Accelerating the replacement of 16 million green ID books through footprint expansion.
Upskilling and reskilling staff to adapt to artificial intelligence (AI) and new technologies.
Implementing continuous development, quarterly refresher training, and effecting consequence management.
Conducting interim financial statement audits for Quarter 2 and Quarter 3.
Kiosks and DHA footprint expansions into banks
Mr Leon du Preez, Acting Chief Information Officer (CIO), provided the briefing on the information communication technology (ICT) strategy shift.
Outlining the kiosk project status, he said DHA had contracted 91 virtual interactive self-service kiosks from Commerce Africa in November 2022. The contract period was three years (2022–2025). The scope was to provide a live capture platform integrated into the national population register (NPR), the Home Affairs National Identification System (HANIS), and the Automated Biometric Identification System (ABIS) for Smart ID cards, passports, and certificate reprints, aiming to accelerate the phase-out of the green ID book. The integration partner services were terminated on 12 March 2025 due to underperformance. The total cost spent on the 91 contracted units was R20.63 million.
Mr Du Preez said the new strategy prioritised integration with banking platform (Priority 2) over kiosk integration (Priority 7). The shift was driven by the need to limit DHA-funded brick-and-mortar expansion, leverage strategic partnerships -- banks, SARS and SA Reserve Bank (SARB)-- reduce cost structures (bank model offers a lower total cost of ownership), and accelerate time-to-market.
Master services agreements had been concluded with seven banks: ABSA, Nedbank, Tyme Bank, FNB, Capitec, Discovery, and African Bank. The current phase involved developing an Application Programming Interface (API) integration platform based on new technology (facial recognition + fingerprint). Development and testing were underway and were expected to conclude by the end of November. Pilot testing was scheduled from January to March 2026. The expansion aimed to eventually target 1 000 bank branches.
Recommendation
The DHA recommended pausing kiosk installations pending a life-cycle cost and feasibility assessment (capex + opex + time-to-value). Kiosks remained a potential interim channel in non-modernised offices while the bank model was implemented.

OSBP PPP initiative
The process had progressed well. Negotiations with prospective service providers were underway, and Nest and Young, including Bowmans, were assisting in crafting the final agreements. The final agreements with the bidders were expected to be finalised by 31 March 2026.
Consequence management
For the second quarter of 2025, 17 misconduct cases were dealt with, with 13 requiring conclusion within 90 days. Four dismissals and three written warnings were outcomes from the 27 cases finalised between April and September, primarily related to the irregular processing of IDs.

The Department was working with the current banks and additional ones. Master agreements had been signed with seven banks: ABSA, Nedbank, Tyme Bank, FNB, Capitec, Discovery, and African Bank. Border Management Authority briefing on its annual report
Commissioner Masiapato presented the Border Management Authority's (BMA's) second annual report covering the 2024/25 financial year.
He said the BMA had implemented over 27 pieces of legislation, managing the legitimate movement of persons and goods. Overall, it had achieved 81% of its 16 annual targets (13 achieved), a noticeable shift to the positive side from 60% in the previous year.
Programme 1: Corporate Services
Three targets achieved -- standardised communications framework, updated disaster management plan, and rollout of BMA business continuity management plan to 13 ports of entry (PoE).
One target not achieved -- installation and connection of local area network/wide area network (LAN/WAN) connectivity at 13 PoEs (zero achieved). Ten targets achieved/over-achieved. 100% of legitimate travellers processed within five minutes through the movement control system (MCS). Conceded failure, as accurate statistics were unavailable due to MCS instability and log challenges.
Bid adjudication for redevelopment of the six priority land PoEs not concluded in 2024/25 due to capacity challenges.

He confirmed the Committee’s determination to support BMA funding and the integration of SAPS functions.
Ms Machi expressed concern about the R90.5 million deficit, and asked what discussions had been held with NT on securing sustainable funding. She asked what specific internal control improvements had been made that had resulted in the reduction of qualifying audit paragraphs from four to two. She requested measurable data on the interception, arrest, or seizure impact of the joint operations conducted with SAPS and SARS.
The Chairperson thanked the BMA team for the briefing. Given the challenges, he asked the Minister, as Executive Authority, to elevate the funding difficulty to the President. He noted AGSA's concerns about manual systems, and asked the Commissioner for an interim plan to mitigate the severe staffing challenges, such as one person facilitating both exit and entry at borders. He asked about the plan regarding drones, and how the BMA would fund the R9 million cost gap for lifestyle audits, and what funds it could shift from limited resources. What was the status of the DHA's asset transfer to the BMA and the transfer of police functions? He asked the Commissioner about engagement with the DPW on mending the 4 750 km border fence and the short/long-term plan, as well as the current engagement status with neighbouring countries on border control challenges.
BMA's response
Commissioner Masiapato responded to the comments and questions raised by Members.
Funding engagement
The Minister would reflect on engagement with National Treasury. The BMA appreciated the Committee’s engagement with the Select Committee on Appropriations. Following this, the Parliamentary Budget Office (PBO) was tasked with conducting a scientific assessment to juxtapose transferred operational budgets against the BMA's responsibilities, checking if the budget transferred was commensurate. This was vital, as the BMA had been forced to start paying rental costs for offices previously covered by other departments.
Single scanner at Durban port
He confirmed that the single SARS-owned scanner at Durban Harbour operated at 0.05% scanning capability, focusing mainly on customs duties. Most containers, especially transit containers moving to the Democratic Republic of Congo (DRC) or Tanzania, entered the country untouched. The BMA had identified areas where static scanners could be installed as containers exited the harbour, but this required funding.
SAPS transfer:
The Justice, Crime Prevention and Security (JCPS) DG’s cluster had resolved to proceed politically. The Chief State Law Advisor had provided a clear legal opinion confirming the BMA's exclusive mandate for border law enforcement. However, SAPS officials had resisted administratively, refusing to provide the necessary technical details such as payroll and assets, to finalise the transfer.
Border fence/DPW
The R40 million Beitbridge fence contract had been cancelled after the Special Investigating Unit (SIU) investigation, and the money was being returned to the DPW to restart procurement. The DPW had provided R281 million for the repair and maintenance of land port infrastructure, not the border fence. Drones were assisting in border minding as an interim measure.
Lifestyle audits
The BMA was ready for lifestyle audits of its top 100 officials, including the executive, by the SIU, but the R9 million funding required was unavailable. It was relying on the State Security Agency (SSA) for vetting in the interim.
Neighbouring countries
Engagements started this year with Zimbabwe, Mozambique, and Lesotho. Botswana wanted 24-hour borders, but the BMA could not reciprocate due to capacity issues. Joint action plans had reduced truck waiting time on the N7 corridor at Oshikango from three hours to 20 minutes.
Financial information and audit outcomes
Ms Chonco said the BMA's
audit capacity amounted to three internal officials, augmented by outsourcing. She responded to the Committee's comments and questions on the following issues:
Lease budgets transferred
These budgets were mostly held in Agriculture (R62 million balance). A meeting was scheduled for 30 October.
COE budget
The budget split had been inherited (85% COE), and required an injection into the goods and services budget to resolve.
Reduction in audit findings
The reduction from four to two qualifying paragraphs was achieved due to receiving new staff in 2024/25, and focused work on the AGSA recommendations.
Funding shifts
Shifting limited funds was difficult because all needs were critical. The BMA had prioritised operations.
CARA
The R3.4 billion application submitted included non-recurring costs like scanners and drones.
DHA asset transfer
The majority of assets, such as desks and vehicles, had been transferred. Technical items/improvements required BMA assessment to meet generally recognised accounting principles (GRAP) standards before Section 42 transfer. This was expected to be resolved by the end of the third quarter.
Minister's comments
Minister Schreiber confirmed that the JCPS resolution on the SAPS transfer provided legal clarity. He had sent a formal letter to the acting Police Minister this week after an informal engagement. He stressed that the SAPS must comply with the BMA Act.
He confirmed direct engagement with the Minister of Finance regarding funding elevation, and the submission of a comprehensive request to NT and CARA. He also offered to write a formal letter to the President if required. He asserted that Parliament ultimately appropriated funds and had to assert its power to budget adequately for the BMA, which he viewed as a necessary, wise investment for economic and security reasons.
AGSA comments
Ms Thobakgale repeated the concern shared by the DHA, emphasising the need for the BMA to strengthen its internal processes to ensure sufficient and appropriate evidence supported reported performance achievements, and addressed the reliability findings.
Chairperson’s closing remarks
The Chairperson appreciated the BMA's achievements, despite limited resources. He affirmed the need to monitor the audit action plan and consequence management. The Committee would interact with the Portfolio Committee on Police regarding the transfer of functions. He noted the importance of monitoring the work of the DG and the Minister on funding.
