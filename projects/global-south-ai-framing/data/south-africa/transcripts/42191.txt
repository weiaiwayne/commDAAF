Meeting Summary
Video
The Portfolio Committee on Basic Education convened to review the implementation of the Basic Education Laws Amendment (BELA) Act and associated regulations. Members emphasised the urgency of finalising regulations to ensure equitable access, address language policy inconsistencies, and remove barriers in school admissions, underlining that all children should receive quality basic education in line with constitutional principles.
The committee raised concerns that regulations had been published mainly in English and Afrikaans, questioning the criteria for language selection in a country with eleven official languages. The department explained reliance on the Office of the Chief State Law Adviser’s guidelines but acknowledged the need for expanded multilingual translation, public participation, and accessible formats, including Braille.
Discussions highlighted infrastructure deficits, particularly in rural and under-resourced areas, noting a backlog of approximately R140 billion, which impeded access and quality. The department reported exploring blended financing models, including public-private partnerships and the education outcomes fund, to address these gaps and expand grade R inclusion. Members requested measurable progress, including data on schools offering grade R, the integration of African languages, and the rollout of bilingual examinations, which currently cover 11,000 schools. Learner placement challenges, especially in Gauteng with over 82,000 unplaced learners, were raised, prompting proposals for dedicated parent query channels and early provincial engagement in January.
The committee addressed teacher qualifications, noting that many Grade R practitioners lacked NQF Level 6 certification. The department described a task team facilitating enrollment, funding, bridging courses, and absorption into the system as provincial budgets allowed. Members also discussed compliance with the Children’s Amendment Bill, streamlining ECD programme registration, and ensuring curriculum diversity in line with parental rights.
Concerns regarding conditional grants, fiscal discipline, and provincial financial management were raised, alongside slow implementation of language policies, particularly in historically Afrikaans-medium schools. The department indicated ongoing monitoring through district and provincial dashboards, data verification, and SGB support mechanisms.
The committee commended the department’s transparency, data improvements, and accountability initiatives but called for clearer metrics, independent evaluations, and specific targets for rural, farm, and township communities. Procedural matters regarding provincial briefings and regulations submission were deferred. The meeting concluded with recognition of the department’s work, festive wishes, and a commitment to continued oversight to ensure equitable and sustainable educational reforms for all South African children.
Meeting report
Opening Remarks
The Chairperson stated that, in addition to an update and progress report on the Basic Education Laws Amendment (BELA) regulations, there would be a discussion on a topic that had emerged in previous meetings, where members had expressed a desire to understand the progress in finalising the BELA Act regulations. She emphasised the importance of highlighting the significance of implementing this act, given the journey that both the Department and Parliament had undertaken to ensure amendments to the South African Schools Act that promote equality in the education system. She noted that the act sought to remove barriers in admissions, address language policies used by schools, and clarify certain provisions of the South African Schools Act to enhance the school environment. The aim was to ensure that it was conducive to the provision of quality basic education in the country.
The Chairperson further stated that this was a key focus area for the portfolio committee, not only to ensure the implementation of the act but also to facilitate the swift finalisation of the regulations. She highlighted the importance of this point.
She then noted that the committee was still within the 16 Days of Activism for No Violence Against Women and Children. She remarked that the government’s classification of Gender-Based Violence and Femicide (GBVF) as a disaster represented a positive step. She emphasised that it should be a meaningful measure, not merely a classification, and that resources should be mobilised and departments coordinated to work with communities in addressing the scourge of GBVF in the country. She added that, given the Department of Basic Education’s responsibility for over 30 million children, it was important that the committee contributed actively to ensuring that the classification brought relief to women and children in the country.

DBE Presentation
Mr Livhuwani Mahada, Director in the Director-General’s office, Dr Janeli Kotzé, Acting Director for Early Childhood Development (ECD), Mr Enoch Rabotapi, Chief Director for the National Institute for Curriculum and Professional Development (NICPD), Ms Simoné Geyer, DDG for Delivery and Support, Adv Zukile Ntshwanti, Senior Legal Researcher at Office of the Chief Justice and Mr Hubert Mathanzima Mweli, Director-General for Basic Education, took the Committee through the presentation.
Reasons for Extension Requests
Key reasons from various stakeholders for extending public comment deadlines, including mid-year pressures, translation needs, thorough stakeholder analysis, broader rural engagement and requirements of constitutional language compliance.
Dbe’s Reasons for Conceding to the Request
Principles derived from constitutional obligations and case law support extended public participation, allowing meaningful opportunities, ensuring accessibility and enabling the public to engage effectively in legislative processes.
Progress to Date
An update on publication and translation timelines for Admission and Capacity Regulations, approval of Learner Pregnancy Regulations, and the status of remaining regulations before OCSLA or internal task teams.
Readiness risks indicate transport challenges due to outstanding payments and limited progress in practitioner upskilling, with only half of Grade R practitioners enrolled for qualifications.
Update on Sections 4 & 5
Provincial data on schools, former Model C and Afrikaans medium status, language additions, completion of admission and language policies, and engagement levels on introducing additional languages.
Children’s Amendment Bill 2025
An introduction to Part D, focusing on amendments to improve the ECD regulatory environment and address systemic challenges.
Key Provisions in the Bill
Major provisions include single integrated registration, statutory placement of the NCF, differentiation between modalities, and streamlined norms and standards to simplify regulatory oversight.
Process to Date
A summary of drafting history from DSD’s initial Bill, creation of a technical task team, public consultation in 2024, amendments by DBE and current progress toward OCSLA certification and Cabinet processing.
Consultations Leading up to Public Consultation
A record of consultations across HEDCOM structures, DOH, COGTA and SALGA, focusing on health and safety responsibilities, local government roles and alignment on draft by-law provisions.
Way Forward
Future steps include completing OCSLA and SEIAS certification, Cabinet processing, parliamentary introduction, and ongoing review of regulations, norms, and standards through participatory processes.
Recommendation
A request for the Portfolio Committee to discuss BELA regulations, Grade R admissions implementation, qualification upgrading and the Children’s Amendment Bill 2025, and to provide guidance.

Minister Gwarube acknowledged the need to collect data on Grade R enrolments, as Grade R is now compulsory, and confirmed that all data collection complies with POPIA. She committed to updating the Committee early in the year with a snapshot of admissions and pressures, assuring that no data breaches had occurred.
The Chairperson clarified that the choice of language and translation in the law-making process is a decision of the executive, not the legislature. While Parliament ensures translation into all official languages, including Braille, during public participation, the initial decision on which languages to publish the bill in is made by the Department. The Chairperson questioned why the Department had chosen to translate into Afrikaans despite submissions from stakeholders, such as Equal Education, highlighting the process as exclusionary to the majority of South Africans.
Minister Gwarube acknowledged the criticism, noting that the original limitation was the publication of the BELA Bill in English and Afrikaans. She accepted that the Department and the executive should ensure greater access to translation services for public participation and confirmed that this criticism was well understood and valid.
Minister Gwarube noted that Mr Brown had distributed a list of provincial admission coordinators to facilitate public queries. These coordinators would allow members to direct parents to the appropriate provincial offices to resolve admission issues. She thanked the Chairperson and excused herself.
The Chairperson stated that she would personally write to the Department and the Office of the Chief State Law Adviser to note that the Department had not responded to concerns raised by civil society, particularly the request from Equal Education for an extension. She said that the only extension granted by the Department was for another matter, not in response to that request, and emphasised that this should be recorded.
The Chairperson commended the Department for presenting, for the first time, a clear picture of specific areas of concern, particularly regarding admission and language policies that had been used as barriers in former Model C schools. She highlighted the usefulness of the presentation in showing the number of former Model C schools in each province that still used single-medium instruction (Afrikaans), those that had added another language, and those that had incorporated African languages.
She acknowledged that the number of schools incorporating African languages remained very low, with KwaZulu-Natal being the only province showing significant progress. She requested that the Department provide a spreadsheet for oversight purposes, detailing remaining schools in all provinces that had not reviewed their language policies or incorporated additional languages. The spreadsheet should indicate the school, the single medium of instruction used, and clear reasons for not incorporating another language, such as demographic considerations or ongoing SGB reviews. This would allow oversight to assess whether the reasons for non-compliance were justified.
The Chairperson further raised the issue of Grade R practitioners and access to the Unisa programme. She noted that some practitioners were reportedly being rejected due to insufficient qualifications. She requested that the Department clarify how it ensured that practitioners met the requirements, whether bridging courses were needed, and how 2,000 practitioners could access the programme effectively.
In conclusion, the Chairperson expressed appreciation for the progress made, particularly regarding admissions and the implementation of compulsory Grade R. She highlighted the importance of monitoring and oversight to ensure effective implementation of the amendments and requested information on when the two regulations required for submission to Parliament would be provided.
Mr Mweli noted the heavy rain and invited Advocate Ntshwanti to respond regarding the timing for submission of the two regulations to Parliament.
Advocate Ntshwanti explained that the Department must first approach the Department of Monitoring and Evaluation for a socio-economic impact assessment certificate. She noted that it was difficult to provide a precise timeline for when the regulations would be tabled in Parliament, particularly due to delays in transmission processes within the Office of the Chief State Law Adviser, which requires approximately 65 working days (about three months) to finalise.
Mr Mweli thanked members for their engagement and emphasised the Department’s appreciation for accountability.
The Chairperson reminded the Department that a question had not yet been fully addressed: concerns raised by NAPTOSA regarding access for Grade R practitioners to Unisa programmes due to low APS scores.
Mr Rabotapi responded, explaining that the 2 121 practitioners referenced were those eligible to apply. Exclusions were mainly due to a lack of a matric qualification or age factors. For eligible applicants who could not gain direct admission into the B.Ed Foundation Phase programme (e.g., due to low APS scores), Unisa offers a one-year bridging certificate in higher education, after which they can register for the B.Ed programme.
He also highlighted a technicality regarding matric equivalency: only those with formal technical stream qualifications are recognised as matric equivalents, whereas some practitioners have Level 4 ECD qualifications that are not recognised for admission purposes. The Department has raised this issue with Unisa to explore possible waivers.
The Chairperson requested a written response detailing the Department’s interventions for different cohorts of practitioners, including: those with matric, those with unrecognised equivalents, and those without matric or older in the system. She emphasised the importance of outlining engagement with unions and measures to support practitioners’ training and retention.
The Chairperson concluded the session by thanking the Department, noting that it was the final session of the year, and wished them a festive season. She indicated that in January, the Committee would meet again to discuss NSC examination results and monitor continued improvement.
Committee Matters and Closing Remarks
The Committee proceeded with the adoption of the minutes from previous meetings.
The minutes of the meeting held on 24th November 2025 were adopted by Mr Louw and seconded by Ms Ngobeni.
The minutes of the meeting held on 25th November 2025 were adopted by Mr Louw and seconded by Ms T Magagula (ANC).
Mr Mkhize noted that, while he did not oppose adoption for the 25th of November, he was concerned that the previous meeting had not clearly recorded specific follow-up actions or demands, such as addressing the issue of growing regular expenditure. He emphasised that the Committee needs to ensure clear takeaways and accountability from the Department.
The Chairperson then inquired whether Mr Brown was ready with the draft programme for the next term.
Mr Brown responded that a draft had been prepared; however, it could not be finalised or shared, as the Committee had not yet received guidance on focus areas from the Office of the House Chairperson. He offered to share the working draft, but noted it remained preliminary.
The Chairperson advised members that adopting the programme without receiving the official focus areas would necessitate reconvening to align the programme with parliamentary directives. She proposed waiting until the focus areas were received before adopting the programme and indicated that a special meeting could be scheduled if a submission deadline was set by the Office of the House Chairperson. Members, including Ms Magagula, agreed with this approach.
The Chairperson concluded the meeting by thanking members for a long, productive, and robust term. She wished everyone a safe and restful holiday period and encouraged members to prepare for the next term.
The meeting was adjourned.
