Women, Youth and Persons with Disabilities
Strategic Plan
The Department of Women, Youth and Persons with Disabilities (DWYPD) briefed the Committee on its 2025-2030 Strategic Plan and Annual Performance Plan for the 2025/26 financial year.
Ms Sindisiwe Lydia Chikunga, Minister of Women, Youth, and Persons with Disabilities, said that the Department had a mandate to coordinate, advocate for, and empower the socio-economic well-being of the three constituencies—youth, women, and persons with disabilities—who were greatly marginalised. Society was faced with a poly-crisis ranging from climate change to GBV. Reviewing the past three decades, it was observed that more work was required.
The Committee agreed that this was a very important Department with many responsibilities. The Committee asked for the exact figures which indicated the direct impact of the programmes and plans in place to empower women, youth and persons with disabilities. On this, the Committee asked about how the aforementioned constituents could be empowered and included in the opportunities created. The Committee also felt concerned that there was not enough representation of the rural areas and asked how the vulnerable groups could access the Department. The Committee was also concerned that there was lack of reliable data for children under the age of five years who lived with disabilities and for those who were on the Autism Spectrum. The Committee also expressed concern over the many vacancies in the Department and the distribution of funds within the Department.
Meeting report
Opening
The Chairperson greeted and welcomed everyone and outlined the agenda for the meeting; which was subsequently adopted. Thereafter, the Chairperson heard the absentees with apology and then handed over to the Minister to provide a brief overview of the presentation.
Minister's opening remarks
Ms Sindisiwe Lydia Chikunga, Minister of Women, Youth and Persons with disabilities, greeted everyone. She said that the Department had a mandate to coordinate, advocate and empower the socio-economic well-being of the three constituencies: youth, women and persons with disabilities- who were greatly marginalised. Society was faced with a poly-crisis which ranged from climate change to Gender-Based Violence (GBV). Reviewing the past three decades, it was observed that more work was required. There could be no economic growth when women and youth were faced with poverty, high rates of unemployment and GBV. So, too, could there be no ethical and developmental state when persons with disabilities were excluded from society. She appreciated that the three constituencies were at the forefront of the Department. The constituencies required a government to fully rally behind them and form a well-planned transformation agenda, the Department was very serious about this role. The three overarching priorities of the Department included: inclusive economic growth and employment creation, reducing poverty and high cost of living and building an ethical and developmental State.
The Department's work could be classified into four pillars: research advocacy and knowledge management, mainstreaming accountability and monitoring evaluation, catalytic and strategic projects, and bold legislative and policy programmes. The Department would continue to nurture partnerships with research institutions such as universities to produce research that supported the mandate of the Department. This action included an updated youth report to assist with modelling youth trajectories. The Department would commence with the qualitative and quantitative profiling of especially the South African youth who were Neither in Education, Employment, nor Training (NEET), per province and per district. In partnership with academic institutions, the Department would investigate the underlying causes of GBVF, focusing on the hotspots and high-risk communities. On the second pillar; in line with the United Nations' (UN) convention and that of the African Union, the Constitution of South Africa and the White Paper on Persons with Disabilities; the Department would monitor the adherence to Employment Equity targets in both the private and public sectors and advocate for consequence management. The Department was obligated to achieve 7% of employment equity targets for persons with disabilities. Cabinet approved the target this year of 4%. Currently, the Department is below 1%; all departments and Ministers are well aware of the situation regarding the employment of Persons with Disabilities (PWD); the Department would also inform the private sector in an effort to reach the said target.
The Minister said that the Department would assess the capability to conduct research on mainstreaming the rights of PWD in various sectors. During disasters, the Department had no knowledge of whether the PWD were affected or not. The Department was certainly curious about this. The Department established the National Disability Data Observatory to strengthen data collection and reporting systems. Accurate data relating to the PWD was lacking such as the data on PWD who were below the age of five years, the Department was monitoring this as there was a challenge of data collection. The Department enhanced institutional capacity to strengthen disability focal points, leveraging Artificial Intelligence (AI) and emerging technologies. In the fight against GBV, the Department would call on all sectors in the community in the next five years to assist, focusing on prevention. The Department collaborated with other departments to plan how to teach parents to parent their children and educate parents on the rights of children. Here, the focus was on socialisation; to eradicate GBV.
The Department was in the process of constituting the National Council on GBVF for the
Act
which was signed into law in May 2024. The National Assembly was responsible for recruiting board members from the private sector as well as civil society. Letters were written to the Speaker as well as relevant Ministers, and to National Treasury to request funding for said Council; which would assist in the fight against GBV. She was concerned about the high surge in teenage and underage pregnancies in the country. Just last year, the number rose to 78 000 for young women who were 15-19 years of age and 7 000 for girls who were 10-11 years of age. The Department would campaign for those who committed statutory rape to be fully punished by the law, and a back-to-school campaign for those women who were forced out of school due to teenage pregnancy. The Minister of Basic Education supported said campaign. Regarding the empowerment of women, youth and PWD; she recalled from the State of the Nation Address (SONA) the three strategic priorities. She read a quote from the SONA. The Department worked with the private sector on issues of GBVF to empower women to live independently. Capacity building programmes were formed to ensure that the three aforementioned constituencies benefited from the R20 000 000 000 annual transformation fund. The Department would resource and empower the National Youth Development Agency (NYDA) to make it an inclusive and responsive agency.
The Minister said that the Department would ensure that initiatives would be strengthened to reach more unemployed female graduates in collaboration with the Minister of Electricity. The President launched a campaign, wherein the Department collaborated with the Department of Defense to lead the National Youth Service to ensure the training of youth. On the public procurement regulations, she requested the Women's Economic Assembly to support the Department in leveraging the recently signed Public Procurement
Act
to empower women in businesses. For said Act, the Minister of Finance had to draft regulations with the Department to not exclude anyone. She appreciated the availability of the R20b transformation fund and would ensure that an equal share went to the women, youth and PWD. A bold legislative and policy programme would address many issues relating to the three constituencies. Said legislation would enable the domestication of international treaties. The Department would leverage key legislative instruments to empower the constituencies to transform them. Here, the Department collaborated with State-Owned Entities (SOEs) such as Transnet who identified land pieces to assist with the work of the Department. She emphasised that the Department was severely underfunded for the programmes, funding proposals were placed forward, and engagements were made with other institutions to empower the three constituencies. She was grateful for the opportunity to present. She was hopeful for the future and requested support from the Committee. There has been progress made in the work of the Department thus far. She informed Members that she would join the President, who was in the Eastern Cape, to attend to issues of GBV.
Ms Mikateko Joyce Maluleke, Director-General, DPWYPD, then led the Committee through the presentation.
The Department conducted a strategic Planning session in December 2024 to prepare its five year plan and the Annual Performance Plan; the Minister gave the strategic direction for the five years.
Bills for Cabinet approval between 2025 and 2030: The Promotion of Women's Rights, Empowerment and Gender Equality Act, South African Youth Development Act & Promotion and Protection of Persons with Disabilities Act
DWYPD key outcomes/priorities 2025-2030:
MTDP Priority 1: Inclusive growth and job creation
Equitable economic empowerment, participation and ownership for WYPD at the center of the national economic agenda institutionalised.
MTDP Priority 2: Reduce poverty and tackle the high cost of living.
Increase in level of intervention contributing to poverty alleviation, equitable social empowerment, and participation for WYPD.
Expanded access to free sanitary dignity products for girl learners in Quintiles 1, 2 & 3 schools, special and farm schools (period poverty).
Strengthen stakeholder relations and community mobilisation towards the realisation of women's empowerment, youth development and disability rights
MTDP Priority 3: Capable, ethical, developmental state
An efficient and effective department characterised by good corporate governance and ethical leadership
Strengthened integrated and intersectional approach for advancing women's empowerment and gender equality
Strengthened evidence-based decision-making for socio-economic transformation and empowerment of WYPD
Government-wide planning, budgeting, M&E addresses priorities relating to women's empowerment, youth development and the rights of persons with disabilities
Strengthened WYPD rights agenda within global, continental and regional platforms, institutions and engagements towards a better Africa and the world
A disability-responsive state that ensures equitable inclusion, access, and participation through ethical governance, accountability, and enhanced institutional capacity.
Strengthened WYPD mainstreaming in departments
Law reform to promote and protect the rights of women, youth and persons with disabilities
See presentation attached for detailed information on outcomes, targets and indicators per programme and an overview of the 2025/26 budget and estimates
Discussion
The Chairperson opened the floor for comments.
Mr M Feni (ANC, Eastern Cape) greeted everyone and appreciated the presentation. He asked about the disabled centre; he agreed that a clear definition of the term ‘
disability’
was required in order to understand the struggles thereof, as disability was not treated with respect. He was also curious about the employment aspect of PWD. The Committee resonated with the struggles of the PWD, and this could not be delayed. Delays would affect procurement and other benefits for the PWD. How did the Department ensure that sectors were not isolated? He recalled several sectors being denied economic participation, there should thus be no form of discrimination. He reiterated raising such important issues.
Mr P Sibande (ANC, Mpumalanga) requested clarity on filling funded vacancies; what was meant by the employment of 0.1% instead of 3%? This was confusing. He recalled mention of empowering PWD with opportunities, he required clarity on this matter. He recalled mention of several provincial departments as well as the six municipalities monitored. He was concerned that there were many challenged municipalities and he was not clear whether these were municipalities in the rural areas or urban areas, what was the issue in this regard? He was concerned that the focus was mainly on the urban areas and excluded the impoverished who were excluded from the transformation. He recalled mention of the challenges faced, he requested a report on this so that the Committee may assist wherever necessary.
Ms JM Adriaanse (DA, North West) said that she was concerned about the intention behind conducting research so that it was reliable and valid. What was the nature and technicality of the research? Was there an adequate pool of human resources when discussing research? The critical vacant positions in the Department were very concerning, as was the increase in funding; what were the exact figures in this regard? She was very worried about the vulnerability of the youth. She welcomed the interventions and suggested that more could be done. Regarding the unachieved targets that Information Technology (IT) possessed, she suggested that more priority be given to this because IT was growing exponentially, and the country was lagging behind. What were the plans of the Department in this regard?
Ms N Chriwa-Mpungose (EFF, Gauteng) asserted that this Department was one of the most important due to its mandate. She identified a shortfall; the Department did not lead the narrative on those three represented constituents, nor on the solutions. She observed much progress with other departments, which had more tangible evidence of the work being done. She requested figures on the lives impacted and affected, she hoped to see more tangible evidence of work being done. She was concerned about visibility in the township areas; how did the Department plan to increase its presence and reach more communities affected? She observed lack of research in various sectors especially in disability; on Autism Spectrum Disorder which affected many children and left parents feeling crippled. What was the Department doing in this regard? How did the Department echo the voices of PWD who were not heard? She recalled a case of a young man who struggled to be admitted into university, and certain colleges eThekwini which did not cater for PWD at all. This young man also struggled with pain from not wearing the right shoes, which were very costly. How did the Department influence institutions to provide assistance to PWD? The young man's right to education was greatly hindered, in this regard. How could this young man be assisted? She recalled a case of over 600 children on the Spectrum who were not in school and were on waiting lists. What was the Department's intervention in this regard? She agreed on the interventions for the economic empowerment of municipalities, but more could surely be done. She wished to be helpful and asked how the vulnerable groups could reach the Department.
Ms ZN Ngcobo (MKP, KZN) appreciated the presentation. She believed that the APP and Strategic Plan would guide the government, and the said plans should ensure proper funding and be effectively implemented to address the ongoing issues. She requested a detailed breakdown of the funding for the current financial year and plans for how the funding would be utilised and the outcomes achieved. What specific initiatives were in place for women's economic empowerment and to address GBV? How would the Department support the unemployed youth with employment creation, and what was NYDA's role in this regard? What measures were taken to ensure that PWD had equal access to opportunities and services? In line with the Department's plan to create employment and transform the economy; the ethical developmental states would also be essential to achieving such goals.
Ms T Breedt (FF+, Free State) appreciated the presentation. Regarding staff complement, what were the timeframes for the modified organisational structure? How far was the progress? What were the vacancies in the Department? What was the impact on the necessary programmes observed? The Department was entrusted with gender quotas for the other departments; how would the Department address this, taking into account the staff shortage? She observed a decrease in the budget for consultants and an increase in the budget for venues and facilities; what was the reason for this? She requested an update on the Disability Draft Bill. She observed a disconnect between the APPs and the targets to be achieved in relation to the service delivery; what was the Department's opinion on this?
The Chairperson asked about the data which the Department struggled to obtain (children under the age of five years who lived with disabilities); what were the plans to obtain the reliable data thereof? Said children experienced many challenges and were at home. She requested clarity on the 12 stakeholder engagements on empowering women, youth and PWD; which stakeholders were involved in this regard? Could the Department elaborate on the process of establishing five partnerships supporting entrepreneurs who live with disabilities? What were the timelines for the Department to present the Disability Draft Bill? She requested an update on the Women Empowerment and Gender Equality (WEGE) Bill, it had been seven years since said Bill was first formed.
Ms M Bains (ANC, Western Cape) commended the Department for the presentation as it covered many of her questions. The aforementioned strategies required extensive work and time frames for implementation. She was from the West Coast, and observed that PWD there were often not considered. Did the Department have a mandate to oversight the process of such cases being heard and resolved?
Responses from the Department:
Ms Maluleke said that she would respond to some questions, and she would distribute the breakdown of the allocated budget to the Committee. Unfortunately, the Department was severely underfunded, and this hindered the Department's ability to carry out its mandate. She reiterated that every governmental unit should have a gender and disability quota in its directorship, as the directors contributed to policy and were involved in the budget requests. The Department did not have implementation in the courts. The Department was at national level and had a low staff complement. For example, if compensation of employees were removed from the allocated budget, approximately R35 000 000 would remain; and this was insufficient. She recalled that in 2023, the Department requested to host the radio talk show in all the official languages. For this initiative, the Department received much praise and a quotation of R14 000 000 from the SABC; thereafter, the Department went to the European Union (EU) to receive a grant of R14 000 000. However, SABC increased the amount to R28 000 000. Unfortunately, the Department was herein forced to discontinue the initiative due to insufficient funds, resulting in failure.
Ms Maluleke informed Members that the Department of Higher Education was responsible for the Technical and Vocational Education and Training (TVET) colleges, and there should be a gender, disability and youth unit in said department. The Department was understaffed and could not be in all nine provinces. The Department was structured to fail and lacked authority. She implored that the Committee should call on the relevant Ministers. The Department was continuously blamed for the poor structure of government. The Department did not have the statistics on the disability. The Department was obligated to report to the UN Task Force on Albinism, as South Africa had many cases of people with Albinism being killed. The Department had to inquire from various entities to request statistics on this issue; the problem, however, was that the said entities requested additional funds, which the Department could not provide. This remained a challenge. In June 2025, the Department would report to the UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities. Regarding the filling of posts; there were three employees in the research unit with a budget of R1 700 000 which was insufficient. She wished to dedicate an entire day to show the Committee the work being done by the Department, despite the lack of funding. The Department could not produce the same results as Statistics SA, which had an unlimited staff complement, when the Department only had three employees in research.
Ms Maluleke responded on the municipalities; she was aware that there were 252 municipalities. She said that the Department in the MME unit had three or four employees. The Department could not reach everyone. She informed Members that this year, the Department was attending the Group of 20 (G20) Summit, with an issued budget of R5 000 000 from National Treasury. This budget was insufficient for the many meetings the Department was to facilitate. Next week, the Department would meet with Empire Partners Foundation who agreed to develop IT for the Department free of charge. With this assistance, the Department could provide more accurate information. Regarding the issue of the 1%; the Department already requested from Cabinet to increase this to 3% (this related to the filling of funding vacancies which the Committee earlier asked about). On this, she informed Members that the Department of Public Services Administration (DPSA) was called by the Department to report on why the percentage stood at 0.9%.
Mr Mbhazima Shiviti, Chief Director: Resource Management, DWYPD, responded on the filling of funding vacancies within eight months. According to the Regulation of 2023; said filling was amended from 12 months to eight months, and this reflected in the APP.
Ms Phuti Mabelebele, Chief Director: Advocacy and Mainstreaming, DWYPD, informed Members that the Disability was not yet ready to reach Parliament. The inputs were being finalised, in collaboration with other departments. The Department had collected inputs on said Bill from various entities. Regarding the data on disabilities; there was a challenge in the country which lacked a unit focusing on the statistics of PWD. Data on PWD sat with other departments. Children with Albinism were not recorded as suffering from Albinism on their birth certificates. The Department collaborated with the Health Department to amend their protocols. There was a lack of technological devices to detect the disabilities. Distribution of data across all departments remained a challenge; she asserted that there should be a record of disabilities on various documentation. Regarding the children under the age of five who were unaccounted for, there were systematic challenges and protocols that were not responsive. The Department agreed with the White Paper that every province should have a National Disability Rights Machinery which comprised of provincial governmental departments and civil society organisations meeting to establish the disability agenda for each province. The Western Cape held no functional (aforementioned) Machinery, which led to challenges not being addressed and a compromised agenda.
The disability unit now fell under a unit of Social Development, and the disability unit was often not accommodated for which caused challenges. The Department worked hard to resuscitate the said
machinery
, despite the challenges. The Department issued a deadline for the machinery for the second quarter; more information would then be provided, and the machinery could effectively play its role.
Regarding the children on the Autism Spectrum; there was widely a lack of knowledge on said Disorder, even worse for the children in the rural schools. The Department of Basic Education (DBE) instituted Inclusive Education Units in all districts, to ensure equal access to education. More research was required on the Autism Spectrum Disorder, however the Department had a lack of funds, making it difficult to elevate awareness on said Disorder. The Department developed a manual with Autism South Africa, to assist parents and guardians on where to obtain information and assistance for their children. The Department was working hard on this initiative, to empower the communities with information on Autism. The information currently available was medical information only. The Department further developed a document of an action plan to mobilise other departments to initiate programmes for persons with mental illnesses, to address the various disabilities.
Ms Maluleke said that she would respond to all the comments. Regarding the comment on the 12 stakeholder engagements held; the Department had insufficient funding and therefore had to collaborate with other partners. She met with partners and requested that the partners train the youth and empower them with opportunities to contribute to the economy. She reiterated that the Department did not provide water or wheelchairs; other departments were responsible for such provisions. The Department was severely understaffed and lacked in research, however the Department led in such discussions.
Closing
The Chairperson thanked the Department for their responses. She would submit a deadline of seven working days to respond to the outstanding comments. The Committee would engage with other departments in their cluster regarding the gender focal persons in directorship to enforce Disability awareness throughout the departments. She released the Department.
Ms Maluleke pleaded with the Chairperson to allow the Department to present for a full day on all the work carried out in the Department.
The minutes of the meeting on 09 April 2025 were subsequently adopted. The Chairperson wished the Committee well on the upcoming Easter holiday.
The meeting was adjourned.
