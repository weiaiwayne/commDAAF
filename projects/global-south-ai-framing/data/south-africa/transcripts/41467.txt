Date: 02 Sep 2025

Meeting Summary
The Committee on Science, Technology and Innovation held a strategic planning workshop to refine its priorities for 2025–2030. The session focused on aligning the Committee’s work with national priorities, strengthening oversight, and promoting innovation as a driver of economic and social transformation. Key achievements included effective budget monitoring and positioning South Africa as a hub for science and innovation. However, persistent challenges were noted, such as underfunding of the National System of Innovation (NSI), declining business investment in R&D, weak innovation at firm level, fragmented coordination among stakeholders, and uneven access to Science, Technology and Innovation (STI) benefits across society.
The Committee agreed on several deliverables, including developing a five-year strategic plan, monitoring alignment of departmental plans with national objectives, facilitating interdepartmental policy coherence, and enhancing oversight of public-private partnerships in research and development. Members highlighted the importance of strengthening accountability, ensuring meaningful follow-up with entities, and avoiding overloaded agendas that limit engagement. They also called for greater collaboration across committees, especially in areas such as climate resilience, energy, and agriculture, where science and innovation play a cross-cutting role.
Concerns were raised about the Department’s limited budget and capacity, the need to advocate for increased resources, and the importance of addressing inequalities in the STI sector. Proposals included developing a national artificial intelligence (AI) strategy, deepening oversight of initiatives like the Municipal Innovation Fund (MIF), and ensuring the Committee plays a central role in integrating science and technology into service delivery and industrial development. Members also highlighted the need for more effective public communication of the Committee’s work and for ensuring equitable representation of women and historically disadvantaged groups in science.
Budgetary concerns dominated part of the discussion, with Members criticising excessive expenditure on catering, ground transport, and international travel. They argued for more sensible financial practices, noting inflated costs caused by reliance on approved service providers and middlemen. It was agreed that the Committee must review its budget line by line, engage parliamentary administration to address systemic inefficiencies, and ensure that resources are used responsibly. Members also cautioned that inefficient spending could risk both international engagements and domestic oversight work.
In closing, the Committee emphasised the importance of careful preparation for oversight activities, stronger interdepartmental collaboration, cost-effective financial management, and improved staffing support to strengthen its research and communication capacity. The meeting reaffirmed the Committee’s unique role as a cross-cutting body central to advancing South Africa’s science, technology, and innovation agenda, while also highlighting the need to address systemic weaknesses, inequalities, and inefficiencies to ensure meaningful impact.
Meeting report
Committee Strategic Plan
Dr Rene Osborne-Mullins, Committee Content Advisor, emphasised the need for a transformative approach to improving the lives of South Africans, highlighting the importance of aligning the Committee’s work with national priorities and addressing challenges within the global science, technology, and innovation landscape. She reviewed the Committee’s key achievements, including effective budget monitoring and efforts to position South Africa as a leading hub for science and innovation.
Looking ahead, she outlined strategic priorities for 2025 to 2030, aimed at strengthening the role of science, technology, and innovation in advancing both economic and social objectives. The discussion also focused on enhancing oversight of public appointments, improving policy coherence, and promoting innovation as a driver of economic growth. She stressed the importance of identifying specific focus areas to deepen oversight, strengthen public engagement, and ensure that the Committee’s work delivers meaningful impact for the country.
Deliverables
The Chairperson will ensure that the Committee develops a strategic plan for the next five years based on the discussions held during the meeting.
Dr Osborne-Mullins will present examples of focus areas for the Committee to consider as prioritised focus areas for the 7
th
The Committee will monitor how well the Department and the entities’ plans align with national priorities.
The Committee will facilitate inter-department policy alignment workshops to enhance Spatial Development Initiative (SDI) policy coherence.
The Committee will monitor public-private partnerships to boost investment in Research and Development (R&D).
Challenges and Weaknesses
Under-funding: A major and persistent challenge is the under-funding of the NSI. The Gross Expenditure on Research and Development (GERD) as a percentage of Gross Domestic Product (GDP) has been on a declining trend. This is compounded by a significant decline in business-sector expenditure on R&D.
Weak Innovation at Firm Level: South Africa has lost its competitive advantage in medium-technology exports and has a low number of patent applications per million inhabitants compared to other upper-middle-income countries. This indicates a difficulty in translating research outputs into commercial products and economic growth.
Fragmented Ecosystem: The NSI suffers from a lack of coordination and collaboration among different actors, particularly between different levels of government and between research institutions and the business sector. This creates a "valley of death" where promising innovations fail to reach the market.
Persistent Inequality: The benefits of the Science, Technology, and Innovation (STI) ecosystem are not distributed evenly. There is a significant gap between the formal and informal economies, with many grassroots innovators and small businesses, particularly in townships, lacking access to funding, information, and support services.

Discussion
Ms N Mazzone (DA) thanked Dr Osborne-Mullins for presenting such a detailed plan. She emphasised the importance of strengthening oversight and accountability to ensure that the alignment of STI and national entities remains a priority within the proposed focus areas. She observed that the Committee is both interesting and complex, given its wide-ranging areas of focus. The challenge, however, is that its capacity becomes diluted, as equal attention must be given to each area since all are doing important work. She reminded Members that the primary responsibility of Parliament is to hold the government, its departments, and its entities accountable.
She stressed the need to define clearly what “strengthening oversight and accountability” entails. While the Committee has no role in making appointments because that remains the responsibility of the Department and the Minister, it does have the duty to scrutinise those appointments. She noted that the strategy presented by the Committee staff provides only a foundation or framework. The task now is to add depth and detail, particularly around oversight and accountability, where the plan is still vague.
Ms Mazzone further highlighted the importance of clarifying the purpose of each oversight visit to government entities. Drawing on her background in public enterprises and experience with Eskom and its affiliates, she explained that issues such as climate resilience and sustainability cannot be addressed in isolation. For example, mineral resources and energy are overseen by different committees. Thus, discussions around green hydrogen economies must involve not only trade and industry but also the Department of Energy, as it is directly responsible for establishing and managing green hydrogen facilities.
Similarly, when discussing zero carbon emissions, the Department of Environment plays a role in monitoring emissions, but the Department of Energy is the one capable of generating significant emissions. Ms Mazzone stressed that it is in such nuanced details that the Committee’s institutional knowledge becomes essential. Members bring expertise from diverse areas, which is a powerful asset. She concluded by commending the outline presented and emphasised that it is now the Committee’s responsibility to add the necessary nuance and substance to give full effect to the plan.
Dr S Thembekwayo (EFF) expressed gratitude to the Committee staff for their work. She raised concerns regarding the appointment of executive staff and board members, which currently rests solely with the Minister, asking in what capacity the Committee could play a role in this process. She further recalled that, in the past, the Committee held physical meetings with entities, during which Members posed critical questions. When entities were unable to provide immediate answers, they would often commit to following up with written responses. However, in most cases, those follow-ups were never received, leaving many of the Committee’s questions unanswered. One of the factors contributing to the lack of responses from entities is that the Committee tends to cram too many programmes into a single day. She noted that while it may seem advantageous to hear from all entities in one sitting, it is unrealistic to expect meaningful feedback under such conditions. Dr Thembekwayo stressed that this is a challenge that must be addressed. Going forward, the Committee should consider meeting with only one or two entities at a time to allow for deeper engagement, as the current approach restricts dialogue and undermines the Committee’s ability to carry out its work effectively.
Regarding the legislative process and the amendments reflected in the presentation, she asked what exactly the Committee’s role is, and how it is expected to introduce or make amendments, given that the process appears to differ from the way other committees, previously served, have operated.
Dr W Boshoff (FF+) remarked that it would not be an exaggeration to describe the Committee as “the committee of everything,” given that Science, Technology, and Innovation underpin nearly every aspect of society. He noted, however, that the Department’s relatively small size, coupled with the absence of major scandals involving corruption or large-scale financial mismanagement, has caused it to be overlooked, likening it to a “middle child” that struggles to attract attention.
He highlighted this problem, referencing the Department of Agriculture’s current campaign against foot-and-mouth disease. Although the Department of Science and Innovation has vaccine production capacity, it has not been included in the planning. Instead, vaccines are being imported from Botswana while the Department of Agriculture’s own facilities remain in poor condition. Dr Boshoff also pointed to challenges around the animal identification and traceability system, which is central to addressing the disease. Although the Council for Scientific and Industrial Research (CSIR) has developed a diagnostic test, it is not being utilised. Instead, an industry-based system has been adopted, one that advantages large feedlots at the expense of smaller producers.
He urged the Committee to recognise its unique role, that is, its Members have direct input into the work of other committees and must therefore remain vigilant in monitoring how departments operate, especially where crises in one department may overlap with the work of another. Dr Boshoff stressed that this role is not only about oversight but also about advocacy. The knowledge gained through committee discussions and oversight visits should not be confined to the Committee itself but shared more broadly with the public, ensuring it contributes meaningfully to addressing national challenges.
Mr V Nkosi (ANC) welcomed the strategic planning presentation, describing it as an excellent contribution that ensures science and innovation are not treated as an afterthought, but rather placed at the centre of government, education, industry, and society to drive sustainable development and social transformation.
He stressed the need to strengthen oversight, cautioning that it should not be limited to occasional site visits. Instead, it should involve continuous monitoring. As an example, he pointed to the objective of using innovation to improve service delivery and build a more capable state. The Municipal Innovation Fund (MIF), he noted, is one initiative that the Committee must continue to monitor closely to determine whether it is truly providing municipalities with the necessary resources for technology and innovation projects.
Mr Nkosi further emphasised the importance of the Committee championing collaboration across departments. Greater integration of science and technology into various sectors and communities, he argued, is essential for ensuring meaningful impact and advancing the Committee’s mandate.
Mr T Mjadu (MKP) remarked that the presentation effectively captured the global STI landscape, highlighting the artificial intelligence (AI) revolution, geopolitical competition in technology, the global focus on sustainability, and South Africa’s strength in “big science” initiatives such as the Square Kilometre Array (SKA). However, he cautioned that South Africa faces deep strategic vulnerabilities. He noted that while nations are locked in a global race for technological supremacy, South Africa is falling behind. Business investment in Research and Development (R&D) is declining, and although the country produces strong research outputs, these are not being translated into patents, products, or competitive companies. This weak innovation at the firm level underscores the gap between research excellence and economic impact.
Mr Mjadu also raised concerns regarding AI. While the presentation cited ethical AI as a global trend, he questioned whether South Africa has a concrete policy and regulatory framework to ensure that AI development is safe, inclusive, and grounded in human rights. He proposed that the government consider developing a draft AI Strategy White Paper on responsible AI for public consultation. Further, he asked how parliamentary oversight could help prevent algorithmic bias in the allocation of grants, policymaking, and the delivery of social services.
Ms T Mchunu (ANC) thanked Dr Osborne-Mullins for her presentation, noting that it had provided the Committee with valuable insight into its strategic purpose. On oversight and accountability, she agreed with the presentation’s emphasis that both the Department and its entities must remain aligned with national priorities. She added that it is equally important for the Department and the Portfolio Committee to strengthen and consistently monitor the Department’s visibility.
On climate resilience, she stressed that this must become a greater focus area, particularly considering the Climate Change Bill adopted by the 6
th
Parliament. The Committee, she argued, should closely monitor the research being conducted by the CSIR and ensure that outcomes are tracked. She highlighted the importance of joint portfolio committee meetings, explaining that while many such engagements had taken place, there must be proper follow-up. After each engagement, the Committee needs clarity on what was agreed to and when follow-up actions will occur. This, Ms Mchunu explained, will strengthen interdepartmental collaboration at the level of Directors-General, Deputy Directors-General, and Ministers, ensuring better integration and synergy across government.
Ms Mchunu reflected on the value of joint meetings, citing the Committee’s engagement with the minerals and petroleum committee, where departments presented to one another. Such sessions, she said, provided critical insights into the Department of Science, Technology, and Innovation’s work in those sectors. Moving forward, she emphasised that the Committee must continue to play a central coordinating role in convening discussions across departments and, where possible, conduct joint oversight visits to sites.
Ms Mchunu also raised advocacy concerns, particularly in relation to budget constraints. The Department under oversight operates with a limited budget. While it must be encouraged to use its resources efficiently, the Committee also has a role in advocating for an expanded budget. She stressed that STI should be recognised as a foundational portfolio that underpins the work of many other departments. She noted that in its presentations, the Department positioned itself as central to job creation, particularly through opportunities in Science, Technology, Engineering, and Mathematics (STEM), and she agreed this role should be strengthened. Finally, Ms Mchunu underscored the importance of communication, urging the Committee to ensure that its work and the work of the Department are effectively shared with the wider public.
The Chairperson thanked the Portfolio Committee Members for their valuable contributions, emphasising that the purpose of the session was to strengthen the Committee’s programming and refine its strategic focus areas. She observed that, based on Members’ inputs, it was evident that the Committee is on the right trajectory. She further noted that a joint meeting with the Portfolio Committee on Energy is scheduled for two weeks’ time, where deliberations will centre on energy innovations, particularly the application of hydrogen.
She expressed concern about the volume of presentations and the limited time available for meaningful engagement with presenters. She highlighted the need to reimagine the Committee’s approach to preparation and interaction with departments and entities, to ensure more dynamic and substantive exchanges. Reflecting on a recent engagement with the Department of Mineral Resources and Petroleum, the Chairperson commended the fluidity of the dialogue, where responses were provided directly to Members, thereby enriching the quality of the discussion. She suggested that this model should inform the structuring of future meetings.
The Chairperson acknowledged her tendency to allow Members generous time to respond to presentations, noting the importance of Members’ contributions in guiding departments on how best to fulfil their mandates. However, she cautioned that when individual Members dominate discussions, it diminishes the time available for interaction with presenting entities. She emphasised the need for deliberate measures to balance contributions and ensure adequate space for constructive engagement.
She reaffirmed the Committee’s distinctive role as a “department of everything,” underscoring the importance of collaboration with other portfolio committees. Joint meetings, she explained, enable research and technological insights from the Committee to be constructively applied across government. She said that fostering stronger collaboration between the Department of Science, Technology and Innovation and other relevant departments remains a priority.
In response to the matter raised by Dr Boshoff on agriculture, the Chairperson acknowledged that a joint meeting with the Portfolio Committee on Agriculture has not yet taken place. She underlined the importance of continuous engagement with all portfolio committees in Parliament, given that the Committee’s research spans all sectors and is not confined to any single department. This central positioning, she noted, places the Committee at the core of developing solutions to the challenges confronting other departments.
Finally, she emphasised the need to manage the pace of meetings more effectively. In the previous quarter, the Committee had been constrained by the need to accommodate multiple entities within a single session, which limited the depth of engagement. She concluded that in future planning meetings must prioritise quality and meaningful interaction over quantity.
Ms Mazzone expressed her astonishment at Parliament’s willingness to allocate excessive amounts of money to catering. She questioned how a committee of this size could justify such expenditure, pointing out that Members are perfectly capable of eating at home. She further noted that no entity presenting before the Committee had ever remained behind for refreshments, making such catering unnecessary. Given the country’s current economic climate, she highlighted the importance of setting a realistic example and ensuring responsible use of public funds. Drawing a comparison, she stated that no business would ever spend such exorbitant amounts on catering for its board meetings. Moreover, the quality of the catering provided does not reflect the cost, rendering the expenditure unjustifiable. Members already earn a salary, she argued, and if anyone is hungry, they can simply purchase their own meal.
Turning to oversight visits, Ms Mazzone raised serious concerns about the high ground transport costs, which she described as disproportionate and unacceptable. She shared her experience of travelling at nearly 200 km/h in a Hyundai van on unsafe dirt roads, recounting the frightening moment when a tyre burst in front of them. She emphasised that it was only by the grace of God that no one was harmed. She stated that such incidents highlight the urgent need to review the service providers used by Parliament, as the costs charged are bordering on extortion. To illustrate her point on inflated travel costs, she explained that while Mr G Mankay, Committee Assistant, was speaking, she opened the CheapFlights.com app on her phone and easily found numerous available flights to Japan in all classes. This directly contradicts claims that flights were unavailable. She insisted that Parliament must interrogate the service providers it relies upon, as millions of rands could be saved with more competitive sourcing. She concluded by urging the Committee on Science, Technology and Innovation to lead by example; if the Committee is tasked with promoting innovation in the country, it must also be innovative and prudent in its own financial practices.
Ms Mchunu also addressed the matter of catering, stating that even simple provisions such as coffee accompanied by a light snack would be sufficient. She noted that when joint meetings are held, each Committee generally takes responsibility for its own catering arrangements, and this practice should continue to avoid unnecessary expenditure.
On the issue of the international trip, Ms Mchunu pointed out that the agency had indicated that flights were fully booked. She highlighted the importance of being presented with more than one option so that the Committee can properly evaluate alternatives and make informed decisions.
With respect to the processing of legislation, Ms Mchunu highlighted that the budget would need to be revised should legislation require public hearings, as travel for such hearings presents financial challenges. She also sought clarity on whether the R3 million allocation is a standard budget provided to all committees or whether it is specific to this Portfolio Committee for the current financial year.
Turning to the upcoming oversight trip to Japan, she emphasised that it is an important engagement for the Committee. She emphasised the value of benchmarking with international counterparts, stating that such exchanges would significantly strengthen the Committee’s oversight role. She concluded by reaffirming the need to identify practical ways of reducing venue hire and catering costs to ensure more efficient use of resources.
Mr Mjadu expressed his dissatisfaction with the expenditure figures reflected in the presentation, arguing that the amounts were excessive and could not be justified. He stated that public funds must be utilised responsibly and with strict regard for value for money. To this end, he proposed that the Committee set aside a dedicated session to re-examine the budget line by line, with the aim of identifying areas where costs could be reduced.
In particular, he raised concern over the R400 000 allocation for oversight activities, which he stated did not accurately correspond with the Committee’s experience during the visit. He emphasised the importance of realistic budgeting, cautioning against inflated allocations that do not reflect operational realities. Mr Mjadu reiterated that the Committee has a duty to ensure that every rand spent is justified, efficient, and aligned with its oversight mandate.
The Chairperson addressed the excessive catering costs, emphasising that expenditure in this area must be significantly reduced. She noted that large amounts of food are often provided at meetings, yet most of it remains uneaten, and the quality does not justify the expense.
Turning to transport costs, she explained that she had been briefed that Parliament has a pre-approved panel of service providers, which restricts the Committee from sourcing alternatives outside of this list. However, she remarked that the costs would be considerably lower than those currently reflected in the budget if she were to look for the flights and transport services.
The Chairperson requested clarity from Mr Makay on whether the R3 million allocation represented a standard budget applicable to all committees or whether it was specific to this Committee. She aligned herself with Ms Mazzone’s concerns regarding transport costs, noting that the vehicles provided, while adequate in quality and condition, did not justify the excessive amounts being charged. She concluded by cautioning that the pricing of such services borders on extortion and must be urgently addressed.
Ms S Isaacs, Committee Secretary, provided clarity on the budget allocations. She explained that, in the Budget Speech for Parliament, the House Chairperson had indicated that each Committee would receive an annual allocation of R3 million for the current parliamentary term. She noted that Mr Makay’s role was to prepare projections on how the Committee might utilise its allocation. In practice, however, the Committee seldom spends the full amount. Unspent funds are reallocated to other committees whose activities require greater resources. For example, during next year’s local government elections, the relevant Committee is expected to exceed its R3 million allocation and will rely on funds not utilised by smaller committees.
Addressing concerns about costs, she highlighted that middlemen significantly drive up expenses. Parliament uses a panel of approved service providers and operates on a three-quotation system, always opting for the lowest bid. Nonetheless, ground travel remains disproportionately expensive. She explained that this is due to strict rules governing chauffeur-driven vehicles, with additional charges incurred when travel is outside standard hours, often extending into the evenings. Further, in rural areas such as the Northern Cape, suitable vehicles are scarce and must be sourced from Cape Town, adding considerably to the cost.
She suggested that these concerns be escalated to Parliament’s administration at a higher level, as they point to systemic inefficiencies. Ms Isaacs also informed Members that Parliament is developing a system that would allow for direct bookings with airlines, which could substantially reduce costs. By way of example, she noted that a flight to Johannesburg can be booked privately for approximately R1 300 to R1 500, while Parliament is currently paying as much as R7 000 for the same journey.
Mr Mjadu emphasised that the budget allocation was excessive and required urgent adjustment.
Dr Thembekwayo expressed her agreement, supporting the proposal that a thorough investigation be conducted into the Committee’s expenditure. She noted that only once the findings were received could the Committee make an informed determination as to which costs were desirable and which were not, in relation to the allocated budget.
The Chairperson observed that there was broad consensus within the Committee on the concerns raised regarding the budget. She cautioned that, if matters were not addressed, the Committee’s current budget would be insufficient to cover both the planned international trip and its broader programme of work. She explained that this would risk a scenario where the Committee travels to Japan, returns, and is left without the means to continue other critical activities.
The Chairperson confirmed that she would engage with the relevant officials responsible for budgetary matters and stressed the need to review the system comprehensively. She stated that the Committee could not continue to operate under a framework where it was charged up to five times the normal cost of services. She concluded by noting that, if necessary, extended meetings would be convened to address these inefficiencies and ensure the Committee’s resources are used more effectively.
Closing Remarks
The Chairperson thanked the Committee Members for their contributions and active participation, noting that their input had significantly enriched the planning of the Committee’s work. She highlighted that there are several matters requiring further attention, particularly the oversight programmes. Also, she observed that, during one oversight visit, the Committee had to adjust its schedule at the last minute and, at times, engage in activities that were not part of its mandate. She emphasised the need for careful and thorough preparation for all planned oversight activities to ensure effectiveness and relevance.
She stressed that expenditures must be carefully reviewed and adjusted to achieve cost savings. The Committee should consider the financial viability of its programmes and make necessary adjustments while continuing to attend key events to remain informed. Reflecting on her recent attendance at the National Research Foundation (NRF) Awards on behalf of the Committee, she expressed concern at the lack of diversity, noting that very few awards went to Black recipients, and the majority were granted to older white men. She highlighted the stark underrepresentation of women in science, underscoring ongoing racial and gender imbalances in the science, technology, and innovation sector. She said that these issues must remain a priority to ensure equitable representation in the work conducted by entities, departments, and the government.
The Chairperson also raised concerns about the Committee’s limited staffing capacity. She noted that, previously, the Committee had a dedicated staff member responsible for communicating the Committee’s work to the public. Currently, the Committee operates without such support and has no dedicated researcher. While Dr Osborne-Mullins, as content advisor, has admirably taken on research responsibilities, the Committee lacks the structural support available to other parliamentary committees. Given that the Committee oversees research, the absence of a dedicated research team limits its capacity to monitor departmental activities. Nevertheless, she commended Dr Osborne-Mullins for ensuring that Members are kept fully informed and up to date on key issues.
The Chairperson concluded by noting that the challenges the Committee has faced provide valuable learning opportunities that will strengthen its work going forward. She commended the Members for their expertise and high level of engagement, affirming that the Committee functions as a cohesive and effective team. She wished the Committee well in the upcoming quarters and expressed confidence in its continued success.
The meeting was adjourned.
