Title: Advanced Agriculture and Food Cluster: CSIR & TIA Briefings
Date: 14 Nov 2025
Committee: 
URL: https://pmg.org.za/committee-meeting/42058/
============================================================

Meeting Summary
Video
The Council for Scientific and Industrial Research (CSIR) and the Technology Innovation Agency (TIA), entities of the Department of Science, Technology and Innovation (DSTI), briefed a joint sitting of the Portfolio Committees on Science and Technology and Innovation and Agriculture on their research, development, and innovation programmes aimed at modernising the agricultural sector. A central theme of the engagement was the critical challenge of coordination between government entities and the slow pace of commercialising research into tangible products and benefits for farmers, especially smallholders. Members expressed significant concern over the disparity between the advanced technological solutions presented and the persistent on-the-ground challenges, such as Foot and Mouth Disease (FMD) outbreaks.
The CSIR presentation focused on its Advanced Agriculture and Food cluster, highlighting the development of the Livestock Identification and Traceability System (LITS), and its planned expansion into a national System for Agricultural Biosecurity and Epidemiology Research (SABER). It also showcased a point-of-care diagnostic test and a plant-derived vaccine for FMD. A significant hurdle identified was that existing legislation currently blocks the deployment of the CSIR's novel diagnostic technologies within South Africa, forcing trials to be conducted overseas.
The TIA outlined its four agricultural clusters, representing a portfolio of over R1 billion, focusing on Beef and Dairy Genomics, Animal Health, and Agro-processing. It highlighted collaborations and some success stories, such as vaccine dossiers submitted for approval and a wheatgrass juice product launched in the retail environment.
During the discussion, Members raised concerns that the entities were operating in isolated silos, resulting in inefficiency and a lack of tangible impact. They questioned the return on substantial public investments, demanding accountability for why more innovations were not successfully reaching the market or enhancing farmer productivity. They repeatedly pressed for details on how advanced technologies would be made accessible, affordable, and scalable for smallholder and communal farmers.
Meeting report
Chairperson Shiviti welcomed Members from both Portfolio Committees. She said the session aimed to facilitate information sharing between the two departments and coordinate their oversight work. She emphasised that agriculture represented a critical sector supporting employment, rural livelihoods and national food security, while acknowledging challenges such as the repeated Foot and Mouth Disease (FMD) outbreaks causing market disruptions.
She said two entities from the Department of Science, Technology and Innovation (DSTI) had been invited to brief the Committees on available research and development solutions. She emphasised that while the STI Committee's focus was from a research and development (R&D) perspective, it remained crucial to coordinate mandates with the Agriculture Committee to prevent resource duplication and ensure optimal use of resources through collaborative efforts.
DSTI presentations
Dr Mlungisi Cele, Director-General (DG), DSTI, emphasised the Department's mandate, guided by the White Paper on Science, Technology and Innovation and the Decadal Plan, to promote responsible science that addresses societal needs, focusing on priorities like future-proofing education, energy, and health. A key focus was enabling agricultural innovation to support the Agriculture and Forestry master plans and the aquaculture research and technology development strategy, ensuring sustained food security and conservation of natural resources and genetic material.
Dr Cele emphasised the Department's focus on identifying innovation opportunities and developing new technologies across value chains, from early-stage research to commercialisation. He recognised the crucial role of the Technology Innovation Agency (TIA) and the Council for Scientific and Industrial Research (CSIR) in this work, and committed to fostering alignment and collaboration. This was exemplified by the biosecurity hub, which involves the CSIR, the Agricultural Research Council (ARC), and the University of Pretoria, aiming for optimal resource use to modernise the agricultural sector and achieve significant socio-economic benefits. The DSTI provides innovation support, aligned with the priorities of line function departments, particularly agriculture, building on existing collaboration through the national biosecurity hub.
The DG said that memoranda of understanding (MoUs) would be pursued to ensure synergy in precision agriculture and resource planning. He noted close collaboration with national and provincial departments on decision support tools for farmers and programs like "One Food, One Health." He specifically acknowledged the TIA's early involvement in the Livestock Identification and Traceability System (LITS) business plan and the CSIR's current support for its implementation. He stressed that these programmes were supported by strong cooperation agreements between the involved departments, ensuring strategic coherence, accountability, and mutual understanding, and thanked the entities for their exceptional work.
CSIR briefing
Dr Rachel Chikwamba, Group Executive: Advanced Chemistry and Life Sciences; Dr Nthabiseng Motete, Executive Cluster Manager: Advanced Agriculture and Food Cluster; and Dr Essa Suleman, Research Group Leader: Aquaculture and Animal Health, briefed the Committee on the work of the CSIR’s Advanced Agriculture and Food (AAF) cluster.
They explained that the AAF cluster’s value proposition was to harness advanced technologies to improve the performance of the agriculture sector. This work was aligned with national priorities and supported the agriculture and agro-processing masterplan. A central theme of the briefing was the critical challenge posed by the lack of an integrated national software platform for managing over 40 controlled and notifiable agricultural diseases. To address this, the CSIR was developing the System for Agricultural Biosecurity and Epidemiology Research (SABER). The system was envisioned as a unified national software platform for real-time reporting, visualisation, and artificial intelligence (AI) in agricultural biosecurity, biosafety, surveillance, and epidemiology.
SABER was being developed as an expansion of the CSIR's existing LITS, a pilot system which registers animals and tracks animal events such as health checks and vaccinations. The presenters clarified that LITS did not compete with the Red Meat Industry Services (RMIS) platform, and was designed to be integrable. Specific CSIR technologies showcased included a point-of-care diagnostic test and a plant-expressed vaccine for FMD. The Committee was shown the functionality of an interactive FMD map. A key challenge identified was that existing legislation currently permits only the Agricultural Research Council to perform diagnostics for many notifiable diseases, which currently blocks the deployment of the CSIR's novel point-of-care diagnostic technologies within South Africa.
The CSIR board concluded that reducing the burden of disease through enhanced surveillance and epidemiology, facilitated by a national platform like SABER, would improve agricultural production, economic growth, and food security.

Discussion
Mr A Trollip (Action SA
) said he had listened with great interest to the presentation, as there was a lot that sounded impressive, especially the work on FMD and the creation of a new plant-based vaccine. He wondered why, against the background of this very impressive presentation, the country continued to struggle with FMD outbreaks since losing its World Organisation for Animal Health free status in 2019. He observed that it sounded as if the CSIR was operating in its own vacuum or silo, the ARC in its own, and Onderstepoort Biological Products in another. The presentation from the CSIR and the lived reality of FMD management in the country seemed completely incoherent. He wanted to know why this splendid presentation did not manifest in getting the disease under control.
Ms L Arries (EFF) wanted to know how the CSIR collaborated with other government departments and communities to strengthen local food systems and improve access to food. She also asked if the CSIR was involved in the development of sustainable agricultural technology to address drought and soil degradation, and what role it played in ensuring food safety and nutritional value in the local food system. She inquired about the research and innovation the CSIR had developed to improve food production and reduce post-harvest losses. She wanted to know what innovations or technologies had been developed or supported to improve agricultural productivity, particularly for emerging farmers. Her final question was what funding or research had been allocated to develop drought-resistant crop decision-making regarding farming tools.
Dr W Boshoff (FF+) welcomed the interesting presentation, but shared concerns related to Mr Trollip's point. He recalled that when the RMIS platform was recently presented to the public, the Minister of Agriculture had been present. He wanted to know what the contact from the Department's side with the CSIR was, and whether the Department of Agriculture was serious about liaising with the CSIR. It seemed to him as if the CSIR had, in fact, taken over functions that Onderstepoort used to fulfil and should still be fulfilling.
Regarding the LITS system and its expansion into the SABER system, he noted that the two were said to be technically compatible. However, he wanted to know, from a practical situation on the ground, if there was a willingness from the RMIS to make their system part of the wider LITS or SABER system, or if there was a more competitive way of thinking between the two institutions. He also relayed concerns from interest groups within the livestock industry that in the RMIS, big feedlots were very well represented, which could create an information imbalance. This imbalance could allow large feedlots to know what was happening on each farm, giving them an edge in determining prices. He asked if the CSIR's system would increase that risk.
Mr V Nkosi (ANC) first addressed the point-of-care diagnostic test kit for FMD. While designed for remote use, he worried that the cost and technical demands of extensive genomic testing or satellite systems could exclude smaller farmers. He stressed that technology localisation was crucial for inclusive growth, asking how advanced solutions like genomics and drone surveillance were being made accessible and affordable for the majority of small-scale farmers. Secondly, given the reliance on big data from genomics, drones, and surveillance systems, he inquired about the strategy for creating a single, secure, national agricultural data platform that integrates both animal health and production data. Thirdly, he emphasised that technological focus must be complemented by broader policy reforms and grassroots capacity development.
Noting that smallholders often faced adoption barriers, he said that tailored training and financial support should accompany technological rollouts. He asked how the CSIR would ensure effective coordination and data sharing among multiple stakeholders to prevent fragmentation and improve response times. Finally, he inquired how the long-term sustainability of funding, maintenance, and upgrades for these digital systems would be ensured, given the rapid pace of technological change.
Ms N Ndalane (ANC) asked how the CSIR's research supported industrial development in South Africa and what the expected outcomes were. Her second question was what challenges the CSIR faced in implementing these initiatives, and how they were being addressed. Lastly, she wanted to know the plans for the Advanced Agriculture and Food cluster, and how it would continue to support the South African agricultural sector.
Mr T Mjadu (MKP) raised a concern regarding the entity's request for public funds for the cyber platform, asking for the total budget and a line-by-line cost justification. He asked about the total public money already spent on LITS and the measurable benefit it had delivered in disease control. He further highlighted the misalignment between government regulation, which currently blocks the use of new diagnostic tools, and the CSIR's development of them. He asked why public funds were still being allocated to technologies currently forbidden by law, and which government official was responsible for the failure to update the relevant legislation.
He requested one specific example of a CSIR technology successfully containing a major disease outbreak, and asked for the exact numerical performance targets for the cyber platform. He questioned the justification for the government funding of two separate systems -- the RMIS and LITS/SABER -- instead of one unified national platform, and asked how much money had been wasted on this duplication. Finally, he questioned the CSIR's creation of a state monopoly, asking how it would prevent the misuse of farmers' data by the government, and what guarantees were in place to prevent South African agricultural data from being shared with foreign entities like the UN's Food and Agricultural Organisation (FAO).
Dr S Thembekwayo (EFF) asked what measures were in place to ensure that emerging and communal livestock farmers could access and benefit from the monitoring technologies on value-added nutrition and food safety. She noted that many farmers were not even aware of the CSIR's involvement and asked how the CSIR planned to reach out to them. She also inquired how the CSIR was leveraging technologies like precision agriculture to improve agricultural productivity and sustainability, and which metrics were used to measure the impact and evaluate the success of its initiatives. She also asked how emerging farmers could be assisted to adopt climate-resilient farming practices. Her last question was how the CSIR was involved in addressing soil degradation, and whether it was developing any sustainable agricultural technologies to address this issue.
Ms T Mchunu (ANC) asked about capacity building and technical skills in institutions of higher learning. She wanted to know what measures or plans were in place to engage with these institutions to ensure skills transfer to future generations. Her second question was on the sustainability of funding, maintenance, and regular upgrades of the digital systems, and how this would be ensured in the long term, given the rapid pace of technological change. Regarding financial sustainability and FMD, she asked if the CSIR had a plan or method, in collaboration with the Department of Agriculture, Land Reform and Rural Development (DALRRD), to assist all farmers, including private, subsistence, and communal farmers. She elaborated that large private farmers with big livestock farmers were currently struggling to access assistance for vaccines and preventing infection. She asked if private farmers could pay to access CSIR facilities directly, and if the DALRRD would assist in facilitating this.
Mr C Smit (DA) felt that most questions around FMD had been exhausted. His question specifically concerned support from the DSTI for agricultural research in entities like the ARC. He recalled that during an oversight visit to the ARC, scientists had reported struggling to keep up with new technology in terms of equipment and facilities. He stated his belief that the country was not investing enough in science in general, and that agriculture was an absolute science. His question was specifically about financial support from the DSTI institutions, like the ARC, to keep up with the necessary facilities and the latest technology. He argued that this would allow South Africa to compete, become the science hub of Africa, and unlock economic growth potential.
Co-Chairperson Pule began by referring to Mr Trollip's comment on entities working in silos. She said that the presentation did not provide comfort that these entities were working together. She specifically wanted to know how the CSIR and the TIA worked together to avoid a situation where they, too, were working in silos. She asked how they linked up their respective mandates, particularly the CSIR's research and development with TIA's innovation commercialisation, to avoid duplication and maximise efficiency, and to ensure alignment with the livestock sector.
She then asked what measures were in place to ensure that emerging and communal livestock farmers could access monitoring and surveillance technologies, considering affordability, training, support, and connectivity. Another question for the CSIR was how it ensured that the technologies it developed were scalable and commercially viable, specifically in moving from a prototype to farmer and industry adoption.
To the TIA, she asked what role commodity organisations, such as those for beef, sheep, goats, and dairy and farmer organisations, played in guiding and adopting the technologies developed under these clusters. Lastly, she asked about the export readiness implications of these technologies, such as the traceability, animal health certification, genetics, benchmarking, and processing standards, and how they would enhance South Africa's competitiveness in international red meat and dairy markets.
TIA's briefing
The briefing was led by Mr Loyiso Tyira, Chairperson, and Mr Mohohlo Molatudi, Acting Executive: Bioeconomy, Technology Innovation Agency (TIA). They outlined the Agency's strategic initiatives within the agricultural sector, positioning itself as a key implementer of South Africa's bioeconomy strategy. The core of TIA's approach was the management of four dedicated agricultural clusters, which were collaborative, industry-focused programmes designed to use innovation and technology to drive economic growth, food security, and rural development. These clusters represented targeted interventions in high-potential areas:
The Beef Genomics Programme, a large-scale R149 million initiative to revolutionise the industry through genetic science;
The Dairy Genomics Cluster, which has established a framework for more accurate genetic evaluations;
The Animal Health Cluster, aimed at strengthening local R&D and commercialisation for veterinary solutions; and
The Agro-processing Cluster, which focuses on transforming raw materials into higher-value goods.
These strategic programmes were supported by a significant portfolio valued at over R1 billion for the 2024/25 financial year, funding a diverse range of entities from universities and science councils to small, medium and micro enterprises (SMMEs) and grassroots innovators. A key theme throughout the presentation was the emphasis on collaborative partnerships, with each cluster being implemented through consortia that bring together public institutions like the ARC and universities with industry bodies such as SA Studbook and Milk SA. This model amplified impact by combining scientific expertise with the TIA's commercialisation mandate.
Finally, the presentation highlighted concrete outputs and success stories, including the impending commercialisation of the Respiclos and Tetanus vaccines in 2026, and the launch of a wheatgrass juice product by a supported SMME in a major retail group, demonstrating a clear pathway from research and development to tangible market and societal impact.

Discussion
Mr Mjadu contended that the report revealed a shocking misallocation of public funds, noting that R1.1 billion was spent while the animal health cluster had commercialised zero products after a R119 million investment. He demanded accountability, asking why not a single product had reached the market despite this expenditure. He further questioned the favouritism in allocating contracts, observing that the same few universities, such as the University of Cape Town, consistently received all the work. He wanted to know what was being done to stop this practice.
On job creation, he referred to the agro-processing cluster in the Eastern Cape, which he stated had created only four permanent jobs with R6 million in funding. He found this unjustifiable in a province with massive unemployment, and which was a poor region. He also criticised the entity's reported successes as embarrassing, saying the launch of a single grass juice in a SPAR supermarket was a publicity stunt, rather than a credible national bioeconomic strategy. He asked if the entity and the Committee could genuinely consider this a major achievement.
Furthermore, he criticised partnerships with large corporations like Tiger Brands, labelling them a "scandal waiting to happen." He argued that using public money to support SMEs partnered with massive corporations amounted to subsidising big business, not development, and opened the door to the cooperative capture of public innovation funds with no guarantee of benefits for the poor. He demanded to know why public funds were being used for projects that ultimately benefited a giant company. He concluded by asking whether the entity would support a full forensic audit into all agriculture programmes to determine where the R1.1 billion had truly been spent.
Mr Trollip said he had the same reservations about the TIA presentation as he had with the CSIR's. He acknowledged the talk of investments in beef and dairy genomics and the agro-processing programme in the Eastern Cape, which promoted sustainable practices. However, he contended that during recent oversight visits to the Eastern Cape, the Committee had not seen the impact of these initiatives. He contrasted this with past successes, citing the development of the Bonsmara cattle and Dohne sheep breeds through public-private collaboration, which had become world-renowned. He argued that this was the level of impact expected from such investments.
He provided a concrete example from an oversight visit, where a farm in the Alexandria area was preparing to grow soya, sunflower, and canola, despite no commercial farmers growing those crops there. He recalled a previous instance in the Eastern Cape, where an initiative to grow cotton was launched, which included the development of a cotton gin in Adelaide [163:24]. He stated that this gin had never processed a single bale of cotton, and that in areas like
Ngqushwa
, where the cotton had been planted, it was done only once and never again.
Mr Trollip remarked that the current initiatives presented reports to the Committee under the assumption that its Members did not operate on the ground, and did not see the stagnation in the commercialisation of black farmers. They see black farmers still producing the same animals their forefathers did, in the same way and in the same areas, with little technological advancement. While he was initially worried about a lack of collaboration with breed societies, he noted some mention of it towards the end of the presentation. He emphasised that the goal should not be to reinvent the wheel, but for the Agency to identify the best, already profitable, practices from the commercial sector and help upcoming farmers to adopt them. He saw little evidence of this, leading him to agree with Mr Mjadu that the billion-rand investment was bearing minimal fruit.
Finally, aligning himself with Mr Mjadu’s concerns, Mr Trollip questioned the scale of the training programmes, noting that the presentation mentioned training only 71, and then 46, farmers. He asked what difference such small numbers would make. He concluded that he did not believe the presenter or the Department was taking the Portfolio Committee seriously, as if its Members were unaware of the realities in the agricultural sector.
Mr Nkosi acknowledged that the animal health cluster had successfully submitted dossiers, strengthened R&D capacity, and fostered collaborations with public institutions, positioning South Africa as a potential regional leader. However, he noted that the lack of commercialisation at this stage indicated that while innovation output was on the rise, translating this into market-ready products remained a challenge. His question was how effective the TIA was in ensuring that innovative projects progressed beyond the research and development stage, specifically from technology readiness levels 3 to 5, to become commercially viable products at levels 8 to 9. He asked what specific mechanisms were in place to bridge this "valley of death."
Ms L Sapo (ANC) had three points, beginning with a comment on supporting agricultural entities. She expressed a problem with how government-owned agricultural colleges and the capacitation of technical and vocational education and training (TVET) colleges were supported. She had an issue with the prioritisation of universities and centres of technology over TVET colleges and agricultural schools, arguing that the government had absolute control over the latter. She said that while universities should still be supported, government must take cognisance of where it had direct control, and should not neglect the TVET sector, where so much money was needed.
She also requested clarity on the monitoring and evaluation of the funds invested and the impact made. She echoed Mr Trollip's sentiment that a lot of money was invested, but the impact in communities was totally different. She wanted to know what monitoring and evaluation frameworks the entity had established to measure the social and economic impact of the agricultural innovations it supports.
Ms Sapo then turned to the issue of stakeholders, linking it to what Mr Mjadu had raised earlier, asking if there was clear coordination between the public and private sectors. She wanted to know how the entity ensured that stakeholders played a critical role in changing people's lives, rather than just being listed for the sake of having them. She questioned how useful these stakeholders were, what their specific role was in achieving objectives, and how the existing public-private partnerships were integrated to ensure they had the necessary impact.
Co-Chairperson Pule asked whether the entity shared its database of supported farmers with the DALRRD. She wanted to verify that the farmers listed in the entity's records were also registered in the Department's system, as this would demonstrate concrete coordination between institutions and prevent working in silos.
CSIR's response
Dr Chikwamba requested the opportunity to supplement verbal responses with written submissions, particularly where specific numbers were required. She provided high-level responses before delegating detailed answers to her colleagues, and advised that issues of budgets and numerical data would be submitted separately in writing.
She addressed Mr Trollip's question on the disparity between the CSIR's work and the lived reality of challenges like FMD, and said that as an innovation organisation, many of their solutions, such as the LITS system, were at an early stage of development. She noted that an SMME had licensed the foot and mouth diagnostic technology, but due to current legislation, it was being trialled outside the country. Regarding vaccine development, she confirmed the CSIR's desire to work closely with the ARC, which holds the relevant mandate, but acknowledged being hampered by infrastructure challenges, which they hoped would be addressed.
On the issue of the CSIR working in silos, Dr Chikwamba stated firmly that the CSIR collaborates with the ARC and Onderstepoort Biological Products (OBP), with ongoing discussions about upgrading facilities for vaccine production. In response to Ms Ndalane's question on industry support, she said the CSIR supports SMMEs by lowering barriers to entry through technology, capability, and infrastructure, helping them to convert ideas into marketable products. She cited examples, including breakfast cereals, cosmetic products, and higher-end technologies like diagnostics and vaccines.
Dr Motete responded directly to concerns about siloed operations. She asserted that there was no silo operation between research and technology organisations, highlighting collaboration with the South African National Space Agency and the DSTI on the 'system of systems' for agricultural modernisation, which was being piloted with the Eastern Cape provincial Department of Agriculture. This system integrates national soil data to help farmers adapt to climate change. She acknowledged that the CSIR's work in agriculture was less known than the ARC's, and that they were making efforts to increase visibility and partner more strongly with commodity associations and the Agriculture Business Chamber to reach more farmers.
Mr Rethabile Khutlang, Research Group Leader: CSIR, provided an update on the LITS system. He confirmed that phase one was complete and actively being piloted, with phase two focused on improvements. He emphasised that the system was fully integrable, built entirely on open-source components with transparent animal protection indices (APIs) to allow easy connection for entities such as the RMIS. He reported that the system's development had been highly consultative, and included modules for animal registration, movement control, and key event recording, with new modules for ear tag management and vaccine traceability in development. He said that the pilot was running in all nine provinces, with over 10 000 inspection points on the system.
Dr Suleman addressed the persistence of FMD. He explained that the disease was endemic in the buffalo population of the Kruger National Park, presenting a constant source of potential outbreaks. He said that disease management was complex and required coordination across multiple partners, including the CSIR and the ARC, on diagnostics, surveillance and vaccines. Regarding the point-of-care diagnostic, he confirmed that a key design principle was to make it accessible to smallholder farmers in remote areas. He cited its successful use in Rwanda, enabling smallholders to prove their herds were disease-free and access international export markets.
Finally, on data sharing with the FAO, Dr Suleman assured the Committee that while data could be shared to improve early warning systems, it remained the property and priority of the country of origin, and South Africa did not lose access or benefits from its data.
TIA's response
Mr Tyira addressed several issues before delegating to his colleague. On the question of partnerships with large companies like Tiger Brands, he clarified that the TIA did not fund these corporations. Instead, it leverages them for market access within its "quadruple helix" model, using their position as major buyers to commercialise innovations from SMMEs. He cited a similar upcoming partnership with Takealot for online sales and township-based manufacturing. He added that the TIA was exploring how to coordinate private sector R&D budgets to align with national innovation goals, and had signed cooperation agreements with several sector education and training authorities (SETAs).
Responding to Ms Sapo's concern about TVET colleges, he confirmed that the Minister had emphasised this, and the TIA's new strategy included specific targets for engaging with TVETs and previously disadvantaged universities, which the board was monitoring.
Mr Molatudi provided further context. He clarified that the R1.1 billion investment was spread across various sectors, including human health and industrial biotechnology, not just agriculture. He acknowledged the great responsibility of using public funds in a high-risk, trial-and-error environment, but stated the TIA was employing mechanisms like corporate partnerships to improve success rates. He cited the lack of Biosafety Level (BSL) 3 facilities as a major systemic limitation for vaccine development.
Addressing Mr Trollip's point about not reinventing the wheel, he assured the Committee that the TIA works with industry to define specific "pain points" so that public funds are not spent on solutions without a clear market problem. On metrics, he stated that the TIA had trained over 5 000 farmers and more than 15 000 students and women through its programmes. He added that the four SMMEs supported by the Eastern Cape cluster created downstream employment.
On Mr Mjadu's question about funding concentration, he referred to a presentation slide showing that 43% of the TIA's agriculture funding went to SMMEs. He acknowledged the challenge of dominant universities, and said the TIA had targets to support other institutions as part of its transformation efforts. He confirmed that internal monitoring and evaluation processes were in place to assess the impact of programmes.
DSTI's response
Dr Cele made three overarching points. Firstly, he acknowledged that coordination across the government and with the private sector remained a significant challenge, though it was a work in progress. Secondly, he confirmed that commercialising research was another major hurdle for the national system. Thirdly, he stated that financial investment in R&D was regressing -- it was currently at 0.6% of gross domestic product (GDP), well below the state's 1.5% target, with private sector investment being particularly lacking. He also conceded that the sector needed to improve how it communicated its work to the public. He concluded by confirming an agreement with the Department of Agriculture to strengthen their partnership and ensure better coherence between their respective entities.
DALRRD's response
Mr Mooketsa Ramasodi, DG, DALRRD, expressed appreciation for the engagement. He emphasised that the agricultural sector's growth since 1994 was largely driven by research and innovation. On the speed of commercialisation, particularly for vaccines, he explained that any new technology must, by law, undergo rigorous processes to ensure it is safe, high-quality, and efficacious. However, the Department was working to shorten these timelines. Regarding livestock systems, he stated that the LITS and the RMIS platforms were not competitors but provided complementary, interoperable services that together would broaden the sector's reach.
He concluded by reinforcing his commitment with Dr Cele to ensure that agricultural science received the necessary attention and reached all farmers.
Follow-up comments
Co-Chairperson Pule raised a final point before concluding. She emphasised the need for the Departments of Science and Innovation and Agriculture to present a more unified front to the Committee. She expressed concern that when entities like the CSIR and TIA presented, it did not feel as though their work was being coordinated by the DALRRD as the sector leader. She cited the example of animal identification technology, questioning whether its intellectual property should be registered under a science body, rather than falling under the mandate of the Department of Agriculture. She asked the DG to assist in clarifying these roles.
She also urged the two Directors-General to play a more active role in connecting the CSIR and TIA with stakeholders in the agricultural sector. She argued that this would ensure their innovations had impact, particularly for small-scale businesses and farmers who needed the technologies most. Her vision for the future was for all entities, including the CSIR, the TIA, Onderstepoort Biological Products and the Agricultural Research Council, to present a single consolidated report to the Committee on issues relating to agriculture.
Dr Cele (DG of the DSTI) responded by acknowledging her critical comments and stated that the Department would consider them as part of its ongoing effort to improve collaboration within the system.
Chairperson Shiviti fully agreed with the need for consolidated efforts. She stated that the main aim of joint Portfolio Committee meetings was to ensure all government entities worked in unison to find solutions, rather than operating in silos. She expressed her hope that both Departments would utilise each other's capabilities to solve problems related to food security and vaccine development.
She expressed gratitude to the Members of both portfolio Committees for a meaningful discussion on technologies for the agricultural sector. She specifically thanked the two DGs for leading the discussions and the chairpersons of the CSIR and TIA boards for their presentations.
The meeting was adjourned.
