Meeting Summary
Video
The Committee received briefings from the Department of Social Development (DSD) and the South African Social Security Agency (SASSA) on the investigation into system vulnerabilities in the Social Relief of Distress (SRD) platform and on progress in meeting conditions set by National Treasury. It was highlighted that system vulnerabilities in the SRD platform were first exposed through public reporting, prompting the commissioning of a comprehensive assessment by an external firm. The assessment identified weaknesses in encryption, content-security protocols, unauthorised APIs, fraudulent imitation websites, and risks within the payment architecture. The Department reported that most of the recommended security upgrades had been completed, including the introduction of biometric verification, improved encryption, reinforcement of firewalls, and shutting down of approximately 70 fraudulent websites. Ongoing risks included discrepancies in submitted information, and multiple applicants using the same contact number. The Committee was informed that cybersecurity monitoring is now conducted continuously through a security operations centre, supported by artificial-intelligence-driven threat-intelligence tools. Internal audit processes confirmed significant improvement in system security, with reductions in cases of identity theft compared to the previous year.
The Committee also received a report on compliance with National Treasury conditions governing grant administration. These conditions required SASSA to intensify income verification, conduct bank and credit-bureau checks, strengthen beneficiary authentication, and provide quarterly reporting. SASSA reported progress across all conditions, noting that income checks through banks had been completed for the first half of the financial year, with registration checks still under development. Biometric verification had been fully rolled out for in-office applications, with online systems due for upgrade. Cross-checks against government databases had been conducted, though some datasets were received late and required further analysis. The Committee heard that 259 000 grant reviews had been initiated, largely for child support and older person’s grants. Of these, around 201 000 beneficiaries responded, resulting in thousands of suspensions, lapses, and value adjustments, collectively generating significant projected annual savings.
During deliberations, Members raised concerns about the persistence of fraudulent SASSA-mimicking websites, calling for mechanisms to prevent their creation entirely. Members welcomed the Department’s progress but emphasised the need for continuous vigilance and suggested the establishment of a dedicated cybersecurity centre or cyber-range to proactively test and protect systems. Questions were raised about the inability to conduct bank account and credit bureau checks at the point of registration, the feasibility of improving data sharing between departments, and the reliance on external institutions for verification. Members also queried inconsistencies in eligibility determinations, including cases where unemployed, first-time applicants were declined. Concerns were also raised about the disability grant assessment process, particularly for individuals with chronic but manageable conditions who had no income and struggled to access consistent treatment or nutrition. Issues relating to the reliability of online applications were discussed, with Members noting cases where applicants received no acknowledgement or follow-up communication despite completing online submissions. The Committee stressed that an efficient digital system was essential to reducing queues at SASSA offices.

Minister's remarks
Ms Sisisi Tolashe, Minister of Social Development, thanked the Chairperson and greeted the Members of the Portfolio Committee. She also acknowledged the Deputy Minister of Social Development, senior officials from National Treasury; and the broader leadership of the Social Development portfolio. She expressed appreciation for the opportunity to brief the Committee on measures being implemented to address critical system vulnerabilities within the Social Relief of Distress (SRD) grant platform. She further congratulated former Committee Member, Ms Abrahams, on her appointment as Deputy Minister of Trade, Industry and Competition, offering warm wishes for her success in the new role.
She noted that the reported system vulnerabilities were first brought to light by two first-year students at the University of Stellenbosch in the previous year. The matter received extensive media attention, prompting the Committee to call for a thorough investigation into the SRD system and other grant administration platforms. Members were reminded that President Ramaphosa had introduced the COVID-19 SRD grant in response to the severe socioeconomic impact of the pandemic. To meet urgent needs during that period, the Department and SASSA rapidly developed digital systems for application processing, verification and payment. This occurred during a time of rapid digital transformation, when cyber vulnerabilities were becoming increasingly sophisticated across all sectors. Following the Committee’s directive, Masegare and Associates Incorporated was appointed to conduct a comprehensive vulnerability assessment and penetration testing of the SRD online platform. Their mandate included identifying weaknesses, assessing risks and developing a full threat detection, prevention and mitigation plan. The findings, previously presented to the Committee, confirmed vulnerabilities in four critical areas: weak encryption, unauthorised application programming interfaces, fraudulent websites imitating official SASSA channels and risks within the beneficiary payment system. Recommendations included strengthening authentication and cybersecurity controls, removing fraudulent websites, improving verification and payment security, and enhancing IT governance and accountability. She reported that SASSA had been actively implementing these recommendations. Measures underway include rolling out biometric verification for online transactions, upgrading security protocols and intensifying collaboration with entities such as the South African Revenue Service (SARS), the Unemployment Insurance Fund (UIF) and the banking sector. She emphasised that vulnerability assessments are not a once-off exercise but an ongoing requirement. Quarterly assessments were, therefore, being conducted and these were supported by enhanced security mechanisms and monitoring tools capable of detecting and responding to cyber threats promptly. Internal audit processes also continue to evaluate the effectiveness of controls, governance and risk management.
Reference was made to the recent announcement by the Minister of Finance extending the SRD grant until March 2027. She described the extension as an important commitment to supporting vulnerable individuals in the current economic climate. Improved digital verification tools will help ensure that only qualifying beneficiaries receive assistance. The extension will also provide sufficient time to finalise policy proposals for the introduction of a basic income support mechanism, as directed by Cabinet. A two-day colloquium was recently held to refine proposals linking social protection to labour market and employment programmes. The Minister stated that the Department intends to submit the policy proposal to Cabinet before the end of the financial year, after which it will be published for public consultation.
She drew attention to the second presentation scheduled for the meeting, which focuses on SASSA’s implementation of conditions imposed by the National Treasury on budget allocations. These reforms strengthen fiscal accountability, transparency and governance. The Minister stated that Section 41 of the Appropriation Act grants the Minister of Finance expanded powers to ensure compliance with these conditions. The involvement of National Treasury officials in the meeting was welcomed, given the longstanding concerns about fraudulent activities undermining the integrity of the social grants system. She stressed that these measures are preventive rather than punitive and are intended to ensure that grants are paid to the correct person, at the correct time and place.
Minister Tolashe concluded by noting that SASSA made significant progress but that sustained commitment and focus remain essential. She indicated that the Department intends to safeguard the gains made and reinforce accountability within the social assistance system. She then handed over to Ms Mvulane, and her team to deliver the departmental presentation.
SASSA Presentation
Ms Zodwa Mvulane, Executive Manager: Corporate Services, SASSA, addressed the Chairperson and Members, noting that the relevant official had been unavailable during the planning process and that she had been requested to oversee the proceedings. She explained that the presentation had a dual focus. Firstly, it provided an update to the House on the vulnerabilities identified within SASSA and the measures recommended to address them. She confirmed that the majority of the recommendations had been implemented, as reflected in the presentation, which indicated completed measures in green. However, two areas remained in progress, marked in amber, to highlight ongoing work. Secondly, Ms Mvulane addressed the conditions set by National Treasury. She noted that four of the five conditions required the department to implement measures ensuring accurate data and that grants were disbursed to the correct beneficiaries. The fifth condition required the Department to report progress on a quarterly basis. She stated that this segment of the presentation aimed to brief the Committee on the progress made and the positive outcomes achieved thus far.
Ms Mvulane then handed over to Mr Makondo for the first part of the presentation. She indicated that he would be followed shortly by the Executive Manager for Grants Administration, who would present on the conditionalities set by National Treasury.
Mr Jabulani Makondo, Acting Chief Information Officer, SASSA, confirmed the technical readiness of the presentation, ensuring it was visible to the Committee before proceeding. He presented an update on progress addressing vulnerabilities in the SRD system. He explained that the presentation aimed to brief the Portfolio Committee on measures implemented following the Masegare report and other assessments. He provided context, noting that the SRD system was established in 2020 to support individuals who had lost employment. The system processes approximately 17.8 million applications, with an average of 8 million approvals per month. Applicant information is validated through real-time interfaces with government databases, including the Department of Home Affairs (DHA), SARS, Government Employees Pension Fund (GEPF), and the Department of Public Service and Administration (DPSA), with facial biometrics deployed to verify identities. Banking checks are also used to prevent fraudulent grant payments.
Mr Makondo recalled that in 2024, Stellenbosch University students raised concerns about system vulnerabilities, prompting a commissioned assessment by Masegare and Associates Incorporated. He emphasised that vulnerability assessments are ongoing, as system updates and policy changes may introduce new risks, with critical vulnerabilities prioritised for immediate remediation.
Key vulnerabilities addressed include:
Content Security Policy (CSP): Misconfigurations allowing code injection have been corrected.
Encryption: Data transmitted between applicants and the system is now properly encrypted.
Server directories and backup files: Sensitive information moved to secure environments.
System downtime: High availability and redundancy implemented across two data centers.
Facial biometrics: Applicant identities are verified online.
Fraudulent websites: Around 70 SASSA-mimicking websites have been shut down.
Ongoing risks include discrepancies between submitted and database records, and multiple applicants per cell phone, with mitigation processes underway.
Mr Moses Mbedhli, General Manager: Internal Audit, SASSA, provided independent assurance on the progress. He confirmed that vulnerability and penetration tests had been conducted, focusing on previously exploited issues. Security measures, including strict firewall and script controls, were found effective. He noted a reduction in reported identity theft cases from 1 100 in 2023 to around 500 between April and September, reflecting improved protections, though some cases may remain unreported.
Mr Brenton van Vrede, Executive Manager: Grants Administration, SASSA, presented a progress report on compliance with National Treasury conditions. He outlined the purpose of the report, provided an overview of the conditions, reviewed data submitted in the first and second quarter reports, and concluded with recommendations. The presentation aimed to demonstrate SASSA’s compliance with Treasury directives for the first half of the financial year and highlight mitigation strategies supporting effective financial management, operational efficiency, and transparent governance. He noted that National Treasury conditions had recently been amended. Previously, unsatisfactory compliance could result in withheld transfer funds, but the new allocation removed this clause, though non-compliance could still lead to irregular expenditure.
Key progress against the conditions includes
:
Bank income checks: Contracts finalised and the first round of biannual checks completed for all grants. New registration checks are still under development.
Credit bureau checks: Conducted twice in the first half of the year; registration checks will be implemented going forward.
Other income and asset checks: Ongoing, with engagements to clarify Treasury requirements.
Database cross-checks: Conducted against DHA, Correctional Services, Biokinetics Association of South Africa (BASA), UIF, and Government Employees Fund (GEF); SARS data received later and under review.
Biometric checks: Fully implemented for SRT and face-to-face applications; online social grant system expected to go live by the end of November.
Quarterly reporting: First and second quarter reports submitted within 30 days, covering reviews, suspensions, cancellations, and savings achieved.
Mr van Vrede highlighted that 259 000 reviews were initiated in the first two quarters, predominantly for the Child Support Grant (104 000) and Older Person’s Grant (102 000). Of these, 201 000 beneficiaries responded, approximately 60 000 were issued suspension notices. Review outcomes included 8 600 grant value adjustments and 34 000 lapses, generating monthly savings of R28.5 million and projected full-year savings of R340 million. He confirmed that notifications for the second half of the year had commenced, focusing on high-risk beneficiaries, with a temporary pause in November and December to manage office capacity. Biometric checks had been fully rolled out from 1 September for face-to-face applications, and online systems are being updated accordingly.
Ms Mvulane concluded the presentation by emphasising the progress in cleaning the grant database and the savings achieved. She noted specific cases, such as CODA and PESL, where higher beneficiary numbers reflect Expanded Public Works Programme (EPWP) and foster care grants, which are being verified.
The Chairperson thanked Ms Mvulane and all presenters for their comprehensive presentations. She acknowledged the work done to achieve savings without affecting rightful beneficiaries, noting that these efficiencies were commendable. She then handed over to Members for deliberations and questions on the presentations, highlighting that the briefings clearly addressed both vulnerabilities and compliance with National Treasury conditions. The Chairperson also observed that the recently added Treasury requirements had already been complied with and emphasised that ongoing vigilance is necessary to safeguard public funds.

Mr Makondo thanked the Chairperson and Members for their questions, noting that the delegation would take turns responding.
Ms Mvulane proposed that they begin with questions relating to system vulnerabilities before addressing the operational matters. She and Mr Makondo then jointly responded to the issues raised.
Mr Makondo began by addressing concerns regarding the vulnerability of SASSA’s digital platforms. He explained that it was not possible to permanently prevent individuals or entities from creating fake websites that mimic official S platforms, as such sites could originate from within or outside South Africa. He assured Members, however, that a number of protections had been implemented. He indicated that, prior to enhancements to the CSP policy and security configuration, fraudulent websites could interact with SASSA’s systems and exchange data. This was no longer possible. If a beneficiary unknowingly accessed a fake website and attempted to enter their information, no data exchange or feedback would occur, which served as a clear indicator that the site was not legitimate.
Mr Makondo further explained that SASSA collaborates closely with the .ZA Domain Name Authority and related industry bodies responsible for domain registrations. SASSA has formally notified these entities that only the agency is authorised to register domain names associated with its identity, and the authority monitors suspicious domain registrations. In addition, he highlighted the existence of SASSA’s Security Operations Centre, which monitors SRD-related transactions and scans for suspicious online activity across global networks. He stated that these combined controls provided a reasonable level of comfort that attempts to mimic SASSA’s systems were being actively disrupted.
Responding to a question about the agency’s cyber-range and cybersecurity posture, he confirmed that SASSA maintains a 24-hour cybersecurity operations centre with threat-intelligence capability supported by artificial intelligence systems. These systems analyse multiple datasets to detect suspicious patterns, and the centre is empowered to shut down services or block access immediately when potential threats are identified.
Mr Makondo also addressed concerns relating to identity theft. He reminded Members that, prior to the introduction of biometrics, it was possible for individuals to exploit harvested personal data to fraudulently apply for grants. The biometric system now requires applicants to submit live facial images, which are automatically validated against DHA data. If the facial verification fails, the system blocks access to the application process.
An official began with the Chairperson’s questions on review targets. He reported that SASSA had set an internal target of 420 000 reviews per quarter, which was further broken down into smaller operational targets. He emphasised that, in order to avoid inconveniencing beneficiaries through indiscriminate reviews, SASSA uses complex data-analysis models to identify higher-risk beneficiaries who are most likely to no longer qualify. In the second half of the year, SASSA expanded its methodology from using single-source data to analysing multiple data sources concurrently such as PERSAL records, bank data and other government databases. Beneficiaries who appeared across multiple datasets were prioritised for review.
Turning to the Chairperson’s question about SRD applicants who had never worked yet appeared on UIF or SARS databases, he explained that while he could not comment on how such names entered those databases, the SRD system applies a cross-check using bank-account information. If an applicant appears on a SARS or UIF database but has an empty bank account, SASSA assumes the external data is outdated or incorrect and approves the application. Only when both the external data and bank deposits show income does SASSA decline the grant.
He confirmed that SASSA has an MoU with all institutions that provide data. However, he acknowledged that MoUs are not enforceable mechanisms and do not compel other departments to deliver data consistently. SASSA has requested National Treasury to make data-sharing obligations binding on all relevant departments, given that its statutory duties depend on such data.
In relation to the Chairperson’s request for further data disaggregation, he confirmed that more detailed breakdowns could be provided. He explained that, in the first half of the year, the focus had been largely on income testing. Although checks against multiple government databases had been conducted, most of the suspensions in that period resulted from beneficiaries failing the means test based on declared income. Of the 260 000 notifications issued, approximately 200 000 beneficiaries had presented themselves for review. Suspensions for non-attendance would only appear in the third-quarter report.
He then addressed the disability-grant queries. He explained that the disability-grant assessment is governed by legislation requiring medical practitioners to determine whether a disability prevents an applicant from participating in the labour market. Many applicants seek the disability grant because they meet the income requirement but not the medical eligibility requirement. Illnesses that can be managed with medication do not meet the legislative threshold for disability. He acknowledged, however, that the absence of an income-support mechanism for people aged 18 to 59 created pressure on the disability-grant system, and he noted that the Department’s proposed basic-income support would help close this gap.
On online applications, he acknowledged the Chairperson’s concerns. He noted that the online system lacks immediate, interactive feedback that exists during face-to-face applications. When documents are missing, unclear or incomplete, applicants are notified by email, but many do not see or read the communication. As a result, they often return months later unaware that their application was incomplete. He stated that SASSA’s long-term goal was to shift towards a system similar to the SRD model, in which automated pre-checks reduce the burden on applicants. Proof-of-concept work was underway, though legislative amendments may be required because the SRD grant is governed by different legal provisions from the permanent grants. SASSA also aims to streamline processes at local offices to ensure a more seamless experience.
The official also addressed operational concerns regarding the new income-verification conditions and the use of bank data exchanges. He explained that current processes rely on large-scale batch data exchanges with banks, which take up to a week to complete per means-test category. The system was developed during the SRD grant’s introduction and is not designed for real-time, single-record verification at the point of application. Shifting to such a system would require rebuilding the entire architecture and renegotiating processes with banks. He confirmed that such redesign is possible but would involve extensive work. He noted that where fraudulent bank accounts had been detected, SASSA shares the information with banks’ fraud-investigation units, although SASSA cannot compel banks to act. The entity has since introduced checks to ensure that only fully Financial Intelligence Centre Act (FICA) compliant accounts are used for grant payments. Banks are also phasing out light FICA processes that previously allowed accounts to become active without full verification.
Ms Mvulane concluded by stating that the delegation had responded to the questions as fully as possible. She noted that the Chairperson intended to submit written questions, which would allow the Department to provide more comprehensive explanations, particularly on issues such as SRD income-flow assessments and the distinction between medical and economic considerations in disability-grant eligibility. She acknowledged the gaps raised by Members and confirmed that these would be addressed in the written responses. She then handed over to the Director-General.

The Minister encouraged Members to continue submitting questions proactively, even outside formal sessions, to enable the Department to provide information timeously. She also extended an invitation to Members to attend the upcoming celebration of the 20th anniversary of SASSA, noting that the institution, which was once described as a teenager, was now maturing into a responsible adult, capable of distinguishing right from wrong and implementing necessary corrective measures. She concluded by once again thanking the Chairperson and Members for the opportunity to engage.
The Chairperson thanked the Minister, and senior officials for their contributions and presence. She reflected on the practice articulated by the former Chairperson of the Portfolio Committee, who emphasised that Members served as channels for the concerns raised by communities during their constituency work. She expressed appreciation for the information shared by the Department and its entities, noting that it equipped Members to respond accurately and confidently to the public. She then announced the conclusion of the portion of the meeting and indicated that the Committee would proceed with its internal business. She clarified that internal business sessions were not closed meetings but indicated that attendees who had other commitments and did not see value in remaining for the consideration and adoption of Committee reports were free to depart. She thanked all officials for their contributions to the meeting and wished them well in their ongoing work.
Committee business
The Chairperson informed Members that the Committee had saved time on the agenda, enabling them to continue with constituency responsibilities after the internal session. She proceeded to introduce the first item of internal business: the consideration and adoption of the Committee report on the referral relating to the inquiry into statutory rape, which she handed over to Ms Yolisa Khanye, Committee Content Advisor the portfolio for presentation and facilitation.
Ms Khanye thanked the Chairperson and greeted Members. She explained that she had the full eight-page report but had extracted the Committee’s deliberations and recommendations into a shorter document for ease of presentation. She asked whether she should present only the summary or take the Committee through the full report.
The Chairperson indicated that, as Members had received the full report and raised no objections, Ms Khanye should proceed with the summary.
After confirming that the document was visible, Ms Khanye began with the Committee’s observations and findings. The Committee noted that sexual abuse constituted a national crisis but had not been elevated to a national priority comparable to the COVID-19 response, where daily data was publicly reported. A similar approach was recommended for sexual abuse statistics. The Committee observed that although various departments were undertaking relevant work, efforts were occurring in silos, resulting in weak collaboration with civil society, fragmented referral systems, and insufficient consolidated data on statutory rape, sexually transmitted infections (STIs) in minors, and underage pregnancy. There was also a lack of clarity on budget allocations for child protection services and non-profit organisation (NPO) funding. The Committee identified contradictions between legislative frameworks, including that the age of consent was 16, yet the Choice of Termination of Pregnancy Act allowed minors to terminate a pregnancy without parental involvement. This, combined with the Children’s Act defining anyone under 18 as a child, created confusion and could hinder reporting of sexual abuse. Further concerns included inadequate training of police on sexual abuse cases, a critical shortage of social service professionals, and the persistent unemployment of social work graduates despite a 2019 Cabinet directive to absorb them. The Committee emphasised the need for more social workers in schools. It also highlighted that boys were significantly affected by abuse but often overlooked in interventions. The Committee concluded that the high prevalence of child sexual abuse reflected a failure to fully uphold the best interests of the child.
Ms Khanye then presented the Committee’s recommendations. These included the development of a reporting tool providing daily national statistics on child sexual abuse and gender-based violence (GBV), published across online and broadcast platforms. The Committee recommended that the DSD establish a protocol for integrated pathways between the Departments of Health, Justice, Police, Basic Education, and DHA, particularly on trafficking, through a MoU or agreement. The MoU recommended an integrated, confidential online reporting and tracking system; a strengthened accountability mechanism for stakeholders listed in Section 110 of the Children’s Act, and greater collaboration with academia, civil society, and Chapter 9 institutions to support evidence-based interventions, especially for children in places of safety. The Committee recommended clarity on inconsistencies between the Children’s Act and the Choice of Termination of Pregnancy Act, joint provisions from DSD and the police on follow-up procedures when sexual abuse cases are withdrawn and increased focus on boys as victims. It further recommended engagement with the Department of Communications and Digital Technologies to improve marketing of helplines such as Childline. Given National Treasury’s rejection of DSD’s R15 billion request to employ social workers, the Committee urged DSD to work with the Department of Trade and Industry to explore requiring the mining sector and other industries to fund social service professionals. Premiers should also be engaged to mobilise additional resources. Traditional leaders should be involved in awareness and education efforts, as cultural practices have been identified as barriers to reporting statutory rape. The Committee also recommended joint engagements among relevant portfolio committees and stakeholders on issues arising from public participation processes.
Ms Khanye concluded and invited further guidance from the Chairperson.
The Chairperson thanked Ms Khanye for the presentation and confirmed that, as no Members had comments, the Committee would proceed to adopt the report for transmission to the Portfolio Committee on Women, Youth and Persons with Disabilities by 30 November.
Mr Ramaila adopted the report, and Ms Makgato seconded it.
The Chairperson confirmed that the report on the inquiry into statutory rape and teenage pregnancy was adopted and would be transmitted accordingly. She moved to the last agenda item which was an update on the CDA Summit.
Ms Ntsabo noted that the work reflected in the report had been undertaken by the Portfolio Committee as part of a joint referral. During the CDA Summit on the 26th, a 45-minute session was set aside for all committees involved to adopt the interim main report to meet the 30 November deadline. She also provided an update on summit logistics and requested Mr F Bulawa, Committee Assistant, to brief Members.
Mr Bulawa reported that the travel application awaited managerial approval and was expected that day. Once approved, travel bookings would be finalised and itineraries circulated, likely by Friday. Transport had been arranged from Parliament to the airport, with an evening flight secured to avoid traffic delays. Transfers in Johannesburg were arranged, including a standby vehicle for emergencies. He appealed to Members not to request changes once bookings were issued due to challenges with the current travel agent.
Ms Ntsabo added that on 3 December the Committee would hold a joint meeting with the Select Committee on Social Services, following a speaker’s referral. The Minister of Social Development is required, under the Social Assistance Amendment Bill, to consult Parliament when appointing members to the Independent Appeals Tribunal. The committees will consider the Minister’s recommended candidate list. She stated that she would circulate the Minister’s letter, advertisement, recruitment information, and the recommended names after the meeting.
The Chairperson thanked Ms Ntsabo and summarised that the Committees would meet on 3 December 2025 to consider and endorse the candidates recommended for the Appeals Tribunal. She invited any final comments but, seeing none, thanked Members and adjourned the meeting.
The meeting was adjourned.
