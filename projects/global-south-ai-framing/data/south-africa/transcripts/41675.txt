Date: 01 Oct 2025

Meeting Summary
Video
The Standing Committee on Premier and Constitutional Matters (WCPP) met with Premier Alan Winde and the Western Cape MEC for Police Oversight and Community Safety, and Department for a briefing on the Western Cape Safety Plan (WCSP) 2.0.
The updated Safety Plan, aligned with the Provincial Strategic Plan and Integrated Crime and Violence Prevention Strategy, focuses on multi-stakeholder partnerships, reducing violent crime and substance abuse, addressing root causes of crime through social and economic interventions, strengthening law enforcement capacity, and improving infrastructure and road safety. Key initiatives target high-risk communities, with pilot projects in Mitchells Plain and planned rollouts in Delft, Nyanga, and other areas.
Committee members raised concerns about the lack of reflection on Safety Plan 1.0 outcomes, insufficient targets, accountability of municipal law enforcement and LEAP officers, and the need for broader consultation with affected communities. Questions were also posed on the role of Community Policing Forums (CPFs), the integration of illicit industries into legitimate sectors, the impact of the built environment on crime, and equitable distribution of SAPS resources.
In response, the Premier explained that the plan is still in draft form and incorporates both strategy and implementation elements. Targets will be informed by data and historical trends, with Cabinet approval required. The plan emphasises collaboration with SAPS, municipalities, and communities, particularly through CPFs, and recognises the need for a whole-of-government approach, including social development, economic development, and infrastructure departments. The Safety Council will oversee coordination and progress, while LEAP officers operate as peace officers under the Criminal Procedure Act, with hotspot-focused deployment. The overall safety budget across departments totals R3.9 billion, including allocations for LEAP, safer schools, road safety, K9 units, and parenting programmes.
The Chairperson noted that the draft plan requires refinement and requested that Committee members provide their written feedback within the following week. Public hearings will be considered once the Safety Plan is finalised.
Meeting report
Western Cape Safety Plan: briefing
Acting Head of Department, Mr Hilton Arendse, together with Ms Amanda Dissel, Director: Policy and Research, presented the Western Cape Safety Plan (WCSP) on behalf of the Department of Police Oversight and Community Safety.
The presentation covered the updated Western Cape Safety Plan (WCSP) 2.0, which aligns with the provincial government’s vision of fostering “a government that people trust” and creating a resilient, safe society. Safety was emphasised as a crucial foundation for economic growth, job creation, and community well-being.
Strategic Foundations: WCSP 2.0 is informed by the Provincial Strategic Plan, the Integrated Crime and Violence Prevention Strategy, as well as rural and metropolitan safety priorities, developed through extensive consultations with SAPS, municipalities, civil society, and community structures.
Metro vs Rural Challenges: The metro areas face violent crime, gangsterism, extortion, and gender-based violence, while rural regions also grapple with livestock theft, poaching, land invasions, socio-economic challenges, and undocumented migration.
Objectives:
Promote multi-stakeholder safety partnerships.
Reduce violent crime and substance abuse.
Address root causes of violence through social and economic interventions.
Strengthen law enforcement capacity, responsiveness, and public trust.
Enhance infrastructure and transport safety.
Decrease road fatalities, especially pedestrian and alcohol-related incidents.
Focus Areas:
Integrated Violence Prevention:
Safer schools, youth development, parenting support, and substance abuse prevention.
Safe and Secure Communities and Infrastructure:
Community safety initiatives, improved urban design, surveillance systems, and hotspot policing.
Effective, Responsive, and Trusted Law Enforcement:
Support for municipal law enforcement, LEAP programmes, K9 units, and oversight of SAPS.
Inputs on Draft Plan: Stakeholders recommended a stronger emphasis on road safety, gender-based violence, clearer definitions (e.g. hotspot policing), better data indicators, rural–urban balance, devolution of policing powers, and amendments to provincial legislation.
Governance Structures: Implementation will be supported by provincial, municipal, and community safety forums, as well as specialised committees on issues such as firearms control, anti-gang measures, and extortion prevention.
Next Steps: After stakeholder engagement, the draft plan was revised, integrated with partner feedback, and tabled before Cabinet for approval in September 2025. It has now entered the implementation phase.
Premier’s comments
Premier Alan Winde noted that on the slide indicating that the Department would present to the Standing Committee in October 2025, Police Minister Firoz Cachalia had raised a scheduling concern. The Minister is expected to receive an updated national policy following his provincial visits, which is anticipated within the next month. As a result, the Premier explained that the October briefing date may need to be adjusted to ensure alignment with the forthcoming national plan.
The Police Minister had shown particular interest in the province’s hotspot policing initiative, which the Premier regarded as an encouraging development. The Premier also mentioned that he had recently met with the newly appointed Children’s Commissioner and requested that the Commissioner be included and invited to participate in the Safety Council.
Discussion
Ms B Van Minnen (DA) thanked the presenters and sought clarity on several issues. Referring to the rural vs metro challenges, she requested further explanation on the link between climate change and crime, noting that this could also affect rural communities. On firearms, she asked if the plan addresses conviction rates, citing a recent Bellville case where individuals found with illegal firearms were released. She also raised concern about smuggling routes, from both marine areas and across land borders such as from Namibia, asking if these have been factored into the plan. On the objectives of the Safety Plan, she asked if addressing the root causes of violence through social and economic interventions reflects a whole-of-government approach, meaning all departments and entities should align their planning and budgets accordingly.
Mr B Herron (GOOD) noted that while this is Safety Plan 2.0, the document fails to acknowledge the original Safety Plan 1.0, its outcomes, successes, or shortcomings. He argued that this omission weakens the justification for introducing a revised plan. He reminded the Committee that the first plan (2019–2030) aimed to reduce the murder rate as a key indicator of success and that this plan should therefore begin with a reflection on progress and lessons learned. His critique was not of the concept of a safety plan itself, but of the lack of continuity and accountability. Also while the new plan identifies 15 indicators on page 11, no targets have been set. He expressed concern that infrastructure interventions have been narrowed to CCTV and lighting, excluding broader urban design and housing considerations that were integral to the first plan.
Mr Herron urged that the plan must address spatial inequality and apartheid-era settlement patterns that continue to shape violence and exclusion. He stressed the importance of involving the Infrastructure Department and municipalities in redesigning unsafe environments. On accountability concerns for law enforcement, he said municipal law enforcement officers, who play a major implementation role, are not directly accountable to the Provincial Minister of Safety, as they fall outside the national Policing Act.
Mr P Johnson (DA) supported the plan’s objectives but stressed that its success depends on a whole-of-society approach involving all spheres of government and the public. He asked if communities directly affected by crime had been consulted in developing the plan and whether their input shaped the current draft.
Mr K Sayed (ANC) echoed comments on community participation, asking when these consultations took place and how the legislature could play a stronger oversight and facilitation role in amplifying community voices. He noted that the plan appears heavily enforcement-driven and asked how issues of accountability, particularly those linked to the memorandum of understanding between the three spheres of government, were being managed.
Mr Sayed questioned how the plan addresses extortion and gang-related crime, referencing past arrests of high-profile figures like Nafiz Modack. He sought clarity on how the province would work with SAPS to ensure sustained action against gang leaders. He asked how prevention and development measure, such as trauma counselling, substance abuse treatment, and youth employment, would be adequately resourced and aligned with the national Medium-Term Strategic Framework’s prevention-first approach. Lastly, he queried if the absence of a ring-fenced budget means each department is expected to align its existing budget to support the plan’s objectives.
Mr M Ngqentsu (ANC) asked if the document before the Committee constitutes a plan or a strategy, noting its restrictive and conceptual nature. He pointed out inconsistencies, such as the lack of dated targets and incomplete responses to key questions within the document. The plan did not sufficiently acknowledge previous reviews or outcomes from earlier safety initiatives. The Committee should be presented with evidence of progress, challenges, and lessons learned. Highlighting that crime in Cape Town has reached crisis levels, he criticised the plan for failing to reflect this urgency or provide a credible short-term response.
On firearms, he noted that the plan merely calls for their destruction without addressing the broader proliferation problem. He also found the plan weak on environmental design considerations and questioned the effectiveness of CCTV systems, asking how many are operational and have aided investigations. He cautioned that the plan may be overambitious in its training commitments, referencing earlier unfulfilled promises to train administrative staff. Finally, he raised concerns about the accountability of law enforcement units such as LEAP officers and asked how the plan intends to strengthen oversight of these entities.
Mr M Booysen (DA) expressed concern about the political dynamics influencing the implementation of the Safety Plan. He noted that it would be a mistake not to reflect on what has been learned since the first plan was introduced and to consider current developments, including those arising from the Madlanga Commission. He acknowledged that both Safety Plan 1.0 and 2.0 have yielded successes that should be recognised. However, he cautioned that resistance to the devolution of policing powers often stems from vested interests, such as politically connected individuals, criminal enterprises, and extortion networks, that stand to lose from stronger provincial control.
Mr Booysen urged politicians across party lines to engage constructively, noting that all are victims of crime, not beneficiaries. He called for a clear, unified vision for making the Western Cape a model province for effective safety reform. Referring to past briefings, he noted that LEAP officers and the boots-on-the-ground approach have contributed significantly to crime reduction, as confirmed by the Provincial Commissioner. He emphasised the need to redirect efforts towards addressing gaps identified in the first plan and to find practical ways to overcome institutional and political obstacles highlighted by the Madlanga Commission.
Ms D Stephens (PA) commended the Department for producing a promising document but emphasised the need for tangible results. She asked how the Safety Plan translates on paper into safer communities, especially in areas still plagued by gang violence. Were the 100 newly deployed law enforcement officers under the plan adequately trained and equipped to handle extortion and violent crime? She also requested clarity on the training provided to LEAP officers and how it differs from SAPS training.
The Chairperson thanked members for their contributions and noted that while there may be differences in approach, all members share a common goal, ensuring effective cooperation across all levels of government and creating equal opportunities for all residents of the province. Although he was not part of the legislature during the implementation of Safety Plan 1.0, the new framework appears to be a step forward toward a safer and more equitable Western Cape. However, the success of the plan depends on execution and clarity about who is responsible for implementation at each level.
The Chairperson referred to a previous strategic plan presented by the Office of the Premier, which adopted a holistic life-cycle approach to improving citizens’ lives through interdepartmental collaboration beyond law enforcement alone. He invited the Premier to elaborate on how this integrated, multi-departmental approach would be incorporated into the work of the Cabinet and the Provincial Safety Council.
The Chairperson remarked that organised crime operates as an integrated criminal economy, often with international links, and cautioned against assuming that such crimes are confined to urban areas, as rural regions are also affected through production, trafficking, and smuggling routes. He asked how institutions such as SARS financial investigation unit, the Financial Crimes Unit, and the Asset Forfeiture Unit fit into the Safety Plan’s framework.
He observed that the Safety Plan appears to be a strategic framework informed by previous planning and linked to government performance assessments. He requested the Premier and Department to outline the next phase of the Plan’s development, particularly in broadening partnerships, community participation, and the roll-out of initiatives within local communities.
Premier & Department r
esponses
Premier Alan Winde noted that both the presentation and document remain in draft form. He emphasised that inputs and recommendations from the Committee and political parties are welcome. As mentioned earlier, Minister Cachalia’s forthcoming input on the updated national safety policy may allow additional time before the renewal of the Western Cape Safety Plan.
Mr Hilton Arendse outlined how the Department envisions the plan being implemented across communities, noting that it includes both short- and long-term initiatives. The plan primarily targets high-risk communities, where safety and development structures or programmes will be established within each of the 21 identified areas. These interventions will be tailored to the specific dynamics and challenges of each community. He explained that while some areas may require more interventions than others, the focus is on identifying and addressing the most critical high-risk zones. Using Mitchells Plain as an example, he noted that although it is a large area, Tafelsig and Beacon Valley have been identified as key zones requiring intensified focus. The Premier’s “overlay project” which integrates multiple services in these areas for maximum impact, was referenced as a guiding approach.
Mr Arendse highlighted that the Department is co-creating solutions with communities to ensure that interventions reflect local realities. He clarified that the plan’s timelines are being developed collaboratively with communities rather than predetermined. Responding to concerns that the plan might be overly focused on law enforcement, he explained that it is structured around three core pillars: integrated violence prevention, which focuses on individuals and substance abuse; safe and secure communities, which emphasises infrastructure and environmental safety; and effective law enforcement, complemented by job creation and youth development initiatives to provide alternatives to gangsterism. Pilot projects embodying this holistic approach are already underway in Mitchells Plain.
On infrastructure, Premier Winde reiterated that the plan is a draft and will be revised to incorporate stakeholder feedback. He agreed that reflecting on the previous safety plan, highlighting its successes, gaps, and lessons learned, would be beneficial. He also cautioned against producing an excessively long document, stressing the importance of a targeted and practical plan. The Department intends to review current crime statistics, especially in light of Cape Town’s ongoing safety challenges, and make any necessary amendments.
Mr Arendse clarified that the plan functions as a hybrid document, combining elements of both strategy and implementation. It includes tables outlining specific targets, timelines, and accountability measures. The Department is currently reviewing indicators to ensure alignment with outcomes and targets, although final approval of these targets will rest with the Provincial Cabinet.
Discussing the built environment, he cautioned against setting unrealistic commitments but underscored the importance of ensuring that infrastructure projects in identified communities also guide future development planning.
On accountability and oversight, Mr Arendse stated that the City of Cape Town is accountable to the Province for the Metro Police and the Law Enforcement Advancement Plan (LEAP). LEAP deployments are reviewed regularly through management meetings, and redeployments require approval from either the Premier or the Minister. Collaboration between the City, SAPS and the Province remains strong, especially on hotspot policing. Monthly meetings with key stakeholders and organisations are held to assess progress and address challenges, including those linked to the SAPS limited technological monitoring capacity.
Mr Arendse noted that there are now over 4,000 surveillance cameras across the City, SAPS and the Western Cape Government. Regular monitoring ensures functionality, and under the collaboration agreement, camera feeds are now shared between SAPS and the City to aid investigations and improve conviction rates. The Department is also exploring the integration of artificial intelligence technologies to identify suspicious behaviour and firearms more effectively.
He highlighted the growing concern of firearm-related offences and organised crime, noting that organised criminal networks are expanding beyond Cape Town into nearby municipalities, which are increasingly affected by the spillover of gangsterism.
On departmental accountability, Mr Arendse explained that the Provincial Safety Coordination Centre was established to strengthen coordination across departments and consolidate reporting on various safety initiatives. On the role of the Asset Forfeiture Unit, he cited an example from the Overberg region, where confiscated boats from criminal entities were donated to local municipalities for community use.
Mr Arendse concluded by welcoming the continued involvement of the Provincial Legislature in facilitating coordination between departments and stakeholders, noting that this collaboration would strengthen the Western Cape Community Safety Plan and enhance its impact across the province.
Provincial Minister of Police Oversight and Community Safety, Ms Anroux Marais, welcomed members’ input, reaffirming that the ultimate goal is to build safer and more resilient communities. She described the Mitchells Plain pilot project as deliberately non-political, explaining that politicians were excluded to prevent politicisation of safety initiatives. The project has involved extensive community engagement, including NGOs, sport federations, and all relevant provincial departments, with an emphasis on listening to residents’ lived experiences.
Director-General Harry Malila thanked members for their contributions and reiterated that the Western Cape Safety Plan remains a draft. He noted that it was unusual for a draft plan to be brought to Parliament before Cabinet endorsement, but the Department wanted to obtain parliamentary and community input early. All comments received will inform the refinement of Safety Plan 2.0, which will follow Cabinet approval.
He stressed that the plan represents a whole-of-society approach to crime prevention, integrating communities, other departments, and the Provincial Legislature. The new version will place stronger emphasis on violence prevention, with departments such as Economic Development and Social Development playing roles in addressing underlying socio-economic drivers of crime. The plan aligns with the Provincial Strategic Plan and other frameworks, particularly through youth employment initiatives.
Premier Winde contextualised the discussion within the broader strategic planning process at the start of the new government term. Reflecting on the original Western Cape Safety Plan, he explained that it was developed six years ago as a response to rising crime levels and limitations in policing powers. While the province could not afford the 3,000 officers originally envisioned, 1,200 LEAP officers were deployed, and a review of their achievements is now necessary. The crime environment has evolved with extortion and the growing number of gangs not as prevalent when the original plan was adopted.
The Premier Winde reaffirmed his call for a revised policing model linked to devolution, acknowledging the need for improved coordination within the province’s constitutional mandate. He highlighted that LEAP officers undergo rigorous training, 8 months of instruction and 10 months of field experience, focused on hotspot deployment. Although not police officers under the Police Act, they operate as peace officers under section 334 of the Criminal Procedure Act, meaning they can make arrests but must hand over suspects to SAPS for processing.
The Premier noted the challenges with the Memorandum of Understanding (MOU) with SAPS, expressing concern over delays in implementation. The province looks forward to working with Minister Cachalia to strengthen the MOU and accelerate collaborative action.
On firearms, Premier Winde reiterated that the conviction rate for firearm-related offences remains below 5%, making it a top priority for engagement with national counterparts. He also stressed the importance of improving administrative support in police stations and integrating CCTV technologies into investigations.
He concluded that the Safety Council, which focuses on growth, safety, education, health, and good governance, will oversee the plan’s progress once approved. The Council serves as a platform for coordination across government and society to tackle crime as an obstacle to job creation, education, and quality of life. Ultimately, the Western Cape Safety Plan 2.0 will be a refined, collaborative framework that directly reports to the provincial legislature and aligns with the province’s broader vision of safety and development.
Follow up questions
Ms Van Minnen referred to the inputs received from SAPS and, in light of recent media reports about risks within the Western Cape, asked if SAPS itself might be at risk given the current circumstances being reported in the news.
Mr Sayed noted that his earlier question on the budget had not yet been addressed. He also raised concern about illicit industries transitioning into legitimate sectors, asking how the Safety Plan intends to address this, particularly in the fishing industry and the oceans economy. He queried how the “whole-of-government” approach applies in this context, specifically what role the Department of Forestry, Fisheries and the Environment plays and how the provincial government can assist that department in tackling these challenges.
Mr Sayed asked what role Community Policing Forums (CPFs) have in the plan to ensure that it is rooted in community participation and ownership. He suggested that it might be appropriate for the Committee to adopt a resolution to hold a public hearing on the Safety Plan to facilitate broader engagement. This could be discussed further when the Committee considers its resolutions.
Mr Herron thanked the Department for its responses. On the Mitchells Plain overlays, he noted that these are not reflected in the theory of change and, if they are part of the plan, they should be explicitly included, particularly on page 10 of the document. He also referred to the process of setting targets, recalling that in the previous Safety Plan, Cabinet had adopted a goal to reduce the murder rate by 50% over ten years. He expressed concern that this appeared to be an arbitrary figure, asking how such targets are determined.
Mr Herron also cautioned against downplaying the importance of the built environment, arguing that the plan must directly address the spatial and socio-economic drivers of violent crime. Failure to do so would undermine the plan’s effectiveness, as the province’s spatial history continues to shape patterns of violence.
On accountability, Mr Herron reiterated his legal concerns about the status of law enforcement officers, noting that the South African Police Service Act does not create such a category. This is why the metropolitan police plan contains no reference to law enforcement as a distinct branch under the City of Cape Town’s Safety and Security Department. His concerns were confirmed when he approached the Minister of Justice and Constitutional Development, who stated that, legally, “law enforcement officers” do not exist.
Mr Herron said in May 2022 the Police Minister clarified that when Section 334 of the Criminal Procedure Act granted peace officer powers to municipal law enforcement officials, it was referring specifically to traffic officers and traffic wardens. Since that response, unless there has been a legal amendment, this remains a serious issue. He cautioned that partnerships with entities potentially acting outside the scope of the law must be rectified. He also referred to a statement in the Plan about addressing the inequitable distribution of SAPS resources and requested the source or data supporting that claim.
Mr Ngqentsu commented that the Premier’s remarks on peace officers and law enforcement should be situated within the broader debate on whether South Africa should establish a single police service under the command of the National Commissioner. He remarked that a 61-page document cannot comprehensively be described as a “plan,” suggesting that it lacks sufficient detail. On cooperation agreements, he referenced the recent launch of hundreds of metro police officers in the province, during which statements were made about the potential deployment of neighbourhood policing, yet this does not appear in the draft Safety Plan. He acknowledged that the plan is still in draft form but proposed that the process should include public participation to ensure broader input from communities before finalisation.
Premier & Department r
esponses
Addressing whether Western Cape SAPS is at risk, Mr Arendse replied that while SAPS faces its own challenges, the department engages with them on a day-to-day basis. “Risk” depends on the definition used, but in terms of collaboration, the department does not view engagement with SAPS as a risk. He did, however, identify the lack of adequate resources as a potential risk, linking this to the issue of inequitable allocation of SAPS resources. He emphasised the importance of trust in the partners they work with while acknowledging national-level risks.
Mr Arendse explained that no separate budget has been allocated yet, as the Safety Plan is intended to be integrated into the work of the relevant departments. On illicit industries, he noted that the plan currently focuses on community safety and recognises how illicit industries feed into gangsterism, but these issues are not formally incorporated into the document at this stage and will require further consideration.
On the role of Community Policing Forums, Mr Arendse stated that engagement occurs at district level with SAPS, municipalities, and CPFs involved. At the community level, the pilot in Mitchells Plain, with future rollouts planned for Delft, Nyanga, and other areas, will see CPFs playing a strong role in implementing community-level safety and development initiatives. On the inclusion of job creation, he acknowledged that the document should address this area.
On targets, Mr Arendse noted that these will be informed by data and historical trends, rather than being arbitrarily set by politicians. Politicians will ultimately approve targets after being presented with evidence and alternatives. On the built environment and its role in driving crime, he acknowledged that further consultation with provincial stakeholders is needed and that not every detail must appear in the Safety Plan.
Addressing the size of the plan, Mr Arendse explained that while the current document is 60 pages, it is intended as an implementation plan rather than a strategy, which is captured in the broader Provincial Strategic Plan. The Safety Plan seeks to balance incorporating all stakeholder inputs with maintaining focus and avoiding unnecessary detail.
On firearms, he clarified that the Plan cannot commit to ending illegal firearms entirely within a set timeframe, although it can set goals for reduction.
Premier Winde provided clarification on the budget, noting that the overall safety budget across all departments is R3.9 billion, with R350 million allocated to LEAP for 2024/25, R91 million for safer schools, R148 million for road safety management, and an outstanding allocation required for K9 units. The social services budget includes R45 million for parenting programmes. Data for planning comes from SAPS and the Premier’s Policy Unit and Data Team, including the police-to-population ratio, which shows severe under-resourcing in areas such as Delft (1 officer per 729 people compared to 1:429 provincially).
On setting targets, Premier Winde acknowledged the difficulty, noting that while the ultimate goal is zero murders, practical targets such as a 50% reduction in the murder rate provide achievable, measurable goals.
On Mr Ngqentsu’s proposal for a single national police service, Winde emphasised that the Safety Plan exists because centralisation has not addressed local challenges effectively. Transferring all local traffic officers, law enforcement officers, and security personnel to national command would not solve safety issues. He stressed the importance of devolving policing powers and taking a localised approach to crime prevention.
The Chairperson thanked Premier Winde for his input, noting that the Safety Plan is a work in progress and that the Committee will have opportunities to provide input to Cabinet following the alignment of deadlines after the 25th. He expressed appreciation to Committee members for their deliberations and to the department for its presentation and responses.
Resolutions
The Chairperson noted that the draft Safety Plan requires improvement and acknowledged that there is room for significant debate, for example, between proponents of devolution versus a single police service, which is a matter for the respective political parties.
The Chairperson stated that the effectiveness of public participation in the plan to date could also be debated. He proposed a resolution that Committee members submit their inputs on the draft plan within the month of October.
On suggestions for a public hearing, the Chairperson expressed caution since the plan is still in draft form and spans multiple committee components. He referenced the upcoming Community Safety Act, which includes provisions for public hearings, noting that the Safety Plan is linked to this Act and its eventual amendments. He suggested that public hearings could be considered once the final Safety Plan is available, but in the interim, inputs should be facilitated on the draft plan.
Mr Sayed recommended that a full public hearing be conducted once the plan is finalised. In the meantime, he proposed a hybrid approach where organisations and entities could submit inputs which would then inform Committee oversight.
The Chairperson clarified that once the Safety Plan is finalised, it will return to the Committee, at which point inputs from organisations will be invited.
Mr Herron asked if the inputs provided during the current meeting would be formally recorded and submitted as part of the inputs.
The Committee secretary suggested that, due to the volume of responses, members streamline their inputs and email them to the secretary.
Mr Booysen recommended that these inputs be included in the Committee’s submissions.
The Chairperson proposed a resolution to formally request feedback based on this meeting.
He requested that Mr Herron put his feedback in writing and that Committee members provide their responses within the following week.
The minutes of the Committee Meeting of 26 September 2025 were adopted and the meeting adjourned.
