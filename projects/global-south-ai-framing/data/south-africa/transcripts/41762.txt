WC Annual Reports 2024/25
The Public Accounts Committee met to consider the Department of the Premier's 2024/25 Annual Report. The Department achieved a clean audit outcome, spending 98.5% of its R2.1 billion budget and achieving 95.45% of its performance indicators. No fruitless, wasteful, or irregular expenditure was recorded.
The Department reported that its governance and internal control systems remained stable. Collaboration with the Auditor-General and internal audit structures continued to strengthen financial and performance oversight across the provincial administration. Governance was described as an established discipline anchored in ethics, fiscal prudence, and compliance with the Public Finance Management Act.
Members commended the Department for its audit performance and interrogated the reported R30.6 million underspending. The Department stated that the variance resulted mainly from recruitment delays in the energy portfolio and minor timing differences within Business Intelligence and Corporate Services. The 1.5% underspend fell within National Treasury’s tolerance threshold and did not affect service delivery.
Members questioned the alignment of performance indicators with measurable outcomes and the Department’s approach to transversal risk management. The Department stated that its indicators were reviewed annually and that risk mitigation was coordinated through the Governance, Risk and Compliance Committee. Risks reported to the provincial Cabinet included fiscal pressures, climate and energy security, and ICT safeguards.
The Committee examined the Department's interaction with directives from the Department of Public Service and Administration. The Department had raised concerns about several directives that conflicted with its digitised e-recruitment system. It had requested that the national Department assess the provincial system before enforcing manual processes. The Department reported that the online platform accounted for the vast majority of applications and was accessible across the province.
Questions on irregular expenditure under assessment were addressed. The Department confirmed that twelve cases, totalling R1.47 million, were under investigation. These involved instances where prior approval for procurement had not been obtained or where contracts had lapsed before renewal. Once verified, the matters would be finalised through consequence-management procedures.
Members raised concern over staff retention and the concentration of expenditure on personnel within Corporate Assurance and Legal Services. The Department attributed this to high staff turnover and delays in filling technical posts. It outlined plans to accelerate recruitment, strengthen organisational culture, and deploy technology to improve efficiency.
The Committee acknowledged the Department’s strong governance record and fiscal management. It urged that risk management, staff retention, and consequence-management processes remain prioritised.
Meeting report
Chairperson’s Opening Remarks
The Chairperson opened the meeting by thanking Members and departmental officials for their introductions. He reminded Members that before the Premier and the Department provided their introductory remarks, each Member would be allowed two questions, an initial question and a follow-up. He explained that he would take three Members at a time to allow the Department sufficient time to respond and requested that Members provide page numbers when asking questions, as this would make the discussion easier to follow.
The meeting began with Member introductions. Mr F Christians (ACDP) introduced himself as the Chairperson of the Committee, followed by Mr M Booysen (DA), Mr N Masipa (DA), Mr T Walters (DA), Mr P Johnson (DA), Mr G Bosman (DA), and Mr L van Wyk (DA).
This was followed by departmental introductions, including Mr Alan Winde, Premier of the Western Cape, and other officials.
Introductory remarks by the Premier
Premier Winde thanked the Chairperson and said it was always a pleasure to appear before SCOPA, particularly when the Department had achieved the kind of audit outcome it had. He said the Department of the Premier played a critical role in promoting good governance, leading from the front not only in its own financial management and audit outcomes, but also across the provincial administration.
He recognised the contribution of Director-General Dr Harry Malila and the Department’s collaboration with the Auditor-General of South Africa (AGSA). He said commitments made to the AGSA extended across both local and provincial government spheres. He expressed pride in the fact that all provincial departments and entities had achieved unqualified audits, noting that such success required consistent effort.
Premier Winde added that good governance must become a habit—rooted in ethics, discipline, and doing things the right way—so that departments could focus on service delivery. He highlighted the importance of internal audit functions and the need to periodically revisit any issues that might arise. He commended the Department’s team for exemplifying ethical conduct and accountability, particularly in areas of fruitless, wasteful, and unauthorised expenditure, where the Department continued to lead by example. He concluded that the Department’s performance demonstrated the kind of leadership expected if government as a whole was to maintain high governance standards.
Dr Harry Malila, Director-General, DotP, thanked the Chairperson, the Members, and the Department’s team, both in person and online. He said the Department had earlier appeared before the Standing Committee and had presented his Accounting Officer’s report, as reflected on pages 8 to 15 of the annual report.
He was pleased to report that the Department had no issues under the revenue component. Under expenditure, it had spent 98.5% of its R2.1 billion budget, while achieving 95.45% of its performance indicators. There were no confirmed cases of fruitless, wasteful, or irregular expenditure during the year, and governance remained sound.
He reaffirmed the Premier’s statement that the Department’s role extended beyond its own vote to encompass leadership, guidance, and enabling functions across the provincial government system. This included performance management, financial and non-financial oversight, and governance engagement with various government leaders.
Dr Malila said the Department continued to strengthen its systems, particularly within supply chain management, with compliance and risk issues now minimal. The Governance Committee remained active, as reflected in both internal audit and audit committee reports. The Department engaged constructively with the Auditor-General, not only for its own purposes but in support of good governance across the provincial administration.
He expressed satisfaction with the Department’s performance in 2024/25, noting that the clean audit outcome reflected the quality of its financials and internal controls. The Department would continue to focus on achieving impact through its expenditure and supporting conformance with governance regulations. While much work remained for the next four years, he was confident that the Department was well-positioned to sustain its strong performance and governance record.
Discussion
The Chairperson thanked Premier Winde and Dr Harry Malila, DG, DoTP, for their introductory remarks and invited Members to raise questions on Section C: Governance. He reminded Members to refer to the relevant page numbers when raising questions. He indicated that questions would be taken in groups of three to allow sufficient time for departmental responses.
Mr M Booysen (DA) commended the Department on its strong audit performance, noting that such outcomes were not easily achieved. Referring to page 134 of the Annual Report, he drew attention to the recorded underspending and asked whether it resulted from over-projection or other factors. He contrasted this with instances in other provinces where significant funds were unaccounted for, saying that while all amounts in this case were accounted for, the underspending still required explanation.
Dr Malila said the Department’s total budget for the year was slightly over R2 billion, with an overall underspend of R30.6 million. He said the largest portion of the underspending occurred in Programme 2, where R14 million had not been utilised, mainly due to delays in recruitment within the energy portfolio. He explained that the Department had faced difficulties attracting suitably qualified specialists, as only a small number of candidates possessed the required technical expertise.
He added that there had been minor underspending of R3.6 million in the Business Intelligence environment and a smaller amount within Corporate Services. These variances were primarily linked to timing differences in filling vacancies, as it was not possible to predict staff movements with complete accuracy. He assured the Committee that the underspending had no impact on performance delivery, with all core targets achieved and financial management remaining sound.
Premier Winde said the Department’s financial discipline should be viewed as a positive example of effective governance. He said the Western Cape Government maintained strict controls over expenditure throughout the financial year, avoiding end-of-year spending sprees and accruals that distorted fiscal integrity. He said the province’s approach was based on careful planning, daily oversight of payments, and a commitment to ethical management.
He noted that while some may regard underspending as undesirable, the Department viewed it as evidence of prudence and accuracy rather than inefficiency. He described the outcome as “hitting the bullseye,” highlighting that financial management was not about exhausting the budget but about ensuring that every rand was spent purposefully and responsibly.
Dr Malila said the Department’s underspending of 1.5% for the financial year was well within acceptable limits and reflected sound financial management. He noted that National Treasury regarded under-expenditure levels of up to 2% as an appropriate benchmark, as this allowed sufficient flexibility for financial systems to operate effectively.
He explained that any expenditure above this threshold could risk unauthorised spending, particularly when salary or project-related payments were processed at the financial year-end. He said the Department’s ability to remain below this benchmark demonstrated a high level of compliance and careful budget management.
He added that two components of the underspending had already been submitted for rollover approval—one relating to the Microsoft licensing allocation and another to funds earmarked by the Provincial Parliament for the Commission for Children.
Dr Malila said he was satisfied with the Department’s overall financial performance, noting that the continuous monitoring of expenditure at both monthly and annual levels had contributed to the strong fiscal discipline demonstrated throughout the year.
Ms P Lekker (ANC) raised a question regarding the Department’s performance indicators and their alignment with measurable public value. She asked whether the Department had conducted any assessments or surveys to determine whether the indicators were improving and demonstrating responsiveness to citizens of the Western Cape.
The Chairperson reminded Ms Lekker to provide the relevant page numbers in the Annual Report to enable the Department to respond accurately, noting that there were multiple indicators across several pages.
Ms Lekker explained that her question was prompted by information received during an earlier engagement with the Auditor-General of South Africa (AGSA) and the internal audit team. She noted that 32 actions had reportedly been achieved against performance indicators and sought clarification on progress and alignment.
The Chairperson emphasised that the Department had not been present at the earlier meeting and required precise references to the indicators and relevant pages to respond appropriately. He directed Ms Lekker to refer to pages 68 and 69 of the Annual Report rather than slides, noting that these pages contained the relevant performance information.
Ms Lekker confirmed that her question referred to the indicators highlighted on pages 68 and 69, noting that 75% of the targets had been achieved while 25% remained outstanding, as reflected in the corresponding colour-coded references. She indicated that her query was linked to both the report and the accompanying slides previously shared with the Committee.
The Chairperson acknowledged that Ms Lekker’s clarification was helpful, as it allowed the Department to follow the Committee’s discussion without the briefing notes. He instructed her to continue referencing the Annual Report for clarity.
Ms Lekker further indicated that she would return to the specific pages to provide precise references before posing her remaining question, which concerned the professionalisation of the Department and the expected timeline for alignment.
Mr G Bosman (DA) noted that he would wait for Ms Lekker to identify the relevant page numbers before raising his own questions. He stated that his question pertained to pages 112 to 111 (sic) of the Annual Report.
The Chairperson reminded Mr Bosman that the Committee was focused on Section C: Governance, covering pages 116 to 147, and that questions should be confined to this section before moving to other areas.
Mr Bosman acknowledged this but indicated he would hold his question for a later point in the meeting.
Mr B Petrus (PA) referred to page 121 and asked for clarification on how the Department was addressing key risks and whether there had been improvements in mitigating these risks.
The Chairperson sought further clarification on which specific risks were being referenced to ensure an accurate response from the Department.
Dr Malila reported that no further questions had been received from Ms Lekker as she had not provided page references for her earlier queries. He indicated that the Department would await her clarification before responding to those matters.
He said that one outstanding question had been submitted by Mr Petrus relating to pages 121 and 122 of the Annual Report. Dr Malila explained that page 121 outlined the Department’s Governance, Risk and Compliance Committee (GRC) activities, while page 120 listed the members of the governance structures, including program managers who participate in all committee discussions.
Dr Malila detailed that the Department identified and addressed various risks throughout the year, as guided by the provincial risk plan. These risks, contained within the corporate governance framework, included ICT security, employee well-being, safeguarding of information, and overall governance improvement across the Western Cape Government. He further noted the Department’s oversight of value for ICT expenditure and the transition from Broadband 1.1.0 to Broadband 2.0, which had been mitigated through contract awards.
He explained that pages 120 to 122 set out the transversal risks affecting the Western Cape Government, presented quarterly to Cabinet. These risks covered fiscal and economic matters, climate-related issues, energy challenges, trade uncertainties, and tariff considerations. Dr Malila reported that mitigation measures had been implemented and that the Department actively managed these risks through its Governance Committee, Enterprise Risk Management policy, and engagement with program managers.
Dr Malila concluded that the Department was satisfied with its risk management processes and was confident that all identified risks were being effectively monitored and addressed.
Mr T Walters (DA) raised a question related to Ms Lekker’s earlier query, referring to page 139 of the Annual Report. He noted that the page provided a summary of work conducted by the Audit Implementation Forum (AIF) during the reporting period. He highlighted that 32 actions were scheduled for implementation, all of which had been completed, which he described as commendable.
Mr Walters sought clarification on the comparative implementation rate from the previous year, which was indicated at 60%, to ensure understanding of the progress made. He further queried two common themes identified during engagements conducted within the Department of the Premier. He noted that one recurring observation was that certain activities were “not effectively executed,” suggesting potential transversal challenges across the Department.
He also asked for clarification on the reliability of the data sources referenced in the report. He noted that the Premier’s Department aimed to be data-driven and empirically oriented, and he requested further detail on any issues with data accuracy, calculation methods, or comparative analyses that had been highlighted on page 139.
Risk management and employee well-being
Mr G Bosman (DA) raised questions on risk management and strategic alignment, referencing pages 118 to 122 and pages 133 to 134 of the Annual Report. He noted that the Governance Committee (GovCom) had been established to assist the accounting officer but had only convened three times during the reporting period. He asked whether this was fewer meetings than the previous year and whether the reduced number of meetings had affected the identification and management of key risks, particularly regarding ICT security infrastructure within the Western Cape Government.
Mr Bosman further queried how the report’s observation of increased strain on employee well-being was being managed, and how it aligned with the broader Western Cape Government wellness strategy. He highlighted that, while the Department of the Premier was one of the smaller provincial departments, it played a significant coordinating role across government. He emphasised that effective risk management relied not only on computer systems but also on personnel capacity and well-being, suggesting that these considerations may need to be more prominently reflected in GovCom deliberations.
Dr Malila explained that the Department was aware of several directives issued by the Department of Public Service and Administration (DPSA). He said some of these proposals had been raised with the Auditor-General during the audit process, as the Department believed they were inefficient.
As an example, he referred to the directive requiring that all job applications be printed and reviewed in hard copy by selection committees. He pointed out that this contradicted government’s move to an e-recruitment system, which had already been successfully implemented, audited, and proven effective. The Department had therefore written to the DPSA, requesting that it audit the provincial system to determine whether any inefficiencies existed before requiring a reversion to manual processes.
He said there were three such directives currently under discussion with the DPSA, including matters related to professionalisation and recruitment practices. The Department had submitted written correspondence and was awaiting a formal response. While the Department supported all directives intended to improve governance, it also assessed whether they were practical and compatible with innovation and efficiency. Directives that strengthened systems were implemented, but those that would reverse progress were challenged, and alternative approaches were proposed. The Department had also shared its best practices with the DPSA to contribute to system-wide improvement.
These matters had been recorded in the management report issued by the Auditor-General, and the Department had provided appropriate management responses. Dr Malila emphasised the Department’s commitment to innovation and automation, commending Ms Louise Esterhuyse, Chief Director: People Management Practices, and her team for the progress made in digitising internal systems.
On the issue of employee well-being, he said this continued to affect staff morale and performance. The matter was regularly discussed at Provincial Top Management (PTM) meetings, including one held the previous day, where Ms Esterhuyse and her team had presented analytics on work-related stressors and their impact on mental health. The data showed that workplace pressures were contributing to mental health challenges, which in turn affected productivity and attendance. Similar patterns had been observed in the health and education sectors. Consequently, the Western Cape Government had elevated employee well-being as a transversal priority across departments.
Regarding internal audit findings and data quality, he confirmed that corrective actions had already been implemented. The key issue identified by internal audit related to the reliability of data sources used in reporting. The Department had since strengthened its data governance practices to ensure that information was credible, verifiable, and able to withstand external scrutiny. He stressed that data accuracy was vital for effective service delivery, as reliable data enabled the Department to assess and improve its performance. The Data Governance Framework had been approved by the Provincial Cabinet, formalising these measures.
On governance structures, he explained that while the Department usually held quarterly governance meetings, the previous year’s schedule had been adjusted due to the restructuring of audit committees. The committees had been aligned with the province’s four strategic priorities, with each focusing on a specific area such as economic growth or social development. This restructuring aimed to shift audit committees beyond compliance toward assessing the impact and effectiveness of programmes.
The three governance meetings held during the transition period were focused on alignment and did not compromise the quality of oversight. The audit committee reports reflected no major concerns. The Department planned to resume its normal quarterly governance cycle going forward.
He concluded by inviting Ms Esterhuyse to add to his comments regarding the directives issued by the DPSA.
Ms Louise Esterhuyse, Chief Director: People Management Practices, reported that the Department had received several new directives from the Department of Public Service and Administration (DPSA).
She said the first directive concerned the composition of selection committees, which the DPSA indicated should consist of four members instead of three. However, the Public Service Regulations still prescribe three members. The Department had written to the DPSA seeking clarification on this discrepancy but had, in the interim, implemented four-member selection committees in line with the directive.
The second directive related to pre-entry integrity assessments. The DPSA had informed the Department that it would begin providing integrity assessments for Senior Management Service (SMS) members from 1 April of the following year. In the meantime, the Department had included integrity-related questions in its interview processes and was in the process of procuring an integrity assessment tool for employees on salary levels 9 to 12 through the provincial assessment centre.
She further explained that the DPSA had directed that departments conduct both manual and online job application processes. The Western Cape Government had, however, been operating an e-recruitment system for salary levels five and above for more than ten years, while maintaining a manual system only for salary levels 1 to 4. The Department was therefore not willing to revert to manual applications for higher levels and had drafted a letter from the Director-General to the DPSA requesting it to indicate any identified problems with the current e-recruitment system.
The final directive concerned departmental induction, which was required to be completed within six months of appointment. Ms Esterhuyse said a circular was being drafted from the Director-General to all Heads of Department (HODs) to remind them of their responsibility to ensure that departmental induction took place within the prescribed period. The circular would be issued within the current quarter.
Mr Bosman asked how long the Department had been waiting for a response from the Department of Public Service and Administration (DPSA) on the matters raised. He remarked that communication with national departments often involved lengthy correspondence processes, and he wanted to know the typical turnaround time for responses. He also sought clarity on whether the DPSA was in the process of developing a pre-entry integrity assessment for provinces to implement, and whether provinces had been consulted in developing this tool. He further asked if the Western Cape Government’s existing integrity assessments had been taken into account in the national process.
Ms Lekker apologised for earlier connectivity issues and referred to the information provided in the report. She requested clarity on the implementation of the online and manual application systems, asking whether there was any data comparing the number of applications received manually against those submitted online. She wanted to know if there was a rationale for continuing manual applications or whether the current volume of online submissions rendered them unnecessary.
On the issue of induction, she asked what the previous timelines were within departments for conducting induction programmes after appointment, and how soon new employees were typically inducted under the current system.
The Chairperson thanked Members for their inputs and sought assurance that the transition to online applications did not disadvantage any prospective applicants. He asked how the Department ensured that all individuals, including those living in rural areas with limited access to digital platforms, were able to apply for vacancies on an equal basis.
Ms Esterhuyse said the average waiting period for a response from the DPSA was about two to three months. She explained that the DPSA was developing an integrity assessment tool for SMS members, which would be incorporated into the Department’s recruitment and selection process. The tool would form part of the existing assessment battery used during recruitment. She noted that provinces had not been requested to provide input into the tool’s development, as this remained the DPSA’s responsibility.
She added that the Western Cape Government operated its own Provincial Assessment Centre, which catered for employees on salary levels 9 to 12. Through this centre, the Department procured its own integrity assessments for those levels.
On the question of manual and online applications, Ms Esterhuyse said the number of manual submissions was minimal, with most applications received online. The system was designed to be accessible via smartphones, allowing applicants to apply easily from any location. She said approximately 3 000 to 5 000 applications were received online per post, and there were no indications that applicants were being disadvantaged by the system. Earlier challenges experienced by visually impaired applicants had also been resolved, ensuring full accessibility.
Regarding induction, she explained that there were two types of induction programmes. The first was departmental induction, which was conducted by supervisors when new employees joined a department to familiarise them with their specific work environment. The second was the provincial induction offered by the Provincial Training Institute (PTI), which took place two to three times a year. The concern raised by the Auditor-General related to departmental induction, which needed to be completed within the first six months of an employee’s appointment.
PFMA Compliance
The Chairperson referred to page 197 of the report, which dealt with Public Finance Management Act (PFMA) compliance and details of irregular expenditure under assessment. He requested further clarification on the figures reflected in that section.
Dr Malila explained that the matter related to irregular expenditure under assessment for the 2024/25 financial year, amounting to approximately R1.47 million. He invited officials to provide further detail on the specific items under review.
An official informed the Committee that there were twelve matters under assessment, with the largest amount being R684 000, followed by R524 000 and R499 000. All these cases related to instances where prior approval had not been obtained from the delegated authority to appoint a service provider, resulting in non-compliance with procurement prescripts. In some cases, contracts had expired but services had continued, rendering the expenditure irregular.
He emphasised that all the listed cases were still under assessment and had not yet been confirmed as irregular. Once the investigations were finalised and the transactions met the criteria for irregular expenditure, they would be formally recorded as such and processed accordingly. The official added that some cases also involved non-compliance with cost-containment measures, where minor overspending had occurred. These instances were identified through internal control mechanisms and included in the assessment process for further review.
Ms Lekker referred to pages 200 to 203 of the report, which indicated no irregular expenditure. She questioned this in light of the earlier explanation that certain amounts were still under assessment. She sought clarity on what consequence management processes were followed when approval had not been obtained for expenditure. She asked what had prompted officials to proceed without the necessary authorisation and what actions would be taken once investigations were concluded. She further requested that the Committee be provided with information once the assessment process had been finalised. She also commented that R684 000 was a significant amount and suggested that the Department reflect more clearly on the cost-effectiveness and justification for such spending.
The Chairperson added to the discussion, referring to page 199, which dealt with disciplinary steps taken. He requested elaboration on the paragraph relating to final written warnings and also asked for further clarification on matters listed on page 200, items B, C, and D. He then invited other Members to raise additional questions.
Mr Bosman referred to page 153 and raised concern about high personnel expenditure under Programme 6 (Core Governance and Legal Services) and Programme 5 (Corporate Assurance), where 94.3% and 89.2% of expenditure respectively were allocated to compensation of employees. He observed that while these figures reflected significant investment in personnel, there had also been underspending due to staff exits and delays in filling vacancies. He acknowledged that recruitment was constrained by DPSA regulations but asked what immediate management interventions were being implemented to address the situation.
He noted that despite reduced staff capacity, both units continued to meet their performance targets, and he was interested to know how this was being achieved. He asked for a breakdown of the interventions being implemented to address recruitment delays and to strengthen employee retention. He added that many skilled employees were being recruited by the private sector and asked what non-financial incentives were being considered to encourage staff to remain in the Western Cape Government. He said this was important to ensure that employees continued to feel valued and motivated, even within the constraints of limited budget appropriations.
The Chairperson remarked that the Department appeared to be losing many skilled employees to municipalities that offered higher remuneration. He noted that while the Department invested considerable effort and resources in training its staff, many subsequently left to take up positions elsewhere. Referring to a previous engagement with the Provincial Treasury, he recalled that it had been emphasised that staff retention was not solely about financial incentives but also about creating a supportive and positive working environment. He asked the Department to indicate what non-financial advantages or workplace conditions it could offer to encourage employees to remain within the organisation.
Dr Malila responded to the issues raised regarding irregular expenditure, underspending, recruitment, and retention. He explained that irregular expenditure under assessment involved two components: potential irregularities identified through internal controls and investigations conducted by the Department. He noted that for the 2024/25 financial year, there were around twelve matters under assessment, totalling approximately R1.4 million. He emphasised that, relative to a budget of R2.1 billion, these were minor issues.
He said that once investigations were complete, a report would be compiled and submitted via the CFO, after which confirmed irregular expenditure would be addressed through the relevant administrative or disciplinary processes. Dr Malila explained that irregular expenditure could arise from human error or, in some cases, fraudulent activity. In cases of human error, corrective actions could include discussions with the employee, training, warnings, or other disciplinary measures proportionate to the issue. Where sufficient proof of fraudulent activity existed, criminal steps could also be taken. He stressed that corrective actions aimed to balance accountability with opportunities for learning and growth, especially in a system that encouraged innovation.
On the issue of underspending in Programmes 5 and 6, he noted that staff exits were the primary cause. He provided examples from legal services and internal audit, highlighting that vacancies often remained unfilled for three to four months. He also observed that internal auditors were sometimes recruited by line departments after demonstrating strong performance, which contributed to capacity pressures. While some turnover (5–10%) was normal and beneficial for career development, the Department was experiencing higher levels of internal staff movement, which affected operational capacity. To manage this, the Department continuously monitored staffing levels and pressures through its budget committee, which reviewed vacant posts and assessed where units were under strain.
Dr Malila then invited Ms Esterhuyse to respond regarding retention, capability building, and recruitment improvements.
Ms Esterhuyse emphasised the importance of organisational culture in retaining staff. She noted that the Western Cape Government had a strong culture program, resulting in relatively low attrition for an organisation of its size. She identified staff poaching, particularly by the City of Cape Town, as the biggest challenge, acknowledging that other sectors, such as health, faced similar issues.
Regarding recruitment, she explained that the first step after a post becomes vacant was to confirm budget availability. Once this was determined, the recruitment process proceeded smoothly, although she noted that shortlisting often faced delays because managers did not always have time to participate in the process. She stressed that shortlisting remained the responsibility of the recruitment panel.
Ms Esterhuyse added that the Department’s People Strategy included exploring the use of artificial intelligence in recruitment processes to improve efficiency in the future.
An official explained the details on consequence management and the items reflected on pages 199 and 200 of the report. He said that in cases of gross negligence, the Department would act from a disciplinary perspective. Regarding irregular expenditure, he noted that most cases involved significant value or essential services rather than unnecessary procurement or contracts being improperly extended. The Department also considered mala fide intent; employees who deliberately failed to comply with processes and later apologised would face consequences.
He referred to the asterisks in the report, explaining that a single asterisk represented a restatement of an opening balance, which was an old matter, while a double asterisk related to a salary overpayment to a former employee, which was ultimately found to be a valid expense following investigation.
The official added that the bottom column on page 200 showed 18 cases amounting to R29 000, with individual amounts ranging from a few hundred rand to a maximum of R3 849. He explained that these cases were still under assessment. He further clarified that the entries on page 199 and lower down on page 35 are all related to old salary overpayments. Following a recommendation by the Auditor-General, the Department had reviewed all historical salary overpayments and treated them as potential losses from a governance perspective. As investigations concluded, some amounts would be recovered while others would be written off. He confirmed that this explanation addressed the content of the two pages in question.
The Chairperson referred to the disciplinary and criminal steps outlined on page 199, specifically relating to a service provider procurement process.
An official responded that a court case was currently ongoing in relation to the matter. Regarding the internal officials involved, the procedural steps were considered compromised, though not deliberately. The officials had been issued written warning letters, as measures that could have mitigated the issue had procedures been more tightly followed.
Closing Remarks
Premier Winde thanked the Committee for the opportunity to report and emphasised that such engagements were an important part of good governance and accountability. He praised the Department for its outstanding performance, noting that while some minor issues were highlighted, they were insignificant compared to the overall work and impact of the Department across government and municipalities. He acknowledged the efforts of all individuals, including those in offices ensuring work continued while reporting processes were underway.
Dr Malila echoed the Premier’s appreciation, thanking the Committee and Premier for their leadership. He acknowledged the Department’s managers and staff, noting that nearly a thousand employees across the province worked to improve service delivery for residents. He committed to continuing efforts to meet expectations and deliver results.
Mr Walters complimented the practice of different Members expressing thanks, describing it as a positive tradition. He observed that the province was implementing a distinctive governance model, characterised by change management within strict constraints, and noted visible progress from the previous year. While recognising the Department's and Executive's efforts, he emphasised that oversight by the Legislature was also critical in maintaining accountability and ensuring alignment with the province's long-term vision. He concluded by noting that the meeting had been productive and provided valuable insights.
The Chairperson asked if any members of the public were present and noted that no notifications had been received. He thanked the Premier and the Department for attending the meeting.
An official added that earlier in the day, the Department had hosted 59 second-year Bachelor of Community Service students from the University of the Western Cape at the Western Cape Provincial Parliament (WCPP). The students had been observing the annual report process and wished to attend the Committee meeting but were unable to stay due to university transport schedules.
Meeting was adjourned.
