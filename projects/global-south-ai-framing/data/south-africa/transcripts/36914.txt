Meeting Summary
Video
Companies and Intellectual Property Commission (CIPC)
The Portfolio Committee on Trade, Industry, and Competition met on a virtual platform to receive a briefing from the Companies and Intellectual Property Commission on its 2023/24 Strategic and Annual Performance Plan.
The Commission highlighted that the key issues it was responding to in South Africa were the Energy Crisis and its impact on entrepreneurship and the sustainability of businesses, the State Capture Report and issues of fraud and corruption, greylisting and the implementation of a Beneficial Ownership Register to address international concerns about financial transactions in South Africa, economic growth, unemployment and indirect or direct job creation. Internationally, the Commission was responding to the geo-political challenges, the environmental challenges, and migrant and human displacement. Targets were being stretched while remaining very decisively focused on those issues. The Commission was ever-evolving and constantly improving its practices. It was embracing technological advancements, such as 5G networks, artificial intelligence (AI), and machine learning. The Intellectual Property (IP) strategy was aimed at promoting innovation and the development of a knowledge economy and leveraging competitive and comparative advantages towards advancing the transformation of the South African economy. The strategic intent was to build the required competencies and capabilities to become a world-class modern regulator fit-for-purpose, agile, adaptive, relevant, and ahead of the curve. The focus would be on organisational re-design, upskilling of staff, strengthened governance and organisational policies, system upgrade and SMME support. The reduction of red tape was an ongoing target. The Commission would prioritise funding towards key performance indicators which had been set for the entity. A total of R664 m was allocated to expenditure and capital assets for the 2023/2024 financial year. There was a reduction of capitec expenditure to focus on priorities.
Members probed the proactive determination of beneficial ownership in the light of the greylisting of the country. Did the Commission plan to penetrate the veil of trust in the registration of companions? Did it rely on voluntary disclosures? What was the Commission's relationship with the banks in trying to verify a lot of the information it received? The website was extremely informative and helpful, but could more be done to steer visitors to the website in the right direction?  How was the Commission managing its engagement with sister departments, like the Department of Home Affairs, where sometimes there were major challenges that impacted negatively on the Commission’s ability to meet targets, especially in company registrations? How was the Commission coping with the downtime on the Home Affairs networks? How was the Commission working with sister departments to improve efficiencies concerning the ease of doing business? Why was the XBRL format required for submitting company finances and how did the Commission intend to respond to the additional problems and costs imposed on businesses by the requirement to submit in XBRL format? What measures had been put in place to address fraud and corruption in collecting societies?  How was the Commission intending to address the challenges with collecting societies to ensure they kept track of artists’ royalties and that those royalties were paid to the artists timeously?
The Committee adopted the report on Budget Vote 39: Trade, Industry, and Competition.
Meeting report

Opening Remarks
There were two items on the agenda: the formal consideration of the Budget Vote Report on the dtic for 2023/24; a briefing by the CIPC on its 2023/24 Strategic and Annual Performance Plan (APP).
Formal Consideration of the Budget Vote Report
The Committee Secretary was requested to read the recommendations contained in the Budget Vote Report.
The following recommendations were made:
The Portfolio Committee on Trade, Industry, and Competition, having considered the proposed 2023 Budget Vote 39: Trade, Industry and Competition, recommends that the House adopts Budget Vote 39: Trade, Industry, and Competition.
In addition, it recommended that the Minister of Trade, Industry, and Competition should consider reviewing the various prescribed income sources of the entities falling under his mandate, in accordance with the relevant legislation, with the intention of increasing existing income sources and introducing new ones, where applicable. The Minister should submit a progress report in this regard within six months of the adoption of this report.
There was no further input, nor were any changes made.
The Budget Vote Report was adopted by the majority. The DA reserved its rights.

Presentation by the
Companies and Intellectual Property Commission (CIPC)
Adv Rory Voller, Commissioner, CIPC, briefed the Committee on the plans of the CIPC. He said that the key issues that CIPC was responding to in South Africa were the Energy Crisis that had a severe impact on entrepreneurship and the sustainability of businesses; the State Capture Report with a renewed emphasis on conducting investigations; greylisting and the implementation of a Beneficial Ownership Register; economic growth in the aftermath of the Covid-19 pandemic remained a priority; unemployment and so indirect or direct job creation remained another priority. Internationally, the CIPC was responding to the geo-political challenges, the environmental challenges, and migrant and human displacement.
Critical to the work of the CIPC was collaboration on various levels and technological advancements, of which 5G networks, artificial intelligence (AI), and machine learning were focal. The Intellectual Property (IP) strategy was aimed at promoting innovation and the development of a knowledge economy and leveraging competitive and comparative advantages towards advancing the transformation of the South African economy.
The CIPC’S strategic intent was to build the required competencies and capabilities to become a world-class modern regulator that was fit-for-purpose, agile, adaptive, relevant, and ahead of the curve in making a strategic contribution to industrial resilience and competitiveness. The focus would be on organizational re-design, upskilling of staff, strengthened governance and organisational policies, system upgrade, and SMME support. CIPC was aligned with the dtic group outputs, in particular, the reduction of red tape.
Mr Muhammed Jasat, CFO, CIPC, presented the financial report. The CIPC will prioritise funding towards key performance indicators which have been set for the entity. R664m is allocated to expenditure and capital assets for the 2023/2024 financial year. The CIPC will focus on key priorities as per its Annual Performance Plan (APP) and Annual Operational Plan (AOP). The Commission’s annual expenditure is expected to increase at an average annual rate of 3.2%, from R664m in 2023/24 to R716m in 2025/26. Goods and services spending is expected to increase at an average annual rate of 4.3%, from R192m in 2023/24 to R209m in 2025/26. Capital asset expenditure is expected to decrease at an average annual rate of 17.5%. This is due to the CIPC currently investing in ICT infrastructure to automate services.

The Chairperson thanked the Commissioner for the presentation.
Discussion
Mr C Malematja (ANC) was very impressed with the presentation. There was not much to say except, 'Job well done!"
Mr D Bergman (DA) said the presentation was probably one of the most energetic presentations he had ever heard. With regards to the proactive determination of beneficial ownership, did the CIPC plan to penetrate the veil of trust at all? Did it rely on voluntary disclosures? What was CIPC's relationship with the banks in terms of trying to verify a lot of the information received? Was there a relationship that allowed them to cooperate?
Mr Bergman had visited the CIPC website and undertook a few transactions. He found the website comprehensive and helpful. He knew that the Commission did roadshows, but in terms of trying to build entrepreneurship, and getting more compliance, if people were able to pay a nominal fee, and receive checklists, and maybe some templates, that could probably be better than trying to navigate the website or coping with information overload on a roadshow. That would probably steer people in the right direction.  Being new to the Committee, he had missed a lot of the historical improvements but was CIPC not trying to achieve too much within the current year and with a reduced budget, when the Commission had to focus on the greylisting and verification of directors and businesses? He believed that enforcement had to be a focus and verification had to be a focus. The Commission was spending in so many different fields when it needed to deal with greylisting. One could look at the economy of Mauritius - Namibia was another economy that was greylisted in the Southern African Development Community (SADC) – but the problem was that the SA economy was much bigger which meant a longer turnaround time. On top of that, Russia and America were currently fighting on SA soil and that could bring further complications. So CIPC had to make sure that the ducks were in a row and he would put an emphasis on verification, cleaning up SA systems, and cleaning up ‘who was who in the zoo’ in terms of company registrations. CIPC should look at the right economies that had been greylisted, i.e. those that were similar to the size of the SA economy.
Mr Bergman said, with regards to musicians and artists, in terms of collection of revenue, the challenge had always been finding the right system. With the invention of the likes of Spotify and all those technologies, the Copyright Bill might be a little bit outdated, even though it had not come into existence as yet. One might find now that it limited revenue generation by the artists even before the Bill was enacted – he was asking for Adv Voller's opinion. Had other systems around the world been investigated in terms of how they monitored royalties for artists and musicians, and how was CIPC going to ensure that the collection and revenue systems, first of all, were geared to collect the revenue and that the data given to CIPC was sufficient to ensure that the right royalties were being paid to the musicians and artists?
Mr W Thring (ACDP) said that during his visit to Rwanda, he found that the government there had a one-stop shop and it was possible for businesses that had all of the necessary documentation in place to be registered within one day, i.e. 24 hours. When he looked at the CIPC website, he saw that the CIPC indicated that the turnaround time was five days. With regards to removing red tape, one of the characteristics that the President spoke about at the State of the Nations Address, was to speed up the ease of doing business in South Africa. Was there any move to remove whatever other red tape there was to reduce those five days down to one day like Rwanda was doing? He understood that the economies were different. He understood SA's economy was one of the largest economies on the continent, second only to Nigeria, but what progress had been made to reduce the turnaround time of registration?
Mr Thring asked about how CIPC worked with sister departments, like the Department of Home Affairs, where sometimes there were challenges. The synchronisation between CIPC and Home Affairs sometimes presented challenges for businesses. He had also seen in the media that there had been some challenges with compliance, not because of the company's problems, but purely because the Department of Home Affairs had its own internal deficiencies. So how was CIPC working with sister departments to improve efficiencies concerning the ease of doing business?
He asked about the use of the XBRL format. Previously businesses presented the annual financial statements in a PDF format, but now the XBRL format was required and, apparently, some businesses were experiencing challenges with the new format, to the extent that they needed to hire specialists purely to submit their financials. What was being done to assist businesses to submit their annual financial statements?
Mr S Mbuyane (ANC) (the connection was very indistinct as he was on an aircraft that was about to take off) asked how the CIPC was going to ensure that there was no corruption in terms of the distribution of royalties and was CIPC going to check if those services were being regulated? If not, how were they trusting the societies? How was the Commission intending to address the challenges with collecting agencies to ensure the societies kept track of artists’ royalties and that those royalties were paid to the artists timeously? What outreach programme is the CIPC considering that SA had six deep rural provinces?
The Chairperson observed that it was difficult to hear Mr Mbuyane but the questions were about distribution/collecting societies, education, and outreach to rural provinces. Had the Commissioner heard Mr Mbuyane’s questions?
Adv Voller stated that he had caught part of it so he was comfortable to answer Mr Mbuyane.
Adv Voller thanked Members for their comments. He told Mr Malematja that CIPC would be pushing along and doing what it could to meet all of those particular targets. Mr Bergman made a lot of very interesting observations that he agreed with 100%. Adv Voller said that he would tie one observation up to the question Mr Bergman had asked about beneficial ownership and the penetration issue. CIPC was currently in phase two of the verification and authentication and triangulation of data. Without that, it was a meaningless system and one could put any junk in there. How would one know that Mr X was the beneficial owner and not laundering money? CIPC was going to touch on a couple of levels. The one was that CIPC was working very, very closely with fellow regulators in the interdepartmental committee, with SARS (South African Revenue Service), the Financial Sector Conduct Authority (FSCA), and tying up with the second part of Mr Bergman's question, CIPC was working very, very closely with the banks. The Commissioner had a meeting with BASA (the Banking Association South Africa) on a quarterly basis, and those meetings would be even more frequent because of the issues of greylisting. So CIPC was going to look at how to build from a technology-based system. Adv Voller admitted that it was not foolproof, but CIPC would look at how to triangulate the data between all of the entities to see if there are anomalies in that particular data.
Adv Voller said that one had to remember that beneficial owners, for example, had to use the banking system many times, so it was a question of how to use banking information to see if Mr X was whom he said he was. What did SARS have? What data did the regulated industry bodies that were also required to receive data have? What did the South African Reserve Bank have and what about the FIC (Financial Information Centre)? CIPC worked extremely closely with all of those bodies in that particular phase, saying that they needed to have a red flag system and they needed to learn how to do the drill down for penetration, as Mr Bergman rightfully said. Company X owned Company Y that owned Company Z, but who was the warm body behind all of that? That was built into the system, and CIPC was using the Belgium model that said when one was a shareholder of a company that was owned by another company and another company, the person had to provide a diagram of all of the companies tied to the warm bodies so that those authorities could verify those particular companies and that there were registered directors of those companies, et cetera, et cetera. Adv Voller said he should also raise the point as it was going to be in the next round of the Companies Law Amendment Bill because it had not been addressed in the General Amendment Bill, and that was a deterrent, a criminal deterrent. In the next round of the Companies Law Amendment Bill, CIPC wanted to put in a criminal deterrent. He had spoken to those with experience, for example, Mauritius, and had learnt from them. Mauritius had said, just that week, that one of the biggest deterrents was the issue of criminality, and so, basically, he was looking at how one could prosecute. Mr Bergman's point that it was also about the size of the economies, and who one was working with. CIPC was working with very large volumes of companies and a very, very large amount of data. It could not be done physically, so CIPC was going to have to use technology-based triangulation, taxonomies, standardisation of information, and criminal prosecution as a deterrent to get to a holistic picture. Sometimes playing catch-up was a good thing, in the sense that CIPC could use what had been done by other economies.
He liked the point Members had made about the size of the economy. SA could not be compared to Namibia which had maybe two and a half million people and a much smaller registry that was totally manual. Adv Voller worked with that registry very, very closely because he was helping them. Being the largest economy on the African continent, SA had to look beyond the continent and so was working with the likes of the CRF grouping in Europe, Australia and countries like that. He had a dedicated team that worked every single day on the issue. They were working on how regulators looked at the technology-based verification, as well as the use of standardised data and structured metadata to try and get a holistic picture. So to answer the first question, he confirmed that CIPC would drill down; it would use the bank's information. BASA was on board and CIPC worked with a very well-respected beneficial ownership organisation, called Open Ownership, which was also assisting from the perspective of drilling down and making sure that CIPC was able to verify the information obtained.
Adv Voller explained that Company law was built on a trust system; it was built on a bona fide system, i.e. whatever one sends to the registry, whatever is put down in the registry documents, by legal rights, CIPC
should accept that. But CIPC had the legal right to scrutinise and verify the information so CIPC had been down the route of verification systems for many, many years. A question was asked by Mr Thring about Home Affairs, which his response would touch on. For many, many years, CIPC had had a direct link to the Department of Home Affairs system. So, if Mr Bergman were to register a company and he put down his full name and his details, CIPC was able to verify in real-time that he was who he said he was.  He explained how even Mickey Mouse would have been accepted as a name by the previous British system which had been a bona fide system. However, junk in the registry had no value at all. CIPC had resolved the issues of verification but BO (beneficial ownership) was a whole new ballgame.
He appreciated Mr Bergman’s comments on the website. CIPC was always looking to improve its website.  He did not think that CIPC’s website was the best. CIPC was going through a process of continuously getting it better and simplifying it because simplification was the most important part, together with making sure that one was able to find what one was looking for quickly and easily. He was keen to hear Mr Bergman’s views and to have a discussion about it. He invited Mr Bergman to email him. He valued the fact that stakeholders used the website and gave suggestions for improvement. He added that when it came to how the Commission was going to utilise its website even better, it was looking at APIs (Application Programming Interfaces) to enable the movement of disclosure data pinging into the system far more quickly. That would form part of the Companies Amendment Bill because CIPC wanted to know how to pay for that. CIPC wanted to start a subscription service for its data. Big data users, like banks, and credit bureaux, would pay an annual fee for which they could ping CIPC as many times as needed, making it easier for CIPC to move data and making it easier for access to CIPC’s data. The website had full API functionality but it had not been rolled out as fees had to be taken into consideration and the current Companies Act did not make an allowance for that.
The Commissioner noted the concern about having a lot of work to do in the APP and how confident he was that he would meet those targets. That went back to the point the Minister had made about stretching one’s capability, stretching one’s to-do lists. He had told his staff to stretch the targets and if they met 70% of them, it was good. He agreed that they should reach 100% of their targets and the dtic said they should meet 95% of the targets, but, if one put down the targets that one put down every year, one would not move the dial in the way one could by stretching oneself and one’s targets. That was the reason he was putting a lot of pressure on his managers and his staff to understand that CIPC was stretching the targets. He had had extensive meetings with his staff on the issue and some of his managers and his executive members were highly upset because they did not know how they were going to do it. He had told them to think out of the box; to think differently about how they would meet those targets because that was the only way to move the dial and to make a difference.  The only way to transform was by stretching the target, otherwise, one was going to stay the same. If one did the same thing, one was going to get the same results. He would wait and see what happened. It might be a problem and it might take maybe two to three years to get to the targets, but he had to start with the stretch targets sooner rather than later to try to push the boundaries to improve.
Regarding the issue about the artists, the collecting societies, and the Copyright Amendment Bill, Adv Voller said that CIPC did play a part as far as the legislation was concerned, but the Department of Trade, Industry, and Competition was responsible for policy and legislation. It was responsible for looking at the regulations as well as looking at the holistic picture. The Copyright Amendment Bill tried to address the issues about technology and how technology came into the picture. For years CIPC had been asking how one could know if the data sent to it was correct. One question was what system was used to collect data. CIPC had done some research and so had told the collecting societies what was expected from them when they sent a distribution plan to CIPC. They had to indicate what system of data collection was used, what the global picture of data collection of royalties was concerned with, and what the society had used in that particular data collection. There had been major fights between CIPC and the collecting societies and they had engaged senior legal advisors and senior counsel and had threatened to take CIPC to court because CIPC refused to accept the cursory information they were supplying. CIPC wanted a deep dive into data collection and to understand how the societies made sure when and where a song was played, etc. It was difficult but what systems did they have in place to work out the royalty percentages? It was not perfect. He could say, based on some of the copyrighted work done, that even around the world, the collection of data for royalty pay-outs was not perfect. His team had done a lot of work in that space, particularly Kadi Petje, and he was happy to share the work that he had done on data collection.
Adv Voller suspected that Mr Thring had read the website differently from what was intended. CIPC had offered a one-day turnaround for company registration since time immemorial, or probably seven/eight/ nine years. Mr Thring might like to register a company and the Commissioner would show him the system. It was 10:36 am. If his name was approved, which would take about half a day, and if he sent three or four names and all of them were cleared, he would get an automatic response saying the names were clear. If he continued the process of registration, his company would be registered by 16:00 that day.  Adv Voller would guarantee that 90% to 95% of company registrations would be completed within one day. The five days that CIPC was talking about were for the outliers, i.e. bigger companies and public companies that were going to have to go through the MRI to make sure that they met the requirements of governance in a public offering and to make sure they met the requirements of shareholding, issuing and launching offices. CIPC checked all of those documents. If the company to be registered came from an offshore jurisdiction, the company would have to send the details of the offshore registration. That was the reason it took longer and was not fully automated. The target that he was setting was to bring the registration of those companies down from five days to three days.
He added that CIPC could prove that the companies were registered within one day as the Auditor-General audited those timeframes when he did his performance audits. CIPC had always managed to meet the one-day target for ordinary companies.  CIPC was 100% committed. He could guarantee that the AG would soon confirm that the one-day targets for private companies were reached in the year under review.
Adv Voller said that CIPC experienced challenges with the Department of Home Affairs because it verified data to make sure that it had the right information on its registry. CIPC needed a trusted source to verify data such as names and that was with Home Affairs but, as everyone knew, the Home Affairs IT system went down every single day without fail. It was unreliable and could be down for varying amounts of time from minutes to hours and longer. CIPC was building a workaround. It would keep its own register containing names verified previously by CIPC on the Home Affairs database. The CIPC system could ping against that verification if Home Affairs went down and previous clients could be verified. If the person had not been verified before, the system would go into a holding space until the whole interface came back up.
He confirmed that CIPC worked very closely with other regulators, especially the FSCA (Financial Sector Conduct Authority). Some of the good work over the years that the CIPC had done as far as customer interfacing, customer service, and user technology were concerned, had been recognised. The FSCA Commissioner, Commissioner Unathi Kamlana, had asked Adv Voller to send a team to the FSCA in late 2022 to help him with the customer interfacing and a service delivery model. Adv Voller sent a team for four months that had done great work to help the FSCA. CIPC seemed to be on the cutting edge as far as innovation and the use of technology were concerned but the Commission had to continuously monitor his clients and make the experience better for them. That was why he appreciated constructive input such as Mr Bergman’s. It was easy to forget what other people were experiencing and so in processes to improve on that, it was a continuous journey. The work would never end as far as the customer service-related aspect was concerned.
The Commissioner stated that XBRL had been in use for five years, since 2018 when then Minister Rob Davies launched it at the Johannesburg Stock Exchange. A large number of companies started their financial statements in Word and then converted them to PDFs. The XBRL system automatically converted the PDF file into an XBRL file. There was absolutely nothing for the company to do, except to lodge the PDF file. From the outset, CIPC had worked very, very closely with the service providers of accounting software and all firms had to use accounting software, except maybe very small ones that did cash-based accounting. But all the other companies would have an accounting software package and those packages had XBRL built into the package. So when one did the financials, it was transposed into PDF, and when the company launched them through the accounting system, they were automatically captured as XBRL into the system. CIPC had given companies the assurance from the beginning that the use of XBRL would not increase costs to the company at all. XBRL was used as a tool for verification, and standardisation.
He responded to Mr Mbuyane about royalties. CIPC worked with the collecting societies, not in the sense of holding their hands, but in the sense that CIPC was almost a watchdog making sure they collected what they were supposed to collect, making sure they distributed what they were supposed to distribute. CIPC had a compliance programme and a compliance checklist to make sure that they toed the line as it were. In discussions with the societies, the Commission found they had lots of issues, but it was getting to the stage where each understood the other and royalties were being unlocked. Most importantly, collecting societies were not audited for many, many years but CIPC had been auditing them for the past couple of years. He was quite confident that they were getting to the stage of compliance, and the Commission had an understanding of how they worked. As he had said, royalty collection and distribution was a complex area, even internationally. South Africa had to build a system to improve on that. Programmes of education and awareness were helping.
As he said, CIPC worked closely with other departments. On a weekly basis, his team traversed the length and breadth of the country, taking the message of company registration to the communities. The Commission had a strategic focus on all South Africans understanding the work that the CIPC did, and how it fit into the bigger picture of the other departments.
The Chairperson thanked Adv Voller for the work that CIPC was doing. She was impressed with the idea of stretching targets. The Committee appreciated the work that he was doing in terms of company registration, which was important for the ease of doing business and the issues that he was dealing with in terms of the state capture report and greylisting. She thanked him for his commitment and for encouraging his entity to work outside the box for the South African taxpayer to get more ‘bang for the buck’.
Announcements
The Secretary announced that the Committee would receive a briefing from the Export Credit Insurance Corporation of South Africa (ECIC) on its 2023/24 Strategic and Annual Performance Plan (APP) the following Tuesday, 16 May 2023.
Closing Remarks
The Chairperson declared the meeting closed.
