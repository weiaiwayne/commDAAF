Title: DT and TBCSA G20 preparation and participation; SEFA progress report on implementation of Tourism Equity Fund & support to SMMEs in the sector; Briefing with Minister
Date: 03 Jun 2025
Committee: 
URL: https://pmg.org.za/committee-meeting/40854/

Meeting Summary
Video
The Portfolio Committee on Tourism convened virtually to receive briefings from the Department of Tourism (DT), the Small Enterprise Development and Finance Agency (SEDFA) on South Africa’s preparations for the 2025 G20 Tourism engagements and the status of the Tourism Equity Fund (TEF). The meeting opened with the Committee's endorsement of its Acting Chairperson Ms A Hlongo, ensuring continuity following the passing of the previous Chairperson.
The DT presented its strategy for hosting the G20 Tourism Working Group, under South Africa’s 2025 G20 Presidency themed,
Solidarity, Equality and Sustainability
. The DT outlined key priorities such as support for -, Small and Medium and Micro Enterprises (SMMEs) through digital innovation, tourism financing, and improved air connectivity. Outreach programmes were planned across selected districts in Mpumalanga and the Northern Cape to drive community engagement and awareness. These activities aimed to link local tourism sectors, including rural and underrepresented areas, to global opportunities.
The Tourism Business Council of South Africa (TBCSA) aligned itself with the DT’s initiatives and highlighted the inclusion of promotional tour packages on the G20 website, aimed at enhancing visibility and uptake of local tourism offerings. Committee members raised concerns about the tangible outcomes of the initiatives, including long-term strategic investments, inclusive procurement practices, air connectivity challenges, and the sustainability of rural outreach efforts. Specific inquiries about how SMMEs, particularly those led by women and youth, were being incorporated beyond consultation, into meaningful service delivery and procurement opportunities related to the G20 events.
The DT addressed these by detailing partnerships with institutions and the private sector through initiatives such as the tourism hackathon, which engaged youth from across the country. The hackathon aimed to develop and commercialise innovative tourism solutions with support from partners like the United Arab Emirates (UAE), Microsoft, and local technical hubs. Budgeting, interdepartmental collaboration, and community inclusion were covered, with assurances of equitable procurement practices and shared responsibility with provincial partners. Plans were in place to ensure post-G20 legacy benefits through continued projects, for example, the Tourism Investment Conference and the Sustainability Village.
The SEDFA presented progress on the TEF’s implementation since the relaunch, noting that R466 million had been approved across 23 transactions by January 2025. Despite this, implementation was halted at the request of the DT in February 2025. SEDFA identified several operational and systemic challenges, such as incomplete applications and internal capacity gaps, but assured the Committee that governance and support systems were being strengthened.
Members expressed concern over the delay in disbursements, lack of rural penetration, transparency of approvals, and overall pipeline management. Questions also focused on sustainability measures, return on investment, transformation indicators, and regional equity.
In response, SEDFA outlined support mechanisms including coaching and municipal engagement and confirmed its commitment to transparency, internal oversight, and community-based outreach through its national branch network. The DT acknowledged oversight shortcomings in the current service level agreements with SEDFA and indicated that funds may be reallocated if implementation falters again. Future plans included reassigning the fund to a more capable implementing agent, strengthening contract management, and aligning fund objectives more closely with tourism sector dynamics.
The Committee welcomed the DT's intention to embed its G20-related work into long-term strategic plans and reiterated the importance of timely, inclusive, and accountable delivery, especially concerning rural development, air access, transformation, and youth involvement. The meeting closed with a commitment to intensified oversight and future briefings to track progress.
Meeting report
Opening
The Committee Secretary, Mr Disang Mocumi, opened the meeting and welcomed all present. He said that ordinarily, in the absence of a Chairperson, the Portfolio Committee (PC) would have to elect an Acting Chairperson for each meeting until a permanent Chairperson is elected. Following the passing of the Committee Chairperson recently, Ms A Hlongo (ANC) had been elected to act in the position by the PC and proposed that Ms Hlongo continued acting until the appointment of a permanent Chairperson. Continuity would be ensured and assist the administration in continuing administration tasks executed in terms of communication certainty.
The PC Members agreed to this with no objections registered.
Chairperson Hlongo opened the meeting, welcomed all present and outlined the agenda for the day.
Preparation and Participation in The Hosting of the G20 Tourism Meetings and Engagements
The Minister of Tourism, Ms Patricia De Lille, led the presentation. She began with the background, saying that South Africa was the current G20 President for the year 2025 with the theme
Solidarity, Equality and Sustainability
. Amongst others, the country’s G20 Presidency sought to address the three strategic priorities of the South African Government of National Unity’s 7th Administration, namely to:
drive inclusive growth and job creation
reduce poverty and tackle the high cost of living
build a capable, ethical and developmental state.
A G20 Tourism Working Group (TWG) was established, comprising senior officials, as well as travel and tourism experts. The TWG served as a forum for the growth and development of sustainable tourism by promoting dialogue, exchange of knowledge, and sharing best practices.
The Minister told the PC that the G20 Tourism priorities were to enhance travel and tourism startups and MSMEs through digital innovation, tourism financing and investment to enhance equality and promote sustainable development, air connectivity for seamless travel and enhanced resilience for inclusive, sustainable tourism development. The presentation outlined the details of the two recent meetings of the TWG, including the outcomes. Outreach events were planned based on the G20 priorities in three municipal districts in Mpumalanga and one district in the Northern Cape (NC). The activities would form part of the build-up programme leading to the Tourism Ministerial G20 meeting. The purpose was to share information on the summit and aimed at advancing participatory democracy, and creating awareness of South Africa’s (SA) hosting of the G20. The presentation delved deeper into the purpose of the outreach programme for each of the chosen provinces and regions, as well as communication strategies for the diverse target audiences in relation to upcoming engagements.

Discussion
The session began with the Chief Executive of the Tourism Business Council of South Africa (TBCSA), Mr Tshifhiwa Tshivhengwa, being invited to make some remarks. He informed the meeting that as the TBCSA supported the G20 work done by the DT, no separate presentation will be given. He wished to add the belief that SA’s opportunity to host the G20 serves as a global endorsement of SA’s ongoing leadership in successfully organising major international conferences. The TBCSA had collaborated with the DT on every step. An addition from the TBCSA was to ensure that a webpage link was created to the G20 website, promoting tour packages for the expected delegates. Industry role-players at all levels were engaged in forming part of the programme, thus ensuring visibility and facilitating a substantive take-up by the visitors of the various tourism offerings the country had to offer, pre- and post-summit.
Ms H Ismail (DA) asked what long term strategic partnerships and investments had already been secured with other members of the G20 to boost the economy of the country in the long run. She then requested more specific details on the tangible outcomes of binding agreements and measurable return on investment achieved through G20 engagements that would directly boost SA's tourism economy, improve tourist safety, unlock foreign investment and advance the tourism sector recovery plan’s goals. She remarked that the DT's presentation heavily promoted community engagements and outreach but did not clearly show how those engagements would translate into long term economic benefits. She asked for more specifics on how the DT intended to ensure that the outcomes of these engagements, particularly in rural communities, were sustained beyond 2025 and linked to real tourism enterprise development.
She acknowledged that the presentation effectively covered empowerment and inclusion, which was well received. However, it did not clarify the selection criteria of service providers to ensure procurement processes promoted genuine participation, upheld fairness, encouraged competition and prevented elitism. Could the DT specify how SMMEs, youth, and women-owned tourism businesses were being included, not only in outreach but in the actual procurement and service operations related to hosting the G20 meetings, including the hackathon and district events? Lastly, regarding air connectivity, the PC observed several provinces, such as Limpopo or the NC, faced limited flight access, making it difficult for tourists to travel there affordably. What concrete steps would the DT take, together with the Department of Transport and provinces, to improve domestic air routes that currently limit tourism growth?
Ms E Linde (DA) said the focus on digital innovation, particularly the tourism hackathon was a forward-thinking initiative that aligned with global trends, and it empowered the youth sector. She commended the emphasis on the partnerships with universities and the SMMEs that could foster job creation through local economic development. Those were critical steps towards decentralising tourism benefits, and she encouraged the DT to ensure that these efforts translated into lasting opportunities for rural communities. Regarding the G20 TWG budget, could the DT provide a breakdown of allocations, funding sources and measures to ensure cost-effectiveness and prevent wasteful expenditure for the vast programme? Secondly, while the outreach programs targeted various stakeholders, how did the DT ensure that marginalised communities, especially in small rural areas, were genuinely included in the decision-making processes and what tangible benefits would they receive from these initiatives?
Several deliverables were outlined in the report, such as the action plan for the SMMEs and the air connectivity report, but what specific measurable targets had the DT set to evaluate the success of these deliverables and by when would these be achieved? She commended the initiative of the tourism magazine, framed as a legacy project, but how would the DT ensure that the winning solutions were practically implemented and scaled beyond this competition? What mechanisms were in place to track long-term impact and avoid this becoming just a once-off event? What coordination is happening with other sectors? The presentation highlighted a collaboration need between the tourism and transport sectors. What concrete steps had the DT taken to engage with the Department of Transport and how would this inter-departmental cooperation be sustained? She concluded by cautioning against aspiration goals without clear accountability frameworks to ensure deliverables that should benefit all South Africans and not just the urban elites.
Ms L Ligraba (ANC) asked who the travel and tourism experts were that formed part of the TWG, how many are there and what factors were considered in their selection. To date, how many dialogues or other various platforms has the TWG created to enable the sharing of knowledge and best practice, particularly among the SMMEs operating in the sector? What was the estimated value that would be derived from the whole process of preparing for the G20 Summit, the number of tourists estimated to boost the economy and the number of jobs that would be created? What practical plans were in place to action the deliverables on enhancing travel and tourism startups, as well as SMMEs through digital innovation, and who would they partner with? She noted the four programmes which were held to expose visitors to the South African tourism landscape. What other areas were targeted and were the DT looking into using this opportunity to shine a light on Villages, Townships & Small Dorpies (VTSDs)? How were the institutions selected to form part of the hackathon challenge, and what were the benefits of the competition?
Ms N Mashabela (EFF) said the presentation spoke of inclusive sustainable tourism, but failed to mention land ownership, redistribution or community control of tourism assets. She asked what concrete plans existed to ensure black communities control tourism infrastructure, especially in communities like Mkhondo and Siyabuswa, where the events were planned. She said the presentation highlighted site visits, dinners, receptions and travel, but with little clarity on the costs, procurement and beneficiaries. Who were the service providers for catering, venues, transport and event coordination? Which local SMMEs from the host municipalities were given first preference?
Ms Mashaba said black South Africans were deeply concerned about the harmful and misleading narrative being spread internationally, a narrative that falsely portrayed black South Africans as perpetrators of genocide against the white population. Prominent global figures such as Donald Trump and Elon Musk had irresponsibly amplified this rhetoric, telling the world that there was a so-called white genocide occurring in SA. Despite the damage this propaganda did to the national image and social cohesion, there had been no public or corrective communication from the DT or from South African Tourism (SAT), which was deeply troubling. It suggested tacit agreement with this dangerous narrative which not only misrepresented the truth but also posed a significant threat to the tourism sector, economy and the country’s reputation globally. She said that the absence of any strategy in the DT's plans to counter this misinformation was unacceptable. She then asked what proactive steps the DT was taking to challenge and dismantle this false narrative of white genocide. What communication strategies were in place to assure the international community that SA was a safe, welcoming and inclusive destination for all visitors? She asserted that there should be a clear, robust communication strategy to confront this propaganda and to reassert the reality of a democratic SA where all lives are valued and protected. Without such a response, falsehoods would be allowed to shape SA’s global image to the detriment of both her people and the economy.
Responses
Mr Victor Vele, Director-General (DG), DT, led the responses and said some of his colleagues would respond to specific issues. He wished to indicate that some of the programme costs are not for the DT's account. These will be paid by some of the partners such as the eThekwini Municipality and provinces. The costs were allocated to marketing budgets, and the partners will be responsible for the financial management of the programmes.
Dr Sipho Ngomane, Chief Director: Tourism Sector Support Services (CD: TSSS), DT, responded to the outcomes of the programmes beyond 2025. All 18 universities of technology and institutions specialising in travel, tourism and hospitality across SA will participate in the process. Out of those 18 institutions, two of the top four performing students were selected to participate in the hackathon process, and these students would form seven groups with five participants per group. All groups will meet at the University of the Western Cape (UWC), where the participants will be taken through the requirements of the hackathon. The focus areas are on innovation to sustain or to improve the SMMEs and the startups in the tourism sector. These groups would work together between May and September 2025. The final competition will be held in Mpumalanga alongside the G20 Ministers Meeting. Three groups will be selected and fast-tracked to develop the proposed hackathon prototype.
Dr Ngomane said the DT was partnering with the private sector in order to deliver on the hackathon initiative, and government institutions, such as the Department of Communications and Digital Technologies (DoCDT), were key leaders in terms of the Task Force on Intelligence, Data, Governance and Innovation. The DoCDT linked the DT up with the UWC and the digital innovation hub and the National Electronic Media Institute of South Africa (EMISA).
He said the DT had partnered with the Office of the Premier in the Western Cape and engaged private institutions with expertise in hackathons and artificial intelligence, including Microsoft South Africa. Additional collaboration institutions include the TBCSA, the Hilton Group and IT Web, due to their experience in hosting hackathons. The United Arab Emirates (UAE) had partnered with the Ministry of Tourism, fulfilling a commitment made during the Brazil G20 presidency to support SA with this initiative. This partnership aims to implement solutions with long-term impact, ensuring that the top three winning groups would receive assistance to refine, commercialise and bring the developed concepts to market. He emphasised that the participants were young people and assured the PC that event-related service providers, such as catering, would be sourced from women-owned SMMEs.
Dr Shamilla Chettiar, Deputy Director-General: Destination Development, said the DT was set to host the first inaugural Tourism Investment Conference in September 2025, as a legacy project of the G20. The opportunity that the G20 platform provided was an unprecedented number of both government and business participants in one space. The DT was working with the provinces and other entities, including the private sector, to package all the tourism products the country had on offer for investment. Work continued behind the scenes to pitch these investment opportunities to various partners before the said conference. Although nothing had yet been secured, the G20 Summit would be an ideal platform to launch these efforts. She said the investment conference would continue as an annual event in the future.
Ms Anemé Malan, DDG: Tourism Research, Policy, International Relations and Strategic Sector, DT, said in terms of long-term strategic partnerships were established through the TBCSA amongst others. Close collaboration exists with the Department of Home Affairs (DHA) and the Department of Transport. International partnerships would be strengthened through the hosting of the G20, whilst a number of bilateral agreements and Memoranda of Understanding (MoUs) exist, citing the example mentioned earlier of the UAE. She said all procurement focused on the prioritisation of SMMEs, women and youth, and the appointment of local companies. When procuring venues, the Department of International Relations and Cooperation (DIRCO) guidelines on standards and other requirements were used. SMMEs, women and youth were still prioritised.
The DT worked closely with the Department of Transport in improving air connectivity and was in the process of implementing the Tourism Route Development Marketing Plan, approved by Cabinet, to enhance connectivity across the country. She emphasised the importance of partnerships with provinces and other stakeholders, including cost-sharing for implementation. Mpumalanga had already committed to hosting upcoming events under this initiative. The DDG reiterated the cooperation with DIRCO in organising events throughout the year of SA’s G20 Presidency, ensuring long-term benefits for both the tourism sector and the country. She highlighted benefits for SMMEs and local communities, including free grading of establishments leading up to the Summit. Additionally, tourism monitors stationed in event areas would support security efforts and as liaison officers for delegations, providing greater exposure. Another initiative, the sustainability village concept was being rolled out, with its Indaba set to take place in Mpumalanga to promote and showcase sector products and services.
The DG said the full impact of the G20 Summit would become evident through a post-summit evaluation, which would include assessing the broader tourism impact generated by various work streams. He highlighted the importance of distributing the benefits effectively. The Minister had taken a conscious decision, following administrative recommendations, not to group the Kruger National Park with the Mpumalanga province. Consequently, a range of activities would take place within the province, while ministerial engagements linked to the Summit would be hosted inside the Kruger National Park. This approach aimed to ensure the localisation of benefits. A major advantage of the G20 Summit was a significant marketing opportunity for SA, with exposure on major international news networks such as CNN, Al Jazeera, BBC and others. This coverage would reduce the need for paid promotional efforts. The success of the G20 Summit would bolster SA’s tourism marketing and enhance global visibility.
Minister De Lille thanked the Members for their positive comments and looked forward to the support of the PC to ensure that the country hosted a successful G20 Summit and related events. The planning committee met every Monday and comprised the DT, TBCSA and SAT. From time to time, additional experts were invited for advice. She thanked the TBCSA for the role they were playing. She urged Members to visit the TBCSA website, where information was available, pointing out that the TBCSA had funded this initiative. She also thanked SAT for hosting the sustainability village, showcasing the best of the SMMEs across the country. Minister De Lille said the DT had a deep bias towards SMMEs as well as woman and youth empowerment.
The DT worked very closely with the host province, Mpumalanga and had a joint operational committee that met weekly. The province helped to identify possible excursions the delegates could undertake while in the province. She directed Members to the two presentation annexures, indicating these provided more information on the venues for the outreach programmes and provided an overview of the hackathon concept. She emphasised that the outcome of the hackathon process was an attempt by the DT and TBCSA to ensure the G20 Summit included young people. The UAE partnership provides the prize money for the first, second and third prizes. So far, US$100 000 was pledged for the prize money, $50 000 for the first prize, $30 000 for the second prize, and $20 000 for the third prize. Some of that money was earmarked for institutions and universities towards participant assistance.
She said the DG had already written to all nine provinces and the eight metros to submit projects for investment purposes. These would be assessed by a screening committee, assisted by Infrastructure South Africa (ISA), to ensure that the projects were bankable. Minister De Lille attended to an outstanding issue, namely the inclusion of VTSD in the G20 legacy projects.
The Minister said the government stance on the genocide narrative is being addressed by the President and Government Communication Information Systems (GCIS) on behalf of the whole country. The campaigns were beginning to show success as the narrative was being negated, and the DT was thus covered. She continued to say that despite the genocide claims, tourism between especially the United States of America and SA had grown by more than 10% and all incoming flights were full. She agreed with the Members that communication strategies around the issue could be improved. She noted that a number of private sector organisations had also responded. She had seen statements by the TBCSA as well as the Southern Africa Tourism Services Association. She further confirmed the partnership with the Department of Transport to market the route and SA as a destination.
On 12 February 2025, the DHA announced a new trusted tourist tour operator scheme to process visitor visas for visitors coming from India and China. In the first three months, a growth of over 11 000 more visitors coming from India and China was recorded. South African Airways are considering implementing a direct flight between India and China. She thanked the Members for the positive comments and said the DT would continue to consult with the PC on scheduling the community outreach dates. The aim was to ensure the PC had an active oversight participation role and use the opportunity to engage with the communities. Lastly, in terms of the budget, the DT was guided by National Treasury (NT) protocols and regulations on sponsorships, partnerships and investment pledges and remained within that framework so as to comply with procurement regulations.
The Acting Chairperson said the PC appreciated the invitation to the outreach programmes as oversight work can be carried out and stakeholder engagement forged. She requested that the four TWG deliverables should contain clear timeframes to add value to the PC oversight visits. She noted that the deliverables were part of the five-year strategic plan of the DT, and it was important that these be infused into the annual performance plans of the DT and South African tourism industry. The issue of air connectivity could not be over-emphasised. She said the involvement of the Department of Transport was crucial, as there was a need for SA's comprehensive air transport strategy. She requested that the DT at a future briefing, include an update on the work done to bring more airlines to SA. She recalled that the DT had said a subscription fee was paid to the United Nations Tourism (UNT). The DT should explain whether the UNT, as an acknowledged partner, provided services through the subscription fees or if additional fees had been charged for services provided. Lastly, Ms Hlongo said the DT should provide full details of the projects included in the investment portfolio that were part of the presentation.
Tourism Equity Fund Status
Ms Thulisile Manzini, DG, Department of Small Business Development, introduced the presentation, expressing condolences to the PC at the untimely passing of the Chairperson, Ms Lungi Gcabashe. She continued by noting that since the last briefing to the PC on approved transactions up to September 2024, significant developments had occurred. She said developing a viable pipeline, preferably of black-owned tourism enterprises, with equitable national distribution, was a long-term project, considering the complexity of the due diligence processes involved. These included assessing the capital readiness of funding applicants.
She said the immediate impact of SEDFA’s establishment of 12 deals had been approved, totalling over R135 million. By 22 January 2025, the date of the most recent Investment Committee meeting, this figure had risen to R466 million for 23 deals. However, no further transactions had been approved as the DT requested to spend the fund’s implementation in February 2025.
At that point, five deals worth R140 million were awaiting approval by the Tourism Equity Fund (TEF) Investment Committee, while another 53 deals valued at R837 million were in various stages of due diligence prior to awarding. She then handed the presentation over to Mr Nkosikhona Mbatha, Chief Executive Officer (CEO), SEDFA,
Mr Mbatha addressed the TEF foundational aspects and said that the TEF was launched in January 2021 as part of the DT's efforts to create a more inclusive tourism sector. In September 2023, the revised framework for the TEF was gazetted and received Cabinet support. This marked a significant step in restarting the program and aligning it with the National Tourism Sector Strategy 2016-2026. On 31 October 2023, the TEF was relaunched in collaboration with the DT, private sector institutions and development finance institutions (DFIs). On 6 November 2023 to 29 January 2025 TEF was under implementation approving 23 transactions valued at R466 million. On 12 August 2024, DT requested the SEDFA to expedite implementation setting 31 March 2025 as a deadline. From 29 January to 14 February 2025, DT commenced engagements with SEDFA regarding an instruction to pend the implementation of the Fund. The last transactions were approved on 22 January 2025. The TEF offered both grants and soft loans to eligible businesses in the tourism sector, and the fund composition was outlined.
The presentation then turned to the Implementation Phase performance of the TEF. By September 2024, the TEF had approved over R135 million with the biggest allocation approvals seen in KwaZulu-Natal, Limpopo and Gauteng, respectively. He further broke down the performance in numbers, stating that a total of 703 jobs were created for the period. A summary of the Implementation Phase Pipeline as of 22 January 2025 was presented, and of the 500 applications, 58 applications at an estimated value of R951 million were planned for completion by 31 March 2025, and were distributed in stages.
The CEO said there were some challenges to be addressed to expedite implementation. These included incomplete applications, internal capacity, over-priced asset acquisitions and time delays beyond applicants’ control. Mr Mbatha concluded by listing the TEF game changing projects.

Discussion
The Chairperson opened the discussions by saying that it was encouraging to see the TEF was making tangible progress, with R466 million approved, R125 million dispersed, and over 700 jobs supported so far. The game-changing projects, particularly those black-owned, women-led and based in provinces like KZN, Limpopo and NW demonstrated the TEF's immense potential to transform ownership and participation within the tourism economy. It was, however, clear that implementation challenges remained, especially around the pace of disbursement, pipeline management and support for applicants navigating the process with a pipeline value of nearly R1 billion. It was vital that the established systems and partnership were designed to scale delivery while maintaining accountability and impact. She encouraged the team to continue working closely with local stakeholders, including municipalities and tourism SMMEs, to expand access, improve turnaround times and ensure support reached the rural and under-serviced communities.
Ms Ismail asked SEDFA to provide more information on approved beneficiaries, the type of business, ownership, and location. Secondly, she wanted to know what mechanisms had been introduced by both the entity and the DT to improve the low uptake of loans by businesses in rural communities. She noted the jobs created but wished to find out the cost per job created, as this would indicate the return on investment. What was the estimated economic growth generated by the TEF-funded projects? With transformation often dismissed as a buzzword, could the DT provide verifiable data showing how many of the TEF-funded businesses were truly black-owned, managed and operational, and how many of these operated in historically excluded rural or township areas? What lessons had been learned from the TEF that could be applied to future funding initiatives? What measures were in place to ensure the long-term sustainability of the projects funded by the TEF?
Ms Ismail requested SEDFA or the DT to share any success stories or case studies of businesses that had benefited from the TEF. Lastly, were there any partnerships or collaborations planned with other organisations to support the TEF, for example, the South African Township and Village Tourism Association, to expand its footprint in townships and villages?
Ms Linde referred to a slide that indicated the DT had requested SEDFA to expedite the implementation progress to complete implementation by 31 March 2025, which seemed unattainable. What had happened since January to June 2025? She said the presentation reflected ongoing delays around the awarding timelines. What mechanisms were in place to ensure transparency and prevent political interference in the selection of applications, particularly given the delays in the finalisation of the agreements with the DFIs? She said there was very little in the presentation about the outcomes, the monitoring frameworks or clear performance indicators. How would the DT ensure that the R600 million in ring-fenced funding achieve measurable and sustainable transformation outcomes in the tourism sector and what performance indicators would be used to track this? Lastly, she questioned the stagnation of the TEF, as there seemed to be no clarity on time frames and commitments, which were essential for accountability. Why had there been no progress reported on the disbursement or implementation systems and a specific timeline for the rollout of the funding to qualifying tourism businesses?
Responses
Mr Mbatha responded that SEFDA could provide information to the PC on each location, business type, and other documents on the businesses funded. A lesson learned, was the need to have a dedicated team working around the clock making sure funds were utilised on time. Some businesses were challenged to compile all the required information, or the information provided in the applications was insufficient for SEDFA to make a decision. Given that the two entities had merged, the capacity to provide pre-investment support, coaching, training and support resided in-house. This service was provided by 53 branches, and in the 52 co-locations across the country. The support included assistance with inter-alia, business licences, zoning and property transactions, and engaging different municipalities. Lessons were learnt in the property space, such as the importance of conducting valuations upfront.
Mr Mbatha said the building of internal capacity had been prioritised, enabling improved oversight, all meetings conducted, and investment committees met on time and were able to process transactions. The SEFDA had advertised and secured additional capacity from two businesses, which were tasked to assist in processing and finalising transactions to ensure that allocated funds were effectively utilised. To address sustainability, close collaboration occurred with the DT to engage various tourism bodies, helping businesses across different areas gain access to markets. Regarding governance, a committee comprising representatives from the DT, SEDFA, and departmental experts was constituted to provide guidance on approval conditions. He assured the PC all was in order in terms of governance processes and structures.
The Acting Chairperson welcomed the presentation, emphasising the PC remained dissatisfied with the slow TEF implementation pace, following its inception and the legal challenges experienced. Secondly, the geographical distribution of disbursed funds was a cause of concern. It was understood that funding decisions were based on applications. However, more could have been done to promote the TEF and support applicants in complying with funding criteria. Given its role in advancing transformation, the DT should strengthen oversight of agreements with implementation agents to ensure timely and effective disbursement of transformation funds. Beyond implementation, SEDFA was urged to align funded tourism projects with the mandate of the Department of Small Business Development, ensuring that funding criteria for small businesses take into account the nature of the tourism enterprises.
Minister’s closing remarks
Minister De Lille concluded the discussions by saying that in September 2023, Cabinet approved an agreement for a service level agreement with the DT. However, the contract was changed to disperse funds over three to four years, which led to the DT having to return the balance of the funds to NT. She and the Minister of Finance intervened and were advised to move the funds to another implementing agent.
During January 2025, the DT ceded funding from SEDFA to another implementing agent, but a close-up report is still awaited. The DT had learned that oversight must be improved and contractors service level agreements monitored. Contract management should be built into the supply chain management processes and dedicated officials manage the contract and implementation. From November 2023 to March 2025, the DT had not been successful in meeting the Key Performance Indicators (KPIs) and targets set by SEDFA. The delay stems from challenges and weaknesses within the latter, which should have been disclosed to the DT. A follow-up meeting should compare performance with the service level agreement and KPIs to give Parliament a clear picture of the real status. NT would no longer allocate funds if the DT underspends in the current financial year.
The meeting was adjourned.
