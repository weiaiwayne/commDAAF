Meeting Summary
Video
The Portfolio Committee on Public Service and Administration convened a meeting with the Government Employees Medical Scheme (GEMS) to receive an update on the scheme's mandate, performance, governance model, and financial sustainability.
The Deputy Minister emphasised the importance of legislative oversight in ensuring that GEMS continued to fulfil its role as a key healthcare provider to public servants. The Chairperson of the GEMS Board outlined the scheme’s legal structure, governance framework, and commitment to affordability and access. Strategic presentations by the executive team further clarified GEMS’s alignment with the national healthcare policy, especially the transition towards universal health coverage and its role in supporting the forthcoming National Health Insurance (NHI) framework.
The executive management team highlighted the scheme’s hybrid operating model, its low non-healthcare expenditure ratio (3%), and robust governance structures. They provided clarity on how medical schemes operate under the Medical Schemes Act, and distinguished GEMS as a member-owned, non-profit entity. Operational and financial reports indicated that while GEMS remained financially stable, with over R23 billion in reserves and strong liquidity, it faced rising cost pressures from in-hospital claims and an increased disease burden. They confirmed that GEMS meets all the regulatory solvency requirements and is engaging regulators on risk-based capital reforms.
The Committee welcomed the scheme's transparency and raised critical questions about long-term affordability, fraud mitigation, and its readiness to align with national health reform goals. The Chairperson referred to a letter received from the National Education, Health and Allied Workers Union, which had complained about exorbitant and illogical annual premium increases, particularly the 13.5% increase, which it claimed exceeded the industry average of 10%. The union had also raised Board governance concerns, citing that in 2023, GEMS had held 52 board meetings for R14 million, compared to Discovery’s nine meetings for R1.5 million, and had called for a strategic turnaround plan to improve the scheme’s administration and management.
Members also challenged the scheme’s assertions on affordability, particularly for the lowest-income public servants, pointing out that monthly contributions were financially burdensome for these groups, and left little room for basic necessities. The GEMS's outsourcing model also attracted criticism, with claims that this could lead to worker exploitation. The problem of members exhausting their benefits within a few months, leading to service terminations and dissatisfaction, was raised, and the waiting period for new members before they could access benefits, was also queried.
GEMS provided a strong response to the Section 59 investigation report, which had found evidence of systemic discrimination by medical schemes against Black and Indian healthcare providers. GEMS acknowledged the findings, and committed to being proactive in addressing structural and systemic bias. They highlighted their participation in the Claims Risk Forum and the Fraud, Waste and Abuse Forum facilitated by the Council for Medical Schemes as part of their efforts to reform industry practices. They emphasised their willingness to move forward constructively and be part of the solution, reinforcing that their actions were guided by the principles outlined in the report and their internal review processes.

GEMS Presentation
Formation and background
Dr Stan Moloabi, Principal Officer, GEMS, began with an overview of the formation and background of GEMS. He said that GEMS was established with the recognition that access to healthcare was a basic human right, as enshrined in South Africa’s Constitution. In 2004, while the national population stood at approximately 52 million, only 16% were members of medical schemes. At the time, many public servants, especially those in lower-income categories, lacked access to affordable medical coverage that reflected their specific needs. Cabinet had adopted a resolution in 2002 mandating the DPSA to create a dedicated medical scheme for public service employees. A formal project to investigate and design such a scheme had been initiated, involving extensive consultations with organised labour, government departments, the healthcare sector, and health economists. These consultations had been critical in ensuring that the benefit design, affordability model, and governance mechanisms were inclusive and evidence-based. Labour unions, in particular, had played a key role in shaping a scheme that would align with collective bargaining frameworks and build trust among employees.
Dr Moloabi noted that in January 2005, GEMS had been formally registered with the Council for Medical Schemes (CMS) in accordance with the Medical Schemes Act. The scheme was designed as a restricted membership scheme for public servants and their eligible dependents. Its model enabled risk pooling within a defined population, allowing for targeted benefit design and employer subsidies. GEMS had officially begun operations in January 2006, following a strong government-supported rollout strategy that focused on awareness and member education. The result was exponential growth, particularly among lower-income employees, who had previously been excluded from medical aid due to cost barriers. The scheme had introduced a tiered set of benefit options, tailored to different income bands and healthcare needs, enhancing both inclusivity and member choice.
GEMS had refined its benefit offerings based on member feedback and consultation with labour. For example, the Sapphire option was revised to create the Tanzanite One option, addressing concerns over benefit limitations.
Dr Moloabi concluded his section by reaffirming GEMS’s foundational goals: to ensure equitable healthcare access for public service employees and to remain aligned with national health priorities through affordability, sustainability, and responsible governance.
Strategic positioning
Ms Masingita Chavalala, Executive Manager: Office of the Principal Officer, said that while GEMS was established in 2005, its core mandate remained unchanged -- to ensure adequate, efficient, cost-effective, and equitable healthcare coverage for public service employees, with additional options available for those who seek more extensive cover.
She outlined GEMS’s vision: to be an excellent, sustainable, and effective medical scheme driving transformation in the healthcare sector, in alignment with the principles of universal health coverage (UHC). Its mission was to provide equitable access to affordable and comprehensive quality healthcare that promotes member well-being. As part of its 2022–2026 strategy approved by the Board, GEMS had adopted a strategic thrust: to be a strategic partner in realising UHC in South Africa. This aligned the scheme with government’s National Health Insurance (NHI) plans, and GEMS remained committed to supporting the implementation of NHI as a key pillar in its mandate.
She then described GEMS's five core organisational values, each symbolically represented by a colour in the GEMS logo:
Collaboration (Grey) – Central to GEMS’s operations, driving stakeholder satisfaction through shared goals and results-oriented teamwork;
Innovation (Gold) – Represents creativity and the continuous improvement of services and benefit designs, ensuring responsiveness to member needs;
Member Value (Green) – Reflects the growth and competence the scheme strives for through skilled personnel, fast service delivery, and responsiveness to life-and-death scenarios;
Excellence (Blue) – Denotes GEMS’s pursuit of consistent, high-quality service delivery, rooted in performance, reliability, and a member-centred culture; and
Integrity (Red) – Stands for the scheme’s zero-tolerance stance on corruption, supported by strong ethical policies, whistleblowing channels, and member trust.
Ms Chavalala explained GEMS’s role in supporting the NHI framework, referencing initiatives such as the Tanzanite One option, which mirrors the proposed NHI basic benefit package. She described the concept of care coordination, whereby Members nominate a general practitioner (GP) or family practitioner to manage their care and refer them to specialists when needed. This approach ensured holistic member health management and mitigated unnecessary provider-induced demand. She also outlined how GEMS promotes equitable and income-based contributions, enabling members to select benefit options appropriate to their income and healthcare needs. The scheme prioritises better healthcare outcomes by benchmarking against industry standards and enforcing service provider performance monitoring to ensure members receive value for money.
Ms Chavalala emphasised GEMS’s commitment to maintaining low non-healthcare costs, making it one of the most efficient medical schemes in South Africa. This efficiency was driven by internal cost-saving measures that prevent administrative overheads from impacting member contributions. On leadership and governance, she reaffirmed that GEMS was governed by a Board of Trustees in compliance with the Medical Schemes Act. The scheme had maintained 18 consecutive unqualified audits, highlighting its sound financial controls and ethical governance practices. GEMS also provided ethics training to staff, particularly those in direct contact with members and service providers.
She then introduced the scheme’s benefit options, which include:
Tanzanite One- Entry-level, care-coordinated network option with defined hospital network access and out-of-hospital benefits requiring family practitioner (FP) nomination;
Beryl - Another entry-level option offering in-hospital benefits and network-based out-of-hospital care;
Ruby – Mid-level option with a personal medical savings account and block benefit structure;
Emerald - High-level option with comprehensive benefits and a primary care extender benefit;
Onyx - Top-tier option with the highest level of comprehensive coverage; and
Emerald Value – A discounted version of Emerald, with care coordination and defined network use.
She concluded by reiterating that through coordinated care, ethical governance, innovative offerings, and a strategic focus on universal healthcare, GEMS remained firmly committed to its mandate and the well-being of public service employees.
How medical schemes operate
Dr Moloabi resumed his presentation and introduced the next section, which aimed to explain how medical schemes operate, noting their importance in the context of GEMS's regulated environment.
He began by stating that a medical scheme was a non-profit organisation governed by a board of trustees and registered with the Council for Medical Schemes (CMS), as required by the Medical Schemes Act 131 of 1998. GEMS, like all registered medical schemes, did not have shareholders and was prohibited from distributing profits. All income was derived from member contributions, and any surplus funds had to be transferred to the scheme’s reserves, which served as financial buffers in times of uncertainty. These reserves were intended to sustain the scheme for up to four months in the event of revenue shortfalls, ensuring continuity for the benefit of its members.
He compared the operation of a medical scheme to a stokvel: members contribute regularly into a collective pool, and the scheme uses those pooled funds to pay healthcare claims as needed. Schemes were budgeted annually, balancing contribution income against expected healthcare costs, with actuarially advised adjustments made for future sustainability.
Dr Moloabi highlighted that restricted (or closed) medical schemes, such as GEMS, served specific employee groups -- in this case, public service employees. In contrast, open schemes were available to any adult who was not already a member of another scheme and who could afford the contributions. He noted that, as of the current reporting year, South Africa had approximately 71 medical schemes, of which 20 were open and around 51 were closed.
He then explained the enrolment process for members. Eligibility for GEMS requires employment in the public sector. Once enrolled, members choose a benefit option aligned to their income and health needs. Monthly contributions were then made, comprising both a member portion and an employer subsidy, in accordance with agreements in the public sector. He outlined the claims process. After a member receives healthcare services, the provider submits a claim to the scheme for reimbursement. If the provider was part of GEMS's designated network, there were typically no co-payments. However, if a provider charged above the agreed tariffs or was outside the network, the member may incur out-of-pocket expenses. Alternatively, members who pay providers directly may submit claims to the scheme for reimbursement at regulated rates.
Dr Moloabi stressed that the third-party payer model, whereby the scheme pays providers directly, could open the system to fraud, waste, and abuse (FWA). He explained that members often did not see itemised accounts, especially during vulnerable hospital visits, which made monitoring abuse more complex. He then described how medical schemes manage risk through mechanisms such as waiting periods, particularly to prevent anti-selection, a practice where individuals join a scheme only when anticipating large claims due to known conditions, and then exit shortly after receiving costly treatment.
He described open enrolment and community rating. All applicants must be accepted and charged the same contribution for a selected benefit plan, regardless of age or health status. This promoted equity but also resulted in adverse selection pressures. Schemes had to maintain reserves equal to at least 25% of their annual contribution income. For large schemes like GEMS, this could be overly conservative. He noted that risk-based capital models, like those used in insurance, were being considered as potential alternatives. With prescribed minimum benefits (PMBs), all schemes must cover a specified set of chronic and life-threatening conditions. Dr Moloabi explained that a 2009 court ruling had invalidated the reference price list that regulated provider tariffs. Since then, many providers have charged above scheme rates, pushing up costs significantly.
Dr Moloabi clarified the distinctions between medical schemes and medical insurance products, as confusion between the two was common among the public. He said that medical schemes were governed by the Medical Schemes Act and regulated by the Council for Medical Schemes (CMS), whereas medical insurance products fell under the Short-Term Insurance Act and were overseen by the Financial Sector Conduct Authority (FSCA). In terms of membership, medical schemes were required to admit all qualifying applicants without discrimination based on age or health status. This principle was essential to promote inclusivity and support the cross-subsidisation model on which medical schemes rely. In contrast, medical insurance providers may assess applicants’ risk, and could deny cover to high-risk individuals, often applying maximum entry age limits.
Regarding their financial orientation, medical schemes were non-profit entities. They were not allowed to generate profits or distribute surplus funds, and any excess must be reinvested into member benefits or scheme reserves. Medical insurance, however, was profit-driven, designed to return profits to shareholders. Dr Moloabi noted that estimates in the industry suggested that up to 40% of premiums collected by medical insurance products may contribute to profit, although this varied. When it came to claims, medical schemes were obliged to reimburse actual, necessary medical expenses in line with scheme rules and clinical appropriateness. Medical insurance, by contrast, paid pre-determined, fixed amounts in the event of specific incidents, regardless of the actual cost of treatment, and may exclude certain conditions from cover.
Medical schemes must also offer comprehensive hospital benefits as part of their plans, including coverage for PMBs, a legally mandated set of essential healthcare services, including for 26 chronic conditions. Insurance products, however, were not required to cover PMBs or chronic conditions, and may apply caps on payouts or limit coverage to certain events. Dr Moloabi emphasised that these fundamental differences made medical schemes more appropriate for long-term, comprehensive healthcare support, particularly for vulnerable populations such as public servants, while insurance products may provide only limited, event-based financial relief.
Operational functioning
Dr Vuyo Gqola, Chief Operations Officer (COO), GEMS, said the scheme operates using a strategically outsourced model, meaning that many of its core operational services, such as claims payment, hospital and medication authorisations, were handled by accredited third-party service providers. These included administrators and managed care companies accredited by the Council for Medical Schemes. Outsourced services encompassed member administration, claims processing, membership registration, clinical risk management (including hospital admissions, oncology, chronic care, and medication), as well as call centre operations and member support.
Despite its outsourced structure, GEMS retained several core strategic functions in-house, managed from its head office. These include tariff negotiations with hospital and pathology groups, alternative reimbursement model development, product development, benefit design, internal research, wellness programme delivery, and financial oversight. The scheme was also in the process of insourcing certain strategic initiatives to further strengthen internal capacity.
GEMS maintained full strategic control through its Board of Trustees, comprising six employer-appointed and six member-elected trustees, as stipulated by the Medical Schemes Act. The Principal Officer and executive management team were responsible for the operational execution of board-approved strategies. Oversight of service providers was managed through strict service level agreements (SLAs) and performance metrics, including health outcome targets. Regular independent audits and performance reviews were conducted to evaluate both financial and healthcare service efficiency.
Dr Gqola said that GEMS's non-healthcare costs, now referred to as directly attributable insurance expenditure, remained among the lowest in the medical schemes industry. Currently, the GEMS ratio stood at 3%, compared to 4.1% among restricted schemes and 9.5% among open schemes, according to the most recent CMS annual report. This demonstrated that outsourcing to for-profit service providers did not necessarily increase costs, and may even enhance efficiency.
Turning to the daily operational metrics, Dr Gqola shared a snapshot of GEMS’s scale and performance:
Over 1 800 call centre calls handled daily;
Approximately 1 300 member queries processed per day via multiple channels;
195 new principal members registered daily, accompanied by an average of 668 dependents;
R168 million in benefits processed each day;
65 babies born daily through the scheme;
R107 million paid daily in PMB claims;
R25 million in specialist claims paid daily;
Approximately 1 500 hospital admissions authorised daily; and
Nearly 389,000 individual claim lines processed daily.
These figures underscored GEMS’s role as a large-scale scheme deeply embedded in the healthcare needs of public servants and their families.
Challenges facing GEMS
Dr Gqola outlined several challenges confronting GEMS and its members, many of which mirror those faced across the broader medical schemes industry. These included rising healthcare costs, driven by unregulated provider tariffs, medical inflation, new medical technologies, and supply-induced demand. She explained that healthcare providers, particularly specialists, could charge far above industry norms, sometimes up to R4 000 per consultation -- a major concern noted in the Health Market Inquiry. An ageing membership profile undermined the model of cross-subsidisation, as younger, healthier individuals tended to delay joining schemes until their health deteriorated. There was fraud, waste, and abuse within the system, partly due to third-party billing arrangements. This remained a significant cost pressure, and GEMS continued to implement systems to mitigate it. There was also competition from short-term medical insurance products, which were not governed by the Medical Schemes Act and may offer misleadingly low-cost alternatives that lack comprehensive coverage.
The 25% regulatory reserve requirement, which dictates that medical schemes maintain reserves equivalent to a quarter of their annual contribution income, was another challenge. While GEMS complies with this requirement, the scheme, alongside others, was engaging the CMS to explore the potential for risk-based reserving models. Such models could more accurately reflect the financial realities of large versus small schemes, rather than applying a blanket reserve ratio across the sector.
In conclusion, Dr Gqola reaffirmed that GEMS continued to adapt to evolving healthcare challenges through a balance of internal strategic capacity and strong outsourced partnerships.
Financial standing and sustainability of GEMS
Ms Karina van Lingen, Chief Financial Officer (CFO), GEMS, provided an overview of the financial standing and sustainability of the GEMS.
She addressed the regulatory reserve ratio, a key financial requirement under the Medical Schemes Act, explaining that GEMS had previously held a significantly high level of reserves, particularly following the COVID-19 pandemic. A deliberate strategic decision had been made to gradually reduce this reserve level to align with the statutory minimum of 25%, a process that had occurred steadily over recent financial years. The scheme now sat at the targeted 25% level. However, the GEMS was in ongoing discussions with the CMS to explore a transition toward a risk-based capital model, a more nuanced reserving framework used in the insurance sector. Such a model could potentially allow for a lower reserve requirement for a scheme of GEMS’s size and profile.
She reported that the scheme currently holds approximately R23 billion in reserves, which were managed by a group of asset managers. These funds, belonging to GEMS members, were strategically invested to yield returns that supported the overall financial position of the scheme. Positive investment returns played a role in stabilising member contributions and maintaining scheme affordability.
Addressing one of GEMS’s biggest financial challenges, Ms Van Lingen highlighted the claims ratio, the portion of income spent on healthcare claims. She reported a steep post-COVID increase in claims, driven primarily by:
An increased burden of disease;
Rising fraud, waste, and abuse;
Supply-induced demand, where healthcare providers drive up utilisation; and
The most significant pressure point -- hospital admissions and related in-hospital costs, which had exceeded budget expectations. This area was receiving focused attention, as it threatened the long-term financial sustainability of the scheme.
Despite these challenges, Ms Van Lingen emphasised GEMS’s strong liquidity position. Over the last two financial years, the scheme has maintained a liquidity ratio between four and five, far exceeding the industry norm of two. This indicated that GEMS could comfortably meet all its short-term liabilities, confirming its status as a going concern, with no financial distress signals.
In forecasting for the current financial year, she noted that net claims incurred were expected to exceed the budget by nearly R1 billion, again reflecting high claims volumes. Despite this variance, GEMS was still projected to remain compliant with the 25% reserve requirement, although the bottom-line performance would be slightly weaker than budgeted.
Ms Van Lingen concluded her presentation by reaffirming that GEMS continued to prudently manage its finances through a robust benefit design process, a sound investment strategy, and ongoing claims management interventions. These efforts collectively supported the scheme’s ability to deliver affordable, high-quality healthcare access to members. She confirmed that GEMS remained financially sustainable in the long term, and current claims trends would inform pricing decisions for the 2026 benefit year, guided by actuarial modelling and performance indicators from 2025.
Section 59 investigation
Dr Moloabi provided clarity on matters relating to the recently released final report of the Section 59 investigation. On Monday, 7 July, the Chairperson of the Section 59 investigation panel had officially handed over the final report to the Minister of Health, who had subsequently released the report to the public. The purpose of his briefing was to articulate the GEMS's position about the findings of that report.
The Section 59 investigation had been initiated in 2019 following allegations from a group of healthcare practitioners that medical schemes were engaging in racially discriminatory practices, particularly in the identification of fraud, waste, and abuse. The investigation had examined the practices of several schemes, including GEMS and Discovery Health, focusing on the period from 2012 to 2019.
Dr Moloabi reminded the Committee that the interim report, released in January 2021, had found that black practitioners were more likely to be identified as having committed fraud, waste, and abuse compared to their non-black counterparts, a term the report used descriptively. Importantly, the interim findings had clarified that there was no evidence of explicit or intentional racial bias in the algorithms or methods used by the schemes and administrators. However, the outcomes of these systems nonetheless revealed racially disparate impacts, which the panel classified as a form of structural discrimination.
The interim report issued several key recommendations, including:
Drafting policies to eliminate implicit bias, with contributions from diverse stakeholders;
Developing a research and monitoring framework to detect and prevent racially skewed outcomes;
Conducting annual impact assessments of fraud, waste, and abuse systems, with submissions to the Council for Medical Schemes (CMS);
Issuing an unconditional public apology.
Dr Moloabi said that the final report, released over four years later in July 2025, had reaffirmed the panel’s analysis and the recommendations from the interim report. GEMS, along with other schemes mentioned, had made further submissions during the intervening years, which the panel had reviewed. The final report emphasised the need for systemic reform and made the following additional recommendations:
Development of early warning systems to notify healthcare providers when circumstances arise that may lead to the application of Section 59(3) of the Medical Schemes Act, which allows for suspension of payments;
Revision of the audit and claims claw-back timelines to ensure procedural fairness;
Creation of a mechanism for providers to engage with schemes when accused of fraud, waste, or abuse;
Full transparency regarding software, algorithms, and artificial intelligence (AI) tools used in fraud detection;
Assurance that fraud, waste, and abuse systems did not violate Section 9 of the Constitution or the Equality Act.
GEMS welcomed the clarification from the panel that there was no finding of racist intent in its conduct. Dr Moloabi quoted the report directly:
"The panel wishes to emphasise that any finding relating to race discrimination and a recommendation based on this finding was not a finding that the scheme and administrators were racist. In the public domain, the concepts of engaging in discrimination and being a racist were too easily conflated."
The report distinguished between unintentional structural discrimination and deliberate racism, with the panel urging ongoing efforts to correct systemic flaws.
In response, the GEMS reiterated its commitment to transparency, fairness, and equity in the administration of member benefits. Dr Moloabi said that GEMS had already taken extensive steps since 2021 to strengthen its fraud, waste, and abuse management framework to mitigate disparate outcomes. These actions included enhanced engagement with healthcare providers and alignment with the CMS and Section 59 experts. He affirmed GEMS’s zero-tolerance stance on fraud, waste, and abuse, adding that the scheme would continue improving its systems in line with constitutional and legal requirements. He acknowledged the leadership of Dr Zaid Kimmie and others in guiding the Section 59 Panel, and emphasised the value of continued collaboration with providers and regulators.
In closing, Dr Moloabi restated GEMS' commitment to its mandate. As the largest medical scheme in South Africa, processing over 389 000 claims daily, GEMS was a non-profit organisation (NPO) entrusted with providing equitable access to affordable healthcare for public service employees. He concluded by underscoring that GEMS remained a critical partner in the realisation of universal health coverage, pledging to implement enhancements based on the Section 59 findings and to ensure FWA systems reflected constitutional values.

Discussion
The Chairperson emphasised the critical role of GEMS in ensuring that public servants and their dependents had access to value-for-money medical care. He particularly welcomed GEMS’s tiered benefit structure, which he noted offered lower-income public service members access to affordable and quality healthcare.
He drew attention to a letter addressed to him from the National Education, Health and Allied Workers Union (NEHAWU), which had raised several concerns regarding the current functionality and administration of GEMS. It had asserted that GEMS, despite being the largest closed medical aid scheme in South Africa, had experienced a deterioration in both structure and service quality in recent years. It attributed these challenges to the outsourcing of administration functions to third-party, for-profit service providers such as Medscheme. The union believed this shift had negatively affected service delivery. Key issues highlighted included:
Exorbitant and illogical annual premium increases, particularly the 13.5% increase, which NEHAWU claimed exceeded the industry average of 10%;
Board governance concerns, citing that in 2023, GEMS had held 52 board meetings for R14 million, compared to Discovery’s nine meetings for R1.5 million;
A call for a strategic turnaround plan to improve the scheme’s administration and management.
The Chairperson requested that GEMS respond to these concerns, particularly the premium increase, board remuneration, and the need for a potential turnaround strategy, after all Committee Members had engaged.
Mr T Langa (IFP) posed several critical questions related to the findings of the Section 59 investigation. While the investigation had found no explicit racial bias in the algorithms used, he asked how GEMS and other schemes accounted for the racially skewed outcomes in identifying fraud, waste, and abuse among practitioners. He inquired about the specific mechanisms being implemented to ensure that future FWA audits did not result in disproportionate targeting of black healthcare providers. He sought clarity on how schemes would conduct annual racial impact assessments of FWA systems, and who would be responsible for overseeing the accuracy and independence of these analyses. He asked what progress had been made since the interim report, in redesigning FWA policies, particularly regarding input from diverse stakeholders, as recommended by the panel. He sought information on how schemes plan to ensure transparency around the algorithms and AI tools used for monitoring claims, and whether these tools were subject to independent audits for racial fairness. Given the panel’s acknowledgement of structural discrimination, he queried how schemes planned to differentiate between unintentional system bias and accountability for affected practitioners.
Mr Langa asked what form the recommended unconditional public apology would take, and how schemes planned to ensure that it was meaningful to practitioners who may have experienced reputational or financial harm. He questioned whether GEMS had made progress in developing early warning systems to notify healthcare providers of potential FWA concerns before punitive measures were implemented. He asked how the Council for Medical Schemes would ensure compliance with constitutional equality provisions in the design and enforcement of FWA systems. He inquired whether there would be mechanisms for affected practitioners to seek redress or restitution for potential income or career losses due to flawed FWA processes, and how such mechanisms would be administered. He asked about GEMS’s investment strategy and how members benefited from the returns on these investments. He requested examples from previous financial years to illustrate the impact.
Ms S Gcilishe (EFF) raised several substantive concerns. Firstly, she challenged the scheme’s assertions on affordability, particularly for the lowest-income public servants, such as cleaners and lower-grade workers earning between R3 500 and R5 000 per month. She pointed out that monthly contributions of approximately R1 200 were financially burdensome for these groups, and left little room for basic necessities.
She expressed scepticism about GEMS’s boast of low non-healthcare costs through outsourcing, arguing that the EFF opposed outsourcing models as they could lead to worker exploitation. She specifically questioned whether outsourced workers employed by GEMS's service providers earned enough to afford the scheme's own medical plans, and if not, whether the practice was truly something to be proud of.
On the issue of systemic bias, Ms Gcilishe referred to the findings of the Section 59 investigation. She noted the disparity between the treatment of black practitioners and those with non-African surnames when submitting similar claims. She urged GEMS to clarify what concrete steps it had taken to eliminate such bias from its systems. She raised the issue of GEMS’s reserves, querying why the reported billions in reserves could not be used to reduce member contributions or expand benefit coverage. He concluded by emphasising that despite GEMS's public assurances, the scheme remained unaffordable for many, echoing NEHAWU’s concerns.
Ms G Mchunu (MKP) raised the issue of Members exhausting their funds early in the year, noting that this often resulted in public servants terminating their GEMS membership or losing employer contributions. She stressed that this situation could negatively impact the health of employees and subsequently reduce productivity in public departments.
She asked what measures were in place to prevent early fund exhaustion. Were there plans for more flexible benefit structures or rollover options to allow members to retain unused benefits at year-end? How could employer contributions be restructured to incentivise more efficient usage of healthcare funds and prevent premature depletion? Were there options for enhanced employer contributions or additional benefits to support employee health and wellness?
Ms Mchunu also asked for a comparison between GEMS and other medical schemes in terms of value for money, and what distinguished GEMS from its competitors. She queried GEMS’s strategy for minimising fraudulent claims and how it could better partner with the Health Professions Council of South Africa (HPCSA) in investigating fraud by healthcare providers. She also asked what consequence management mechanisms existed for public servants found to have committed fraud under the scheme. She concluded by requesting information on initiatives aimed at improving healthcare access for employees in rural areas.
Ms W Tikana-Gxotiwe (ANC) began by reiterating a longstanding concern about outsourcing, noting that this issue had been consistently raised in Committee discussions. She called for greater transparency regarding the costs paid to outsourced service providers, particularly those managing payment administration on behalf of GEMS.
She then addressed GEMS’s limited reach across the entire public service. With approximately 1.2 million public servants and only 800 000+ GEMS members, she questioned GEMS's strategies to expand its membership. She asked whether those not currently covered were deterred by affordability concerns, or gaps in the scheme’s tier system.
She emphasised the problem of members exhausting their benefits within a few months, leading to service terminations and dissatisfaction. She queried the waiting period for new members before they could access benefits, and asked for clarification on how the scheme’s annual premium increases compared to standard public service salary increases. She expressed concern that premium increases might effectively cancel out annual salary gains, thus reducing members’ disposable income. In closing, she called for a balance between economic realities and the scheme's design to ensure GEMS remained accessible and beneficial to all categories of public servants.
GEMS's response
Chairperson Tutu opened the response on behalf of the Board of Trustees of GEMS, providing a reaffirmation of the scheme’s founding principles and operational model. She reminded the Committee that GEMS was established as a restricted, not-for-profit medical scheme dedicated to serving public service employees. It was governed by the Medical Schemes Act, regulated by the Council for Medical Schemes, and ultimately accountable to its public servant membership base.
Dr Tutu emphasised that GEMS operated on a multi-administrator model, which was designed to encourage efficiency, transformation, and competition within the scheme’s operations. In light of criticisms about outsourcing, she noted that while some functions were outsourced for operational reasons, a number of services had been insourced to ensure direct control and better alignment with the scheme’s objectives. She said this model remained effective, and both member and provider satisfaction levels were consistently high.
On the matter of financial sustainability and contribution increases, she clarified that the scheme had recorded 19 consecutive unqualified audits and maintained an AA+ financial strength rating. The reserves stood at 27.04% as of March, well above the statutory requirement of 25%. This strong financial position had underpinned the Board’s decision to approve a 13.4% contribution increase for 2025, aimed at preserving benefit levels and ensuring the scheme’s ongoing solvency.
Responding directly to NEHAWU’s assertion that GEMS was unaffordable and in need of a turnaround strategy, Dr Tutu strongly rejected this characterisation. She maintained that GEMS was on a stable footing, both administratively and financially, and that the Board remained committed to constructive dialogue with labour unions, the Public Service Coordinating Bargaining Council (PSCBC) and Parliament, to further enhance the scheme’s affordability and accessibility for lower-income members.
Dr Moloabi supplemented the Chairperson’s remarks by addressing specific concerns raised by Members of the Portfolio Committee. He began by acknowledging the findings of the Section 59 report, which indicated a statistical pattern of unfair outcomes affecting black healthcare providers, particularly African doctors. While the report did not find intentional bias, it nonetheless highlighted systemic disparities in the fraud detection mechanisms across various schemes, including GEMS. He assured the Committee that GEMS was actively working to review its systems and enhance fairness in the claims adjudication process.
In response to concerns about affordability for lower-income public servants, Dr Moloabi highlighted the availability of the five benefit options under GEMS, particularly the Tanzanite One option, which was fully subsidised for employees earning below R15 000 per month. He explained that these members received a 100% subsidy from the employer, and that this tier was specifically designed to ensure no out-of-pocket contribution for the lowest earners. He acknowledged, however, that not all public servants were on GEMS, and many of those who remained uninsured, or subscribed to other schemes, often cited cost or lack of awareness as barriers. He confirmed that GEMS was actively conducting outreach and educational campaigns, including roadshows and digital communication, to improve enrolment and member understanding of benefits.
Regarding the issue of fund exhaustion early in the year, which could lead to service disruptions or member resignations, he said this was largely due to members selecting options that did not align with their healthcare needs. GEMS was working on enhanced benefit design and member education initiatives to help individuals make informed choices about their coverage. He also mentioned the introduction of benefit rollover features and flexible funding structures as possible strategies under consideration, to mitigate premature depletion of benefits.
On the question of employer contributions, Dr Moloabi explained that the government currently subsidises 75% of contributions for members on salary levels 1–5. There was no upper cap on the subsidy, meaning lower-income members received proportionally greater support. However, he noted that employer contribution levels were determined within PSCBC processes, which fell outside the scope of GEMS’s mandate.
He concluded by addressing the Committee’s concerns around fraudulent claims and GEMS’s efforts to reduce abuse by both members and service providers. He outlined partnerships with the Health Professions Council of South Africa and other enforcement agencies to detect, investigate, and take action against fraud. He also emphasised that consequence management for fraudulent activity was a shared responsibility between GEMS and government departments, particularly in cases involving public servants.
Ms Van Lingen was invited to elaborate on how the scheme’s investment income benefited members. The CFO explained that in the previous financial year, GEMS had earned R1.7 billion more than expected in investment returns. This positive performance had directly contributed to reducing the necessary contribution increase for 2024 by between 1.5% to 2%. Without this investment income, the 2024 contribution increase, set at 13.4%, would have been even higher. This example illustrated how the financial strategies employed by GEMS ultimately alleviated cost burdens on members. She explained that during the COVID-19 pandemic (2022–2024), GEMS had intentionally kept contribution increases low to support Members through a challenging economic period. To achieve this, the Board had approved the use of R10 billion from scheme reserves, essentially using savings to offset member contributions. However, this strategy had reduced reserve levels, requiring a normalisation in 2025 to maintain the legally mandated 25% reserve ratio. The 13.4% increase for 2025 was thus a necessary correction to rebuild reserves and sustain the scheme’s long-term solvency.
Dr Moloabi then responded to questions about the affordability of GEMS for low-income public service employees. He clarified that many lower-income members could access fully subsidised cover through the Tanzanite One option, which allowed employees earning below a certain threshold to join GEMS without paying anything out of pocket, depending on the number of dependents added. To support access, GEMS employed client liaison officers (CLOs) in all nine provinces. These officers provided in-person education and assistance at pay points, workstations, and outreach events, ensuring that public servants were well-informed about how to access and use their benefits.
He acknowledged that some members, particularly those with significant healthcare needs, may experience exhausted benefits, especially if they selected lower-tier options not designed for high utilisation. This sometimes led to out-of-pocket costs and dissatisfaction. He said GEMS was actively reviewing benefit design and education strategies to help members select options aligned with their health profiles.
He also addressed a question comparing GEMS to other schemes. Based on the latest annual comparison, GEMS was on average 25% cheaper than comparable schemes before subsidies were applied, and up to 67% cheaper once the government subsidy was factored in. The CFO confirmed these figures.
Addressing ongoing concerns about outsourcing, Dr Moloabi clarified that GEMS was not a fully outsourced entity. He described GEMS’s growth from a small staff of under 10 people in 2006 to nearly 500 employees today, as part of a deliberate insourcing strategy over the years. Many core corporate functions, such as human resources (HR), finance, internal audit, information communication technology (ICT) and facilities (including security and cleaning services), had previously been outsourced, but were now fully insourced. The only functions that remain outsourced were claims processing, call centres, and managed care services, which required specialised infrastructure and were regulated by legislation. These services were performed by accredited administrators under strict oversight.
Dr Vuyo presented a timeline slide showcasing the insourcing milestones GEMS had achieved since 2014. These included:
Establishing internal healthcare networks and provider liaison teams;
Annual tariff negotiations with hospitals and laboratories;
Launch of alternative reimbursement models and value-based care initiatives; and
Expansion of internal analytics, telemarketing, and network management capabilities.
She emphasised that GEMS was not typical of medical schemes that outsource all administrative functions. Instead, the organisation actively seeks to build internal capacity and ensure strategic oversight over all service areas. As part of the 2024–2026 strategy, GEMS planned to further insource or co-source walk-in centres, integrated call centre functions, and network management operations. Dr Vuyo concluded by reaffirming the scheme’s commitment to responsible insourcing, operational efficiency, and equitable access to healthcare services for all public servants.
Dr Moloabi acknowledged that the earlier question regarding the Section 59 report had largely been addressed in his response to Mr Langa. He reiterated that GEMS was actively engaging with the systemic issues raised in the report. The scheme was involved in a Claims Risk Forum, which facilitated collaboration with the industry, and also participated in the Fraud, Waste, and Abuse Forum convened by the Council for Medical Schemes. These platforms enabled GEMS to remain proactive in implementing the recommendations of the advocate-led panel, and showed a firm commitment to being part of the solution.
Addressing the question on GEMS’s reserves and why these were not used to lower costs, he deferred to the CFO’s prior explanation, which clarified how the reserves had been leveraged to assist members.
In response to Ms Mchunu’s concern regarding benefit exhaustion and the absence of rollovers, he offered a detailed clarification. He said that only one type of medical scheme benefit option offered rollovers, namely, a savings plan. This type of plan allowed members to carry forward unused funds for day-to-day services like GP consultations and over-the-counter medications. However, most GEMS options operated on a pooled benefits model, where contributions funded a collective pool rather than being saved individually. He emphasised that members did not save up their contributions to spend later, but rather purchased risk coverage for unforeseen medical events.
Dr Moloabi gave an example. A member may contribute R5 000 monthly (R60 000 annually), but in the case of an intensive care unit (ICU) admission, actual costs could run into hundreds of thousands of rands. Such expenses were covered from the pooled fund. He stressed that if members frequently exhaust specific benefits, they should consider switching to options that provide more coverage in those areas. He acknowledged that misunderstandings around how medical aid works contributed to what was commonly known as the expectation gap. Educating members, he said, remained a key goal.
On the matter of consequence management involving the HPCSA, he said that when investigations confirmed misconduct by healthcare professionals, GEMS was required by its rules to report the matter to the appropriate statutory bodies. This occurred only once wrongdoing was definitively proven. He assured the Committee that GEMS complies with regulatory requirements, outsourcing only those services that it was not authorised to handle internally due to its status as a non-self-administered scheme. However, the scheme was actively working on an insourcing program to strengthen internal capacity.
On the issue of GEMS’s reach within the public sector, Dr Moloabi noted that the public service included about 1.2 million employees, while GEMS currently had over 880 000 members. He explained that part of the gap arises because some members were counted as spouses under principal members, affecting the total count. GEMS monitors the number of eligible public servants not enrolled in the scheme and actively promotes the value-for-money benefits offered. Still, he acknowledged that loyalty to longstanding alternative schemes remained a barrier for some employees.
Discussing waiting periods, he referred to a significant challenge faced in 2017, when GEMS's reserves had dropped to approximately 7% due to a rapid scheme growth and the absence of underwriting practices. The scheme had allowed unrestricted access to healthcare, which had led to anti-selection where individuals joined, claimed for treatment, and exited the scheme. To mitigate this, GEMS had introduced waiting periods in line with legislation:
A condition-specific waiting period (up to 12 months) for pre-existing conditions;
A general waiting period (up to three months) for other claims, with prescribed minimum benefit claims potentially covered sooner; and
Each case being reviewed individually to determine the applicable waiting period, all aimed at protecting the sustainability of the scheme.
Dr Moloabi addressed a concern about annual increases. He acknowledged the perception that GEMS's contribution increases exceeded public service salary increases. While public service salary adjustments may be around 7%, there was often a misunderstanding that any increase by GEMS nullified these gains. He explained that GEMS’s increases were based on medical price inflation (CPI-M) and utilisation rates, which had been consistently higher than budgeted. The scheme was working to align its increases more closely with the medical inflation index, and sought stakeholder support to encourage responsible claim behaviour without compromising access to appropriate clinical care.
Dr Vuyo addressed affordability concerns, stating that only 5% of GEMS members depleted their benefits annually, usually around August or September. To support them, GEMS provides ex gratia funding, non-entitlement-based grace funding, amounting to R90 million per quarter. Urgent applications (e.g., oncology) were processed within 48 hours, with an additional R40 million allocated for ratified urgent cases. GEMS also educates members on using generics, avoiding co-payments via contracted provider networks, and registering for chronic condition benefits.
In terms of value-added services, Dr Vuyo highlighted GEMS's inclusive definition of beneficiaries, accommodating extended family dependents, unlike other schemes that limit cover to immediate family only.
Further discussion
Mr Langa returned with investment-related concerns, specifically asking if any of the six fund managers contracted by GEMS were black-owned, and what explained the variance in asset allocation between them (e.g., Prudential vs Coronation).
Ms Tikana-Gxothiwe clarified her earlier statement, emphasising she had not accused GEMS of corruption or criminality, but had expressed a preference against outsourcing unless strictly necessary.
Mr J Malinga (MKP) raised three concise questions -- whether outsourcing decisions were made in consultation with members through trustees; clarification on a perceived R2 700 limit on the Emerald Value Option; and why prescription medication was allegedly excluded from the Beryl Option.
Dr Vuyo clarified that the Beryl Option did cover prescription medication. She said the R2 700 may reflect day-to-day limits, rather than full annual or chronic care benefits, which were substantially broader and included unlimited PMB cover.
Ms Van Lingen confirmed that all asset managers employed were B-BBEE Level 1 compliant and evaluated on ownership metrics during open tenders. Each manager must annually report how they contributed to transformation, particularly supporting black-owned asset managers. Asset allocation gaps were driven by an investment strategy balancing risk and return, rather than favouritism, with different managers handling different types of portfolios (e.g., income-focused vs. absolute return strategies).
Mr Langa voiced dissatisfaction with the lack of clarity regarding black-owned fund managers' B-BBEE compliance. He urged GEMS to revisit empowerment through genuine black ownership.
Deputy Minister Kekana supported deferring the issue as a work in progress, and recommended that GEMS return to update the Committee.
The meeting was adjourned.
