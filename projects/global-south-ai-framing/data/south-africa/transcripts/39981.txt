Meeting Summary
The Standing Committee on Public Accounts renewed its Memorandum of Understanding with the Special Investigating Unit and received a briefing from the Auditor-General of South Africa on the 2023/24 National and Provincial Audit Outcomes.
The revised Memorandum was designed to strengthen the relationship between the Unit and the Committee, enabling them to work more effectively together to address issues of governance and accountability across the public sector. It would allow both to continue their important work of enhancing oversight, improving governance, and tackling corruption and maladministration in the public sector. An added paragraph that would expedite the process of seconding the Unit’s officers to state institutions was of particular importance.
The Auditor-General then made a presentation on public sector financial management and audit outcomes. Members praised the Auditor-General’s office for the improvements in audit outcomes while acknowledging that significant challenges remained.
The Chairperson stressed the need to balance realistic expectations with systemic issues, and highlighted key areas for further oversight, including preventing financial loss, aligning budgets with objectives, and addressing capacity building in high-impact entities. He acknowledged the challenges faced by new leadership attempting to make systemic changes, as some issues were deeply ingrained and would take time to address.
Members raised concerns about the increasing number of late or non-submissions of financial statements, especially by state-owned enterprises. They asked about the impact of budget cuts on financial health, material irregularities in the school nutrition programme, and the alignment of performance targets with tangible service delivery objectives. They called for lifestyle audits of officials in supply chain management who seemed to be living beyond their means, and for particular scrutiny of the IT sector, Alexkor and entities which repeatedly failed to submit financial statements, such as the North West Transport Investment Group and Coastal TVET College.
The Chairperson called for further engagement with the Auditor-General to target high-risk areas and develop a strategic approach for oversight. The Auditor-General emphasised the importance of using the material irregularity process to hold entities accountable. She also encouraged SCOPA to focus on addressing financial governance and discipline, with a long-term view of improving public sector performance.
Meeting report
Signing of Memorandum of Understanding with the Special Investigating Unit
The Chairperson welcomed Adv Andy Mothibi, Head, Special Investigations Unit (SIU), along with his officials, to the meeting. He explained that the Standing Committee on Public Accounts (SCOPA) and the SIU were set to sign a Memorandum of Understanding (MOU). He noted that the MOU under consideration was not new but rather a renewal of an arrangement from the 6th Parliament. When the 7th Parliament had begun and the Committee had been constituted, it had reviewed its work and the previous arrangement to assess whether continuing the relationship was appropriate. The Committee had considered numerous presentations by the SIU, which detailed their work and demonstrated progress in addressing various issues such as maladministration, breaches of the law, and criminal activities. The value of the SIU to the Committee’s work was self-evident, making it logical to renew the arrangement. He emphasised that the terms of the MOU were designed to ensure cooperation in executing their respective constitutional mandates. He acknowledged that while the Committee wished to investigate and follow up on each case of corruption independently, it lacked the capacity, expertise, and time to do so. Therefore, the appropriate course of action was to identify an independent institution within the state, such as the SIU, to assist it in gathering the information necessary to take further action. This would also ensure that other state institutions received the information they needed to act in line with the Constitution. The SIU was a crucial partner in the Committee’s work, further justifying the necessity of the arrangement.
The Chairperson outlined the relationship between the Committee and the SIU for the benefit of members of the public who might be watching. He noted that the challenges encountered by South Africans, Parliament in general, and the Committee in particular often originated in reports by the Auditor-General of South Africa (AGSA) that highlighted breaches of supply chain rules, over-payments for contracts, project delays, and other irregularities. Additionally, such reports often revealed instances where assets purchased through procurement processes could not be located. The Chairperson further elaborated that while the Committee could summon individuals to Parliament repeatedly to answer these questions, this approach was insufficient. Therefore, a capable body was needed to investigate such matters in detail. Under the arrangement with the SIU, the Committee could refer its concerns to the SIU for investigation. The SIU would assess the information provided and determine whether there was sufficient cause to apply for a proclamation from the President to authorise an investigation. This proclamation would be reviewed and evaluated by the Department of Justice and Constitutional Development (DOJ) and the Presidency. Once all legal and procedural conditions were met the proclamation would take effect, enabling the SIU to proceed with its investigation. The Chairperson explained that once the SIU's findings were available, the Committee would use the information to summon officials or Ministers responsible for the relevant entity to account. This might include questioning whether anyone had been held accountable or, in cases involving a Minister, asking the President why action had not been taken against them.
The Chairperson emphasised that this collaborative arrangement was essential for addressing public sector irregularities and ensuring accountability at all levels of government. He thanked the officials from the SIU for their dedication to fulfilling their constitutional responsibilities and for their attendance and engagement in the meeting. He also extended his thanks to the advocacy team and the staff, acknowledging the challenges they faced in carrying out their work. He noted that the efforts of organisations like the SIU and the AGSA often put their staff at personal and professional risk, as those committing wrongdoings were frequently determined and dangerous. He emphasised the Committee's concern for the safety of the SIU and AGSA staff and urged them to report any threats or dangers they might face. He assured them that the Committee was a platform where they could raise such concerns. It was the Committee's responsibility to ensure that the Executive took the actions necessary to safeguard their ability to carry out their duties effectively and securely.
The Chairperson invited Adv Mothibi to present on behalf of the SIU.
Remarks by the SIU Head
Adv Mothibi expressed the SIU's gratitude for being invited to renew and re-sign the MOU, initially entered into at the end of the 6th Administration. It was an opportunity for the SIU to renew the agreement while also taking into account the additional measures needed going forward. The renewal was not just about strengthening the relationship but ensuring that matters from various state institutions were reported to SCOPA with findings and systemic recommendations which, if implemented, would improve administration practices, as well as prevent corruption, malpractice, and maladministration. The SIU was pleased to have contributed to the improvement of the MOU and thanked the Chairperson and the Committee for allowing them to provide input into the process. The improved MOU was designed to strengthen the relationship between the SIU and SCOPA, enabling them to work more effectively together to address issues of governance and accountability across the public sector. This MOU would allow both the SIU and SCOPA to continue their important work of enhancing oversight, improving governance, and tackling corruption and maladministration in the public sector.
Adv Mothibi indicated that the SIU was eager to continue supporting SCOPA in its monitoring and investigative roles. He drew attention to a new provision suggested by the SIU for inclusion in the MOU. It was an additional paragraph addressing the secondment of SIU members to various state institutions to undertake specific activities when required. This secondment provision was rooted in the enabling legislation governing SIU operations, which provided for SIU members to be seconded to state institutions through structured secondment agreements. He explained that the arrangement would expedite responses to matters referred by SCOPA. While the legislation already allowed the SIU to second members without a formal agreement, the inclusion of such agreements in the MOU would ensure smoother cooperation. He pointed out that in some cases state institutions might show reluctance, which could reflect underlying administrative challenges. In such instances, SCOPA’s support could encourage state institutions to cooperate. The secondment provision would enable the SIU to address matters promptly, even before a proclamation was formalised, as it would be able to gather information, identify irregularities, and prepare for full investigations in the interim. By the time the proclamation was issued, preliminary work would already have been completed, accelerating the whole process. Adv Mothibi viewed this provision as a significant improvement to the MOU.
Adv Mothibi noted that several matters referred by SCOPA to the SIU were already under consideration, such as the National Skills Fund and the intelligence report on ESKOM. The SIU was actively monitoring the status of proclamations, including the one for the National Skills Fund. He noted that recent proclamations had progressed to the Presidency through the Ministry of Justice and Constitutional Development, with some published as recently as that week. He anticipated the signing of the National Skills Fund proclamation and the others still in the pipeline.
Turning to prevention, Adv Mothibi stressed the need to strengthen corruption prevention efforts. These were lagging across government. He highlighted the importance of this aspect in the fight against corruption and linked it to the implementation of the MOU. The SIU had drafted a National Corruption Prevention and Risk Management Framework that would progress through the Justice, Crime Prevention and Security (JCPS) Cluster, Cabinet, and eventually public consultation. The framework was designed to support the National Anti-Corruption Strategy and would focus on systemic recommendations to address corruption risks. He drew attention to international standards set by the United Nations Convention Against Corruption, which emphasised prevention as a critical element. The corruption-fighting value chain would remain incomplete without this component. The SIU planned to use its systemic recommendations derived from investigations to enhance preventive measures. Once these recommendations are presented to SCOPA, the SIU would collaborate with state institutions to develop actionable programmes, monitor their implementation, and assess improvements in control environments. Adv Mothibi highlighted the importance of AGSA in corruption prevention, noting that the SIU also had an MOU with AGSA. He proposed that AGSA play an active role in assuring SCOPA and state institutions that control environments were improving. Stronger controls would mitigate irregular, fruitless and wasteful expenditure, ultimately enhancing service delivery.
Signing of the MOU
The Chairperson emphasised that SCOPA's distinguishing characteristic among parliamentary committees was its commitment to non-partisan work. The Committee refrained from considering which institution was involved or where it was based, to ensure that all inquiries and questions were guided solely by the interests of the South African people. He emphasised the importance of collaborating with independent institutions like the SIU to maintain public trust in the Committee's work. The public signing of the MOU symbolised the transparency and integrity of SCOPA's commitment to its mandate. The Committee would publicly account for the areas of work that arose from the commitments made in the MOU. This transparency would allow the public to assess progress or identify where it may be lacking.
The Chairperson and Adv Mothibi formally signed the MOU. Mr G Skosana (ANC) witnessed the agreement on behalf of the Committee while Adv Ntuthuzelo Vanara, Chief Legal Counsel, SIU, witnessed on behalf of the SIU. The signing was followed by a handshake which marked the formal commencement of the collaboration.
