Meeting Summary
National Empowerment Fund (NEF)
Industrial Development Corporation of South Africa [IDC]
The meeting of the Portfolio Committee on Trade, Industry and Competition focused on the presentations of the Industrial Development Corporation (IDC) and the National Empowerment Fund (NEF) regarding their annual performance plans.
The IDC presented its strategic focus on promoting industrial capacity, job creation and transformation, despite facing significant challenges such as decreased funding approvals and a drop in jobs expected to be created. Members of the Committee raised critical concerns about the IDC's effectiveness in achieving its mandate, particularly in industrialising the South African economy and addressing historical economic inequalities. Discussions highlighted the IDC's need to shift its focus to more modern industries, diversify its investment portfolio, and better align with macroeconomic policies to foster inclusivity and transformation. Governance issues, such as the long tenure of board members and the interim CEO's position, were also scrutinised.
The NEF outlined its role in driving black economic empowerment through funding black-owned businesses, particularly in rural and township areas. The Committee's discussion centred on the NEF’s impact on job creation, the need to increase its support for larger-scale ventures in the township economies, and the challenges it faced due to cash constraints and economic headwinds. Members questioned the NEF's funding strategies, its focus on smaller-scale businesses, and its plans to scale up successful initiatives like the Black Business Council's cash and carry model. The NEF's responses emphasised the importance of scaling up effective models and the need for more significant capital to sustain and expand its impact. They also addressed concerns about the need for better integration of youth and black entrepreneurs into the economy.
The meeting concluded with the Committee expressing the need for substantial and impactful actions from both the IDC and the NEF, stressing that the current approaches might not be sufficient to meet South Africa's industrial and economic transformation goals. Governance issues, and the need for strategic alignment with broader economic policies, were highlighted as critical areas for improvement.
Meeting report
The Chairperson officially opened the meeting, welcomed everyone, and apologised for the late start. The agenda was adopted, with Mr W Thring (ACDP) moving for adoption, and Mr T Chance (DA) seconding the motion.
Industrial Development Corporation: Annual Performance Plan 2024/25
Mr Isaac Malevu, Chief Financial Officer, Industrial Development Corporation (IDC) presented the annual performance plan for 2024/25 to 2026/27, focusing on its role as a state-owned development finance institution (DFI), established in 1940. The IDC's primary mandate was to promote industrial capacity development in South Africa and across Africa, with assets totalling R154.9 billion as of March 2024. The organisation offered a range of financial products, including debt, equity, and guarantees, tailored to support small and micro-enterprises, entrepreneurs, and projects that drive industrialisation.
The IDC's strategic focus remains on fostering industrial capacity while promoting job creation and transformation. The plan outlines efforts to support a greener economy, enhance partnerships, and strengthen organisational capabilities to ensure sustainable growth. However, the IDC faces challenges, including a 16% decrease in funding approvals and a 48% drop in jobs expected to be created, compared to the previous year. Despite these setbacks, the IDC has committed R22.3 billion to transformation funding, emphasising support for black-owned businesses, women entrepreneurs, and youth.
The economic outlook for South Africa remains modest, with gross domestic product (GDP) growth projected to rise from 0.8% in 2024 to 2.5% by 2029. Given the persistent high unemployment rate, the IDC recognises the critical need to stimulate economic growth. To address this, the IDC's key focus areas for 2024/25 to 2026/27 include sustainable industrialisation, supporting key network industries such as energy and logistics, and improving the performance of IDC subsidiaries. The plan also highlights efforts to diversify the economy, promote low-carbon transitions, and support regional value chains.
Financially, the IDC plans to allocate R71 billion over the next three years to key sectors such as energy, mining, and agro-processing. The organisation aims to maintain financial sustainability by managing risks associated with commodity price fluctuations and rising interest rates. The presentation also showcases several transformation case studies, where the IDC has funded black industrialists in various sectors, contributing to job creation and inclusive economic growth. Despite the economic challenges, the IDC remains committed to advancing sustainable and inclusive industrial development in South Africa.

Ms Nomavuso Mnxasana, Non-Executive Director, IDC,  addressed the topic of impairments, noting that as a DFI, the organisation inherently takes on more risks compared to commercial banks. While the goal was to reduce the level of impairments to an acceptable level, it was important to recognise that they would never be able to match the low impairment levels of commercial banks due to the nature of their operations. She said that SMMEs were more likely to face challenges related to non-performing loans because of their inherent vulnerabilities. However, the IDC was actively managing this risk. At every board meeting, they review the pipeline of deals -- examining which deals had come to the table, which had been approved or declined, and the reasons for those decisions.
She also addressed the question regarding the gap between approved and disbursed funds. She explained that there were often conditions precedent that must be fulfilled before disbursement could occur. These conditions could be related to governance, compliance with the Companies Act, or the Public Finance Management Act (PFMA). Even if a deal was approved, the funds would be disbursed only once these conditions were met. This careful approach was to ensure compliance and to mitigate potential risks.

National Empowerment Fund: Annual Performance Plan 2024/25
The National Empowerment Fund (NEF) presented its APP for 2024/25, outlining its role as a catalyst for broad-based black economic empowerment (B-BBEE) in South Africa. Established under the NEF Act of 1998, the NEF's mandate was to drive sustainable black economic participation through innovative investment and transformation solutions. The NEF was committed to promoting inclusive growth by funding black-owned businesses, with a focus on empowering black women, supporting rural and township development, and creating jobs.
The NEF's strategy was aligned with government objectives, emphasising the transformation of the South African economy, job creation, and supporting black industrialists. It offers financial and non-financial support to entrepreneurs, including business plan advice, mentorship, and post-investment services. The NEF's products and services cater to various sectors, providing funding from R250 000 to R75 million, with larger amounts managed through partnerships with other financial institutions.
In the 2023/24 financial year, the NEF achieved 86% of its performance targets, with notable successes in job creation, disbursements, and entrepreneurial training. However, challenges such as cash constraints and economic headwinds, including high-interest rates and subdued demand, had impacted its ability to meet its approved targets. It continued to play a crucial role in advancing black economic participation, with a strong emphasis on supporting black women entrepreneurs and businesses in rural and township areas.
The presentation also highlighted the NEF's impact through various case studies, demonstrating its commitment to job creation, women's empowerment, and supporting businesses in key sectors like manufacturing, tourism, and renewable energy. Despite the challenging economic environment, the NEF remained focused on its mission to drive sustainable economic growth and transformation in South Africa.

He said that in ten years, if the NEF could say they had created 20 large businesses that started small and now employed 40 000 to 50 000 people, that would be considered a success. However, he criticised the small-scale SMME philosophy, saying it originated from the West, where the economic base was already well-established, and questioned its effectiveness in the South African context. He believed it would always result in small-scale successes, with the bigger players remaining dominant.
He also raised the issue of non-financial interventions related to shelf access, questioning why, after identifying small-scale farmers and providing them with funds, deals were not in place with major retailers like Pick n Pay and Woolworths, to bring them in. Black property developers were another important area of investment, particularly in initiatives to re-gentrify buildings in cities like Cape Town and Johannesburg, which had been hijacked. He observed that very few of the property developers involved in these initiatives were black, which he saw as a significant investment opportunity. He also noted that a lot of gentrification was happening in Soweto and other areas.
He made two final points. First, he believed that there seemed to be a lack of knowledge or perhaps fear regarding digital industries, which were currently sustaining economies across the board. He urged them to find young people inventing in fields like artificial intelligence and digital technologies, as these areas had much greater potential to bring back money, and could even elevate the NEF to the size of the Industrial Development Corporation (IDC). He pointed out that the rapid escalation of digital technologies could generate significant returns, even if they employed fewer people, and that the focus should shift to redirecting funds towards more labour-absorptive and labour-intensive initiatives. He highlighted the importance of recognising the business capabilities of young people, some of whom might be as young as 18 or 22, and urged them to look at what was happening in countries like China and the US.
Finally, he suggested that institutions should not wait for politicians like Minister Parks Tau to take action. Instead, he encouraged them to produce and challenge through research and comprehensive papers on macroeconomic planning that would benefit their interests. He proposed that they come up with an industrial strategy, collaborate across entities, and present a paper to the ministry outlining what needed to happen in South Africa over the next 10 to 20 years to overcome economic stagnation and rising unemployment. He emphasised that the best minds should be involved in these solutions, and argued that it should not be left to the Treasury. He advised them to reposition themselves creatively and constructively against the Treasury, as they seemed to be passive in the debate, merely following Treasury's lead rather than taking a more vocal and bold stance on the macroeconomic development of South Africa.
Mr Thring asked about the percentage of investments the NEF directs towards rural and township economies, specifically those outside the established economic hubs. He questioned why the NEF had taken so long to move away from these hubs, especially considering the plan to disburse R300 million to businesses outside the five main metros. He stressed the importance of quality education in black economic empowerment, commenting that substandard education could cripple a nation's economy, whereas quality education could lead to a thriving economy. Given that black South Africans were the majority, he argued that providing quality education to every black child would, by virtue of numbers, lead to a black-owned economy. He asked what education initiatives the NEF had in place to improve the quality of education, particularly for black children.
He raised concerns about the NEF's projected 12% increase in employee compensation, noting that inflation was currently below 5%. He questioned the rationale behind this double-digit increase, especially when most employees faced austerity measures and inflation-linked salary increases. He pointed out that the NEF's largest budget expenditure was on salaries, amounting to R267.7 million for 182 staff members. He questioned how this large expenditure might undermine the NEF's financial ability to assist businesses and grow the economy. He noted a discrepancy between the NEF's reported salary expenditure (R267 million) and National Treasury's figure of R207 million, and asked for an explanation of this variance. He observed that while 95% of NEF employees were black, the board did not reflect the same diversity. He questioned why this blend of colours within the definition of "black" was not represented at the board level.
Lastly, he asked about the NEF's expected 7% return on investment (ROI) before impairments and how the ROI would look after impairments -- the reduction in the estimated value of assets. He also wanted to know how this ROI compared to other development finance institutions.
Mr Chance inquired about where the organisation sees itself within the ecosystem of private equity and venture capital, citing examples such as the South African Venture Capital Private Equity Association, which manages assets of about R200 billion, and was driven by the private sector. He said numerous venture capital funds existed, including the SAE fund, established by big business and government, which manages about R1.5 billion as a wholesale fund that works through retail venture capital (VC) funders. He acknowledged the common saying that "capital is colourblind" but noted that, in reality, there had not been much interest from established capital in investing in township businesses. He attributed this to several reasons, including the fact that most black people in South Africa had not been able to accumulate generational wealth over time, and therefore lacked the assets to back their own businesses, making access to finance the biggest issue.
He then referred back to his earlier question to the IDC about de-risking projects and blending finance, asking about the organisation's engagement with South African Local Government Association (SALGA) members to rethink the funding and non-funding support space. He posed a question about whether the organisation would consider shifting its focus from being a fund manager to providing pre-investment support and supporting the funding industry by helping small business owners become finance-ready. He observed that many impairments occurred because small business owners did not know how to manage money or how to prepare for a business properly. He suggested that there could be an argument for rethinking the entire funding landscape in the country and initiating a conversation with the private sector to explore how to scale and maximise resources, noting that government and business had different mandates. He also pointed out that while the organisation had many highly qualified employees, including those with BAs, MScs, and accounting qualifications, they were not necessarily investors. He suggested that it might be worthwhile to have a conversation about this.
He discussed the concept of the "rule of ten" in fund management, where a typical fund manager might have one or two investments that yield ten times the return, a few that perform moderately, and a couple that fail. He asked whether the organisation, when investing, keeps this concept in mind, and inquired about their exit strategy, specifically whether they had a term of investment such as three, five, or ten years, and how they execute their exits -- whether through a trade sale or by selling to entrepreneurs. He concluded by asking for clarification on how the organisation had managed to realise about R5 billion in exits.
Ms Zuma-Sambudla mentioned that the NEF was said to be not funded by the state, and wanted to know who was funding them and where they got their funds from. She also inquired about the companies that were more than 50% black-owned, asking how the organisation ensures that these companies were not using fronts. She wanted to know what remedies were in place if companies were found to be using fronts, and whether there were any penalties. Finally, she asked about the Jozini Tiger Lodge project, noting that it sounded very good and big. She questioned whether this project was owned by the NEF or by a black-owned company.
The Chairperson then provided some context to her questions, clarifying that they were aware that the NEF had initially been funded through demutualisation with the initial capital. However, he noted that the presenter had thanked the Deputy Director General (DDG) for making funds available, which were appropriated through the parliamentary process. Despite this, the NEF claims not to be state-funded. He wanted to ensure the presenter understood her questions clearly as they responded.
Mr Mkutu acknowledged that his colleagues had already addressed some of his questions and comments but wanted to add a few more points. He asked for a sense of the demographics, particularly concerning youth, and emphasised that this question should be understood in the context of the high level of youth unemployment in the country. He asked how the organisation ensured the integration of youth into its initiatives.
He also raised the issue of resource allocation, suggesting that instead of funding many small projects, there should be a refocus on creating a sustainable pipeline of projects that were of an appropriate scale. He stressed the importance of this approach, and linked it to the idea of shifting resources to create new economic centres, particularly in townships and rural areas, rather than concentrating solely on metropolitan areas. He highlighted the potential for unlocking significant economic value in rural provinces if resources were consistently invested in creating new opportunities for value creation in these areas, which could help with outward migration and the circulation of value, particularly in rural townships.
On the matter of technology, he mentioned the technological stations across the country run by the Department of Science and Technology through the National Research Foundation (NRF). He wondered to what extent there was coordination between the organisation and these technological stations, particularly for projects ready for commercialisation. He asked whether there was any coordination with small businesses, science, and innovation at this level.
Ms M Seate (ANC) began by referring to the organisation's products and deliverables, noting that part of what they did in incubation included providing training and financial literacy. Her first question was about the extent to which these services were provided, especially for what she described as "neurologically diverse young people," who carry innovative ideas but may require significant funding. She emphasised that these individuals often had unconventional ideas and solutions that might not be readily apparent or easily understood by others. She wanted to know how far the organisation goes in providing training and support for such individuals.
Her second question focused on the township revitalisation strategy, specifically referring to the success of the Black Business Council (BBC) cash and carry model in revitalising township spaza shops. She welcomed the impact this strategy had on township revitalisation, and suggested that this model could be applied nationally. She asked whether the organisation could identify economically depressed areas, such as those in the Free State, North West and Eastern Cape, and turn this approach into a working model that government could recognise as a solution for revitalisation and grounding entrepreneurs.
Lastly, she emphasised the need for a monitoring and evaluation tool to gauge successful stories, and to identify where intervention was needed. She stressed the importance of having a system to determine which strategies work and which do not, through research and tangible scientific data analysis that could provide long-term investment predictions. She wanted to know if the organisation had monitoring, evaluation and research components in place to support this effort.
Mr Gigaba began by addressing the organisation's need for funding, asking how much funding they ideally required. He pointed out that while the organisation needed funding and had been able to leverage some resources and forge partnerships, there had been talks of a potential merger with the IDC due to weaknesses in the organisation's balance sheet. He asked for clarification on this matter, noting that there would be an appropriate time to engage further on the idea of a merger. He expressed concern that the NEF and IDC mandates were vastly different, and merging them might not be advisable. He emphasised that what they expected the IDC to do was very different from what the organisation was currently doing, so suggesting that bringing the two together would be ill-considered.
His second point was a comment regarding top management. He expressed the hope that the issue of vacancies would not recur, mentioning that this concern had been raised with virtually every entity. He suggested that perhaps the Chairperson should consider having a committee focused specifically on governance, not only within the entities, but also within the Department, as he sees this as a gap that needs addressing.
Finally, he made a point about the importance of strategic and deliberate interventions to support economic empowerment, drawing a parallel with the Afrikaner economic movement from 1922 onwards, which was a bold and strategic effort to bring Afrikaner capital on board. He connected this with another Member's earlier comments, emphasising the need for the organisation to shift from being passive recipients of policy to becoming deliberate, intentional, and active advocates of a macroeconomic framework that supports their work. He asked, if there were to be successful interventions to build and empower black businesses, what would the organisation like to see happen? He clarified that he was not looking for an answer that simply asked for more money, as he believed money alone did not solve the problem. Instead, he wanted to know what range of interventions, from the structure of the economy to ownership patterns, value chains, and institutions like legislation that support black economic empowerment, the organisation would want to see in place to support their work.
The Chairperson began by noting that the Committee was trying to probe further into the core of what the NEF does. He asked for a clear and concise statement that could be communicated to others, summarising the NEF's primary function into one clear point. This was important to ensure that the distinction between the NEF's work and other entities was understood. He then moved on to discuss critical industries, referring to the BBC cash and carry model mentioned in the presentation. He described it as a bulk buying system, a concept that had been in demand because the majority of South Africans were easily displaced in the market. He mentioned that the Committee had previously asked the Competition Commission whether they had conducted studies on the existence of what seemed to be a "Mafia network" in bulk buying, which made it difficult for local competitors to compete, particularly at the level of spaza shops. Now that this issue had been acknowledged, he posed the question of whether it would be possible to spread such a model across the country to stimulate economic activities at the local level.
He also raised concerns about property space, specifically mentioning Infrastructure SA, which had huge plans, projects, and state budgets. He inquired about the NEF's interaction with Infrastructure SA to support those in the infrastructure and built environment sectors, highlighting that the state had the resources to enable and support such endeavours.
He asked about the relationship between the failure rate and the collection rate observed by the NEF, wanting to understand the connection between these two metrics. He also inquired about the mechanisms in place for follow-ups, particularly in cases where there have been failures.
Lastly, he brought up the topic of industrial parks and revitalisation, noting that many industrial parks, such as those in the textile sector, had collapsed and been repurposed, with some even becoming churches. He questioned the role of the NEF in ensuring participation not just in service industries, but also in the production side of the economy.
He asked for the NEF to elaborate on these issues and address the questions posed by the Members, noting that they aimed to conclude the session in about ten minutes. He also mentioned that while some responses could be provided in writing later, he hoped the NEF could address as many questions as possible in the remaining time.
NEF's response
Dr Nthabiseng Moleko, NEF Chairperson, began by addressing the overarching question raised by the Chairperson, asking what the one thing was that the NEF wanted the Committee to ensure was done. She said that based on their research and analysis, the NEF aimed to "massify and scale up the things that work," specifically highlighting the BBC cash and carry model. The NEF had recently visited Limpopo to observe the success of this model and its impact on black businesses. The model provided a point of sale for black business owners and allowed them to source at scale, benefiting even those financed by the Land Bank. This arrangement enabled entrepreneurs to make margins and profits that ensured their survival, despite unfavourable terms in retail that often challenge their working capital and cash flow sustainability. She emphasised that this model was effective and should be scaled up across the country.
To answer a related question, she mentioned that they had conducted a strategic analysis to determine the funding required to scale up their efforts, arriving at a figure of R4.4 billion for the year. This amount was necessary not only for current sustainability, but also for ensuring the NEF's long-term growth and impact. She expressed dissatisfaction with the current job numbers, noting that the job crisis was far worse than the 12 million unemployed people typically cited, as it excluded discouraged work seekers. She argued that scaling up their efforts would significantly improve the employment situation.
She also clarified the distinction between state funding, the NEF's balance sheet, and their funding model. The NEF had been capitalised at its inception with just over R2 billion, but since 2010, it had not received additional allocations from its primary funder, the DTIC. Instead, the NEF had focused on raising third-party funds through engagements with banks, departments, and various sectors to compensate for the reduced allocations. She highlighted that these third-party funds were often tied to specific mandates and had to be used for particular interventions, with strict adherence to beneficiary requirements. Looking ahead, she explained that the NEF's ability to allocate funds depended on the capital they collected, including third-party funds. She acknowledged that the allocation of capital from the DTIC or other departments was not guaranteed, which had necessitated their proactive efforts to raise funds from alternative sources.
She concluded by stressing the importance of an evidence-based strategy for informing sectoral development. She identified sectors such as small-scale agriculture, small-scale manufacturing, retail, infrastructure, and renewable energy as potential game-changers for the country, especially in distressed provinces. However, she noted that capital constraints had slowed progress, referencing a period in 2014/15 when the NEF had no money to allocate, leading to moratoriums. To address these challenges, the NEF strengthened its human capacity to raise funds and partnered with the private sector, as exemplified by its efforts with the Solidarity Fund earlier in the year. Their goal was to use blended finance to de-risk black-owned businesses and maximise the impact of available capital.
Mr Mziwabantu Dayimani, Acting Chief Executive Officer, NEF, began by expressing agreement with the Committee's comments on the township economy, highlighting that the NEF was actively exploring ways to increase the manufacturing capability of township businesses. He explained that they had chosen to focus on vehicle bodies, or the secondary market for vehicles, because it represented a low-hanging fruit. Although they were engaging with original equipment manufacturers, they were aware of the high entry requirements and significant capital needed for that sector. Their approach was to start with the more accessible secondary market and gradually develop businesses within the OEM value chains, eventually leading them into the original manufacturing space.
He also agreed with the Committee's sentiments about the importance of property as a sector, noting that the NEF had a strong property portfolio and saw more opportunities in this space. He acknowledged that while they had had engagements with Infrastructure SA, they had not yet fully concretised their relationship, but they planned to take this up to capitalise on available opportunities.
On the topic of compensation, he mentioned that the NEF had capped salary increases at 6% for the current year, aligning with government's benchmark. He asked for more information on the specific numbers the Committee Member had referred to so that they could provide a more detailed response. He also addressed the issue of the NEF's R267 million concerning the number of employees, explaining that their approach involved hiring employees for specific opportunities, with contracts that may not be renewed after a certain period. He promised to unpack these details further and provide a written response to the Committee.
Regarding the venture capital ecosystem, he clarified that the NEF focuses on market failures, distinguishing their approach from venture capital players who typically fund de-risked projects. While the entity did engage with venture capital players and was a member of the VC body, it adopted different business models that were more aligned with its developmental mandate. He also touched on their exit strategy, explaining that when the NEF exits a business, they typically sell to the partners involved in the transaction, aiming to ensure value for those partners rather than maximising returns as commercial funders might.
He briefly mentioned the Jozini transaction, noting that the two main players were based in KwaZulu-Natal and that the community was also a shareholder, making it a broad-based transaction.
Lastly, he clarified the issue of funding, reiterating that the NEF had initially been allocated R2.4 billion as recapitalisation from National Treasury when it was established. However, since then, they have not received formal recapitalisation from National Treasury. Instead, they had received project-based funding through the DTIC for specific initiatives, such as the township economy fund and the distressed businesses fund during the COVID period. He emphasised that the NEF had had to learn to self-sustain, particularly for its operations, as the funds they raised were typically designated for specific projects and could not be used for general operations. He concluded by mentioning their collaboration with IDC and the Black Economic Empowerment (BEE) Commission to address various funding issues within the BEE family.
Closing remarks
Ms Susan Mangole, Acting Deputy Director-General (DDG): Incentives, DTIC, began by expressing gratitude for the opportunity to discuss the two institutions together, and highlighted the importance of the Committee's inputs. She pointed out that many of the other players' funding came primarily from their own balance sheets. The goal, she emphasised, was to maintain the ability to raise money through other policy initiatives, such as those related to BEE and the Competition Commission, to reduce reliance on state funding.
She then clarified the innovation programmes within the Department, explaining that there were two main programmes designed to support innovation. The first programme focuses on technology development and research, specifically through the Technology Human Resource for Industry programme, which was supported by grants. The second programme, managed by the IDC, was responsible for commercialising these innovations. Once the research leads to a prototype, the IDC or other players step in to take the innovation to the commercialisation stage.
Finally, she acknowledged the Committee's suggestion about collaboration between institutions. She said they would take this message back and discuss it with the chief economists of the relevant institutions to identify emerging industries, and explore new ways of working together to create a more significant impact.
The Chairperson began by highlighting the need to support entrepreneurs funded by both the IDC and the NEF, particularly by exploring the space of incentives. He pointed out that this space seemed to be reserved for a few, but the resources available through these incentives could be vital in addressing funding challenges. He suggested that the NEF consider the downstream industries associated with the growing trend toward electric vehicles. He mentioned several areas of focus, including charging stations, battery infrastructure, smart grids, and fleet electric services. He also emphasised the importance of focusing on technology and gaming industries and the property space, and advocated creating shelf space for entrepreneurs to compete with larger entities.
He requested that the rest of the responses be provided in writing, so they could be shared with the Committee Members. He expressed gratitude to the IDC, the NEF, and the DTIC for their participation and hoped the comments made during the meeting would be beneficial. He stressed the Committee's commitment to engaging vigorously and doing things differently to achieve better results.
Finally, he mentioned that he would attend a Competition Commission conference the next day. Due to a standing rule, the Committee needed to appoint someone to act as chairperson during his absence, stating that they had consulted legally to ensure this was the correct course of action.
Mr M Gigaba (ANC) was appointed as Acting Chairperson for the Committee meeting on Wednesday, 4 September, following his nomination by Mr Mkutu and seconded by Ms Seate.
The meeting was adjourned.
