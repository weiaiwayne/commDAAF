Annual Reports 2024/25
The Select Committee on Education and Creative Industries (National Council of Provinces) met to consider the 2024/25 annual report and audit outcomes of the Department of Science and Technology and Innovation (DSTI).
The Department reported on its key annual achievements, including the launch of the Nuclear Medicine Research Infrastructure (NuMeRI) facility, progress on its Vaccine Innovation and Manufacturing Strategy, and the achievement of a fifth consecutive clean audit.
The Auditor-General of South Africa (AGSA) confirmed that the Department and its major entities had received clean audits with strong financial controls, while noting compliance issues at the Technology Innovation Agency (TIA) and urging improved achievement of service delivery targets.
The Department reported an 85% achievement of its annual performance targets, detailing major investments in human capital development, support for small enterprises and grassroots innovators, and expanding international partnerships.
Committee Members raised questions concerning the development of local technical skills for the Square Kilometre Array (SKA) project, the placement of postgraduate students, the commercialisation rate of supported innovators, and internal control improvements. Concerns were also raised about procurement from persons with disabilities and the impact of late funding approvals.
The Department outlined plans for skills transfer linked to the SKA project, initiatives to better track graduate outcomes, and mechanisms to monitor supported enterprises. It committed to providing quarterly reports on addressing the AGSA’s findings and to submit written answers to outstanding questions within seven days.
The Committee extended the deadline for adopting its draft oversight report by one week, encouraged Members to review its recommendations, and mandated the Committee Secretary to arrange a visit to the NuMeRI facility. The Committee also agreed to convey its support to a hospitalised Member.
Meeting report
Adoption of the agenda
The Chairperson greeted all attendees, extending his greetings to the Minister and officials from the Department of Science and Technology and Innovation (DSTI), as well as to Committee Members. He asked the Committee Secretary, Mr Andisa Kula, to display the meeting agenda.
Ms N Du Plessis (DA, Gauteng) moved for the adoption of the agenda. She noted an error regarding the meeting time, as the meeting had been postponed by 30 minutes, and proposed that discussions end at 12:30 instead of 12:10 as originally stated.
Ms T Breedt (FF+, Free State) seconded the motion to adopt the agenda with this amendment.
Apologies/Early departure
Dr Blade Nzimande, Minister of Science, Technology and Innovation, interjected to request permission to leave the meeting early due to a bereavement in his family, of which he had just been informed.
The Chairperson acknowledged the request, expressed the Committee’s heartfelt condolences to the Minister and his family, and granted permission for him to leave early.
Following the apologies, the Chairperson confirmed that the Committee would engage appropriately to convey its support to Ms J Adriaanse (DA, North West), who was in hospital.
He noted that she had been very engaged in efforts to improve services and that the Committee would follow up with her.
Mr M Mokwele (ANC, Limpopo) moved for the adoption of the apologies, and the motion was seconded by Ms D Fienies (ANC, Northern Cape).
The Chairperson thanked the Members and handed over to the Minister, acknowledging the bereavement he was facing.
Minister’s opening remarks
Minister Nzimande thanked the Chairperson, Committee Members, and other participants, and expressed his appreciation for the Chairperson’s kind words and the opportunity to present the Department of Science and Technology and Innovation’s (DSTI) Annual Report for 2024/25. He stated that science, technology and innovation were essential for a country to meet its developmental goals.
Minister Nzimande explained that the DSTI’s work was guided by its 2019 White Paper on Science, Technology and Innovation (STI) and its 10-Year Plan for STI (2022-2032). He said the Director-General (DG) and his team would present key departmental activities informed by these policies.
Minister Nzimande highlighted several key departmental achievements. He noted that through the Mandela Mining Precinct (MMP), the DSTI had delivered groundbreaking technologies, including sensors to detect loose rock formations at a safe distance.
He reported that through the Agricultural Bioeconomy Innovation Partnership Programme (ABIPP), the DSTI had leveraged over R300 million and expanded farmer support to deepen the impact of agricultural innovation.
Minister Nzimande stated that in May 2024, together with the University of Pretoria (UP) and other partners, the DSTI had launched the Nuclear Medicine Research Infrastructure (NuMeRI) facility at Steve Biko Academic Hospital. He described NuMeRI as a state-of-the-art facility, the only one of its kind in Africa, intended to advance drug development and clinical research for cancer, tuberculosis and other diseases. The DSTI had contributed R720 million to the facility, which was one of 13 research infrastructure platforms. He invited the Committee to visit.
He said that through its Vaccine Innovation and Manufacturing Strategy (VIMS), the DSTI aimed to promote the domestic design and development of vaccines. He clarified that the goal was for VIMS to oversee the entire venture from research to production.
Minister Nzimande noted that through the Hydrogen Society Roadmap, the Platinum Valley Initiative, and the Green Hydrogen Commercialisation Strategy, the DSTI was intensifying efforts to support energy security and enable a just transition.
He reported that significant progress had been made in developing Fourth Industrial Revolution (4IR) capabilities. The DSTI entity, the National Advisory Council on Innovation (NACI), had initiated the development of a national artificial intelligence strategy for South Africa, which was being finalised with the Department of Communications and Digital Technologies (DCDT).
Minister Nzimande said that in response to the withdrawal of United States funding for key scientific projects, he had appointed a working group to advise on the impact. The DSTI was preparing to take the working group’s report to Cabinet and then to Parliament.
On the DSTI’s performance, Minister Nzimande reported that the Department had received an unqualified audit opinion without findings—a clean audit—for the fifth consecutive year, with no irregular, fruitless or wasteful expenditure. Total procurement expenditure on goods and services from designated groups amounted to R89 million.
He expressed concern about underspending on transfers and subsidies to some entities and stated his intention to meet with each entity’s board and senior management to address this. The presentation would also detail work on developing human capabilities and expanded cooperation with African and Global South partners.
Minister Nzimande concluded that while the DSTI was pleased with its overall performance, there were concerning areas which he, the DG and the executive were addressing.
The Chairperson thanked Minister Nzimande for his consistent engagement. On the visit to the NuMeRI facility, he said the Committee’s administration would make arrangements with the DSTI. He informed Minister Nzimande that the Committee would now engage with the departmental officials.
Briefing by Auditor-General of South Africa (AGSA) on Department of Science and Technology and Innovation (DSTI) 2024/25 audit outcomes
Mr Solomon Jiyana, Senior Audit Manager: Science, Technology and Innovation, AGSA, reported that all DSTI portfolio entities – the Council for Scientific and Industrial Research (CSIR), the Human Sciences Research Council (HSRC), and the National Research Foundation (NRF) – achieved clean audits for the 2024/25 financial year, demonstrating strong financial and performance reporting controls. Section 43 entities also performed well, although the Technology Innovation Agency (TIA) had compliance findings and required material adjustments.
Mr Jiyana stated that internal controls and governance structures, including internal audit units and audit committees across the portfolio, were assessed as effective. This contributed to stable institutional capability, which was further strengthened by the appointment of a new DG for the DSTI from 1 June 2025.
He reported a mixed outcome on the achievement of key performance targets. The CSIR and HSRC achieved 100% of their targets, while the NRF achieved 67% and the DSTI achieved 70%. The AGSA urged the portfolio to prioritise meeting its service delivery targets.
He noted an improvement in tracking the impact of postgraduate funding, with the NRF and DSTI implementing new monitoring mechanisms. The AGSA’s own monitoring showed that thousands of graduates had entered the labour market and that significant work opportunities had been created.
Mr Jiyana highlighted the portfolio’s substantial contribution to local economic benefits, including over 212 Northern Cape suppliers receiving more than R1 billion in project-related opportunities between 2021 and 2025.
He reported that irregular expenditure had increased to R19 million, largely due to pending condonation decisions, although most cases had been disclosed. The AGSA highlighted the need for strengthened consequence management, especially within the TIA.
Mr Jiyana recommended that the Department address internal control weaknesses (particularly in the TIA), monitor the implementation of action plans, track the impact of funded research and students, and ensure performance targets and service delivery commitments were consistently met.

Briefing by Department of Science and Technology and Innovation (DSTI) on its 2024/25 annual report
Dr Mlungisi Cele, DG, DSTI, and Mr Robert Shaku, Chief Financial Officer (CFO), presented the Department’s annual report.
The Department reported that all DSTI portfolio entities received clean audits, except for the Technology Innovation Agency (TIA), which had compliance issues and required material adjustments.
Dr Cele stated that the Department achieved 85% of its overall performance, meeting 58 of its 68 targets. This represented an improvement compared to the 2022/23 financial year, though it was slightly below the previous year’s achievement. He noted that internal controls and governance structures were found to be effective across the portfolio.
He reported that performance target achievement was uneven across entities. The CSIR and the HSRC achieved 100% of their targets, while the NRF and the DSTI itself achieved lower percentages.
Significant progress was noted in tracking the impact of postgraduate funding, showing strong graduate outputs and the creation of work opportunities, with thousands of students supported through NRF and related programmes.
Dr Cele highlighted major investment in human capital development, detailing that the DSTI had funded 1 978 Doctor of Philosophy (PhD) and 4 293 honours and master’s students, with the majority being Black South Africans, women, and youth. A further 715 graduates were placed in work preparation programmes.
Support was provided to 51 small, medium and micro enterprises (SMMEs), 160 grassroots innovators, and 20 technology startups through partnerships such as the Empire Partner Foundation (EPF), resulting in tangible job and income creation.
On technology localisation, he reported successes including the development of a Learner Positioning Unit (LPU) box with 70% local content and demonstration pilots with Eskom.
Dr Cele outlined growing international collaborations in artificial intelligence (AI), intellectual property (IP) protection, sustainable energy, and scientific mobility. This included cooperation with 29 African Union (AU) and 40 European Union (EU) partners, alongside expanded cooperation with China across information and communication technology (ICT), space, astronomy, mining technology, and life sciences.
A vacancy-filling target of 90% was not met, with the Department achieving 78%. Dr Cele attributed this shortfall to delays in obtaining concurrence from the Department of Public Service and Administration (DPSA).
Mr Shaku reported that the Department spent 96.1% of its R9.44 billion budget. Underspending was mainly due to procurement delays at the South African National Space Agency (SANSA), which had prompted a rollover request.
He confirmed the DSTI had maintained a clean audit outcome for three consecutive years. The only irregular expenditure, amounting to R861 000, was historic and was awaiting condonation from National Treasury.
The presenters concluded by referring the Committee to the attached presentation slides for more details on the DSTI’s Annual Report for 2024/25.

Discussion
The Chairperson noted that technicians for the Square Kilometre Array (SKA) and MeerKAT projects were sourced from abroad, a situation similar to other projects requiring imported specialised skills. He inquired how soon South Africa could develop its own local capacity to build and maintain the telescopes to end reliance on foreign expertise.
The Chairperson asked if there was a plan to place postgraduate students to ensure their enhanced skills were utilised. He questioned how many of the 160 supported grassroots innovators had commercialised their work and how the Department monitored the use of funds provided to these innovators.
Ms Plessis wanted to know what specific measures were being taken to improve internal controls and efficiency in demand and supply chain management, as highlighted by the Auditor-General of South Africa (AGSA).
Ms Du Plessis congratulated the Department on its unqualified audit outcome but asked about bursary student performance and pass rates, and whether the programme delivered a satisfactory return on investment.
Ms Du Plessis questioned why there was a low percentage of persons with disabilities reflected in both expenditure and involvement with small, medium and micro enterprises (SMMEs).
Ms Du Plessis asked if all the supported SMMEs were operational and sustainable, and stressed the importance of transparency regarding which companies the government partnered with.
Ms Du Plessis congratulated the Department again on the improved return from the EPF collaboration; however, she asked if any additional e-health or e-government innovations were being developed. She wanted to know what accountability measures were in place for the coding teaching initiative, noting that some provinces were hindering its progress.
Ms M Kennedy (EFF, Limpopo) sought clarity on whether the late approval of funding by the National Research Foundation (NRF) and professional registration by the South African Council for Natural Scientific Professions (SACNaSP) had caused any negative impact.
Responses
Mr Imraan Patel, DDG: Research Development and Support, addressed the question on skills transfer. He clarified that the MeerKAT telescope remained a fully South African-funded and procured project. The MeerKAT extension was a partnership with Germany, while the full Square Kilometre Array (SKA) project fell under the jurisdiction of the SKA Observatory (SKAO), which had different procurement rules. The DSTI had managed these arrangements effectively, though skills transfer opportunities on some turnkey instrumentation in the extension were limited.
He reported that major infrastructure work for the SKAO was underway under a contract with a Chinese company, as China was a member state, and the DSTI worked closely with its counterparts on skills transfer. He noted that South African labour involvement would increase as more dishes were built, though Chinese engineers performed the initial technical work on the first dishes.
Mr Patel stated the multi-year project was a priority for embedding technology and skills transfer. Beyond technical skills, the DSTI focused on broader human capital development through the SKA’s bursary programme, which supported postgraduates, technicians, and artisans from local communities, including those with strong mathematics and science backgrounds.
He highlighted a specific need for diesel mechanics to maintain vehicles on the large site, as petrol vehicles interfered with the telescopes. The DSTI was prioritising artisan training through a Technical and Vocational Education and Training (TVET) college partnership to develop this skill locally. He assured the Committee that skills development, skills transfer, and supplier development were being coordinated with the South African Radio Astronomy Observatory (SARAO).
Mr Patel explained the systemic difficulty in tracing all students after they left tertiary institutions, acknowledging the AGSA’s previous findings on the matter. He stated that the DSTI was implementing plans to improve tracking and noted that targeted programmes, like the SKAs, showed positive outcomes where most beneficiaries continued studies or found employment. He added that the DSTI was establishing alumni networks and enhancing its IT systems to better track graduate outcomes—a key policy requirement for growing a diverse research and innovation system.
Dr Mmboneni Muofhe, DDG: Socio-Economic Innovation Partnerships, responded to the question on grassroots innovators. He stated that the DSTI could not yet provide the number of commercialised companies from the last financial year’s cohort, as most were still in the development phase. The DSTI selected innovators with commercial potential and provided funding and technical support, such as through technology stations, to advance them toward the market. Tracer studies were being initiated to monitor their progress.
Dr Muofhe explained that the DSTI, primarily through the Council for Scientific and Industrial Research (CSIR), supported digitisation initiatives such as e-government innovations. He described the Municipality Innovation Maturity Index, a partnership with the University of KwaZulu-Natal (UKZN), which assessed municipalities' readiness to adopt innovative solutions to improve service delivery, in support of the national District Development Model (DDM).
He said monitoring of monitoring of supported enterprises occurred through quarterly and annual reports and regular review meetings, which allowed the DSTI to identify struggling ventures. Tracer studies helped track long-term progress, and funding could be discontinued for enterprises not meeting their milestones, acknowledging that not all funded businesses would succeed.
Mr Shaku addressed the AGSA’s findings on internal controls within supply chain management. He confirmed that the Department required quarterly progress reports from its entities, including the CSIR, NRF, and Technology Innovation Agency (TIA), on rectifying these issues and would share the updates with the Committee.
He reported that spending on procurement from persons with disabilities had improved from 2% to over 4% but remained challenging. He cited difficulties in finding suitably registered suppliers on the Central Supplier Database (CSD), which required Companies and Intellectual Property Commission (CIPC) registration, annual financial statements, and South African Revenue Service (SARS) compliance.
Ms Gugulethu Zwane, DDG: Institutional Planning and Support, addressed the impact of late approvals. She acknowledged that delays had resulted in non-compliance with Treasury regulations, as noted by the AGSA. She attributed this to the unusual late approval of the Medium-Term Expenditure Framework (MTEF) but committed to better planning and engagement with entities to prevent recurrence.
Dr Cele offered to provide written responses to any outstanding questions.
The Chairperson agreed to this proposal and requested that the DSTI submit expanded written answers within seven days. He thanked the Department and the AGSA for their comprehensive and valuable reports, which were essential for the Committee’s oversight role and for ensuring legislative compliance.
Committee matters
Dr Solomon Mthombeni, Content Advisor, explained that the draft oversight report, which had been shared, was approximately 70 pages long. As the Committee had conducted its oversight visit over the preceding two weeks, it was decided to extend the deadline for adopting the report by seven days. The report was scheduled for consideration and adoption the following Tuesday.
He encouraged Members to review the report and to email him or message the Committee’s WhatsApp group with any additional information that should be included. He highlighted that the most critical elements of the report were its recommendations for the Department and the Nelson Mandela Bay (NMB) Municipality, which included deadlines for action, and urged Members to examine them closely.
The Chairperson concurred, stating that thorough reflection was necessary to ensure the report represented the Committee’s collective work and could be adopted without contention.
The Chairperson asked Mr Kula to lead communications with the Department to arrange the oversight visit to the NuMeRI facility and to follow up with Ms Adriaanse who was hospitalised.
He stressed the need for all Members to be prepared for their tasks and affirmed the Committee’s moral support for Ms Adriaanse.
The Chairperson thanked the Members for their participation.
Meeting adjourned.
