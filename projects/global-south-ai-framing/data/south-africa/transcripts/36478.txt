Meeting Summary
The Committee met with the Department of Communications and Digital Technologies (DCDT) to receive a consolidated presentation on the performance of its entities during the first two quarters of the 2022/23 financial year. The Committee was also briefed on the performance of two entities reporting to the Presidency, the Government Communication and Information System and the Media Development and Diversity Agency.
The South African Post Office (SAPO) reported that it had missed its revenue target of R1.166 billion by R538 million, and even though its expenditure of R1.350 billion was below the projection by R614 million, its financial situation remained dire. Total liabilities amounted to R8.2 billion, made up of R3.2 billion owed to the Postbank and R5 billion to unpaid creditors.
The SABC attributed its poor performance to declines in audiences, lack of adequate marketing for newly acquired content, and procurement delays affecting its digital projects. Loadshedding was leading to losing viewers and thwarting attempts to attract advertisers. TV licence fees were 39% below budget due to debt collection agencies (DCA) not meeting collection targets. The risk of its ability to operate as a going concern remained high.
The State Information Technology Agency (SITA) indicated that it was in a healthy financial position, as it could cover both its long-term and short-term liabilities. However, it had 151 findings from the 2020/21 Auditor-General's (AG's) report. The audit results reflected a financially qualified audit opinion, with findings on compliance with applicable legislation for the past two financial years. The main root causes for the qualification were supply chain management processes, property, plant and equipment, and intangible assets. However, SITA's internal audit had closed 85 of the 151 findings.
Members' questions were focused on the entities' needs to address the AGSA findings, issues relating to vacancy rates and unfilled positions, and reasons for poor performance. The effect of the delayed appointment of the SABC board on the corporation's performance was raised, with Members asking if the SABC’s turnaround strategy would be considered for revision, and what the powers of the chief executive officer were in the absence of the board. They also wanted to know if the R2.4 billion bailout for SAPO would be utilised to repay its historic debt or implement its turnaround strategy.

South African Post Office (SAPO)
Quarter one
Of the 13 planned targets, six key performance indicators (KPIs) were achieved (46%) which was a 21% improvement compared to the prior quarter’s performance. The  R628 million revenue was below the budget of R1.166 billion by R538 million (46%).
Expenditure amounted to R1.350 billion, which was below the projection by R614 million (31%). Staff costs of R866 million were below projection by R360 million (29%). Salaries amounted to over 68% of total expenses. Expenses in both quarters continued to outstrip revenue. SAPO’s cash flow position remained dire, as the entity continued to struggle to meet its financial obligations.
Quarter two
Seven KPIs were achieved (54%) which was a 17% improvement from the previous quarter. SAPO posted revenues of R686 million, which was R503 million (42%) below budget. It had decreased from the prior year’s revenue by R153 million (18%). The year-to-date revenue was R1.314 billion which was outstripped by the R2.678 billion year-to-date expenditure.
The financial situation remained dire. Total liabilities amounted to R8.2 billion, including R3.2 billion owed to the Postbank and R5 billion to unpaid creditors. The R5 billion had increased from the R4.7 billion reported in Q1, and this amount included unpaid statutory obligations.
South African Broadcasting Company (SABC)
Quarter One
The SABC’s poor performance was driven by declines in audiences, lack of adequate marketing for newly acquired content, and procurement delays affecting digital projects. It was still experiencing challenges in filling vacancies. There were 489 vacancies resulting in a cost saving of R42.5 million on employee costs at the end of the quarter. There was still no over-the-top (OTT) revenue collected at the end of Q1. Declining audience shares and poor advertising revenue performance exposed the SABC to a great going concern risk.
Quarter two
The declining audience remained the primary factor that resulted in the overall poor performance outcome from non-financial and financial perspectives. The SABC was unable to secure its targeted advertising revenues. There were 492 vacancies, translating into a vacancy rate of 18.2% at the end of September. It launched its OTT platform on 17 November, which was expected to yield revenue with effect from the next quarter. TV licence fees were 39% below budget due to debt collection agencies (DCAs) not meeting collection targets. The risk of its inability to operate as a going concern remained high.
State Information Technology Agency (SITA)
Quarter One
For the quarter under review, SITA managed to deliver on nine of the 16 planned targets, resulting in a performance for the quarter of 56.3%. This was 6.9% and 20.1% lower than the previous two quarters respectively. It did not meet the budgeted revenue of R1.481 billion, coming in at R1.304 billion and missing its target by R177 million. This was mainly due to the service level agreements (SLAs) not being finalised, and some government orders that had not yet been received to allow billing to take place.
The overall gross surplus was R214.4 million, which was less than the budget of R294.3 million by R80 million. Although the solvency was at a ratio of 2.78:1, and liquidity at 2.27:1, there were declines compared to the previous reporting periods. The indications were that SITA was in a good and healthy financial position, as it could cover both its long-term and short-term liabilities respectively.
SITA had 151 findings from the 2020/21 Auditor-General's (AG's) report. The audit results reflected a financially qualified audit opinion, with findings on compliance with applicable legislation for the past two financial years. The main root causes for the qualification were supply chain management (SCM) processes, property, plant and equipment (PPE), and intangible assets. However, the Agency indicated that its internal audit had closed 85 (56.3%) of the 151 findings.
Quarter two
SITA had managed to deliver on five of the 17 planned targets, resulting in a 29.4% performance. This was down by 45.6% when compared to the same period in the previous financial year. The SITA board had since written to the Minister saying they had engaged with management to provide a detailed action plan on how the targets that had not been achieved would be addressed.
The overall gross surplus had been R343 million -- lower than the budgeted R443.5 million by R100.5 million. The year-to-date gross surplus achieved was R557.4 million, which was R180.5 million below the planned R737.9 million.
The cumulative revenue amounted to R2.762bn, which was R233 million below the budgeted amount of R2.995 billion.

Responses
Mr Philly Mapulane, Deputy Minister of Communications and Digitial   Technologies, commenced by saying that the situation at SABC was worrisome. Revenue was declining, and the Department had requested the SABC to share its plans on how it would arrest this decline. More funds had been requested for a bailout, but the situation remained unchanged. The Group CEO had been engaged on this matter, and she had informed the Department that management was working on it. Hopefully, the board would soon be appointed to hit the ground running when appointed. It should be accounting for this aspect – how it received the money for the turnaround strategy, but nothing was happening. The President was still applying his mind to the board appointment.
It was the prerogative of the President to release or appoint Ministers for whatever reasons he deemed fit. At this time, one executive was left behind, so there was a bit of continuity in the Department.
The Director General (DG) of the Department was still acting, due to delays in the recruitment process. The Department was also finalising its organisational structure, but it has since been finalised. The position of the DG had been advertised, and the vacancy would soon be filled.
Ms Nonkqubela Jordan-Dyani, Acting DG, DCDT, replied that the R2.4 billion given to SAPO should not be considered a bailout, but the Department needed to provide a breakdown in terms of the activities that would support the SAPO turnaround. Yesterday, the Department met with the National Treasury to review the post conditions for funding the Post Office. This was related to what had been presented in the past, linked to the new SAPO strategy. The new strategy would utilise the recapitalisation by innovation, new business streams related to data hubs, branch modernisation, logistics modernisation and software development for SAPO, to make it competitive in the digital space.
The SAPO had requested an allocation towards the reduction of the staff, and an allocation had been earmarked for this and the settlement of liabilities. This was not a bailout, but an injection to assist in the turnaround activities of SAPO.
SABC
Mr Madoda Mxakwe, Group CEO, SABC, replied on the causes of the audience decline, which had been impacted by the analogue switch-off (ASO). The previous board had raised the issue of risks associated with the ASO and its impact on revenue, and between last year and this year, the impact on audience decline had been over 40%. This was why the revenue had been impacted immensely. The reduction in revenue resulted from audience losses. For example, Uzalo, which had been the best-performing show on the SABC platform last year in February and brought in about ten million viewers, now has fewer than five million viewers.
The second issue crippling the audience ratings was loadshedding, because the clients bought slots with the understanding that people would be watching those programmes. A mitigating plan was put in place, but it did not make up for the losses during the prime time.
The SABC had a five-year strategy, and it was segmented into three interrelated phases. The first phase was stabilising the SABC over 12 to 14 months, which was done successfully. The second phase addressed business transformation and the need to introduce revenue models that would enhance revenue generation, a fit-for-purpose structure, and a target operating model fit for the SABC, and this was all done. The challenge was in the third phase, which talks about the need to future-proof the sustainability of the SABC financially. The SABC had about 120 activities on the work done for the bailout conditions – all those activities with the Department and Treasury had been done.
The biggest challenge was now regulatory and legislative, relating to the strategy. The SABC bill may be finalised in three years, but the initiatives involving the regulations and legislation of the SABC had to be agile and competitive. Currently, the SABC did not have a board, and this has been the situation for about five months. The challenge, in line with the delegation of authority, was that all business plans could be approved by management in the absence of the board, but it got stuck at the executive committee (EXCO) level, because there was no one else. Unfortunately, the competition and the market do not wait for the SABC to finalise its governance issues. As soon as the SABC was assisted with the regulations and legislation, it could move forward.
The SABC deals with profit and non-profit objectives, and spends about R1 billion annually on the latter part, which was the unfunded public mandate. This covered the various South African calendar events; no other broadcaster was obliged to do so by law. ICASA was expecting the SABC to cover all of this, and the fact that R1 billion a year was spent on it showed that, the SABC was able to fulfil the public mandate. Its competitors did not have the burden of an unfunded public mandate.
The EXCO had developed robust revenue mitigation plans, but these plans had to be approved by the board. A new OTT SABC Plus platform had been introduced which was getting a lot of subscribers, and management wanted to use digital agencies to monetise the platform, which had been done at EXCO, but now awaited board approval.
Mr Mxakwe said the Group CEO of the SABC could not act as the board, because that was governed by the prescripts of the SABC Act. However, a letter had been received from the Department designating the CEO as the accounting authority, in line with the Public Finance Management Act (PFMA) provisions. This move was welcomed, because it would have assisted in business continuity. Due to the interim nature of the appointment, the acting accounting authority could not make decisions that would have long-term financial implications. However, the nature of the business was such that any decision that was made would have financial implications. Therefore, he was not necessarily on the board and could not make any substantive decisions.
Mr Reggie Nxumalo, Group Executive: Sales, SABC, said that in both quarters, many strategies had been put in place to deal with the issue of revenue. Trading models were used within the SABC, but customers were also looking for value for their spending. If they bought a million eyeballs, they expected to get a million eyeballs for their advertisements. If the SABC provided fewer than a million, it sought additional value, which was why loadshedding greatly impacted the SABC’s revenue.
The SABC continued to look for more clients, and last week, it hosted a sales and marketing conference where clients were invited and taken through all of the SABC’s range of platforms that could be used for advertising. They were throwing everything at the problem to resolve the SABC’s issues.
In the Section 189 process that had taken place, no job description had changed, but the attrition rate had been below 10%. Many of those who were left were in the operations division and had retired.
Education, children and religion did not sell -- which was the unfunded part of the SABC mandate. It could not get advertisers for those programmes, despite several attempts. 63% of the schedule across all three channels was determined by the ICASA licence conditions. This meant that while the competitors could chase audiences with popular content, the SABC had to follow a schedule from licence conditions determined 20 years ago. It had to go to a news bulletin during prime time. It had to break the current affairs content within prime time, while the competitors were broadcasting only "soapies" and dramas across their schedules. They were not competing on the same playing field, because their licence conditions did not include an unfunded public mandate.
The SABC was also working with the supply chain management (SCM) team to extend the exemptions for content creation, which commodity processes could not govern. In the last exemption granted, it had been found that it did not include the ancillary services to generate the content, but still conformed to SABC’s editorial policies. The content would be delivered in a different format, which would not include things like graphics and studio facilities. The definition had now been extended. The acquisition model had been adjusted to be simpler. The SABC had gone digital, and there was now a 21-day turnaround time on all proposals submitted to get a response to the producer for the content. The SABC had about 1 564 proposals that must be assessed, vetted and scored, and going digital made the process more conducive and faster.
The SABC Plus application had been launched. The platform was launched on 17 November, and it had since doubled the audience, although it had not yet reached critical mass but was rapidly approaching it. This allowed the SABC to experiment a little bit more with content from across the different provinces. Uzalo’s audience share had remained the same throughout the time period as the number-one performing show on the SABC for the last 52 weeks, but the total availability of audiences had declined, and this was what the advertisers were looking for. The total market had dropped from ten million to five million. Be that as it may, the SABC continued to review its programmes weekly in terms of storyline, marketing campaigns and social media campaigns.
Referring to the vacancy rate, he said that at the end of Q3, the total headcount at the SABC was 2 304 permanent employees, having started the financial year with a headcount of 2 307. At the end of January, the total turnover was 186 employees, but voluntary resignations were 111. In the same period, the SABC had recruited a total of 238 employees to new positions, of which 51 were internal opportunities for current staff. To mitigate the critical vacant positions, the SABC EXCO approved a programme that accepted over 163 interns who were qualified and were appointed to real positions. The SABC had partnered with the Media Information and Communications Technologies (MICT) Sector Education and Training Authority (SETA) and the Film Foundations. 133 had come from the SETA, and 13 from the Film Foundation. The internship programme was ending at the end of the financial year, but over 80% of those interns would be absorbed into permanent positions.
Overall, the turnover remained healthy at 5% and the retention rate was positive, sitting at 94%. The reason for the voluntary resignations might be the three-year annual increase implemented on 1 April, 2021. The SABC was now entering the third year of that salary freeze. Should the revenue increase significantly, the SABC would consider granting an increase.
The Chairperson said that the South African Post Office had posted on the virtual chatline that it was adequately covered in terms of responses.
National Electronic Media Institute of South Africa (NEMISA)
Mr Aaron Tshidzumba, CEO, Nemisa, said the Institute had achieved 73% of its targets in Q1, which had increased to 82% in Q2. It achieved 80% in Q3.
There were three targets in Q3 that were a challenge. The first was digital literacy training, which was below the target of 20 000 for the quarter, but the total target for the year was 50 000 and in the first quarter, it had already achieved 43 000 people trained. Thus far, it was at 67 829 people trained overall,  which was more than the annual target.
Another target that was a challenge was a new programme that had been introduced to train small, medium and micro enterprises (SMMEs) for digital entrepreneurship, for both formal and informal businesses. By Q3, there were just 2 184 trained, with a target of 10 000 for the year. A catch-up plan had been put in place for this with accelerated delivery, which was bearing fruit.
The other challenge was the creative media short courses for young people. The yearly target was 500 young people to be trained. By the end of Q3, Nemisa was sitting at 57% of this target, but currently, it is at 504. Its catch-up plan had brought some results.
Film and Publication Board (FPB)
Dr Mashilo Boloka, CEO, FPB, said the Board had finalised its own digital roadmap, which outlined how it would transform. This mandate came with the new Act. Critical areas included classification in view of the narrative that the FPB had a new mandate for monitoring harmful and prohibited content, and the business processes in dealing with public complaints and other matters. These were the first phase areas of the digital roadmap that would be implemented. It had no financial support, but a request had been made to Treasury, and while nothing had come forth, discussions were still ongoing.
The FPB had tried to use its own generated income to implement some of the projects, one of which included the classification of games. It had considered other ways to implement some projects with its own money. The FPB would try to implement the roadmap with its own funds.
State Information Technology Agency (SITA)
Mr Bongani Mabaso, CEO, SITA, said that after the audit process, the Agency had developed an audit action plan which had been workshopped with the AGSA to ensure that it would respond adequately to the findings and address them. This audit action plan was being implemented, and a war room had been set up to closely monitor the progress of the plan and respond quickly to any delays regarding its implementation. Significant progress had been recorded as the plan was being implemented.
Most of the critical positions had been filled, except for three -- the heads of infrastructure, audit and risk -- but two of these positions had been advertised. There were acting officials in those roles. The managing director had been appointed, and he would assume office in April.
SITA had developed interventions to address the performance shortcomings, as presented by DDG Shelembe in the presentation.
Postbank
A Postbank official said the bank had missed its target for the quarter due to key issues it was still addressing, including the moratorium lifted in November last year. The Postbank could now take on several procurement services that it required and had been historically delayed. This included the information technology (IT) modernisation programme. There had been an early approval for this, and it was in the process of finalisation and implementation. This would allow the Postbank to resolve the variation notice from the Reserve Bank.
The recruitment process had already commenced, and interviews were currently being held for the CEO, chief financial officer, company secretary, chief audit executive and chief information officer. The process should be concluded within the next two months.
As for the banking licence, Postbank was awaiting the amendment bill. This would complete the process of the banking licence application and enable Postbank to stand on its own.
Most of the challenges stemmed from the legacy IT environment, but Postbank was advanced in implementing its IT modernisation. The hardware would be arriving within the next two months.
It was also looking into the finalisation of the card scheme as required by the Reserve Bank.

Government Communication and Information System (
GCIS)
Ms Nomonde Mnukwa, Acting CEO, GCIS, took Members through the presentation, which covered the entity's performance, budget versus expenditure, missed targets and governance matters. The Department achieved 30 of the 32 targets (94%) in both the first and second quarters. As per the corrective action and catch-up plan, the output indicator on the number of Internal Communicators' Forums (ICFs) held had been overachieved in Q2 to make up for the underachievement in Q1.
She drew attention to the dwindling budget of the GCIS which over time posed a risk to their capacity to deliver. They had raised this matter in a meeting with National Treasury, and it had allocated the GCIS an additional R35.884 million for the 2023 medium term expenditure period (MTEF) as follows:
R20 million for key campaigns - gender-based violence and femicide (GBVF), the economic reconstruction and recovery plan (ERRP) and anti-corruption.
R7 million for ICT upgrades.
R8.884 million for compensation of employees (cost of living adjustment).
The vacancy rate had remained at an acceptable range of 8.4%  in quarter 1 and 9.8% in quarter 2, against the best practice of below 10%.
Media Development and Diversity Agency (MDDA)
Mr Mzu Kashe, Acting CEO, MDDA, took the Committee through the presentation and said that the MDDA had now relocated to the SABC's GSM Building. The SABC had become its new landlord.
The MDDA's annual performance plan (APP) had 19 output indicators. Of these, 11 had targets for Q1, and ten had been achieved. There were 13 targets for Q2, but the entity had achieved only 11 of them.
Regarding the budget for Q1, the Board met at the end of January 2022 to approve projects, and the chairperson signed the grant agreements at the end of March 2022. The first tranche of payments had been made. As anticipated, there were slower payments on the Economic Development Fund (EDF). Board costs had depicted a slightly lower than budgeted expenditure, which may have resulted from the Board not reaching its full complement. Administrative costs had a favourable variance, mainly due to cost containment measures, National Treasury's restrictions on procurement being implemented, and most staff members working from home. The MDDA had been filling vacant posts, and as of 30 June only three vacant positions had remained. The new HR and print managers were appointed and started at the beginning of the new financial year.
The vacancy rate had increased from 7% in Q1, to 9.3% in Q2.

Responses
GCIS
Ms Mnukwa replied that the GCIS generally did receive additional funding to cover the general election communications. However, the GCIS had engaged Treasury last year in December, indicating that even the seven cross-cutting campaigns that had to be communicated were grossly underfunded, and the R20 million allocated would cover only three. The door was not closed for the GCIS, as during the bidding process in May 2023, Treasury would be willing to hear the GCIS again when it submitted its additional funding requirements. Communications for general elections would be included in the bidding for funding, and the GCIS would use a similar approach as it had used during the Covid-19 pandemic.
MDDA
Dr Hlengani Mathebula, Board chairperson, confirmed that the entity was performing well, and the staff were competent to execute the mandate of the entity. The CFO had resigned last year and interviews had been conducted for that position. The process had been concluded, and an appointment could be made at the end of the month.
As for the CEO, there was a process in which she had elected to invoke Section 11A of the Labour Relations Act, where it was determined that she would not be able to sit with the board to deal with the issue of incompatibility. She wanted an independent arbiter, the Commission for Conciliation, Mediation and Arbitration (CCMA), to arbitrate on issues between the board and her. The verdict of the CCMA came out, found the CEO guilty, and recommended a summary dismissal. The board had sat on 25 January, accepted this recommendation, and summarily dismissed the CEO. At the engagement with the Committee on 14 March, the board would submit a full transcript, the verdict, and the sanction of the chairperson.
Regarding the CEO's performance in Q2, the issue of irregular expenditure had been reflected, and part of the CFO’s salary was transparency with the Committee. The MDDA had approached Treasury on how it must treat the matter. The Treasury had not yet responded, but once it was condoned, it would disappear from their books. Otherwise, it would be reflected as an irregular expenditure.
Ms Mnukwa said the GCIS mentioned it could not invest in IT infrastructure due to lack of funding on slide 30. There would therefore be challenges to improve efficiencies, as the GCIS could not digitise and the IT infrastructure had reached the end of its life. The bandwidth was also limited, preventing the GCIS from achieving its 4IR aspirations.
The entity had a digital support programme in its APP which ensured that all traditional print and broadcast institutions were digitised. There were also a number of initiatives that demonstrated that it was moving with the times. It had partnered with Nemisa and Facebook on some initiatives.
The Chairperson welcomed the inputs and indicated that the Committee had made a number of recommendations for funding interventions for the GCIS. The Committee expected a detailed report regarding the CEO and CFO matters.
The meeting was adjourned.
