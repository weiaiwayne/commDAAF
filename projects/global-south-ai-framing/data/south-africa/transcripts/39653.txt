Meeting Summary

SA Human Rights Commission
Information Regulator SA
The South African Human Rights Commission (SAHRC) and the Information Regulator met with the Portfolio Committee to present reports on their annual performances for the 2023/24 financial year and their funding proposals.
The SAHRC showed significant improvement in its annual performance for the 2023/24 financial year, achieving 82.3% of its targets, up from 50% the previous year. The Commission had reached 11.3 million people and addressed 6 833 complaints regarding human rights violations. It had received an unqualified audit opinion, with a total of 31 findings raised by the Auditor-General, down from 54 the previous year. An audit action plan was in place to resolve 90% of these findings, but challenges remained in aligning expenditure with performance, managing contracts effectively, and ensuring timely settlement of accounts. The Commission was focused on improving its supply chain and procurement processes to enhance overall efficiency and accountability.
The discussion focused on several key issues regarding the South African Human Rights Commission (SAHRC), including employee turnover and stability; language rights and accessibility; visibility and access to rural areas; financial management and office space; litigation and legal services; research and reporting; and consequence management and audit findings.
The Chairperson emphasised the importance of proactively addressing audit findings to avoid being caught off guard by the Auditor-General's (AG’s) report. He encouraged the Commission to adopt strong consequence management practices to create a disciplined environment and comply with legislation.
Overall, the Committee recognised the SAHRC’s efforts, but urged it to improve in several areas, including visibility, financial management and research capacity, to better fulfil its mandate. The discussion also reinforced the need for better coordination with other institutions and departments to maximise resources and reach more vulnerable populations.
The Information Regulator's presentation outlined its performance in key areas, including protecting personal information, promoting access to information, and public education. In the 2023/24 financial year, 100% of complex and simple complaints had been resolved, with assessments conducted for the Protection of Personal Information Act (POPIA) and Promotion of Access to Information Act (PAIA) compliance. Public awareness of privacy rights remained low, but efforts were ongoing through education programmes. The Regulator faced increasing security breaches and complaints, necessitating more resources. A new funding model has been proposed to sustain operations and support its upcoming transition to an independent public entity by March 2025, with legislative amendments planned to strengthen its enforcement powers.
Several key issues related to the Information Regulator’s operations and progress were discussed. These included challenges with responsiveness and staffing;  public education on privacy rights; State Information Technology Agency (SITA)-related challenges; litigation matters and compliance;  internal transition and project workstreams; and South African Police Service (SAPS) cases.
Overall, the meeting highlighted the Regulator's achievements, challenges with external partners like the SITA, and ongoing efforts to improve compliance and protect personal information. The Chairperson and the Committee expressed support for the Regulator’s initiatives, and pledged to assist in securing future funding and resolving SITA-related issues.
Meeting report
SAHRC 2023/24 Annual Reports
Opening remarks
Mr Chris Nissen, Chairperson, South African Human Rights Commission (SAHRC), expressed his appreciation for the work of previous commissioners. He noted that the current group of commissioners was appointed in December 2023, and emphasised their commitment to strengthening government's system. This included speeding up hearings and inquiries, balancing rapid response, visibility, and the complaint process, and prioritising pro-human rights budgeting. These focus areas were guiding the Commission's agenda.
To enhance governance and leadership, the new commissioners decided to assign commissioners to different provinces to improve visibility and accessibility. This strategic move not only strengthened governance, but also ensured the SAHRC operates as one cohesive institution with a shared plan to advance development by protecting, promoting, and monitoring rights across South Africa. The commissioners contributed significantly to improving the SAHRC's governance through regular oversight meetings, which helped alert the secretariat to risks that could hinder progress and ensure close monitoring of the Commission’s performance.
Pro-human rights budgeting was a key priority in the 2020-2025 revised strategic plan. The Commission recognised that this approach was essential for closing the inequality gap and improving access to basic services. In their first ten months, the commissioners focused on stabilising the institution and increasing its visibility, efficiency, and impact. Last week, they held a joint strategic meeting to plan for the 2025-2030 financial years, with plans to introduce a rapid response protocol to improve how the Commission handles real-time human rights violations during emergencies not covered in the current operational plan.
The Commission was facing two major challenges. First, it filed an appeal with the Constitutional Court regarding the binding nature of its directives, as it wants its findings and recommendations to be implemented diligently. This legal uncertainty could strain the Commission's already limited resources. Second, the Commission’s recommendations to government departments were often not implemented, despite improvements through the commissioners' increased involvement in the provinces.
Additionally, although the Commission had faced issues with vacant senior management positions, most of these positions for the 2023/24 financial year had been filled. The new commissioners had also successfully had the union recognised, and ensured sustainable staff development within the Commission.
CEO's overview
Adv Chrystal Pillay, Chief Operations Officer and Acting Chief Executive Officer (CEO), SAHRC, emphasised the Commission's commitment to not only meeting its objectives, but also to ensure that its operations were run with the highest standards of governance, transparency and efficiency. She highlighted the Commission’s progress toward achieving organisational and financial stability. Over the past financial year, the organisation's overall health had improved, with a focus on promoting stability by involving staff in decision-making and fostering inclusivity in the workplace.
One ongoing initiative was the organisational design project, which aims to realign the Commission’s structure to better fulfil its constitutional mandate. The Commission would report on its institutional performance and progress with the audit action plan, a critical focus area for addressing identified shortcomings. These efforts had been key in reducing risks and preventing repeat audit findings.
While there had been some challenges in meeting all annual performance targets, the Commission had reached a more stable level of performance and performance management. This progress was part of the office's turnaround strategy, aimed at stabilising the Commission and fostering a high-performance culture. The institutional performance plan has been developed as a framework to drive this improvement. Key vacancies, including the roles of chief operational officer, chief information officer, chief financial officer and head of strategic support and governance, had been filled to support the plan's successful rollout.
Using an evidence-based approach, governance structures have been strengthened to improve performance monitoring, evaluation and reporting. This contributed to more reliable performance reports and expanded the work of the performance management and evaluation reporting committee, further enhancing accountability and transparency.
The next five years would be crucial for the Commission, especially with the recent Supreme Court appeal judgment and the potential outcomes of the Constitutional Court, which may require the Commission to review how it executes its protection mandate. Additionally, as the government reduces spending, the Commission must adapt to budget cuts that would impact its capacity and resources.
Adv Pillay concluded by reaffirming the Commission's commitment to working collaboratively with the Committee and other stakeholders to ensure it continues to deliver on its mandate with excellence.
SAHRC annual performance and financial report
Dr Talifhani Khubana, Chief Financial Officer (CFO), SAHRC, presented the annual performance and financial report for the 2023/24 financial year, and for the first two quarters of 2024/25. The presentation provided a detailed overview of the Commission's performance over the past financial years, highlighting achievements, challenges and areas for improvement.
Year-on-year performance overview:
Achievement of Targets:
The SAHRC achieved 82.3% of its targets for the 2023/24 financial year, marking a significant improvement from previous years -- 64% in 2020/21, 61% in 2021/22, and 50% in 2022/23.
Reach:
The Commission had reached 11.3 billion people, an increase from 8.6 billion in 2020/21 and fluctuating figures in the subsequent years --16.4 billion in 2021/22, and 11.2 billion in 2022/23.
Redress mechanisms:
The Commission had addressed 6 833 complaints regarding human rights violations, a slight increase from previous years -- 6 623 in 2021/22, and 6 662 in 2022/23.
Recommendations made:
A total of 131 recommendations and directives were issued to improve human rights conditions in the country, a substantial increase from just five in 2020/21.
2023/24 highlights
Key initiatives included:
Child Rights Ambassadors conference;
Right to Read and Write campaign;
Various inquiry reports on schools’ infrastructure, water issues, malnutrition, school uniforms, and July 2021 unrest;
National Schools Moot Court programme.
Financial Statements and Audit Outcomes:
Expenditure trends (2020-2024):
A limited correlation existed between expenditure and performance. The reliance on surplus funds for balancing budgets was problematic, with 2024/25 expected to be a turning point.
Surplus trends:
The budget surplus stemmed from poor planning, inadequate accountability, and slow filling of vacancies.
Audit summary for 2023/24:
Financial statements and annual performance reports were submitted on time, with the Auditor General of South Africa (AGSA) issuing 75 requests for information (RFIs) and raising 31 findings -- down from 54 the previous year. An audit action plan has been developed to resolve 90% of the findings by February 2024. The Commission had received an unqualified audit opinion for its financial statements.
Key areas for improvement:
Expenditure management: Settle accounts within 30 days.
Contract management: Revise processes to ensure alignment with market value.
Procurement management: Avoid deviations from standard processes.
Consequence management: Strengthen accountability for irregular expenditures.
Audit of pre-determined objectives:
Material misstatements were identified in the annual performance report, particularly regarding the initiatives to address systemic human rights violations.
Reasons for improved performance:
The implementation of the institutional performance improvement plan (IPIP), focusing on effective human resource management, improved audit outcomes, and revised governance structures, contributed to the 2023/24 success.
The Commission prioritised recruiting senior management to enhance leadership, but had faced challenges in retaining critical skills.
Annual performance plan for 2024/25:
The Commission achieved 75% of planned targets for Programme 1, and 100% for Programsme 2 and 3 in the first quarter.
There were no significant increases in the baseline appropriation, raising concerns about resource availability, given the Commission's broad mandate and limited staffing relative to South Africa's population of 63 million.
Overall, the presentation highlighted both achievements and ongoing challenges, emphasising the need for continued improvements in governance, financial management and operational efficiency within the SAHRC.

Discussion
Mr D Klopper (DA) raised concerns about the perceived toxic work environment within the SAHRC. Given its role in promoting human rights, he felt strongly that the Commission should set an example in resolving internal disputes peacefully. He questioned how the public could have confidence in the SAHRC if it could not resolve its own issues.
He also expressed concerns about several ongoing issues, including repeated findings in audits, performance management, procurement contracts, the payment of invoices within 30 days, irregular expenditure, and lack of consequence management. He emphasised the importance of paying invoices on time, especially for small businesses that struggle when payments are delayed. He believed that the lack of consequence management was why there were also repeated audit findings in other areas of the report.
Mr Klopper questioned the significant increase in the training budget, from R655 000 in 2022/23 to R1.7 million in 2023/24, and requested an explanation for this. He also asked why no disciplinary action had been taken against the official responsible for fruitless and wasteful expenditure.
Another concern he raised was the shift from underspending to overspending, pointing out that this needed to be addressed. With the Commission facing budget constraints that limited its ability to fulfil its mandate, he asked what steps were being taken to reduce administrative costs, optimise personnel expenses, and minimise non-essential spending to free up funds for core activities, rather than simply relying on National Treasury for a budget increase.
He also inquired about the irregular expenditure of R2.7 million in the 2023/24 financial year, questioning when it had become clear that the procurement in human resources (HR) had not been properly managed, as it had been part of repeated findings. He asked when hiring someone with the necessary skills was done, given that this issue had persisted for three years. Lastly, he questioned why the Committee should support a budget increase for the Commission when targets were not being met, and there was no consequence management in place.
Mr S Nomvalo (MK) expressed concern that the SAHRC lacked visibility as a Chapter 9 institution, even though it existed as a constitutional body and should be one of the most visible institutions. He noted that human rights were violated daily across South Africa, yet the Commission often failed to respond effectively when these violations were reported. He emphasised that measuring the SAHRC’s success was easy by comparing its constitutional mandate to its actual actions and outcomes.
He reminded the Commission that it had significant powers, granted by national legislation, to perform its duties. These included investigating and reporting on human rights violations, securing appropriate redress when violations occur, and conducting research. He asked whether the SAHRC had a dedicated research unit and how many papers it had published in the financial year under review. He also inquired about the educational programmes the Commission had implemented and where they were conducted.
Mr Nomvalo stressed that those most affected by human rights violations were often illiterate individuals living in deep rural areas, who may not be aware of their rights. He urged the Commission to focus on reaching these communities if they were serious about fulfilling their constitutional obligations. He quoted the Constitution, which requires the Commission to ask relevant state organs each year to provide information on measures they had taken to realise rights related to housing, healthcare, food, water, social security, education, and the environment. Since 90% of government departments in South Africa dealt with these socio-economic rights, he asked if the Commission had requested reports from these departments and, if so, to list them.
He also noted that the SAHRC was responsible for monitoring other government departments. He asked if the Commission had ever conducted research on the widows from the Marikana massacre to assess how they had been affected, their current social status, and whether they needed assistance. He emphasised that this research was critical, as the massacre was a violation of the right to life, leaving many people, including widows, to face severe consequences, such as losing their breadwinners.
The Chairperson asked how many offices the SAHRC had across the country, how many were rented, who the landlords were, the length of the rental contracts, and how much was spent per month on each office. He agreed that increasing public access to the Commission was important, as it helped the SAHRC to better understand the real challenges related to human rights in the country.
However, he expressed disappointment that the presentation did not mention efforts to engage with the public for education and other relevant matters. He also criticised the Commission for not reporting on the suspension of the CEO, saying that this lack of transparency made it seem like the issue was being handled behind closed doors.
Finally, he asked whether an audit action plan had been adopted to address the issues raised by the AGSA, noting that this would help the Committee to hold the Commission accountable.
SAHRC's response
Commissioner Nissen explained that there were serious allegations against the suspended CEO; hence, she was put on precautionary suspension to begin the investigation. The report on the suspension was going to be sent to the Committee in writing by 18 October.
He said that the Commission was aware that the public’s access to them was important. He mentioned that the commissioners had done some outreach and educational programmes in all nine provinces. A compiled report on the Commission’s visibility and involvement would be sent to the Committee. He agreed, however, that while human rights violations in South Africa occurred every day, the Commission could not reach everywhere. Monitors were working hard, but he agreed that they could do more with being more visible and accessible.
He said that when the Marikana massacre happened, many commissioners had been involved from the point of observation. He accepted the suggestion to follow the widows and victims.
Regarding the toxic environment within the SAHRC, he responded that the new cohort of commissioners were working hard on trying to pull together the organisation, and made sure that they met with the staff. In the same breath, he said they could not control employees complaining to outside parties. The Commission had dealt with issues around trading, bursaries, recognition of the Commission, and staff engagements.
Dr Henk Boshoff, SAHRC Commissioner, explained that the Commission understood its constitutional duty to gather information from other government departments. The Commission planned to go beyond just collecting this information by preparing a report based on it. Letters had already been sent to the relevant departments, along with a questionnaire for them to complete. The commissioners had met with the Ministers of Human Settlements and Health, and a meeting with the Minister of Water and Sanitation was scheduled. He emphasised that the Commission was responsible not only for collecting the information, but also for sharing it with all relevant stakeholders.
The Chairperson asked about the legal cases involving the Commission, both for and against it. He wanted to know how the Commission secured legal services and whether they had a panel of attorneys they worked with.
Dr Khubana said that the SAHRC had ten offices across the country -- nine provincial offices and one head office. The Western Cape's office lease had expired, and only the Department of Public Works and Infrastructure (DPWI) knew who owned the building. There were plans to share a new office in the Western Cape with the Commission for Gender Equality (CGE). In the Eastern Cape, the lease with SKG Africa Limited -- a level 2 black economic empowerment (BEE) company -- expired in March 2025, and a search for a new office was underway. The Limpopo office’s lease runs until March 2027, with the building owned by SAB and Team Properties (a level 1 BEE contributor). The KwaZulu-Natal office lease expires in March 2025, and discussions with DPW have begun. The Free State lease, held with Link Properties (a level 2 BEE contributor), expires in May 2029. Other office leases include Northern Cape (expires March 2027), Mpumalanga (expires September 2026, held with SKG Africa Limited), North West (expires June 2027, held with the Lu Family Trust, a level 2 BEE contributor), and Gauteng (expires April 2029, held by Growth Point Properties, a level 1 BEE contributor and JSE-listed company). On average, the SAHRC spends R9.6 million annually on office rentals.
The Commission had requested a one-time investment of R35 million from National Treasury to procure human rights houses in each province instead of renting, potentially saving R8.4 million per year for the next five years. There was also interest in partnering with institutions like the Independent Electoral Commission (IEC) to leverage their provincial presence and develop apps to improve public access to the SAHRC. Additionally, they had asked Treasury for R10 million to fund mobile clinics for use in remote areas.
Regarding the audit action plan, Dr Khubana clarified that the Commission was already working on its own plan while the audit was ongoing. Instead of simply addressing audit findings, their approach was focused on identifying and addressing root causes. For example, irregular expenditures stemmed from poor contract management and a failure to properly assess tenders' technical functionality. He noted that not having their own legal panel contributed to supply chain issues. A skills audit had revealed that many supply chain employees lacked the necessary SCM (supply chain management) background, leading to competence and skills challenges. They had restructured the SCM department to resolve these issues and planned to establish a legal panel.
Dr Khubana added that only 2% of invoices had been paid late (after 30 days), and the Commission was planning to assign a finance officer whose sole responsibility would be handling invoices. The Commission had also adopted a hybrid working model, with 70% of employees working from the office, which would save R8.6 million over five years, redirecting those funds to the Commission’s programmes.
Training had been a priority since 2020, with over 50 staff being trained, and in 2024/25, the Commission was aligning training programs with both individual development needs and institutional goals.
On the issue of consequence management, Dr Khubana agreed that stricter enforcement would improve discipline and compliance. The recent skills audit and structural review should lead to better appointments, particularly for contract management roles.
He justified the Commission’s request for additional funding, stating that while they had spent the full budget for 2023/24, they had also had to use surpluses from previous years’ underspending. This overspending would continue for the next three years until the reserves were exhausted.
Adv Pillay emphasised that visibility and accessibility were key priorities for the Commission. As part of their five-year planning cycle, they recognised the need to expand their national presence, especially in remote rural areas. To address this, they launched a project aimed at improving access in these regions. She said that the Commission had several strategies in place, including partnerships with various stakeholders, to raise awareness about their work and enhance accessibility.
In the last financial year, the Commission had resolved 6 500 human rights violation complaints. While they did have a research unit, it was currently understaffed due to the resignation of two senior researchers. Efforts were underway to strengthen both the research unit and the Commission’s monitoring mandate. She also mentioned the development of two key reports -- the "State of Human Rights" report and the "International Human Rights Review" -- both of which were submitted to Parliament.
Addressing the issue of the litigation panel, Ms Pillay clarified that the Commission had a panel of advisors registered with the central supplier database (CSD). However, they followed supply chain processes for specific litigation cases to procure the appropriate legal counsel.
Mr Klopper asked why employees were leaving the SAHRC, and why they were required to sign non-disclosure agreements (NDAs).
Mr Nomvalo suggested that the Commission should investigate whether the unrecognition of the Amabhaca people and their language was a violation of Section 9 of the Constitution. He also raised concerns about the lack of sign language interpreters in various departments, asking whether this violated the rights of deaf people, as they cannot receive assistance without interpreters. He recommended that the Commission make recommendations to institutions that could restore any rights found to be violated.
Commissioner Nissen responded by saying that the Commission would look into the concerns regarding the Amabhaca and sign language issues, and report back. They would also investigate similar concerns about the Khoi-San language and other groups in Limpopo.
Chairperson's comments
The Chairperson noted that employees tended to seek better opportunities, and it was natural for staff to move to larger organisations like the National Prosecuting Authority (NPA). He supported the idea of recognising language rights, especially since sign language was now an official language in South Africa. He pointed out that currently, interpreters were hired by the Department of Justice and court administration only as needed, and suggested that the Commission should take the lead in advising on the future direction of language recognition for underrepresented groups.
He acknowledged that while the Commission was making progress, there was still room for improvement, and encouraged healthy competition between agencies to accelerate the delivery of services. He supported the idea of mobile offices and recommended that the Commission engage with the Department of Public Works (DPW) and the Department of Justice to potentially share court buildings, which could reduce rental costs.
He expressed concern about the Commission’s stability, referring to past letters from the National Education, Health and Allied Workers Union (NEHAWU) about staff suspensions, and highlighted the Committee’s plans for physical oversight of the Commission. He stressed the importance of consequence management and advised the Commission to conduct its own audits before the Auditor-General's (AG’s) findings to avoid surprises. He urged them to align their spending with performance, as there was a mismatch where 60% of targets were met, but 90% of the budget was spent.
Regarding budget cuts, the Chairperson acknowledged that all entities were affected, and the Committee would consider budget increases based on each entity’s spending efficiency. He agreed that reforming supply chain management was crucial due to issues with decentralisation, and urged the Commission to streamline its invoice handling.
On the matter of legal services, he criticised the three-quote system for causing delays in urgent legal matters, and suggested that establishing a legal panel would be more efficient.
He shared the Committee's desire for the Commission to reach more people, especially in remote areas where human rights violations may be going unreported, but noted the challenge of limited resources. He encouraged the Commission to explore using magistrates' offices as a cost-effective solution, and reassured them that the Committee was working on filling the Commission's vacancies.
Finally, the Chairperson raised concerns about recurring complaints against commissioners during Commission processes. Mr Klopper asked for clarification on the non-disclosure agreements (NDAs) employees had been asked to sign. Mr Nissen responded that he was unaware of the NDAs.
Information Regulator Annual Report 2023/24
Chairperson's opening remarks
Adv Pansy Tlakula, Chairperson: Information Regulator, shared the organisation’s mission and vision. They aimed to become a world-class institution and were making strides in this direction by taking global leadership roles in access to information and data protection. In Africa, they lead the network of African Information Commissioners, and internationally they sit on the International Conference of Information Commissioners. Additionally, they had been selected to serve on the executive committee of a global network of data protection authorities.
However, the Information Regulator faced challenges, particularly with handling the large volume of Protection of Personal Information Act (POPIA) complaints while having a limited staff. They were recently listed as a Schedule 3A entity, meaning they were now an independent national public body. This shift was positive, as they would manage their own budget, rather than relying on the Department of Justice’s funding. However, it also presented challenges, especially around conflicting responsibilities outlined in the Public Finance Management Act (PFMA) and the Information Regulator Act. While the PFMA required an executive or accounting authority, the Information Regulator Act designated the CEO as the accounting officer.
The organisation also faced financial pressures as it prepared to lease office space, develop new systems, and open bank accounts. On top of this, they were working on a funding model to address legal challenges. Currently, there were five court cases against their decisions, including a dispute with WhatsApp and challenges from several government departments, such as Justice, and Forestry, Fisheries and the Environment.
Annual and quarterly reports
Adv Tshepo Boikanyo, Executive: POPIA, and Ms Glen Zulu, CFO, Information Regulator, presented the 2023/24 annual report, the 2024/25 quarterly reports, and the budget proposal for the 2025/26 to 2027/28 financial years.
They said that the Promotion of Access to Information Act (PAIA), enacted 24 years ago, was due for amendment to strengthen its enforcement powers, aligning them with the provisions of POPIA. These amendments were intended to improve the Regulator's authority in ensuring compliance.
Annual Report
Programme 1: Protection of personal information
100% of complex and simple complaints had been investigated and resolved within the required timeframes.
13 responsible parties were assessed for POPIA compliance.
A guidance note on direct marketing was developed and approved.
Programme 2: Promotion of access to information
100% of both complex and simple complaints received were resolved.
108 public and private bodies were assessed for PAIA compliance.
Programme 3: Education and communication
Research showed 33% awareness of privacy rights and 19% awareness of access to information rights.
11 education programmes were focused on personal information protection, and 10 on access to information.
36 public awareness programmes were conducted on information rights.
Programme 4: Legal services
Six pieces of legislation or government policies were reviewed, with reports submitted.
Programme 5a: Office of the CEO
75% of recommendations from the enforcement committee were considered and finalised.
Programme 5b: Corporate services
One research report was completed.
Programme 5c: Finance
100% of the allocated budget for goods, services, and equipment was spent, with 155% procurement against the approved plan.
Quarter 1 and 2 Performance Report for 2024/25
86% of the Regulator's targets were achieved for both the first and second quarters.
Funding Proposal
The Regulator was requesting a review of its baseline allocation over the medium-term expenditure framework (MTEF) to ensure long-term financial sustainability and accommodate rising costs.
New systems were needed to align with the shift to an accrual basis of accounting, which would require hardware, licences and subscriptions.
Compliance costs and funding support:
The Regulator faced increased expenses due to changes in accounting systems, governance structures, and separation from the Department of Justice's shared services, including payroll and bookkeeping.
Additional capacity was required for several administrative functions such as information technology (IT), human resources, supply chain, and finance.
Core functions' funding support:
Growing numbers of security breaches and complaints highlighted the need for more resources in the POPIA division to handle compliance and litigation.
More funding was needed to expand research on emerging risks, such as artificial intelligence (AI), and increase public awareness efforts.
Proposed funding model:
The Regulator proposed a new funding model, which includes imposing fines on public and private bodies for non-compliance with the PAIA and POPIA. The fines would be payable immediately and a percentage of penalties could be retained by the Regulator.
Fees may be charged for assessments and training conducted for information officers, with legislative amendments required to formalise these changes.
Progress on Regulator's establishment as a Schedule 3A public entity:
The Regulator would become an independent entity, separate from the Department of Justice, by 31 March, 2025.
A transition team had been set up to manage this process, and the Regulator had requested National Treasury to open a bank account.
Benchmarking with similar public entities was underway to establish financial systems for procurement, payments and payroll.
This summary highlighted the Regulator's accomplishments in protecting personal information and promoting access to information, while also outlining its challenges, funding needs, and the steps being taken towards financial independence.

Discussion
Mr Klopper raised concerns about the serious issues with the State Information Technology Agency (SITA), which were impacting service delivery and distorting finances. He noted that with ongoing budget cuts, it seemed overly optimistic for the Information Regulator to expect National Treasury to increase their funding. He was also unclear about the number of complaints being handled, particularly the difference between simple and complex cases.
He referred to a case in Krugersdorp, where personal information of sexual assault victims was leaked, and another incident where personal data had been circulated on WhatsApp. He questioned why the Information Regulator was only considering action against the South African Police Service (SAPS), rather than taking immediate steps, since this violation had happened before. He also asked for an update on the listing transition project team.
Mr Klopper inquired about how many legal cases the Regulator was currently facing due to their infringement notices being challenged in court, and whether they had appointed a panel of attorneys to handle these matters. Given that data breaches reported to the Regulator had risen from 500 to 1 700 in 2023/24, he wanted to know what was being done to address this increase. He expressed concern that the Regulator often learnt about data breaches from the media, and questioned whether victims were also being left uninformed.
Finally, he pointed out that only 33% of the 853 government entities had submitted compliance reports, asking what actions the Regulator was taking to ensure greater compliance.
The Chairperson requested that the Information Regulator clarify the difference between complex and simple complaints. Regarding the SITA, he asked the Regulator to provide more details about the specific issues involved. He also inquired about how the Regulator secures legal services.
Information Regulator's response
Adv Tlakula explained that the PAIA still needed to be amended because it lacked sanctions for non-compliance. Currently, the Regulator could only encourage private or government bodies to comply, but could not take further action. In contrast, with POPIA, the Regulator could issue enforcement notices when non-compliance was found.
Mr Jacob Jansen, Chief Legal Officer: Information Regulator, confirmed they now had a panel of attorneys in place, pending a final signature. Regarding litigation, they were currently handling five to six cases. Only one case, involving the Department of Justice, had gone for review in relation to infringement notices.
Other cases included a POPIA matter with an American company over WhatsApp, and several PAIA matters -- a Durban high court case, the Hardwick musical royalty case (which was resolved, but still awaiting a decision on legal fees), a case with the State Security Agency (SSA), a case with the Department of Forestry, Fisheries and Environment (DFFE), and one involving a municipality.
Adv Tlakula confirmed that they had issued an enforcement notice to SAPS a year ago and that SAPS had complied with it.
Adv Boikanyo confirmed that there was a second case involving SAPS that they could not discuss, but it was similar to the 2023 case. In the first case, SAPS was issued an enforcement notice requiring them to implement a policy to prevent further security breaches. Although SAPS had put a policy in place, it had failed to prevent another breach, which had led to this second case.
He also explained the difference between simple and complex complaints. Simple complaints were resolved within three months, while complex ones could take up to a year.
Regarding the numbers Mr Klopper had asked about, Adv Boikanyo reported that in the 2022/23 financial year, the Regulator had received 925 complaints; in 2023/24, they had received 1 044 complaints, and so far in the current financial year, they had received 577. For security breaches, there were 590 in 2022, 1 727 in 2023, and 1 207 so far in 2024.
He highlighted that the rising number of security breaches was a major challenge, showing that responsible parties did not have sufficient measures to protect personal information. The Regulator monitored these trends, which could include simple mistakes like sending an email with personal information to the wrong person. To manage this, they used a risk matrix to assess the likelihood of such breaches.
The Regulator, along with their chief information officer (CIO), was exploring ways to automate some manual processes, which could reduce the workload. Through the EDUCOM division, the Regulator also provides training to responsible parties, emphasising compliance with Section 19 under Condition 7 of POPIA, which requires them to have proper security measures in place to protect personal information from unlawful access.
Adv Tlakula explained that the Regulator’s role in security breaches was limited by law. They were responsible only for assessing whether the security measures put in place by responsible parties were adequate and reasonable to protect the integrity and confidentiality of personal information. However, there was also a criminal aspect, which fell under the Cybercrimes Act. To address this, the Regulator had arranged a meeting with the Minister of Police, as some of these breaches involved extortion.
Adv Collen Weapond, Member: Information Regulator, explained that if the Regulator was not informed of a security breach, it counted as non-compliance with Section 22 of POPIA. In such cases, the Regulator would issue an enforcement notice that highlights the specific section of non-compliance, ensuring that the responsible party complies with POPIA within a set timeframe.
Adv Boikanyo added that if the person affected by the security breach was not informed, it would be a violation of Section 22(1) of the POPIA. When the Regulator issues an enforcement notice in such cases, it would specify the timeframe within which the responsible party must comply.
Ms Zulu said the main challenges with the SITA were related to two issues. Firstly, the service level agreement (SLA) for the project set a timeline of 10 months for the delivery of the complaints management system, which had been procured in 2021. However, this deadline was not met. Secondly, when the budget allocated for the project was not spent within the financial year due to delays, it had to be carried over to the next year, affecting other commitments. Although the SITA had now completed the system, the deliverables did not meet the Regulator's terms of reference. SITA had also mistakenly deployed the system on its own website, making it difficult for users to access, as it should have been hosted on the Information Regulator’s website. This error required the system to be decoupled, which incurred additional costs. A dispute had arisen because SITA had requested more money, despite not meeting the agreed deadlines. The Regulator had considered taking over the system, but had found that SITA used proprietary technologies that were hard to access and incompatible with cheaper, more common platforms like Microsoft. Additionally, maintaining the system had been very expensive.
Mr Tando Luyaba, CIO, Information Regulator, explained that SITA had built the complaints management system as part of their e-services portal. This meant users had to go through the SITA portal to register complaints with the Information Regulator. The Regulator had made it clear that the system should be hosted on their own website, which had led to the need to decouple it from SITA's website.
Although the system had now been decoupled, it still required ongoing maintenance. However, it was built in such a way that only SITA could support it. To resolve this, the Regulator had started developing an internal system that would be easier to maintain in the future.
Ms Zulu said they had developed a project plan with five workstreams to manage the transition process.
1) Legal stream:
Focuses on the funding model and the memorandums of agreement (MoUs) between the Regulator and the Department of Justice.
2) Resources stream:
Handles the budget, banking, and systems, ensuring compliance with financial regulations and setting up the budget structure and cost centres within the Regulator.
3) Communication stream:
Manages both internal and external communication, including change management to keep staff informed of milestones and prevent concerns related to staff transfers.
4) Policy and governance stream:
Focuses on the Regulator’s progress toward independence and developing policies in line with generally recognised accounting practices (GRAP).
5) Compliance stream:
Oversees audit-related matters, including the establishment of an audit committee.
Chairperson’s closing remarks
The Chairperson congratulated the Regulator on becoming an independent entity and promised support. He noted that the Regulator would need help engaging with National Treasury as they navigate the listing process. He also acknowledged that amending laws took time, which could slow the Regulator’s progress.
He expressed concern over the fact that only 10% of research documents informed people of their right to privacy, leaving many unaware that their privacy was being violated. Although the Regulator was not solely responsible for public education on this issue, he appreciated their efforts.
The Chairperson also mentioned ongoing challenges with court modernisation and digitisation, largely due to SITA. He acknowledged that SITA-related problems were growing, but promised to look into it and provide support where needed. In terms of future funding, the Committee would ensure that entities reporting to them received budget support, especially those like the Information Regulator, which had performed well.
The meeting was adjourned.
