Meeting Summary
The Portfolio Committee convened to be briefed by the Auditor-General of South Africa (AGSA) on the audit outcomes of the national Department of Tourism (NDT) and its entity, South African Tourism (SAT), for the 2023/24 financial year. This was followed by a presentation by the Department on its performance during the year under review.
The AGSA reported that the NDT had received a clean audit, reflecting strong financial governance and sound management. However, there have been challenges, including delays in Development Bank of Southern Africa (DBSA)-managed projects, and underspending due to unfilled vacancies, and delays in expanded public works programmes.
SAT had received a qualified opinion due to material misstatements in cash balances, marketing expenditures, and payables. There had been a significant rise in irregular expenditure, reaching R24.2 million, largely due to non-compliance with procurement and supply chain management (SCM) processes. This non-compliance, coupled with weak financial controls, had resulted in operational inefficiencies. AGSA said SAT needed to strengthen its internal controls, implement stronger consequence management, and fill key vacancies in leadership and oversight positions to improve financial integrity.
AGSA urged the Portfolio Committee to intensify its oversight of both the NDT and SAT, especially regarding the financial mismanagement issues within SAT, monitor corrective actions, ensure tighter compliance with procurement regulations and better risk management processes, and ensure due diligence for DBSA-managed projects, specifically addressing project delays and the need for better project management. It also had to follow up on the corrective measures AGSA recommended to ensure improved governance and stronger financial control mechanisms in both entities moving forward.
The Department's presentation on its 2023/24 performance outlined its constitutional and legislative mandates, emphasising the Tourism Act of 2014 and the National Development Plan’s (NDP) role in boosting tourism as a driver of employment and economic growth. The performance overview showed that the Department had achieved 93.48% of its annual targets. It reported that R2.372 billion -- 96.4% of its final R2.46 billion appropriation -- had been spent. Details of the achievements and challenges in its four programmes were highlighted.
Minister De Lille also informed the Committee about South Africa's recent assumption of the G20 tourism portfolio from Brazil, and said an inter-ministerial committee meeting had been held to discuss preparations, with a tourism ministerial meeting scheduled for next September.
Members' main concerns were directed at SAT's performance, with the Chairperson calling for a turnaround strategy at the entity. There were questions regarding the appointment of the SAT board chairperson, given his previous role as a board member during times of mismanagement. Reference was made to irregular and wasteful expenditure, and delays in paying service providers, and the Committee called for greater oversight over its operations.
Meeting report
The Chairperson opened the meeting by welcoming Members, Ms Patricia de Lille, Minister of Tourism, the Auditor-General of South Africa (AGSA) delegation, Mr Victor Tharage, Director-General of Tourism, and guests. After receiving apologies from Members who were absent and the Deputy Minister, who was on official business in Limpopo, she introduced the agenda, which included a briefing by the Auditor-General of South Africa (AGSA) on the audit outcomes for the National Department of Tourism (NDT) and South African Tourism (SAT) for the 2023/24 financial year, and a presentation of the NDT's performance for the year.
NDT and SAT 2023/24 audit outcomes
Ms Nompakamo Matanzima, AGSA Business Leader, began the presentation by highlighting the importance of the tourism sector in South Africa, particularly its contribution to job creation and the country's gross domestic product (GDP). She emphasised AGSA’s commitment to transparency, accountability, and integrity, noting that the audit would reflect the performance of the tourism portfolio in alignment with the medium-term strategic framework (MTSF).
She explained the focus on infrastructure development, which seeks to drive economic growth, preserve cultural heritage, and promote South Africa as a premier domestic and international tourist destination. She concluded by stressing the critical importance of stability in the tourism sector.
Mr Siphesihle Mlangeni, Senior Manager, AGSA, then introduced the presentation, and confirmed that the NDT had received a clean audit opinion, commending the Department’s financial management, governance, and leadership. It had spent 96.4% of its R2.46 billion budget. AGSA had identified delays in the Development Bank of Southern Africa (DBSA)-managed infrastructure projects and underspending, primarily due to delays in filling vacancies and in implementing the Expanded Public Works Programme (EPWP). However, no financial misstatements were found, and governance remained stable. The Department achieved 93.48% of its annual targets, but was affected by vacancy issues.
SAT received a qualified audit opinion, mainly due to material misstatements related to cash balances, marketing expenditure, and payables. AGSA flagged a rise in irregular expenditure to R24.2 million, driven by non-compliance in procurement and supply chain management (SCM) processes. Weak financial controls were highlighted, alongside concerns over reporting accuracy.
AGSA recommended improvements to internal controls within SAT, especially around cash flow management and procurement processes. It emphasised that consequence management should be implemented to hold officials accountable for non-compliance. AGSA also urged SAT to fill critical vacancies needed to enhance financial oversight and accountability.
The Portfolio Committee was urged to:
Increase oversight of both entities, particularly focusing on corrective actions within SAT.
Monitor procurement compliance and ensure adherence to regulations.
Provide closer scrutiny of the DBSA-managed projects under the NDT, ensuring they were completed within appropriate timelines.
AGSA also emphasised the importance of stronger financial oversight, and highlighted the need for the audit committee to play a more proactive role, especially within SAT, to ensure future improvements in governance and financial management.
The Committee was urged to follow up on AGSA’s recommendations and ensure that both the NDT and SAT take corrective action to improve internal controls, procurement processes, and financial compliance.

Discussion
Ms E Linde (DA) noted the recurring recommendations made by AGSA to the Department, and questioned the lack of implementation. She asked whether AGSA had legal grounds to enforce compliance when the Department did not follow its recommendations.
Ms L Tito (EFF) highlighted the ongoing issues within SAT, particularly how SAT consistently hindered the Department’s overall performance. She emphasised the need for concrete measures to ensure AGSA’s recommendations were followed, expressing concerns that the same issues would arise next year. She inquired about AGSA’s monitoring role and the steps it would take if SAT failed to comply.
Ms L Ligaraba (ANC) acknowledged the Department's satisfactory performance, but raised concerns about discrepancies at SAT, specifically mentioning issues with payables, the resignation of board members, and the instability it caused. She expressed worry over SAT's failure to meet expectations, noting that some projects were only 15% complete with three months left in the financial year. She underscored that SAT's poor performance was undermining the Department’s overall efforts, particularly in terms of marketing.
Ms H Ismail (DA) expressed concern about infrastructure projects, specifically mentioning issues with "white elephants," vandalism, and a lack of coordination between provinces and municipalities. She questioned whether AGSA would support a recommendation for new financial feasibility studies for projects that had lagged behind, given changing economic circumstances.
She also raised concerns about SAT’s governance, highlighting irregular payments and the lack of clarity on whether internal audits had been conducted to oversee financial matters. She noted that the resignation of board members and the instability in SAT’s governance structures were major concerns. She asked for AGSA’s advice on how to address these governance issues to ensure progress and stability moving forward.
Ms G Mchunu (ANC) commended the Department of Tourism for achieving a clean audit, highlighting the positive image it reflected on the Department. She praised the Director-General and the team for their efforts, noting that the Portfolio Committee took pride in this achievement.
She expressed deep concern about the situation at SAT, particularly the fruitless and wasteful expenditure and the lack of consequence management. She questioned whether AGSA had identified internal audit processes within SAT, and if not, emphasised the need for monitoring and detection of irregularities at an early stage to safeguard public funds.
She referred to the irregular expenditure highlighted on page 12 of the report, noting the R9.9 million spent outside the scope of the contract and R7 million spent on events such as rugby and golf. She sought clarification on these expenses, and asked if AGSA had strict recommendations for improving accountability within SAT.
She fully supported ongoing monitoring of SAT, particularly the oversight by the accounting officer, the Director-General and the Ministry, to ensure improved performance. She also raised concerns about infrastructure projects progressing slowly, with some still only 15% complete despite nearing the end of the financial year. She stressed the importance of constant monitoring to prevent irregular or wasteful expenditure in such projects.
The Chairperson acknowledged the issues raised by AGSA, noting that many of the concerns highlighted had been previously discussed during a Committee workshop. After the workshop, the Committee engaged with South African Tourism on these matters, and intended to discuss them with SAT further during their upcoming session.
She pointed out that SAT lacked an internal audit division, with the board audit committee currently filling this role. She emphasised that this function should be handled by an internal audit division led by management, allowing the board audit committee to focus on oversight rather than operational tasks. This could require an amendment to the organogram, and it depends on the availability of funds to hire qualified personnel for this role.
The Chairperson further expressed concern about project management within the Department, emphasising the need for technical experts such as engineers and planners to oversee infrastructure projects. She noted that while the finance department could manage funds, it may lack the necessary expertise to monitor implementing agents effectively. She urged the Department to strengthen its capacity in project management to ensure that the Department’s oversight over SAT was effective.
She called for the appointment of a Deputy Director-General (DDG) responsible for ongoing oversight of SAT, ensuring regular reporting to the Minister. This would allow the Minister to intervene early, if necessary, based on reports from the DDG before engaging with the SAT board.
In closing, she commended the Department’s overall performance, but expressed concern that SAT’s irregular and fruitless expenditure could undermine the NDT’s success. She urged the Department to focus on mitigating these issues and enhancing oversight.
AGSA's response
Ms Matanzima responded to Ms Linde’s question regarding AGSA’s recommendations, emphasising that recommendations were made annually to promote transparency. AGSA tracks these recommendations in collaboration with the accounting officer and accounting authority. She referred to the Public Audit Amendment Act of 2018, explaining that it gives AGSA legal grounds to notify the accounting authority of material irregularities if non-compliance leads to financial loss or potential harm. AGSA collaborates with internal auditors to track issues throughout the year, but the lack of a head of internal audit at SAT hampers this collaboration.
Addressing Ms Tito’s concerns about monitoring, she said that AGSA conducts root cause analysis and status of record reviews to identify areas that may affect compliance or lead to regression in audit outcomes. These assessments allowed for proactive measures to be put in place.
On the points raised by Ms Ligaraba regarding instability at SAT, Ms Matanzima agreed that the frequent changes in the board posed challenges to AGSA’s investigations, as each new board required AGSA to restart engagements. She noted that the lack of continuity in the board had been discussed with the Minister and the Director-General.
In response to Ms Ismail, she acknowledged the need for technical oversight in infrastructure projects, as the Department's mandate did not include direct delivery of infrastructure. She highlighted the importance of monitoring mechanisms to address delays and cost overruns in projects managed by implementing agents.
Responding to Ms Mchunu’s concerns about irregular and fruitless expenditure, Ms Matanzima noted that internal audits and investigations had taken place, with reports available from the Department. She stressed the importance of compliance with supply chain management regulations, citing non-compliance highlighted on slide 12 regarding irregular expenditure.
Addressing the Chairperson’s points, she supported the need for qualified finance professionals in the internal audit unit and reiterated the necessity of strengthening oversight of SAT by the Department. She suggested enhancing the role of the Department’s representative within SAT to ensure better regulation and oversight.
Mr Mlangeni responded to the Chairperson’s proposal regarding the project management unit (PMU) issue. He supported the idea of conducting a cost-based analysis led by someone with the necessary technical expertise. He mentioned that AGSA’s strategy involved holding out-of-audit cycle engagements with the Department to share insights from AGSA’s activities and discuss best practices from other institutions, aiming to transition from identifying issues to creating solutions.
Mr Paseka Nenzinane, Senior Manager: Infrastructure, AGSA, stressed the need for stability within SAT’s management and board to improve monitoring and oversight. He reiterated the importance of an effective project management team within the Department and the need for consequence management when recommendations were not implemented. He stressed that every role player in the accountability process must fulfil their duties to ensure progress.
Mr Nicholas Mokwena, Deputy Business Leader: Tourism Portfolio, AGSA,  addressed concerns regarding supply chain management and AGSA’s recommendations for implementation control plans. He advocated the use of procurement plans to prevent non-compliance by ensuring all parties were aware of procurement timelines in advance, thus allowing for proper evaluation and execution of procurement activities.
Chairperson's concluding comments
The Chairperson acknowledged the responses and reiterated the need for stronger oversight within SAT, suggesting that a Deputy Director-General (DDG) level official should be appointed to manage this role, as the current director may lack the necessary authority. She thanked the AGSA team for their transparency and honesty in the presentation, noting that the recommendations would guide the Portfolio Committee going forward.
The Chairperson concluded the session, thanking AGSA for their well-prepared presentation and allowed a break before the meeting with the Department of Tourism.
Department of Tourism 2023/24 annual report presentation
Minister's overview
The meeting reconvened to consider the Department of Tourism's annual report for the 2023/24 financial year.
Minister De Lille highlighted that the Department had completed the policy review process, culminating in the gazetting of the new white paper on 4 October. The National Tourism sector strategy had been updated to align with the National Development Plan (NDP) through five strategic pillars, setting the framework for the Department's medium-term development plan.
She acknowledged the cost containment measures mandated by National Treasury, emphasising the shared responsibility between the executive authority and accounting authority. She expressed gratitude for AGSA’s guidance, recognising their support in enhancing the Department’s operations.
She addressed the instability of the SAT board, clarifying that the perception of board changes causing instability was misleading. She pointed out that mismanagement and the misappropriation of funds had occurred even during stable board tenures. The Minister highlighted investigations into specific transgressions, including those related to the dome and South African rugby, and indicated her intention to seek a proclamation from the President to facilitate recovery of misappropriated funds through the Special Investigating Unit (SIU).
She reported that there had been consequences for the acting CEO, who had been found guilty of misconduct and subsequently dismissed. However, this action alone did not address the recovery of financial losses. She concluded by emphasising the need for a stable administration and board, signalling her commitment to improving governance within SAT.
She then handed over to the Director General for further details on the Department's performance.
DG's introductory comments
Mr Tharage began by acknowledging the presence of Mr Mohith Maharaj, the Acting Chief Financial Officer (CFO). He commented that the Department might have lost significant funds without Mr Maharaj’s contributions, particularly in financial management. He emphasised the importance of his role in preparing the financial statements without external contractors, highlighting the meticulous review process to ensure no material misstatements.
He addressed the follow-up meeting with AGSA, the Minister and himself, indicating that he had requested remedial plans from SAT, which must be reported on monthly. He stressed the necessity of ongoing engagement to provide the Minister with accurate updates.
Regarding the Department's representation on the SAT board, he said that recommendations had been made to ensure that the person representing the Department did not oversee decisions, allowing for unbiased issue reporting.
He expressed a commitment to achieving a clean audit for the entire portfolio, noting that while they had received a clean audit, the struggles of SAT could undermine overall achievements. He said that capacity issues related to infrastructure had been a concern since the beginning. He shared that the Department had received approval to appoint a built environment specialist to enhance oversight.
The Minister of Public Service and Administration had granted approval for the appointment of two additional positions on a contract basis -- one at the level of chief director, and the other at director level. The recruitment process was underway, with advertisements already closed and committees approved for the selection of candidates.
Mr Tharage stressed the need for in-house capacity within the NDT to enhance the structural integration of strategy and business cases for tourism projects. He said project proposals had to go through the DBSA approval process and obtain final approval from the Minister of Finance before implementation.
He clarified that the delays observed in project implementation were not related to feasibility, but rather to execution. While some projects underwent a review process through the Government Technical Advisory Centre (GTAC), it had been identified that several lacked feasibility studies or environmental impact assessments (EIAs). This inadequacy contributed to fruitless and wasteful expenditure, as highlighted by AGSA.
He said that once decisions were made regarding a project's viability, the Department proceeded with its execution. He stressed the importance of strengthening capacity to manage projects effectively and ensure their completion. Regular briefings to the Committee by the DBSA on the status of projects were recommended to improve oversight.
He concluded by reflecting on the importance of thorough planning to avoid unnecessary expenditures without achieving the desired outcomes, underscoring that careful assessments were crucial before committing additional funds.
Department's 2023/24 performance
Mr Tharage provided an overview of the Tourism Department's performance, reporting an achievement rate of 93.48%, as noted by AGSA, with expenditure closely aligned at approximately 94%. He highlighted that Program 1 had achieved only 76.92% of its targets, primarily due to issues in filling vacancies. The filling of positions required a multi-step approval process involving the Minister of Public Service and Administration and the Minister of Finance to confirm budget availability. The DG expressed optimism about addressing the vacancy situation moving forward.
The Department had received a clean audit from the Auditor-General, with no material findings on financial statements or performance.
Programme 1: Administration
Achievements included 57.97% procurement from small, medium and micro enterprises (SMMEs) and 41.58% from women-owned businesses. The Department had paid 99.35% of invoices within 30 days, though it missed its 100% target due to late submissions. However, the vacancy rate was high at 11.5%, leading to recruitment strategies aimed at improving the gender balance, as senior management service (SMS) representation of women fell short at 46%.
Programme 2: Tourism Research Policy and International Relations
This focused on monitoring tourism projects and incubators. The Department developed a Tourism Statistics Plan and launched two knowledge systems (Tourism Skills & Employment Portal and Tourist Guide Information System) to enhance tourism research capabilities. South Africa’s tourism interests were advanced in global forums like the United Nations World Tourism Organisation (UNWTO), the G20, and the Brazil-Russia-India-China-SA (BRICS) bloc.
Program 3: Destination Development
There were significant projects, such as a maintenance and beautification programme across five provinces and monitoring of 29 community-based tourism projects. The Department had exceeded its target by creating 4 234 work opportunities through Working for Tourism initiatives.
Program 4: Tourism Sector Support Services
Incentive programmes like the Green Tourism Incentive Programme (GTIP), as well as various business incubators, were implemented. Notably, over 3 908 unemployed youth were trained in tourism safety standards, exceeding the target of 2 500.
The Department had spent 96.4% of its R2.46 billion budget, with underspending mainly due to unfilled vacancies and delayed job creation targets. The AGSA had confirmed a clean audit, although issues had arisen with DBSA-managed projects, including non-compliance with supply chain regulations and oversight failures.
Human resources (HR) data showed a workforce composition of 87.6% Africans, and 4.3% persons with disabilities. The SMS representation of women was 46%, with a target of 50%.
Areas identified for improvement included addressing compliance issues related to the DBSA, enhancing recruitment and retention, particularly among women and disabled employees, and improving project oversight to minimise duplication and logistical delays.
In conclusion, the Department emphasised a proactive approach to facilities management, focusing on sustainability and reducing recurring costs rather than reactive repairs.

Minister De Lille informed the Committee about South Africa's recent assumption of the G20 tourism portfolio from Brazil. An inter-ministerial committee meeting had been held to discuss preparations, with a tourism ministerial meeting scheduled for 12 September 2025. The first working group meeting would be virtual, while the second would be a physical meeting at the Indaba in May 2025, with around 300 delegates from G20 countries expected.
She highlighted the importance of collaboration with parliamentary structures, as this was the first G20 summit to be hosted in Africa. The actual summit was planned for 27-28 November, 2025, with over 130 meetings to take place beforehand. She mentioned the preparation of tourism packages for over 3 000 delegates, encouraging collaboration with the private sector and tour operators.
The Chairperson acknowledged the Minister’s insights, and indicated that the Committee would explore how Parliament could engage effectively in the G20 preparations.
Discussion
Ms Tito raised concerns regarding the appointment of Dr Gregory Davids as chairperson of SAT, questioning why he was appointed given his previous role during times of mismanagement. She sought clarity on who ensured project alignment with the tourism mandate, and expressed concern over the Department's expenditure in relation to performance targets. She questioned the consequences of fruitless and wasteful expenditure related to travel bookings, inquired about the impact of expired contracts and unfilled vacancies, and sought information on initiatives for improving domestic tourism packages for the G20.
Ms Ligaraba congratulated the Department on achieving a clean audit opinion and meeting performance targets, including creating 4 234 work opportunities against a target of 4 133. She praised the training of 3 908 youth and 276 SMMEs on safe tourism operations. However, she expressed concern over the underperformance of SAT, and asked if the Department had an action plan to address AGSA’s findings. She raised issues regarding the recruitment process, the cause of resignations, and the delay in payments. She emphasised that board oversight was essential, and that the current situation could adversely affect job creation and tourism efforts.
Ms E Linde (DA) expressed relief over the Department receiving a clean audit, but raised serious concerns about SAT’s performance, highlighting ongoing issues with irregular expenditure. She stressed the importance of the new CFO, urging them to train SAT staff for better reporting practices and addressing administrative issues rather than assuming theft as the primary cause of financial problems. She requested that the new CFO present an action plan to address material irregularities. She inquired about the number and nature of internal investigations being conducted by the SIU regarding financial mismanagement and accountability.
She pointed out a discrepancy between the AG’s report, indicating 93% performance achievement, and the Department's report, which cited 89%, raising concerns about the reliability of the information being presented. She commended the DG for his clarity in presenting action plans and achievements for Programmes 2 and 3, and requested regular updates on these reports for continued oversight.
Ms G Mchunu focused her inquiry on page 21, which addressed areas requiring improvement. She sought clarity on the measures that would be implemented to strengthen compliance and oversight within the supply chain management process, specifically asking for preventive measures to avoid future instances of non-compliance.
Ms Ismail raised several questions regarding the Tourism Master Plan. She inquired about the correlation between national and provincial tourism master plans, asking how aligned the strategies were and how they fit into the national framework.
Regarding the instability at SAT, she sought clarity on the reasons behind board member appointments, specifically questioning why Dr Davids, a previous board member during times of mismanagement, had been reappointed as chairperson. She asked for details about the ongoing investigations related to the dome and SA Rugby, and whether the issues pertained to financial misconduct or other irregularities.
She expressed concern about the Department's handling of internal audits, requesting that progress reports on remedial plans for SAT be provided to keep the Committee informed. She questioned why issues had escalated to the point of irregularity without earlier detection and consequence management.
Addressing infrastructure projects, she requested quarterly updates, and information on the vacancy of the head of the internal audit committee. She emphasised the need for proactive measures to prevent future delays and compliance issues, particularly concerning the certificates for the Compensation for Occupational Injuries and Diseases Act (COIDA).
She requested clarification on claims against the Department, and asked for examples of unfair dismissals to better understand and prevent such occurrences. With respect to the Pilanesburg Incubation Programme, she sought details on costs and any unauthorised expenditure.
She also raised concerns about the vetting process for tour guides and the disparity in payment practices among them. She asked how the Department was facilitating SMMEs, and suggested that officials should be held accountable for wasteful expenditure related to unfulfilled bookings.
Ms T Mchunu (ANC) expressed concern regarding the stability of the SAT board in terms of governance and performance. She challenged the Minister’s assertion that board changes did not contribute to instability, emphasising the importance of maintaining governance audit integrity. She urged the Department to expedite the vacancy filling process due to the public service's lengthy approval requirements, which could hinder performance.
She asked if there was an internal plan to ensure the transfer of knowledge and capacity from contract-based posts once they end. She praised efforts to support persons with disabilities, and inquired about the existence of exit interviews for resigning employees to better understand their reasons for leaving.
She requested detailed presentations on the White Paper and master plan to better equip Committee Members for discussions. She appreciated the initiation of an audit on incubation programmes, and sought information on the benefits for SMMEs from previously disadvantaged areas, including the percentage of beneficiaries. She asked for more details on astrotourism innovation and how it would benefit communities.
Lastly, she questioned the Department's EPWP budget utilisation, and whether plans were in place to ensure full access to this budget, stressing the need to consider youth employment, particularly for students in tourism-related courses.
The Chairperson referred to the importance of integrating interns into the Department’s technical teams to train and upskill young graduates, ensuring they possess the necessary technical skills. She noted that delays in project implementation could lead to project failures, and advocated holding professionals and contractors accountable by tracking their compliance and terminating or reappointing them as needed.
She called for a turnaround strategy at SAT, acknowledging the appointment of the new CEO, who had an auditing background. She encouraged the CEO to leverage her skills to identify financial loopholes and implement necessary changes. She expressed support for the DG’s approach to maintaining oversight through a director, rather than the DG sitting directly on the board. She appreciated the commitment to increased interaction with SAT, calling for visible improvements by early January to enhance performance ahead of the financial year-end audits.
Minister's response
Minister De Lille began by thanking the Committee, and reaffirmed that any unanswered questions would be addressed later. She clarified that perceptions of instability within the SAT board stemmed from historical mismanagement rather than the current board's composition. She noted that there were years when the AG had been unable to audit SAT’s financial records.
She explained that the ministerial actions taken were necessary under the law to address governance issues. She pointed out that her involvement with the Tottenham Hotspur deal had resulted in significant savings for the country, emphasising her commitment to accountability.
She raised concerns about excessive board meetings, revealing that out of 19 special meetings, proper protocols were not followed. The chairperson and deputy chair had dominated discussions, creating an environment where other board members felt intimidated. This lack of oversight had led to significant financial implications, as they had accrued over R900 000 in board fees within six months from an allocated budget of R1.4 million.
She would meet with the board to ensure all members understood their responsibilities under the Tourism Act, the Public Finance Management Act, and the governance principles outlined in the King IV Report. She emphasised the need for transparency and proper governance to protect taxpayer funds, asserting her commitment to improving SAT’s operational integrity.
She explained her rationale for appointing Dr Davids to the SAT board, emphasising his expertise in governance and risk. She acknowledged the perception of instability within the board, stating that historical mismanagement and governance issues had persisted over the years, complicating the current situation.
She highlighted her obligation to act in accordance with the law when addressing governance issues, noting that her actions were necessary for accountability. She elaborated on the challenges faced, citing excessive board meetings without proper procedures, and the need for transparency regarding board fees, which had exceeded budget allocations.
She had requested an independent investigator to examine the allegations made by board members, reinforcing her commitment to financial oversight. She was determined to improve SAT's governance structure, and confirmed her acceptance of all the recommendations made by the Committee.
Regarding tourism, she reported that domestic tourism had contributed R121 billion to the economy in 2023, while international tourism had added R95 billion, supporting 1.7 million jobs. She acknowledged the importance of addressing visa issues and establishing an inter-ministerial committee to streamline the visa process, noting the collaboration with the Tourism Business Council and hiring a private entity, Genesis, to review visa backlogs.
She outlined agreed-upon measures regarding SAT, stating that the remedial actions from the AGSA would be incorporated into the service level agreement, which had to be monitored monthly. She noted that while the Department had achieved a clean audit, SAT had received a qualified audit, necessitating increased oversight.
She discussed the recent agritourism initiative in collaboration with the Northern Cape government and economist, Dr Wandile Sihlobo, emphasising the resources allocated for building a science information centre next year.
She acknowledged the challenges in filling vacancies, stating that delays affected both the Department and DBSA. She highlighted the need for timely action, citing the high number of applications -- 9 000 applications for two positions for a one-year contract --received for positions within the Department, indicating a strong demand for government jobs. She mentioned the ongoing process of filling the DDG for the Corporate Services position, which had faced complications due to the availability of deputy ministers.
She clarified that the CFO was not a board member, and had resigned amidst ongoing investigations related to irregular expenditure, including R9.9 million for services outside the contract scope, and R7.4 million related to the rugby event. She acknowledged that the SAT board's governance had issues, noting that excessive meetings had been held without adherence to proper procedures.
She said that rigorous contract management within supply chain management processes was needed to prevent contractors from performing poorly. She pointed out that current contracts lacked penalties, which led to non-compliance, prompting her decision not to renew the contract with DBSA until such penalties were included.
She highlighted that R1.2 billion was being allocated to the Tourism Equity Fund, with 80% directed toward existing businesses for growth, and 20% for new ventures. She had had to call a meeting with the Minister of Small Business Development to address violations of the service level agreement (SLA).
Regarding the Tourism Master Plan, she confirmed consultations with municipalities and the private sector, ensuring inclusivity in tourism packages for the G20. She expressed enthusiasm for the completed policy review process, and emphasised the need for implementation of new policies.
She expressed concerns about officials not attending engagements in the Northern Cape, indicating that the DG would address this matter.
She acknowledged significant challenges with the EPWP, indicating that funds meant for infrastructure projects had been diverted to employ 2 305 tourism monitors. She and the DG had agreed to realign the EPWP funding, allowing infrastructure projects to apply for necessary funds directly.
She emphasised the need to strengthen project management skills within the Department, citing the appointment of a built environment professional and plans to bring in candidates with engineering and quantity surveying backgrounds for practical training.
She noted that an audit had revealed that many projects would be dropped due to inadequate feasibility studies, and a cleanup of the project list would occur. The Minister mentioned a budget of approximately R30 million for demand-led skills training to address sector needs, adding that it was important to use local universities for skills development rather than relying on service providers.
In terms of digital transformation, she highlighted collaboration with UJ University to improve government efficiency through technology, including the use of artificial intelligence (AI). She pointed out that South Africa was lagging behind other countries like Mauritius and the Seychelles in adopting these technologies. She assured the Committee that a comprehensive digital transformation strategy was being developed.
DG's response
Mr Tharage addressed concerns about staff resignations, clarifying that many were ministerial staff who would legally remain with the Department until the end of the financial year. He provided specific examples of senior management who had left for new opportunities, highlighting that these changes affected meeting diversity targets, especially among women.
Regarding discrepancies in performance reporting, he explained that the Department had reported 93%, while the AG had indicated 89% based on audited data, with the clean audit outcome reflecting overall performance.
He detailed the process for investigating and documenting fruitless and wasteful expenditure incidents, stating that if no valid reason for an expense existed, the Department would seek recovery of the funds. He emphasised the effectiveness of their system in managing these expenditures.
He said the powers of the DG in overseeing the board's operations were limited. While the board was the accounting officer responsible for the entity's financial accountability, his role involved advising the Minister on holding the board accountable, based on the information provided by them. He acknowledged the challenges of being an accounting officer, highlighting the need for clear powers and stating that stability within the finance department relied on the ongoing support of the CFO. This context was crucial for understanding the governance dynamics at play within the Department.
To strengthen compliance, he mentioned regular internal communications, meetings with the DBSA, and site visits to address issues. He also noted the necessity of ongoing collaboration with the Accountant General to resolve problems effectively.
Chairperson' concluding remarks
The Chairperson thanked the Department and AGSA for their presentations. She requested the DG or a DDG to attend the upcoming SAT meeting to address the remaining questions. She stressed the need for quarterly reporting on the audit action plan of the National Department of Tourism. She applauded the Department for achieving a clean audit, while stressing the importance of timely payments to service providers.
The meeting was adjourned.
