Meeting Summary
Video
Ad Hoc Committee on Gen Mkhwanazi’s Allegations
The Ad Hoc Committee heard evidence from Deputy Minister Shela Boshielo on allegations about corruption and dysfunction within the South African Police Service, focusing on the disbandment of the Political Killings Task Team. The Deputy Minister detailed her experience, roles, and interactions with senior police leadership. She first learned about the Minister’s directive to disband the Political Killings Task Team through social media and was subsequently informed by the Minister. She expressed disappointment to the Committee over the lack of consultation. The Committee questioned her about the strained relationship between suspended Police Minister Mchunu and National Police Commissioner Masemola, highlighting tensions and poor communication that hampered coordination and operational effectiveness. Dr Boshielo emphasised the need for collaborative engagement between Executive and accounting officers in government and described how leadership divisions worsened internal frustrations.
The Committee raised several concerns, notably the lack of effective communication and cooperation between senior SAPS officials, which they identified as exacerbating divisions and impairing the police service’s ability to function efficiently. There was also significant worry about allegations of corruption and political interference, especially about the tender scandal involving Mr Vusimusi ‘Cat’ Matlala and the purported attempt to bug the Deputy Minister’s phone. The Committee pressed Dr Boshielo on how these issues were handled internally and the overall impact on the integrity and operational capacity of SAPS. Additionally, the Committee pointed to the underperformance in critical areas such as Detective Services, the slow investigation of corruption within SAPS, and the failure to meet crime-fighting technological targets. The urgency of restoring public trust through accountability, transparency, and improved leadership, was noted as well as strengthening oversight institutions like the Independent Police Investigative Directorate and parliamentary committees. Dr Boshielo concluded by advocating for a police service that respects the Constitution and collaborates effectively across leadership levels to rebuild public confidence.

Regarding the tender, he was adamant. He called a meeting with the National Commissioner, SCM, and CFO. They explained their process, but he insisted. After four days, they presented the correct information, but he still insisted the tender be cancelled. I do not know all reasons, and tenders are not my responsibility.
Mr Nomvalo: The Committee was informed that the Minister instructed the National Commissioner regarding the tender to appoint internal audit, and then the tender was cancelled following the audit report. Are you aware of that?
Deputy Minister Boshielo: I think I answered that. The Minister had the conversation with the National Commissioner, who told this Committee that he cancelled the tender and thanked the Minister.
Mr Nomvalo: Was the cancellation due to the audit report, or a unilateral decision by the Minister?
Deputy Minister Boshielo: I think they agreed with the National Commissioner on the process. The tender was cancelled after four or five months, not immediately.
Mr Mncwango: There is a worrying coincidence. Things happened in December: the meeting cancelling the tender, the letter affecting PKTT, and Mr Matlala meeting former Minister Cele on 6 December, informing him of a tender of R350–360 million, with strings attached for campaign financing. It appears that information leaked, angering certain quarters, leading to the directive of 31 December. What would you say to someone connecting these dots?
Deputy Minister Boshielo: I do not know who met whom. The letter was 31 December, but the tender matter was in January. The tender was awarded in June, before we assumed office on 2 July, when Minister Cele was still Minister. Other alleged interactions, I do not know.
Mr Mncwango: On record, you will have sight of this. Regarding PKTT operations, did they do a good job? You must have read reports.
Deputy Minister Boshielo: Referring to the report presented on 5 March, which gave background on formation, cases dealt with, successes, budget and other matters, I think they achieved some success in their work.
Mr Mncwango: Based on that report, are the reasons for disbandment in the directive plausible?
Deputy Minister Boshielo: I said in my statement that it was practically impossible for PKTT to be disbanded on 31 December. A consultative process would have provided comprehensive grounds for the Minister to confirm the disbandment decision. The instruction to immediately disband PKTT posed serious risk of impossibility of implementation. A proper report would have contained the status of investigations, operational impact, and assessment of whether PKTT still served its purpose.
Mr Mncwango: My last question is on your conclusion. Paragraph 47 highlights the need to reinforce and rebuild trust in SAPS, beginning with integrity, accountability, principled leadership. Can you unpack principled leadership?
Deputy Minister Boshielo: Principled leadership is working together as a team. When there is a problem, addressing it immediately and providing a solution. Where things are not working, calling the relevant people to fix it. It is not about taking sides.
Final questions
Mr Skosana: When I was asking, I think maybe I've omitted that, I just wanted to find out whether the Department of Planning and Monitoring have indeed consulted, or perhaps raised issues, especially when you told us that there was no delegation of powers. They were aware of that, because I believe that department is responsible as well to make sure that everything works well. All the departments in particular, were they aware of what was happening between you?
Deputy Minister Boshielo: Honestly, I would not be able to answer that one on behalf of other departments.
Mr Skosana: I'm not saying on the other department. I'm just saying that because the Department of Planning and Monitoring, what the President has said is that all the departments, all the departments, there will be a violation of everybody. Forget the lifestyle audit. What I'm saying is they were aware that you have a problem, not that we're not working. I think let's put that aside, the issue that they were in consultation. Perhaps we've been ever consulted or felt perhaps we have done better by going to them to say we are stuck here, not necessarily the President. I'm talking about that department, because that's their responsibility.
Deputy Minister Boshielo: I did not know about that responsibility and that instruction.
Ms Mathys: Mine's not really a question, but just for our leaders to please follow up with the National Commissioner. I don't recall us getting a copy of the report of the investigation that led to the cancelling of the tender. And just for clarity, we are not saying that we support or do not support the cancelling of the tender. The issue here is whether it was done properly, whether there was political interference, and how the matter went, so we must get the report, and also who were the investigators that investigated the tender that led to it being cancelled. But to be clear, we are glad that it was cancelled, we just want to know if it was done properly.
Adv Arendse: Just to continue, the fish and pond and sea analogy, it seems to me that you're a very small fish in a huge pond, and I want to thank you as the team of evidence leaders for your cooperation and for your evidence here today and your insights. I think the tendency is to say, 'No, you hear Mr ‘Cat’ Matlala, you hear about Mr Brown Mogotsi in this thing.' But even though there are millions and millions, hundreds of millions of Rand involved, that's much bigger than that. That's what we've heard from the Portfolio Committee over a period of so many billions of Rand that's been lost, stolen, leaked and misappropriated.
Firstly, we are talking about a huge department, maybe the biggest in government. It's difficult to know where to start. This rot that we now know has manifested: is it the whole appointments procedure, the appointment of ministers, the appointment of deputy ministers, the allocation of functions to you? For example, you may have been appointed. No one would have had a problem with the allocation of powers and functions. But you may have been appointed allocations, tasks and powers that are completely innocuous, and that would have had no effect on what had happened. That could have been done deliberately, intentionally, by design or accidentally. We don't know.
Or is it the appointment of the most senior management, the National Commissioner, Deputy Commissioners, divisional heads – some by the President; others by the Minister? The procurement, of course, can you be held accountable for the appointment of an Evaluation Bid Committee, the Adjudication Committee, and then the authority you finally sign off on the big procurement – the organisational design? Your evidence here today was – and that's important; it's not useless evidence – that you weren't even aware of the PKTT until, I think you said firstly it was in a letter, and then you became more aware of it on the 5th of March when you went to Parliament.
And like many of us just heard in the presentation there, issues of Crime Intelligence are just way above your head. Then there's this oversight here in Parliament itself. Is that effective? Or is it often the case that it's too late, the horse has already bolted? The infiltration in all these other sections of the police departments has already taken place, and by the time we come here, the best we can do, if at all, is some damage control.
Then there's the difficult question, which you tried your best to avoid because you're not a lawyer, but there's the issue of Executive authority and operational management. Is there a bright line, or is there some vague area where ministers and deputy ministers should still assume some control, but obviously people who are honest and committed to cleaning up the process? So there's the issue of competence and whether people are qualified to hold certain positions, and vetting processes. Are people just being appointed, and the next thing you hear, no, actually they've got some hidden or dark past and they should not have been appointed?
So thank you, Chair, Deputy Ministers, a bit of a speech. But I just thought I'd summarise my own thoughts in view of your evidence today, and we'll take it further tomorrow with the Acting Minister.
Final thoughts from Members
Mr Sauls: I wanted to do this privately, Deputy Minister, but upon reflection, I think it's best I do it publicly. Thank you for being a black female South African with such academic prowess, representing yourself in the way that you do. Thank you that we can say you are there on merit and your qualifications are not fake. You are a legitimate doctorate holder, and so you must be the face of what this country has fought for: a black female South African academic of your calibre. Thank you.
Deputy Minister Boshielo: I’m humbled.
Chairperson: He says an African female from one of the most rural provinces of the Republic with a doctoral degree, accompanied by 30 other qualifications, and not from one institution, from many institutions. This is what we have been saying, Deputy Minister Dr Boshielo, about the two female advocates that work with Senior Counsel, but from the progressive perspective, it's very encouraging to see young African females defying the odds, rising against adversity, to raise their hands to be counted amongst the best. I know when you were the MEC of Education in Limpopo, you used to call yourself Deputy Mother because you were acting in loco parentis on behalf of the parents who had left their children under your supervision.
And what the members are saying here is that you must continue and live to be the inspiration that you are when you come here, the SC announces to South Africans that you are a doctor with 30 other qualifications. It lives up to our mission in the National Development Plan, when we say we want to build a global citizen. But you know the cherry on top is when you say your doctoral degree is on technology, which is the economy we live in today. And Ms Sangoni is going to agree with me 120%, when we think about technology, in fact, you must inspire many other South Africans to say, when we think about technology, we must run like Usain Bolt on the tracks, we must run like our own Akani Simbine on the tracks when technology comes to mind, because technology is the world we live in today, and it comes in paradigms all the time. It’s going to be with us, and that is why we now talk about the Fourth Industrial Revolution. I have had others talking about the Fifth Industrial Revolution. Academically, I don't know if the Fifth Industrial Revolution is already there, but anything is possible with technology. And also for that, we want to thank you so much. Members have given you accolades, and you are the first female witness to appear before us. It will only be fair on my part, and living up to my reputation as the benevolent Chair, to give you a chance to say something if there is anything that you would like to say.
Deputy Minister Boshielo: Thank you very much, Chairperson. I'm actually trembling because I'm being humbled to appear before the Committee. The support, I must say, that has been given by the Senior Counsel and its colleagues, and also the members, when they were asking questions, they were direct and not personal. But also, I learned a lot from what they were saying, the calmness that was asked. You could see it was in good spirit, and I'm more than prepared and now energised to even do my best so that we can get this organisation to be right. And also with Mr Cameron, I know you warned me, and Mr Skosana and Mr Cameron, I really, really appreciate all of you. Mr Sauls, I don't want to cry. I honestly don't want to cry, but you really humbled me. You really humbled me, because that's what we want to show, that as Black or African women, we are able and capable, because others will think we got positions because of favour or because we slept with somebody, but your observation today is what I wanted to portray to everybody today. So thank you very much, and really, really makes me want to cry. And Chairperson, thanks very much for your fairness, and you know how you conducted this. And Ms Mathys, and Ms Sangoni, all of you, I'm grateful and I'm humbled. Thank you very much.
Closing Remarks
Chairperson: Members, let me make this announcement. First of all, I must congratulate you for the manner in which you did your business today. And I always say that it is the quality of interactions in a team that wins games, and I now see that we have become a goal-scoring machine. Thank you so much for your conduct in the manner in which we do our work, always very agile and incisive. SC, thank you so much for today's business, and Deputy Minister Dr Boshielo, we want to thank you. The ever-working staff of Parliament, thank you so much. The exercise that we have embarked upon is enervating, but you always appear indefatigable. So we want to thank you so much for that, and the members of the media who are with us here this week. Tomorrow is Acting Minister Cachalia, and then we have IDAC on Thursday and Friday. We're going to start at 10:00 tomorrow.
The meeting was adjourned at 17:17.
