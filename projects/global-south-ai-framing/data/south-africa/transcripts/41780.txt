Western Cape Government Annual Reports
The Standing Committee on Police Oversight and Community Safety met to consider the 2024/25 Annual Reports of the Department of Police Oversight and Community Safety (POCS), the Western Cape Liquor Authority (WCLA), and the Office of the Police Ombudsman.
The Minister emphasised that safety underpins development and social cohesion, noting persistently high levels of violent crime and the need for an integrated, whole-of-government response. The Acting Head of Department reported that the Department had achieved most of its Annual Performance Plan targets, with one deliverable outstanding. He also outlined measures to enhance technology integration and data sharing across law enforcement entities.
Members questioned the WCLA on its strategic alignment within the safety portfolio, persistent Board and staff vacancies, the uneven geographic distribution of liquor outlets, slow licence turnaround times, and a large number of incomplete applications. The WCLA acknowledged the staffing and governance challenges and outlined efforts to improve turnaround times and strengthen joint enforcement with the South African Police Service.
During the engagement with the Police Ombudsman, Members noted a decline in the number of complaints received. The Police Ombudsman cautioned that this should not be interpreted as a sign of growing public confidence. They presented the newly introduced Alternative Dispute Resolution mechanism aimed at expediting case outcomes and confirmed ongoing investigator vacancies within their office.
Members requested tangible outcomes from the cooperation agreement between the Department, SAPS, and the City of Cape Town. In response, the Department reported that 48 joint operations had been conducted and that collaborative workstreams were in progress regarding camera inventories, data-sharing systems, and technology solutions. Members also expressed concern about the Department’s human-resource challenges, including high staff turnover, increased incapacity leave, and employment equity imbalances. The Department attributed the turnover to market-related factors and an ageing workforce.
The Committee resolved to conduct an on-site engagement with the Office of the Police Ombudsman, to undertake an oversight visit to the provincial CCTV centre, and convene a joint meeting with the City of Cape Town and the Minister to review the Law Enforcement Advancement Plan (LEAP), assess intergovernmental cooperation, and follow up on vacancies and human-resource matters. The meeting concluded with a moment of silence in honour of police and LEAP officers who had lost their lives in the line of duty.
Meeting report
Opening remarks
The Chairperson noted that no public submissions had been received for the meeting. She indicated that there were no members of the public present at that stage but mentioned that attendees might join as the meeting progressed.
She informed Members that the proceedings were being live-streamed on YouTube to allow members of the public to follow the discussions remotely.
The Chairperson stated that the purpose of the meeting was to consider the 2024/25 Annual Reports of the Western Cape Department of Police Oversight and Community Safety (POCS), the Western Cape Liquor Authority (WCLA), and the Office of the Police Ombudsman.
She outlined the programme for the meeting, noting that Ms Anroux Marais, Minister, Western Cape POCS, and Mr Hilton Arendse, Acting Head of Department, Western Cape POCS, would first provide introductory remarks, after which Members would be allowed to ask questions. The Committee would then engage with the WCLA and the Western Cape Provincial Police Ombudsman, followed by a short break before proceeding with the Department’s presentation.
She emphasised the importance of maintaining progress during the meeting and informed Members that each would be allocated three minutes per round of questioning. Additional rounds would be accommodated if required, but questions were expected to remain focused on the reports under discussion.

Opening remarks by the Minister
Minister Marais opened her remarks by greeting Members and noting that, while there were no members of the public present, some might be joining the proceedings online.
Minister Marais stated that as Members of the Provincial Legislature, they were entrusted with a collective responsibility that transcended political affiliations and party lines. She said this responsibility was to ensure the safety, dignity, and well-being of every person living in the Western Cape.
She emphasised that creating a safer province must be a shared objective among all stakeholders. Safety, she noted, was not merely one policy goal among others, but the foundation upon which sustainable development, social cohesion, and public trust were built.
Despite the progress made, Minister Marais acknowledged that the province continued to experience unacceptably high levels of violent crime and senseless killings. For this reason, she stressed the importance of continuously empowering communities — not only by supporting the South African Police Service (SAPS) to enhance policing outcomes, but also by strengthening community-led safety initiatives and coordinating violence prevention activities across all government Departments.
She further stated that the safety portfolio should not be viewed as a single Departmental programme, but as a key driver of an integrated, whole-of-government and whole-of-society approach. When Departments aligned their strategies, when municipalities and provincial structures worked together, and when coordination took place both horizontally and vertically, governments could move beyond good intentions towards achieving lasting impact.
Minister Marais added that the people of the province were looking to the government for leadership — not only in law enforcement and oversight, but in shaping a society where families could feel safe walking in their neighbourhoods, where young people were protected and given opportunities, and where safety became a lived reality rather than an aspiration.
She expressed appreciation to officials of the Department for their continued dedication, commending their willingness to go beyond the call of duty by attending community engagements late at night and working closely with residents to develop interventions that addressed safety challenges. She specifically extended gratitude to Mr Simion George, Chief Executive Officer (CEO), WCLA and retired Major General Oswald Reddy, Western Cape Provincial Police Ombudsman.
Minister Marais concluded her opening remarks by thanking Mr Arendse for his leadership and commitment. She indicated that this would be his final appearance before the Committee, as a new Head of Department would assume office in January 2026.

Mr Arendse thanked Minister Marais, the Chairperson, Members, and colleagues. He stated that the Department was presenting its Annual Report together with those of the WCLA and the Office of the Police Ombudsman.
He reiterated Minister Marais’s sentiments, highlighting that the Department had a deeply committed team whose members took community challenges personally and worked with dedication to make a tangible difference. However, he acknowledged that the Department operated within the constraints of limited resources, scope, and mandate. Despite these challenges, he stated that the team had performed admirably and achieved the majority of the objectives set out in the Annual Performance Plan.
Mr Arendse reported that there was only one deliverable that had not been achieved, which would be explained later during the engagement. He expressed pride in the Department’s achievements and noted that both he and Minister Marais valued the team’s hard work and professionalism.
He concluded by saying that the Department looked forward to the Committee’s engagement and guidance on how to improve performance and strengthen delivery in the next financial year.

Introductory remarks by the Western Cape Liquor Authority (WCLA)
Mr George thanked the Chairperson and provided introductory remarks. He noted that several years ago, the Authority became an entity under the custodianship of the Department. This alignment, he explained, was intended to advance the Western Cape Government’s alcohol-related harm reduction strategy, recognising safety as a central component of regulation.
He stated that this structural shift demonstrated the Province’s commitment to integrating safety within regulatory frameworks. He further explained that alcohol availability and accessibility were strongly linked to violence and trauma in communities, and that the Authority’s work directly supported the broader objectives of the Western Cape Government and the Department.
Mr George emphasised that the Authority sought to present its Annual Report with honesty and transparency, reflecting both its achievements and its ongoing challenges. He noted that while the entity prioritised its regulatory mandate, this had sometimes led to underinvestment in essential support functions such as information and communications technology (ICT) systems, human resources, and organisational development. Over time, these gaps had created operational strain. However, he indicated that the Authority had since made deliberate efforts to address these shortcomings and that the improvements would be reflected in the current reporting period.

Discussion on Part A: General Information
The Chairperson noted that Members were expected to have read and internalised the WCLA’s Annual Report. She stated that the discussion would focus on Part A, which contained general information, including abbreviations, Mr George’s overview, the statement of responsibility and confirmation, as well as the strategic overview outlining the Authority’s mission, vision, and values. It also covered the legislative and other mandates and the organisational structure, concluding on page 14.
She reminded Members to indicate the relevant page number when posing questions to allow for easier reference. Each Member would be allocated three minutes per question to ensure smooth progress. Further rounds of questions would be allowed if necessary. She then opened the floor for questions on Part A, covering pages 5 to 14.
Mr Ngqentsu referred to page nine of the report, noting that the CEO had observed that the liquor sector was a key driver of economic growth in the Western Cape. He asked whether, given this acknowledgement, the WCLA was strategically located within the Department, rather than under the Western Cape Department of Economic Development and Tourism (DEDAT). He reasoned that economic entities were typically housed within the economic development portfolio, whereas the Authority operated within a Department primarily focused on policing and safety.
He further elaborated that the liquor industry contributed significantly to both the formal and informal sectors, with the informal segment being largely unregulated. He suggested that this unregulated growth might be contributing to the proliferation of illegal liquor outlets across the province. He therefore asked for the Authority’s view on whether it might be more appropriate for it to be placed under the economic development portfolio.
Mr Ngqentsu then referred to page 14, which detailed the organisational structure. He noted the presence of two vacancies on the Authority’s Board and requested clarification on how long these positions had remained vacant. He asked to what extent these vacancies had affected the Authority’s operations and whether there were any plans in place to fill them.
Mr George explained that prior to the introduction of the new legislation in 2012, the regulation of liquor had been located within the DEDAT. Drawing from his experience in both POCS, he noted that there were varying arguments regarding the most appropriate placement of the Authority.
He stated that, nationally, the regulation of liquor generally fell under the Department of Trade, Industry and Competition (DTIC), and in some provinces, under similar economic Departments. However, the Western Cape had adopted a different approach based on an understanding of trauma and public safety concerns. The current placement of the Authority within the POCS allowed the regulation of liquor to be viewed through the lens of safety, focusing on the impact of alcohol on communities and society. He pointed out that studies, although slightly dated, had shown that between 50% and 60% of trauma incidents were linked to alcohol consumption.
Mr George emphasised that while the liquor industry was an important economic driver, the nature of the product as a habit-forming and potentially toxic substance required stricter regulation and safety oversight. He said this focus on safety did not hinder economic growth or investment, as the sector continued to attract investors both locally and internationally, particularly in the Western Cape. He added that the liquor industry remained highly resilient, maintaining double-digit returns — often between 20% and 30% regardless of broader economic conditions. This demonstrated, he concluded, that the sector did not require additional economic support compared to other industries
.
Mr George further added that the two vacancies on the WCLA Board had existed since March 2024. He clarified that initially there had been one vacancy following the appointment of the new Board, and a second vacancy arose in November 2024 when the term of Adv Sibonile Khoza as Board Chairperson came to an end. He acknowledged that these vacancies had created operational challenges, particularly regarding quorum requirements, but noted that the remaining Board members had made themselves available to ensure that meetings could proceed.
He stated that while the vacancies had made coordination more demanding, the Board had managed to maintain functionality through careful planning and the commitment of its members. He added that during a previous engagement with the Committee on the matter, the Authority had indicated its capacity to continue operations despite the reduced number of members.
Mr George also noted that, upon reviewing the sitting fees for the Board, it was found that they were among the lowest in the province. He said this issue would be raised with the custodial Department and the Provincial Treasury, to align the remuneration to at least the level paid to members of the Western Cape Gambling and Racing Board.
Mr Ngqentsu stated that he had been researching to support his argument regarding the WCLA’s classification as a key economic driver. He noted that the Authority had identified itself as an important contributor to the provincial economy, a view he shared and believed was supported by evidence. However, he expressed concern that this economic role appeared to be in contradiction with the Authority’s stated vision and mission. He observed that, while the Annual Report on page 11 described the vision as reducing alcohol-related harm through effective regulation, and the mission as leading and promoting public participation to mitigate the impact of alcohol, neither made reference to economic growth or development. He regarded this omission as a misalignment between the Authority’s practical economic contribution and its strategic direction, and asked whether the Authority acknowledged this contradiction and intended to address it.
The Chairperson thanked him and added a question of her own. She referred to the recommendations made by the Committee on broadening the advertising process for Board vacancies in order to attract a wider pool of applicants. She asked whether any progress had been made in implementing those recommendations.
Mr George responded that, following the Committee’s recommendation, Minister Marais had supported the proposal for the re-advertisement of the Board vacancies. He informed the Committee that the Department was currently managing that process and was responding to the communication received from the Committee and Parliament regarding the matter.
Addressing the point raised by Mr Ngqentsu, he explained that the WCLA operated within a specific legislative mandate, which was prescriptive and centred on the principle of regulation in the public interest. He clarified that any licence granted by the Authority — particularly through the Licensing Tribunal — had to demonstrate that it would serve the public good. He further noted that the granting of a licence was not permanent but rather a concession that required the licensee to meet public interest conditions continuously. He added that the concept of public interest could include economic considerations where the granting of a licence facilitated local economic activity. He concluded that these factors should be viewed holistically, recognising that economic benefit and public safety could coexist within the regulatory framework.
The Chairperson asked if there were any further questions. She remarked that the point raised could form a sound basis for a Committee resolution, suggesting that the Committee could further explore the matter. She then invited follow-up comments from Members.
Mr Ngqentsu welcomed the suggestion and stated that developing a resolution on the issue would be meaningful. He cited the example of the Gauteng Government, whose vision aligned with broader provincial priorities such as economic growth, job creation, and poverty reduction. He proposed that the Western Cape should consider a similar approach to ensure realignment between the WCLA’s mandate and the province’s economic objectives. He added that whether the Authority remained under its current portfolio or was relocated, its role should be clearly defined to ensure that it contributed meaningfully to economic growth rather than focusing solely on regulation and harm reduction. He expressed full support for the Chairperson’s proposal.
Mr P Johnson (DA) noted that he supported the remarks made earlier by Mr George regarding the remuneration of Board members. Drawing from his experience as the former Chairperson of the Finance Committee, he stated that the current sitting fees were not market-related, particularly when compared with those of members serving on the Western Cape Gambling and Racing Board. He agreed that the sitting fees should be reviewed and increased to ensure fairness and competitiveness.
He further suggested that the number of governing Board meetings should also be reconsidered, noting that five meetings per year might be insufficient. He proposed that the number be increased to at least two per quarter, while acknowledging that Board members also served on various subcommittees. He concluded by reaffirming his support for the proposed review of Board remuneration and meeting frequency
.
Mr George stated that he appreciated the comments made and reiterated that certain decisions, such as the placement of the WCLA, were determined at the provincial government level. He noted that while the WCLA could provide input and indicate preferences, the ultimate decision rested with the government of the day.
Another official added that alignment between the vision and mission of the Liquor Authority and its operational objectives was indeed critical. He noted that discussions would be held with the Authority to ensure that its strategic direction reflected both the safety and economic dimensions of liquor regulation. He emphasised that if the vision and mission focused solely on safety, applications for liquor licences would naturally be assessed through a safety lens, overlooking the economic imperative. The official stated that internal discussions would take place to address this alignment.
Mr Booysen remarked that the issue represented a “catch-22” situation, as the country continued to struggle with balancing socio-economic factors. He stated that alcohol abuse had wide-ranging consequences, including health and social development issues. He proposed that Mr George consider engaging with all affected Departments and committees to develop an educational programme demonstrating the Liquor Authority’s concern for public welfare. He further suggested that such an initiative adopt a holistic approach, beginning at the school level, where alcohol abuse was already evident.

He explained that the Safety Plan already incorporated inter-departmental engagement and that the Liquor Authority’s work formed part of its violence prevention initiatives. However, he cautioned that direct collaboration with Committees to develop joint projects might compromise their independence, as Committees were responsible for oversight and accountability. He stated that if Committees advised or instructed the Department to pursue joint projects with relevant Departments, the Department would comply accordingly.
Discussion on Part B: performance information
The Chairperson noted that the discussion on the broader issues surrounding the liquor industry could continue at a later stage, but the Committee needed to focus on the substantive part of the report, which was Part B on performance information. The Chairperson stated that this section formed the bulk of the report and indicated that the Committee would proceed on the assumption that the preceding content was accepted as read.
The Chairperson directed Members to Part B, commencing on page 15, and proposed that the Committee review pages 15 to 24. This section covered the Auditor’s Report on predetermined objectives, as well as the situational analysis of the service delivery environment, the organisational climate, key policy developments and legislative changes, and progress towards the achievement of institutional impacts and outcomes. The Chairperson stated that once this section was concluded, the Committee would move to the performance information component. Members were then invited to raise questions on pages 16 to 24.
Ms D Stephens (PA) asked how the Authority had improved its monitoring and enforcement of liquor licences to prevent illegal trading and underage sales. She further enquired about the measures implemented to reduce alcohol-related violence and accidents in communities. On the matter of transformation and support, she asked how many liquor licence applications had been approved for historically disadvantaged individuals and what support mechanisms were available for small business entrants.
Mr Ngqentsu referred to the situational analysis on page 17, noting the paragraph describing the harmful effects of alcohol consumption, particularly the statement that the harm was higher in deprived communities where liquor outlets were largely unregulated. He said this observation was significant and asked what the content of the situational analysis, and that paragraph in particular, suggested about the Western Cape as a province in relation to the Liquor Authority’s mandate. He further referred to page 18, which reflected a breakdown of licence applications, including approximately 360 approvals and 59 refusals.
He asked about the geographical distribution of these applications, requesting clarity on where applicants were predominantly based — for instance, whether they were concentrated in areas such as Kraaifontein, Mitchell’s Plain, or Bishop’s Court. He stated that this information would help the Committee understand who the applicants were and from which communities they were drawn, given the notable difference between approved and refused applications.
Mr George replied that several years ago, the capacity of inspectors within WCLA had been significantly increased as part of the Western Cape Government’s broader strategy to create safer communities. He explained that this initiative aimed to ensure that all complaints received were properly investigated and that every licence holder was inspected within a 12-month period. He added that Section 64 of the Liquor Act 59 of 2003 required problematic licence holders to reapply annually for renewal, allowing the Authority to prohibit non-compliant traders from continuing operations.
He stated that situational analyses now inform the Liquor Authority’s compliance strategy for specific areas to identify links between liquor availability, violence, trauma, and crime. This approach, he said, allowed the Liquor Authority to respond more effectively to local challenges and strengthen the relationship between compliance efforts and community safety.
Regarding support for new entrants into the industry, Mr George reported that a walk-in centre was being finalised to assist applicants who lacked access to online systems. He mentioned that the Authority had developed an online application platform but recognised that some people required in-person support. Mobile facilities would also be deployed to various regions to increase accessibility. These initiatives would be accompanied by a communication strategy to ensure that communities were aware of the available services.
Responding to questions about unregulated liquor outlets, he noted that the prevalence of illegal trading was mainly linked to zoning challenges in informal or residential areas. He explained that local government approvals were necessary before the Liquor Authority could issue a licence, and many applicants from informal settlements struggled to meet zoning requirements, which were often costly and complex. He added that while data on the geographic distribution of licences was not readily available, the Liquor Authority would explore its systems to determine whether such information could be extracted. He clarified that only approved licences were geo-plotted, meaning that applications which were refused were not captured spatially. A mechanism would be developed to improve tracking in this area.
Mr Arendse provided an overview of the geographic distribution of licensed liquor outlets in the Western Cape. He noted that the highest number of licences was recorded in Stellenbosch with 590, Paarl with 380, followed by Plettenberg Bay with 150. Within metropolitan areas, Strand had the most licences, followed by Milnerton and Durbanville. In key restaurant zones, Strand had 197 licences, and Somerset West 178, with certain western towns recording up to 250 licences. In township areas, Khayelitsha had 139 licences, Mitchell’s Plain 100, while other townships had fewer than 50. These figures demonstrated the concentration of licensed liquor outlets across urban, peri-urban, and township areas of the province.
The Chairperson noted a contradiction, observing that most questions to the licensing authority were about unlicensed establishments and liquor-related harms, which fell outside its mandate. Addressing enforcement against unlicensed premises was not the entity's core function. She suggested that the Committee should perhaps hold a separate hearing on how unlicensed premises are dealt with to gain a proper understanding of that area. She then asked if there were any further questions.
Mr Ngqentsu acknowledged that the Acting official had attempted to respond, but he wanted a sense of the extent to which working-class communities were benefiting or being affected. His follow-up question was, during the period under review, what support the entity had provided after discovering unregulated operators, and importantly, how many it had successfully supported in becoming licensed.
Mr George responded that while their scope extended to non-compliance by licensed premises, they were mindful that the distribution network included regulated outlets. He explained that when they assessed off-consumption premises and requested records of liquor volumes traded, it sometimes became apparent that a licence holder's records were disproportionate to the size of their premises, suggesting they were likely selling to illegal operations. In such cases, their sanctions were geared towards the licensed operator, and they would inform the South African Police Service and other enforcement agencies about the unlicensed premises, thus collaborating on those operations. He emphasised that where licensed premises enabled the unregulated market, the WCLA focused on them and did not simply dismiss them as "not our problem."
Regarding the question on supporting the unlicensed to become licensed, Mr George stated that any entity inquiring with the WCLA was provided with an outline of the application process, available on their website, detailing the required supporting documentation and procedures. He clarified that their responsibility was to indicate how to apply and who to approach, but they did not handle the applications for the entities. This assistance was provided daily. However, he noted it was extremely difficult to provide the specific information the Member requested. While they could count the number of people assisted, they did not keep specific records that would allow them to identify or isolate individuals based on other criteria.
Mr Ngqentsu said that he had made that statement because earlier in the year, he had taken one of the WCLA officials to Atlantis to interface with close to 100 unregulated operators. He said he had not made a follow-up to see how many of those had successfully applied and been supported. For him, the role of the entity should not just be about closing down operations, but also about providing support to enable people to operate legally.
The Chairperson hooked onto that point, asking if, in the WCLA's experience, a high number of license applications from an area would indicate that it potentially also had the most unlicensed premises, or if that was not related at all.
Another WCLA Official responded that when asked to assist unlicensed outlets to get a license, it was inferred that an application would result in a license if all criteria were met. However, this forgot the community involvement aspect of the process. He explained that the WCLA was an independent body with a tribunal that applied its mind independently. If they went to an unlicensed outlet against the will of the community, they would be imposing their will on the community, which was not the right way to do it. It was a fine line they should not overstep. They could advertise and inform people of the need to license, but the standard processes still had to be followed. For the entity to go and facilitate it directly would mean imposing its will on the community.
Mr George addressed the Chairperson, apologising and stating that he was trying to figure out the question. He asked for clarification, posing whether the question was about the ratio or percentage of applicants emanating from challenging areas with unregulated operations.
The Chairperson confirmed that her question was whether a large number of liquor applications from an area could be seen as an indicator that there may be a large number of unlicensed premises there. She analogised that it was like catching more drunk drivers where there are more breathalysers. She clarified that she was asking if the volume of applicants indicated either a desire for legality or served as a red flag highlighting potential unlicensed activity in that area, and whether such a relationship existed at all.
Mr George said that he understood the Chairperson's challenge. He stated that it was common knowledge that applications from areas with many illegal operators were already minimal, as the challenges there were understood. He shared an experience from seven years ago regarding an alcohol harm reduction initiative to create a "high street" in Khayelitsha, which involved getting the local government to zone areas for legitimate retail and licences. The challenge was with local government, and to his knowledge, no licences were ultimately granted from that initiative. He agreed that people generally wanted to be legitimate, but it was incumbent on all government entities to find ways to create properly zoned areas. He acknowledged it was a complicated issue.
Mr Booysen added that he shared the experience of how difficult it was to establish such zones. His question was about outlets that were "slaughterhouses" in communities, where killings occurred. He asked if the WCLA received this information from SAPS and what reasons they considered when deciding not to renew a licence for such an outlet.
Mr Ngqentsu said that the acting official had misunderstood him out of context. He clarified that he was not imposing a blanket approach of support that ignored the law or community involvement. He stated that outlets becoming "slaughterhouses" was a consequence of the broader crime challenge. His question was, given the evidence of crime around liquor outlets, what could be done to ensure that legal outlets did not give rise to such crimes.
Mr George responded that the WCLA had a clear and distinct approach for the regulated and unregulated spaces. He explained their communication strategy to make communities aware that a problematic licence did not have to be renewed. He detailed a process where incidents like murders at an outlet would trigger an inspection and possible suspension, with the Liquor Licensing Tribunal (LLT) deciding on the licence's future. He affirmed collaboration with other agencies on the unlicensed space.
Mr Arendse said he liked the discussion. He responded to the question on preventing liquor outlets from giving rise to alcohol harms by explaining the establishment of a Provincial Safety Coordination Centre. This involved overlaying WCLA data on outlet locations with crime statistics. The intention was not to automatically close outlets but to engage owners in high-crime areas to be part of the solution. This was part of a broader approach that included awareness campaigns. He confirmed they were working with the WCLA on this programme.

He detailed the investigative process, which involves gathering evidence from the complainant and witnesses, engaging SAPS for their response within 30 working days, and analysing documents like occurrence books and dockets. On the complaint categories raised by the Chairperson, he explained that 'Inquiries' often involved people contacting his office for help with matters outside its mandate, such as motor vehicle insurance or municipal issues, which they would then refer to the relevant body. She confirmed that this information could be shared with the Committee if required.
The Chairperson sought clarification on whether this oversight extended to law enforcement and traffic divisions within the City of Cape Town. The official confirmed that it did not, noting that data from these entities would require a separate request.
The Chairperson then introduced Part B of the Annual Report, covering performance information from pages 32 to 45. This section addresses Programme One: Administration; Programme Two: Secretariat for Safety and Security; Programme Three: Provincial Policing Functions; and Programme Four: Institutional Programme Performance, including subprogrammes.
Mr Booysen referred to page 34, querying the functionality of partnerships between the Department and district municipalities beyond the Garden Route District. He asked whether the agreements and funding were effectively implemented in other districts and whether underperforming districts should forfeit their partnerships to those that were more functional.
Ms Stephens requested an update on the implementation of recommendations from the previous annual report aimed at improving community safety outcomes. She also sought clarification on how the Department measured the effectiveness of Community Policing Forums (CPFs) and neighbourhood watches in reducing crime in high-risk areas, and on the progress and success metrics of the LEAP.
An official explained that the effectiveness of the LEAP was monitored primarily through homicide statistics, tracked weekly, monthly, and quarterly across the province. The Department focused particularly on areas where LEAP was deployed, assessing the impact on serious violent crime, especially murders. Operations under LEAP prioritised visible policing, stop-and-search operations, recovery of firearms and drugs, and addressing liquor-related crimes, recognising these as primary contributors to violence. Successes were measured through arrests and operational outcomes.
Regarding CPFs and district municipalities, an official confirmed that all district municipalities, including the other municipalities, were functional and implementing community safety projects. Funding was effectively utilised, although Central Karoo faced challenges due to political instability and recent senior appointments. The Department supported 152 CPFs, including facilitation of their elective annual general meetings, and provided nearly R1 million in funding for those that applied.
Mr Arendse highlighted efforts to innovate and improve outcomes since the last annual report. He noted that the collaboration agreement had fostered closer working relationships between SAPS, the City of Cape Town, and the provincial Department, particularly through engagement on a first-name basis, enhancing operational coordination. Strengthening partnerships with local and district municipalities was considered critical, as effective law enforcement in these areas had a direct impact on crime levels in the city, especially with criminals shifting operations to the outskirts.
He further noted ongoing innovations, including the development of the Provincial Safety Coordination Centre and the SAPS, aimed at improving station capabilities and overall policing effectiveness. Attention was also given to staff safety, recognising that personnel often worked and lived in high-risk areas. While some results might not be immediately visible within the current financial year, the Department anticipated positive impacts on community safety over the next one to two years through continued external engagement and co-creation of solutions with communities.
Mr Ngqentsu noted a gap in reporting on commercial crime and municipal investigations, citing recent raids in Matzikama and the City of Cape Town, and highlighted concerns regarding collusion between officials, politicians, and business entities.
The Chairperson reminded members that the discussion should focus on the annual report rather than general questioning.
Mr Arendse explained that the Department does not have oversight over all crime types, such as cybercrime or commercial crimes, and focuses instead on violent crime affecting community safety. He emphasised that while such data may help understand root causes and trends, it falls outside the Department’s mandate to report comprehensively on all crimes.
Ms Kaizer-Philander referred to page 48 of the report regarding the Department’s oversight of compliance with the Domestic Violence Act 116 of 1998. She asked about the plan to monitor additional police stations beyond the 40 visited during the financial year and the mechanisms for issuing protection orders.
Mr Bhekithemba Simelane, Director of Monitoring and Evaluation, Western Cape Department: Police Oversight and Community Safety, responded that during the financial year, 40 out of 152 police stations were assessed, while 30 were covered in the previous year. The Department aims to cover all 152 stations within five years. He explained that a standard monitoring tool is used when physically visiting stations, reviewing documents and registers to verify information. Reports are compiled by the office of the Head of Department and forwarded to the Provincial Commissioner for response and recommendations. This process will continue in the next financial year.
Mr Booysen raised a suggestion regarding the Chrysalis programme. He noted its popularity in rural areas but highlighted the transport challenges in getting cadets to the Tokai facility. He proposed that the Department engage with the Garden Route District Municipality to utilise a recently closed resort in Oudtshoorn, which had accommodation and facilities like swimming pools, to establish a second training site. This would allow the programme to be split, with metro cadets training in Tokai and rural cadets in Oudtshoorn, thereby improving accessibility. He suggested that the existing budget could be divided between the two locations, with adjusted scheduling, and mentioned alternative resorts in the Central Karoo if Oudtshoorn was unsuitable. He believed this meritorious suggestion would extend the programme's coverage across the province.
Mr Arendse responded that he could not provide an immediate yes or no but would take the suggestion back to the organisation for consideration. He cautioned that practical implications needed examination, noting that splitting the budget would not automatically halve costs, as similar setup and maintenance expenses would be required for both sites. He indicated that while the concept of replicating Chrysalis in rural areas would be explored, it might lead to a different, potentially better, intervention rather than an exact duplicate. He committed to returning with a formal response and requested the Committee's support in the budget process if the proposal advanced.
The Chairperson noted the Committee's keen interest in seeing programmes rolled out in the Garden Route area, where demand was increasing. She then moved the meeting to Programme 4: Security Risk Management, on pages 68 to 77, and subsequently to Transfer Payments (pages 78-85) and Capital Investment, asking for questions. As there were none, she proceeded to Part D: Human Resource Management, covering pages 109 to 114, which included workforce planning and employee wellness. She then offered the section on people management oversight from page 115 onwards, which contained numerous tables and statistics, for questions.
Mr Ngqentsu had several questions on the human resources section. Referring to page 117, he compared the current vacancy data with the previous year's annual report, noting that the vacancy rate for levels 3 to 5 had increased from 1.8% to 2.0%, which he viewed as a regression and a crisis. He reiterated a point made earlier that vacancies force remaining staff to do the work of two or three people.
On page 118, he asked for a definition of "critical occupations" and how it related to the level of vacancies.
On page 123, which detailed 10 resignations, he asked for the basis of these resignations. He used an analogy of methane in a mine causing mice to flee, questioning if there was a similar underlying issue ("methane") causing staff to leave the Department. He also noted 11 expired contracts and nine retirements. Of the 10 resignations, he highlighted that nine officials had refused to give their reasons, a number that had increased from 7 the previous year. He expressed concern that this consistent trend indicated a specific age group was leaving and asked for the Department's analysis of this pattern.
He concluded by stating he had requested the Employment Equity (EE) plan from the project office to understand how transformation and equity were being approached in the Department.
Mr Arendse responded to the points raised. He did not describe the vacancy situation as a crisis, noting that the Department had delivered almost 100% of its deliverables despite vacancies. He attributed the challenge in filling posts to a national directive requiring concurrence from the Premier’s office and job evaluation processes, which caused delays. He acknowledged the impact on staff, who had to work more or overtime, but also saw it as an opportunity for others to develop. He confirmed a plan was in place to fill identified and funded posts.
On the question of the ten resignations, particularly the nine who did not provide reasons, Mr Arendse declined to speculate on their motives. He observed that the cohort leaving (aged 40-49) was at a peak career stage and highly marketable. He did not see the number as a major concern and emphasised his open-door policy to address staff issues.
Regarding employment equity, he confirmed the Department was oversubscribed with Coloured females. To correct this and improve equity, he stated that when shortlisting for external appointments, he explicitly excluded Coloured females and Coloured males to focus on recruiting African males and females, who were underrepresented.
On the definition of critical occupations on page 118, he explained these were roles where job-sharing was not feasible, such as security officers who need to be at specific posts, and safety specialists crucial for advising on staff and Departmental security.
Mr Ngqentsu followed up, referring to employment equity data on page 126. While agreeing with the focus on correcting Coloured representation, he questioned the rationale for having nearly equal numbers of African and white males in professional roles (11 vs. 10), given the provincial demographics. He asked for the plan and roadmap to resolve this disparity.
He returned to the issue of resignations without reasons, suggesting that a refusal to disclose motives could insinuate a toxic environment, based on his experience in the labour movement. He pressed for an answer on what this pattern indicated about the Department.
Mr Arendse replied that the parity between African and white males shown in the table was a historical issue that current recruitment policies were designed to correct. He reiterated his method of excluding oversubscribed groups from shortlists to ensure new appointments improved equity figures.
On the perceived toxicity, he firmly stated he would not "look for a snake under every rock." He expressed trust in his team and respect for individuals' decisions not to disclose their reasons for leaving. He did not see the resignations as a "mass exodus" or sufficient evidence to label the Department as toxic, defending the loyal staff who enjoyed their work. He emphasised that the focus was on changing the culture to make people feel more appreciated and motivated.
Mr Ngqentsu raised further questions on labour relations. He noted there were seven disputes and asked about their nature and whether the officials who lodged them were still employed. He questioned if there was a logical relationship between these lodged disputes, the recent resignations, and the refusal of officials to disclose their reasons for leaving. He observed that in the last financial year, there were 11 disputes, indicating a continuing pattern of labour relations issues alongside resignations where reasons were not declared.
He then referred to pages 138 to 139 on incapacity leave, noting that 30 officials had used it, a significant increase from 10 the previous year. He also noted a slight drop in sick leave, from 314 to 297. He asked what this overall picture represented.
Mr Arendse responded to the question about a correlation between labour relations matters and resignations. He stated that if a resignation was linked to a disciplinary process, it was because the official had been a "bad boy," and leaving was the best option. He emphasised that he personally signed off on all disciplinary matters, which were based on sound principles and mostly involved progressive discipline and counselling.
On the increase in incapacity leave, Mr Arendse explained that as the accounting officer, he personally approved and monitored each case. He analysed patterns, such as leave taken on Mondays and Fridays, and engaged with supervisors and individuals, especially where doctors' certifications were lacking. He attributed some increase to an ageing workforce more prone to ailments and the pressures of work.
He stated that the picture was of a living, working Department of human beings with various challenges. He affirmed a zero-tolerance policy on criminal activity or corruption, often recommending dismissal for such offences, as staff in a safety and security Department must be beyond reproach. He concluded that he was comfortable that the processes were being managed and saw no reason to infer, based on speculation, that these issues were the cause of the resignations.
The Chairperson noted that the meeting was running out of time but allowed for one last follow-up question before moving to resolutions.
Mr Ngqentsu stated that while he respected the provincial government, he felt the official's response was evasive. He reiterated that his core question was about the connection between officials who lodge disputes, the number of resignations, and the growing use of incapacity leave, which had jumped from 10 to 30 cases. He argued that a significant rise in sick and incapacity leave is uncommon in a professional public sector environment and can be an indicator that workers feel compelled to stay home due to a toxic work environment. He concluded by asserting that, as a public representative, the growing number of resignations, disputes, and leaves was a serious concern and pointed to potential toxicity within the Department of Police Oversight and Community Safety.
Mr Arendse stated that he had already responded and did not share that view. He said he could not admit to that characterisation as it was not his perception, view, or experience.
Closing remarks
The Chairperson noted the meeting was running out of time and concluded the substantive agenda, confirming that Part E (Public Finance Management Act compliance) and Part F (Financial Information) would be dealt with by SCOPA.
Mr Booysen extended thanks on behalf of the committee. He referenced the Auditor-General of South Africa's (AGSA) report for the province, expressing appreciation that the Department had achieved a clean audit and was not flagged for any issues. He commended Minister Marais and Mr Arendse for their work. He observed that many questions highlighted the Department's lack of enforceable powers, stating that oversight alone was not enough. He expressed hope that the Department would be granted such powers in the future.
Mr Ngqentsu suggested that the committee observe a moment of silence for the LEAP officer and other police personnel who had been killed while defending the province.
The Chairperson agreed, specifically mentioning a LEAP officer murdered the previous week, and led the committee in a moment of silence for all Departmental and police personnel who had lost their lives in the past year. She then invited any concluding remarks from the Department.
Mr Arendse thanked the Chairperson and Members for the session and the robust discussion. He extended his gratitude to the team behind Minister Marais for their hard work and commitment, acknowledging the challenging nature of their work in community safety, which he described as psychologically draining compared to his previous work in innovation and technology. He thanked the LEAP officers, SAPS officers, and SAPS management for their service. Finally, he thanked Minister Marais for her trust and leadership and acknowledged her strong supporting team.
Minister Marais thanked the Committee for its professional engagement. She expressed her appreciation for Mr Arendse and the Departmental team, affirming that they had wonderful working relationships. She concluded with an anonymous quote: “Safety is an intentional practice, not a coincidence, and requires a consistent mindset of carefulness and awareness.” She thanked Mr Arendse for his work.
Consideration of Committee resolutions and actions
The Chairperson asked the Committee to stay behind to discuss resolutions while excusing other attendees, noting that lunch was available. She called for any resolutions or recommendations to be recorded, specifically mentioning the issue raised about the Liquor Authority and whether to expand on it immediately or at the next meeting. She confirmed with Mr Matthews and listed several items for the Committee's planning, including discussing the use of artificial intelligence (AI) in oversight, an on-site engagement with the Western Cape Police Ombudsman, and a visit to the CCTV centre.
Mr Ngqentsu supported the suggestion to convene at the Western Cape Police Ombudsman, noting the Committee had never met with that entity. He also proposed convening a meeting with the City of Cape Town and Minister Marais regarding the LEAP to build cooperation between the SAPS, the City, and the Provincial Government. He suggested identifying areas of convergence and infusing a practical role for the Safety Council.
The Chairperson acknowledged these as good suggestions. She instructed Mr Matthews to record the resolutions and concluded the meeting, reminding Members of the next meeting with the Department of Cultural Affairs and Sport (DCAS).
The meeting was adjourned.
