Meeting Summary
Video
In this joint meeting, the Portfolio Committees were briefed by the Department of Communications and Digital Technologies (DCDT) and the Department of Science, Technology and Innovation (DSTI) on the progress of the development of the National Satellite Communication (SATCOM) strategy.
Cabinet had previously approved the national broadband policy and its associated strategies through SA Connect to roll out information and communication technology infrastructure, using terrestrial wireless and fixed networks. The SA Connect objective was to provide connectivity and close the gap in sparsely populated geographic and far-flung areas in the country. A space-based communications satellite network was required to complement broadband connectivity provisioning, as it would cover vast areas, thus bridging the digital divide in society. To give effect to this realisation, the SATCOM strategy had been developed by the DCDT in conjunction with the DSTI, Sentech and the South African National Space Agency (SANSA).
The SATCOM strategy was aimed at addressing the challenges of the digital gap by advancing the deployment of a South African-owned satellite system. The benefit of having a South African-owned satellite system included improving efficiencies in the education and healthcare system; service delivery in rural areas; catalysing economic growth; developing new industries and markets; and the creation of new skills and increasing the access to broadband. There were different funding options --  the SATCOM strategy could be funded by National Treasury, through public-private partnerships, by debt funding, or a combination of the three.
There had been stakeholder engagements with Sentech, SANSA and other departments. There was satellite expertise in the country, but there would be collaborations with international partners. The next steps for the SATCOM strategy included approval from the Cabinet for public consultation; receiving written submissions; processing, analysing and incorporating the submissions into the strategy; and submitting the refined strategy for final approval.
Concerns were raised about the cost-benefit analysis and rural connectivity. The reliance on foreign communication systems posed a national security risk. Members were also concerned about the male-dominated executive positions, South Africa lagging behind other countries in satellite technology, and the potential for corruption in the procurement processes. It was recommended that the acting positions be eliminated to ensure stability in leadership. Members were interested in the finalisation of the strategy, stakeholder engagement with other departments, the detailed project plan, feasibility studies, and the plans to bridge the divide in rural communities. The Committees expressed their disappointment that South African satellites were not in space. Members emphasised the urgency for South Africa to have its own satellite. Questions were asked about the legal and regulatory landscapes governing satellite operations in South Africa. The importance of skills development was also raised.
The Portfolio Committee on Science, Technology and Innovation also discussed the choice of destination for an international study tour to be undertaken.

Progress on the National Satellite Communications strategy
Ms Jordan-Dyani said that the purpose of the presentation was for the Committees to note the progress update on the development of the national satellite communication (SATCOM) strategy.
Background
Cabinet had previously approved the national broadband policy and its associated strategies (SA Connect), to roll out information communication technology (ICT) infrastructure using terrestrial wireless and fixed networks. The SA Connect objective was to provide connectivity and close the gap in sparsely populated geographic and far-flung areas in the country. A space-based communications satellite network was required to complement broadband connectivity provisioning, as it would cover vast areas, thus bridging the digital divide in society. To give effect to this realisation, the SATCOM strategy had been developed by the DCDT in conjunction with the DSTI, Sentech and the South African National Space Agency (SANSA).
Mr Tinyiko Ngobeni, Deputy Director-General: ICT Infrastructure Development and Support, DCDT, took the Committee through the presentation.
Why the SATCOM strategy?
The SATCOM strategy was aimed at addressing the challenges of the digital divide gap by advancing the deployment of a South Africa-owned satellite system. The finalisation of the SATCOM strategy would address several government priorities. The deployment of the satellite system would yield the following benefits:
improved efficiencies in education and healthcare systems and government’s ability to deliver services in rural areas;
reduction of communication cost;
catalyse economic growth and support for other economic sectors;
development of new industries and markets which would support job creation;
creation of new technical skills during the design, build, launch and operation phases; and
increase broadband access that would speed up the country’s development and global competitiveness and facilitate the achievement of the National Development Plan (NDP) targets.
Problem statement
The problem was to bridge the digital divide in rural and underserved communities, stimulating and sustaining inclusive economic growth and trade, reducing the cost of access and curbing capital outflows, and delivering on national priorities.
Stakeholder engagements
On 26 November 2024, an updated socio-economic impact assessment system (SEIAS) sign-off certificate was re-issued. On 13 December 2024, the DCDT ministry had made an input into the SATCOM strategy, which was incorporated into the strategy document. On 28 November 2024, the Director-General cluster for economic sectors, investment, employment and infrastructure development, had supported and approved the SATCOM strategy to be submitted to the Cabinet for approval to publish for public consultation. On 4 March 2025, the Director-General Cluster for Justice, Crime Prevention and Security (JCPS) supported and approved the SATCOM strategy to be submitted to the Cabinet for approval to publish for public consultation.
Ministerial oversight input
The Ministry had asked for the following to be included in the strategy document:
a brief content on the strengths, weaknesses, opportunities and threats analysis;
an outline of funding options;
a high-level implementation plan indicating major project activities and anticipated timelines to launch; and
the prerequisite skills and capabilities required to implement the satellite project and plans.
Funding options
Four funding options would be considered.
Option one: National Treasury funding of 100% capex
The government could allocate funding for the satellite project as a strategic asset for the country.
Option two: Public-private partnership
This strategy proposed the establishment of the satellite company, SATCO, as the implementing agency that would be responsible for coordinating all government satellite requirements, and could secure investment from government, strategic anchor investors from the Brazil, Russia, India, China, and South Africa (BRICS) bloc, and private business in South Africa.
Option three: Debt funding
Various funding institutions, such as the Development Bank of Southern Africa (DBSA) and the Industrial Development Corporation (IDC), could be approached to ensure a blended financial model with assistance from the National Treasury’s Budget Facility for Infrastructure (BFI).
Option four: Combination of options one, two and three
This could provide the flexibility required to limit government's capital investment.
Satellite skills
There was currently satellite expertise in the country to manage the implementation of the satellite launch. The SATCOM strategy envisaged collaboration with international partners to leverage their skills in areas where there was a shortage of capacity in the country. Partnerships would be undertaken in a mutually beneficial manner to ensure the development and transfer of unique and specialised skills to meet the country’s needs. Telemetry, testing and command services were being undertaken in the country for foreign missions. This was a competence rendered by SANSA operations, which was needed for the envisaged domestic satellite.
Next steps
The SATCOM strategy would be submitted to Cabinet for approval to publish for public consultation. Once approved, the SATCOM strategy would be gazetted, wherein the public and all stakeholders would be invited to make written submissions for a period of 30 working days. All the submissions would be processed, analysed and incorporated into the strategy. A national workshop may be convened to explore substantive issues, including clarity on divergence. A refined SATCOM strategy would be resubmitted through the Cabinet system for final approval.
Mr Peter Mello, Chief Director, DCDT, said that there was a steering committee with the DGs of the DCDT and the DSTI. It consisted of officials from the DCDT, DSTI, SANSA and Sentech. They had been mainly responsible for the development of this strategy. There were engagements on a quarterly basis, and an
ad hoc

In 2024, there had been a consultative workshop with relevant stakeholders from the Department of Transport (DOT), the Department of Defence (DOD) and other key stakeholders who were interested. These inputs had been incorporated into the strategy. The strategy was long overdue. There had been extensive progress since the last meeting on the next steps that had to be followed. Once approval had been received from the Cabinet, consultations with the public would take place.
There had been inquiries from private companies looking for this particular strategy. The governance protocol was being followed. Once the public consultation was completed, there might be a need to have another workshop. This workshop would cater for the people who had an interest in investment and the country's satellite programme. A lot of work had been done.

Mr Du Toit thanked the Members for the strategic questions that the DSTI should reflect on to enhance and guide its work. Many of the important comments and questions raised not only applied to satellite communication, but also holistically to space science and technology. It was regrettable that there was a male-dominated delegation, as this was not consistent with the DSTI’s ethos and commitment to the transformation of the national system of innovation.
The potential of science, technology and innovation would be unleashed to make an optimal contribution in South Africa through partnership. This could be done only if the enterprise of science, technology and innovation became truly inclusive and provided opportunities for all South Africans to contribute. This was the reason that the transformation agenda was important for the DSTI. Two weeks ago, there was a national transformation summit on science, technology and innovation, and one of the key aspects was gender. There had been some important progress, but more needed to be done. There was an awards programme that focused on women in South Africa in science, technology and innovation. One of the key focus areas was the space sector, which ensured that it created opportunities for future space leaders among women in South Africa. There was a deliberate investment amongst women and young girls to follow careers in the science, technology and innovation sector. The DSTI had a firm commitment, which was shared with the DCDT. This would be reflected throughout the DSTI's actions.
Members spoke about the geopolitical dynamics of these initiatives and the vulnerabilities faced from a national strategic interest, a national sovereignty perspective, and the reliance on specific partners. This did not apply only to satellite communication, but to other aspects of the space enterprise -- for example, global navigation and satellite systems. There was a focus on diversification, by not being reliant on the global positioning system (GPS), but also exploring collaboration with the Baidu system in China. This also applies to earth observation satellites. This was the reason it was important for the DSTI and the SANSA to maintain a broad and diverse portfolio of international partnerships with the global north, global south and the African continent.
He understood why a categorisation had been made about competition and a space race in Africa. It was important to emphasise the commitment to Pan-African collaboration, which was in their collective strategic interest as a nation. South Africa was actively involved in the programmes of the African Space Agency in Cairo. In 2024, SANSA hosted a meeting of all the leaders of the African national space agencies in Tshwane as part of the G20. As part of the space economic leaders' meeting under the G20, there would be a focus on the African space agenda. The DSTI was committed to collaborations with other African space agencies.
He applauded the success of Botswana and others. The Botswana satellite was not a communications satellite, but rather an earth observation satellite. It included a strategic contribution by South African companies that had collaborated. South African technology was used in the Botswana satellite. There was a strategic focus not only on procurement, but also on building a national indigenous capability. Several countries had satellites. However, if those satellites were analysed, it was often through procurement contracts, with little local technology and skills development.
It was important for South Africa to build its own national strategic capabilities with regard to the regulatory and international aspects. There had been a close collaboration with DIRCO. South Africa was very active in the United Nations space. It often adopted leadership positions in the various United Nations bodies dealing with space, such as the Convention for the Peaceful Uses of Outer Space. The DSTI worked closely with the DCDT in the bodies of the International Telecommunication Union (ITU) and the World Radio Communications Conference, which deal with the regulation of the international space. The voice of Africa needed to be firmly heard in those instances. This would allow the rule settings of space to become an inclusive enterprise and sensitive to the needs of Africa and the developing countries.
One of South Africa's strategic interests was to protect astronomy from interference from satellite communications. For many years, the DCDT had been an excellent supporter of the DSTI’s efforts with regard to the square kilometer array (SKA) and the implementation of the Astronomy Geographic Advantage Act. This allowed the DSTI to protect South Africa, observe capacity, and to retain a pristine environment for doing global astronomy of excellence. These issues were important and on the DSTI’s agenda.
The DSTI was committed to having close collaboration with SANSA and its entities. He agreed it was important to continue this momentum. The DSTI was working closely with the DCDT, not only on the aspects of satellite communication, but broader digital innovation, such as artificial intelligence (AI). This indicated that the DSTI and the DCDT would be working well together in the Seventh Administration of Parliament.
Mr Leshope addressed Sentech's concerns about the prominent launchers of satellites, excluding South Africa. There were launch capabilities that had been diversified and brought into the world. There were other options available. South Africa could look to other countries for partnerships. There had been co-existence of satellite and terrestrial platforms for a while now. It would not necessarily be one over the other. Each one would fill a particular gap in a particular market. Satellites came in handy because of their ubiquity capabilities in terms of coverage. There were instances where terrestrial coverage did not make business sense. This was due to the population being too small for a return on it. The satellite capability would then make a lot of sense.
There has been a strong development around communications on the go, such as the mobility of communications and autonomous vehicles. There were now developments around satellite telephones. This was the future, and South Africa had to get ready for it. Terrestrial technologies provided fixed reception, such as being tied to a house. The benefit from a satellite would be the mobility aspect of it, such as connecting to a car or mobile. The satellite innovation had been much faster. Low earth orbit satellites were coming on board. Entities were shooting up over 3 000 satellites in one business day to demonstrate the future focus point. It was not a space that it believed South Africa should not participate in, as a result of the prospects of what was coming.
From its manufacturing through to its launching, a satellite was tested for failures and commissioned properly to ensure that it would last long in space. One of the prevention measures put in place was to partner with a reputable satellite production and satellite launch entity. Another prevention measure was to work with other existing satellite providers to ensure that there was concurrency of services. The same services that were put into its own satellites may have to be put into alternative satellites. Once South Africa entered that space, it had to play broadly.
As far as corruption and mismanagement of funds were concerned, there was a plan to put a thorough and above-board governance process in place for the deployment of the satellite. An open approach to attract partners was envisaged. Sentech was championing the project, and would put in place the best processes from a governance point of view. This would ensure that there was no mismanagement of funds or corruption on the satellite. Various players in Africa supplied satellites. There were close to 12 companies that were providing satellites over Africa. This was consolidated into the entire country fleet of satellite providers, which related to the satellite economy and spending.
The country was spending a huge amount on satellites. From a Sentech perspective, it was spending R300m per annum. From a consolidated perspective, it was close to R1bn per annum. This expenditure and the cash outflows were impacting somebody else’s economy, but not South Africa’s. The country's gross domestic product (GDP) from the exchange of services and goods was disadvantaged as a result of that. It would be best to retain that spending inside the country to strengthen the economy and protect jobs.
There were different funding options. In the public-private partnerships, the state would take the majority shareholding, such as the control of the satellite. He said that it was living in the economies of shared platforms. The closing out of other players may inhibit South Africa’s competitiveness and diverse market attraction. The Southern African Development Community (SADC) countries were being looked at for satellites. The utilisation of the satellite would predominantly be for South Africa.
From a legal perspective, there have been engagements with the regulator of the International Telecommunication Union (ITU) as far as orbital slots are concerned. The process was clear. The stakeholders were engaged and active. Sentech had made its applications to the regulator. At the last ITU conference, there were discussions about access to its own orbital slot. The discussion was advanced on this matter. There were strong ground station management capabilities in South Africa, yet it had not launched its own satellite. This was an area for development as far as the manufacturing and development of a geostationary satellite was concerned. South Africa has been managing satellite and satellite-related technologies for a long time. He had attended a satellite launch in the west, and as the satellite left the launchpad, it had been monitored by an American-based company. As it approached the orbit, the satellite had been handed over to a South African monitoring station. This demonstrated the capabilities of South Africa. These capabilities had to be exploited for South Africa to launch its own satellite.
Academia was involved in the process, but not as direct partners in the satellite. Academia was involved in the process of developing insights into what needed to be done, what was the best to do, and what existed out there. There had been broad stakeholder engagement. Some of the stakeholders included SANSA, the National Treasury, the Independent Communications Authority of South Africa (ICASA), the DTIC, DSTI, DOD, DCDT, and mobile network operators.
Expanding the cash on external providers limited one's control over the cost. There was a need to reduce the cost-to-communicate. This could not be done if one was not the owner of the investment. It was like putting a margin on already established technology. If one did the investment, there would be an opportunity to go to the bare minimum of return and recovery. Sentech was looking at this from a cost-reduction benefit, to drive the cost-to-communicate down with the control embedded in it. It did envisage private investment. A satellite was a viable case. There were many satellites launched daily. It would not be difficult to attract investors. Local investment should play a dominant part. This would allow the country to have dominant control, instead of diluting the distribution. Even if it were not above 50%, the investment of government would still be larger in the process.
There was currently a satellite lease model. In this instance, one would buy capacity from somebody else, utilise it, and return it to the owner when it is done. This was not beneficial. The residual value of that remained, and could be used elsewhere after establishment costs had been paid. The next model was a condo site -- this was when one bought a satellite or partnered with somebody to fully own a portion of a satellite. Alternatively, one could buy a satellite from a transponder of an already existing satellite. There was some level of control to this. One could own the satellite transponder segment for the entire life of that particular satellite. The preferred model was a satellite fully owned by South Africa. In a condo site, there was a joint control of a satellite with a partner. There was no full control over that satellite.
The growing cybersecurity attacks, and South Africa generating a lot of information, meant that the information had to be protected. This was the reason that the model of having full control by owning a satellite, was preferred. If one had to go with a condo site, there would be quicker service, with a lead time of about three years. In seven years, South Africa had to be at the stage of fully owning a satellite. It envisaged a quick-to-service or quick-to-market model. It could initially be a condo site, but the ultimate goal was a fully owned South African-based satellite.
Mr Ngobeni said that the other options did not fall away -- they would continue to be part of the strategy. There was a focus on a broader approach of South Africa launching and owning a satellite. The other options were the ones that would be explored in between, while waiting for South Africa to launch its own satellite. The slides had indicated the timelines for the implementation plan. The intention was for South Africa to launch its own satellite. Due to the timelines, other options had to be explored, as satellites were used in the country. ICASA was involved in the regulatory and legal constraints. There was active participation in the processes of the ITU, because it was important to obtain an orbital slot. The approval was needed from the ITU, which required consultation with different countries to assess how it would impact the planned or existing satellites. South Africa should be able to meet the regulatory processes. It did require a licensed operator in the country. There had to be an independent electronic communications network service license to apply for the spectrum resource that could be utilised. From a legal diligence perspective, there was a need to appoint a transactional advisor for support. Since this was a highly specialised nature in this area of the satellite, there was a need to engage with international partners.
The National Treasury had a budget facility for infrastructure funding. It was a rigorous process to fund infrastructure projects. One of the requirements was to conduct a detailed feasibility study and a detailed funding option, including its risks, mitigations and preferred option. This was normally recommended during the project preparation stage, supported by the development funding institutions, such as the DBSA.
As soon as the strategy was adopted or approved by the Cabinet, the next step would be to register this project with National Treasury through the facility that allowed these other activities to be undertaken. The need for a South African satellite started a few years ago. The initial reaction had been for Sentech to create a business plan. There had been a detailed analysis, with a feasibility study and assessment. This was where the data had been derived from. At this stage, that work could not be taken forward until the strategy was completed.
One of the key activities during the initial stage of the implementation plan was the review of the business plan. Most of the work had been done. However, the work had had to be updated due to changes that had taken place. This would assist with shortening the processes that had to be undertaken.
He welcomed the request to share more information during the workshop. In the subsequent briefing, a detailed presentation could be provided to empower the Committees with more information about the project. The strategy finalisation was not part of the timeline. It was a precursor to these timelines. The strategy had to be approved to undertake some of the work and the commitment that had been made. The expectation was for the strategy to be presented to the Cabinet by May. If it did, there would be 30 days available for public comments. The comments should be analysed by the end of quarter one or by the beginning of quarter two. He hoped Cabinet would have approved the final strategy by the end of quarter two. The SEIAS report would be redone after the strategy was finalised.
The participation of industry in the development should be increased. Industry had participated in the workshop. Before a satellite was launched, one would have to look at the key users of that satellite's capacity. The process was still going to happen, even if there was a prior commitment before a satellite was launched. The understanding was that during the development of the strategy, the industry must be involved. This would be strengthened by having a workshop or special engagement with the industry.
Mr Leshope said the safeguards that would be put in place depended on the option that the Cabinet approved. He explained the options and the end goal of a South African satellite. The issues of corruption and wastage have been identified as key risks. Mitigations would be developed with the transactional advisor ahead of the procurement stage. By the time the procurement stage commenced, the controls to mitigate corruption and wastage would be in place. In the value chain of the satellite, there were various players who would be responsible for key activities that required funding.
The remote areas were far from terrestrial infrastructure, like fibre. A satellite would help these areas because there would be coverage. The only thing that had to be put in place was a device at home. It did not have to invest more in infrastructure to connect those people. It would be cost-effective for the provider. This meant that the providers would be able to charge less, because more people would be connected without additional investment in infrastructure. For instance, with the SA Connect programme, satellites had been leveraged to reach some of these areas. This was where Sentech, through its satellite capabilities, had deployed connectivity through the satellites. It had assisted in that manner. It would have been costly and taken a lot of time to trench and deploy fibre infrastructure to only reach a few people. The return on investment would be difficult in that regard. Satellite could assist with circumventing that challenge and addressing the issue of bridging the digital divide in rural areas. Once the satellite was launched, the whole country would be covered. The service would be there -- those people just had to get the device that was needed to connect to the satellite.
The DCDT was looking at the digital inclusion strategies as part of the G20 process. The cost of services and devices was important. The infrastructure problem may be solved by deploying satellites, but one would still have to deal with the issue of accessibility. There was an impact when multiple satellites were launched. Before a satellite was launched, approval from the ITU was needed. The intention to launch a satellite must be submitted. An assessment would be done, considering the existing and planned satellites. This would determine if the countries would accept or support the application to use that orbital slot. The sooner South Africa was able to conclude the process, the sooner it could apply for an orbital slot. The process was there, but the strategy had to be finalised to get concurrence from the ITU and other countries.
Ms Jordan-Dyani said that the satellite programme was mainly designed to ensure South African services across various spectrums. It was primarily for communications, but it could be leveraged for other sectors, especially in the digital economy sphere and the digital transformation agenda of the country. There had been an engagement with the private sector when the strategy was being developed and revised in 2021/22. This was the reason that it was framed as a national satellite strategy. It was not just government-owned owned, but there was going to be private and public collaboration. This had to be one of the priorities and the policy pronouncement.
There had been an urge to move faster because there were contracts with international organisations that would lapse in 2027. This strategy had taken so long due to fiscal constraints and conflicting priorities. Initially, the strategy was designed with the DCDT in mind. However, other stakeholders had to be a part of it. This would allow it to be a viable case. There was both the government buy-in and the external stakeholders to sustain it. There was now a much better business case for viability. It was an investment of 20 to 25 years. The business plan had to be viable, and there should be a return on investment during the 20 to 25-year lifespan. The private-public partnership funding model would be put in front of the National Treasury in the big budget fund application. There had been engagements with the DBSA. There had to be a comparison with the capabilities and limitations of those capacities from a government perspective versus the magnitude of the project. It had to be financially viable. If there were going to be comparisons with international partners, the expertise had to be on board. There would be a transactional advisor. The DBSA was helping with the programme. There would be engagements with the private sector.
There was a strong emphasis on the issues of skills and gender. There was a desire to onboard engineers. Sentech was establishing an academy that would train young engineers. There were emerging technologies. A number of artificial intelligences had been launched in the Western Cape, in collaboration with the University of Stellenbosch. It was focused purely on the country's defence capabilities. The DCDT was working with the graduates on that programme to determine how it could integrate this within the satellite programme. These new technologies were being used to ensure data capability and efficient and modern technology deployments. There were still more experts who could come on board in terms of youth and women in the programme.
Mr Mello said there had been extensive consultation with the key stakeholders who had a keen interest in the deployment of satellites. A peer review committee had been established, and two to three workshops had been held at SANSA with experts to review the strategy. There had been another engagement with government agencies and departments for a whole day at Sentech, where written inputs on the strategy were received. There would be another opportunity for public consultation and the industry to make specific inputs, based on the previous inputs that had been made. The certificate from the Presidency had been received to proceed to the Cabinet for public consultation.
Co-Chairperson Shiviti was pleased with the progress of the work that had been done. The fact that the strategy had been presented to all the stakeholders and was also with the Cabinet committees showed that progress had been made. She was hopeful that the project would launch soon within the timeframes. She was looking forward to South Africa launching its satellite and having coverage everywhere. The Committees had an interest in the strategy, and were happy with the work that had been done. She hoped that the quarterly reports would bring new insights. The Committees were looking forward to launching a South African satellite. If Botswana could do it, South Africa could too..
Chairperson Sangoni said the Committees were encouraged by the progress that had been made. There was an arbitrary racial construct of Africa, where people had sought to take the northern part of Africa and had called it “White Africa”, and the rest, below the Sahara Desert, was called “Dark Africa”. This was the reason the sub-Saharan Africa phrase was not allowed in meetings.
Members had raised issues about gender representation, stability, adequate participation in the space race, resources to benefit Africa and the risk to national security. The geopolitical trends were shifting. This project was needed to strengthen and solidify South Africa’s space as a regional hegemon, but also in the interest of national security. There needed to be movement when there were discussions around digital sovereignty and national security. The country could no longer speak without actions, and then be shocked if people were to act against the country.
Mr A Sauls (PA) said he felt bad that a comparison had been made with Botswana. It was as if South Africa had not launched a satellite that was exactly like the one Botswana had launched. In 2018, the ZACUBE weather satellite had been launched, which was the same as BOTSAT-1. In 1999, South Africa launched the SUNSAT  advanced microsatellite programme. There were two satellites up there. South Africa was now looking bad, but it was going to launch a third satellite. There was a difference, because it was a communication satellite.
Chairperson Sangoni said that the Committees would meet at the end of the next quarter for progress.
International study tour
Chairperson Sangoni said that there would be a discussion on the international trip that the Committee on Science, Technology and Innovation wanted to undertake. It would discuss the countries that were viable to visit.
Ms Shanaaz Isaacs, Committee Secretary, said that the motive for having the briefing on the international relations and cooperation programme was to see if the Committee could undertake an international study tour. There was strong emphasis on countries such as France, Japan, China and Belgium. She asked Mr Du Toit to indicate which countries would be more beneficial to visit before the Committee made a decision.
Mr Du Toit said that these regions were located in the DSTI international offices. There was an office in Brussels where there could be a visit to Belgium, although he meant the European Union (EU) more than Belgium as a country. There could be engagement with the Belgian and Flemish parliaments. France was an hour’s train ride away from Brussels. There was a second office located Tokyo, with the potential to visit Japan and China. There was an extensive bilateral science, technology and innovation partnership with France. The French government and President Emmanuel Macron had a big focus on science, technology and innovation. A few weeks ago, France hosted the Global Artificial Intelligence Summit. There was a big focus on the competitiveness of the French economy in terms of investing in science, technology, and innovation. There was a range of institutions within the Paris region which could be visited, many of whom would have collaborations with South Africa. Paris was an important seat for science, technology and innovation. This was where the United Nations Educational, Scientific and Cultural Organisation (UNESCO) headquarters were. UNESCO had a strong focus on science and innovation. Paris was also the headquarters for the Organisation for Economic Cooperation and Development (OECD), which was a leading forum for policy reflection on science, technology and innovation. There was rich potential for engagements in Paris. If the choice was Europe, he recommended that it be combined with Brussels.
Two weeks ago, there had been a South African-EU summit in Cape Town. The President had met with the other presidents of the EU. There was a strong focus on science and innovation collaboration between South Africa and the European Union. This covered diverse areas, such as marine, Atlantic science, space science and the hydrogen economy. The European Parliament, located in Brussels, had a committee similar to this one. It would be an interesting package to combine Brussels and Paris.
Japan had been recognised as a global innovation power because of its long-standing focus after the Second World War, rebuilding its economy through investment in science, technology and innovation. There was a good bilateral collaboration with Japan in a number of areas, including the hydrogen economy. Japan also invested in South Africa through the automotive industry to enforce competitiveness in the industry. Professor Tshilidzi Marwala was the director of the United Nations University, and was based in Tokyo. This could be an additional engagement.
China was a rising global science power of the world. There were bilateral collaborations with China in areas such as artificial intelligence. Money was a big focus for engagement with China. In China, there was a national consensus on the importance of science and innovation. Interestingly, almost all the Chinese leaders whom he had met were engineers or former scientists. There was a lot to learn from the Chinese policies on the importance of science and innovation. A powerful case could be made for all of these destinations. It would be the Committee’s prerogative to decide. The DSTI would be ready to support the Committee in its decision. The DSTI had experience in organising these visits. He recalled visits by this Committee to Brussels, Japan and Switzerland.
The Chairperson said that Minister Nzimande had strongly advised they should go to France. Members who had been to Japan had advocated Japan. The Committee had to decide which country would be best for a study tour.
Deputy Minister Gina agreed with Mr Du Toit. The EU held South Africa in very high esteem. The flagship relationship that South Africa had with the EU was good. If she had to choose one option, it would be Tokyo, Japan. It was important to get into those laboratories when it came to the issues of green hydrogen, ammonia and battery fuel cells. Japan had progressed so much -- one could feel it in the cars and buses. It would be important to interact with those scientists. South Africa could benefit from many other countries, but her first choice was Japan.
The Chairperson said that the Committee had a difficult choice to make. Both Japan and France had been recommended. There was a suggestion of doing France and then taking a train to Belgium. This would allow the Committee to do two trips in one. There was a serious interest in Japan from the Members. Some Members had previously done a trip to Japan and South Korea, and there had been a positive experience. She did not hear anyone advocating for China. Unfortunately, the Committee was not allowed to do two trips, like Japan and South Korea. However, with France, it would be cost-effective to take a train to Belgium.
Mr Du Toit said that it was not possible to fly to Japan directly from South Africa. There was always a second country. This meant that it would not add to the cost. Singapore could be an additional offer. It would not add to the cost because it was one flight.
The Chairperson felt conflicted.
Ms Mazzone said her choice had always been Japan. It was obvious that the way South Africa was moving, it was necessary to look at the Japanese model. There was green hydrogen, battery cells and clean energy used in the cities. There were self-propelled cars to fetch people. Japan went much more broadly than what Europe could offer at this stage. There were always interactions with the European Commission in South Africa, but Japan did not come here a lot due to the long distance. Japan could bring presentations and examples to South Africa, but it was different to see the advancements of their facilities. This was just phenomenal. On Instagram, there was a famous meme of "Japan was living in 2050.” This showed the things that Japan was doing now that other countries would be able to do only in 2050. As someone who was regularly in Europe, there were great relationships focusing on science. However, the entire world was focusing on science. The focus should not only be on people who are focused on science but also finish the process. Japan was a country where every child had done mathematics and science. It had the highest number of people graduating in science, engineering and advanced mathematics. It was important to know the reason for this. Why was Japan getting this right? Those reasons could be brought back to South Africa.
The Chairperson said that this was open for discussion. Members should bring their ideas to the table.
Ms Mchunu said the Committee had to take the majority decision, which was in favour of Japan. This would allow the democratic process to continue. The Committee would find a way to get information on Brussels. She said that all the initiatives and strategies emerging would assist the Committee in understanding how other countries were involved in science, technology, and innovation. It was important for South Africa to see how these countries were bringing in young children to study mathematics. It would help to empower the South African youth to be more passionate about science, technology and innovation.
The Chairperson said that Japan was the decision. Ms Isaac would assist with the preparations.
Dr W Boshoff (FF+) said that he had no opinion. There were good reasons to visit any of these places.
The Chairperson said that Ms Isaacs would assist with the process, and the Committee would liaise with the DSTI on the arrangements.
The meeting was adjourned.
