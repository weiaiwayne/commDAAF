Meeting Summary
Video
The Joint Standing Committee received presentations from the Military Ombud and the Department of Defence (DOD) covering their operational performance, legislative framework, and the issue of deployment allowance management.
Committee Members highlighted ongoing challenges regarding the independence, investigative powers, and operational efficiency of the Military Ombud’s Office, as well as accountability for deployment allowances totalling R813.3 million, allocated above the prior year’s budget for troop deployments. Members noted structural and operational weaknesses within the Military Ombud framework, particularly the inability to initiate independent investigations, reliance on the executive for enforcement, and significant delays in implementing recommendations. Some cases had remained unresolved for over two years, with certain backlogs tracing as far back as 1993. The Committee emphasised that these systemic delays undermined the Ombud’s independence and the SANDF's 2025-2030 strategic plan.
The Committee raised concerns regarding the Military Ombud Act (Act No. 4 of 2012), noting that over five years had passed without substantial legislative amendments. Members recommended reforms including empowering the Ombud to initiate investigations beyond written complaints, making recommendations binding with mandatory timelines, establishing escalation mechanisms, ensuring operational independence with autonomous human resources and budgeting, and instituting structured reporting to Parliament.
Regarding deployment allowances, Members highlighted ongoing complaints from Southern African Development Community Mission in the
DRC
(
SAMIDRC
) deployments, where troops reportedly had had to purchase tents and sleeping equipment. The six-slide presentation, received only minutes before the meeting, was deemed insufficient, prompting a decision to request a comprehensive breakdown of the R813.3 million in allowances, to be submitted prior to the next Committee meeting.
The South African Military Ombud acknowledged its limitations in initiating investigations independently, confirming that these issues were under review in ongoing legislative amendments. He noted that operational independence was maintained, but some functions remained dependent on the DOD and Treasury. Implementation delays were attributed to compliance challenges rather than investigative shortcomings. He clarified that the Office’s 34% corporate support staff allocation supported essential functions -- policy, strategy, financial management, logistics, HR, information communication technology, and facilities -- necessary for operational effectiveness. Monthly liaison forum meetings with the DOD aimed to expedite outstanding cases, though attendance and seniority issues limited effectiveness. The Ombud also highlighted that complaints outside the statutory mandate, including disputes involving veterans and statutory forces, demonstrated a need for clearer public understanding of the Office’s jurisdiction.
The Chief of Staff of the SA National Defence Force (SANDF) addressed outstanding complaints, HR issues, and structural delays within the SANDF. He emphasised that the 29 outstanding cases did not reflect inaction, but arose from policy gaps, legislative review requirements, and procedural complexities. He confirmed that 38 HR policies were due for review, with five prioritised as critical, and that deadlines were being developed to improve implementation. Decentralised HR processes were cited as contributing to inconsistent policy application across units.
The Minister of Defence highlighted South Africa’s distinction as one of fewer than 50 countries globally, and one of three in Africa, with a Military Ombud. She emphasised the importance of ongoing policy and operational engagement with the Ombud and the DOD, and stressed that many operational challenges stemmed from policy, rather than legal constraints. She noted the Department’s HR budget constraints, projected to exceed R4 billion, necessitating centralised prioritisation for staffing and operational decisions. She underscored a preference for engagement and consensus in implementing Ombud recommendations, while retaining authority to direct action when necessary. She committed to collaboration to ensure efficient budget utilisation and operational effectiveness.
The Co-Chairperson emphasised the importance of addressing outstanding legislative and operational issues, particularly relating to the Ombud's independence, grievance mechanisms, and deployment allowance accountability. He said the Committee would continue to monitor progress closely, ensuring systemic issues were resolved and resources were managed effectively to support the SANDF and the Military Ombud
.

Military Ombud's 2024/25 annual report
Lt Gen Masondo, South African Military Ombud, said that in terms of its legislative mandate under the Military Ombud Act, 2012 (Act No. 4 of 2012), the Office investigates written complaints from serving and former members of the SANDF regarding conditions of service, as well as complaints from the public relating to the official conduct of SANDF members. The Office was required to resolve complaints fairly, economically and expeditiously, while safeguarding the rights of complainants.
For the year under review, the Office had achieved an 89.58% resolution rate, exceeding its 75% target. It reported 69 active cases at year-end, indicating a low carry-over into the 2025/26 financial year. A total of 68 outreach activities had been conducted nationally, expanding the Office’s footprint and stakeholder awareness. Governance performance was reported as strong, with 100% submission of accountability documents, and no fruitless or wasteful expenditure recorded. However, structural challenges persisted, including concerns regarding institutional independence, delays in SANDF cooperation, and a shortfall in compensation of employees (COE) funding.
The Ombud noted that 237 historical complaints from former civilian employees in the Hoedspruit cluster fell outside its mandate, and had been referred to the Secretary for Defence. These were excluded from normalised performance trends. Excluding this anomaly, the Office had managed approximately 353 cases, with a normalised resolution rate of 83.84%.
Financially, the Office reported an over-expenditure of R3.941 million on COE against an allocation of R45.619 million, attributing this to structural underfunding. Procurement delays and prolonged vacancy-filling processes were also highlighted as operational constraints. Under Goods and Services, R16.219 million had been spent, while procurement delays continued to affect expenditure.
The Office recommended that the Committee note the report, support a review of the COE baseline in line with approved posts, and monitor the DoD's implementation of recommendations and corrective measures to address systemic challenges.

Response to recommendations
Lt Gen Ramantswana proceeded to present on the responses to the outstanding Military Ombuds' recommendations not yet implemented by the DOD, or still in process of being implemented, for the 2024/25 financial year.
Nature of complaints
During the 2024/25 financial year, the Military Ombud’s Office received a range of complaints, predominantly concerning education and training, officer appointments, and grievance procedures. Key complaint categories included:
Officer appointments: Several members had raised concerns regarding eligibility and alignment with Department of Defence policy, particularly for members with previous disciplinary records.
Grievances: Several complaints were related to delayed grievance resolution or perceived unfair treatment in accordance with procedural requirements.
Technical and service-related allowances: Members submitted grievances concerning delayed payments or unresolved benefits.
Implementation of recommendations
The Department had made progress in implementing Ombud recommendations. However, many matters remained in process due to ongoing policy alignment, aimed at ensuring consistency in treatment across the SANDF. Examples included:
Officer appointment recommendations issued between October 2024 and November 2025 were pending due to policy adjustments to accommodate members with prior disciplinary records.
Grievance-related recommendations, including technical allowances and service benefits, were at various stages of implementation, with several matters nearing finalisation.
Certain cases, such as service termination benefits or medical injury claims, were contingent on procedural steps, including completion of human resources (HR) documentation and vacating Defence Force housing.
Across 28 key cases, most recommendations were either in the final stages of implementation or undergoing policy alignment to ensure compliance with departmental prescripts. Cases already resolved included members whose grievances had been addressed through promotion adjustments, or reassignment in line with qualifications and skills.
Outstanding allowances
Outstanding allowances for deployed members were being addressed systematically, with the Department in the process of creating payment codes, aligning financial processes, and coordinating with relevant HR units to ensure full settlement. Challenges included administrative processing delays, compliance with financial regulations, and ongoing policy adjustments to safeguard equitable treatment.
Conclusion
The Department was committed to implementing all Military Ombud recommendations and addressing outstanding allowances. Policy alignment processes were ongoing to ensure fairness, prevent inconsistencies, and guarantee compliance with DOD prescripts. The Department anticipates that most outstanding matters would be resolved in the coming weeks, with continued oversight and follow-up to ensure timely completion and operational efficiency within the SANDF.

Discussion
Mr Niehaus thanked both the Military Ombud and the Department of Defence for their presentations. He observed that the presentations highlighted deep structural and operational weaknesses within the Military Ombud framework, particularly concerning its investigative powers, independence, and delays in implementation. These concerns aligned with longstanding issues raised in previous Committee meetings, including the 2019 Portfolio Committee legal opinion on autonomy and the November 2025 Joint Standing Committee meeting, which had highlighted frustrations over the Ombud’s mandate and slow response to recommendations.
Mr Niehaus stressed the urgent need for legislative reform of the Military Ombud Act (Act No. 4 of 2012). He noted that internal consultations between the Department and the Ombud had made little progress, and that policy drafting remained incomplete despite prior commitments. He highlighted several key concerns:
the Ombud was restricted to investigating written complaints from specific parties, and could not initiate investigations independently;
while operationally independent, the Ombud relied on the executive for enforcement, with the Minister able to accept or reject recommendations without timelines or mandatory reasons; and
there were significant delays in implementing recommendations, with some cases exceeding two years and backlogs from 1993 still partially unresolved.
Mr Niehaus emphasised that more than five years of delay in finalising legislative amendments undermined both the Ombud’s operational autonomy and the SANDF's 2025-2030 strategic plan, while prolonging injustices for complainants. He recommended reforms, including:
empowering the Ombud to initiate investigations beyond written complaints;
making recommendations binding on the Minister and the SANDF. with mandatory timelines;
establishing escalation mechanisms for non-compliance, including parliamentary oversight;
ensuring operational independence as a Schedule 3 public entity, with autonomous budgeting and HR functions; and
instituting structured reporting to Parliament, with penalties for delays.
He noted that reliance on the Department and the SANDF for administrative functions contributed to ongoing delays.
In conclusion, Mr Niehaus urged the Department to report on progress with the legislative review and proposed amendments. He stressed that failure to act within the current administration’s term may require the Committee to consider a Committee Bill or Private Members Bill to address systemic gaps and secure the Ombud’s independence.
Mr C Hattingh (DA) acknowledged that the Military Ombud had achieved an 89% resolution rate and reduced carry-over cases, reflecting administrative performance. However, he highlighted the persistent issue of 29 outstanding recommendations that remained unimplemented despite ministerial instructions. He emphasised that this was a compliance issue, rather than an investigative problem, with delays often linked to policy alignment, ongoing processes, or pending reimbursements, including matters dating back to 2018 and 2019.
He noted that even when the Ombud upholds complaints and the Minister issues instructions, the Department and SANDF often failed to execute them, pointing to internal bottlenecks within SANDF structures, particularly Human Resources and service-level implementation. He questioned accountability, deadlines, and consequences for non-compliance. He also raised concerns about the Office’s staffing structure, noting that 34% of the approved 89 posts were corporate support roles, leaving a limited number of staff conducting investigative work, and questioned the sustainability of this structure.
Addressing the Minister, Mr Hattingh highlighted ongoing institutional and operational independence challenges, stressing the need to amend the Military Ombud Act. He requested clarity on the status of legislative amendments initiated in 2020, including when they would reach Parliament, whether the process had stalled or been abandoned, and whether the amendments would explicitly address enforcement, compliance, and accountability. He asked who was responsible for ensuring compliance, and what actions had been taken against officials causing delays.
Finally, he called on the Minister to introduce mandatory implementation deadlines -- for example, 60 or 90 days for all accepted Ombud recommendations, to ensure timely execution, strengthen accountability, and prevent indefinite delays within the SANDF.
Mr P Noe (ANC, Free State) focused on outstanding cases, Ombud performance, and procurement delays. He expressed dissatisfaction with broad departmental responses, emphasising the need for detailed updates, including timelines and progress. On performance, he acknowledged the energy and commitment of Lt Gen Masondo and his team, but urged the Department to reflect on how delays might demoralise staff. Regarding procurement delays, he sought clarification on whether these stemmed from the Ombud’s Office or external processes, stressing the importance of identifying bottlenecks to ensure efficient resource management.
Mr Hala asked for clarification on staffing and reporting at the Military Ombud’s Office, noting the impact of unfilled posts on the Office’s ability to fulfil its mandate, and requested a detailed explanation of the “three others” complaints referred to in the presentation.
Ms Sokanyile raised issues relating to procurement delays, expenditure, staffing, and legislative reform. She highlighted how procurement and staffing challenges risked undermining performance, and emphasised the need for clear timelines, structured follow-up, and legislative reform to address systemic implementation delays.
Mr M Ntshingila (MKP) noted that while the Ombud’s Office had met or exceeded performance targets, unresolved cases and policy gaps undermined effectiveness. He suggested an organisational design review to assess the necessity of all positions, and urged greater urgency in implementing ministerial instructions.
The Chairperson noted that human resource (HR) management and conditions of service remained primary drivers of dissatisfaction, and raised concerns about the high referral rate of complaints to other institutions. She requested clarity on these referrals and systemic issues contributing to them, and directed the Ombud and Department to respond comprehensively.
Responses
Lt Gen Masondo began by addressing the concerns raised by Mr Niehaus regarding the investigative powers and independence of the Military Ombud’s Office. He acknowledged the limitation that the Office currently could not initiate investigations independently, and confirmed that this was being considered within the ongoing legislative review process. He noted that many matters fell within the Office’s mandate but could not be pursued due to the absence of formal complaints.
On the issue of operational independence, he explained that while the Office was fully independent in conducting investigations, challenges remained in satisfying operational requirements, particularly those dependent on the Department of Defence and Treasury. To address this, proposals such as shared services and service-level agreements (SLAs) have been discussed to enhance autonomy without increasing the burden on the fiscus. These measures aimed to improve the Office’s ability to function effectively while maintaining accountability and collaboration with other oversight institutions.
Regarding implementation delays highlighted by Mr Hattingh, Lt Gen Masondo acknowledged that these reflected compliance challenges rather than investigative shortcomings. He supported introducing clear deadlines for ministerial instructions to expedite implementation and reduce delays. He also noted that while the Office understood departmental challenges, attempts were made to ensure timely compliance with instructions, thereby improving accountability and service delivery.
He addressed Mr Hattingh and Mr Noe’s concerns about the Office’s structure and procurement delays. He explained that the Office’s support structure of 20 posts versus 69 operational posts was appropriate, as the support posts were essential for the Office to perform its functions outside core investigative work. Procurement delays, he clarified, primarily occurred outside the Office, with requirements submitted promptly to the DoD, but approvals and processing could be slow. For example, planned perception surveys had been delayed due to procurement challenges, impacting the Office’s ability to assess stakeholder satisfaction in a timely manner.
Responding to Mr Hala and Ms Sokanyile, he confirmed that vacancies significantly affected service delivery, emphasising that prolonged unfilled posts hampered operational effectiveness and frustrated both staff and complainants. The Office continued to liaise with the DoD to expedite recruitment. Regarding budget utilisation, he highlighted that procurement delays hindered full expenditure of the operational budget, forcing the Office to return unspent funds despite ongoing operational needs.
Addressing Mr Nthsingila’s point on organisational design, Lt Gen Masondo noted that the full complement of 89 posts was required. He explained that regional offices were planned to enhance accessibility for complainants, and that sufficient staff were necessary to meet operational and independent status requirements, including procurement compliance.
Finally, responding to questions from the Chairperson, he explained the high referral rate to other oversight institutions. He clarified that the Office redirects complaints outside its mandate, rather than dismissing them, assisting complainants to engage the correct institutions. He also noted a recurring issue -- many SANDF members approach the Ombud before exhausting internal grievance processes, often due to frustration with delays in the grievance system. In these cases, the Office urges the Department to adhere to prescribed time frames to prevent premature escalation of complaints.
Lt Gen Masondo concluded by reaffirming the Office’s commitment to operational independence, enhanced legislative reform, and collaboration with the DoD to address delays, resource constraints, and systemic challenges affecting service delivery.
Further discussion
Mr Hattingh sought clarification on the Military Ombud’s Office structure. He noted that Lt Gen Masondo had earlier disputed concerns regarding the proportion of corporate support staff -- 34% of the approved 89 posts. However, he referred to page 10 of the Office’s annual report, which states that 66 positions were dedicated to core complaint resolution, while 34% were allocated to corporate support. He highlighted that this confirmed his earlier concern that a significant portion of resources was devoted to support functions rather than operational investigative work. He requested that Lt Gen Masondo reconsider his response in light of the report, and clarify how the current structure supports the Office’s operational effectiveness in fulfilling its core mandate.
Mr Niehaus posed two follow-up questions. The first was related to the legislative process required to address the structural and operational challenges faced by the Military Ombud’s Office. He emphasised that these issues were legislative in nature, and could not be fully resolved without amendments to the law.
His second follow-up addressed the Liaison Forum referred to by Lt Gen Masondo, which had been established to address delays in implementing recommendations. Mr Niehaus sought clarification on the effectiveness of this forum, including the frequency of its meetings and why its existence had not resulted in a more significant reduction in outstanding complaints. He highlighted that despite the forum, a considerable number of cases remained unresolved, raising questions about its practical impact in improving compliance and accelerating implementation.
The Chairperson raised a concern regarding slide nine of the presentation, specifically the “Other” category of new complaints, which totalled 302 cases -- representing more than half of all new complaints. The Chairperson requested a detailed breakdown of this category, seeking clarity on the specific issues and matters included. This information was necessary to understand the composition of complaints that did not fall within the standard categories, and to assess whether additional policy or operational measures were needed to address these concerns effectively.
In response to Mr Hattingh’s query regarding the corporate support structure, Lt Gen Masondo explained that the 34% allocation to corporate support reflected essential functions, including policy, strategy and planning, financial management, logistics, human resources, information communication technology (ICT), facility security, and reception. He noted that current staffing levels were insufficient for optimal performance, necessitating intensive internal support to achieve operational efficiency. The overall 34% was determined based on the scope of these functions relative to the Office’s core investigative responsibilities.
Regarding Mr Niehaus’ question on the effectiveness of the Liaison Forum with the DoD, Lt Gen Masondo confirmed that the forum meets monthly to address outstanding complaints. However, challenges included insufficient senior departmental representation and irregular attendance by some members, which limited the forum’s impact. To complement this, he meets monthly with the Chief of the Defence Force to resolve issues that cannot be addressed at the forum level, ensuring smoother operations. These structures, while not prescribed by legislation, had been implemented to maintain effective interaction and workflow between the Ombud’s Office and the Department.
Addressing the Chairperson’s concern about the “Other” category of complaints, he clarified that it included 237 complaints from former civilian employees of the Department, as well as complaints from veterans regarding benefits. It also captured issues that fell outside the strict legislative mandate of the Military Ombud.
Mr Johann Behr, Director: Intake Analysis, Military Ombud, elaborated that the Office often received complaints that the public associates with the military, even when they were outside the Ombud’s narrow legislative mandate. This included disputes involving veterans, statutory forces not integrated into the SANDF, and civilian disputes with military implications. He emphasised that this inflow reflected a misunderstanding of the Office’s mandate, and the public’s expectation that any military-related grievance could be addressed by the Military Ombud.
Lt Gen Ramantswana addressed questions regarding outstanding complaints and HR-related issues in the SANDF. Referring Members to slide seven of the Military Ombud’s presentation, he highlighted the total number of cases finalised during the financial year versus active cases still under the Military Ombud’s oversight. He emphasised that the 29 outstanding cases known to the DoD did not indicate inaction -- rather, substantial efforts were made to resolve grievances in collaboration with other institutions, ensuring due diligence at every stage.
He explained that delays often arose because complaints may highlight policy gaps, require legislative or procedural review, or have financial implications. For example, promotion-related grievances must align with existing policy instruments, legislation, and prior court outcomes, and may require consultation with legal and financial advisors. These processes make implementation complex, and cases were not merely “signed off” without careful consideration to prevent creating further issues. He assured Members that deadlines were being developed for implementation, and emphasised that progress was monitored closely with the Military Ombud to ensure accountability.
Addressing the Chairperson’s question regarding the “Other” category of complaints, the Chief of Staff noted that three key cases involved public members or retired personnel outside the direct HR scope. He confirmed that the majority of grievances were HR-related, reflecting dissatisfaction with policy application or interpretation at different command levels. The decentralised nature of the SANDF HR system meant policies may be applied differently across units, giving rise to complaints. He cited promotion policies as an example, where the Army, Air Force, and other services had unique procedures, though guided by central principles.
Lt Gen Ramantswana said that 38 HR policies were due for review, with five identified as critical. Resource prioritisation was focused on these critical policies to ensure effective implementation and address systemic delays. He stressed that delays were largely structural, linked to policy review capacity and procedural requirements, rather than a lack of departmental effort.
He concluded by reaffirming that the DoD continued to engage actively with the Military Ombud and other relevant institutions to resolve complaints efficiently, while balancing the need for policy alignment, legal compliance, and operational effectiveness. He expressed confidence that ongoing structural and procedural improvements would enhance grievance management and strengthen the interface between the Department, the Military Ombud, and SANDF personnel.
Minister's response
Minister Motshekga thanked the Chairperson and Committee Members for the engagement, noting that most of the questions had already been addressed by the Ombud and Department.
She began by highlighting a point of national pride -- South Africa was one of fewer than 50 countries globally, and one of fewer than three on the African continent, to have an Ombud’s Office. She emphasised that this was a notable achievement, and should be celebrated.
On operational matters, Minister Motshekga said that she maintained regular engagement with the Ombud, the boards, and the Chief of Defence Staff, both formally and informally. She described these interactions as productive and ongoing, allowing for discussion and resolution of key issues. She noted the importance of continued dialogue on the Ombud’s autonomy, acknowledging that while operational independence was important, most challenges were administrative rather than legal.
Regarding the implementation of Ombud recommendations, the Minister said that where instructions could not be implemented, the Department had the right to approach the courts. In most cases, the courts had agreed with the Department. She emphasised that her approach prioritises engagement, discussion, and mutual agreement rather than unilateral enforcement, but clarified that she could overrule the Department if reasonable justification was not provided.
Minister Motshekga addressed concerns about staffing and administrative constraints, noting that financial pressures, including an overspent HR budget, required centralised control over appointments and prioritisation within the global departmental budget. She committed to working with the Ombud to prioritise posts, ensuring operational effectiveness while mitigating resource constraints. She also highlighted that many recurring Ombud complaints were related to HR policies, and resolving these policy gaps would reduce administrative burdens for the Office and improve service delivery.
On the matter of ministerial instructions, she clarified that the law required written justification for decisions, and that implementation was balanced with operational feasibility and financial implications. She reaffirmed that her Office seeks to resolve issues through engagement and consensus rather than strict enforcement, while retaining the authority to direct implementation when necessary.
Minister Motshekga concluded by reaffirming the Department’s commitment to a cooperative relationship with the Ombud. She emphasised the critical role of the Office in mitigating tensions, resolving disputes, and supporting effective administration, particularly in labour relations within the SANDF. She noted that while some Committee Members may advocate a fully independent Section 9 body reporting directly to Parliament, she was not convinced that legal reform was required for administrative autonomy, stressing that many issues could be managed through policy and engagement. She would leave it to Parliament and the Committee to decide whether a legislative route was necessary.
Mr Hattingh said he would not repeat his outstanding question regarding the command of the SANDF. He focused instead on the prevalence of grievances within the Department. He asked whether the Individual Grievance Regulations of 2011 had been fully implemented, and whether the grievance board, as set out in Section 16 of those regulations, had been formally established and was functioning effectively. He emphasised the need for clarity on these points to ensure that the grievance mechanisms within the SANDF were operational and adequately addressed the issues raised by personnel.
Mr Niehaus expressed concern regarding the apparent disconnect between the Minister’s position on legislative review and the information previously provided by Lt Gen Masondo. He said that if he understood the Minister correctly, she had little appetite to pursue a formal review of the Military Ombud Act, relying instead on informal arrangements with the Ombud to resolve issues. He stressed that such informal arrangements may work at times, but they were inherently inconsistent, which underscored the importance of the statutory legislative review process to ensure continuity and enforceability. He emphasised that the independence of the Military Ombud, particularly the ability to initiate investigations independently of submitted complaints, was a legislative matter that could not be secured through informal coordination.
Mr Niehaus expressed concern that while the current working relationship between the Minister and the Ombud may be functional, historical instances had demonstrated that this was not always the case. The Minister’s reluctance to advance legislative amendments placed the responsibility on the Committee to assume a proactive role, potentially through a Committee Bill or a Private Members Bill, to safeguard the Ombud’s independence and operational effectiveness. He concluded by requesting clarity from both the Minister and Lt Gen Masondo, noting the critical need to reconcile their positions in order to determine the way forward for legislative review, and ensure the Military Ombud’s mandate and independence were adequately protected.
Minister Motshekga acknowledged Mr Hattingh’s query regarding the 2011 Individual Grievance Regulations, and said she could not provide an immediate response. She would need to consult the Department’s legal team to confirm whether the grievance board, as contemplated in section 16 of the regulations, had been fully established and was functioning effectively. She committed to returning to the Committee with a thorough and accurate response once the necessary verification had been completed.
Regarding the concerns raised by Mr Niehaus about legislative review, she reiterated her earlier position that it was ultimately the Committee’s prerogative to pursue a Committee Bill or Private Members Bill should they wish to advance amendments to the Military Ombud Act. She emphasised that in her engagements with the Military Ombud, the issues raised about autonomy were largely policy matters, rather than legal constraints. She stressed that alignment of policy within the Department could address many operational challenges without requiring legislative change.
On administrative matters, particularly the appointment of staff within the Military Ombud’s Office, Minister Motshekga highlighted the Department’s ongoing HR constraints. She explained that while certain administrative decisions -- such as appointments -- may be necessary for operational independence, the Department must balance these against budgetary limitations, including an overspending HR budget projected to exceed R4 billion in the new financial year. Consequently, decisions on appointments and operational priorities must be coordinated centrally to ensure financial sustainability.
The Minister also addressed concerns about budget implementation and procurement delays, noting that while the Office had an approved budget, administrative inefficiencies and late procurement processes could hinder effective utilisation. She indicated that the Department was working with the Military Ombud to ensure that approved budgets could be implemented efficiently, while acknowledging ongoing challenges in HR and other operational areas.
She concluded by reaffirming the Department’s commitment to policy alignment, administrative efficiency, and collaboration with the Military Ombud. She stressed that while certain approved initiatives, such as outreach programmes, were important, they must be balanced against financial realities, requiring prioritisation and careful planning to manage scarce resources effectively.
The Chairperson thanked Minister Motshekga for her responses, and noted that any outstanding matters should be addressed in writing.

He expressed concern that the six-slide presentation had not met the original objective of his request, suggesting there may have been a miscommunication. He welcomed any further detailed breakdown the Department could provide during the meeting, but reiterated that this had been the actual purpose of the initial request.
Mr Niehaus expressed strong dissatisfaction with proceeding with the presentation on deployment allowances, noting that it had been received only during the meeting, contrary to the Committee’s agreed practice of receiving materials in advance. He described this as disrespectful to the Committee, and said it prevented Members from properly interrogating the information provided.
He highlighted that concerns and complaints from soldiers deployed in the DRC had not been adequately addressed in the report. The limited information presented, he argued, did not reflect the depth or scope originally requested. In line with Mr Gotsell’s earlier comments, he proposed that the Department be instructed to prepare a proper, comprehensive presentation, submitted in good time for the next Joint Standing Committee meeting, allowing Members to fully engage with and assess the information.
He concluded by stating that the Committee must send a clear message to the DoD that such disregard for agreed procedures and insufficient reporting would not be tolerated.
A decision was made that no presentation was to occur.
The Chairperson acknowledged the challenges inherited by the Minister, noting that the Department faced significant funding and resource constraints. He emphasised the need to balance Committee concerns regarding the Military Ombud's Office with the practical dilemmas outlined by the Minister, including policy, institutional, and internal capacity issues.
He highlighted the importance of ensuring that the Ombud's Office operates independently of personal relations, noting that while the Minister may have strong working relations, this may not always be the case. He stressed that any necessary policy or institutional reforms should be undertaken promptly, with clear timelines provided to the Committee.
He noted that the Committee would consider the desirability of legislative reforms, including the potential designation of the Military Ombud's Office as a Chapter 9 institution, taking into account financial and resource implications. He emphasised the need for continuous engagement with the Department to ensure reforms were effective. He also highlighted that internal capacity reforms should extend beyond HR to include technology solutions, such as artificial intelligence, which could enhance the efficiency of the Ombud's Office.
He concluded by stressing the importance of ongoing institutional, legislative, policy, and cultural reforms. He thanked the Minister for her availability, engagement, and commitment to providing written responses to outstanding issues.
Committee matters
Outstanding minutes for the meeting on 20 February were considered.
Amendments were made, before it was moved for adoption by Mr Hattingh and seconded by Mr R Cebekhulu (IFP).
The meeting was adjourned.
