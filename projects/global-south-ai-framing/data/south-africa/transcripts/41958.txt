Annual Reports 2024/25
The National Advisory Council on Innovation (NACI) 2024/25 Annual Report focused on its operations, impact of its strategic initiatives, expenditure and challenges. NACI explained its role is advisory and its outputs are designed to be high-quality, transformative, and integrated into national policy frameworks. Institutional renewal and the eventual establishment of NACI as a standalone entity are expected to enhance capacity, accountability, and its ability to influence South Africa’s innovation ecosystem effectively.
Committee members asked about the overexpenditure on goods and services and requested an itemised account. They sought assurance that funds utilized contributed meaningfully to NACI’s mandate. Attention was drawn to NACI’s limited production of advisory documents, with only four pieces produced over the year despite a substantial budget of nearly R20 million. Members also questioned NACI’s international engagements and expressed concern over the costs of international travel. They sought evidence linking these engagements to practical outcomes benefiting South Africa. In response, NACI highlighted that international participation was integral to benchmarking, acquiring expertise, and strengthening domestic policy development and aligning South Africa’s Science, Technology, and Innovation (STI) strategies with global best practices.
The Committee probed the outcomes of the inaugural National System of Innovation (NSI) Transformation Summit. Members sought concrete examples of how the summit’s recommendations had translated into practical improvements for ordinary South Africans. NACI explained that over 300 stakeholders across government, academia, and private sector participated, producing recommendations now integrated into the Department of Science, Technology and Innovation (DSTI) strategic and annual performance plans.
There were questions on audit accountability since NACI currently operates under DSTI and this limited independent oversight. However, NACI confirmed that once established as a standalone entity, it would receive independent audits, thereby enhancing accountability and transparency.
Members also raised concerns about NACI being not sufficiently visible to the public or to key government stakeholders and lacking influence for the implementation of its recommendation, as well as its high vacancy rates. In response, NACI acknowledged the challenge of ensuring implementation across government. To address this, the NACI Amendment Bill and the renewal process of the institution seek to reposition NACI as a broader advisory body, serving not only DSTI but the entire government. The Council indicated reliance on temporary staff and departmental support to fill critical positions.
Further discussion focused on NACI’s engagement with local government and private sector. Members were concerned about the limited integration of innovation at municipal levels, citing an average Municipal Innovation Maturity Index (MIMI) score of two out of five. NACI described ongoing initiatives, including Regional Innovation Support Programs and multi-stakeholder forums, aimed at promoting innovation ecosystems at provincial and local levels. On private sector R&D, NACI emphasized efforts to incentivize investment through sector innovation funds, tax incentives, and strategic recommendations, acknowledging that increasing funding is a long-term endeavour requiring sustained effort.
On the Committee’s concern about potential duplication among DSI entities, NACI assured it that while different entities may engage in related areas such as Artificial Intelligence, their approaches are complementary rather than duplicative.
Meeting report
The Chairperson noted that this was Ms Morgan’s first engagement with the Committee since assuming her new responsibilities as Acting CEO and welcomed her warmly. This session marked the final engagement in the Committee’s cycle of reviewing 2024/25 annual reports of all DSI entities, including previous engagements which she described as informative, productive, and collaborative. This meeting provided an important opportunity to reflect on NACI’s performance, its accountability for public funds, and its advisory role within the broader National System of Innovation (NSI).
Apologies were noted from the Minister of Science and Innovation who was attending a UNESCO Committee meeting and from the Deputy Minister who was participating in a Cabinet Committee meeting. The DSI Director-General was also absent due to his attendance at a SKA Council meeting in Australia.
Opening remarks by DSI Deputy Director-General
Mr Imran Patel, Deputy Director-General (DDG) for Research Development and Support, appreciated the Committee’s consistent engagement, stating that these sessions are “extremely useful and constructive” for both the department and its entities. The insights and recommendations raised by the Committee are taken seriously and are instrumental in improving the effectiveness of departmental operations.
Mr Patel highlighted that NACI is assuming a much more strategic and prominent role within the department’s institutional ecosystem. As the department advances its Decadal Plan for Science, Technology and Innovation, NACI is envisioned to provide stronger strategic leadership, foresight capacity, and coordination among key role players and stakeholders.
DSI is confident that, over the next few years, the vision outlined for NACI in the Decadal Plan will become increasingly evident. This would, however, depend on ensuring that NACI’s existing statutory responsibilities are effectively carried out within proper governance frameworks and compliance mechanisms.
Introduction by NACI Chairperson
Mr Tilson Manyoni acknowledged that this engagement was taking place following several leadership transitions within the organisation. He proudly announced that NACI’s former CEO, Dr Mlungisi Cele, had been appointed as the new DSI Director-General. Ms Anneline Morgan had been appointed as Acting CEO and had “hit the ground running” to ensure operational continuity and stability during the transition. He commended her leadership and assured the Committee that the organisation had continued to function smoothly, without major disruptions to its work programme.
Mr Manyoni introduced the NACI executive team and said that NACI had prepared a comprehensive report on its 2024/25 performance. He aligned himself with Mr Patel’s remarks, emphasising that NACI’s role is being amplified as it enters a new strategic phase. Over the next three years, NACI intends to play a massive role in shaping South Africa’s science, innovation, and technology landscape, positioning itself as the strategic spearhead of the Department’s transformation and innovation agenda.
National Advisory Council on Innovation (NACI) 2024/25
Annual Report
Ms Anneline Morgan presented on NACI’s legislative mandate, performance achievements, governance and human resource matters, financial performance, and a summary of key conclusions and future plans.
NACI derives its mandate from the NACI Act, Act No. 55 of 1997, which establishes NACI as an advisory body to the Minister of Science and Technology, and through the Minister, to Cabinet, on all matters related to science, mathematics, innovation, and technology including indigenous technologies in promoting and achieving South Africa’s national objectives. NACI’s central function is to coordinate and stimulate the National System of Innovation (NSI). The Council is tasked with promoting science, technology, innovation, and technology transfer, as well as advising the Minister on the implementation of the White Paper on Science, Technology and Innovation and the Decadal Plan. It also identifies Research and Development (R&D) priorities for inclusion in government funding.
To fulfil this role, NACI produces the Annual R&D Indicators Report, which tracks trends in investment and identifies areas of both growth and decline across the national innovation system. NACI also has the responsibility to promote science and natural sciences education through STEM skills development. It also maintains and develops the National STI Information Portal, which serves as a central knowledge and data-sharing platform for the national system of innovation.
Ms Morgan underscored the importance of international cooperation, noting that NACI had hosted the G20 Chief Science Advisors’ Meeting and continues to build strategic relationships under multilateral platforms such as BRICS, especially in foresight and science and technology policy areas. It also plays a role in developing new STI-related legislation when required. The Council had been instrumental in conducting the foresight exercise that informed both the White Paper and the Decadal Plan. Knowledge dissemination is integral to NACI’s operations, and this is achieved through continuous policy dialogues, stakeholder engagements, and public communication on its products and findings.
Performance Outcomes and Achievements
• NACI’s first performance outcome focused on providing evidence-based, efficient, and timely policy advice to the Minister. During 2024/25 it produced four policy advice documents, exceeding its target of three:
- The first policy advice report addressed constraints in R&D investment in South Africa, analysing the reasons for the decline in R&D expenditure by major businesses and providing recommendations to the Minister on how to reverse this.
- The second policy advice focused on recommendations towards the development of a National Artificial Intelligence (AI) Strategy for South Africa. This was developed in collaboration with the Department of Telecommunications and Digital Technologies, following extensive stakeholder consultations across public and private sectors.
- A Cybersecurity Framework, prepared in partnership with subject experts and multiple government departments to strengthen national preparedness in digital security.
- The Hydrogen Economy Advisory, aimed at fostering innovation, entrepreneurship, and R&D investment in hydrogen technologies, and proposing the establishment of a Hydrogen Transformative Fund to stimulate small and medium enterprise participation.
Over the course of the year, NACI held four expert engagements and more than 100 consultations across the system. This input had been adopted by the Department of Communications and Digital Technologies, contributing directly to the formulation of the National AI Framework. Also noted was the policy advice on the establishment of a Transformative Hydrogen Fund to support early-stage technologies and the commercialisation of green hydrogen innovations in South Africa.
• The second outcome was to contribute to the building of a National System of Innovation monitoring, evaluation and learning capability to assess the health of the NSI and its contribution to sustainable and inclusive development. NACI finalised the draft STI Indicators Report by March 2025 and produced three M&E reports.
The first of these was a synthesis report towards the Decadal Plan on STI, which provided key analytical input to the Plan. The second was a synthesis report on industrialisation through innovation, examining the link between innovation and South Africa’s industrial development. The third was an inception baseline diagnostic assessment on transformation in the NSI, aimed at understanding equity, diversity, and inclusion within the innovation ecosystem. This work would be finalised in 2025/26. NACI had also made continuous updates to its National STI Information Portal, specifically enhancing the STI statistics section, now available online.
• The third outcome of contributing towards building an effective and responsive NSI. Under this outcome, NACI provided leadership in institutionalising foresight across the system, in line with the 2019 White Paper on STI and the Decadal Plan. During the 2024–2025 year, NACI participated in several international forums and expert groups, including the International Academic Symposium on Foresight for Science, Technology and Innovation Policy, the Future Foresight Symposium, and the United Nations ADAPT Experts Committee on Technology Foresight and Technology Assessment. These engagements allowed NACI to share its experiences, strengthen its knowledge base, and learn from international best practices in foresight, planning, and evaluation.
• The fourth outcome aimed to transform NACI into a smart, efficient, and learning organisation. NACI ensured visibility of its work through participation in the Africa Science Focus Podcast Series on
SciDev.Net
, where it discussed evidence-based science advisory for policymakers. The Council also conducted provincial roadshows, notably at the University of KwaZulu-Natal, to create awareness around the 2024 STI Indicators Report following its launch. A key highlight was hosting the NACI Transformation Summit in March 2025, under the theme
“Science, Technology and Innovation at the Centre of Government, Education, Society and Industry.”
The summit’s recommendations were submitted to the Minister as part of NACI’s formal policy advice.
Ms Morgan summarised NACI’s overall performance, confirming that most targets had been achieved or exceeded. The only partially achieved target was the foresight exercise, where substantial work had been completed but final outputs would continue this year.
Financial Overview
Mr Robert Shaku, DSI CFO, explained that NACI’s finances are administered as part of the Department of Science, Technology and Innovation (DSTI) and are audited by the Auditor-General within the departmental framework. NACI was allocated a 2024/25 budget of R21 million, which comprised R14.6 million for compensation of employees and R7.1 million for goods and services. Actual expenditure shows a minor variance of R25,000 on goods and services and compensation of employees had a variance of R69,000. Since NACI operates within the department’s consolidated budget; the overspending was absorbed by the department and did not affect overall compliance or breach of financial management standards and no audit findings were raised.
Way Forward
Ms Morgan informed Members that the Department of Science, Technology and Innovation had initiated a process to reposition NACI as an independent entity, and that a draft Amendment Bill had already been prepared.
Discussion
Mr T Mjadu (MKP) acknowledged the comprehensive nature of the report but he requested further clarity on financial transparency, value for money, institutional efficiency, and accountability within NACI’s operations.
He first expressed a concern about the R7.1 million expended on goods and services. The report did not specify how these funds had been utilised, making it difficult for the Committee to assess if spending had been appropriate and aligned with NACI’s core mandate. He requested an itemised breakdown of this expenditure with explanations for each item to ensure full transparency in the use of public funds.
Turning to performance outputs, Mr Mjadu observed that NACI’s primary mandate was to provide evidence-based policy advice on science, technology, and innovation to government. However, it had produced only four key advisory reports for the entire financial year. He questioned if this level of output was commensurate with the organisation’s budget and resources, suggesting that the current productivity levels may not adequately reflect value for the expenditure incurred. In his view, the South African public was not receiving sufficient returns on the investment made into NACI’s work.
Mr Mjadu also raised questions about international engagements undertaken by NACI. While he acknowledged the value of global collaboration and knowledge exchange but cautioned against costly engagements without demonstrating tangible domestic benefits. He requested that NACI provide evidence of measurable outcomes or policy impacts that had resulted from these international activities, particularly in its advisory work to government.
On NACI’s Transformation Summit and renewal and repositioning agenda, Mr Mjadu expressed scepticism about the impact of these initiatives. While the report used extensive terminology on transformation, it provided little evidence of practical change. He requested that NACI furnish three clear and concrete examples of how the transformation process had improved its functioning or benefitted for ordinary South Africans.
Lastly, Mr Mjadu noted that NACI’s finances were audited as part of the DSTI audit and not independently. In his view, this arrangement limited the Committee’s ability to evaluate NACI’s financial performance and internal controls separately. He proposed that NACI should in future undergo an independent audit to enhance accountability. He emphasised the need for improved transparency, clearer performance measures, and demonstrable public benefit from NACI’s work to strengthen public trust and institutional credibility.
Ms N Mazzone (DA) appreciated the comprehensive briefing. NACI’s central mandate as an advisory body was ensuring that science, technology, and innovation (STI) policies contribute meaningfully to South Africans. However, she expressed growing concern that NACI appeared to be losing visibility and influence in the broader government ecosystem. The Council’s critical advisory role seemed to have diminished in prominence, particularly since the restructuring and separation of the Department of Science and Innovation from the Department of Higher Education. She questioned if this administrative change had inadvertently led to reduced coordination and prioritisation of NACI’s work within departmental and intergovernmental structures.
Ms Mazzone requested that NACI provide the Committee with a clear overview of the stakeholder engagements it had conducted across government and other sectors. Such information would assist in determining if NACI remained actively involved and influential in shaping national STI policy and in ensuring that its recommendations were being integrated into decision-making processes across various departments.
She had raised this persistent concern in previous committee meetings that NACI’s relevance and impact were not sufficiently visible to the public or to key government stakeholders. NACI should demonstrate more concretely how its policy advice and research contribute to addressing pressing national challenges, including unemployment, economic growth, and youth development through science and innovation. Many South Africans still do not fully understand how innovation can improve their lives or drive economic transformation. NACI had a vital role in bridging this gap, by not only producing technical advice but also communicating the social and economic value of science in accessible and practical terms.
Another point of concern she raised was the coordination and duplication of functions among the DSTI entities. Ms Mazzone observed that there appeared to be overlaps and ambiguities on institutional responsibilities, with multiple entities often working on similar projects without clear lines of accountability. She cautioned that this could lead to inefficiencies and diminished impact and asked NACI to clarify if such duplication existed.
Turning to institutional governance, Ms Mazzone referred to previous committee discussions about staff turnover and the preservation of institutional knowledge within NACI. She enquired if there had been progress in addressing this challenge, noting that the loss of experienced personnel and the lack of continuity could undermine its ability to deliver consistent, high-quality policy advice.
Lastly, she requested that NACI provide an update on investment trends in STI domestically and internationally. Had there been an increase in private sector investment and foreign funding in South Africa’s STI sector and what was NACI’s assessment of the current trajectory of such investments?
Mr T Ramongalo (DA) commended the NACI presentation and annual report. He raised concerns about exceeding the goods and services budget and asked for the causes of this overspending. What mitigation measures had been put in place to prevent recurrence?
Mr Ramongalo referred to NACI’s indication that a high vacancy rate had impacted operations. He questioned how it intended to function effectively amid ongoing fiscal constraints, given that filling posts requires funding that may not be available. He asked if there was a sustainable operational plan that allows it to continue fulfilling its mandate without necessarily filling all vacant positions, and how associated costs would be managed.
On its inaugural National System of Innovation (NSI) Transformation Summit, Mr Ramongalo noted that the summit had concluded that post-apartheid NSI transformation had not achieved its intended goals. He requested the summit’s adopted recommendations so the Committee could exercise more effective oversight of their implementation. He asked if NACI had begun acting on any of those recommendations.
Mr Ramongalo emphasised the critical role of local government as the primary implementer of national policies at grassroots level. Referring to the 2024 STI Indicators Report, he expressed concern that the average Municipal Innovation Maturity Index score stood at only two out of five, indicating limited innovation capacity and poor implementation of STI initiatives within municipalities. He called for a more coordinated and synergised approach across all spheres of government to ensure that municipalities are adequately empowered to drive innovation and effectively implement STI policy directives.
Dr S Thembekwayo (EFF) addressed the impact of the inaugural National System of Innovation (NSI) Transformation Summit. She enquired how NACI intended to monitor and follow up on the numerous recommendations that had emerged from the summit and were subsequently referred to DSTI. It was important to ensure that these recommendations are implemented and lead to tangible, systemic change within the NSI.
Dr Thembekwayo spoke to racial diversity and inclusivity, specifically questioning how NACI planned to target and recruit persons with disabilities as part of its diversity strategy. True transformation required visible representation and inclusion of people with disabilities within the institution.
Lastly, she highlighted the importance of staff development and retention for institutional sustainability. She asked what provisions the revised Performance Management and Development System (PMDS) policy would include to address employee career growth and development, create graduate training and workplace exposure opportunities, and promote upskilling for improved organisational performance.
Mr V Nkosi (ANC) asked how NACI planned to address the ongoing decline in national R&D investment, with particular emphasis on strategies to incentivise greater private sector participation and to increase overall funding to achieve the national R&D expenditure target.
Mr Nkosi sought clarity on the mechanisms in place to ensure that stakeholder engagements including summits, consultations, and workshops translate into concrete policy reforms and result in measurable improvements within the NSI. He cautioned against engagements that remain theoretical without producing visible impact or reform outcomes.
Lastly, Mr Nkosi enquired about the strategies NACI is implementing to strengthen its internal capacity, especially in human resources, institutional resilience, and adaptability to rapidly evolving technological and scientific developments. NACI’s ability to remain responsive and agile in a changing innovation landscape was central to its effectiveness.
NACI response
Mr Manyoni appreciated the insightful questions and constructive feedback. He began by addressing the NSI Transformation Summit, describing it as a historic milestone for NACI. It was the first summit in its history, convening over 300 stakeholders from government, higher education, business, and civil society. The summit aimed to enhance private sector participation in the STI ecosystem and to generate a set of actionable recommendations with relevance beyond DSTI. The theme of the summit,
“Placing STI at the Centre of Society, Government and Industry,”
underscored the collaborative approach adopted.
He reported that following two days of extensive engagement, participants adopted a set of declarations outlining priority actions for transforming the national innovation system. These declarations were formally presented to the Minister and the DSTI. The Department had since resolved to incorporate the summit’s resolutions into its implementation. He assured the Committee that NACI would make the declarations available for its oversight.
On NACI’s policy advisory outputs, Mr Manyoni clarified that NACI functions as an advisory body rather than an implementing agency. Its mandate is to generate evidence-based policy advice either at the request of the Minister or through Council’s own initiative to inform national decision-making on STI matters. He emphasised that quality and strategic value of advice are more critical than quantity.
He again outlined the four major policy advice documents produced during the reporting year, each focusing on a transformative area of national importance. These initiatives were designed to yield long-term transformative impact, provided the Department implements them as advised.
On NACI’s institutional capacity, he acknowledged challenges in human resources and staff retention, noting that the organisation’s structural design has historically limited its ability to recruit and retain skilled professionals. However, NACI had begun a repositioning and renewal process over the past three years. The goal is to ensure that NACI becomes fully resourced and well-capacitated to respond effectively to national STI priorities.
Mr Manyoni highlighted plans to establish a dedicated Monitoring and Evaluation (M&E) capability within NACI, as proposed in the forthcoming legislative amendment. This would enhance coordination across government departments and allow NACI to aggregate STI activities at a national level.
On Ms Mazzone’s question on interdepartmental coordination, Mr Manyoni agreed that collaboration with other departments remains a priority. He cited examples such as the hydrogen initiative, which was developed jointly with the Department of Trade, Industry and Competition and the Department of Energy. The envisioned new structure of NACI would institutionalise such partnerships more systematically.
Addressing Mr Mjadu’s concern on impact and value for money, he stated that while NACI does not implement projects directly, its influence lies in the policy and strategic direction it provides to the Minister and Cabinet. The effectiveness of NACI’s work depends on the extent to which the Department acts upon the recommendations made.
Mr Manyoni concluded by affirming NACI’s commitment to transparency, accountability, and continuous improvement. He expressed optimism that the planned repositioning and new strategic orientation would enable NACI to serve not only DSTI but also the broader government and society more effectively.
Ms Morgan clarified the key cost drivers for NACI’s expenditure, noting that a substantial portion of spending under “goods and services” arises from commissioned research work, which necessitates the engagement of independent experts and consultants. She also pointed out that policy dialogues and engagements, such as the Transformation Summit, are significant cost items. The Summit required additional funding from the Department, as the event exceeded NACI’s internal budgetary scope.
She explained that NACI has submitted the Summit’s recommendations to the Minister and the Department, and that the Department has committed to integrating these recommendations into its annual plans and strategic activities. These outcomes will also be shared with the Committee in due course.
On international engagements, Ms Morgan highlighted that NACI’s work necessitates global collaboration, as science, technology, and innovation are inherently international. NACI participates actively in BRICS foresight working groups and other multilateral and bilateral partnerships, which enable benchmarking against global best practices. This strengthens NACI’s capacity to provide informed policy advice. NACI is preparing to host a G20 science and technology stream in the upcoming reporting cycle, reflecting the Council’s growing international role.
On policy influence and advice uptake, Ms Morgan acknowledged that while NACI provides policy advice to the Minister, a key challenge remains ensuring its implementation across government. To address this, the NACI Amendment Bill and the renewal process of the institution seek to reposition NACI as a broader advisory body, serving not only the Department but the entire government. This repositioning includes the revival of the Inter-Ministerial Committee (IMC) on Science and Technology and the Presidential Plenary on STI, which would enhance NACI’s reach and impact.
On private sector and foreign investment in R&D, NACI’s role is advisory rather than implementation focused. The Council provides evidence-based recommendations to the Department, which is then responsible for operationalising them through its programmes.
On local government and grassroots innovation, NACI aims to strengthen municipal-level innovation ecosystems. The institution’s STI Indicators Report now assesses performance at various levels, including the local and provincial spheres. Collaboration with the Department is ongoing to ensure the decentralisation of innovation, supporting service delivery and community-level problem-solving.
Turning to human resources, Ms Morgan outlined that NACI works closely with the Department’s HR systems for recruitment and staff development. Employees have personal development plans, and training needs are supported accordingly. NACI also engages the Department to ensure representation of persons with disabilities and equitable racial diversity. She acknowledged the high vacancy rate and explained that temporary staff and secondments from the Department have been used to sustain operations. The ongoing renewal process would review the organisational structure and job descriptions to align more effectively with NACI’s evolving mandate.
Mr Shaku clarified that NACI is not yet a standalone institution, and its budget, HR, IT, and governance support are still managed through the Department. As such, NACI is audited as part of the Department, though plans are underway to establish it as a fully independent entity through legislative amendment.
He detailed the expenditure breakdown, noting that the largest portion of R2.7 million was spent on employment through agencies, due to the use of temporary staff to fill vacancies resulting from funding constraints. About R2 million was allocated to Council members’ honoraria for their advisory work, while additional spending covered travel, accommodation, venue hire, and office expenses for events such as the Transformation Summit. The CFO emphasized that these expenditures directly support NACI’s policy advisory function and are considered value-for-money investments, given the strategic importance of NACI’s outputs.
DSTI response
Mr Patel reflected on the value-for-money principle guiding the work of the Council, noting that NACI comprises high-level experts who, in many cases, are already employed within other public entities or agencies. As such, they are not remunerated additionally for their contributions except in specific cases where payment is justified. This arrangement enables NACI to draw on significant intellectual and institutional capacity without incurring unnecessary expenditure. Members’ diverse professional backgrounds and networks contribute substantial strategic value, as evidenced by the transformative potential of several advisory notes, such as on the Hydrogen Investment Fund and AI Strategy, which have already influenced department priorities.
On the Transformation Summit recommendations, Mr Patel clarified that many of the resolutions emanating from the inaugural Summit have already been integrated into the Department’s Strategic Plan and Annual Performance Plan (APP). Since the Summit was held early in the financial year, several of its outcomes were immediately incorporated into ongoing programmes. Remaining recommendations requiring further refinement are being monitored by the Planning Unit which ensures that outstanding matters are addressed during the planning cycle. Mr Patel assured the Committee that when the Department presents its next APP, it will be able to demonstrate precisely how each recommendation has been incorporated into department priorities.
On private-sector investment in research and development (R&D), Mr Patel acknowledged that while there has been a persistent decline, the Department is actively pursuing solutions. Drawing on the insights of the NACI report, he noted that structural and financial constraints within the private sector have limited R&D contributions. However, positive signals suggest improvement as broader economic conditions such as energy stability continue to normalise. The Chief Financial Officer, under instruction from the Minister and Director-General, is leading a process to consolidate opportunities that could be presented to the private sector to enhance its participation.
He highlighted several existing instruments aimed at leveraging collaborative investment, including Sector Innovation Funds, which pool both government and private resources to support targeted industries such as forestry and manufacturing. Over time, these instruments have evolved toward matching-fund models. Additional mechanisms such as R&D tax incentives also exist to encourage greater business involvement. DSTI, working in conjunction with agencies, continues to refine these approaches to create stronger incentives and a more enabling environment for sustained private-sector participation. However, reversing the decline in R&D investment is not a short-term process but one that requires consistent policy effort and coordination.
Addressing concerns about duplication of functions across science and innovation entities, Mr Patel clarified that while overlapping thematic areas such as artificial intelligence may appear in multiple institutional agendas, each entity’s focus is distinct and complementary. For example, while the Council for Scientific and Industrial Research (CSIR) may focus on applied aspects of AI, NACI’s role is primarily policy- oriented. He reassured the Committee that the Department continually works to align and streamline functions to ensure that resources are not wasted and that inter-agency collaboration remains productive. He proposed that future department presentations include a consolidated overview of all entities, detailing their specific responsibilities and areas of interface.
Mr Patel underscored the importance of embedding STI efforts at all levels of government. He outlined the Department’s collaboration with provincial and local governments through initiatives such as the Regional Innovation Support Programme and the District Development Model. These partnerships have matured significantly over recent years, with the establishment of projects across numerous districts and municipalities. A Geographic Information System (GIS) has been developed to map the Department’s work geographically and ensure better integration of innovation activities within local contexts. As part of the next performance cycle, project managers will be required to indicate if their programmes are linked to local government or specific regions, strengthening the alignment of national innovation efforts with community-level development priorities.
Mr Patel said that while considerable progress has been made, further effort is required to deepen the role of local government in the STI ecosystem. He reiterated the Department’s commitment to collaborative governance, efficient resource use, and continual improvement in translating scientific innovation into tangible societal and economic impact.
The Chairperson thanked Mr Patel and NACI for their comprehensive presentation and engagement as well as Committee members for their valuable insights, questions, and contributions which enriched the discussion.
Meeting adjourned.
