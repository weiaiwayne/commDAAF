The Portfolio Committee on Health held a virtual meeting to receive comprehensive briefings from entities in the health portfolio on their Annual Reports for the 2023/24 financial year. These entities included the Office of Health Standards Compliance (OHSC), the Office of the Health Ombuds, the South African Medical Research Council (SAMRC), and the National Health Laboratory Services (NHLS).
The OHSC report highlighted the organisation's commitment to ensuring safe healthcare services in South Africa. Despite challenges, including natural disasters and staffing issues, OHSC received an unqualified audit opinion for the seventh consecutive year. Committee members raised concerns about complaints, delays in investigations, and budget constraints. OHSC responded, outlining proactive measures and collaborations with the National Health Department.
The Office of the Health Ombudsman reported resolving over 96% of the 11 000 complaints received within five days. Challenges included staff shortages and complex cases. Committee members discussed solutions like the Health Ombudsman Bill and emphasised the need for prompt complaint resolution. The Committee supported the Bill and stressed the importance of the Ombudsman's independence.
The SAMRC demonstrated financial prudence despite budget cuts. The organisation achieved a clean audit and focused on vital areas like health promotion and disease prevention. Committee members lauded SAMRC's work but emphasised the necessity for faster transformation and inclusive representation, especially in senior management.
The NHLS achieved 70% of its targets and maintained sound financial management, receiving an unqualified audit opinion for the fifth year. Challenges included financial control and staff attrition. Committee Members questioned the impact of load shedding and financial sustainability. NHLS responded with strategies to address irregular expenditures, improve infrastructure, and enhance staff retention efforts.
Meeting report
Office of Health Standard Compliance (OHSC) Annual Performance Report 2022/23
Dr Simphiwe Mndaweni, CEO, OHSC, led her team in presenting the annual report to the Portfolio Committee. The Presentation Highlighted the following:
Overview of the Office of Health Standard Compliance (OHSC)
The OHSC operates as an independent regulator, focusing on ensuring safe and quality healthcare services. The entity is guided by values such as respect, accountability, transparency, and integrity. Established in 2013, the OHSC's mandate includes monitoring compliance with health standards, addressing complaints, and promoting health and safety.
Strategic Plan and Objectives:
The OHSC's strategic plan for 2020-2025 comprises five outcomes aligned with the medium-term strategic framework goals. These outcomes cover functional standards, compliance monitoring, facilitating compliance, certification, and overall improvement in healthcare quality.
Programme Performance:
Programme 1: Administration:
- Achieved 64% of the target for filling vacancies within four months.
- Maintained a low vacancy rate of 4.6%.
- Conducted extensive community engagements, surpassing the target by conducting 34 sessions.
- Successfully engaged with the private sector, conducting 17 sessions against the target of eight.
Programme 2: Compliance Inspectorate:
- Conducted 781 out of 788 planned inspections due to unavoidable circumstances.
- Initiated inspections in the private sector for the first time, conducting 51 out of 52 planned inspections.
- Re-inspected 106 health establishments, addressing compliance issues.
- Challenges included natural disasters and security issues affecting inspections.
Programme 3: Complaints Management and Office of the Health Ombud:
- Resolved 93% of low-risk complaints within 25 working days.
- Struggled with investigations, completing only one case within six months and five within 12 months.
- Received 2,647 complaints, showing varied submission rates from different provinces.
Programme 4: Health Standards Design, Analysis, and Support:
- Produced three reports analysing compliance status, trends, and weaknesses in the Limpopo province.
- Conducted workshops to guide public and private sectors on implementing norms and standards, exceeding the target of 24 sessions.
Programme 5: Certification and Enforcement:
- Issued 251 compliance certificates within 15 days, encouraging compliance among health establishments.
- Initiated enforcement actions, issuing 100 written warnings to non-compliant health establishments.
Financial Performance:
The OHSC had a budget of R192 million. Performance stood at 65%, with 26% non-achievement and 9% partial achievement. The office maintained a staff complement of 123 out of 129 funded posts, resulting in a 4.6% vacancy rate.
Audit Outcome:
The OHSC received an unqualified audit opinion from the Auditor General (AG) for the seventh consecutive year, demonstrating financial accountability and transparency.
Challenges and Mitigations:
Challenges included natural disasters, security issues during inspections, delays in information submission by health establishments, and staff shortages in investigations. Mitigations included internal process revisions, capacity building, and appointing additional investigators.

Responses
Dr Ernest Kenoshi, Chairperson, OHSC Board, addressed several concerns raised by the Members, particularly those related to the high level of activity in Gauteng. He explained that this was due to the OHSC being situated in Gauteng, which allowed members of the public to walk into the office to lay complaints or ask questions. He also noted that Gauteng has the highest concentration of private hospitals, which is why most inspections of private healthcare facilities occurred there. He responded to a question about adverse events in obstetrics and the issue of hypoxia, stating that the OHSC’s inspection tools for all hospitals include a section specifically dealing with maternity and labour wards. These tools ensure that there is adequate equipment, staffing, and facilities to provide a safe space for mothers and babies.
Mr Kenoshi addressed the question of revenue management and the need for the OHSC to increase its resources beyond what it receives as an allocation. The OHSC, like other Government Departments, should start charging fees for its services. He noted that even the Industrial and Commercial Workers Union (ICU) charges government Departments for conducting investigations. He stated that the OHSC needs to charge for its services, but this requires approval from the DoH and Treasury. Processes have started to obtain the necessary approvals and determine the actual fees. It was confirmed that Mr Rajesh Mahabeer, OHSC Board Member, who was present at the meeting, is leading a committee dealing with this issue in close collaboration with the National Department of Health.

Mr Julius Mapatha, Chief Financial Officer, OHSC, addressed the question about the cost implications of labor unrest. He explained that the impact on the OHSC usually occurs when its teams are already on the ground in a particular area or province and labour unrest happens in that place, hampering their ability to continue with an investigation or inspection. The teams are then forced to return back to the office. He noted that they have not yet quantified the implications of labour unrest happening in a larger geographical area.
Mr Mapatha responded to a question about overspending on programme one. He explained that the template the OHSC uses from National Treasury limits it to comparing the actual and the budget. However, he indicated that the overspending has been funded by prior year surpluses approved by National Treasury.
Mr Mapatha, on revenue generation, stated that several activities are identified to generate revenue. The act allows it to charge fees for services rendered, such as conducting inspections, and to impose a fine for persistent non-compliance. He mentioned that they are still in discussions with the NDOH on these aspects. He addressed a question about charging fees for services rendered regarding inspections. He mentioned that the entity received a legal opinion from the state law advisors indicating that it cannot differentiate when charging a fee between a public and a private health establishment due to the current setup of legislation.
Mr Mapatha addressed a question about the adequacy of its budget, mentioning that when OHSC presented its Annual Performance Plan (APP) earlier this year, it estimated there were just over 4 000 health establishments, both private and public. However, the actual number of health establishments in the country far exceeds what it normally indicates in the APP. In fact, there could be more than 50 000.
Mr Mapatha said that the NDOH and a team from OHSC are still working on determining the actual number of facility health establishments in the country as defined in the National Health Act. For now, they are working on a conservative estimate of about 15,000.
Based on inspecting these 15,000 facilities, which include GPs, they estimate that they will need about 800 million with a total staff complement exceeding 700. They also looked at the Care Quality Commission (CQC), which was the model against which the OHSC was benchmarked. The CQC has more than 3 000 staff members and inspects in excess of 17 000 facilities in a year. He mentioned that the CEO alluded to having 323 staff members. This was based on inspecting a number of 4 000 facilities. However, the actual reality on the ground far exceeds the 4,000 or even the estimated 15 000 facilities.
Dr Donna Jacobs, Executive Manager: Complaints Management, OHSC, began by addressing some issues within the Complaints Management Programme. She noted that complaints within Gauteng province are evenly spread, with tertiary hospitals like Rahima Moosa, Tembisa and Charlotte Maxeke receiving more complaints due to their higher risk ratings. However, she emphasised that 98% of all complaints received yearly are low-risk-rated. She discussed the staffing needs of the Complaints Management Programme, which currently consists of 31 staff members, 21 of whom are permanent and ten on temporary or contract basis. She mentioned that these temporary contracts will soon lapse, leading to staff shortages. They have been advocating for an additional 20 staff members and for the conversion of the ten temporary posts to permanent ones.
Dr Jacobs addressed the issue of unmet targets within the Complaints Management Programme, particularly those related to the investigation of high and extreme risk complaints. The reason for this shortfall is the limited number of permanent investigators. However, she expressed optimism about future progress due to the board’s intervention in providing additional contract investigators. She also spoke about the long outstanding cases, or backlog cases, pending from 2017/2018. The reason for these outstanding cases is largely due to staff capacity and the need for more investigators. She mentioned that the OHSC has developed a backlog reduction pathway to address each of these cases, which are being closely monitored by the Health Ombud and the chairperson of the CSE committee of the OHSC board.
Mr Rapule Phiri, Deputy Director, OHSC, began by acknowledging the difficulty of mitigating unrest during inspections. He mentioned that they have implemented a strategy of early notification of the Heads of Department (HODs) of the provinces. This means that all HODs are aware of when and where they will be conducting inspections within each quarter of the financial year. This communication allows them to be notified of potential incidents or unrest in specific areas.
Mr Phiri noted that this strategy has proven effective, particularly in Gauteng, where they were alerted about the state on the ground and were able to avoid travelling there. As part of their preparation for inspections, HODs also search the internet for potential hotspots across the country, particularly in areas where they will be conducting inspections. If they find hotspots, they make contact with local police to confirm if there is potential for unrest. He mentioned that they were advised to liaise with security clusters to see if they could provide information as and when they received intelligence. This is an avenue they are exploring as an institution to see if it could assist them in averting what they have experienced in the previous financial year.
Ms Winnifred Moleko, Executive Manager: Health Standards Design, Analysis and Suppor, OHSC, began by addressing the question related to the early warning system and the issue of integration. She explained that their office is expected to monitor risk indicators related to serious norms and standards breaches. It anticipates health establishments to self-report incidents that have occurred. Although the response has not been as expected, she noted that once the entity interacts with the health establishments that have responded, it is able to work with them to prevent any recurrence of such incidents. It also receives information from media alerts and responds to those alerts. While its process is proactive, such as inspections, it also has reactive mechanisms. During these inspections, OHSC ensures that it identifies things that could put patients at risk, such as missing equipment or lack of oxygen cylinders.
Ms Moleko indicated that the OHSC is in discussion with the NDOH to develop other mechanisms to ensure its early warning system is reactive. Regarding the integration of information, she mentioned that it interacts with the complaints unit within the early warning system to triangulate its information. It does this through its early warning system forum, where it checks any indicators of risk reported by health establishments, media alerts, and also the complaints received by their office, and acts on them through investigation or inspections.

It proposes the creation of a freestanding public entity with all necessary facilities, including human resources, investigations, and legal support. While it will require a budget from Treasury, it cannot guarantee financial solutions.
Prof Mokoena mentioned, as was highlighted by OHSC, alternative forms of funding were discussed. This could involve deriving additional revenue streams from investigations conducted by the Office of the Health Ombud. However, he noted that it has not seriously considered this due to potential legal and ethical constraints, especially considering that most complaints come from individuals with limited resources. He stated that while the entity does not require a large library, it does need a library of key resources and references. Prof Mokoena addressed the question about the complexity of cases, noting that it’s not easy to answer. Dr Karmani Chetty, CEO, NHLS led her team in presenting the Annual Report to the Portfolio Committee on Health. The presentation Highlighted the following:
Overall Performance:
The NHLS reported an overall achievement rate of 70% of the set targets for the year. Despite challenges, it managed to maintain an unqualified audit opinion for the fifth consecutive year, indicating sound financial management.
Financial Performance:
The NHLS showcased a robust financial performance and it achieved an accounting surplus of R3.2 billion, a significant increase from the previous year's R77 million, largely due to the recalculation of the provision for doubtful debt. Revenue declined slightly to R12.7 billion, mainly attributed to the drop in COVID-19-related revenue. However, the NHLS successfully managed expenses, leading to a net cash inflow of R1.6 billion.
Operational Highlights:
The NHLS administered over 110 million tests, showing a 3.6% increase in test volumes. It improved its IT infrastructure, enhancing connectivity and bandwidth across 277 sites. Additionally, it emphasised its contribution to public health, particularly in areas such as communicable diseases, cancer research, and occupational health.
Programme Performance:
The NHLS structured its work into different programmes. In Programme One (Laboratory Services), it achieved 50% of the set targets, facing challenges like extended load shedding and international shortages of essential test kits. Programme Two (Academic Affairs, Research, and Quality Assurance) attained 73% of its goals. Programme Three (National Institute for Communicable Diseases) and Programme Four (National Institute for Occupational Health) both achieved 100% of its targets, highlighting its efficiency in these critical areas.
Challenges and Mitigations:
Challenges, such as extended load shedding and shortages of vital test kits, impacted the NHLS's performance. Despite these challenges, it outlined mitigation strategies, such as renting additional laboratory space, implementing point-of-care solutions, and investing in new equipment.
Financial Sustainability:
The NHLS demonstrated financial prudence, maintaining a cash reserve of R5.1 billion. It managed to improve its cash flow ratio to six months, ensuring financial stability for the organisation.

Discussion
The Chairperson expressed his gratitude and welcomed the comprehensive presentation from the NHLS. He noted that after all these years, it is still not getting an unqualified audit with findings, which he found a bit worrisome. He mentioned that the Committee had strongly raised the issue of application for condonement and he himself had raised this fact last year. He expressed hope that the NHLS would apply soon, emphasised the importance of the country's developmental agenda, and acknowledged NHLS's critical role in it. He mentioned the importance of improving the hours of the NHLS to enhance the health ecosystem and health outcomes in the country. He noted that just the other day, one of their specialist physicians developed COVID, highlighting the NHLS's critical role in supporting public health through surveillance and outbreak response activities. He also noted the NHLS's positive contribution towards updating the influenza vaccination guidelines and conducting a range of COVID-19-related research activities, cholera investigation, HIV research, etc. He commended it for its procurement and contract management compliance improvement, as there were no material findings in that regard.
Ms Clarke asked about the financial implications of the restatement of the financial statements and the findings of the audit, which she noted painted a concerning picture of financial control. Despite this, she acknowledged that the financial position is strong, expressing her admiration for its ability to pay creditors and debtors within 36 days. She then asked about plans to address backlogs at the forensic chemistry lab, including what these plans are and what infrastructure solutions are being added to them. She also wanted to know about the number of specimens that reach their expiry date and have to be disposed of due to this issue. She pointed out that the backlog in blood tests is 34% as opposed to the expected 60%, and toxicology is at 7% versus the expected 20%. She noted that this must have a significant impact on forensic testing.
Regarding the TB recovery plan, she asked how the number of tests compared to pre-COVID times. She also inquired about the cost implications of load shedding in terms of purchasing Uninterruptible Power Supplies (UPS) systems and solar power, as well as the time lost during power cuts. She asked about the current situation regarding shortages of Gini expert test kits and CD4 test kits. Finally, she requested an expansion on the irregular, fruitless, and wasteful expenditure mentioned in the annual report.
Ms Hicklin expressed her surprise at hearing that someone was pleased with how provincial departments pay their bills. She pointed out an outstanding bill of R5.89 billion from provincial departments of health, 70% of which (R4.17 billion) is over 90 days overdue. She questioned how any business could withstand such a financial constraint. She acknowledged that the NHLS is in a healthy financial position with cash reserves for six months, but found the size of the bill extreme. She also noted that the National Institute for Communicable Disease (NICD), the National Institute for Occupational Health (NIOH), the forensic chemistry labs, and the National Cancer Registry could be considered unfunded mandates as they don’t receive any funding from  National Treasury. She expressed concern about the detrimental effect this could have on the decreasing conditional grant from National Treasury and asked about plans to mitigate this. She touched on the issue of load shedding, stating that it must severely impact the entity's ability to perform its functions. She emphasised the implications of having to go out on tender for solar panels and generators and having to restart tests and dispose of samples due to load shedding.
Ms Hicklin raised concerns about the attrition of staff, including pathologists and Heads of Department, to the private sector and asked about plans to address this issue. She also asked about the limitations POPIA is having on its ability to do surveillance of public health in an emergency. She asked how it plans to mitigate the stringent deficit resulting from a decreased conditional grant.
Mr Xaba expressed his gratitude and welcomed the presentation. He then posed a question about the effectiveness of the proposed corrective measures aimed at improving the percentage of TB genealogy tests performed within 40 hours and other energy targets affected by the energy crisis. He emphasised the importance of understanding the effectiveness of these measures.
Ms Gela asked about the recurring findings from the AG in the areas of quality financial management and prevention of irregular, unauthorised, and fruitless and wasteful expenditure. She asked about the constraints affecting management and the board in resolving these outcomes and how the entity plans to address them. She asked how the board’s audit and risk division would be capacitated to improve audit outcomes. Lastly, she asked about the measures field management has adopted to ease the risk mitigation action plan to improve performance and compliance.
Ms Hlengwa asked about the forensic chemistry laboratories’ struggle to keep up with the growing backlog of toxicology cases. She asked how their agreement to allocate additional resources to speed up work has affected the budgetary allocation. She also brought up the issue of a body found in 2015 in Phoenix, Devon, that has yet to be claimed.  What should be done about this long-overdue case? She expressed concern for the people working in Phoenix and raised another issue related to DNA. She pointed out that there is a crisis in cases of accidents or similar incidents due to the inability to obtain DNA.
Ms Moroane asked about the plans in place to improve the NHLS footprint in the Northern Cape and Free State. What is the target to develop and implement a Point of Care Testing (POCT) plan, which was not met in the financial year? She asked about the feasibility and cost benefit assessment strategies being implemented in 2023 and 2024 in preparation for the rollout of the POCT.
The Chairperson asked about those mentioned as breaching the five pillars of procurement, which include being equitable, fair, cost-effective, transparent, and competitive. He asked how this would be addressed in the current financial year.
The Chairperson encouraged the board of management to reflect on the root cause of recurring challenges and provide sustainable mitigation strategies. He urged them to ensure compliance with rules and regulations, which is why this point keeps coming up. He recommended that measures be taken to prevent irregular expenditure and that management should improve its preparations and review of annual financial statements. He noted that these were forwarded and then corrected, resulting in an unqualified order. He suggested that this should be done upfront and also emphasised the need to strengthen consequence management.
Responses
Prof Eric Buch, Chairperson, NHLS Board, responding on outstanding debt from provincial payments, acknowledged that this issue had been discussed several times before and apologised for not explaining it in greater detail due to its historical context with the Committee. He explained that there is a historic debt with various reasons for its existence. However, he emphasised that the important thing is that in the financial year, the amount of services provided to the provinces is not too far off from the amount the entity has received in payments. He clarified that this includes payments for both old and new debt, covering its current expenditure. He expressed concern about potential headwinds due to increased financial pressures in the provinces. He then addressed the question about the forensic chemistry laboratories and their costs, and a similar question about the funding of the NICD and the NIOH. He stated that his board believes it is responsible for delivering and has managed the NHLS well. He praised the executive team for their excellent job under the CEO in running the organisation.
Prof Buch pointed out that the NHLS tariff increases are very low, under 5%, and it has managed to continually enhance its efficiency and build up some reserves. He stated that it could either sit on these reserves or ensure that national assets like the NIOH and the NICD are maintained and fully functional. He emphasised its commitment as a Board to ensure that its entities and teaching responsibilities are properly met and the entity delivers for the nation. This commitment also applies to the forensic chemistry laboratories.
On whether SAPS or the Department of Justice (DoJ) should be paying for the services they provide, or whether it should work with the conditional grant Prof Buch presented two options: it could spend what it gets on the conditional grant on the forensic chemistry laboratories and potentially present a worse situation next year, or it could make an investment to solve the problem. He stated that forensic chemistry laboratories are now part of the national responsibilities of service provision of the NHLS. The NHLS plans to invest in staff, facilities, power, equipment, and skills, including proper storage facilities and refrigeration. He expressed its desire to fix the problem in the interest of the country and continue to invest beyond the conditional grant. At the same time, it will lobby for increases in the conditional grant. He emphasised that he wouldn’t be responsible for his Board to let things decay and crumble if the entity does not get more funds for the conditional grant.
Like everyone else in the country, Prof Buch acknowledged that the NHLS is impacted by load shedding. He admitted that a number of its turnaround times missed targets due to load shedding, but it has taken measures to deal with this. Their perspective as a Board, is it is governed by the Act and has responsibilities in it that it wants to develop. It has taken a wide number of measures around issues of irregular expenditure and order of financial statements.
Dr Michael Sachs, Board Member, NHLS, emphasised the fiscal squeeze faced by the public service in which the NHLS operates. He pointed out the danger of government having ambitious plans not backed by financial resources, as this imposes pressures on institutions like the NHLS. He said if the government wants these institutions to function well and deliver NHI, it must fund them. It is dangerous to place such pressure on an institution like the NHLS without being willing to fund it, as these are responsibilities of government.
Mr Koena Nkoko, Board Member, NHLS, said that the Audit and Risk Committee takes note of the issues raised by this Committee. He emphasised that all issues raised in the Auditor-General’s report are on its radar and will ensure that there is an added effort and speed in addressing those issues. He expressed satisfaction with the internal audit function and risk function so far. He expressed hope to continue ensuring that this unit remains independent of management and that management responds to the issues raised through this governance structure.
Dr Chetty expressed her gratitude to the Chairperson and acknowledged that one of the key issues raised by several members was the matter of irregular expenditure. She explained that in 2016, the irregular expenditure was about R990 million. In 2017 and 2018, the NHLS discovered that previously procured tenders only paid for equipment and didn’t account for reagents. This led to the buildup of what it calls catalogue items, such as reagents that go with equipment and stationery. Declaring all catalogue items increased the irregular expenditure, along with a problem in 2017 and 2018 with several contracts where there was an overspend. This was part of the disciplinary process for the previous CEO, CFO, and supply chain staff. She stated that the entity has been trying to regularise expired contracts over the years, resulting in a steady reduction in irregular expenditure. She noted that in the last financial year, there were a few expired contracts, most of which have been regularised. She said that it reduced catalogue items quite significantly and expected to eliminate them completely over the next year or two. She added that for the last financial year, the entity included external testing as part of the irregular expenditure and is in the process of regularising it. She stated that it has had amounts condoned by Treasury amounting to R6.6 billion over these years. The amount not condoned is an outstanding amount of R1.8 billion. Treasury has recently condoned an amount of R700 million, and the Board has derecognised an amount of R179 million. The NHLS is in the process of submitting for condonation of an amount of R931 million from the current year.
On expired contracts, Dr Chetty mentioned that the NHLS had gone out of tender on courier services, which have been regularised. Laboratory services are currently being advertised, and document storage and retrieval are also in the process of going out on tender. She stated that messenger services have been terminated and final payments to Afrinate have been concluded. She expressed confidence in the plan to sort out irregular expenditure, noting that it is a much larger organisation than most other public entities. She then discussed the provincial debt, stating that most of the historic debt was from KZN prior to 2016. She explained that the tactic has been to get most provinces to clear up historic debt over the years. She noted that Free State, Limpopo, North West, and Western Cape did not have any debt right up to the financial year of 2021/2022. She admitted that there are problems with the Eastern and Northern Cape, and that Gauteng has also been attempting to pay its historic debt. However, she expressed concern about the increase in debt in the last financial year, indicating that provinces are struggling.
Ms Pumeza Mayekiso, Chief Financial Officer, NHLS, addressed the question on the financial implications of the restatements made in relation to the 2021/2022 financial year in the annual report. She explained that the corrections were twofold: a correction of errors and reclassifications. The adjustments mainly came through as a result of corrections identified together with auditors regarding tax related to its subsidiary, SABP. Concerning the reclassification, an amount was processed to reclassify an item from provisions, which is recorded under its liabilities of R334.8 million, and moved that particular item under employment benefit. Regarding the correction of an error made in relation to SABP, the amount amounted to just under four million rand. This was a correction concerning tax.
Prof Koleka Mlisana, Executive Manager: Academic Affairs, Research & Quality Assurance, NHLS, addressed the question and concern raised about pathologists and their attrition. She acknowledged that a significant number of pathologists are attracted to the private sector, presenting a challenge and discussed the strategies in three areas. Firstly, the NHLS has addressed the difficulty in attracting HODs. As of 1 October, it has appointed about seven HODs, some attracted from the private sector, by making these positions permanent instead of fixed-term contracts. Secondly, it created HOD developmental posts. These posts provide support for individuals who may not yet meet the criteria for HODs, allowing them to take on these positions. Currently, there are about eight vacant HOD positions across the country. Lastly, given that a significant percentage of recently qualified pathologists tend to migrate to the private sector due to higher salaries, the NHLS is exploring a two-year retention strategy for every registrar trained by it. This would ensure that people serve the NHLS for a minimum of two years post-qualification. It is also looking at increasing the number of registrars it trains with the hope of saturating the private sector and retaining a reasonable number of pathologists. It continues to explore flexible HR policies to retain experienced pathologists, including those retiring, with a policy looking at post-retirement pathologists. A significant number continues to leave the country and it will continue finding various ways and methods of retaining pathologists.
Dr Clothilde Oliphant, Chief Operations Officer: Strategic Initiatives, NHLS, reiterated the strategies it intends to implement. The aim of its strategy is to increase its service offering and the processing capacity of the laboratories. In terms of infrastructure, it secured additional space to accommodate an expansion in the blood alcohol section and to establish a new toxicology section. This new toxicology section will process all new toxicology requests at Victoria. It is also planning similar expansions for the Durban HCL. She acknowledged a challenge with cold room infrastructure at two of its  HCLs and mentioned that they are procuring additional mobile cold rooms to align their storage facilities with requirements.
Regarding equipment and other instruments, she noted that significant investments had been made to replace ageing and obsolete equipment that caused delays in the past. It is also procuring additional instruments to expand the service. Regarding human resources, the NHLS ensures that catering staff can live at each of the NCLs (unconfirmed), fill all vacant positions, and have instituted shift systems and overtime work. She clarified that DNA testing in cases of unnatural deaths falls under the responsibilities of the Forensic Sciences Division of SAPS and issues related to mortuaries and unclaimed bodies are managed by the South African Forensic Pathology Services of the Department of Health.
Dr Chetty addressed the issue of expiry dates for specimens, saying that the NHLS has been meeting with various role players, including forensic pathologists and SAPS, and has had initial meetings with the National Prosecuting Authority. They are developing a plan to deal with old specimens to determine their viability. In cases with no identifiers, the NHLS will discuss with the forensic pathologist what to do with those specimens. She noted that the forensic pathology and forensic chemistry services didn’t previously have any support staff, however they have since employed support staff, and security staff, and filled all posts with analysts. They are also looking at additional posts for both new and existing labs.
Regarding the TB recovery plan, she provided an example of how the number of tests has been increasing. During 2020, the entity conducted about 2 million tests at the height of COVID. In 2023, with the TB recovery plan, it expects to conduct about 2.5 million tests.
Dr Chetty mentioned a problem with test kits which has now been resolved. The Portfolio Committee was informed that the NHLS has awarded a new TB molecular tender that’s in the process of being implemented. This will be shared among three major companies because the WHO has accredited some other companies to be part of TB screening. Regarding the implications of solar, she mentioned that the average price from its business case would be about R65 million. This is a significant investment just for the head office and something it has to consider for all its laboratories. She addressed an issue around load shedding and sample finishing. To mitigate this risk, the entity has increased generator power and is using some biosafety fridges in some of its other labs for storage. She also spoke about issues around patient information in emergencies and adherence to POPIA while balancing it with the need for research. She also said there has been an increase in capacity in the Northern Cape which now offers services that were not available before. Additionally, the NHLS has capacitated Greenpoint to perform anatomical pathology samples for the Northern Cape. It covers all 228 facilities in the Northern Cape and expressed satisfaction with its capacity there.
Dr Chetty addressed the issue around the review of financial statements, acknowledging the point made by the Auditor General about significant changes from the unordered financial statements to the audited financial statements. She explained that this was due to a change in grant which required the entity to restate its doubtful debt. This restatement made a significant difference in the changes. She mentioned issues around plants and equipment and with grant funding but expressed confidence in its unordered financial statements. To avoid material findings, the aim is to be as close as possible to its audited financial statements. She mentioned that the NHLS has raised donor funding for point-of-care testing and has already started implementing it. Its middleware interfaces between point of care and its lab information system. This is crucial because when the provinces do testing, those results do not go into an information system. However, with the point-of-care system, results go into the lab information system. She stated that they were rolling out blood gas, HVA1C, and troponin as part of a donor-funded project. It has also invested some of its own funding to utilise point of care more effectively.
The meeting was adjourned.
