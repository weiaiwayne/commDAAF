Strategic Plan
The Portfolio Committee on Environment, Forestry and Fisheries met to receive briefings from the Department of Forestry, Fisheries and the Environment (DFFE), the Marine Living Resources Fund (MLRF), iSimangaliso Wetland Park Authority, and the South African National Biodiversity Institute (SANBI) on their 2025–2030 Strategic Plans and 2025/26 Annual Performance Plans.
DFFE outlined six strategic priorities, including implementing the Climate Change Act, enhancing iconic sites, developing sustainable wildlife industries, and expanding community participation in the ocean economy. It acknowledged challenges such as budget shortfalls, slow infrastructure investment, and regulatory bottlenecks, but reported increased allocations and improved programme implementation.
Members questioned the removal of research surveys from the APP, delays in support for small-scale fishers, reduced bursary opportunities, and lack of formal agreements with security forces. The Department said budget constraints had necessitated programme shifts and assured coordination with disaster management structures and security agencies.
MLRF highlighted priorities around regulatory compliance, sustainable fisheries, and socio-economic development. Members raised concerns over harbour maintenance and permit backlogs. The entity confirmed plans to fast-track permits via an online system and reported job creation through the Expanded Public Works Programme.
iSimangaliso prioritised wetland rehabilitation, green job creation, and infrastructure upgrades. It reported revenue growth and stronger partnerships but noted high community expectations and funding pressures. It confirmed efforts to streamline processes and involve communities in conservation.
SANBI focused on biodiversity conservation, research, policy support, and training. It reported increased visitors and training outputs but cited financial constraints and delays in infrastructure improvements.
Members asked about job sustainability, revenue targets, and monitoring frameworks. SANBI noted its efforts to improve collections and diversify income.
The Committee noted the plans and budgets, committed to sustained oversight, and requested that outstanding responses be submitted in writing.
The Chairperson highlighted that the Department and its entities were expected to contribute to three national priorities: promoting inclusive growth and job creation; reducing poverty and the cost of living; and building a capable, ethical, and developmental state.
Meeting report
Opening Remarks by the Chairperson
The Chairperson reminded the Committee that, in the previous year, the government had released a 30-year review of South Africa’s democracy since 1994. The review reflected on the progress made, the challenges that persisted, and potential strategies to address them. It noted that the country had made important strides towards building a non-racial, non-sexist, and united society and improving the quality of life for all citizens.
Ongoing Socio-Economic challenges
Despite these gains, the Chairperson noted that economic growth was insufficient to generate jobs for the entire working-age population. As a result, large segments of society remained excluded from economic opportunities, and disparities in access to education, healthcare, and basic services continued. She emphasised that the forestry sector required rethinking and that strengthening climate resilience across municipalities was critical.
Tourism and environmental rights
The Chairperson highlighted that South Africa possessed comparative advantages in tourism, especially through its natural heritage and well-managed parks. However, she cautioned that further effort was needed to achieve the transformative change envisioned at the dawn of democracy. She reminded Members that Section 24 of the Constitution guarantees the right to an environment that is not harmful to health or well-being and mandates environmental protection for present and future generations
.
Strategic alignment and oversight role
She explained that the 30-year review, the midterm review, and related planning documents had shaped the strategic focus for the seventh administration, as set out in the
2024–2029 Medium Term Development Plan
. The strategic and annual performance plans of the Department of Forestry, Fisheries and the Environment (DFFE) and its entities were expected to align with this broader framework.
Commitment to national priorities
The Chairperson said the Department and its entities were expected to contribute to three national priorities: promoting inclusive growth and job creation; reducing poverty and the cost of living; and building a capable, ethical, and developmental state. To this end, the Department had identified six focus areas and eight targeted outcomes centred on environmental protection and sustainability.
Commemoration of Earth Day
In conclusion, the Chairperson noted that the meeting coincided with World Earth Day, emphasising the importance of collective responsibility to protect the environment and promote sustainability. She called for renewed commitment to advancing environmental rights for the benefit of current and future generations.
Opening remarks by Minister
Significance of Earth Day
Dr Dion George, Minister, Department of Forestry, Fisheries and the Environment (DFFE), opened the meeting by noting that the engagement coincided with Earth Day commemorated globally on 22 April to raise awareness and promote environmental protection. He reminded the Committee that Earth Day had been observed annually since 1970. He stated that the 2025 theme,
“
Our Power, Our Planet”, called for global support for renewable energy, which was particularly fitting for South Africa as the country continued its
transition toward sustainable energy sources.
Alignment with Government Priorities
Dr George emphasised the critical role played by the Department of Forestry, Fisheries and the Environment (DFFE) in improving the lives of all South Africans, particularly within the context of the country’s mega-diverse natural heritage. He indicated that the recently established Government of National Unity (GNU) had identified three strategic objectives for the next five years, namely promoting inclusive economic growth and job creation; reducing poverty and lowering the cost of living; and building a capable, ethical, and developmental state.
He reported that the Department’s Strategic Plan (2025–2030) and Annual Performance Plans (2025/26) were aligned with these priorities through six strategic focus areas that cut across its broad environmental and developmental mandate. He thanked the Committee for the opportunity to present these documents, along with the corresponding plans for the Department’s six public entities, namely the Marine Living Resources Fund (MLRF), iSimangaliso Wetland Park Authority, South African National Biodiversity Institute (SANBI), South African National Parks (SANParks), South African Weather Service (SAWS), and South African Forestry Company Limited (SAFCOL).
Vision and Planned Outcomes
Dr George outlined the Department’s five-year vision as one that prioritised a protected and sustainable environment to benefit both present and future generations. He stated that this vision would be driven by eight intended outcomes, which included improved governance and institutional performance; the development of a skilled, ethical, and diverse workforce; stronger compliance with environmental legislation; the conservation and sustainable use of ecosystems; progress towards a low-carbon and climate-resilient society; a reduction in environmental threats to human health; enhanced job creation and equitable access to opportunities; and a cleaner, healthier environment.
Strategic Priorities: The Big Six
Dr George presented the Department’s “Big Six” strategic priorities, guiding implementation over the 2025–2030 planning period.
These are:
Climate Change – centred on implementing the newly enacted Climate Change Act, with initiatives including planting one million trees and enhanced polar research efforts.
Kruger, Kirstenbosch, iSimangaliso Icon Status Strategy (KISS) – aimed at elevating South Africa’s flagship conservation sites into globally recognised eco-tourism destinations.
Fair Industry for Lions, Leopards, and Rhinos (FILLER) – focused on conserving iconic species and addressing illegal poaching and the captive predator industry.
Fishing for Freedom – designed to integrate coastal communities into the ocean's economy and promote sustainable marine resource use.
Regulatory Efficiency Strategy for Environmental Turbocharge (RESET): This strategy is intended to streamline and modernise environmental regulatory processes for greater transparency and service delivery.
Money – a commitment to sound financial governance, leveraging partnerships and external funding to support high-impact environmental programmes.
Dr George explained that these priorities reflect the Department’s commitment to sustainability, economic inclusion, and institutional effectiveness across its broad mandate.
Marine Living Resources Fund (MLRF)
Dr George further explained that MLRF plays a pivotal role in supporting the sustainable management of fisheries and aquaculture. Over the next five years, the MLRF will prioritise well-managed fisheries and aquaculture, aim for sustained economic growth, and strive to improve livelihoods for coastal communities. These objectives reflect the commitment to balancing environmental preservation with socio-economic development, ensuring that South Africa's marine resources are utilised sustainably while benefitting those who depend on them.
iSimangaliso Wetland Park Authority
As South Africa’s first World Heritage Site, iSimangaliso Wetland Park continues to focus on preserving its rich biodiversity and heritage. The park’s new strategic plan strongly emphasises conservation, sustainable tourism, and empowering local communities. Key goals include enhancing community engagement, forming partnerships with private businesses to bolster tourism, and exploring innovative revenue streams, such as the sale of green energy, to ensure financial sustainability and support the park’s long-term conservation objectives.
South African National Biodiversity Institute (SANBI)
The SANBI is a leading organisation in the field of research, conservation, and policy advice on biodiversity. It is responsible for managing 11 national botanical gardens and two zoological campuses nationwide. Over the next five years, SANBI will focus on improving garden management, producing comprehensive biodiversity reports, enhancing human capacity in the biodiversity sector, and reinforcing financial sustainability and governance structures to ensure the longevity of its operations. These efforts are critical in advancing South Africa's biodiversity conservation efforts and supporting the country's commitments to global environmental goals.
South African National Parks (SANParks)
Guided by Vision 2040, South African National Parks (SANParks) is shifting away from traditional "fortress conservation" and embracing a more inclusive, people-centred approach to nature conservation. This new strategy, shaped by input from over 1,600 stakeholders, seeks to integrate indigenous knowledge and community involvement in managing national parks. The plan aims to ensure that these parks are well-preserved and contribute to sustainable community development and tourism. Over the next five years, SANParks will continue to focus on making national parks more accessible, inclusive, and sustainable, while preserving South Africa’s natural heritage for future generations.
South African Weather Service (SAWS)
The South African Weather Service (SAWS) is critical in safeguarding lives and property by providing early warnings and forecasts about extreme weather events. While it cannot control the weather, SAWS offers impact-based forecasts that help communities take the necessary precautions. The organisation is also at the forefront of South Africa’s participation in the United Nations' "Early Warnings for All" initiative, which aims to protect everyone globally from hazardous weather events by 2027. This initiative is part of a broader effort to enhance disaster risk management and climate resilience in South Africa.
South African Forestry Company Limited (SAFCOL)
The South African Forestry Company Limited (SAFCOL) operates as a commercial enterprise and a socio-economic development entity in South Africa and Mozambique. Its "Strategy 29" is designed to diversify products, boost profits, promote sustainable development, and foster community growth. By focusing on economic and social dimensions, SAFCOL aims to balance profitability with environmental responsibility and social impact, ensuring that its operations benefit the economy and local communities.
South Africa’s G20 Presidency
South Africa’s presidency of the G20 presents a unique opportunity to align the country’s international engagement with its national priorities, particularly in conservation, climate change, water management, waste reduction, and ocean health. The G20 presidency also offers a platform to advocate for global efforts to combat drought and desertification, address air quality issues, and promote sustainable development. As part of this, South Africa is preparing for two major G20 meetings in July at Kruger National Park and in October in Cape Town, intending to advance national and international environmental agendas. The Department remains committed to delivering a successful G20 year, a top priority for the Department.
DFFE Presentation
Ms Nomfundo Tshabalala, Director-General (DG), DFFE, welcomed the Chairperson’s opening remarks about the sector's challenges, noting that the Department had carefully considered these challenges during its strategic planning process.
Mandate of the Department
Ms Tshabalala outlined the Department’s mandate, which is rooted in the South African Constitution. The core objectives are to prevent pollution and ecological degradation, promote conservation, and ensure the sustainable development and use of natural resources.
Progress and Key Achievements
Significant progress has been made in recent months, including the Minister's approval of the Strategic Plan and Annual Performance Plan in March 2025, which were subsequently tabled in Parliament in April 2025.
Ms Tshabalala highlighted the successful implementation of several key initiatives, including the Climate Change Act, national greening programmes, and the Oceans Economy Programme.
The Department’s Big Six Priorities
The Department has identified six key priorities to guide its work over the next five years:
Climate Change: Implementing the Climate Change Act and expanding greening initiatives.
Iconic Sites: Transforming national treasures like Kruger National Park and Kirstenbosch into world-class destinations.
Wildlife Management: Ensuring sustainable management of South Africa’s iconic wildlife.
Ocean Economy: Promoting marine conservation and connecting coastal communities to the ocean economy.
Regulatory Streamlining: Modernising environmental regulations.
Fiscal Responsibility: Ensuring sound fiscal management while attracting donor funding.
Challenges Facing the Department
Despite progress, the Department faces significant challenges. Funding shortfalls remain a major concern, particularly within the Expanded Public Works Programme and the Waste Bureau. Further, regulatory compliance and enforcement remain ongoing, requiring enhanced monitoring and improved enforcement mechanisms.
Notable Successes
The Department has experienced notable successes, including increased budget allocation. For the 2025/26 financial year, the Department secured an additional R157.8 million, with a cumulative increase of R478.1 million over the Medium-Term Expenditure Framework. Achievements in the Oceans Economy Programme and national greening initiatives underscore the Department’s capacity to deliver tangible results despite financial constraints.
Key Targets and Goals for 2025/26
Ms Tshabalala provided an overview of the Department’s key targets for the upcoming financial year. These include maintaining unqualified audit opinions, ensuring timely Environmental Impact Assessment applications, rehabilitating degraded coastal ecosystems, supporting municipalities in aligning Climate Change Response Plans with national targets, expanding the conservation estate, enforcing chemical and waste management regulations, and increasing tree planting under sustainable forestry practices. Additionally, patrols in the Exclusive Economic Zone and strengthening small-scale fisheries cooperatives are key focus areas.
Budget Allocation for 2025/26
The Department’s total budget allocation for 2025/26 is R9.081 billion. This budget is distributed across various programmes, with the largest share dedicated to Environmental Programmes. The budget also covers Administration, Biodiversity and Conservation, Chemicals and Waste Management, Fisheries Management, Climate Change and Air Quality, Oceans and Coasts, Forestry Management, and Regulatory Compliance and Monitoring. Detailed allocations were provided in the presentation document shared with the Committee.
Recommendation
The Department recommended that the Portfolio Committee on Forestry, Fisheries and the Environment note the 2025/26 Strategic Plan, Annual Performance Plan, and the associated budget allocations.

MLRF Presentation
Mr Wickness Rooifontein, Chief Financial Officer (CFO), MLRF, briefed the Committee on the Fund’s 2025–2030 Strategic Plan and 2025/26 Annual Performance Plan. The presentation focused on key areas such as regulatory compliance, socio-economic opportunities, and sustainable fisheries management.
Progress and Achievements
Mr Rooifontein highlighted the development of the Strategic Plan and Annual Performance Plan (APP), which were designed to align with the Department of Forestry, Fisheries, and the Environment’s (DFFE) Big 6 Priorities. Regarding operational achievements, the Fund has successfully implemented joint operations with law enforcement agencies and ensured that all proclaimed fishing harbours are operational.
Key Priorities
The Marine Living Resources Fund’s strategic plan reflects the Big 6 Priorities outlined by the DFFE, focusing on:
Fishing for Freedom: Strengthening regulatory compliance and supporting small-scale fishing cooperatives.
Climate Change: Promoting sustainable aquaculture and enhancing socio-economic conditions for coastal communities.
RESET: Streamlining regulatory processes and improving governance.
Challenges Faced
The Fund has encountered some challenges, particularly resource constraints that hinder the effective management of proclaimed fishing harbours. Furthermore, delays in issuing permits within the required service delivery timeframes have also posed operational difficulties.
Successes and Impact
Despite these challenges, the Fund has made significant progress. All 12 proclaimed fishing harbours are fully operational, and 40 joint operations with law enforcement agencies have been successfully conducted. Furthermore, the Fund has contributed to job creation, generating 796 Full-Time Equivalents (FTES) and 1,591 work opportunities through the Expanded Public Works Programme (EPWP).
Targets and Goals for 2025/26
The strategic plan sets out several key targets and goals, including:
Enhancing regulatory compliance through joint operations and maintaining operational fishing harbours.
Increasing employment and socio-economic opportunities by supporting co-management structures and small-scale fishing cooperatives.
Promoting sustainable fisheries and aquaculture, including publishing yearbooks on aquaculture production and supporting the broader oceans economy value chain.
Recommendations for Improvement
The presentation included recommendations for optimizing operations and addressing existing challenges. This includes engaging with the Department of Public Works and Infrastructure (DPWI) to improve operations in proclaimed fishing harbours and filling vacant funded posts to streamline permitting. The development of an online permitting system is also suggested to reduce delays.
Budget and Expenditure Estimates for 2025/26
The total budget allocation for the Marine Living Resources Fund for the 2025/26 financial year is R657.6 million, which is distributed across several key subprograms. These include:
Fisheries Management: R47.4 million
Aquaculture Development and Fresh Water Fisheries: R43.8 million
Monitoring, Control and Surveillance: R140.7 million
Marine Resources Management: R42.9 million
Fisheries Research and Development: R65.4 million

Discussion
School Outreach and Funding
Mr A De Blocq Van Scheltinga (DA) asked how DFFE planned to reach double the number of schools compared to the baseline and whether Ms Siviwe Gwarube, Minister, Department of Basic Education (DBE), had been consulted to achieve this. He asked whether sufficient funding was available.
Declining Bursary Numbers
He asked why bursary numbers were declining and whether this was linked to the ending of the Groen Sebenza programme.
Target Setting Concerns
On Programme 2, he questioned the rationale behind the target of 157 days and why it was not rounded to 150 days.
He raised concerns regarding how targets for inspections and dockets were set. He asked why the Department did not aim to fine 100% of those in non-compliance, stating that the focus should be on ensuring compliance rather than achieving quotas.
Marine Species Monitoring and Conservation Plans
Under Programme 3, he queried why penguins and humpback whales were selected for monitoring instead of other threatened species. He noted that the Draft African Penguin Biodiversity Management Plan had taken too long to finalise while penguin populations continued to decline by 6–8% annually. He asked why the APP still referred only to a draft, despite already completed stakeholder engagements.
Antarctica Treaties and Relief Voyages
He asked for the reasons behind the amendment of the Antarctica Treaties Act and proposed that the Committee receive a full briefing on South Africa’s involvement in Antarctica.
Regarding the three relief and scientific voyages to remote stations, he asked whether voyages through the EEZ and to Antarctica were still taking place or whether they were no longer prioritised for measurement.
Greenhouse Gas Emissions and Revenue Targets
He noted that DFFE was not targeting a reduction in greenhouse gas emissions relative to 2022 levels and said this was not progressive.
He asked whether the lower revenue projections under targets 4.3 and 4.6 were due to the withdrawal of the United States of America.
Climate Change Positions and G20 Meetings
He requested clarity on the intended purpose of climate change positions. Regarding the G20 meetings, he said clear outcomes should be prioritised over simply hosting events. He noted that several targets did not appear to adhere to the Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, and Time-bound (SMART) principles.
Beneficiary Counting and Biodiversity Targets
Under Programme 5, he asked whether there was a risk of double-counting beneficiaries, given that SANBI, SANParks, and iSimangaliso reported separately, despite receiving transfers from DFFE.
He asked whether adding 100,000 hectares to the conservation estate would assist South Africa in achieving the 30 by 30 target, whether the land identified was of high conservation value, or whether it was simply a numbers-driven exercise, and whether any new sites had been identified for Ramsar wetland designation.
He queried why captive lion breeding and canned hunting were not mentioned in the biodiversity and conservation objectives.
Ocean and Biodiversity Economy Beneficiaries
He noted that while there were 1 000 beneficiaries in the ocean economy, only 120 were reflected for the biodiversity economy. He suggested that there should be greater internal competition to improve performance.
Programme 6 Budget Reductions
He expressed concern over the significant budget cuts in Programme 6 and asked whether targets had been adjusted accordingly.
Cleaning and Greening Initiatives
He asked why cleaning and greening initiatives remained a national departmental priority despite budget constraints and their apparent alignment with municipal responsibilities.
National Waste Management Strategy
He enquired whether the revised National Waste Management Strategy had been published, noting that it was not mentioned in the APP.
Licensing Targets and Tyre Stockpiles
Under Programme 7.3, he queried why the licensing target was less than 100%.
He raised concerns about the significant gap between the number of tyres collected and those processed and asked how DFFE planned to address the backlog in existing tyre stockpiles, given the reduction in processing facilities.
Registrations and Fire Protection Associations
On Programmes 8.1 and 8.2, he asked why the number of registrations was lower than the baseline and the connection between Programme 8.2 and the KISS objective.
He asked what Fire Protection Associations (FPAs) were being assessed for, why the number was set at 40, and whether this was linked to umbrella organisations' non-payment of FPAs. He noted that FPAs in the Free State, Northern Cape, and North West provinces had reportedly gone without funding for several years.
Patrols and Fisheries Research Voyages
Under Programme 9, he asked what the patrols entailed, which vessels were being used, and whether these patrols were effective.
He noted the absence of fisheries research voyages in the APPs, despite their importance to the scientific management of fisheries. He said the exclusion was concerning, especially given the recent compromises and cancellations of voyages, and stressed that the lack of monitoring would prevent problems from being addressed.
Transfers and Subsidies to Foreign Governments
He asked why significant amounts under "transfers and subsidies" were allocated to foreign governments and whether these funds were being utilised effectively.
Climate Change Negotiation Positions and International Finance
Ms R Nalumango (ANC) asked the Department to clarify the four climate change negotiation positions. She also asked what the US$120 million raised from international sources would be used for and what conditions were attached to the grant.
Support for Beneficiaries and Fishers
She requested details on the support given to the 780 biodiversity economy beneficiaries and asked whether there were plans to increase support for small-scale fishers in the medium term.
Budget Allocations and Capital Investment
Mr W Peach (DA) noted that the Medium-Term Revenue Framework (MTRF) allocations and capital expenditure were just above 1% of the budget. He asked how this compared to global or national benchmarks and whether there was scope to increase this percentage.
Prosecutions and Inter-Agency Support
On prosecutions, he suggested that the Committee receive a briefing from the National Prosecuting Authority (NPA), as there was concern that the NPA was not providing sufficient support to DFFE to secure appropriate outcomes.
Tire-Derived Fuels and Industrial Use
In the short term, he asked whether the use of tire-derived fuels (TDFs) in industries such as cement, forestry, papermaking, and Eskom had been adequately investigated.
Maritime Protection and Defence Collaboration
Concerning fisheries, he expressed concern regarding patrols within South Africa’s EEZ, emphasising that the zone had been exploited and required protection. He questioned whether the Minister had approached the South African Navy for assistance, even in the absence of a formal mandate. He proposed that a Memorandum of Understanding (MoU) be established between relevant Departments and suggested that the South African National Defence Force (SANDF) could contribute to job creation and resource protection initiatives. Furthermore, he inquired whether the South African Air Force (SAAF) still played a role in maritime patrols, noting that this function had previously been carried out but seemed to have ceased.
Disability Employment and Behaviour Change
Ms J Khumalo (ANC) asked what actions DFFE was taking to improve progress toward the 2% employment target for persons with disabilities. She also asked how the Department measured the impact of environmental awareness campaigns in schools, for example, through feedback tools or behaviour tracking.
Criminal Investigations and Compliance

Impact of Oceans Economy Programme
She asked what tangible impact the Oceans Economy Programme had delivered so far.
Lydenburg Facility Concerns

Alignment with AU Vision 2063 and National Plans
Ms N Makasi (ANC) asked how South Africa’s national climate change goals, as set out in the National Development Plan (NDP) and the Climate Change Act, aligned with the African Union’s Vision 2063. Response
Ghost workers at Lydenburg Farm
Ms Tshabalala said the DFFE would investigate the allegations of ghost workers at the Lydenburg farm.
Environmental awareness in schools
Ms Mmamokgadi Mashala, Deputy Director-General (DDG): Corporate Management Services, DFFE, explained that increased numbers in environmental education initiatives were due to the green schools project supported by the Council for Mineral Technology (Mintek). She said the Department previously had a Sector Education Directorate, which worked to integrate environmental awareness into the Curriculum Assessment Policy Statements (CAPS). The current focus was on improving implementation and impact. A memorandum of understanding had been signed with the Department of Basic Education (DBE) to support integration into the curriculum, and further outreach included expos, excursions, and competitions at various levels.
Employment equity and people with disabilities
On employment equity, Ms Mashala said recruitment adverts were shared through a network including the Presidency and non-governmental organisations (NGOs) to attract people with disabilities (PWDs). Retention efforts included reasonable accommodation measures overseen by the Transformation Unit, and a gender focal point was responsible for assessing inclusivity in the workplace. She added that the Department filled vacancies as soon as resignations were submitted to minimise turnover.
Environmental compliance and enforcement
Ms Vanessa Bendeman, DDG: Regulatory Compliance and Sector Monitoring, DFFE, said legal timelines defined the number of days indicated in environmental impact processes — 107 days for standard assessments and 57 for strategic projects. The Department tracked inspections and enforcement trends and referred dockets to the National Prosecuting Authority (NPA) where necessary. She explained that court outcomes often required rehabilitation actions, but non-compliance was frequent at the municipal level.
While prosecution could take years due to tactics such as polluters' changes in legal representation, the Department had trained prosecutors and established working relationships with provincial NPA heads. A memorandum of understanding between DFFE and the NPA was in place, although environmental crimes were not prioritised due to more urgent criminal matters. Donor funding had been used to assist the NPA with capacity.
Marine species monitoring and Antarctic voyages
Ms Marcia Korsten, Chief Director: Oceans Economy, Oceans and Coasts, DFFE, said the focus on penguins and humpback whales was due to their critical population status. She added that 12 other species were also being monitored. The voyage indicator referred to relief and scientific trips to Antarctica and subantarctic territories, but other research voyages, such as those on the Algoa, were also conducted. Amendments to the Antarctica Treaty Act sought to align domestic policy with environmental protocol updates.
Operation Phakisa and ocean economy
Ms Korsten said Operation Phakisa targeted subsectors like marine transport, aquaculture, coastal tourism, and governance, with the goal of R43 billion in investment and 20 000 jobs. The Department served as the secretariat and monitored progress alongside the Department of Planning, Monitoring and Evaluation. A satellite account was being developed to measure the ocean economy’s impact. The Department reported directly on beneficiaries supported by the Oceans and Coasts programme, rather than total jobs across all units.
Climate change and emissions commitments
Mr Maesela Kekana, DDG: Climate Change, Air Quality, and Sustainable Development, DFFE, said emissions targets were embedded in the Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs). Donor funding had declined, particularly from the Global Environment Facility and the Climate Investment Funds. The Department coordinated with entities such as the South African National Biodiversity Institute (SANBI) to access available funding. On staffing, he noted that Cabinet approved climate change negotiation roles annually, and South Africa’s role in the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change’s seventh cycle required strong representation. Despite funding constraints, the G20 meeting had to be hosted, and South Africa’s role was to lead on environmental sustainability discussions.
Air quality and pollutants
Regarding air quality targets, Mr Kekana said the National Ambient Air Quality Standards indicator had been removed due to its long-term nature. Major emitters such as Eskom and the steel industry were being monitored closely to meet the threshold.
Biodiversity and wetland protection
Ms Mohlago Mokgohloa, DDG: Biodiversity Conservation, DFFE, clarified that beneficiary counts were tracked per implementing entity to avoid duplication. She said the Department supported entrepreneurship and worked with agencies under the Department of Small Business Development. The focus on biodiversity conservation included both protected and supporting areas. Ramsar sites were identified based on nine criteria before applications were submitted to the Ramsar Secretariat.
Captive lion breeding and enforcement
On captive lion breeding, Ms Mokgohloa said enforcement was a provincial matter. A prohibition notice was in the system to prevent registration of new facilities.
Budget cuts and project delivery
Dr Nonhlanhla Mkhize, DDG: Environmental Programmes, DFFE, said budget reductions were implemented after signing contracts. The Department had to renegotiate scopes rather than cancel contracts outright. Follow-up treatments were prioritised over initial work to protect existing investments. She added that the municipal cleaning and greening programme started in the previous administration and involved people with disabilities. Although not a core mandate, the Department supported the initiative through agreements with municipalities that required participant lists to be signed by municipal managers.
Waste management and recycling
Ms Mamogala Musekene, DDG: Chemicals and Waste Management, DFFE, said the cleaning and greening programme supported Goal 2 of the national waste strategy. The review of the strategy was underway for finalisation by 2026/27. On licensing, she said a 100% target was set, but some licences required coordination with the Department of Water and Sanitation (DWS). On hydrochlorofluorocarbons (HCFCs), Ms Musekene outlined the phase-out schedule aligned with the Montreal Protocol, with full elimination targeted for 2030. Statistics South Africa (Stats SA) data showed recycling behaviour improved in schools and communities with targeted outreach.
Waste tyres and funding shortfalls
Mr Masopha Moshoeshoe, Acting Chief Executive Officer (CEO), Waste Management Bureau, said the industry plan for tyre waste had been approved but not funded. An explanation had been sought from the Minister of Finance, as the tyre levy was meant to fund waste services. Operational activities had been approved within constraints. Tyre-derived fuel interest was growing, with cement plants in Port Shepstone and Mahikeng planning capacity expansions. However, upfront funding remained a challenge. The Bureau preferred performance-based service contracts over direct asset ownership.
On infrastructure, he explained that 10% of the Urban Settlements Development Grant (USDG), totalling R54 billion over six years, was now accessible for waste infrastructure in metros. Metros needed to show improvements in logistics and disposal tracking to qualify. He noted that both the Bureau and metros shifted to more corporatised models.
Fire management and plantation security
Ms Pumeza Nodada, DDG: Forestry Management, DFFE, said Fire Protection Associations (FPAs) were evaluated using four fire management areas and categorised accordingly. Those in the lowest category were non-functional and required support. She said reporting templates were provided to FPAS, and annual submissions to the Minister were mandatory. Payments were delayed in some provinces but were mostly resolved, with a new structure in the Free State. Forest monitors were deployed as a temporary security measure due to budget limits, and plans were underway to train them through Private Security Industry Regulatory Authority (PSIRA)-accredited institutions. Strategic partners would assist community forest beneficiaries.
EEZ patrols and small-scale fisheries
Ms Sue Middleton, DDG: Fisheries Management, said new patrol targets are aimed at protecting the EEZ from illegal fishing. Patrols would continue under Operation Phakisa using the Department’s four patrol vessels. High costs, especially fuel, remained a concern, and smart technologies like satellites and artificial intelligence were being explored. Fisheries research surveys were no longer in the APP due to their routine nature, but remained in operational plans.
She said there were now 171 registered small-scale cooperatives, and secondary cooperatives would be introduced to handle value-adding activities. Due to budget cuts, only a limited number would receive full support, though postgraduate students would offer all training and mentoring. Co-management structures were being established to ensure sustainable fishing practices.
Clarification on Lydenburg Farm and aquaculture jobs
Ms Middleton confirmed that the Lydenburg farm was privately owned and funding had been provided by the Department of Trade, Industry and Competition (DTIC). A technical assessment was done in 2014. Irregularities, if any, could be investigated. She said the private sector created aquaculture jobs and was no longer reported quarterly in the Annual Performance Plan due to timing challenges.
Other outstanding matters
Ms Tshabalala said the Department’s issue paper guided the G20 work, and South Africa led only one of the working groups. The final declaration would reflect the collective position of all member countries. On bursaries, she said reductions affected programmes like Groen Sebenza due to funding constraints.
She confirmed that although there was no formal memorandum of understanding with the South African Police Service (SAPS), the South African Navy, or the South African Air Force, cooperation was ensured through the broader Inter-Ministerial Committee on Information and Communications Technology and Services (ICTS) cluster. Maintenance and resource constraints remained a challenge. The 1% infrastructure figure related mainly to internal information and communications technology (ICT), and there were concerns that more investment was needed to stimulate economic growth.
iSimangaliso Wetland Park Presentation
Strategic Direction and Leadership of iSimangaliso Wetland Park
iNkosi Mabhudu Tembe, Chairperson of iSimangaliso Wetland Park, and Mr Sibusiso Bukhosini, Chief Executive Officer, briefed the Committee on the entity’s 2025–2030 Strategic Plan and 2025/26 Annual Performance Plan. The plan focused on biodiversity conservation, socio-economic development, and infrastructure investment.
Alignment with National Priorities
The Park hosted two strategic planning sessions in September 2024 to ensure alignment with the Department of Forestry, Fisheries, and the Environment’s Big 6 Priorities. On 31 October 2024, the draft Strategic Plan and APP were submitted to the Department of Planning, Monitoring and Evaluation and National Treasury.
Strategic Focus Areas
The entity’s strategic approach is built around six key priority areas:
Climate Change: Rehabilitation of wetlands and removal of invasive alien species.
KISS: Job creation, socio-economic upliftment, and enabling SMME opportunities.
FILLER: Mitigating biodiversity loss and ensuring the park retains its World Heritage Site status.
Fishing for Freedom: Linking surrounding communities to the ocean economy.
RESET: Streamlining regulatory processes and operational efficiency.
MONEY: Strengthening financial sustainability through revenue generation and investment partnerships.
Challenges to Implementation
Revenue shortfalls were cited as a major challenge, particularly due to reductions in EPWP funding. The Park also noted high expectations from local stakeholders and the need to restore trust in park-community relationships. Wildlife crime remained a pressing concern to be addressed through education and socio-economic partnerships.
Recent Performance and Achievements
Revenue reached R30 million, with marketing campaigns and brand positioning contributing to this growth. The implementation of tourism-based Public-Private Partnerships was highlighted as a key success.
Planned Outcomes and Goals
The strategy includes a range of goals: obtaining unqualified audit outcomes, ensuring prompt payment to service providers, restoring ecosystems through plant removal and wetland rehabilitation, and increasing tourism through targeted marketing. The Park also aims to support local enterprises, implement solar energy infrastructure, and formalise community conservation zones.
Proposed Interventions for Sustainability
iSimangaliso aims to improve community engagement, scale up commercialisation of its facilities through PPPs, and secure additional funding from private investors. The entity is also preparing to assume direct responsibility for conservation activities currently managed by Ezemvelo KZN Wildlife.

R31 million in self-generated revenue
R22 million from GEF7
Planned expenditure includes:
R74 million for personnel
R66 million for maintenance, repairs and security

South African National Biodiversity Institute Presentation
Strategic Planning and Institutional Mandate
Dr Leslie Luthuli, Chairperson, SANBI, Mr Shonisani Munzhedzi, CEO, Dr Theressa Frantz, Chief Director: Biodiversity Research, Assessment and Monitoring, and Mr Elliot Mashile, Chief Operations Officer, briefed the Committee on the 2025–2030 Strategic Plan and the 2025/26 Annual Performance Plan of the South African National Biodiversity Institute. The plan focuses on scientific leadership, conservation, policy support, and transformation.
Progress on Strategic Implementation
SANBI has aligned its strategy with its legal mandate and national priorities. It continues to implement biodiversity conservation initiatives and has made progress in human capital development through targeted training and skills-building.
Key Strategic Priorities
Four thematic areas guide SANBI’s work:
Biodiversity Conservation: Promoting ecosystem health through science-based approaches.
Human Capital Development: Supporting transformation by training a new generation of biodiversity professionals.
Financial Sustainability: Enhancing revenue generation through innovation and partnerships.
Good Governance: Ensuring compliance, strong leadership, and operational efficiency.
Challenges Affecting Delivery
The institution faces constraints related to both funding and staffing. Dependencies on external stakeholders and the high infrastructure cost further impact SANBI’s ability to deliver.
Achievements to Date
Visitor numbers to botanical and zoological gardens have increased. SANBI has expanded its publishing output and successfully trained hundreds of black students and professionals across biodiversity fields.

Targets include increasing visitor numbers to 7 million over five years, completing 250 infrastructure projects, rolling out green energy and water-saving plans, and publishing 500 research papers. SANBI also intends to provide annual support to 13 district municipalities and train 2,265 individuals in biodiversity-related skills. Recommendations include securing additional international and domestic funding, strengthening partnerships to pool resources, and investing in infrastructure upgrades to enhance sustainability and visitor experience.

Discussion
Ecotourism challenges and infrastructure visibility
Ms Nalumango asked iSimangaliso Wetland Park Authority to explain the main reason for its failure to capture the ecotourism market. She also asked whether there was a link between poor infrastructure, low park visibility, and fewer visitors, and how the Authority addressed this connection.
Internship programme continuity
She noted that the Groen Sebenza internship programme would end in May and October 2025 and was therefore excluded from the strategic plan and Annual Performance Plans (APPs). She asked whether there were plans to renew these contracts.
Extended Public Works Programme (EPWP) funding and sustainability
Ms Nalumango highlighted that iSimangaliso Wetland Park Authority was heavily reliant on grants and questioned the impact of the reduction in the Extended Public Works Programme budget from R327 million to R266 million. She asked what the phrase “run out of continuity” meant to explain the R60.6 million revenue drop compared to the previous year. Since there were no plans to revive the programme, she suggested that the funds might be reallocated to other key projects.
Biodiversity funding and regional expansion
She requested an update on the Global Biodiversity Framework Fund and the Wildtrust Fund’s expansion into Mozambique.
Infrastructure and employment creation through SANBI
To the SANBI, she asked how many jobs would be created through the more than 250 infrastructure projects once completed. She also wanted clarity on how SANBI was implementing these projects at the municipal level, as noted on slide 30.
Concerns about SANBI’s presentation format
Mr De Blocq Van Scheltinga found SANBI’s presentation confusing. He pointed out that strategic objectives, visions, and missions were mixed in with the targets and that there were repeated targets on slides 6 and 17. He noted that no baselines were provided to measure progress.
Wetland rehabilitation measurement
He questioned SANBI’s reported rehabilitation of 2 000 m³ of wetlands, suggesting that hectares or square kilometres would be more appropriate units, and asked whether the figure was related to dredging activities.
Quarterly targets and measurement clarity
On slide 22, he said quarterly targets were unclear and asked whether these were cumulative or standalone.
Conservation mandate and reporting gaps
He observed that SANBI’s APPs did not reflect the planned takeover of the national conservation mandate and asked whether the omission meant the transfer had not yet occurred.
Community development and innovation
He praised the linen washing project as a positive idea. He encouraged SANBI to deepen its supply chain development to benefit local communities and to report on these efforts in future.
Biodiversity monitoring and citizen science
On biodiversity monitoring, he asked if this was officially part of SANBI’s mandate, noting its apparent alignment with SANBI’s first and third strategic outcomes—evidence-based policy and active citizenry. He asked how SANBI integrated citizen science and whether it actively participated in such projects.
National Biodiversity Assessment 2025 timeline
He welcomed the upcoming National Biodiversity Assessment 2025 and asked SANBI to commit to a specific release date.
Internship opportunities for postgraduates
Ms Makasi asked SANBI for details on the internship programme for postgraduate students, including the expected number of interns over five years and whether there would be a formal university partnership or open application process.
Work opportunities through environmental programmes
She asked how many jobs had been created through the Ecological Infrastructure Programme and the Greening and Open Space Management Programme.
Support to district municipalities
On page 21, she asked whether SANBI was still supporting district municipalities and, if so, requested the names of the 13 districts involved.
Community empowerment around iSimangaliso
To iSimangaliso Wetland Park Authority, she asked what additional measures were being taken to empower communities neighbouring the park beyond job provision.
Tourism product development and reporting gaps
She noted that 11 tourism products were achieved in the first two quarters, followed by “N/A” for subsequent quarters, and asked what this meant.
Support for small businesses
She asked what support was provided to small, medium and micro enterprises to ensure their full participation.
Public-private partnerships and commercialisation
Ms Makasi asked for more details on the public-private partnership agreement and who the parties were. She requested clarification on the commercialisation agreements already signed.
Equity sharing and benefit distribution frameworks
Mr S Mkhize (MKP) told iSimangaliso Wetland Park Authority that empowerment initiatives often sounded promising but lacked proper implementation. He asked what frameworks were being used to ensure local communities around the park would gain equity or receive royalties from tourism activities, and how such benefits would be distributed.
Biodiversity protection during infrastructure development
He also asked how biodiversity gains would be safeguarded in the context of large-scale infrastructure development.
Audit findings and corrective actions at SANBI
To SANBI, Mr Mkhize asked about the cause of the three material audit findings in the 2022/23 financial year and what steps had been taken to address them.
Red tape reduction and risk management
Ms Khumalo asked iSimangaliso to explain what “reducing red tape” meant and what mechanisms had been introduced to manage it. She also asked for an update on unsettled land claims and whether the parties were willing to agree. She wanted to know whether the Authority was prepared to manage the associated risks in the current or the next five years.
Managing stakeholder expectations and community safety
She noted the presentation’s reference to high stakeholder expectations and asked what these were and how iSimangaliso was managing them. She also asked how communities were involved in combatting wildlife crime. Ms Khumalo asked for further explanation on why iSimangaliso’s offerings had failed to align with ecotourism market expectations.
Visitor growth strategy and partnerships
To SANBI, she asked about the plan to grow annual visitor numbers from 12 000 to 60 000 over five years. She wanted to know whether SANBI intended to partner with other organisations or companies in the same field. She noted SANBI’s 82% increase in income in 2023/24 compared to the 2021/22 baseline and asked for the 2024/25 income target and current progress towards it.
Clarification on resource mobilisation and education programme figures
The Chairperson asked SANBI to outline the types of initiatives that led to the mobilisation of R101 million. She also queried the content on slide 24 under programme three, particularly the link between the R217 808 figure and the 5 000 education groups reportedly participating in the “Kids in Gardens” programme. She requested clarification on both points.
Responses
Mr Bukhosini confirmed that deteriorating infrastructure was directly linked to declining visitor numbers. He explained that tourists are deterred when basic services like water are unavailable. For this reason, infrastructure repairs have been prioritised in the Annual Performance Plans (APPs). While Ezemvelo was initially responsible for this work, iSimangaliso took over after poor delivery and began installing water tanks.
On the Groen Sebenza internship programme
He explained that its continuation depended on grant funding. Without further financial support, iSimangaliso would explore partnerships with organisations like Wildtrust.
On cross-border challenges in Mozambique
He said the situation resulted from past decisions taken under the Trump administration in the United States. iSimangaliso was collaborating with Peace Parks Foundation to offer support. An initial R1.3 million was made available to respond to the challenges, and further discussions were underway.
On the shift from grants to commercialisation
Although iSimangaliso remains dependent on grants, Mr Bukhosini said the Authority was transitioning towards a more sustainable model through commercialisation. He argued that while grants create jobs for one or two years, commercial opportunities, such as maintenance contracts, could last up to 10 or 20 years and provide more security for small, medium and micro enterprises (SMMEs).
On community equity models
He said the 51% community/49% operator ownership model was problematic. Often, community shareholders lacked business knowledge and skills, while investors contributed capital and experience but held less equity. He emphasised that community ownership was important, but education and support were essential to making restitution claims viable and sustainable.
On Public-Private Partnerships
Mr Bukhosini said iSimangaliso was currently only managing legacy agreements such as the Thonga Beach Lodge. New investors would be introduced in the upcoming processes.
On APP terminology and marketing focus
He clarified that “N/A” in the APPs indicated that a specific target did not apply to a given quarter. Marketing efforts were currently focused on two key platforms—the Cape Town market and the Tourism Indaba in Durban—though this did not preclude other promotional work.
On offtake models and community integration
He used the linen washing service to explain the offtake model. Instead of a single operator handling all services, local entrepreneurs were contracted to manage specific business components. This approach created direct commercial links between the park and the community. He stressed that the Authority targeted committed entrepreneurs rather than forming “business communities” where benefits were not equitably shared.
On wetland rehabilitation metrics
He said cubic metres were used as a measurement because the process involved physically removing soil to restore wetland function. The Auditor-General could verify progress by calculating the volume of soil removed.
On target measurement
He said some quarterly targets—such as those for removing alien invasive species—were cumulative, while others—such as the employment of environmental monitors—were tracked annually.
On taking over from Ezemvelo
Mr Bukhosini said that changes had not been included in the APPs because a full legislative review was needed. At present, iSimangaliso is responsible for commercial matters, while Ezemvelo handles law enforcement. Firearm management remained a complex issue requiring careful handling.
On reducing bureaucratic delays
To reduce red tape, iSimangaliso had simplified processes and prioritised prompt payments to service providers, aiming for payment within 30 days.
On land claims and restitution delays
He said the Authority hoped to resolve outstanding matters with the Land Claims Commission, though budget limitations could cause delays, as financial settlements were required.
On managing community expectations
He acknowledged that community expectations were high, particularly around job creation. iSimangaliso addressed this by working with other departments to introduce support programmes and expand opportunities for SMMEs.
Further responses
Mr Munzhedzi explained that SANBI had changed how education beneficiaries were counted. Previously, individual learners were counted, but this proved difficult to verify during audits. Now SANBI counts education groups, hence the revised target of 5 000 groups instead of 217 808 individuals.
On income tracking
The same change applied to income targets. Previously, SANBI measured income as a percentage increase from a baseline. Now, the APPs tracked actual income amounts over five years.
On Strategic Water Source Areas
He said SANBI maps Strategic Water Source Areas and encourages municipalities to integrate these maps and actions into their Integrated Development Plans and Strategic Development Frameworks.
On district municipalities
Dr Frantz listed the 13 district municipalities where SANBI operates, including areas in the Eastern Cape, Western Cape, KwaZulu-Natal, and the City of Cape Town.
On donor funding
He said the R101 million raised had come from various donors, including the Department of Science, Technology and Innovation, which supported the Biodiversity Biobank SA and the Biodiversity Information Programme. The National Research Foundation contributed funds for postdoctoral research fellows. Additional support came from the Global Environment Facility, the Green Climate Fund, and the World Conservation Monitoring Centre, among others.
On biodiversity monitoring and citizen science
Dr Frantz said this was a core responsibility under Section 11(1) of the Biodiversity Act, which includes monitoring the status of species, alien invasives, and genetically modified organisms. Citizen science played a key role in SANBI’s threatened species programme, red listing, and biodiversity atlases.
On the African Bird Project and the Biodiversity Assessment
He said securing sustainable funding was a major obstacle for long-term monitoring initiatives such as the African Bird Project. The launch date for the National Biodiversity Assessment had not yet been confirmed, but SANBI anticipated it would be in quarter three or four of the financial year, with careful consideration to avoid overlap with G20 events.
On boosting visitor numbers
Mr Mashile said SANBI had introduced annual membership cards to encourage repeat visits. National Garden Week had been held for the first time, attracting 120 000 visitors across all botanical gardens. Events and concerts during spring and summer also contributed to increasing visitor numbers.
On infrastructure and tourism projects
Several infrastructure and Public-Private Partnership projects were underway. At the Lowveld National Botanical Garden, plans included developing a zipline to encourage more visitors to stop en route to Kruger National Park. At the National Zoological Garden, SANBI appointed a transitional advisor and registered a PPP to improve revenue and facilities. Proposed developments included a cable car, new restaurants, and upgraded conference venues.
On job creation
Mr Mashile said the number of jobs created depended on project size, each typically subcontracting two SMMEs. Across all projects, more than 500 jobs were expected to be created. Under the Greening and Open Space Management Programme, 297 jobs had been created. The Groen Sebenza programme had produced 1 278 opportunities, of which 301 participants had transitioned into permanent or contract positions in the biodiversity sector.
On work-integrated learning and the value chain
SANBI collaborated closely with universities and Technical and Vocational Education and Training (TVET) colleges. Mr Munzhedzi said SANBI was confident its infrastructure projects contributed to a lasting value chain. For example, restaurant gardens were now largely self-sustaining and supported farming, transport, and storage services. Many associated jobs were not formally recorded but provided ongoing employment.
On revenue and audit matters
Mr Christopher Willis, Chief Director: National Botanical Gardens, said SANBI had a revenue target of R240 million for the year. As of now, R182 million had been achieved, and the full target was unlikely to be met. The 82% increase in revenue was largely due to the reopening of gardens after COVID-19 and a one-off R50 million insurance pay out. To address repetitive audit findings, SANBI was working with the Department of Public Works and Infrastructure on transferring premises and had strengthened revenue collection and monitoring. No new irregular expenditures had been reported.
Closing remarks
The Chairperson reminded the Department to submit written replies to four outstanding questions by 19 May 2025. These relate to the dockets handed to the National Prosecuting Authority during the sixth administration, the preliminary report on the Lydenburg trout farming operations, areas of alignment in the Antarctic Treaty, and the payments of Fire Protection Associations.
The meeting was adjourned.
