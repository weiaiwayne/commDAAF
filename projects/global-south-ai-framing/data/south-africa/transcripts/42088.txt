Meeting Summary
Video
The Portfolio Committee on Higher Education met with the Walter Sisulu University (WSU), the Department of Higher Education and Training (DHET), the National Student Financial Aid Scheme (NSFAS), and university stakeholders to assess the state of governance, administration, teaching and learning, and institutional stability at WSU, and to evaluate whether reported reform measures were improving conditions for students and staff.
The Department noted the University's historical context and reported measurable progress, including stabilised leadership and unqualified audits, while flagging ongoing risks such as governance representation and internal control weaknesses. It highlighted serious campus incidents and the institution's responsive actions, while acknowledging strong academic outcomes and infrastructure investment against a backdrop of severe accommodation shortages.
WSU leadership presented Vision 2030 as its core stabilisation framework, reporting progress in governance, academic restructuring, improved success rates, research development and security upgrades. However, they emphasised that systemic pressures—including over‑75% reliance on NSFAS, student debt of R1.4 billion, severe accommodation shortfalls, and municipal service failures—continually exceeded institutional capacity. They appealed for a differentiated funding model and targeted infrastructure investment to sustain the University's access mandate.
The Student Representative Council (SRC) outlined acute student‑facing challenges: accommodation shortages and unsafe conditions; registration disruptions due to NSFAS delays; withholding of academic documents; unclear bursary balances; and persistent safety concerns. The Council called for urgent infrastructure intervention, improved academic administration, stronger oversight of service providers, and enhanced non‑academic support, including food security and gender‑based violence (GBV) response structures.
The Institutional Forum (IF) argued that stability required more than compliance, highlighting trust deficits, communication breakdowns and low morale. It proposed integrated leadership, stronger stakeholder participation and better monitoring of institutional culture change.
Organised labour unions linked instability to historical governance weaknesses and a unitary model unsuited to WSU’s multi‑campus complexity. They outlined operational constraints for staff, including limited promotion pathways, fragmented information and communications technology (ICT) systems and uneven resource allocation, and proposed investment in infrastructure, human resource (HR) development, phased insourcing and increased baseline funding.
NSFAS acknowledged challenges, defended its payment sequencing which prioritises student allowances and accommodation, and undertook to review the exclusion of Advanced Diploma programmes. It attributed student “defunding” to system‑triggered exceptions like qualification‑code misalignment and dual registration, calling for stronger data collaboration with institutions.
Committee Members consistently questioned whether institutional assurances matched the lived experience of students and staff. The most contentious exchange concerned the Komani water crisis, where management's account of interventions was directly disputed by the SRC and labour, leading. Members questioned whether Parliament was being misled and whether oversight was effective. Members also scrutinised accommodation systems and NSFAS accreditation processes, raising corruption risks and weak standards, and favoured a collaborative accreditation model involving all stakeholders. Members challenged persistent fee disparities for comparable qualifications across institutions and questioned whether state funding should reflect such inequalities, signalling that the issue may inform future legislative work. They criticised mid‑cycle NSFAS policy shifts and payment delays that destabilise institutional planning. Members also pressed the institution on student debt and certificate withholding, debating mechanisms to support graduate employability without undermining financial sustainability.
The Committee resolved to instruct the University's Infrastructure Committee and management to submit a detailed written report on the Komani water situation, providing verified facts, a list of installed and functioning interventions, and clear timelines. NSFAS undertook to escalate the Advanced Diploma funding concern for internal review and confirmed plans to clear outstanding tuition arrears in the next payment cycle.
The Committee indicated that oversight would continue, with further engagement planned with NSFAS and the Minister on funding practices, accommodation systems and Public‑Private Partnership (PPP) risks. It also reiterated its intention to review the Higher Education Act clause‑by‑clause, with a public participation process commencing on 1 April.
Meeting report
Opening remarks by the Department
Mr Philip Tshabalala, Acting Director-General (DG), Department of Higher Education and Training (DHET), provided background on Walter Sisulu University (WSU), stating that it was established in 2005 through the merger of the University of Transkei, Border Technikon and Eastern Cape Technikon. WSU is one of only two higher education institutions in the Eastern Cape, with four campuses in Mthatha, Butterworth, East London (Buffalo City) and Komani, and seven faculties.
He reported that the current institutional leadership had been in place since 2021, with Prof Rushiella Songca as Vice-Chancellor and Principal. Adv Tembeka Ngcukaitobi SC was elected Chairperson of Council in December 2020. While all five ministerial appointees had been ratified, he noted a concern regarding gender representation: women constituted 33.3% (six members) of Council, compared to 66.7% (12 members) men. The Department indicated that improving gender representation and broader diversity remained a priority.
Mr Tshabalala reported that WSU had received unqualified audit opinions for the past three financial years (2022–2024). However, these were accompanied by material findings relating to non‑compliance with legislation and predetermined objectives. He acknowledged that internal control weaknesses had persisted but stated that the institution’s position had improved substantially compared to a decade ago. He emphasised, however, that repeat findings indicated ongoing risk requiring sustained management attention.
Mr Tshabalala addressed serious incidents during the 2025 academic year, including the fatal shooting of Mr Sinethemba Mpambane, Deputy Vice-Chancellor: Institutional Support, outside the Nelson Mandela Drive Campus in Mthatha in June, and a separate protest-related incident that resulted in the fatal shooting of student Mr Mbolekwa.
He explained that the Department had requested a formal report from the University. Council had outlined the circumstances and confirmed several immediate responses: the precautionary suspension of a residence manager (with criminal proceedings initiated); reinforcement of the no‑gun policy across all campuses; the establishment of an independent commission of inquiry led by retired Justice C Jafta; and the urgent appointment of a campus manager. The commission’s terms of reference would include a full review of policies and practices at the time of the student’s death, and WSU was expected to share the final report to enable sector‑wide learning.
He reported strong teaching and learning outcomes in 2023, with an overall undergraduate success rate of 82.4%. NSFAS‑funded students performed marginally above the institutional average, achieving an 83.6% success rate—an outcome he described as encouraging given the socioeconomic challenges faced by many students.
Mr Tshabalala noted measurable progress on infrastructure, including an investment of R258 million in property, plant and equipment during the year. He stated that the Department would continue monitoring implementation before committing additional funding. He underlined that student accommodation remained a concern in both availability and quality and indicated that support would be allocated progressively as fiscal space allowed, with the aim of improving conditions for learning and student wellbeing.
Mr Tshabalala concluded by advising that his remarks covered selected highlights from the Department’s inputs.
WSU Council’s overview
Adv Tembeka Ngcukaitobi, Chairperson, WSU Council, reaffirmed the University’s mandate as an institution of access serving predominantly poor, rural and historically marginalised communities in the Eastern Cape. He reported that WSU operates across four campuses with approximately 32 000 students, over 80% of whom are first-generation university entrants.
He reported improved governance stability, with all Council and executive management posts filled, meetings consistently quorate and a new institutional statute adopted in 2024. Council governance functions through a committee system and remains focused on oversight, accountability, policy-making and dispute resolution.
On strategy, he noted that Vision 2030—adopted in 2021—remains the University’s ten-year transition plan from crisis to stability, with progress cited in accreditation, infrastructure restoration and academic integrity. He added that WSU’s impact profile had been strengthened by a national ranking of 5th in South Africa under the United Nations’ (UN) Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) impact category (Times Higher Education, June 2025), reinforcing its identity as both an “impact” and “access” institution.
Adv Ngcukaitobi stressed that financial vulnerability remains the University’s most urgent destabiliser. WSU is reportedly 75% reliant on NSFAS, and in 2025 about 600 students were defunded mid‑semester, which triggered campus unrest. He noted that NSFAS no longer funds Advanced Diploma programmes, leaving 1 365 students requiring immediate financial assistance. The University continues to support over 2 500 unfunded students annually through special‑case exemptions, contributing to student debt that now stands at R1.4 billion. He called for a differentiated funding model for historically disadvantaged institutions, warning that the current framework structurally disadvantages black universities and is fiscally unsustainable.
He described student welfare pressures as severe, with hunger, homelessness and psychological distress increasingly evident. The University raised R3 million in 2025 for student meals, supported by corporate partners including Shoprite Checkers, but he framed this as short‑term mitigation rather than a systemic solution.
On infrastructure, he acknowledged progress but noted that constraints remain a material risk. Approximately 30% of residences still require refurbishment, while the 2030 target of 50% University‑owned accommodation would require 15 000 beds against a current baseline of 5 000. The Student Housing Infrastructure Programme (SHIP) project for 3 200 beds remains stalled pending departmental approval, and land acquisition—especially in Mthatha—continues to be impeded by unresolved land claims.
He highlighted that WSU operates in a high‑crime environment, particularly in Mthatha, where violent crime remains prevalent. While campus security systems have been strengthened and cooperation with policing authorities continues, he argued that sustainable safety requires more decisive government intervention.
In closing, he noted that although Vision 2030 has reduced unrest through stronger student engagement, protest dynamics are evolving outside formal SRC structures, complicating management. He identified financial exclusion as the primary trigger of instability, reinforcing the urgency of funding reform to prevent recurring crises and protect institutional stability.
WSU briefing on governance progress, systemic challenges and funding appeal
Prof Maggie Linington, Acting Vice-Chancellor, presented Walter Sisulu University's (WSU's) governance and institutional progress under Vision 2030, a ten‑year strategy adopted in 2021 to address an inherited crisis. She reported that governance structures had stabilised, with Council and Senate positions filled, meetings consistently quorate, and a structured committee system supported by revised statutes and policies.
She outlined academic restoration measures, which included the restructuring of faculties from 12 to 7, improved programme accreditation and the introduction of data‑driven student support. She noted external recognition, including a Top 5 national placement in the Times Higher Education Impact Rankings 2025, reflecting strong performance in poverty alleviation, education, economic empowerment and governance. In 2025, WSU enrolled approximately 32 000 students and reported improved success and graduation outcomes, strengthened work‑integrated learning, and placement of over 10 000 students with industry partners. Research performance was improving, with growth in outputs, National Research Foundation (NRF)-rated researchers, expansion of postgraduate offerings and increased international partnerships.
She reported progress through residence refurbishments, construction of new academic facilities, full Wi‑Fi rollout in University-owned residences, and strengthened security measures, including CCTV and perimeter access control. However, she noted that the Student Housing Infrastructure Programme (SHIP) project for 3 200 additional beds remained stalled pending departmental approval, despite feasibility and funding readiness.
Prof Linington emphasised that systemic pressures continued to exceed institutional capacity. Over 75% of students depended on the National Student Financial Aid Scheme (NSFAS), and late payments, defunding and policy shifts were identified as direct threats to stability. Student debt stood at R1.4 billion, while accommodation shortfalls remained severe—with 5 000 university‑owned beds for a population of 32 000 students—forcing many into unsafe living environments. She concluded by appealing for decisive government support, stating that WSU’s access mandate could not be sustained without a differentiated funding model, supportive policy interventions and targeted infrastructure investment.

SRC briefing on student welfare, safety and institutional challenges
Mr Mvelo Abenta, President, WSU's Student Representative Council (SRC), outlined student-facing challenges across campuses. He focused on accommodation shortages, delays and complications with the National Student Financial Aid Scheme (NSFAS) that obstructed registration, and the withholding of academic documents which prevented graduation and limited employment opportunities. He also raised concerns regarding bursary balances and student refunds, stating that students were uncertain about how funds were administered and allocated.
The SRC raised serious safety and security concerns, citing violent incidents and perceived failures by outsourced security providers. It flagged governance-related issues, including reputational risks, inconsistencies in student representation, and dissatisfaction regarding the tenure of the outgoing Vice‑Chancellor. Furthermore, the SRC argued that the rationalisation and consolidation process (2023–2025) had disrupted academic continuity and contributed to instability.
The SRC proposed priority interventions, including: urgent accommodation expansion and maintenance; improved teaching, learning and academic administration; stronger oversight of outsourced service providers; strengthened non-academic support such as food banks, community outreach and dedicated Gender‑Based Violence (GBV) and wellness structures; and improved accountability and alignment between management and student leadership.

Institutional Forum (IF) on governance, operational and student challenges
Mr Sandiso Mbongo, Chairperson, WSU Institutional Forum (IF), framed Walter Sisulu University’s (WSU’s) central challenge as restoring institutional stability while fulfilling a multi-dimensional mission across teaching, research and community impact. He highlighted governance and stakeholder participation pressures, including policy and statutory body functionality, executive vacancies, and risks of governance breakdown that could weaken institutional direction and accountability. Declining trust, uneven communication and morale challenges were also identified as compounding instability and weakening cohesion.
Operational constraints were linked directly to student success, particularly infrastructure limitations, residence conditions and the tension between massification and student success. Funding and wellbeing pressures were highlighted as systemic risks—National Student Financial Aid Scheme (NSFAS) dependency, escalating student debt, hunger and rising demand for mental health services—each contributing to disruption if not addressed structurally.
Social cohesion and conflict management were described as a major risk area, referencing protests, labour disputes, governance conflict, gender‑based violence (GBV) systems, security concerns and the need for early‑detection mechanisms. Inclusion challenges were also noted, including accessibility for persons living with disabilities, with transformation and equity positioned as substantive institutional work rather than compliance.
Proposed responses focused on institutional reform priorities, including: adopting best practices through “project packaging”; integrating leadership across infrastructure and organisational development; strengthening governance agility through more democratic systems; improving institutional identity through accessible communication; strengthening human resources (HR) development and skills pipelines; building strategic partnerships aligned to a “community university” approach; and enhancing monitoring and evaluation to measure culture change beyond activity‑based reporting.

Organised labour on governance model, worker constraints and funding needs
Mr Mziyanda Ncizela, WSU Branch Secretary, NEHAWU, presented labour’s perspective, linking current instability to historical governance weaknesses and inadequate transition planning. He argued that the unitary governance model was poorly suited to WSU’s multi‑campus complexity, contributing to slow decision‑making and uneven progress across campuses.
Key worker‑facing constraints were described as structural and developmental: limited promotion pathways and lack of a promotion policy for support staff; inadequate upskilling opportunities; infrastructure shortages affecting productivity, including insufficient teaching, learning and office space; fragmented information and communications technology (ICT) systems; and perceived disparities in resource allocation that undermined morale. These pressures were linked to staff attrition and broader challenges in aligning institutional culture.
NEHAWU further connected governance and operational weaknesses to student unrest and service‑delivery failures, arguing that inconsistent accountability and delayed execution contributed directly to instability. Governance stress was illustrated through frequent special Council meetings and a call for integrity, transparency and collective accountability.
Proposed interventions included: upgrading ICT and research infrastructure; investing in institutional capacity and digital systems; recruiting more PhD‑qualified academics; establishing structured career development for support staff and continuous professional development; strengthening labour relations and implementation of collective agreements; building third‑stream income through short courses; expanding industry and community partnerships; phased insourcing to improve morale and service quality; and decentralising decision‑making to support innovation and academic freedom. The union further called for increased baseline funding and a dedicated post‑merger recovery grant, alongside strengthened Department of Higher Education and Training (DHET)–WSU oversight and inclusive stakeholder forums to rebuild trust.

Mr Thilivhali Mukondeleli, Senior Manager: University Support, NSFAS, acknowledged concerns regarding the exclusion of Advanced Diploma programmes from NSFAS funding and stated that the matter would be escalated internally for consideration by NSFAS management.
He advised that proposals of this nature should be processed through established consultation platforms, including the Chief Financial Officer (CFO) forum, engagements with financial aid officers, and SRC leadership structures via the South African Union of Students (SAUS). He noted that institutions have had opportunities to contribute to funding policy processes and undertook that NSFAS would conduct a formal internal review.
Mr Mukondeleli explained that NSFAS had prioritised student allowances and accommodation payments to ensure monthly disbursements to students, resulting in tuition payments being temporarily deferred. Outstanding September tuition fees were released on 14 November, and only October and November payments remained unpaid at the time of the meeting. Payments scheduled for the first week of December would cover both months, after which all outstanding tuition payments would be fully settled.
Mr Mukondeleli acknowledged that institutions such as Walter Sisulu University (WSU) had not previously engaged directly with the NSFAS accommodation team. He reported that NSFAS had appointed a new senior manager responsible for accommodation, who was present to listen to stakeholder concerns and begin immediate work to address challenges ahead of the next academic year.
Mr Mukondeleli confirmed that universities were currently distributing student allowances while NSFAS processes relating to direct payments continued. The matter was before the courts and, on that basis, he stated that NSFAS could not comment further.
He clarified that NSFAS does not fund postgraduate qualifications and therefore does not fund or administer postgraduate accommodation, with its scope remaining focused on higher certificates and bachelor’s degree programmes.
Mr Mukondeleli further addressed funding decision timelines, indicating that NSFAS would open the 2025 results submission window shortly to enable early funding decisions in January 2026. The first list of continuing funded students had been released on 7 January in the current cycle and NSFAS aimed to maintain this timeline. He encouraged WSU to submit results before the December closure so that funding decisions could be issued by 6–7 January.
Mr Mukondeleli addressed concerns about NSFAS bypassing institutions, stating that NSFAS had formally requested institutions of higher learning to nominate representatives for the 2026 academic year planning committees. The purpose was to ensure issues such as funded lists and accommodation were resolved collaboratively rather than through unilateral NSFAS decisions.
On student "defunding", Mr Mukondeleli explained that NSFAS classifies many such cases as "exceptions" triggered by system controls. These triggers include qualification code misalignment with the Department of Higher Education and Training (DHET)/South African Qualifications Authority (SAQA)/Council on Higher Education (CHE)-approved qualifications; dual registrations across institutions under one ID; the submission of results reflecting a graduation indicator; and data discrepancies. He acknowledged the need for stronger NSFAS–institution collaboration to reduce data-related disruptions.
He concluded by stating that NSFAS had a strong working relationship with WSU, particularly with the CFO and finance team, and that operational issues were generally addressed as they arose.
Discussion
The Chairperson queried whether WSU had students who applied outside normal institutional processes and whether any “defunded” cases were linked to that cohort. He noted that Members continued to receive complaints from other institutions about unpaid allowances and outstanding tuition following the Department’s announcement.
Mr Mukondeleli responded that allowances were paid once valid registration data had been submitted and processed. He stated that at some institutions, funds released by NSFAS had not reached students because banking details were still outstanding at the financial aid office. He clarified that this example did not relate to WSU but was raised to illustrate how delays could arise from student requirements and institutional processes.
The Chairperson also addressed concerns raised by labour about centralising the Law programme on a single campus. He argued that duplicating the same programme across multiple campuses was inefficient, as it required parallel staffing and diluted scarce resources that could otherwise support high-quality academic recruitment. He cautioned, however, that centralisation was only viable where safe and adequate student accommodation existed to support relocation.
He proposed that, given fiscal limitations on large-scale student housing construction, institutions should be enabled to pursue Public Private Partnerships (PPPs), but stressed these must be centrally guided to prevent contract failure and exploitation. He cited a recent example from the University of KwaZulu-Natal (UKZN) where an 800-bed PPP resulted in significant institutional costs due to weak contractual protections. He emphasised that PPPs must enforce municipal and national building standards, ensure long-term maintenance, and protect universities when assets are transferred back after a contracted period (e.g., 25 years). He noted a case where structural defects had prevented the issuance of an occupation certificate and debt was reportedly sold to avoid liability.
The Chairperson highlighted the scale of WSU’s accommodation risk, noting that while approximately 25 000 students were housed in various arrangements, only 5 000 beds were university-owned. He warned that heavy reliance on private accommodation created financial and institutional exposure, with resources flowing externally that could otherwise support WSU’s sustainability.
He argued that NSFAS should play a more active role in accommodation oversight, citing that NSFAS paid more than R14 billion annually toward student accommodation. NSFAS should not function merely as a passive payer but should be linked to accountability and governance. In this context, he referenced allegations from some accommodation providers that certain institutions allegedly claimed higher amounts from NSFAS than they paid to providers, amounting to corruption. He clarified that this was presented illustratively and was not confirmed as specific to WSU.
The Chairperson also raised serious allegations of unethical conduct within accommodation accreditation processes. These included claims that some staff deliberately allowed university residences to deteriorate to favour private providers, and that improper incentives (such as “cold drinks” and other benefits) were demanded for accreditation and placement. He noted that NSFAS had also faced challenges with unethical service providers during attempts to manage accreditation, citing reports of accommodation being accredited despite being visibly uninhabitable.
He proposed a joint NSFAS–university “dual accreditation” model with representation from students and workers to strengthen integrity, transparency and oversight. He cautioned student leaders to remain vigilant against being drawn into corrupt practices.
The Chairperson sought further clarification regarding the water issue.
Dr Nkgatho Tlale, Deputy Vice-Chancellor (DVC): Institutional Support and Development, WSU, reported that the water crisis, particularly at the Komani campus, remained severe and had been raised repeatedly. He attributed the root cause to municipal failure to supply sufficient water, affecting Komani and three other campuses or delivery sites.
At Komani, the challenge was compounded by two operational constraints: inconsistent municipal water volume and inadequate pressure to pump water to the campus reservoir due to the campus's elevated location. While the source of the failure might sit outside the University's direct control, he stressed that the operational consequences were immediate and disruptive, requiring institutional intervention.
Dr Tlale outlined a three-phase response. In the short term, the University had relied on water tankers, acknowledging this was a costly and undignified measure that entrenched poor living conditions. As a medium-term measure, the University was installing reservoirs at affected sites to store water when municipal supply was available for release during outages. Light-construction reservoirs, including Jojo tanks, would be installed from January, while concrete and steel reservoirs would require longer lead times. For a long-term solution, the University intended to reduce dependency on the municipality by becoming a water services authority in its own right. This would involve groundwater development, including boreholes and a concession‑based water‑solution procurement process, potentially over 15 years, priced competitively against municipal tariffs.
Ms K Khakhau (DA) acknowledged the response but questioned whether the proposed interventions were appropriate and timely given the ongoing hardship. She argued that Jojo tanks were a low‑cost, rapid‑deployment option that could be installed at scale and linked directly to taps without lengthy delays. She emphasised that the long‑term solution should prioritise boreholes over continued reliance on a persistently failing municipality. Without structural improvement in municipal service delivery, she warned the institution risked long‑term dependency on a broken system. She concluded that the critical gap in the University’s response was the absence of firm recovery timelines, and asked whether effective contingency measures would be in place by the start of the 2026 academic year.
The Chairperson agreed that rapid measures were feasible and clarified that Jojo tank‑related work was already underway, noting that earlier remarks may have unintentionally suggested these were only future plans. He also confirmed that the referenced “groundwater solution” was the proposed borehole system intended to provide long‑term independence from the municipal supply.
Ms Khakhau then sought confirmation from organised labour on whether Jojo tanks were already in place.
Mr Masomelele Nongadlela, Member, WSU Council, disputed the University’s framing, stating that the crisis was longstanding—persisting for nearly two years or longer—and that written requests had been submitted to Human Resources months earlier.
The Chairperson intervened to clarify that the University had not stated the tanks were already installed, but that implementation was being pursued. He asked for concrete timelines.
Dr Tlale reported that the tank‑based intervention had Council approval and that management had assured the Committee installation would be completed by the beginning of the next financial year. He conceded that implementation delays were possible but stated Council was monitoring progress and had received firm undertakings on delivery.
Dr Lunga Mantashe, President, WSU Convocation, added that Council had only become aware of the matter during the current year and argued that procurement could not be rushed in a way that compromised process integrity, noting that a formal process had now been established.
Ms Khakhau rejected what she termed the circularity of governance explanations and redirected the issue to management, emphasising that Council oversight could not substitute for operational implementation.
Mr Mxolisi Dube, Acting Executive Director: Operations and Information and Communications Technology (ICT), reiterated that three interventions were underway: resuscitating an existing borehole at Komani; issuing an expression of interest for sustainable water alternatives across campuses; and continuing to monitor municipal supply. He noted that municipal supply had improved over the preceding one to two weeks, prompting a temporary halt to tanker deliveries. He maintained that borehole resuscitation would be completed by end‑December as a stabilising measure while the longer‑term solution was finalised.
Ms Khakhau concluded that the discussion exposed a serious credibility gap: labour and the Student Representative Council (SRC) had reported that the "plan" described to the Committee was not reflected in practice. This raised the prospect of inaccurate reporting from deployed personnel, weak Council oversight, or deliberate misrepresentation to Members. She also noted that the Council's emphasis on Jojo tanks was not consistent with management's borehole-focused response.
Mr Dube subsequently maintained that Jojo tanks were already installed on site. He described a reticulation arrangement where tanks on the lower campus received municipal supply and were pumped to tanks on the higher campus, with gravity‑fed distribution. He indicated that inadequate municipal pressure had previously required tankering to fill the upper tanks, but the institution was currently relying on improved municipal supply, and he maintained that water was presently available on campus.
Ms Lerato Malikane, Secretary-General, SRC, rejected the management’s account, stating that students continued to experience water shortages and were routinely told the cause was burst pipes. This was followed by prolonged digging and extended periods without water.
Mr Ncizela supported the SRC’s position. He argued that the issue had been repeatedly raised with management, that the delays reflected incompetence and a failure to act, and that unresolved infrastructure failures directly undermined teaching and learning. He asserted that Council intervention had become necessary because management had not implemented decisions timeously, and warned that institutional deterioration would continue without decisive action.
Mr Nongadlela added that management had previously provided only temporary water arrangements, which lasted roughly a week before failures recurred. This created a cycle of short‑term fixes, renewed outages and escalating tensions, which was particularly disruptive to classes and basic sanitation needs.
The Chairperson closed the engagement by instructing the Infrastructure Committee, working with management, to submit a written report on the current water situation. He emphasised that the conflicting versions from Council, management, labour and students indicated a breakdown in accountability and a lack of reliable, shared facts on whether interventions existed and whether supply had stabilised.
He stressed that water and sanitation were non‑negotiable requirements for dignity and operational continuity, and that infrastructure failure, including accommodation conditions, directly impacted student throughput and success. He cautioned that students living off‑campus frequently faced poorer conditions that could extend their time‑to‑completion. He reiterated that the quality and security of accommodation materially affected learning outcomes.
He further warned that infrastructure at historically disadvantaged institutions was uniquely vulnerable, and urged students to protect university assets during periods of protest, noting the risk of opportunistic sabotage and the significant costs of rebuilding. He appealed for sustained government support and reform to ensure targeted investment strengthened historically disadvantaged institutions, noting infrastructure losses in excess of R300 million.
Ms Khakhau expressed dissatisfaction with the explanations on the water supply challenge. She noted the contradiction between the official's report that measures were in place and the persistent reports from students and staff that the plan was not working. This suggested either misrepresentation, reliance on outdated information, or a failure of oversight and monitoring. She stressed the Committee could not be complacent on such a basic issue, especially given the municipality's unreliability. She found it unacceptable that management appeared not to be executing the three-tier approach adopted by the University Council, forcing labour and the SRC to repeatedly report the same problem to Parliament.
The Chairperson stated he did not regard the water matter as minor and noted that while an explanation had been provided, the institution had also been instructed to submit details in writing.
Ms Khakhau requested that Adv Ngcukaitobi personally ensure the operational lead "cracked the whip" within the week.
Adv Ngcukaitobi responded that he had assigned the Chief Financial Officer (CFO) to the matter and would enforce accountability at that level.
Ms Khakhau asked the CFO for his understanding and whether funds had been released.
Mr Morgan Nhiwatiwa, CFO, WSU, stated the assignment was effective immediately. He clarified that funding was not the constraint, but that Finance required specifications from Operations for the borehole system and tanks, which he had not yet received. He undertook to obtain these and proceed with procurement after the meeting.
Ms Khakhau asked what Mr Dube was referring to earlier when he implied funding was available, given that Operations had not submitted specifications.
Mr Nhiwatiwa explained the water solution had three phases as outlined by Mr Dube: Phase 1 (procuring water externally) was complete; Phase 2 was a more sustainable intervention; and Phase 3 a long-term solution.
Ms Khakhau redirected focus to the immediate contingency plan, noting tanks were reported to exist yet labour and students reported no effective supply.
Mr Nhiwatiwa stated that, to his knowledge, tanks were on site but acknowledged concerns about the system's adequacy. He confirmed the University had financed a contracted water supply and issued delivery orders, but could not explain why water was not flowing.
Ms Khakhau asked who was responsible for confirming delivery and value for money.
Mr Nhiwatiwa outlined the process: Finance issues an order specifying volumes, the supplier delivers and invoices, Operations verifies and signs off, then Finance pays. He did not know the supplier's identity off-hand and said Mr Dube was best placed to confirm.
Mr Dube identified the supplier as the Chris Hani District Municipality, explaining that the University bought directly to avoid mark-ups, transported water by bowser to fill tanks at Masibulele College of Education, which then fed the campus by gravity. He claimed consistent supply since October.
Ms Khakhau asked whether organised labour and students were “living in a dreamland,” given their contrary reports.
Mr Dube maintained the challenge was longstanding but that supply had been consistent since the short-term intervention.
Ms Khakhau argued Finance could not absolve itself by assuming Operations was performing, stressing the need for continuous oversight to ensure value for money. She stated she was inclined to trust students and labour over management assurances and underscored the urgency and dignity of the issue.
Accommodation quality and oversight
The Chairperson asked the SRC about accreditation statistics.
Mr Abenta responded that most residences had been migrated to the NSFAS system but were not of acceptable quality.
Ms Khakhau stated that where management fails on basic services like water, it is unlikely to prioritise bed quality. She urged the SRC to conduct an independent assessment before year-end and instructed management to stop discouraging students from oversight. She requested that the Committee flag the issue with NSFAS the following week to review accommodation norms and standards, last updated in 2015.
Council–Senate governance tensions
Ms Khakhau asked APSA to explain its claim that the Senate had concerns about Council interference.
Mr Nongadlela stated that the Senate's advisory role was not handled properly, citing rushed consultations—such as one-hour intervals between Senate and Council meetings on the Vice-Chancellor's renewal and appointment—that reduced deliberation to a perfunctory exercise.
Ms Khakhau sought a response from Adv Ngcukaitobi.
Adv Ngcukaitobi stated that Prof Linington reported Senate resolutions to Council, that Council had considered Senate's views, and that minutes reflected unanimous outcomes. He found it unusual that the matter was being raised now through APSA.
Ms Khakhau concluded that Parliament needed to ventilate substantive governance issues, not Senate "emotions," and asked Prof Linington to reconcile APSA's account with the University's position.
Prof Linington stated that she had chaired the Senate meetings because the current Vice-Chancellor would have been conflicted. She noted that the Adv Ngcukaitobi had presented to the Senate on both occasions. In the second meeting, a question arose about the meaning of "consultation" and its policy interpretation, which she said might require internal resolution. She reported providing Council with one written and one verbal report conveying Senate feedback, and that Council had expressed dissatisfaction with aspects of the resolutions.
She concluded that while there was discussion on the meaning of consultation, no final resolution was reached, suggesting the Senate and Council needed a shared understanding of consultation in policy, including the role of the selection committee—a matter on which she could not offer a definitive solution at that stage.
Ms Khakhau responded that senators are generally sensitive and highly invested in institutional life. She cautioned that it was unhealthy if senators felt aggrieved about vice-chancellor appointments, noting that the relevant statutes and policies are longstanding and should not create interpretive confusion. She urged the institution to manage the discontent constructively and sincerely so that senators felt acknowledged and institutional cohesion was preserved.
Ms Khakhau asked Adv Ngcukaitobi for an explanation regarding the extension of the Council's term.
Adv Ngcukaitobi stated that the Council's initial term had been two years. During the statute renewal process, the draft was published for comment. A proposal by Councillor Jarhana sought to incorporate Convocation into governance and align Council terms with management to improve oversight. He argued that two years was too short, citing national patterns where terms range between four and seven years, and that the Council therefore moved to a five-year term. He noted the draft statute drew comments on other clauses but not on the term extension, and that legal advice supported a transitional clause aligning existing councillors' terms with the new statute. He confirmed the statute was approved by the Minister and published in the Government Gazette, completing the process lawfully.
Mr Nongadlela clarified that the concern was not the change from two to five years, but the transitional arrangement. He stated that staff and the university community perceived the Council as overstepping because councillors nearing the end of their original term would effectively serve an additional five years, resulting in up to nine years. He argued all Council members should have undergone a standard appointment process after the statutory change to ensure transparency and fairness, and that extended Council tenure alongside management renewal contributed to a perception that Council was assuming an operational role.
Ms Khakhau asked whether these concerns had been formally submitted.
Mr Nongadlela confirmed they were submitted through APSA’s representation.
Mr Mbongo clarified that views on the transitional arrangement were raised through the IF during the consultation process. He stated the IF’s advice supported a transitional arrangement, arguing that short council terms undermine contextual understanding and that longer tenure strengthens governance through institutional continuity.
Mr Nongadlela maintained submissions were made through APSA’s representation in the IF and directly by APSA.
Dr Lulamile Ntonzima, Council Secretary and Registrar, WSU, clarified for the record that APSA did not submit comments on the transitional clause during the statute amendment process.
Dr Mantashe noted that statute review occurred at an appropriate time given rationalisation and consolidation, denied ulterior motives, and stated that while not all inputs were adopted, the statute was a negotiated instrument.
The Chairperson observed that all parties agreed on the principles, but the difficulty was that there had been no objection to the transitional clause during the public process.
Ms Khakhau noted the situation was awkward as key individuals were not present to confirm whether submissions were made or were minority views that were voted down. She stressed labour’s central role and urged parties to engage directly to resolve the confusion through dialogue.
Mr J Ngubane (MKP) stated the discussion reflected broader governance failures where councils become disconnected from operational realities. He welcomed the Council’s direct engagement but raised concerns about operational divisions allegedly influencing participation in SRC processes in Butterworth without escalation to Council. He referenced a student leader’s letters and his own correspondence with Prof Songca regarding eligible students being blocked from SRC elections around September, to which he said he received no response.
Adv Ngcukaitobi responded that the matter was well known.
Mr Ngubane sought clarity on why Council or its representatives would be involved in determining eligibility for SRC activities and criticised the failure to respond.
Ms Olivia Mokgatle, Executive Director: Student Affairs/Administration, responded that she had been alerted to the matter about the SRC election process and deadlines in August while attending a workshop. The Acting Director and Butterworth colleagues addressed it online, and Prof Nguza was on campus to attend to the concerns. She explained that the student structure sought recognition but was informed of the required process and protocol and failed to submit its application within the timeframe. By the time the structure approached Mr Yawa, the deadline had passed and the office was already adjudicating structures for the 2024/25 SRC elections, with the SRC term extended due to rationalisation and consolidation.
No new structures were recognised that year; rather, the term of existing recognised structures was extended. Mr Yawa reportedly advised the students they could participate as independent candidates, but they did not follow nomination procedures. She stated there was no intention to exclude anyone, the steps were verified with colleagues, and the matter was reported to the DVC: Teaching and Learning and the Registrar. She expressed concern about students bypassing normal channels and noted it was unfortunate these details were not conveyed to Mr Ngubane.
Mr Ngubane said students maintained they applied on time and asked whether the applications were indeed late.
Ms Mokgatle reiterated that students approached Mr Yawa before the formal recognition process commenced and were guided on the procedure but did not follow the required steps. By the time they attempted submission, the deadline had passed. She added that in 2025 the SRC term was extended and no new recognitions would be processed during the year to accommodate the rationalisation and consolidation transition, with previously recognised structures continuing until end‑December. She proposed earlier and clearer communication at the start of each academic year.
Mr Abenta added that student governance includes the Student Parliament at the campus and institutional levels, which approves recognition of student structures. No application for recognition of a new political formation was submitted, so the institution continued only with existing structures. He stated that Student Affairs does not determine participation; structures must submit applications to Student Parliament for processing.
Mr Ngubane welcomed the responses but cautioned that weak communication drives complaints to Members, which could be avoided with better institutional communication.
Mr Ngubane raised concerns about porous and allegedly unlivable conditions based on student complaints and requested that Adv Ngcukaitobi conduct a walkabout to verify the reports.
Adv Ngcukaitobi returned to the issue of WSU's responsiveness, stating it had become extremely difficult to communicate with the University. He noted this first emerged during his time as President of Convocation when graduates and alumni struggled to reach the institution, including those positioned to facilitate bursaries or contributions. He noted multiple Members had raised similar concerns and requested management to address the matter.
Prof Linington responded that she could not explain why institutional communication was not functional, could not claim emails were answered within certain timeframes, and was uncomfortable responding without verifiable data or a monitoring system.
Mr Ngubane rejected the view that the matter was subjective, stating even the Chair of Council required answers and that he had written four to five letters without response.
Mr Ntonzima explained that at the start of the academic year the University is inundated with inquiries. Applicants often receive formal outcomes but continue engaging in the hope of a different answer, creating a perception of non-responsiveness even where responses were issued earlier. He acknowledged delays and stated the University had introduced a chatbot to strengthen monitoring of service levels and improve responsiveness.
The Chairperson raised concerns about the Registrar’s Office, describing it as the “lifeblood” of the institution. He cited an example in Law where 150 first‑year spaces existed but about 4 000 applications were received; despite this, 250 offers were allegedly made and 240 students registered, with some later rejected after registration. He asked how students could be registered beyond capacity and later reversed. He linked this to Auditor‑General concerns and NSFAS dual‑registration flags, noting identification numbers should not reflect registrations at multiple institutions.
Mr Ntonzima explained the distinction between admissions and registrations, stating institutions often over‑admit to meet enrolment targets because many applicants accept other offers. Provisional admission letters are subject to space, and registration operates on a first‑come, first‑served basis. He added that historically WSU was often a last resort and missed targets, affecting class start dates; the strategy was adjusted to reach targets within two weeks.
The Chairperson clarified that the issue was over‑registration, not over‑admission. She stressed that NSFAS flags dual registration when students are registered, not merely offered admission. She put the allegation directly: if a programme target is 70, the University cannot register 90 students, and asked how such over‑registration could occur.
Mr Ntonzima stated that this does not happen at WSU.
Mr Abenta also stated that WSU does not over‑register. He explained that the dual‑registration cases they encountered involved students who were registered at another institution but were physically studying at WSU. He added that WSU did not have a system indicating when an offer expired, resulting in students sometimes waiting indefinitely for NSFAS outcomes. The University communicated with students only once NSFAS funding was confirmed and clearance obtained, at which stage it indicated the status of quotas. He noted that delayed refunds remained a challenge, with students still pursuing refunds for payments made during the 2023 "queue‑full” process. He proposed clearer communication to applicants, informing them that if they did not register within a short, specified period, their offer would lapse.
The Chairperson clarified that the University might not be aware of students who were registered at WSU but studying elsewhere, as such students did not engage on campus. She stated that NSFAS often detected these cases and that registration records could persist even when students had moved to other institutions. WSU also experienced this problem. She advised the University to review its admissions and registration processes and to present proposed reforms to Council. She supported over‑admission provided it remained provisional but reiterated her concern about allegations of over‑registration and indicated she would forward relevant information to the University.
Adv Ngcukaitobi stated that residence conditions varied significantly, with some residences refurbished and others in severe disrepair. He cited Chumani and Silimela as examples of long‑neglected residences and referred to safety risks, including a student death allegedly linked to an electrical fault resulting from poor maintenance. He said Council had demanded a proper maintenance plan as early as 2021. He characterised operations as the University's major weakness, referenced the ongoing commission of inquiry, and noted media reports regarding alleged corruption. He said Council could not run operations directly but could influence them through key appointments, including bringing in Dr Tlale and advertising a Chief Operations Officer (COO)-level operations post—noting that the Mthatha campus was the most critical, hosting approximately 16 000 of the University's 32 000 students.
He also noted leadership instability following the murder of Mr Mpambane and expressed frustration with the lack of progress in the South African Police Service (SAPS) investigation. He emphasised that Council would pursue stronger procurement oversight due to its fiduciary responsibilities, citing past conflict with management over resolutions requiring Council approval for expenditure exceeding R50 million and associated reporting requirements.
Prof Nosisi Feza, DVC: Research and Internationalisation, stated that the library extended its operating hours during examination periods but could not open 24/7 due to staffing constraints, and that alternative rooms were made available.
Mr Abenta disputed this, stating the library only operated until 22:00 during examinations. He said the designated “24‑hour” rooms lacked furniture and were not adequately secured, and argued that security could be deployed to support extended study access.
Mr Ngubane questioned whether management was misleading the Committee and pressed for an explanation as to why the library could not operate for 24 hours during exams.
Prof Feza reiterated that the limitation was the lack of staff and conceded she was not aware that the alternative rooms lacked furniture.
Ms Mlaikane stated that reductions in security personnel affected students and that the “special rooms” were not fit for purpose if they were merely unlocked without proper furniture or security.
Adv Ngcukaitobi agreed the issue required better operational understanding and noted that incorrect information had been presented. However, he cautioned this did not reflect a lack of appreciation for academic needs.
Mr Ngubane referred to WSU employing 43 foreign nationals and asked whether the institution had satisfied itself that no suitable South Africans were available for those roles.
Adv Ngcukaitobi stated he would ask the Executive Director for Human Resources to respond formally but argued that WSU consistently received strong South African applicants, with scarce‑skill exceptions being limited.
Mr Mnyayiza added that many foreign‑national appointments were in science, engineering and technology fields where the institution struggled to attract suitably qualified PhD holders. Appointments were often on three‑year contracts, and the University had to continuously retest the market, though he could not confirm with certainty that no South African candidates existed.
Dr Christians asked why, despite an earlier statement that 308 security personnel were employed, the library was not prioritised during exam peaks. She requested statistics on violent crimes (rape, assault, armed robbery) over the past two years.
Prof Linington apologised for not providing those details, stating the reports existed and were submitted quarterly but were not immediately accessible.
Mr Abenta requested a breakdown of security deployments across campuses, listing safety vulnerabilities including inadequate lighting after cable theft.
The Chairperson questioned the identity of the security provider linked to the earlier fatal incident.
Adv Ngcukaitobi stated it was Ka-Myaluza Security Services.
The Chairperson then questioned Mr Dube about the security company.
Mr Dube stated the current provider in Mthatha was MI7, and the prior provider was Ka-Myaluza Security Services. Additional guards were deployed after the 15 April incident but were withdrawn once the campus stabilised due to budget constraints.
The Chairperson probed Mr Dube's knowledge of meetings related to Ka-Myaluza Security Services, including whether meetings took place at the Intercontinental Hotel in Johannesburg.
Mr Dube confirmed he attended meetings there with the CFO to discuss university business and separation terms after the contract ended. He knew someone who worked as a campus security officer associated with Ka-Myaluza and confirmed his substantive position as Director: Facilities, currently acting in the operations role.
Mr Ngubane requested a written report with evidence supporting management’s claim that students applied late for SRC‑related recognition and participation, given that students had produced evidence while management had not submitted documentation.
Mr S Lonzi (EFF) noted that many issues the Committee was compelled to pursue should ordinarily be addressed through departmental oversight. He argued that because the Department was failing in this role, the Committee was forced into direct engagement with institutions—a pattern now appearing at WSU, where Council was being drawn into unusually hands‑on involvement because operational problems were not being resolved through normal management channels.
He requested the Chairperson’s view and proposed starting the discussion at a policy level. Recalling debates on course costs during a Strategic Planning Camp with the Department, he questioned why, if qualifications are standardised through accreditation, programme fees differ so dramatically across institutions. He warned that unequal outputs would have serious consequences in high‑stakes fields. He argued that disparities enable historically advantaged universities to benefit disproportionately from public funding, while historically disadvantaged institutions charge less for the same courses despite weaker resource bases. He asked what policy interventions could address the imbalance and indicated he would raise the matter with Universities South Africa (USAf) the following week.
Mr Nhiwatiwa agreed. He referred to the fee compact introduced in 2016 with the Department of Higher Education and Training (DHET), which limited fee increases to the Consumer Price Index (CPI). He argued this cap unintentionally entrenched inequality because institutions with historically lower fees remained locked into low‑fee trajectories, while those with high‑fee baselines continued to benefit. He added that the funding formula also disadvantages historically disadvantaged institutions, reinforcing the same gap.
Prof Linington expanded on this, stating that applying the same percentage increase across unequal baselines widens disparities—a 6% increase on R80 000 is materially different from 6% on R20 000. She proposed a sliding scale to correct the compounding effect and suggested establishing a basic tuition fee for specific programmes, after which wealthier institutions could add charges for enhancements they choose to provide.
She cautioned, however, that standardisation must account for structural constraints—some of WSU’s laboratories are not comparable to those at better‑resourced institutions. Policy debates should be guided by equity rather than simplistic uniformity.
Mr Lonzi interjected to test his understanding: did this imply that an institution with superior facilities, such as a world‑class library, would be justified in charging higher fees—effectively suggesting the same qualification is “better” if obtained there?
Prof Linington responded that in an increasingly digital learning environment, a modern physical library is less decisive than robust digital infrastructure. She proposed a differentiated model in which financially stronger institutions could charge more for add‑ons while receiving reduced subsidies, while developing institutions would receive increased subsidies and charge less, balancing opportunity and resourcing over time. She warned that a one‑size‑fits‑all fee policy is overly simplistic and can damage institutional sustainability.
The Chairperson clarified Mr Lonzi’s central argument. He stated that accredited programmes undergo the same South African Qualifications Authority (SAQA) and Council on Higher Education (CHE) processes and are registered on the same National Qualifications Framework (NQF) level, with comparable quality‑assurance requirements. Given this, he questioned why the state—particularly through the NSFAS—pays vastly different amounts for the same NQF‑aligned qualification across institutions.
He illustrated the disparity by noting NSFAS might fund a law qualification at approximately R140 000 at one University versus about R45 000 at another, despite equivalent accreditation and labour‑market outcomes. He acknowledged cost drivers like location, resourcing, class size and staffing profiles, but emphasised these did not justify extreme disparities for the same qualification. He added that Mr Lonzi was exploring the policy and regulatory implications, including the possibility of a Private Member’s Bill, and sought guidance on how to approach the issue.
Prof Linington indicated agreement with Mr Lonzi and stated she could not explain why such imbalances persist.
Ms Fanisa Lamola, Deputy Chairperson, WSU Council, drew a parallel from the accounting profession, stating that parity in graduate competence depends on professional accreditation processes. In accounting, programmes designed to produce Chartered Accountant (CA) outcomes have materially higher resource, staffing and compliance requirements, which increase costs. She argued that if identical NSFAS funding were applied irrespective of professional requirements, the sector would first need substantial investment to ensure all institutions could meet those competency outcomes. CA‑stream programmes require higher‑cost expertise, raising lecturer costs accordingly.
Mr Lonzi asked why “rural” institutions (referring broadly to universities such as Venda, WSU and Zululand) would offer programmes if they are not professionally accredited. He said allowing students to enrol in programmes that do not lead to equivalent professional recognition risked setting them up for failure. He had assumed all university qualifications underwent the same rigorous accreditation and asked whether programmes not meeting professional standards should be disallowed.
Ms Lamola clarified there was nothing improper about a general BCom offering at any institution. However, if an institution wished to offer a specialised CA‑stream, additional professional processes applied. Costs rise with professional endorsement requirements, and if the expectation is equal production of CA-ready graduates, all institutions must be funded and supported to meet those standards. She noted her profession sometimes provides subventions to support institutions that cannot afford the specialised staffing required for endorsed programmes.
The Chairperson intervened, stating Members were not comparing CA‑stream programmes with general degrees. The immediate concern was that a general BCom at WSU, at the University of Cape Town (UCT) and at other public institutions could still be priced very differently despite being the same qualification type. Conflating specialised and general pathways obscured the real issue: why the state funds identical qualifications at materially different rates. He used an analogy that comparing dentistry fees at UCT to a general medical degree at the University of South Africa (UNISA) would be misleading. He said the discussion must remain focused on like‑for‑like qualifications.
Dr Mantashe said the matter had both practical and ideological dimensions, noting policy could not be separated from the philosophical framework underpinning the system. He argued that the assumption that universities function better when autonomous reflects a neoliberal logic that enables institutions to set internal rules and fee structures with limited uniform national control. Fee caps establish ceilings, not uniformity, and historical prestige continues to shape market value, making it difficult to instruct historically privileged institutions on what to charge. He clarified that in accounting, the correct term is "endorsement" rather than "accreditation", noting professional bodies can withdraw endorsement, affecting graduate pathways, and linked the low proportion of Black CAs to historical exclusion and gatekeeping.
He further criticised fragmented institutional rules on credit transfer, arguing that arbitrary differences in credit allocation lead to refusals of transfer despite similar content, causing delays, repeated modules and additional financial burden, with an unquantified but potentially significant national cost. He concluded by arguing for a unified national university system. He suggested technology, including artificial intelligence (AI)-enabled platforms, could help break institutional silos by expanding cross‑institution access to courses.
Adv Ngcukaitobi made two points. First, he cautioned that the Committee must be explicit about the problem it aimed to solve, warning that harmonising fees alone might not improve access depending on the true barriers. Second, he argued higher education had been commodified, with students paying not only for the qualification but also for the institutional "brand", shaped by colonial and apartheid legacies and accumulated advantage. Attempts to intervene are often resisted through arguments about protecting standards and quality. He added that the funding formula replicates Western institutional templates rather than building an African university system responsive to local needs. He advised careful reflection before proceeding with a Private Member's Bill.
Mr Lonzi said his engagement was deliberate because leadership perceptions at historically Black institutions influence how services are delivered. He argued incidents intolerable at historically advantaged universities often receive muted responses at historically disadvantaged institutions, a disparity extending to safety, residence conditions and library access. Operational leadership at historically disadvantaged institutions may normalise conditions never accepted elsewhere, attributing part of the problem to an internalised diminished view of their own institutions. His argument on standardisation extended beyond curriculum and fees to the student experience itself.
Adv Ngcukaitobi agreed that many described conditions were unacceptable. He questioned why administrators tolerated filth and neglect in residences while contractors were paid, and why universities retained service providers who harm students. When councils attempt accountability measures—discipline or non‑renewal of underperforming managers—they are accused of interference, yet councils intervene precisely because neglect has become intolerable. He reflected that during his own student days in the former Transkei, limited resources did not prevent pride and purpose, and structural explanations could not excuse neglect of students.
Mr Lonzi raised concerns about the NSFAS accommodation pilot model. He stated that NSFAS had no reliable mechanism to confirm whether funds paid to intermediary companies were actually paid to landlords. He argued that this “middleman” design was a direct driver of student displacement, as landlords facing bond and operating costs evict students when payments do not flow. He asked the SRC to outline student experiences with the pilot, particularly regarding non‑payment to accommodation providers.
Mr Abenta stated that the NSFAS autopilot accommodation system had largely failed. Landlords experienced prolonged delays, payments often did not match the number of students accommodated, and in some cases, landlords received no payment for months. When payments eventually came through, there was poor traceability. He further alleged that NSFAS sometimes reallocated funds, citing an instance where landlords were instructed around April to remove certain students, yet in November, NSFAS communicated it would repay landlords for students removed seven to eight months earlier—after displacement had already occurred. He supported the view that accommodation management required collaboration between NSFAS, the University and students.
Mr Lonzi added that NSFAS had awarded a tender to four companies to manage the programme, that these companies allegedly lacked Financial Services Provider (FSP) licences, and that they then subcontracted to NetCash, creating a layered intermediary arrangement. He asked the Chairperson to consider the matter as both a legal compliance problem and a governance failure affecting institutions nationally.
Adv Ngcukaitobi reminded Members that he had been appointed by NSFAS in relation to the original four service providers and stated that, together with Werksmans, they had investigated and concluded the scheme was illegal.
Mr Lonzi raised the issue of student debt and the withholding of certificates. He stated that at another institution, significant outstanding student debt had been classified as bad debt, yet qualifications were still withheld from graduates unable to pay. He argued that withholding qualifications traps graduates in unemployment and prevents repayment, proposing that releasing certificates could enable employment and structured repayment arrangements.
Mr Nhiwatiwa responded that student fee debt presented a major sustainability risk, as university revenue relies on government grants and student fees. Debt relief must be paired with controls to prevent recurring accumulation. He reported that, for the first time in years, the institution was developing a proposal with Convocation to reduce or forgive portions of student debt under conditions, enabling certificates to be issued where feasible. Student debt stood at approximately R1.44 billion. He explained the accounting treatment: an impairment provision reduces recognised receivables where recovery is unlikely; a write‑off occurs only when debt is deemed unrecoverable. The institution would not quickly write off debt because services were delivered and cash outlays already incurred.
Dr Mantashe urged the Committee to consider interventions to address escalating student debt nationally, stating the national total was approximately R20 billion, rising from about R11 billion in 2011 despite "free education" reforms. He cited scholarly estimates that R9.2–R10 billion may warrant write‑off. He suggested the state act as guarantor in appropriate circumstances to break the cycle where universities withhold certificates to secure payment while graduates cannot pay without documentation.
The Chairperson commended Council leadership for proactive intervention, describing it as an “activist” governance structure that responds at the early‑warning stage. He referenced sections 43 and 44 of the Higher Education Act, noting the current framework appears to require Ministerial intervention only after collapse or proven mismanagement, by which time damage may be irreversible. The Committee would review the Act clause‑by‑clause and from 1 April the following year would invite public comment from institutions.
He referenced other governance risks, including a prior concern that the Minister’s strategic adviser had been nominated for the National Student Aid Board Chair, raising questions about conflict, though this did not proceed. He also linked governance failure to infrastructure loss, citing a case where major public investment in a campus project was undermined by unethical contractors, leaving structurally compromised student accommodation that poses risk to life. He questioned WSU’s reported R49 million maintenance spend in 2024, stating observed conditions at older residences did not reflect meaningful maintenance and alleging expenditure outcomes were inconsistent with student conditions.
The Chairperson stated that although generally reluctant about commissions of inquiry, he supported WSU's commission process as necessary to establish accountability. He then asked for clarity on what transpired regarding Ka-Myaluza Security and the Butterworth tender.
Mr Nhiwatiwa stated that Ka-Myaluza Security’s contract in Mthatha concluded at the end of 2024. A new tender process for 2024 proceeded while kaMyaluza was still rendering services; kaMyaluza was successful for Butterworth and MI7 for Mthatha. After April incidents and allegations of violence linked to the provider, Council decided to terminate the security contract.
The Chairperson asked whether ka-Myaluza was operating in Butterworth.
Mr Nhiwatiwa said ka-Myaluza was concluding its work in Mthatha on a month‑to‑month basis while procurement processes were finalised. Ka-Myaluza was scheduled to start in Butterworth in May, but the contract was terminated following the incident, although the Butterworth tender had already been awarded on 17 February 2025.
The Chairperson asked whether the CFO and Mr Dube had met the service provider to negotiate an out‑of‑court settlement.
Mr Nhiwatiwa confirmed that after termination they were mandated by management to negotiate settlement terms, following correspondence from the service provider’s lawyers challenging the termination.
The Chairperson asked Mr Ngqulu about his relationship with the provider.
Mr Ndiyakholwa Ngqulu, Executive Director: Vice-Chancellor’s Office for Special Projects, stated he knew the company from its provision of services in Mthatha and knew the owner personally.
The Chairperson challenged how a senior official could know a service provider personally and asked whether it was coincidental that the company won the tender.
Mr Ngqulu stated he was not involved in procurement and therefore did not influence outcomes.
The Chairperson noted allegations that promises were made to the service provider after termination, including suggestions to apply for other tenders, creating serious governance and trust risks. He stated the matter would be deferred to Council for further assistance, as the Committee was not satisfied it was receiving full answers.
The Chairperson raised concerns about a possible variance between the volume of water paid for and what was delivered, suggesting the institution might be paying for 400 kilolitres per day while receiving only 300, with the difference potentially diverted. He linked this to persistent reports of inadequate water pressure and supply. He questioned whether Mr Dube was acting or permanently appointed, stating the seriousness of the crisis required accountable, appropriately positioned leadership. He concluded that continuing water failures undermined dignity and criticised the perceived attitude that treated the issue as minor.
Closing remarks on NSFAS, funding and oversight
The Chairperson stated the Committee would engage with NSFAS the following week and noted WSU likely experienced similar implementation failures. He raised concerns about inconsistent payment sequencing, where NSFAS pays accommodation and allowances while delaying tuition payments despite available funds, calling this administratively irrational and destabilising.
He referenced remarks by the Minister of Finance the previous week, acknowledging some valid concerns. He noted universities also use third‑party service providers to administer payments, but stressed stronger controls and accountability were required to prevent abuse in any intermediary arrangement.
He gave an illustrative example where an intermediary allegedly facilitated an erroneous payment of approximately R14 million to a student, arguing enforcement appears uneven as students are more readily pursued than entities with legal resources.
The Chairperson linked operational breakdowns to mid‑cycle policy changes, citing a 7 March decision to cap funding for non‑accredited accommodation at R2 500 after students had signed leases. While the policy intention might be correct, the timing was inappropriate and disruptive, and the Committee did not support the manner of implementation.
He highlighted defunding decisions in June affecting approximately 11 000 students nationally, linked to application timing, which imposed direct financial burdens on institutions. He criticised institutions that respond by evicting students and issuing fee demands, converting students with no prior debt into debtors. He welcomed WSU’s position of not removing students in these circumstances and urged NSFAS to close funding gaps instead of shifting consequences.
He noted a September announcement that an estimated R11 billion shortfall had been resolved, yet students continued to report unpaid allowances months later, causing reputational harm.
The Chairperson stated that an internal review suggested historically privileged institutions still receive a larger share of infrastructure grants than historically disadvantaged institutions, which was inequitable. Institutions with the greatest backlogs should receive proportionally higher allocations, while well‑resourced institutions should primarily receive maintenance funding. These issues would be pursued further with the Department and Ministry.
He acknowledged the complexity of withholding certificates but proposed that institutions consider issuing confirmations of completion and allowing graduation processes for qualifying students with outstanding fees. Where employers require proof, institutions could verify completion directly to support employability.
Mr Ntonzima stated that where a student needs proof for a job, the institution provides the certificate directly to the employer.
The Chairperson said he would verify this practice.
Mr Lonzi appealed to the delegation’s humanity and requested a commitment that the library be opened for 24 hours during the current examinations.
The Chairperson stated WSU should be recognised as an institution of knowledge production with a responsibility to contribute to addressing national and global challenges. He recalled attending the Chancellor’s inauguration in June 2023 and noted progress was evident then, with possible further improvements since.
He noted that WSU alumni regularly raise concerns affecting the institution and surrounding communities, and that the Committee receives complaints from students and service providers through community networks, though time constraints limited coverage.
He encouraged Members to undertake institutional visits and approach universities as instruments for improving lives. Programmes should produce graduates with skills to address urgent local challenges in the Eastern Cape, such as water provision.
He referred to an initiative launched by former Minister Prof Blade Nzimande to assist a municipality in Alice with water reticulation after shortages affected a campus, arguing that universities should produce professionals who can address municipal service failures.
The Chairperson issued a strong warning against corruption and theft of public resources, especially where funding intended to restore dignity—such as for water and student support—is misappropriated, stating theft from the poor is especially serious.
While the Committee has encountered institutions in worse condition, it was significant that the Council reported most Council‑appointable management posts had been filled, a positive governance indicator. He also noted openness in WSU processes, such as the public nature of aspects of the vice‑chancellor interview process.
He contrasted this with practices elsewhere, citing an example involving alleged conflict of interest in acting vice‑chancellor deliberations and concerns about retention arrangements, arguing such matters should be handled through proper governance channels.
The Chairperson added that some university statutes create governance risks, including weak term‑limit controls for senior positions, contributing to instability and reputational harm. The Committee would continue reviewing statutes and governance arrangements across the sector.
He concluded by appealing to Council, management, staff and student leadership to strengthen governance culture, uphold ethical conduct and safeguard institutional stability. He also expressed concern about online SRC elections and suggested trialling manual voting to test integrity. He thanked participants and concluded the engagement.
The meeting was adjourned.
