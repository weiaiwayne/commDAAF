2025/26 Annual Performance Plans
In a virtual meeting, the Department of Home Affairs briefed the Select Committee on the changes effected to its revised Strategic Plan 2025-2030 and revised Annual Performance Plan for 2025/26. The new Home Affairs @ Home initiative was highlighted as well as outcome indicators and targets, achievements and challenges including updates on service delivery challenges, backlogs as well as the legislation programme for 2025/26. The financial report included budget allocations, steps taken to clear the Auditor General findings on irregular expenditure disciplinary steps taken, and other audit findings.
Committee members asked how DHA is leveraging the newly gazetted SITA regulations to accelerate its digitalisation process; if feedback on visa delays is communicated to applicants; interventions to assist outlying areas to ensure services are provided; if DHA is working with local municipalities to implement the latest control measures aimed at regulating spaza shops and what is being done to combat corruption at South African borders. They asked if earlier challenge of insufficient mobile units has been addressed; what measures DHA is taking to overcome the challenge to pay its service providers within the stipulated 30-day period; if the Home Affairs @ Home initiative will be effective in rural areas where this service is most needed considering network availability; and what mechanisms are in place to ensure that those who resign amid misconduct investigations or corruption allegations are still held accountable.
Meeting report
Chairperson’s opening remarks
The Chairperson expressed dissatisfaction at the late submission of the presentation the night before, despite scheduling the meeting well in advance. The Department must ensure that presentations are submitted well before. DHA must improve administrative coordination. Despite this, the Committee would diligently engage with the Strategic Plan, APP and Budget.
The Chairperson welcomed the news report confirming the arrest of two individuals in Denmark linked to an ongoing investigation into a fraudulent passport scheme. The Committee condemns all forms of corruption and commands the Department and law enforcement to actively address such unlawful activities. The Committee continues to expect zero tolerance for corruption across all levels of public service. This meeting is important as it follows the Committee recent strategic planning. It helps align the Department’s focus with the Select Committee’s programme of action. Separate briefings have been scheduled with the Government Printing Works (GPW) and the Border Management Authority (BMA) in the following week to provide deeper engagement on their strategic plans and APPs.
Minister’s comments
Minister Leon Schreiber apologised about the late document submission and promised to make every effort to ensure that this does not recur. He emphasised that digital transformation remains the Department’s priority in its aim at bringing Home Affairs services closer to the citizens of South Africa. Some of the key initiatives included Electronic Travel Authorisation (ETA), expanded partnership with banks, digital access platforms, and courier delivery of documents. Many of these are already operational. A noted success is the Trusted Tour Operator System (TTOS), which has already enabled approximately 15 000 travelers from China and India to obtain secure and efficient visa access within three and a half months. He is confident that the revised Strategic Plan and APP will accelerate the reform process and yield real benefits for South Africans both in the immediate 12-month period and throughout the next five years.
Department of Home Affairs Strategic Plan and Annual Performance Plan
Mr Tommy Makhode, DHA Director General following the re-tabling of the 2025/26 Budget, DHA had to revise its presentation. The figures presented are provisional and currently unaudited, as they remain under review by the Auditor General.
Reasons for changes to 2025/26 APP
• Concerns raised by Portfolio Committee on timelines provided for legislation development
• The APP changes necessitated a change to the Strategic Plan five-year outcome targets.
Reasons for changes to Strategic Plan 2025 to 2030 Outcome Targets
• The South African birth rate is declining. The birth occurrence for foreign births is increasing due to a higher number of foreign national females giving birth in SA hospitals. A different model for birth registration in SA is proposed, hence the 2nd phase of a DHA study on the development of a model for the management of late registration of birth (LRB).
• Misalignment of working hours between DHA and Department of Health – DHA operates from 07h30 to 16h00 on weekdays only whereas Department of Health operates on a 24 hour basis.
• No provision for planned overtime for 2025/26 will impact on birth registration volumes for 2025/26. During 2024/25 overtime worked resulted in the capturing of 63 495 birth registrations (including weekends and public holidays).
• Insufficient DHA staff complement at health facilities (116 staff appointed vs 283 health facilities with capability to issue birth registration on the spot – 41%).
• During 2024/25, 8 218 foreign births were registered which does not form part of the performance population.
The New Home Affairs: Home Affairs @ home
• The Strategic Plan forms the bedrock of the vision to digitally transform the Department of Home Affairs during the 2025 to 2030 government cycle.
• The desired end-state is to deliver a digitally transformed organisation where all civics, immigration and refugee services are delivered in a decentralised manner through electronic channels, to dramatically expand inclusion and deliver dignity for all.
• The vision gives expression to the apex priorities of the Government of National Unity (GNU), which is to grow the economy and create jobs, reduce poverty, and build a capable state.
• A digitally transformed DHA will make a substantial contribution to these three priorities, by positioning the Department as an economic enabler through immigration reform, expanding access to enabling documents to all, and using technology to revolutionise how government delivers services in South Africa.
State of the Nation Address (SONA) 2025
The following SONA commitments are relevant to DHA:
1. Launch a modern ETA driven by AI to automate tourist and short stay visas.
2. Build the first components of digital identity to eliminate identity fraud, expand social and financial inclusion, and ensure access for all “at a touch”.
3. Implement the Trusted Tour Operator Scheme to boost tourism from the burgeoning source markets of China and India.
4. Eradicate the Permitting backlog
Other Key Departmental Priorities
• Continued implementation of DHA Repositioning Programme to give effect to the White Paper on Home Affairs.
• DHA will contribute to public and social employment through the appointment of unemployed young people for the digitisation of more than 340 million paperbased South African civic records and the modernisation of civic services.
• The continued information and communication technology (ICT) modernisation of DHA, including digital transformation initiatives, ensuring network and system stability, etc.
• DHA will endeavour to introduce digital identity and e-Passports by 2029.
• An accelerated rollout of the smart ID card to all eligible persons. The main aim is to ensure eligible citizens are in possession of smart ID cards and to ultimately discontinue the issuance of the green barcoded ID book. The issuance of smart ID cards will be expanded to naturalised citizens and permanent residents
• The automation of the birth registration process for citizens and foreigners at public and private health facilities.
The Strategic Plan and APP Indicators and Targets were outlined (see presentation).
Upcoming Legislation for 2025/26
• The 2024 White Paper on Citizenship, Immigration and Refugee Protection was approved by Cabinet in April 2024 and will inform the drafting of a single legislation for citizenship immigration and refugee protection. This is currently under review with an “Amendment Paper” under draft – this was previously referred to as a Supplementary Paper. During 2024/25 the Department developed the following legislation, bills and regulations:
• Marriage Bill [B43 – 2024]: The Portfolio Committee published the Bill for public participation and has engaged in public hearings; which are continuing in 2025/26.
• One-Stop Border Post Bill [B12 -2024]: The Portfolio Committee has published the Bill for public participation.
• National Identification Bill: The Bill is being revised and will be submitted to Cabinet for approval in 2025/26 for introduction into Parliament.
• Immigration Amendment Bill [B8 – 2024]: The Portfolio Committee has published the Bill for public participation;
• Immigration Regulations (Third Amendment) was approved and published in 2024 for implementation.
Budget 2025/26 to 2027/28
Mr Gordon Hollamby, DHA Chief Financial Officer stated:
• The 2025 Medium-Term Budget Policy Statement (MTBPS) outlined a pro-growth agenda to address prolonged economic and fiscal weakness. However key risks remain, such as lower economic growth and spending pressures.
• The department's baseline increases by R321.2 million in 2025/26, R1.2 billion in 2026/27, and R369.5 million in 2027/28 to mainly fund the Border Management Authority for infrastructure related functions for ports of entry and the borderline, as well as compensation of employees: improvement in conditions of service. Also, R885 million in 2026/27 is allocated to the Electoral Commission to cover costs associated with the 2026 local government elections.
• For the Department, the Goods and Services budget with no additional funding is not sufficient for all major spending pressures and urgent policy priorities. These need to be funded mainly from current baselines, reprioritisations or shifts between programmes.
• Contractual commitments, especially on contracts with a forex component, must be honored. If not, services could be disrupted and litigation ensue.
• Payment of suppliers within 30 days becomes difficult towards financial year-end in particular.
• Other funding pressures experienced: Digitisation Project; Buildings and facilities; Legal costs; and Security Services
Unauthorised, Irregular and Fruitless and Wasteful Expenditure 2024/25
• DHA did not incur unauthorized expenditure in 2024/25.
• Irregular expenditure confirmed for the 2024/25 financial year = R225 million
• Details were given of the irregular expenditure cases due to unauthorised procurement deviation.
• Fruitless and wasteful expenditure consists of a R99 000 payment made to a wrong supplier in the Northern Cape. The recovery process is underway.
Material Losses through Criminal Conduct
• DHA did not disclose any material losses in the 2024/25 Annual Financial Statements.
• However, AGSA issued a Material Irregularity (MI) in 2023/24 on the department’s inability to collect fines and penalties issued to conveyors in terms of sections 50(3) and (4) of the Immigration Act, 2002.
• The Accounting Officer responded to the MI and AGSA subsequently issued recommendations to be implemented.
• DHA responded to the MI recommendations and has already fully implemented some of the recommendations.
• DHA appointed an audit firm to review the supporting schedules / registers for fines and penalties.
• Following this review, an amount of R133 million was determined to be non-operational, untraceable and irrecoverable, while it is proposed that R139 million be impaired and or derecognised.

DHA response
Mr Makhode responded to Mr Gericke that DHA has signed a strategic partnership with the University of Pretoria (UP) and the PSETA to reskill and upskill officials as part of the broader digitalisation strategy. The Western Cape is actively serviced by 20 mobile trucks, covering regions such as the West Coast, Winelands, Swellendam, Hermanus, Mossel Bay, George, and Garden Route. Two new permanent offices have been opened in Tygervalley and Mitchells Plain. DHA is willing to share the full rural outreach schedule upon request.
Mr Makhode said that like other departments, DHA is looking at opportunities created by the SITA regulations.
Mr Makhode said that consequence management when it comes to internal corruption applies to all levels, including SMS members, middle management, and lower levels. DHA is willing to provide more details about this.
Mr Makhode said that squatter settlements fall under the Human Settlement mandate and not DHA. The BMA will be invited to brief the Committee separately on its response to informal border settlements in areas such as Beitbridge and Lebombo.
Mr Makhode explained that there is an existing MOU with the Department of Cooperative Governance and Traditional Affairs (COGTA) about spaza shops and informal traders where Home Affairs contributes to regulatory development and enforcement. DHA is low in staff complement with 7 200 posts with a vacancy rate of 1.6%. As part of ongoing modernisation, 150 new posts have been recently advertised. Over time, digitalisation is expected to reduce dependence on high staff volumes.
He requested that Ms Matshobeni submit personal details privately so DHA can investigate and intervene directly. Mr Billy must make time to visit the Home Affairs and apply to replace his lost ID before it is used illegally
The R885 million allocation to the IEC is to support preparation for the 2026 local government elections. The IEC operates autonomously and DHA cannot present on its behalf. The Committee is encouraged to request a dedicated IEC briefing.
DHA maintained that late payment within 30 days affects fewer than 2% of cases, mainly due to incomplete invoice documentation. Unresolved misconduct cases involving former employees are now referred to the Special Investigating Unit (SIU) under Proclamation 154, signed by the President. Disciplinary action may continue even after exit.
Mr Makhode said that once Home Affairs has been digitalised, some of the vacancies reported in Gauteng might not be needed.
CFO Hallamby said that the Citizens Affairs programme includes citizen affairs management, which refers to the Office of the DDG. Status services are responsible for issuing IDs and passports. Identification services involves the determination of identity. Citizens Affairs supports South Africans from cradle to grave.
The R885 million allocation to the Independent Electoral Commission (IEC) is an earmarked transfer payment intended to support the administration of the upcoming 2026 local government elections. DHA’s role is limited to transferring the funds. There are no conditions attached from DHA’s side. The IEC is a constitutionally independent institution, governed by its own mandate and reporting procedures. The IEC is responsible for how the funds are utilised.
Mr Hallamby stressed that DHA paid 97.92% of invoices within the stipulated 30-day period for 2024/25. A total of 338 invoices were not settled within 30 days, which constitutes less than 2% of all transactions. He attributed the delays to incorrect invoice details or missing documentation, rather than systemic inefficiency. DHA aims to digitalise and automate the invoice payment process. A tracking system is being developed to monitor the status of each invoice from submission to final payment.
Mr Hallamby said that previous adverse findings by the Auditor-General on procurement deviations have triggered a shift in departmental behaviour. DHA has since adopted a risk-averse approach, reducing reliance on deviations and strictly adhering to standard procurement processes. This shift is intended to ensure full compliance and to prevent the recurrence of AG objections in audit outcomes.
DHA welcomed the revised SITA regulations, which allow an alternative procurement route if SITA fails to provide a requested service within 10 days. It also enables faster progression of the digitalisation programme under reduced bureaucratic constraints.
Mr Yusuf Simons, Acting Deputy Director General: Immigration Services, reported substantial progress on the visa application backlog. DHA began with a backlog of 306 041 applications. The latest update indicates that only 3% remains unresolved, with the outstanding portion comprising 5 026 appeal applications and 483 permanent residency applications. Temporary residency applications have been fully processed. DHA adopted a multi-channel strategy to communicate backlog updates. There were monthly media briefings and statements were issued on progress. DHA has a dedicated contact centre which provides real-time updates to applicants. Applicants could send queries via email or consult immigration practitioners. DHA also relied on VFS Global centres where most visa applicants lodge their applications and receive feedback.
Mr Simons said that two formal concessions were deployed in 2024 to mitigate the impact of delays on applicants. These allowed Individuals with proof of application (receipts) to remain legally in South Africa. The same proof also allowed for legal travel, ensuring continuity in the applicants’ personal and professional lives. There has been a measurable reduction in litigation stemming from processing delays. However, litigation remains a persistent risk due to legal practitioners capitalising on administrative delays to prompt judicial relief. A consequence of clearing the primary backlog is a surge in appeals (nearing 20,000), representing a new pressure point in service delivery.
DHA operates under an MOU with COGTA and other security cluster partners. During the 2023–2024 outbreak of foodborne illnesses, a national operation was conducted from November to January targeting spaza shops. He reported that 396 foreign nationals were arrested, and 98 employers were charged. Violations were referred to municipalities for licence revocation. Municipalities are the primary licensing authorities, but Home Affairs ensures immigration law compliance by owners and employees.
In response to the Chairperson's emphasis on enforcement at large-scale construction sites, Mr Simons cited the George building collapse in May 2024 as a case study. Out of 62 workers, 45 were foreign nationals (72%), the majority of whom were undocumented. Among the 34 deceased, 21 were undocumented foreign workers. This tragedy reinforced the need for routine inspections at construction sites.
The Sasolburg Home Affairs office experienced a ceiling collapse, which caused extensive damage, including to network cabling. Repairs to the building infrastructure are complete, and staff have resumed services such as birth and death registration and others. However, connectivity for live capture systems (used for smart ID cards and passports) is still being finalized by the IT unit. To mitigate the disruption, two mobile units have been deployed to serve the affected community in the interim.
The George Home Affairs office is currently operational and supported by a mobile unit. Surrounding areas are serviced by offices in Plettenberg Bay and Mossel Bay. To manage overcrowding, DHA has implemented a booking system, which helps reduce excessive queuing. Plans are underway to open a new office in the municipality (near Mossel Bay) to relieve service pressure in George and surrounding areas.
Mr Simons said that DHA has conducted a Geographic Access Study to assess the alignment of Home Affairs offices with population density and service needs. This data-driven strategy will inform the placement of future offices to ensure equitable access. DHA is advancing digital transformation, aiming to reduce pressure on physical offices by offering more services online.
The Home Affairs office in Dutywa previously operated at the Magistrates Court. In 2023, Home Affairs was evicted by the Department of Justice. As an interim solution, services are now rendered at the Willowdale office. A mobile unit is deployed to Dutywa on selected days. DHA has been offered space in a newly constructed mall in Dutywa, which is under consideration. In Flagstaff, DHA faces similar challenges. This lack of suitable public infrastructure has delayed the establishment of a permanent office. A private mall under construction has been identified as a possible office site.
Mr Makhode asked that the request to present on challenges in the provinces be done in writing which the Chairperson accepted.
The Chairperson reminded the DG and Minister that the Committee has requested the submission of contact details of officials to reach out to for interventions without disrupting the Ministry. This submission is expected by the end of the day.
Ministry remarks
Deputy Minister Njabulo Nzuza explained that the IEC as a Chapter 9 institution functions independently from executive influence. DHA houses the budget vote for the IEC, meaning it merely transfers the allocated funds but no control over IEC operations or spending. He recommended that the Committee invite the IEC to present its operational plans and financial strategy in preparation for the elections.
The Deputy Minister said that to service the rural areas, DHA has recently increased the number of mobile units to nearly 200, with 100 new units added over the past financial year. These mobile offices are used to service remote areas and schools, reducing disruptions to children's education by ensuring identity documents can be issued on-site. He highlighted recent deployments in Mpumalanga as part of the consistent outreach programme.
He pointed to the rapid growth of mobile technology in South Africa. Smartphone penetration is high, even in rural areas. DHA plans to leverage this trend to broaden digital self-service options, thereby reducing the need for physical visits to Home Affairs offices.
Minister Schreiber thanked the Committee for their constructive inputs confirmed that the Parliamentary Liaison Officer (PLO) will be in contact with the contact details requested. He responded to the concerns about connectivity, long queues, access to services in rural areas and expanding its footprint. DHA aims to expand and decentralise access to services. The central strategy will focus on the expansion of the existing cooperation with a number of banks and kiosk services. Digital channels that include smartphone-based applications and integration into banking apps are already used for transactions in both urban and rural contexts.
These measures are not aspirational but are timetabled for rollout within the current financial year. This aligns with the targets outlined in the DHA Annual Performance Plan. The cumulative effect of these measures will significantly improve client experiences and reduce wait times across the country.
As there is a scheduled meeting on the BMA; he will not go too deeply into it. However, efforts are underway to mitigate current funding limitations to ensure that security and efficiency at ports of entry are not compromised.
The Minister said that Home Affairs @ Home is not a discrete pilot project, but rather the long-term vision of DHA’s digital transformation agenda. He closed by saying he appreciated the opportunity to engage with the Committee.

However, she expressed confidence in DHA’s engagement with National Treasury to address these concerns. The Committee supports the budget allocation and APP and DHA's vision is coherent and realistic, building on the progress of the Sixth Administration. There is a clear continuity of service delivery and institutional memory. There is now hope that citizens in rural areas will experience the benefits of modernised and decentralised services.
The meeting was adjourned.
