Employment and Labour

The Portfolio Committee on Employment and Labour met on a virtual platform to be briefed by the Department of Employment and Labour (DEL), the National Economic Development and Labour Council (NEDLAC) and the Supported Employment Enterprises (SEE) on their 2022/23 Annual Reports.
The DEL reported an improvement in its performance registering an increase from 64% in 2021/2022 to 72% in 2022/23. The Department’s financial year audit outcome was unqualified with material findings.
The SEE reported that it posted +88% sales growth for 2022/23, a great improvement from the previous two financial years when the entity was declining. However, the entity achieved a regressed audit outcome in the period under review. Part of its key priorities going forward will include creating
employment opportunities for people with disabilities, improving on the regressed audit opinion,
and growing sales revenue to R80 million.
NEDLAC reported that it received an unqualified audit opinion with reduced findings.
The Committee asked the DEL about its below-par performance, capacity to achieve set targets, failure to implement an ethics strategy, lack of consequence management, the underperformance of Gauteng and Limpopo in terms of inspection and enforcement services, failure to implement consequence management, fruitless and wasteful expenditure, PriceWaterhouseCoopers’ (PWC) Report and issues around the Unemployment Insurance Fund (UIF) and Compensation Fund.
Members expressed concern about the Information and Communication Technology challenges. These related to underspending, equipment needed to perform tasks not being delivered and the issuing of IT devices to staff. It was proposed that the Department and its entities brief the Committee on the issues faced so that there was a clear picture of the reasons behind ICT’s underperformance and challenges.
The Committee asked NEDLAC questions about its Governance Task Team Process and the time it was taking, the recommendations of the NEDLAC Bill in terms of admission criteria and if the re-evaluation of worker compensation was complete.

The bill clarified the admission criteria and one of the key changes was in respect to the community constituency criteria for admission. Which would be federations of associations such as youth, women or climate change organisations.  While the bill set out key criteria, there were thresholds that the Minister (DEL) after consulting with NEDLAC would be able to determine or amend. There was at the moment a threshold for a union federation at 300 000 members and as per the bill the Minister was allowed to amend this threshold to 400 000. There were also thresholds for organised business. The bill also sets out that parties can decide who the representatives are of the NEDLAC governing structures rather than these representatives being appointed by the Minister. The NEDLAC Constitution set out clear processes for the admission and termination of federations. The bill, once finalised, would be sent to this Committee.
The exercise of re-evaluating worker compensation and benchmarking compensation with other similar public entities had been done. NEDLAC was actually in the process of reviewing its worker compensation to ensure it was still competitive and in line with similar public entities. It was also ensured that there was benchmarking and correlation with the compensation of staff at the PCC. NEDLAC’s personnel expenditure was still 50%. This was because previously travel was a huge expense but with the shift to working online, this has decreased. Less travel meant that expenses related to personnel would decrease. This was discussed with the National Treasury and Treasury was comfortable with NEDLAC’s percentage of personnel expenditure.
The Chairperson noted that there was a question raised about a court case which Mr Maduna did not know much about. She asked the Department to provide a response on the issue of the court case.
Ms Bronkhost asked if the DEL could respond to the question on the court case in writing. Or perhaps Mr Nonstele could provide more information on the exact parties involved in the court case. This was because she was struggling to think of which court case this was.
The Chairperson hoped Mr Nonstele was fine with the DEL responding in writing and that it did not delay things. This was because there was a proposal requesting that the company dealing with ICT appear before the Committee. The CFO raised something quite challenging in terms of ICT and non-compliance. She asked that there be clarification on whether the non-compliance of ICT referred to was in relation to the Department not being in compliance with SITA. Was the Department not in compliance with the SITA Act in terms of ICT procurement?
The CFO said as mandated by the SITA Act, ICT procurement had to be done through SITA. SITA was designed to take advantage of ICT procurement in government. The SITA Act states that departments had to use SITA for the procurement of security services. It also stipulated the mandatory and non-mandatory services departments could procure through SITA. Mr Maduna said further instruction notes were also shared by SITA. Before the procurement of three quotations ICT needed to test procurement through SITA. Unfortunately, the DEL’s relationship with SITA was not great and the Department’s analysis of government procurement was not thorough. This was the reason for the CFO spearheading an engagement with SITA. It was to understand SITA’s engagement model so that the Department could adhere to the Act.
Mr Maduna said most ICT procurement was undertaken centrally because of the Department’s intertwined systems related to funds and provincial offices. This was why ICT procurement involved sizeable amounts. Therefore, when there were errors with one or a few transactions the impact was huge in terms of irregular expenditure. Hence the course of action the CFO was putting forward.
Ms Bronkhost said Mr Vishalin Pillay (Director of ICT at the DEL) had the full details on what went wrong with the ICT procurement and could share these details with the Committee.
The Chairperson said that Mr Pillay explaining what happened with procurement would be acceptable because the amount of irregular expenditure due to issues with ICT was alarming.
Mr Pillay said there was a particular procurement that happened concerning information security services where there was a misunderstanding. The misunderstanding was whether these services were a mandatory service provided by SITA and whether SITA could indeed provide this service to the Department. There were engagements with SITA around procuring cybersecurity services. However, evidence of these engagements was not formalised by a letter from SITA granting the Department permission to go out and procure services by the time the AGSA audited the Department. This occurred in the latter part of 2021. Mr Pillay said the invoicing and activation of services was moved to the period under audit now and therefore the procurement was picked up as an irregular expenditure.
Mr Nonstele welcomed the Department’s request to respond to the question on the court case in writing. He requested that the Department take the Committee into confidence and present to them what was going on with the restructuring of the DEL. Mr Nonstele also requested that the DEL present the PWC Report on the UIF and Compensation Fund to the Committee. He said the Department had not responded to these two requests.
Ms Bronkhorst apologised for the Department not responding to Mr Nonstele’s requests. It was because she thought the Committee would send a formal request for the presentations. A presentation could be made on the progress being made with the reconfiguration of the DEL. In so far as the PWC Report was concerned Ms Bronkhorst knew that the project had been finalised and there was a proposed microstructure. However, consultation on how and when the presentation would take place was still needed. Ms Bronkhorst said she would come back to the Committee with a possible date. Perhaps the presentations could be combined.
The Chairperson thanked the Department and its entities. She asked the Deputy Minister to make remarks.
The Deputy Minister thanked the Chairperson and all the presenters from the Department and its entities. She said all the Department’s presenters were able to present on the annual report and what the Department was doing to mitigate the issues raised by the AGSA.
Where the Committee was unhappy with the DEL’s responses correspondence could be sent through the Office of the Chairperson and the DEL could submit in writing further information. This was so the Committee could feel satisfied that the Department was doing what it said it was doing. The Deputy Minister thanked the Committee for the opportunity to appear before them.
The Chairperson thanked the Deputy Minister and said it was concerning that the Department was spending funds on services that were not being rendered at a time when the country was facing financial challenges. The Department’s irregular expenditure had now increased by R100 000 since the last AGSA presentation. This was unacceptable because the Committee was expected to stand and lobby for the Department to receive more funds if possible. Lobbying for the Department was now more difficult considering irregular expenditure had doubled and the culprit was ICT.
The Chairperson said before the rising of Parliament the people involved with ICT needed to appear before the Committee and report on their issues. The UIF and Compensation Fund matters also touched on the ability and capacity of the Department’s leadership. It was unfortunate that the executive authority and accounting officer were not a part of this meeting, but the issue would be raised on Friday because this was also about the integrity of Parliament. Whether there were missing reports or reports structured in a manner where things could not be picked up. It could not be that the same thing was being said about these two entities for five years. She wanted to commend the Sixth Administration’s ability to pick up on and ensure that issues about these two entities were not swept under the carpet. There had been cries about the UIF since 2019 which was when some of the Committee became Members of Parliament. However, the cries now were louder. Perhaps in the next administration, difficult questions raised by stakeholders would be addressed.
The Chairperson said having a department unit that was skyrocketing irregular and wasteful expenditure was quite challenging when the country was dealing with financial issues and the National Treasury was putting in place budget-related regulations. The Department had responded that the non-compliance was because of procurement. This was gathered from Mr Pillay's response. She did not understand what was going on with ICT and was not confident that she could explain the ICT issue to the public when tabling the Committee’s report.
The Chairperson asked that the Department provide a proper answer to the ICT issue when they returned to the Committee. The world of work has changed, and ICT is now essential. What was happening in this Department? Were there external parties that used to be a part of the DEL manipulating the system? The Department needed to tell the Committee so that Parliament and the people being serviced by the DEL could understand. She asked why the Northern Cape did not have a stable risk committee chairperson. She asked that a response be given to this question at an appropriate time.
The Chairperson said the Committee would be meeting with the CCMA and Productivity SA on Friday. This was in preparation for the Committee’s report. She would check to see if the Committee could not meet with those who dealt with ICT because it would not be right of the Committee to stand up and speak on something they lacked tangible knowledge. Especially something that concerned a department unit that had resulted in irregular and wasteful expenditure that has left the department in a state.
The Chairperson thanked the Committee and reminded them of the meeting on Friday.
The meeting adjourned.
