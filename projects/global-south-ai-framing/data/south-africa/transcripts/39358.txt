Private Security Industry Regulatory Authority
The Portfolio Committee on Police met virtually to receive various briefings from the Civilian Secretariat for Police Service (CSPS) and the Private Security Industry Regulatory Authority (PSIRA).
During the opening remarks, the Chairperson addressed a recent incident involving the Deputy President's VIP protectors, which had been handled poorly by the relevant authorities. This issue was subsequently added to the agenda for discussion at the meeting.
The Chairperson expressed concerns about the transition from the Independent Complaints Directorate (ICD) to the Independent Police Investigative Directorate (IPID), noting a significant gap in the investigation of complaints. Further, the Chairperson highlighted the findings of the 2023/24 Victims of Crime Survey and emphasised the need for effective oversight beyond merely handling complaints.
The Deputy Minister provided an update on handling the VIP Protection Unit issue, including details on the disciplinary process and the necessity for improved wellness support for police officers.
The Civilian Secretariat for Police Service presented the legislative schedule for the Seventh Administration. This presentation outlined various proposed bills, such as the South African Police Service Amendment Bill and the Firearms Control Amendment Bill, along with their anticipated processing timeframes.
During the discussion, Members raised concerns about the time required for legislative processes and the need to address laws affecting police work more comprehensively. Emphasis was placed on the importance of expedient legislative action and effective oversight.

Opening Remarks by the Chairperson
Before starting with opening remarks by the Ministry, the Chairperson wanted to discuss an email everyone should have received regarding the incident involving the Deputy President's VIP protectors who had assaulted members of the public. He wanted the Committee to consider how this incident and the subsequent handling by the relevant bodies, including the South African Police Service (SAPS) and the prosecuting authority, had been slow and frustrating, sending the wrong message to the public. He asked for this matter to be added to the agenda for a short discussion.
He then proceeded with opening remarks, mentioning that the Committee would receive a briefing on the budget and annual performance plan of the Private Security Industry Regulatory Authority (PSIRA) for the 2024/25 financial year. This consideration had been postponed due to lack of time during the budget hearings. He emphasised the importance of successful prosecutions following such operations. He also appreciated having the Civilian Secretariat with them to discuss service delivery compliance, as the Committee had been inundated with public complaints against the police, mainly regarding poor services.

The Chairperson also mentioned the findings of the 2023/24 Victims of Crime
Survey
released by the Stats SA, which pointed to a country in crisis. The Committee would invite Stats SA to present these findings in an upcoming meeting. Briefing by the Civilian Secretariat for Police Service
Adv Dawn Bell, Chief Director: Legislation, Civilian Secretariat for Police Service, outlined the structure of her presentation, which would cover the purpose, background, a list of proposed bills, their time frames and objectives, and recommendations to the Portfolio Committee.
She explained that the purpose of the briefing was to update the Portfolio Committee on the proposed legislative schedule of bills that the Department intended to process during the Seventh administration. On slide three, Adv Bell noted that the briefing's goal was to inform the Committee about the legislative schedule for the Seventh administration.
Moving to slide four, she provided background information, stating that, on 25 March 2023, the leader of government business had informed all executive members that only bills already approved for public participation and introduction to Parliament would be included in the 2023 legislative programme. This decision was made in anticipation that the 2024 election year would not allow sufficient time for processing new legislation, particularly Section 76 bills which involve broader consultations.
On slide five, Adv Bell indicated that as a result, the annual performance plans had been adjusted to reflect only the processing of the Independent Police Investigative Directorate (IPID) Amendment Bill, which had been approved by Cabinet on 24 May 2023, for introduction in Parliament. Other bills, such as the South African Police Service (SAPS) Amendment Bill, were removed from the legislative programme for this period. The former Minister of Police informed the leader of government business in September 2023 about the reprioritisation of the legislative programme, and the seventh administration would deal with the SAPS Bill after the 2024 elections.
Slide six detailed that the newly appointed Portfolio Committee on Police requested a briefing from the Civilian Secretariat on the legislative schedule for the Seventh Parliament. A five-year schedule was developed to demonstrate the various processes and envisaged time frames for finalising legislation.
On slide seven, Adv Bell noted that the development, progress, and prioritisation of legislation are guided by the legislative programme for each financial year and may be influenced by directives from the leader of government business, the Portfolio Committee on Police, and other relevant court judgments. Some bills may need to be revisited and reviewed if they are outdated or if they hinder the operations of entities under the Minister of Police.
Slide eight provided a list of bills on the proposed schedule:
- South African Police Service Amendment Bill (Section 76)
- Firearms Control Amendment Bill (Section 75)
- Second Hand Goods Amendment Bill (Section 76)
- Stock Theft Amendment Bill (Section 76)
- Critical Infrastructure Protection Amendment Bill (Section 75)
- Civilian Secretariat for Police Service Amendment Bill (Section 75)
- Private Security Industry Regulatory Authority Amendment Bill (Section 75)
Adv Bell explained that Section 76 bills affect provinces and require consideration by both the National Assembly and the National Council of Provinces, while Section 75 bills do not have that effect.
Slide nine covered the timeframes and objectives of the bills. The South African Police Service Amendment Bill is scheduled for 2024 to 2026, aiming to align with various acts and policies. It proposes amendments related to the regulation of gatherings and the establishment of various community policing structures.
Slide 11 detailed that the Bill is currently under review by the Office of the Chief State Law Advisor. Once amendments are incorporated, they will be presented to the Minister for approval before being tabled in Parliament. Public comments will be invited once the Bill is before the Portfolio Committee.
On Slide 12, Adv Bell discussed the Firearms Control Amendment Bill, which is also scheduled for 2024 to 2026. This Bill aims to strengthen processes related to firearm and ammunition licenses. It was published for public comments in May 2021, receiving over 118 000 comments, and is now in the final stages of refinement.
Slide 14 focused on the Second-Hand Goods Amendment Bill, with a processing timeframe from 2024 to 2027. The Bill aims to improve the regulation of second-hand goods and combat trade in stolen goods.
Slide 15 addressed the Stock Theft Amendment Bill, scheduled for 2024 to 2027. The Bill aims to update the Stock Theft Act of 1959 to better address modern practices and technologies related to stock movement and trade.
Slide 16 reported that the Bill is under review by the Chief State Law Adviser and will be revised accordingly before seeking approval from the Minister.
On Slide 17, Adv Bell discussed the Critical Infrastructure Protection Amendment Bill, with a processing time frame from 2025 to 2028. The amendments aim to streamline the appointment and implementation processes for the Critical Infrastructure Council.
Slide 18 covered the Civilian Secretariat for Police Service Amendment Bill, anticipated for 2025 to 2028. The Bill aims to align the Secretariat Act with the IPID Amendment Act and the South African Police Service Amendment Bill.
Finally, Slide 19 dealt with the Private Security Industry Regulatory Authority Amendment Bill, with a time frame from 2025 to 2029. The Bill aims to address governance issues within the Private Security Industry Regulatory Authority.
Adv Bell concluded with Slide 20, recommending that the Portfolio Committee on Police take note of the proposed legislative schedule of bills intended for processing during this administration.

He explained that their strategy had been reviewed to develop the 2024/25 Annual Performance Plan. Although he encountered issues with the presentation slide, he continued to describe the plan.
Mr Ralioma detailed the reporting structure, which is divided into four programmes:
Finance and Administration
This programme includes three business units: Finance, Corporate Services, and Operations Research and Development. Output indicators include:
- Percentage implementation of the audit action plan: Targeting 100% implementation for the year
- Revenue collection: Aiming to collect 80% of billed revenue
- Implementation of digital business strategy: Targeting 10% implementation
- Vacancy rate: Aiming for no more than 7% vacancy
- Employee performance rating: Targeting at least 95% of assessed employees to perform at a rating of three or above
- Draft regulations approved by the Council: Targeting one draft regulation
- Sector research and surveys: Aiming to complete at least five research reports and four surveys
- Artificial Intelligence monogram: To be completed to help regulate the industry.
Law Enforcement
This core function includes Compliance and Enforcement, and Prosecution. Output indicators include:
- Number of security business inspections: Targeting 5 975 inspections.
- Number of security officer inspections: Targeting 30 640 inspections.
- Firearms license inspections: Targeting 1 800 inspections.
- Complaints finalised through investigation: Targeting 90%.
- Cases of non-compliance successfully prosecuted: Targeting 95%.
Training and Communication
This programme focuses on training development, accreditation, marketing, and communication. Output indicators include:
- New cooperation agreements with international regulatory bodies: Targeting one agreement
- External stakeholder workshops: Targeting 70 workshops
- Marketing campaigns: Targeting 30 campaigns
- Accredited instructors monitored: Targeting 400
- Accredited qualifications: Targeting 12
- Online assessments: Targeting 25 online assessments for security training providers.
Registration
This programme oversees the registration of businesses and security officers. Output indicators include:
- Turnaround time for registration resolutions: Targeting five days for implementation
- Active registered security businesses reviewed: Targeting 2 000 businesses
- Security officer database reviewed: Targeting 30 450 officers.

Responses
Mr Chauke said that he would delegate the question regarding the offices to the CFO, as well as all finance-related inquiries. He mentioned that the question about the regularity of inspections would be handled by Ms Talent Zwane, Deputy Director: Law Enforcement at PSIRA. She was the head of law enforcement. Mr Chauke added that he would address the questions from Mr Skosana, along with those related to extortion, and that other questions would be unpacked as they proceeded.
Ms Sebogodi explained that, regarding the offices, they aimed to finalise the procurement process by the end of the financial year, with the move occurring in the 2025/26 financial year. She confirmed that this timeline was the intended schedule.
Addressing the finance-related question about the budget for surplus, Ms Sebogodi clarified that, when they budget, they must demonstrate their revenue stream and their ability to fund operations. This meant that, if their revenue stream was projected to be R544 million, they could not budget expenses that exceeded this amount, such as R600 million. Therefore, the budget should break even with no deficit.
Ms Sebogodi further explained that they were allowed to accumulate surplus when collecting revenue. If they collected more than projected, they were permitted to generate a surplus in terms of financial statement presentation. However, budgeting for a surplus or a deficit was not allowed. When they had a surplus, they had to apply to National Treasury to retain it according to treasury regulations and the Public Finance Management Act (PFMA).
Regarding the question about why the projected revenue decreased in subsequent years, Ms Sebogodi stated that they considered their salary income and compliance-related fines, which were expected to decrease over time. Other categories of fines were handled by the Labour Department. She noted that they focused on code of conduct transgressions. When preparing the budget for the next financial year, they would reassess whether the downward projection was still relevant and might revise it upwards if necessary.
Mr Chauke added a clarification before allowing the Deputy Director on Law Enforcement to speak. He noted that there was a potential misunderstanding regarding the 20% figure mentioned in the presentation. He explained that the 20% was related to the medium-term framework and not an annual increase. The annual increase is approximately six percent.
Ms Talent Zwane, Deputy Director-General: Law Enforcement, PSIRA, responded to Mr Gana’s concerns about security companies involved in the so-called construction mafia, stating that PSIRA had not previously received such complaints. However, following a recent meeting with Master Builders South Africa, PSIRA has arranged to be informed if security companies are involved in disturbing construction sites.
Regarding the regularity of inspections, Ms Zwane explained that inspections are conducted immediately upon receiving complaints. PSIRA prioritises these complaints and sends inspectors to investigate, taking action against those who undermine the law. She confirmed that PSIRA conducts weekly inspections of businesses and security officers, including operations in nightclubs, where many foreign nationals have been found and restored.
Addressing Mr Skosana’s question, Ms Zwane clarified that PSIRA does not permit foreigners to work in the private security industry unless they have permanent resident status. PSIRA arrests many foreigners found working illegally, although she was unsure of the deportation process or other actions taken when these individuals are handed over to SAPS.
In response to concerns about security guards being heavily armed, Ms Zwane mentioned that PSIRA has proposed fire amendment regulations to the Minister. These regulations aim to address shortcomings related to the issuing, use, and possession of firearms in the security industry. The proposed amendments seek to prohibit the possession of firearms in public spaces and to require companies to inform PSIRA about the use of firearms. The goal is to limit the use of semi-automatic firearms to specific sectors, such as national key points or anti-poaching operations.
Mr Chauke thanked the Chairperson and added to the discussion on extortion. He clarified that extortion occurs in various fields while the initial question focused on the construction mafia. He mentioned that PSIRA had received complaints about extortion in the context of bicycle duty companies and was investigating specific cases in the Eastern Cape and in Mpumalanga. Mr Chauke noted that extortion is a sensitive issue and people often fear reporting it due to intimidation. He acknowledged that the question raised concerns about PSIRA's capacity to handle such offences and stated that PSIRA plans to allocate more resources to address these issues, working in collaboration with SAPS and crime intelligence. He also mentioned the possibility of reviewing the law in the future to enhance investigative powers for tackling such offences.
Mr Chauke then addressed Mr Skosana’s concerns about government departments not paying security companies according to the law, leading to exploitation. He explained that PSIRA publishes annual pricing guidelines to indicate statutory benefits for security officers and obligatory subscriptions. However, National Treasury has been uncooperative in issuing directives to ensure compliance. The problem is that the lowest price often does not cover the legal obligations, leading to non-compliance. Despite these challenges, PSIRA continues to enforce the law and hopes that the ongoing debate will strengthen their position.
Regarding capacity, Mr Chauke highlighted that PSIRA currently has around 80 inspectors for over 30 000 registered companies, of which 16 000 are active. Ideally, each inspector should cover 210 companies to ensure adequate inspection frequency. However, the current ratio is one inspector for 200 companies, which limits their ability to inspect each company regularly. He mentioned that PSIRA requires approximately 66 additional inspectors, which would cost an additional R40 million annually. Mr Chauke suggested reviewing the funding model to incorporate levies and appropriations to support PSIRA’s operations better.
He also addressed the oversight of training institutions, stating that PSIRA is reviewing and centralising training platforms to improve control over industry standards. He emphasised the need for clearer responsibilities for training and indicated that PSIRA plans to consult with stakeholders to address gaps.
Regarding the decline in digitalisation implementation percentages, Mr Chauke explained that, as projects progress, the focus shifts from implementation to monitoring and improving existing systems, leading to a natural decrease in percentage figures.
On the issue of social cohesion, Mr Chauke clarified that security companies are not mandated to manage crowd control; this is the responsibility of the police. However, security companies often arrive first at protest scenes due to the stretched resources of SAPS. He reaffirmed that security companies are not allowed to use live ammunition, and PSIRA investigates any complaints related to the misuse of ammunition.
Finally, Mr Chauke mentioned that PSIRA plans to present draft regulations for firearm control, initially tabled in 2019, to this Committee. These regulations aim to control high-calibre weapons, restrict their use in public areas, and enhance reporting and tracking of firearms. He invited further public participation on these draft regulations.
The Chairperson acknowledged Mr Chauke's detailed response and expressed appreciation for the feedback. He emphasised that clarity on the use of force is crucial and indicated that the Committee will continue to focus on this issue. The Chairperson stressed the importance of ensuring that any amendments to regulations or legislation are approached with good faith and collaboration among all relevant entities, including the private sector, government, and community organisations. He noted that, in the current South African context, it is essential to have all stakeholders contributing effectively, and improving regulation should strengthen rather than diminish the private security industry.
The Chairperson then raised a question about the PSIRA Council's appointment. He pointed out that the Annual Performance Plan (APP) indicates that the Council was appointed in January 2021, with the term extending to 2026. However, he noted that the PSIRA Act originally stipulated a three-year appointment period, which was extended to five years. He sought clarification on this discrepancy.
Mr Chauke confirmed the Chairperson's observation about a gap in the Council's terms. He explained that there was indeed a new Council appointed earlier in the year, following the end of the previous Council's term in December. He noted that four new council members were appointed around April, but only three were present at the meeting. The Chairperson of the Council was also expected to be present but could not attend due to unforeseen circumstances. Mr Chauke added that an additional council member is expected to be appointed by the Minister, as the current term was intended to have five members, but only four have been appointed so far.
Closing Remarks by the Chairperson
The Chairperson noted that, if there were no additional comments on the presentation, he wanted to briefly discuss the oversight matter shared with the group previously. He invited any closing remarks on the presentation and suggested that, if any comments required more detailed responses, Members should feel free to submit them in writing through the Committee Secretary.
He then addressed the oversight visit proposal, which originated from a request by a Member of the provincial legislature in the Eastern Cape. The Chairperson highlighted the urgency of addressing extortion issues in the region, pointing out the widespread and blatant nature of such crimes. He noted that extortion is not limited to the Eastern Cape but is a national concern, with instances like the "protection fees" demanded for basic necessities such as televisions.
The Chairperson proposed an oversight visit to Nelson Mandela Bay and uMthatha over the weekend of 13 to 15 September 2024. He acknowledged the short notice and the need for quality planning and input from all Members. He suggested that, if the visit could not be adequately planned on such short notice, it could be rescheduled for after the next recess. He also proposed that the visit could be split between Nelson Mandela Bay and uMthatha, with a particular focus on uMthatha, where many businesses have recently closed due to the crisis.
He sought Members' thoughts on the proposed oversight visit and the suggested dates.
Ms Letlape expressed her support for the oversight visit, agreeing that the situation in the Eastern Cape, with issues like protection fees and high crime rates, required immediate attention. She endorsed the proposed dates of 13 to 15 September 2024 and agreed that the visit should be conducted as soon as possible. She emphasised the need for the Committee to observe the affected areas and engage with stakeholders to develop effective strategies to combat crime and support businesses.
Ms Schickerling stated that she concurred with her colleague and emphasised the importance of fulfilling the Committee's mandate to conduct oversight. She agreed that the visit should be done as soon as possible, and she expressed her satisfaction with the proposed dates. She indicated that the Committee would await further communication regarding the arrangements from the Chairperson's office.
The Chairperson thanked everyone and noted that the general sentiment supported the proposed oversight visit. Members would receive communication in the next few hours to confirm their availability. He emphasised that only Members who were confirmed would be included, although alternate Members might join if there were vacancies.
