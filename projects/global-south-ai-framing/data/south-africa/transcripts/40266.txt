The Portfolio Committee on Higher Education and Training held a meeting to assess the readiness of Nelson Mandela University (NMU) and the University of Mpumalanga (UMP) for the 2025 academic year. The engagement was part of a series of the Committee’s oversight work, focusing on governance, administration, teaching, and learning at institutions of higher learning.
The Department of Higher Education and Training (DHET) provided an overview of the governance structures at NMU and UMP. While NMU’s Council had one ministerial appointee vacancy, UMP had nine vacancies across various constituencies. The Committee noted concerns regarding governance irregularities at UMP, including allegations of fraud and financial mismanagement.
Financial sustainability was also discussed, with NMU reporting student debt of R376 million for 2023 and UMP reporting R88 million. The Committee emphasised the need for structured interventions to manage student debt and institutional financial health.
The Committee raised concerns about UMP’s handling of the Vice-Chancellor recruitment process. It questioned the Council’s decision to delay the appointment process and seek a legal opinion to justify extending the incumbent’s term. The Chairperson criticised the lack of urgency in finalising the process and warned against governance practices that could undermine institutional integrity.
The Committee underscored the importance of ethical leadership in universities. It called for measures to ensure that university leadership adheres to the highest ethical standards, including the introduction of lifestyle audits for key decision-makers.
The affordability of student accommodation remained a concern, with Members questioning why some university-owned residences charged fees above the National Student Financial Aid Scheme (NSFAS) cap. The Committee urged institutions to ensure equitable access to accommodation, particularly for financially disadvantaged students.
The Committee directed both institutions to submit detailed reports on governance, recruitment processes, and transformation efforts by 5 March 2025. The Committee reaffirmed its commitment to holding institutions accountable and ensuring good governance in the higher education sector.
Meeting report
The Chairperson welcomed attendees and invited them to help themselves to the available refreshments before the meeting commenced. He noted that while the provisions were modest due to financial constraints, attendees could take coffee and fruit before proceedings began.
He announced that the meeting would commence at 09:01, starting with introductions. The Chairperson introduced himself as Mr T Letsie (ANC) and a Member of the Portfolio Committee. Following this, several other attendees, including Committee Members, introduced themselves.
The Chairperson then acknowledged the introductions. He reminded attendees to ensure they spoke directly into the microphone during presentations to maintain clear audio recordings. He quipped that the Committee did not want to attribute statements to individuals that they had not made.
Apologies
Turning to apologies, the Chairperson noted that all executives, including the Minister and two Deputy Ministers, and three Members were absent and had submitted formal apologies.
Agenda
The Chairperson outlined the purpose of the meeting, stating that it formed part of the Committee’s oversight work. The engagement was with two institutions: The University of Mpumalanga (UMP), a university established under the democratic government, and Nelson Mandela University (NMU), named after South Africa’s first democratic president. He highlighted the symbolic significance of pairing these institutions, stating that one carried the name of Nelson Mandela while the other was among the few universities established by the democratic administration.
He reiterated that the primary objective of the meeting was to assess the state of readiness for the 2025 academic year, with a particular focus on registrations, governance, and management. He urged attendees to silence their mobile phones to prevent disruptions, making light of the fact that even if individuals opened applications such as TikTok, the meeting should not be disturbed.
The Chairperson stated that the Committee had invited various stakeholders to provide insight into the readiness of the institutions. The Institutional Forum had been invited to present on academic projects in which it was involved, while the Student Representative Council (SRC) would offer the student perspective on governance, management, and overall preparedness. Workers had also been invited to share their perspectives, challenges, and recommendations on strengthening the post-school education and training sector.
Correspondence
Before handing over to the Director-General to lead the presentations from the Department, the Chairperson provided an overview of recent correspondence between the Committee and the University of Mpumalanga regarding governance concerns. He noted that on 1 November 2024, he had written to the Chairperson of the UMP Council, Mr Sabelo Mahlalela, requesting a response to allegations of governance irregularities raised by the South African Union of Students (SAUS). The allegations pertained to the Council’s decision to extend the term of the interim Vice-Chancellor beyond the prescribed two-term limit. The letter requested a detailed response within seven working days.
The Chairperson informed the Committee that the university had responded on 10 November 2024, two days after the deadline for submission. The response, addressed to himself, acknowledged receipt of the letter on 4 November 2024. The university stated that the governance concerns were based on assertions made by SAUS and that the allegations stemmed from a Council decision taken on 22 May 2024 to extend the tenure of the Vice-Chancellor, Prof Thoko Mayekiso, for a fixed-term period of 12 months following the completion of her second term. The university justified the decision as necessary to ensure stability and continuity during the transitional period while the recruitment process for a new Vice-Chancellor was underway.
The response further assured the Committee that the decision complied with the applicable legislative and regulatory framework, as well as UMP’s internal policies. The university stated that the move was informed by a legal opinion from senior counsel and was in the best interests of the institution. It emphasised that any governance flaws, if they existed, would have hindered the university’s achievements, including ten consecutive years of unqualified audit opinions.
The Chairperson read the letter aloud to ensure all Members were fully apprised of its contents and the context in which questions would be posed.
Opening remarks by DHET
Dr Nkosinathi Sishi, Director-General, Department of Higher Education and Training (DHET), apologised in advance for any difficulty in articulation, stating that he was unwell and under medication. He sought guidance from the Chairperson on whether to provide comments specifically on Nelson Mandela University (NMU) at that stage or to address all relevant matters comprehensively later in the meeting.
He proceeded to present an update on the state of governance, administration, and teaching and learning at NMU. He outlined the governance structures, institutional forum, financial health, and a synopsis of the 2023 annual report. He reported that the NMU Council comprised 28 members, with external members serving fixed terms. He noted that there was one ministerial appointee vacancy, and the DHET was prioritising the filling of such vacancies across all universities. A notice had been published in the Government Gazette inviting nominations and applications for ministerial appointments to university councils.
Dr Sishi detailed the demographics of the NMU Council, stating that it consisted of 18 external and 10 internal members, with 64% of the members not being employees or students of the university, in line with Section 27 of the Higher Education Act. He further highlighted the demographic composition of the Council: 68% African, 14% white, 14% coloured, and 4% Asian.
Providing an overview of the university’s 2023 annual report, he stated that NMU had received an unqualified audit opinion, but material findings had been identified in respect of the audit performance report. Further, significant deficiencies in internal controls concerning the annual performance report had been flagged. He assured the Committee that the DHET was closely monitoring these findings to ensure that there were no repeat occurrences.
On the issue of student debt, he reported that NMU had incurred R376 million in student debt in 2023. The department was conducting an analysis to obtain a comprehensive view of student debt across all institutions. He noted that, as with other universities, the DHET assessed Council performance using a governance scorecard, which measured performance across five key governance areas. While acknowledging that this was not an ideal tool for evaluating university governance efficacy, he stated that the DHET was exploring alternative mechanisms for Council assessment. He indicated that data from the current assessment tool would be shared with the Committee in due course.
Dr Sishi informed the Committee that the Higher Education Act would be reviewed to place greater emphasis on governance matters across all institutions. He emphasised the importance of the Institutional Forum as a key constituency in university governance and committed the department to strengthening its capacity to ensure that it fulfilled its statutory role effectively.
He further stated that there were no serious governance challenges or major concerns at NMU, aside from the issues he had highlighted. However, he noted that recent developments at the university, particularly student protests disrupting the first day of lectures, were of concern. These protests were related to financial aid, clearance, accommodation, and registration challenges. He reported that the DHET had resolved that National Student Financial Aid Scheme (NSFAS) matters remained a priority following the appointment of a new NSFAS board. A Government Gazette had been published announcing the members of the new board.
Dr Sishi expressed the DHET’s commitment to engaging with the reports presented by the university and thanked the Chairperson for his leadership and guidance.

State of Readiness: Nelson Mandela University
Presentation by the Senior Director of Institutional Strategy
Prof Heather Nel, Senior Director: Institutional Strategy Office, Nelson Mandela University (NMU), extended warm greetings to the Chairperson and Members of the Committee. She expressed her pleasure in presenting on behalf of the university, providing an overview of its strategic direction, achievements, and challenges.
She outlined the context in which the university operates, emphasising its Vision 2030 strategy, which is informed by global and national development frameworks such as the United Nations Sustainable Development Goals, Africa Agenda 2063, and South Africa’s National Development Plan. She highlighted NMU’s commitment to being a values-driven institution that honours the legacy of Nelson Mandela by fostering a transformative culture and addressing societal needs through teaching, research, and community engagement.
Strategic Priorities
Prof Nel detailed NMU’s strategic priorities, including repositioning engagement as an equal mission alongside teaching and research, promoting research and innovation excellence, and advancing transformation and decolonisation. She underscored the importance of digitalisation, noting that NMU is developing a virtual academy to enhance its responsiveness to technological advancements. Additionally, she highlighted the university’s focus on student access and success, particularly in supporting financially needy students and addressing challenges faced by the “missing middle.” The university’s unique strengths, such as its Ocean Sciences campus and community-based medical school, were noted as key differentiators.
Governance and Administration
Prof Nel assured the Committee that NMU’s governance structures are stable and well-functioning, with regular oversight provided by the Council, Senate, and executive management. She provided an update on student registrations for the 2025 academic year, reporting that over 17 200 firm offers had been made, with more than 11 280 acceptances. She also discussed the university’s efforts to support student success through various interventions, including coaching, peer-led programmes, and financial concessions for students facing economic challenges.
Student Success and Challenges
Prof Nel acknowledged the decline in student success rates from 2022 to 2023, attributing this to a range of factors that NMU is actively investigating. She emphasised the institution’s commitment to reversing this trend through targeted interventions and data-driven strategies.
Safety and Security
On the issue of safety and security, she outlined NMU’s comprehensive measures to protect students and staff, including partnerships with the South African Police Service and the installation of SOS towers for emergency responses. She also highlighted the university’s zero-tolerance policy towards gender-based violence and its efforts to support survivors through counselling, legal aid, and safe housing.
Infrastructure and Sustainability
Prof Nel expressed gratitude for the Department of Higher Education and Training’s investment in modernising NMU’s facilities. She detailed efforts to enhance digital capabilities, refurbish existing buildings, and promote sustainability through water and energy solutions. She also addressed the challenges of student accommodation, noting that while the university has increased on-campus beds, many students still rely on off-campus housing, which presents ongoing challenges.
Financial Sustainability
Prof Nel addressed NMU’s financial sustainability, highlighting its consistent unqualified audit outcomes and prudent financial management. She acknowledged the risks posed by a constrained economy and rising student debt but assured the Committee that the university is taking proactive measures to ensure long-term viability. These include optimising academic programmes, improving operational efficiencies, and mobilising resources creatively.
Industry Partnerships and Graduate Employability
Prof Nel highlighted NMU’s strong partnerships with industry, government, and civil society, which provide students with valuable work-integrated learning opportunities. She emphasised NMU’s commitment to producing graduates who are not only employable but also equipped to address societal challenges through innovative and ethical approaches.
She then handed over to the Deputy Chairperson to deliver the concluding remarks, thanking the Committee for the opportunity to present.
Closing remarks
Ms Mbaco expressed the university’s deep appreciation for the opportunity to present before the Portfolio Committee.
She emphasised that NMU remained acutely aware of its responsibility as the intellectual and academic expression of its iconic namesake. The university was committed to advancing a values-driven, humanising institutional culture that empowered students, staff, and communities to realise their full potential.
Ms Mbaco assured the Committee that NMU took cognisance of the Council’s role and valued feedback from stakeholders to ensure continuous improvement in its operations.

Presentation by the Institutional Forum: Nelson Mandela University
Remarks by the Vice-Chairperson of the Institutional Forum
Dr Ongama Mtimka, Vice-Chairperson: Institutional Forum (IF), Nelson Mandela University (NMU), outlined the key areas of focus, which included the higher education funding crisis, economic barriers to accessibility, and the Institutional Forum’s perspective on governance. He stated that the Forum operated in accordance with the Higher Education Act, the university’s institutional statutes and rules, and the Institutional Forum Constitution.
Dr Mtimka reiterated that the governance structure of NMU had been discussed in detail earlier but emphasised that the IF played an important advisory role to the University Council. While Council was responsible for the overall implementation of the Higher Education Act, as well as related policies and regulations, the IF served as an advisory body on governance, transformation, and institutional culture matters. He noted that the IF Constitution had been approved by Council in 2007 and reviewed over the years, with the most recent review taking place in 2018. Discussions were currently underway to conduct another review and update the document.
Composition and Functions of the Institutional Forum
Dr Mtimka detailed the composition of the IF, which included:
- Two members of Council who were not employees or students of the university.
- Two members of management.
- Two members of Senate.
- Two members representing academic employees.
- Two members representing non-academic employees.
- Two members from each recognised employee organisation.
- Two students designated by the Student Representative Council (SRC).
- One member representing the Alumni Association.
- One member appointed by the Equity Office.
He noted that the IF had remained compliant with these requirements and ensured that any vacancies were swiftly filled.
On its functions, he stated that the IF advised Council on issues including:
- Implementation of the Higher Education Act and national higher education policy.
- Race and gender equity policies.
- Selection of candidates for senior management positions.
- Codes of conduct, mediation, and dispute resolution procedures.
- Fostering an institutional culture that promoted transformation, social cohesion, and respect for human rights.
- The language policy of the university.
He explained that the IF structured its standard agenda in line with these key responsibilities while also allowing members to propose additional agenda items ahead of each meeting. He further detailed that the IF played a role in the appointment of senior management by participating in selection processes and subsequently voting on recommended candidates before Council made final appointments.
Readiness for 2025
Dr Mtimka reported that the IF’s first meeting of the year was scheduled for the following week, during which priorities would be established and aligned with Council directives. Key focus areas included strengthening governance, promoting equity, overseeing financial aid concerns, and addressing student accessibility challenges. The Forum remained committed to transparency and inclusive decision-making in accordance with the Higher Education Act.
He confirmed that the student registration numbers provided by management were accurate, as they were consistent with updates received through the university’s general communications. He welcomed measures such as increased shuttle capacity and emergency housing solutions to support students.
Higher Education Funding Crisis and Economic Barriers
Dr Mtimka highlighted the systemic financial challenges affecting NMU and the higher education sector broadly. He stated that while the economy was not generating sufficient employment to enable more families to afford higher education, public funding constraints had further exacerbated financial pressures on students. He echoed concerns raised in recent national discussions, emphasising the need for a collaborative response involving universities, government, and stakeholders to ensure the long-term sustainability of higher education.
He stressed that inadequate investment in education limited national development and constrained economic growth. The IF remained concerned about the increasing financial vulnerability of students, particularly those in the “missing middle” who did not qualify for full state support but lacked sufficient private means to fund their studies.
Response to Protests and Conflict Resolution
Dr Mtimka commended NMU’s leadership for its swift resolution of recent disruptions to the academic programme, noting that protests on 11 and 12 February 2025 had been addressed within 48 hours. He welcomed the university’s effective engagement with the SRC and student bodies, as well as its proactive approach to resolving financial aid and housing concerns.
Financial Sustainability and Long-Term Solutions
Dr Mtimka reiterated that NMU was operationally ready for the 2025 academic year but continued to face financial constraints. He stressed that the university required sustainable funding solutions to ensure uninterrupted academic operations. He commended NMU for maintaining financial stability, highlighting its strong cash backing and prudent management. He stated that in the event of a financial crisis, NMU’s financial reserves would sustain the institution for a limited period, reinforcing the urgency of long-term financial planning.
He concluded by stating that the IF fully supported ongoing discussions on reviewing the higher education funding model. He called for stronger collaboration between government and universities to address student financial aid challenges and ensure broader accessibility to higher education.
Dr Mtimka thanked the Chairperson and the Committee for their engagement, stating that the IF remained committed to supporting governance and transformation at NMU.

Presentation by NEHAWU
Remarks by NEHAWU
Representatives from the National Education, Health and Allied Workers' Union (NEHAWU) and the National Tertiary Education Union (NTEU) expressed appreciation for the opportunity to present before the Committee. They stated that while they were pleased to be invited, they were not entirely satisfied with the current arrangements, as different constituencies were represented in various capacities. Nevertheless, they acknowledged the significance of having the leadership of Nelson Mandela University (NMU) present for the first time in such an engagement.
They indicated that the presentation had been divided between the two unions to ensure fair representation within the allocated time. They noted that their submission was structured in direct response to the questions posed in the Committee’s letter of invitation.
State of Readiness for the 2025 Academic Year
The representatives stated that, from an organised labour perspective, the university’s readiness for the academic year had to be assessed in the broader context of challenges facing both students and staff. Through formal and informal engagements with student leadership, particularly the South African Students Congress (SASCO), they had identified the National Student Financial Aid Scheme (NSFAS) as a primary source of instability within the higher education sector. They argued that while academics, Deans, and the Deputy Vice-Chancellor for Learning and Teaching had adequately prepared for the academic year, disruptions largely stemmed from issues related to NSFAS funding.
They pointed out that the majority of students at NMU were NSFAS beneficiaries and that ongoing issues with the financial aid scheme had placed immense strain on students. They noted several key concerns, including:
- Students being only partially funded or provisionally approved, leading to uncertainty.
- Instances where students were de-funded mid-year, despite having received funding in the first semester.
- Payment inconsistencies, where students would receive funding for one semester but not the next, resulting in accumulated debt and registration challenges for the following year.
- The need for financial concessions to enable students to register despite funding shortfalls.
They asserted that these systemic issues fuelled unrest on campuses and needed urgent intervention at a national level.
Salary Negotiations and Employee Relations
The representatives highlighted delays in salary negotiations, which had become a recurring issue at NMU. They stated that negotiations now commenced later than stipulated in the Conditions of Service document, which required discussions to begin around the end of September each year. However, a proposal from the Chief Financial Officer (CFO) had resulted in negotiations being postponed until after the annual budget speech. As a result, salary negotiations were often only concluded as late as June or July, creating dissatisfaction among employees.
They further raised concerns regarding financial transparency, stating that NMU management routinely cited financial constraints when negotiating salaries. They noted that the last reported reserve value was R830 million, with a deficit also reported, which set the tone for salary discussions. However, they argued that the financial statements provided by management did not accurately reflect the true financial position of the university and called for greater transparency in financial reporting.
Student Debt and Financial Pressures
The representatives linked salary negotiations to the broader issue of student debt, stating that management frequently cited the need to allocate funds for student debt relief as a reason for budgetary constraints. While they acknowledged the importance of alleviating student debt, they contended that this should not come at the expense of fair and reasonable salary adjustments for staff. They urged the university to find a sustainable balance between financial aid and employee remuneration.
Governance and Council Representation
The representatives expressed concerns about the conduct of certain Council members, particularly in subcommittees where organised labour was permitted to engage. They reported instances where organised labour representatives were met with hostility and a lack of respect when voicing their views. They attributed this to the fact that organised labour did not have formal representation on Council, limiting its influence in decision-making processes.
They reiterated the need for greater inclusion of labour representatives in university governance structures, particularly in Council deliberations, to ensure that employee concerns were adequately considered in institutional decision-making.
Conclusion and Recommendations
The representatives concluded by stating that the allocated time had been insufficient to comprehensively address all the challenges faced by workers at NMU. They recommended:
- Urgent national-level intervention to resolve NSFAS funding issues, given their direct impact on student stability and university operations.
- Greater transparency in NMU’s financial reporting to ensure that salary negotiations were based on accurate financial data.
- The inclusion of organised labour representatives in the University Council to improve engagement on governance matters.
- Timely commencement of salary negotiations in line with the Conditions of Service document to prevent unnecessary delays.
They thanked the Chairperson and the Committee for the opportunity to present and reiterated their willingness to engage further on these critical matters.
Presentations by NTEU
Remarks by Organised Labour Representatives
The representatives from organised labour opted to forgo a formal introduction and indicated that their presentation would be split, with the Secretary following shortly. They began by presenting on staff readiness, focusing on line management, academic staff, and professional administrative support staff (PASS).
They reported that the academic and professional staff structures were well prepared for the start of the 2025 academic year. The Deputy Vice-Chancellor (DVC) for Learning and Teaching had developed multiple contingency plans to ensure a smooth start. The academic calendar was deliberately designed to allow for a gradual introduction to content in the first two weeks, providing new students with time to settle in. The management structure had also demonstrated agility in responding to challenges and implementing necessary adjustments.
They noted that the shift to hybrid and online learning, which had emerged during the COVID-19 pandemic, had allowed the university to maintain academic continuity. However, first-year students were particularly vulnerable to disruptions, as they often lacked the necessary digital skills, devices, and data to access online platforms. The longer the disruptions, the greater the impact on both students and lecturing staff, who had to ensure that no student was left behind.
PASS staff played a critical role in the operational implementation of applications, registration, student funding, and housing processes. These functions were managed through the Executive Management Committee’s (EMC) Student Access Committee, which fast-tracked solutions to identified bottlenecks. The representatives commended the efforts of PASS staff, particularly those in student governance, financial aid, student housing, and student finance, who operated under immense pressure at the start of the academic year.
They noted that while online registration had functioned efficiently, in-person assistance at a central venue from 3 to 7 February had been crucial in resolving outstanding issues.
Challenges Facing Staff
The representatives identified several challenges affecting staff:
- Protests as a New Normal:
They stated that student protest action had become a recurring feature at the beginning of each academic year, requiring staff to remain adaptable. Line managers had worked to ensure operational continuity, and some areas had demonstrated exceptional service delivery under difficult conditions—achievements that were not always acknowledged on social media.
- Conditions of Service and Salary Negotiations:
They reported that in 2024, management had given notice of its intention to terminate the current Conditions of Service agreement, leading to ongoing engagements. This uncertainty had negatively impacted salary negotiations. Subsidy reductions, student financial challenges, and throughput rates had constrained salary increases, with the 2024-2025 adjustment falling below the Consumer Price Index (CPI). They warned that NMU salaries were becoming uncompetitive.
- Staff Wellness and Workplace Pressures:
A 2023/24 institutional report had identified work pressure and stress as the primary contributors to staff wellness issues, followed by anxiety. The representatives raised concerns about staff members being targeted on social media, stating that online platforms had become hostile spaces where staff could not adequately defend themselves.
- Funding and Operational Pressures:
Staff had been forced to adapt to ever-changing circumstances, particularly in relation to NSFAS funding challenges. Many staff members provided direct support to students in need, often adopting struggling students and contributing to faculty food bank initiatives. The sight of long queues of students waiting for food parcels was deeply concerning and raised questions about how the sector had reached this point.
- Student Housing Crisis:
The representatives acknowledged concerns raised in the Committee’s press release of 11 February. They noted that the Minister of Higher Education and the Deputy Minister had personally intervened in student housing issues at NMU in 2024, including engagements with landlords over the impact of the NSFAS direct payment model. Disgruntled landlords had become increasingly unwilling to accommodate NSFAS-funded students, exacerbating the housing shortage.
- Exploring Online Learning for Certain Programmes:
The representatives suggested that postgraduate diplomas and advanced diplomas—programmes funded by NSFAS but ineligible for National Research Foundation (NRF) funding—be moved online. This could potentially address accommodation constraints for approximately 1,600 students.
Recommendations
Stability in Critical Leadership Positions
They welcomed the recent appointment of a Director of Student Governance and stressed the urgency of finalising the appointment of a Dean of Students to strengthen student engagement.
Stronger Engagement Between Organised Labour and Management
They called for improved dialogue with Employee Relations (ER), Human Resources (HR), and university leadership, stating that existing platforms should be better utilised to prevent disputes from escalating to the Commission for Conciliation, Mediation and Arbitration (CCMA) and Labour Court.
Enhanced Support for Professional Staff
They recommended aligning the professional staff response structure with the DVC for Learning and Teaching’s academic structure to improve institutional readiness.
Safe Spaces for First-Year Student Support
They called for designated safe spaces where staff could assist first-year students during disruptions, noting that power outages—not just student protests—also posed challenges to student engagement.
Investment in Digitalisation and Automation
They stated that while NMU had ambitious goals for digital enablement, financial constraints hindered progress. They called for funding to:
- Strengthen Information and Communication Technology (ICT) staffing.
- Upgrade and develop digital systems to alleviate workplace fatigue and improve efficiency.
Inclusion of Organised Labour in University Council
They acknowledged the sensitivity of the issue but argued that labour representatives needed a seat at Council to fully understand NMU’s financial challenges. They stated that union leadership had to communicate difficult financial realities to their members, and direct participation at Council level would ensure a more informed engagement process.
The representatives concluded by thanking the Chairperson and the Committee for the opportunity to present and reiterated their commitment to working towards institutional stability and staff well-being.

Presentation by the Student Representative Council: Nelson Mandela University
Remarks by the SRC President
Mr Lathitha Mbuzwana, President of the Nelson Mandela University (NMU) Student Representative Council (SRC), addressed the Committee, expressing deep gratitude for the opportunity to present on behalf of the student body. He stated that he was honoured and privileged to represent the voices of over 32 000 students, each with unique dreams, challenges, and aspirations.
He emphasised that he would not speak solely in his capacity as SRC President but as a student who had personally witnessed the immense potential within NMU’s student population, as well as the challenges they faced. He noted that his mandate was to provide a candid and comprehensive perspective on the realities experienced by students as they lived, studied, and navigated university life.
He structured his presentation around three key areas:
The readiness of NMU for the 2025 academic year from a student perspective.
The challenges that hinder student success.
Practical recommendations for improving student conditions within the broader context of South Africa’s higher education system.
He stressed the importance of continuously evaluating educational institutions, stating that the quality of education received today shaped the future of the country. He emphasised that universities must be equipped to meet the evolving demands of society, producing young leaders who could act as innovators and agents of hope. While NMU had demonstrated commendable agility in adapting to challenges, he cautioned that institutional readiness for the 2025 academic year could not be measured solely by a university’s ability to transition between different modes of learning. A thorough assessment was required, covering academic support, financial stability, mental health services, and campus infrastructure.
Financial exclusion and funding shortages
Mr Mbuzwana stated that financial exclusion remained the most significant barrier to education at NMU. While NSFAS had provided relief to students from disadvantaged backgrounds, the funding allocated was often insufficient to cover the full cost of university education. He specifically highlighted the lack of adequate financial support for Advanced Diploma students, many of whom struggled to secure accommodation due to funding constraints.
He expressed serious concerns regarding the NSFAS funding model, stating that the ongoing de-funding of students during the academic year was disruptive and unjustifiable. He described cases where students received funding in the first semester but were de-funded in the second semester, leaving them with outstanding debt and jeopardising their academic progress. He likened this process to providing food to a child and then abruptly withdrawing it, leading to dire consequences.
Student accommodation crisis
The SRC President highlighted the dire state of student accommodation at NMU, describing it as a significant source of distress. He attributed the worsening situation to the implementation of the NSFAS direct payment model, which shifted accommodation management responsibilities from the university to external service providers. While the change was intended to streamline access to housing, it had instead led to:
- A lack of accountability in accommodation management.
- Substandard living conditions for students.
- Financial mismanagement by service providers.
He called for urgent reforms, stating that the current system hindered student academic success and well-being.
Transport challenges and shuttle services
Mr Mbuzwana described shuttle services as a long-standing issue at NMU, with students repeatedly raising concerns over their inadequacy. He noted that NMU had experienced significant enrolment growth in recent years, placing increasing pressure on transport services. The current shuttle system was insufficient to meet student demand, particularly for those commuting between various campuses.
He cited a recent incident in which the first shuttle from City Hall in Gqeberha (Port Elizabeth) left behind 168 students, meaning they were unable to attend academic activities for the day. He stated that transport challenges contributed to low pass rates, poor graduation rates, and an increased number of students being forced to write supplementary examinations.

Discussion
Clarification on Labour Representation
The Chairperson thanked the President of the SRC for a well-articulated presentation and extended appreciation to all stakeholders who had participated in the meeting. He acknowledged the contributions made by university management, councils, Institutional Forums, organised labour, and the SRCs of both institutions.
He proceeded to clarify the approach taken in inviting organised labour to present. He acknowledged that unions operated in a competitive space and represented different mandates, which could make it difficult to consolidate their presentations. However, he emphasised that the Committee invited workers rather than specific unions, which was why a single presentation was expected. He cited the example of SRCs, where different political organisations often formed a single council but were required to present collectively as representatives of students. He reiterated that organised labour representatives were expected to do the same, setting aside internal differences to present issues affecting workers at large.
The Chairperson noted that he had been generous with time allocations, giving unions 10 minutes instead of the usual 15 minutes split among multiple unions. He pointed out that during a recent engagement at the University of the Witwatersrand (Wits), three unions had been required to share 15 minutes, demonstrating that time constraints were not unique to this meeting.
Concerns Over the NSFAS Accommodation Pilot Project
The Chairperson turned to the recurring issue of NSFAS accommodation, which had been raised by both universities. He acknowledged that the system was failing students and noted that this was not the first time the Committee had warned NSFAS about the mismanagement of student accommodation.
He highlighted a study that compared the success rates of students living in on-campus residences versus those staying at home, which found that students in formal accommodation performed significantly better. He illustrated the difficult living conditions faced by many students, citing cases where students came from overcrowded homes, sleeping in shared spaces without proper study environments. He stressed that student accommodation was not a luxury but a necessity for ensuring academic success.
He emphasised that government funding for student accommodation was intended to enhance both access and success in higher education. He cautioned against investing solely in access without addressing the throughput rate, which determined how many students successfully completed their studies.
The Chairperson recalled that during the sixth Parliament, NSFAS had consulted with Universities South Africa (USAf) before implementing the accommodation cap. He reminded the Committee that the initial decision for NSFAS to take a role in student accommodation was driven by widespread corruption at universities.
He detailed various fraudulent practices uncovered in student accommodation accreditation, including:
- Inflated Accommodation Costs – Universities accredited private providers who charged R3 500 per bed but billed NSFAS R5 500 per student, defrauding the state.
- Ghost Students – At some institutions, NSFAS had been paying for students who did not exist or did not reside in the accredited accommodation.
- Fraudulent Accommodation Claims – He cited an investigation where 51 privately owned residences were found to be claiming NSFAS payments for students who did not reside there.
- Uninhabitable Conditions – He referenced Letaba TVET College, where students were placed in shacks, yet NSFAS had been billed for approved accommodation.
The Chairperson reiterated that these were not isolated incidents, stating that corruption ran deep across institutions. He asserted that every university in South Africa had cases of mismanagement or fraud in student support services.
NSFAS Mismanagement of the Accreditation Process
He expressed frustration that while NSFAS had been tasked with taking over the accreditation process to prevent fraud, it had hired unethical individuals who manipulated the system for personal gain. He explained how some NSFAS-appointed officials had been bribed to falsely classify residences as Grade A, significantly inflating accommodation costs.
He emphasised that the current system—where NSFAS managed accommodation independently—was failing students. However, reverting
solely
to universities was also not a solution, given past corruption. He proposed a hybrid arrangement where:
- Universities managed accreditation and oversight.
- NSFAS conducted quality assurance to verify that accredited housing met required standards.
He stressed that a balance had to be found, ensuring that neither universities nor NSFAS operated without checks and balances.
Additional Issues Raised for Response
The Chairperson then invited Members to pose questions to the institutions. He noted that Nelson Mandela University’s presentation had not fully addressed an allegation that had emerged regarding racial disparities in salaries. He requested the Vice-Chancellor to respond to this matter.

However, the matter was under discussion, and a report was being prepared to determine how affected students could be assisted. Any decisions in this regard would require approval from the NSFAS executive.
Concerning students mistakenly categorised as new applicants, he explained that some students had reapplied for funding instead of allowing their existing applications to roll over, resulting in them being classified as first-time applicants rather than returning students. NSFAS had identified these cases and was in the process of correcting them by cancelling duplicate application records.
On appeals opened in December, he clarified that most of these appeals were for first-time applicants. Continuing students who required propensity letters from their faculties had only received their academic results in early January and were not affected by the December appeal process. He requested the SRC to provide the identity numbers of any students who believed they had been impacted so that NSFAS could investigate.
Regarding outstanding 2024 payments and mop-up processes, he confirmed that payments had been processed the previous Friday, and proof of payments had been shared with financial aid offices. However, some students had not received payments due to exceptions such as qualification mismatches, multiple registrations at different institutions, and exceeding the NSFAS funding limit. These cases were flagged for further resolution.
On late allowance payments, he stated that delays were no longer expected, as universities now received upfront funding to distribute allowances on time. The expectation was that students should receive their allowances by the first of each month. TVET colleges, however, still received direct payments from NSFAS.
Regarding access to the funded student list, he confirmed that institutions had been able to download the list since 7 January. However, delays occurred due to slow processing of academic results. To address this, NSFAS temporarily reverted to a manual system on 13 January before restoring the automated portal on 12 February. Institutions could now access the funded list at any time.
He concluded by affirming that he had addressed all the key issues but remained available to clarify any further concerns.
Student Communication and Accommodation Concerns
The Chairperson emphasised the need for immediate action to address communication barriers between students and university officials. He instructed representatives to ensure that student leaders were provided with the contact details of relevant officials. He stated that if students encountered difficulties in reaching designated individuals, the Committee should be informed in writing.
He announced a joint meeting with university representatives and private student accommodation providers would be held the following week. Initially scheduled for an earlier date, the meeting had been moved to Friday to accommodate other commitments. He expressed concern over persistent issues related to student accommodation and called for a resolution to prevent recurring challenges.

Legal Fees and Disciplinary Processes
Labour representatives alleged that the university spends excessive amounts on legal fees to discipline workers for minor breaches of the code of conduct while overlooking similar breaches by management
. A detailed response to these allegations was requested.
Code of Conduct and Governance
The Chairperson addressed the Committee, highlighting concerns about the enforcement of the code of conduct at universities. He noted that while management often disciplines workers for minor breaches, similar breaches by management are overlooked. He provided an example from the mining industry, where employees use a clocking system to track their working hours. A minor breach, such as leaving before 15:00 is recorded and addressed. However, he alleged that universities spend significant resources disciplining workers for such minor infractions, hiring independent investigators and legal counsel, while similar actions by management are ignored.
He expressed concern that workers do not believe these investigations are truly independent, as universities often bring in senior counsel to represent them at the Commission for Conciliation, Mediation, and Arbitration (CCMA), while workers either represent themselves or are represented by their unions. This, he argued, creates an imbalance and stifles fair debate.
Proposals and Resolutions
The Chairperson stated that the Committee would make proposals and resolutions to address these issues. He emphasised the need for the Director-General to assist in resolving discrepancies in student accommodation costs. He argued that university-owned residences, particularly those funded by the government, should not charge fees above the NSFAS cap.
He shared a personal anecdote about his time as a student in 2004, referencing the Pius Langa Residence at Howard College, University of KwaZulu-Natal (UKZN). He noted that even though the residence was built 21 years ago, the university claims it is expensive to maintain and charges fees above the NSFAS cap. He questioned why government-funded residences should charge above the cap, particularly older residences like John Hughes, which have been operational for many years.
Concerns About Governance and Accountability
The Chairperson expressed frustration with the lack of accountability in university governance. He criticised the UMP for failing to provide satisfactory responses during the meeting and accused them of treating the Committee with disrespect. He stated that public servants are paid to serve the public, yet some institutions appear before the Committee and provide misleading information.
He raised concerns about the one-year extension the university granted to fill key positions, arguing that it was not being used effectively. He noted that if the university were serious about recruitment, it would have initiated the process six months earlier, as stipulated by policy. Instead, delays were encountered, and the university now claimed no suitable candidates were found.
The Chairperson requested the minutes of the Human Resources Committee meetings to assess the UMP’s recruitment processes. He highlighted that two Deputy Vice-Chancellors were appointed nine months late, which he argued demonstrated a lack of urgency and institutional memory.
Role of Ministerial Appointees
The Chairperson emphasised the critical role of ministerial appointees to university councils. He stated that these appointees are responsible for ensuring good governance and representing the interests of the department. He criticised some appointees for failing to hold councils accountable, particularly when decisions were made that undermined the stability of institutions.
He called for a review of the Higher Education Act, arguing that the current legislation does not provide sufficient mechanisms to address governance failures. He proposed standardising institutional statutes across all 26 public universities, ensuring clear definitions of council composition, including representation from government, traditional leaders, and labour unions.
Allegations of Sexual Harassment and Governance Failures at UMP
The Chairperson raised concerns about allegations of sexual harassment at the Siyabuswa Campus of UMP. He noted that the university’s sexual harassment policy was not effectively enforced, and cases were often handled internally without transparency. He called for a more robust approach to addressing such allegations, ensuring that victims are protected, and perpetrators are held accountable.
He also criticised the university for failing to address the harmonisation of conditions of service for staff from the former the Lowveld College of Agriculture, who were incorporated into UMP in 2015. He noted that this matter had been outstanding for years and called for a resolution.
Closing Remarks
The Chairperson concluded by reiterating the importance of universities fulfilling their core mandate of producing skilled graduates to drive economic growth. He condemned the destruction of university property during strikes, emphasising that such actions diverted resources from improving staff and student conditions.
He called on the Director-General to assist in resolving the issues raised during the meeting and urged the Committee to develop concrete proposals to address governance failures and ensure accountability.
DHET Director-General’s closing remarks
Dr Sishi thanked the Chairperson and Members of the Portfolio Committee for their leadership, direction, and passion for education. He stated that the discussions had been productive and proceeded to address key matters directly to avoid any misinterpretation.
He highlighted the presence of two female Vice-Chancellors at the meeting, noting the significance of their leadership in higher education. He expressed concern about the manner in which the exit of one Vice-Chancellor was being handled, cautioning against any manipulation that could undermine her legacy. He urged Council Members to ensure a dignified transition, particularly in recognising the contributions of Prof Thoko Mayekiso, and stressed that his message in this regard should be taken seriously.
Concerns regarding governance at the University of Mpumalanga
Dr Sishi informed the Committee that an anonymous letter, dated 22 January 2024, had been received from a University of Mpumalanga (UMP) Council Member. The letter raised concerns regarding the possible extension of the Vice-Chancellor’s tenure, alleging that the UMP Statute was being violated despite opposition from some Council Members. Based on interactions during the meeting, he suspected that these allegations might have merit and requested that the university provide clear responses to the issues raised by the Chairperson.
He also referred to a letter from the Minister, dated 18 March 2024, addressed to the UMP Council Chairperson. The letter sought a response to the allegations and an assurance that the appointment process would adhere to good governance principles. He noted that the Department had intervened early upon becoming aware of these concerns and had consistently guided the university on governance matters. UMP Council responded on 17 April 2024, stating that legal opinion had been sought on the matter. However, he noted that the responses provided by the university during the meeting reinforced concerns about governance processes.
He outlined four key issues raised by the UMP Council Chairperson regarding the Vice-Chancellor appointment process. These included: (1) the Human Resources Committee (HRC) had commenced deliberations in early September; (2) by 14 March, the Executive Committee of Council had rejected the HRC’s submission, citing insufficient material information; (3) control over the appointment narrative had shifted from the HRC to Council itself; and (4) while the Department did not wish to interfere in university governance, intervention became necessary when governance failures arose.
He acknowledged concerns raised by the former Student Representative Council (SRC) regarding the reappointment of the Vice-Chancellor and a possible term extension. He stressed that these issues had been raised with the Department and the former Minister at an early stage and that all stakeholders, including the Department, bore some responsibility for any governance lapses.
On the issue of legal interventions, he noted concerns about the university’s reliance on legal processes when other avenues for resolution existed. He advised Council to reflect on whether these legal measures were necessary. He also addressed the issue of whether senior management could serve beyond the deferred retirement age of 70, highlighting that Clause 49 of the UMP Statute granted Council the authority to appoint an acting Vice-Chancellor but that such decisions should align with human resource management policies and sound legal advice.
Legal opinion and governance findings
Dr Sishi summarised the findings of the legal opinion obtained by the Department, which identified three key concerns:
- The rationale behind prioritising the reappointment of the Vice-Chancellor over other qualified senior officials within the institution required further examination. The process should not undermine other capable candidates.
- The appointment of the Vice-Chancellor for a fixed 12-month term raised concerns, as legal advice suggested that provisions had been made for an additional 12-month extension if no suitable candidate was found within the initial period.
- The need to preserve the institution’s integrity and legacy was paramount. He urged Council to consider alternative ways to retain the Vice-Chancellor’s expertise, such as appointing her as a consultant to support the leadership transition.
He stressed that UMP was a product of South Africa’s democratic era and that its governance must reflect the highest standards. He appealed to the Council Chairperson to work towards stabilising the institution rather than engaging in disputes that could damage its reputation.
Concerns raised by former Chief Justice and Chancellor
Dr Sishi noted that the former UMP Chancellor, Chief Justice Mandisa Maya, had written to the former Minister requesting intervention on allegations of poor governance. He cautioned that governance failures, if not addressed, could harm the university’s reputation both nationally and internationally. He warned that negative media exposure could impact the institution’s global ranking and credibility.
He called for urgent action on allegations of fraudulent competition, qualification misrepresentation, bullying, sexual misconduct, financial mismanagement, breaches of the Immigration Act, unfair dismissals, and the external sharing of privileged information. He stressed that these were serious allegations that required immediate attention.
He supported the release of an investigative report on these governance failures, arguing that transparency was necessary to restore confidence in the institution. He reiterated that all stakeholders needed to work together to address governance challenges and move the university forward.
Broader higher education concerns
Turning to Nelson Mandela University, Dr Sishi stated that while he would not go into as much detail, he had observed a need for greater focus on the university’s long-term vision. He noted that universities should be driven by a clear strategic direction beyond addressing immediate operational challenges. He called on institutions to refine their visions to ensure they remained relevant and inspiring for future generations.
He commended Nelson Mandela University for its commitment to research, innovation, and sustainability, particularly in areas such as transdisciplinary studies, digitisation, student access and success, Ocean Sciences, and food security. However, he urged the university to further refine its vision to ensure its impact extended beyond its current challenges.
Dr Sishi concluded by acknowledging the leadership of the Chairperson and Members of Parliament, thanking them for their wisdom and guidance. He reaffirmed the Department’s commitment to addressing governance challenges and improving the higher education system. While acknowledging areas that required improvement, he expressed confidence that the sector was making progress and pledged to continue working towards a stronger and more stable higher education system.
Chairperson’s remarks on UMP’s council conduct and ethical standards
The Chairperson stated that the meeting had reached its conclusion and reiterated concerns regarding the actions of the University Council. He asserted that the Council was attempting to undermine a decade of progress with an unnecessary and unjustified shift in approach. He welcomed the legal opinion from the department, which confirmed that the Council had no intention of making an appointment and had instead requested an additional year. He maintained that this legal interpretation was accurate and highlighted that the Council had advertised the position in September but continued to delay the process, with interviews now postponed until June.
He criticised the rationale provided for the delay, arguing that the Council had the option to begin the process earlier. He noted that the institution’s policy only set the latest possible start date for recruitment, which did not prevent an earlier commencement. He pointed out that the institution’s HR department had recommended testing candidates and requested meeting minutes to verify this recommendation. He alleged that Council Members had prior knowledge of discussions regarding an extension but had been evasive when questioned.
The Chairperson referenced a letter from a Council Member to the department, dated 24 January 2024, which indicated that an extension was being considered. He criticised the Council’s conduct, stating that it was unacceptable for individuals in high office to display unethical behaviour. He condemned what he described as repeated dishonesty from the Council. He warned that allowing such conduct to persist would set a dangerous precedent, fostering a culture where unethical behaviour was normalised.
He urged Members to reflect on the findings of the State Capture Report, particularly regarding Parliament’s role in upholding ethical governance. He warned against ignoring clear evidence of misconduct and stated that some individuals in leadership positions exploited their roles for personal gain. He expressed concern about unethical practices within institutions, including attempts to improperly influence officials.
The Chairperson called for decisive action to address these issues, stressing that the Higher Education Act must be enforced to prevent impunity. He instructed that both universities provide written responses by 5 March 2025. He confirmed that the committee had finalised the summons process for another university that had refused to cooperate with Parliament. He noted that the process was costly but necessary to uphold accountability.
He cautioned officials against providing misleading responses, stating that the committee was already in possession of key documents and merely sought confirmation. He urged Council Members to review their own statements, warning that their inconsistent responses would be evident upon scrutiny. He criticised the Council Chairperson for failing to understand the institution’s policies and for not providing clear guidance to other Members.
The Chairperson welcomed the Minister’s decision to require ministerial appointees to apply through the Government Gazette and hold a minimum qualification of a National Qualifications Framework (NQF) Level 7 degree. However, he expressed concern that ethical behaviour could not be determined solely by academic qualifications. He proposed including ethical vetting in the appointment process, including provisions for lifestyle audits without prior notice for Vice-Chancellors and Council Chairpersons. He argued that unethical conduct, rather than a lack of competence, was the primary issue in institutional governance.
He commended the institution for maintaining ten years of clean audits and acknowledged its transformation efforts. However, he requested a comprehensive response from NMU regarding demographic representation in senior management. He stated that the UMP Council’s evasiveness had delayed discussions on the Registrar’s appointment process, which followed a similar trajectory.
The Chairperson instructed UMP to submit a detailed report by 5 March 2025, outlining the progress of each appointment process, expected completion dates, and realistic milestones. He emphasised the need for a structured project management approach rather than arbitrary deadlines. He further requested copies of the advertisements for the Vice-Chancellor and Registrar positions to verify compliance with policy requirements.
Mr Sauls also requested that the institution submit its employment equity plan and actual demographic data for cross-referencing purposes.
The Chairperson thanked attendees and reiterated the request for submissions from both institutions. He wished Members safe travels, noting that the University of Mpumalanga was nearby, while Nelson Mandela University was farther away. He stated that Members would reconvene at the next meeting.
Mr Sauls remarked that even President Cyril Ramaphosa might not withstand a lifestyle audit.
The meeting was adjourned.
