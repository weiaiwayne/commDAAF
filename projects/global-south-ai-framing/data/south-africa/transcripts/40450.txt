Title: PC Science: DSTI on Briefing on Programme 3: International Cooperation Agreements & Science Diplomacy Capital for Africa Initiative
Date: 25 Mar 2025
Committee: 
URL: https://pmg.org.za/committee-meeting/40450/

Meeting Summary
Video
The Portfolio Committee on Science, Technology and Innovation engaged with the Department of Science, Technology and Innovation (DSTI) for a briefing on programme three of the Department. The programme covers the international cooperation work of the Department. The DSTI presented its strategic plans, budget allocations, and progress in implementing research and innovation initiatives aimed at advancing science and technology in priority sectors. The presentation covered ongoing projects, including support for emerging researchers, investment in innovation hubs, and collaboration with international partners to enhance scientific capabilities.
Members raised concerns about funding constraints and their impact on research and development, particularly in critical areas requiring long-term investment. Questions were asked about the alignment of the DSTI’s programmes with national priorities, the effectiveness of existing policies, and the monitoring and evaluation mechanisms in place. Concerns were also raised regarding interdepartmental coordination and stakeholder engagement, with Members emphasising the need for stronger collaboration across government and private sector entities to maximise impact.
Meeting report
Opening
Ms Shanaaz Isaacs, Committee Secretary, conveyed apologies on behalf of Minister Blade Nzimande, who was attending an official business trip abroad. Apologies had also been extended by Mr Daan du Toit, the Deputy Director General (DDG) responsible for the International Cooperation and Resources Programme, who was away on official duties in Switzerland, and the Deputy Minister, who had prior commitments with schools in KwaZulu-Natal (KZN).
Briefing by the DSTI on Programme 3: International Cooperation and Resources on the full specifics of South Africa's active STI cooperation agreements, as well as the Science Diplomacy Capital for Africa initiative
Ms Gugulethu Zwane, Acting Director-General (ADG), Department of Science, Technology and Innovation (DSTI), began by highlighting the Department’s work under Component Three, focusing on science diplomacy and strengthening historically disadvantaged institutions through international partnerships. She then introduced Ms Vinny Pillay, Chief Director: International Resources, DSTI, who led the presentation.
SA's active STI cooperation agreements, and Science Diplomacy Capital for Africa initiative
Ms Pillay opened the presentation by providing context on international cooperation and its role in advancing national and regional interests She discussed the global and regional dynamics shaping partnerships, emphasising the need for strategic engagement to strengthen political and economic ties. She highlighted how diplomacy and trade agreements contribute to economic development and how Africa could position itself more effectively in global decision-making forums. She also addressed key challenges in international relations, such as geopolitical shifts, economic pressures, and the evolving role of multilateral institutions. Stressing the importance of aligning cooperation with national policy priorities, she outlined critical policy frameworks guiding South Africa’s foreign relations and regional integration strategies. This set the stage for discussion on international agreements, regional collaborations, and multilateral engagements.
Bilateral cooperation and BRICS engagements
Ms Anneline Morgan, Chief Directorate: Overseas Bilateral Cooperation, DSTI, focused on bilateral agreements with countries outside Africa, including the Americas, Asia, Europe, and the Middle East. She emphasised their role in strengthening diplomacy, expanding trade, and fostering economic growth. Key areas of collaboration included investment, trade facilitation, capacity-building, and policy exchanges. Specific initiatives, such as the economic and technical cooperation partnerships with global partners, were highlighted, including the Finland partnership with the Water Research Commission (WRC). She also provided an overview of the BRICS steering committee meetings and engagements within the BRICS alliance, which focus on economic cooperation, political dialogue, and South-South collaboration. Recent discussions included investment frameworks, trade liberalisation, and agreements in energy, technology and finance. These partnerships help South Africa influence global economic policies and leverage BRICS engagements for national and regional development.
Regional and multilateral engagements
Ms Mmampei Chaba, Chief Directorate: Multilateral Cooperation & Africa, DSTI, discussed South Africa’s collaborations within the African continent and its participation in global multilateral platforms. She highlighted partnerships with African nations and regional organisations aimed at economic integration, infrastructure development, and policy alignment. Key engagements included South Africa’s role in the African Union (AU), the Southern African Development Community (SADC), and the African Continental Free Trade Area (AfCFTA), all of which enhance regional trade and self-reliance. Beyond Africa, she outlined South Africa’s role in global governance, peacekeeping, and policy discussions in multilateral platforms such as the United Nations. Contributions to UN peacekeeping, climate action, and advocacy for developing nations in economic forums were emphasised, reinforcing the country’s efforts to shape international frameworks on climate change, security, and development.
Ms Pillay concluded by stressing the need for stronger regional collaboration, given international partners’ growing interest and shifting funding priorities. She highlighted engagement with non-traditional partners, including philanthropic organisations, and emphasised the use of bilateral agreements to reinforce multilateral partnerships, particularly within BRICS and the European Union (EU).
Ms Zwane closed by referring to a recent discussion with the Minister on the Department’s five-year strategic plan. She reiterated the importance of positioning South Africa within the global context and strengthening relationships to maximise impact for the country and the continent.

Discussion
Dr S Thembekwayo (EFF) raised concerns over senior positions in the Department being filled on an acting basis, stressing that key roles like the Director and Deputy Director-General should be permanent, and sought clarification on the persistence of acting positions. Regarding the Finland partnership with the Water Research Commission (WRC) and the Department of Water and Sanitation (DWS), she asked whether any research findings could help South Africa address its water and sanitation challenges, citing the Hammarsdale saga as an example. She also raised concerns about the sustainability of the Department's partnerships with the United States, particularly regarding vaccine collaborations, given recent reports suggesting South Africa had been excluded from certain U.S. agreements. She sought assurance on the long-term viability of these partnerships.
Mr T Mjadu (MKP) highlighted South Africa's high unemployment rate and the challenges skilled graduates face in securing employment, noting that the Department allocates substantial funding to STI collaborations, student exchange programmes, and technology transfer agreements with foreign partners. He questioned the effectiveness of these investments in creating local job opportunities, with the risk that they might contribute to the brain drain, where students gain expertise abroad but return without clear pathways to apply their skills locally. The importance of specialised fields like nanotechnology, artificial intelligence (AI), and bioengineering was emphasised, along with the need for policies that integrate returning experts into the local economy. She also asked how the Department justifies its STI funding allocations to ensure they lead to a tangible impact on South Africa's long-term workforce sustainability, and do not function as a money laundering scheme disguised as development.
Mr V Nkosi (ANC) raised concerns about how South Africa would ensure that international partnerships do not lead to overdependence on foreign funding and resources, potentially compromising local innovation capabilities. Clarification was sought on the specific metrics that would be used to evaluate the impact of international cooperation on enhancing local research capabilities and infrastructure. Lastly, a question was raised about how the outlined strategies for promoting diversity, equity, and inclusion would be operationalised to ensure meaningful benefits for historically disadvantaged institutions and communities.
Mr T Ramongalo (DA) raised concerns about the numerous programmes initiated with China, acknowledging their value but requesting copies of the signed memorandums of understanding (MOUs) and agreements for proper oversight. Aligning with Dr. Thembekwayo’s concerns, he questioned the implications of strained US-South Africa relations, particularly regarding its potential impact on STI funding. He sought clarification on whether executive orders issued by U.S. President Donald Trump had any bearing on bilateral relations, especially in the areas of health and vaccines, with specific reference to the President’s Emergency Plan for AIDS Relief (PEPFAR) and the possible effects of funding shortfalls.
He also asked about the Department’s approach to prioritising STI funding, with concerns voiced that government departments often make promises without concrete results. Clarification was requested on whether any quantifiable progress had been made regarding this priority. Finally, the G20 Summit was noted, emphasising the importance of the Portfolio Committee's active involvement. He asked whether the Committee had considered how it might contribute to the summit and the preparations leading up to it. He suggested finding ways to involve the Committee in showcasing South Africa's diverse culture and fostering international collaboration.
Dr W Boshoff (FF+) addressed the term "scientific diplomacy," highlighting it as one of the Department's strengths, owing to South Africa's extensive international relationships. While appreciating the session’s details on scientific relations, he pointed out that in diplomacy, words often reflected attitude rather than reality. "Cooperation" was given as an example, explaining that the term did not always imply mutual collaboration -- it could also mean giving or receiving. He requested the Department to provide an approximate breakdown of how many of South Africa’s international relations were primarily about receiving, giving, or equal cooperation. He inquired how many relationships, if all international scientific relations were to end suddenly, would put South Africa at a disadvantage, resulting in savings, or be equally detrimental to both parties. He acknowledged that exact numbers would be difficult to provide, but requested a rough estimate.
Ms T Mchunu (ANC) commended the Department for ensuring women occupy leading positions, noting that all the presenters were women. She asked about the Department’s contingency plans to address potential shifts in global funding priorities that could affect South Africa's international collaborations, and how the Department could ensure that the benefits of science diplomacy initiatives were distributed equitably across various sectors of the South African community. She inquired about whether an investigation or programme could be investigated to link poverty alleviation projects with initiatives in the Department's Programme Three, particularly those involving collaboration with science students in higher education institutions, including universities and technical and vocational education and training (TVET) colleges.
She expressed interest in the Department’s initiatives related to the mental health of young boys, specifically in relation to the African Union (AU) programmes. She requested more detailed information going forward and further engagement on this critical issue. She suggested that the Department take a more active role in the G20 programmes, proposing that the Chairperson facilitate the initiative through the office of the Speaker, using programmes like the P20 programmes, so that this Committee could be involved along with the other portfolio committees. The signing of agreements with other countries, which began in 2023, had been addressed, and she emphasised the importance of moving forward with these agreements to respond to current global matters.
Lastly, Ms Mchunu recommended that the Committee initiate joint sittings with other government departments, such as the Departments of Health, Women, Youth and Disabilities, and Higher Education. She stressed the importance of discussing these issues collaboratively, particularly in areas such as vaccine manufacturing, with the aim of achieving tangible outcomes within South Africa
DSTI's response
Acting DG Zwane provided clarification regarding the confusion raised by Dr Thembekwayo about acting positions, stating that all senior positions were currently filled. The term "acting" was used because DDG Du Toit was travelling, and Ms Pillay was temporarily representing him in his absence. She emphasised that all senior positions were permanently filled, including those presented in the last slides. Any acting positions were minimal -- possibly one or two -- and the Department was in the process of addressing them in line with the Department of Public Service and Administration's (DPSA's) requirements, ensuring that the positions were either funded or deemed critical to be filled. She reassured Committee that within International Cooperation and Resources (ICR), all relevant positions were occupied, and the unit was functioning as expected.
Ms Zwane welcomed Ms Mchunu’s suggestion on joint sittings. She drew attention to the initiative aimed at shaping young men's attitudes, which had been launched in collaboration with the Department of Higher Education during the previous administration. However, momentum had been lost on certain projects, particularly in terms of collaboration. The importance of keeping such initiatives active, expanding them, and ensuring their continued growth as a critical area of engagement, was emphasised. While joint efforts had been more seamless when the ministry was combined, the Department was willing to engage with other Committee Members to encourage partnerships with relevant departments and sustain the initiative’s progress.
Ms Pillay addressed the questions regarding the U.S. and international partnerships. She acknowledged the uncertainty in the current global environment and the potential risk of reduced funding from international partners like the U.S. However, she highlighted this as an opportunity to explore internal funding sources, not only within the DSTI, but also through collaboration with other government departments to establish a coordinated budget in line with the decadal plan and policy. Given that STI was a cross-cutting element for implementation, she stressed the importance of strengthening internal systems by working with government departments and entities. The possibility of engaging with non-traditional partners, such as philanthropic organisations and foundations was mentioned, though the risk of external reliance remained a concern. Building internal capacity in collaboration with government entities was emphasised as a crucial step in mitigating potential funding reductions. Ms Morgan would be the appropriate person to provide further details on matters related to the U.S., given her close involvement with that portfolio.
Ms Pillay also responded to concerns about skills retention and the brain drain, referring to the Presidential PhD initiative announced by President Ramaphosa. The initiative aimed to train PhD candidates locally, reducing dependency on international training opportunities, and was being implemented in close partnership with the Department of Higher Education and the National Research Foundation (NRF). The importance of interdepartmental collaboration was reiterated, particularly through mechanisms such as the ministerial committee and the STI plenary, which could assist in addressing various challenges.
She addressed the question about the impact matrix, noting that there was room for improvement in developing a set of indicators to measure the impact. These indicators could cover various aspects such as students, publications, and the reach of initiatives to municipalities. She explained that in their international work, the Department collaborates with a range of partners within the local ecosystem, including entities and sector budget support. Additionally, they were working with some municipalities to develop and pilot systems, which could be an area for growth at the local government level to assess the actual impact.
Before concluding, Ms Pillay mentioned that copies of the MOUs with China could be shared, and handed over to her colleagues to address the matters related to the U.S. and the G20.
Ms Morgan responded to concerns about USA cooperation, particularly regarding the sustainability of the partnership and recent executive orders from the U.S. which had impacted funding to South Africa. She said U.S. funding was primarily managed through agencies like the National Institute for Health (NIH), which funds the majority of health research, including in the pharmaceutical sector, along with some contributions from USAID, such as PEPFAR funding. However, the largest impact would be felt by the Department of Health, particularly in the area of clinical trials managed by the Medical Research Council (MRC).
Ms Morgan said discussions with the National Research Foundation (NRF) had taken place regarding DSTI funding instruments, and they were currently finalising an internal assessment of the current funding partnerships with the U.S. Once completed, the information would be shared with the Portfolio Committee. The NRF's findings thus far indicated that the impact on the DST side would be minimal, as much of the funding was co-funded. In the event that projects were halted, additional funding would need to be secured to cover the shortfall for ongoing projects. She elaborated that most of the projects funded by the U.S. were through multilateral consortiums involving multiple countries applying for competitive funding from the NIH and the National Science Foundation (NSF). The most significant impact, however, would be on the health side, particularly in relation to clinical trials conducted under the MRC and managed by the Department of Health. She said discussions with leading scientists, such as Professor Salim Abdool Karim, confirmed that the biggest effects would be felt on the clinical research side.
Lastly, she reflected that the key lesson learned from the current U.S. situation was the need to find innovative models and instruments for collaboration, particularly in funding research. She emphasised the importance of involving non-traditional partners in these consortiums, especially from the private sector and philanthropy. She said research funding had largely been driven by the public sector, and suggested that it was now time to engage private sector partners to support research, ultimately benefiting the country.
Ms Chaba addressed several issues that had been raised during the discussion. Regarding water infrastructure, she said that one of the New Partnership for Africa’s Development's (NEPAD's) flagship initiatives, the Southern African Network of Water Centres of Excellence (SANWATCE), consisted of 11 centres across the region conducting research on topics like water extension, infrastructure maintenance, and resource management. She highlighted an inaugural water infrastructure course held in northern KwaZulu-Natal just before the COVID-19 pandemic, where local engineers and municipal representatives had participated to address challenges in water infrastructure maintenance. A similar course was conducted at Victoria Falls two years ago, and it was successful as well. Looking ahead, she noted that the Department planned to increase funding in the upcoming financial year to support additional municipalities in need of this type of training, with Hammarsdale potentially being included as one of the sites to benefit from such training.
On the topic of diversity, equity, accessibility and inclusivity, Ms Chaba emphasised that these principles were a priority across all G20 working groups, including the research and innovation working group, which was led by the DSTI. Although Agrément South Africa (ASA) had objected to including this priority, it would not hinder the planned activities in this area. She said some initiatives under the G20 included collaborating with the United Nations Educational, Scientific and Cultural Organisation (UNESCO) to ensure that all member countries incorporate strong elements of diversity, accessibility and inclusivity in their STI policies. UNESCO would monitor these policies annually, scrutinising each country's approach to implementing diversity and inclusivity in their STI strategies. She said a framework would be adopted at the end of South Africa's G20 presidency to further promote inclusivity in STI policies. At the DSTI level, the recently appointed gender focal point would ensure that the focus on women in science went beyond discussions to include concrete actions, such as budgeting, policy development, monitoring, evaluation and auditing, to ensure inclusivity throughout the STI sector, from research to the uptake of research results.
Regarding the request for the inclusion of the Portfolio Committee within the G20 summit, Ms Chaba acknowledged that this could be readily welcomed and acted upon. She mentioned that youth and other stakeholders had been invited to the first Research and Innovation Working Group (RIWG) meeting held in the Free State, and there was no reason why Committee members could not be included in the upcoming RIWG meeting at Mpumalanga University on 25 May. She added that 15 African countries, which were a priority for South Africa, would be invited. Invitations to the Portfolio Committee Members could be sent as early as next week, alongside invitations for all other STI-related G20 engagements.
Ms Chaba's concluding comment related to UNESCO's mental health programme for young boys at universities. She explained that funds were in the process of being transferred to the Human Sciences Research Council (HSRC) for the programme's implementation among all historically disadvantaged individuals (HDIs), with plans to roll it out in the coming financial year.
The Chairperson thanked DSTI for their presentation and time, and requested that the ADG and their team leave the platform so that the Members could remain to address Committee matters.
Committee matters
Committee minutes
The Committee adopted the minutes of its meetings of 26 November 2024, 11 February, 18 February and 11 March 2025 without amendments.
Committee reports
Ms Isaacs advised that the reports on the 2nd and 3rd quarters still needed further work, and that the oversight report was still undergoing editing. She requested the Chairperson and the Committee’s permission to have the report adopted next week or the following week, once completed. The Chairperson approved the request
Amended programme and Member consultation
The Chairperson raised concerns regarding issues that had surfaced among Members on social media regarding recent changes to the Committee programme. She requested that these matters be addressed to ensure clarity on the adjustments made and the future handling of similar issues.
Ms Isaacs acknowledged Members' dissatisfaction with the consultation process for programme modifications, explaining that such changes were often unavoidable due to shifts in the broader parliamentary schedule. As a result, the Committee was unable to formally consult on the adjustments. However, she assured Members that no agenda items had been removed but were instead rescheduled. She further explained that last-minute changes were first discussed with the Chairperson, often through a management committee. When immediate decisions were required, the Chairperson's guidance was sought, and Members were informed through designated WhatsApp and email groups.
Two specific changes were highlighted -- programme modifications for the current and following week. The shift was necessary to accommodate an invitation from the Joint Committee on Communications, requiring a swap in scheduled briefings. While this decision ensured programme continuity, some Members were dissatisfied with the lack of prior consultation. Ms Isaacs also clarified that the removal of an item related to the Technology Innovation Agency (TIA) was due to the need for a more comprehensive governance discussion.
The Chairperson advised that before proceeding with discussions on innovation programmes, a formal briefing from the Minister was needed to clarify governance issues within TIA. Consequently, the matter was postponed to the next term.
Dr Thembekwayo suggested using the WhatsApp group as the primary platform for urgent consultations and decision-making, as Members frequently checked messages there. This would facilitate quicker communication and consensus-building.
The Chairperson acknowledged the concerns raised and agreed that all communication regarding programme changes should be conveyed through the WhatsApp group when formal Committee meetings are not feasible. The Secretariat would be responsible for ensuring timely updates. Members were encouraged to provide feedback and corrections as needed to maintain transparency and inclusivity in decision-making. With no further objections, the matter was considered resolved.
Request for directive on the international study tour
Ms Isaacs requested a formal directive on the international study tour, seeking clarity on the tour’s timing and the countries involved, which were identified as having strong relationships. She emphasised the need to formalise the decision so that a minute could be attached to the application. She asked whether the tour would take place this year and, if so, whether it would occur in June, September, or December.
In response, the Chairperson suggested that the issue be addressed in the next portfolio discussion, following the scheduled business of the Portfolio Committee. This would allow for preparation and ensure a productive meeting where agreement could be reached.
Ms Isaacs expressed concern about next week's joint meeting with the Department of Communications and Digital Technologies (DCDT), which had invited the Committee to attend a physical meeting.
Chairperson acknowledged the concern, and assured her that the Committee would have their session to address the matter.
The meeting was adjourned.
