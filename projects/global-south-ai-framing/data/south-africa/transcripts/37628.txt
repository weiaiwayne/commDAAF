Meeting Summary

SA Health Products Regulatory Authority
The Portfolio Committee on Health met virtually for an analysis of the health sector’s 2022/23 annual reports by the Financial and Fiscal Commission (FFC) and the Auditor-General of South Africa (AGSA). The South African Health Products  Regulatory Authority (SAHPRA) presented its 2022/23 Annual Report.
Members raised a myriad of concerns relating to how the FFC foresaw the fiscal consolidation measures impacting government’s National Health Insurance (NHI) plans, given that it was not known exactly how it would be financed or how much it would cost as the Minister of Finance had repeatedly said it was unaffordable. Members also wanted to know how the reduction of personnel would be balanced with the severe strain already experienced with service delivery by the public health system and were concerned about the plans to mitigate hospitals running out of money.
Members asked about consequence management for poor performing entities and were concerned that many were not showing any signs of improvement. Members were worried about medico-legal claims which related to fraud and corruption with medical staff, doctors and lawyers who colluded with one another and amounted to R2 billion. Members also wanted to know how the growing backlog of toxicology cases would be dealt with and were worried that the quality of healthcare would be impacted by some healthcare goods and services constrained by budget cuts. Members suggested that hospitals be restored as opposed to building new ones such as the Kempton Park Hospital and highlighted the appalling conditions of hospitals in the Eastern Cape.
Members were impressed by AGSA’s presentation and asked how budgetary and accountability measures could be implemented to cap increasing expenses on medical claims. However, they were concerned about the lack of improvement in the audit outcomes for the Eastern Cape, the Free State and the Northern Cape and wanted the province to be placed under administration as the Northern Cape required urgent intervention given that irregular expenditure had been reported for four consecutive years.
The Committee suggested that workshops be held with provinces as only 59% of facilities met the ideal clinic standards.
SAHPRA indicated to the Committee that there was huge interest in the African region in local manufacturing, vaccine manufacturing and pharmaceutical manufacturing which would be hugely competitive between countries and South Africa needed to ensure that it has a good regulatory authority. The Committee also heard from SAHPRA that joining the African Medicines Agency (AMA) Treaty was important.
Members expressed their disappointment that SAHPRA received an unqualified audit with findings due to material and misstatements in relation to the percentage of medicine variation application backlogs and record keeping. Members questioned why the entity’s R22,5 million surplus could not be used to address personnel shortages and asked why only 57% of generic medicine was finalised within 250 working days. SAHPRA indicated that having many generic medications was not always beneficial, as the price advantage was lost when there were more than seven generics. Members also pointed out that generic medicine was concerning in relation to what medical aids were willing to cover.
The Committee also wanted to know the severity of litigation cases the entity was faced with.

Context
South Africa's public healthcare system has improved since 1994, but the Covid-19 pandemic worsened health outcomes. The system is strained due to a high disease burden, staff shortages, service backlogs, and a growing uninsured population
In 2022, around 51.5 million South Africans lacked medical aid, relying on public healthcare. This number is expected to rise, putting more pressure on the overburdened system. Infrastructure investment is vital to address backlogs, increase capacity, and improve service delivery
Due to rising public debt, government is implementing fiscal consolidation measures to reduce spending and manage debt. As a result, the National Health budget (inclusive of conditional grants) is expected to decrease from R64.5 billion in 2022/23 to R60.1 billion in 2023/24
The decline in funding raises concerns about the quality of public healthcare, considering the existing backlogs exacerbated by Covid-19
Historically, budget cuts and increasing costs have led to restrictions on medical personnel and reduced investment in healthcare infrastructure and maintenance
Debt costs are increasing and crowding expenditure on important social services like healthcare. Health spending has been decreasing and is expected to continue declining
The initial increase in the health budget was due to the pandemic, but it has since been decreasing. It will temporarily go into negative growth before improving again
SA spends relatively the same proportion of GDP on Healthcare as advanced economies, e.g. Canada and UK
SA Healthcare expenditure is essentially above average for a developing economy
There is a weak correlation between healthcare service spending and health outcomes
Healthcare spending and delivery are highly unequal because of the separating equilibrium of the South African healthcare system and financing
Statistics South Africa in 2021 estimated HIV prevalence rate to be approximately 13.7% of the population – about 8.2 million
This high rate of infection has financial implications including the rollout of ARVs
Inequity in healthcare resources – requiring an acceleration and speedy implementation of health reforms (implementation of the National Health Insurance)
The private sector, which has more resources, provides health services to about 15-16% of the population, while the public sector, with less resources, is responsible for the remainder of the population
The Commission has an ongoing research project aimed at evaluating progress on NHI pilots
South Africa has seen a 55% decrease in under-five mortality rate, indicating improved overall health. However, the rate is still higher compared to similar economically developed countries
Increased health spending correlates with better health outcomes, but factors like the introduction of ARVs have also contributed to the decline
ARV coverage for people with HIV has increased from 3% in 2005 to 73% in 2020
Life expectancy has risen from 56 years in 2000 to 65 years in 2020, but it is still below the average for other countries with similar economic development
Deaths from communicable diseases, maternal causes, and nutritional conditions have decreased since 2005, indicating improved health
However, deaths from non-communicable diseases like cardiovascular diseases, cancers, diabetes, and chronic respiratory diseases have been increasing, putting strain on the healthcare system
Around 10.1 million South Africans have chronic health conditions
Budget cuts in the public healthcare sector are worrying, leading to fewer resources and less capacity to meet growing healthcare demands
A high burden of both communicable and high non-communicable diseases – which require dedicated targeted preventative and preventative programmes
Cancer is ranked second with respect to major causes of death in the world after cardiovascular-related diseases – in 2019 contributed to over 10 million deaths
There is a steady increase in the number of people diagnosed with cancer in SA
Medical-legal claim payments have increased from R265 million in 2012/13 to almost R2 billion in 2018/19
Medical-legal payments account for approximately 1% of health expenditure for the period 2018/19 to 2020/21, with the highest share being in the Eastern Cape at approximately 3%
Since 2018/19, contingent liabilities stand at more than 50% of the Provincial Health Budget
Due to the seriousness, the FFC is currently undertaking a research project on medico-legal claims
Reflections on NHI
Implementing NHI requires significant investments in healthcare infrastructure, human resources, and technology. This could lead to the strengthening of the overall health system in South Africa. It may result in the construction of new healthcare facilities, the recruitment and training of additional healthcare professionals, and the implementation of improved health information systems.
One of the major challenges of implementing NHI is the issue of funding. NHI requires significant financial resources to cover the costs of providing universal healthcare. The South African government needs to explore sustainable financing mechanisms to ensure the long-term viability of NHI. This may involve exploring new sources of revenue, such as increased taxation or contributions from employers and employees.
The implementation of a universal healthcare system like NHI is a complex undertaking. It requires careful planning, coordination, and the involvement of various stakeholders, including healthcare providers, government agencies, and the public. Implementing NHI and addressing potential logistical and administrative challenges may take time.

The public health sector was very sensitive to budgetary cuts and was already under pressure from a growing burden of disease, and the Department will have to develop plans to deal with the budget cuts.
The number of posts across all salary scales is declining over the MTEF period. It is essential to balance the reduction of the number of personnel with service delivery needs, and it should not result in a retrogression of services

The total cost for all salary levels decreases from R881.7 million in 2022/23 to R682.1 million in 2023/24. The decrease in the total cost for all salary levels may hinder the expansion of health services and also result in poor health services
Most programmes showed an improvement in spending performance, however due to the decline in spending performance for Communicable and Non-communicable Diseases and Hospital Systems, overall spending performance deteriorated from 98.8% to 97.4%
Conditional grant performance

District Health Programme Grant shows underspending of 5.4%  (Delayed rollout of HPV vaccination due to prioritisation of measles vaccine, TB outreach affected by weak community outreach systems and poor data capturing)
Health is a concurrent function, with implementation driven by provinces
Wide-ranging cuts to amongst others: Central Hospital Services, Provincial Hospital Services and District Health Services

National Health Laboratory Service
The projected trajectory of total expenditure indicates a notable increase with an average annual growth rate of 9.6%, reaching R15.3 billion by 2025/26 from an initial value of R11.6 billion in 2022/23. A significant 73.8% will be allocated to laboratory tests during the medium-term period. Consequently, expenditure on these tests is anticipated to rise at an average annual rate of 11.1%.
As part of its financial sustainability strategy, the National Institute for Occupational Health (NIOH) anticipates deriving 92.4% of its revenue during the MTEF period from fees charged for laboratory tests. Additionally, 5.6 percent of its revenue will be obtained through transfers from the Department. The funding for laboratory tests is primarily sourced from the fees levied on these tests. It is projected that this revenue will experience an average annual growth rate of 10.1%, increasing from R10.7 billion in 2022/23 to R14.3 billion in 2025/26, in accordance with the expected rise in test volumes.
The maintenance of the communicable diseases surveillance programme retains paramount significance in delivering comprehensive surveillance and guidance pertaining to diverse communicable diseases.
Allocations towards this programme exhibit an average yearly growth rate of 5.1%, ascending from R459.9 million in the fiscal year 2021/22 to R534.1 million in the fiscal year 2024/25. This financial upscaling is intended to fortify various services, including mobile testing initiatives, community outreach programs, border testing activities, and hotline services.
South African Medical Research Council
During the MTEF period, the Council aims to accept or publish approximately 1 900 scholarly articles, books, and book chapters authored by individuals affiliated with and/or funded by the Council. This constitutes an estimated 50.2% (R2.1 billion) of the Council's budget over the medium term. There is a projected decline in the number of accepted and published journal articles associated with the Council in 2024/25.
Approximately 24.8% (R1 billion) of the Council's expenditure over the medium term is expected to be allocated to innovation and technology initiatives aimed at enhancing public health. This is critical in enhancing the capacity of low-income and middle-income countries to produce mRNA vaccines, providing scholarships to cultivate a workforce skilled in vaccine manufacturing, and monitoring COVID-19 viral RNA in wastewater across more than 80 wastewater treatment plants in South Africa. This surveillance facilitates the early detection of COVID-19 outbreaks.
The total expenditure is projected to experience an average annual growth rate of 0.7 percent, increasing from R1.4 billion in 2022/23 to R1.5 billion in 2025/26. The decline in expenditure during 2023/24 can be attributed to one-time allocations for COVID-19 vaccine research in the fiscal years 2021/22 and 2022/23. The financials show a projected surplus of R25.9 million in 2023/24
Occupational Diseases in Mines and Works
During the MTEF period, the fund will emphasise augmenting the number of benefit payments disbursed, with a projected increase from 7 600 in 2022/23 to 8 600 in 2025/26.
The fund also aims to augment the number of certifications finalised from 13,200 to 15,
000 over the same period. Beneficiary payments are anticipated to experience an increase from R449.8 million in 2022/23 to R543.3 million in 2025/26, exhibiting an average annual growth rate of 6.5%. This growth is primarily attributed to the unwinding adjustment on the provision for the outstanding claims liability, which is projected to rise from R312.2 million in 2022/23 to R396.5 million in 2025/26.
Over the medium term, the fund anticipates that 74.4% (amounting to R1.2 billion) of its revenue will be derived from interest earned on investments. At the same time, the remaining 25.6 percent (equivalent to R399.9 million) will be obtained through levies imposed on controlled mines and works. Revenue is predicted to experience an average annual growth rate of 7.8 percent, rising from R433.1 million in 2022/23 to R542.3 million in 2025/26.
A deficit of R1.1 million is projected for 2025/26, following a surplus of R62.1 million in 2024/25.
Council for Medical Schemes
The Council assumes a vital role in supporting the Department and facilitating the coordinated efforts of the medical industry to achieve universal health coverage. During the MTEF period, the Council aims to complete a comprehensive assessment of all medical schemes’ minimum benefits mandated for coverage. It will also promote consolidating options and medical schemes and support the standardisation of healthcare technology, infrastructure, and architecture.
The projected total expenditure demonstrates an average annual growth rate of 4.7%, projected to rise from R193.4 million in 2022/23 to R221.9 million in 2025/26. The Council is expected to generate approximately 95.2%  (equivalent to R616.4 million) of its revenue over the medium term by collecting levies imposed on medical schemes. An additional 3.2% (amounting to R20.4 million) of revenue is projected to be derived from transfers received from the Department. Revenue is expected to follow an upward trajectory in alignment with expenditure patterns. The projected surplus declines from R2.5 million in 2023/24 to R1.4 million in 2025/26.
Office of Health Standards Compliance
The proportion of public health facilities subject to inspection is projected to rise from 14.5% in the fiscal year 2021/22 to 18.4% in 2025/26. This expansion is facilitated by additional budgetary allocations of R10 million in 2023/24, R12 million in 2024/25, and R15 million in 2025/26. The majority of this funding is allocated to the compliance inspectorate programme, which undertakes the majority of the Office's activities and accounts for an estimated 35.7 percent (R182.1 million) of its total estimated expenditure over the MTEF period.
Expenditure is projected to experience an average annual growth rate of 4.9 percent, increasing from R157.5 million in 2022/23 to R181.7 million in 2025/26. The office's revenue is derived entirely from transfers made by the Separtment, which are projected to total R516.1 million over the medium term.
SA Health Products Regulatory Authority
There is a substantial decline in the percentage of new licenses finalised yearly related to good manufacturing practices and wholesaling practices from 77% in 2019/20 to 42% in 2021/22. Similar declines, though of the same magnitude, are recorded in other indicators, including the percentage of clinical trial applications finalised, and the percentage of medical device establishment license applications finalised.
The primary functions encompassing the assessment of drug safety, quality, and therapeutic effectiveness, as well as the process of registering medications, are anticipated to constitute 31%  (R392.4 million) of the Authority's expenditure during the MTEF period.
To enhance operational efficiency and facilitate the expeditious expansion of medication accessibility by streamlining the timelines for scientific evaluation and clinical trials, the authority intends to enhance the skills of its personnel through talent management initiatives and the recruitment of experts in essential domains. Consequently, the number of personnel is projected to rise from 292 individuals in 2022/23 to 331 individuals in 2023/24 and for the duration of the MTEF period. This expansion is expected to result in increased expenditure on employee compensation, escalating from R210.5 million in 2022/23 to R286 million in 2025/26.
Overall revenue and expenditure are anticipated to experience an average annual growth rate of 7%, ascending from R349.4 million in 2022/23 to R428.4 million in 2025/26. Over the MTEF period, the Authority foresees that 54.9% (R696.8 million) of its revenue will be derived from fees levied for the provision of its services, while 40.1% (R478.5 million) will be obtained through transfers from the Department
Conclusion
South Africa has shown progress on a number of health indicators, as witnessed by the increase in life expectancy and a decline in infant mortality
Public health care is under pressure due to heavy disease burden and increased demand together with budgetary cuts, placing health expenditure under increasing pressure
Despite relatively high expenditure as measured by percentage of GDP spending on health care, this expenditure does not always translate into desired health outcomes and South Africa continues to perform below the average for other upper-middle-income countries
There are a number of health reforms, such as the NHI, which aim to enhance the efficacy and efficiency of health care
However major issues pertaining to funding and implementation need to be resolved
The increased expenditure on settling medical-legal claims diverts scarce resources away from service delivery
South Africa is experiencing a growth in the burden of diseases. Further, the Department has shown an under performance against a number of set targets in the Communicable and Non-Communicable programme
Greater focus is needed to improve the spending performance against a number of conditional grants, in particular the District Health Programme Grant, NHI: Non-Personal Services Grant and the Personal Services HP Contracting Capitation
Significant under performance of the Compensation Commissioner for Occupational Diseases in Mines and Works resulting in the non-payment of eligible beneficiaries necessitates an intervention to assist eligible beneficiaries in accessing payments

FFC Responses
Due to time constraints, Dr Mbava said some questions would be responded to in writing. She said the FFC recognised the inequities in the health sector in terms of the skewed nature of healthcare provision and saw that the private sector has more resources than the public sector and that some sort of health reforms are needed in this instance. National health reforms have been proposed. It is acknowledged that the NHI in its current form remains a fiscal risk which needs to be properly counted for its impact on the fiscus. The Commission was doing ongoing research on the efficacy of the NHI and how this could be funded effectively. The FFC has noted budget cuts in the system and sees how this impacts healthcare as well as service delivery in the medium to long term.
The FFC responded to the question on the NHI model’s funding, noting research was done and there is an ongoing body of work. There is concern over the healthcare sector, as much as it may be protected on cost containment, it is inevitable that goods and services and conditional grants will be impacted. To some extent, the clinical posts will be protected, and it will be necessary for all provincial departments to plan around their vacancy and staff turnover rates. Departments should also consider occupation-specific dispensation and the impact thereof as there is a need for much more efficient management, specifically in the Health Department.
Mr Siyanda Jonas, Research Specialist, FFC, responded to the question on the loan taken for COVID-19 and its impact on the fiscal system, noting there would be an impact on the ratio of the GDP to debt ratio and this would increase the debt service costs like any other loan taken by the government for its expenditure pressure. More specifically, an increase in GDP to debt ratio crowds out service costs and social and investment spending.
An issue repeatedly raised by Members was fiscal consolidation – the FFC said this was touched on in the annual submission. South Africa tends to rely excessively on fiscal cuts to reduce expenditure deficits and the FFC notes that this may be warranted in some circumstances, but revenue measures also play a role in the reduction of fiscal deficits or attempting to achieve fiscal consolidation. Some expenditure cuts may be detrimental to growth, so there is a need to be targeted in what expenditure cuts are targeted. Here, as it relates to health, the FFC  notes that some expenditure cuts may begin to affect health outcomes and exacerbate inequality in terms of access to health. There is a need to consider what type of expenditure cuts are undertaken to remain on a fiscally sustainable path. Overall, for the FFC to get back on a fiscally sustainable path, it should have measures which support growth to get out of the fiscal impasse.
The Department of Health reports on the specific hospitals refurbished in its Annual Report. The FFC can request this information and the Committee could also request this from the Department. This would also provide information on whether the Eastern Cape hospitals were included.
Expenditure items related to goods and services can also be obtained. This relates not only to consumables but also to goods and services, vaccine procurement, and machinery used. This will be a broad category and include other expenditure items which may be vital to achieving health outcomes. Cuts may be detrimental and affect health service delivery, so there is a need to investigate this further and see which items are subject to cuts so that the entity does not make cuts on items that enhance or strengthen service delivery.
Mr Thando Ngozo, Research Specialist: Macroeconomics and Public Finance, FFC, said the key message the Commission wanted to relay to the Committee is the fact that key fiscal consolidation measures were being implemented which seem not to be taken into consideration that fiscal consolidation must be pro-growth. The most important aspect is also service delivery. As the FFC  implements this, what is important is for the sector to bring out sharply how this is affecting service delivery.
Dr Mbava said the key message was that while implementing cost-cutting measures, protecting the country’s socio-economic rights to healthcare is important.

Ms Mmule Thipe, Senior Manager, and Ms Musisinyani presented to the Committee. The presentation looked at the overall improvement in the audit outcome of the health portfolio and root causes. The presentation looked at the quality of financial reporting, noting he Western Cape has been producing good quality financial statements which gives indication that the control environment is sound. The AGSA commend KZN for also producing good quality financial statements in the current year. Departments where the audit process identified errors that required adjustments indicates that the control environment requires improvements for the Department to avoid dependence on the audit process to identify matter misstatements to be corrected. The poor control environment will result in material misstatement remaining undetected and financial statements not being supported by reliable evidence thereby rendering the financials unreliable.
Performance reporting
•Audit outcomes for predetermined objectives slightly improved with the Free State receiving no material findings on its reported predetermined objectives. 40% (4/10) of departments were not able to provide supporting evidence for their material and significant reported performance information; 30% also had material findings, while one audit was outstanding.
•It is concerning that credible performance data remain a challenge for the standardised indicators that should serve as a dipstick to measure primary health care in the provinces. Without credible performance data, the departments will not be able to easily identify and respond to any service delivery challenges that might arise i.e. if the Department cannot reliably measure the number of Tuberculosis (TB) patients at a hospital, it might create a shortage of medicine to treat TB patients at that particular hospital, and create a tally of excess medicine at another hospital.
•The limitation to conclude whether the reported achievements are reliable or not, again resulted in us being unable to determine whether departments have adequately delivered and achieved on the current year targets. The AGSA thus cannot confirm nor dispel whether they are on track to achieve the Medium Term Strategic Framework (MTSF) objectives and the 2030 vision.
Root causes:
•Lack of implementation of prior key recommendations made and/or action plans to address deficiencies identified.
• Critical positions not filled timeously with the right skills.
• Lack of adequate processes to maintain proper supporting evidence to substantiate reported financial and predetermined objectives information.
• Lack of internal controls and reviews, and a lack of oversight by leadership to ensure that these systems of internal controls which are critically required, are implemented
Impact of funds available for quality healthcare
• The situation in the sector is still concerning as significant increases in medico-legal claims are putting the sector under immense pressure. The financial strain resulted in increased accruals and late payments to suppliers.
• 19% of the remaining 2023/24 appropriation after deducting transfers, subsidies and compensation of employees, has to fund accruals and payables not yet recognised (unpaid invoices). This amount relates to unpaid invoices reported at R17 847 million for the 2022/23 year (2021/22: R16 087 million). This represents funds that would have been utilised in the 2023/24 year to render services to the citizens, committed to settle the 2022/23 expenditure. This means the sector is consistently borrowing from future allocations to fund current year expenses, forcing management to continuously choose which activities/services to prioritise and which to suspend, further disadvantaging citizens.
• The delayed payments may attract fruitless and wasteful expenditure as suppliers charge interest on late payments. These payments are affecting the budgets of public facilities and, in turn, will have a detrimental effect on the ability of the sector to deliver healthcare services.
• Total claims against the health sector were 75% of the appropriation. If these claims are not managed efficiently, these may result in payouts, as highlighted in the medico-legal claims section below, further putting pressure on the available funds for service delivery.
The presentation went on to look at irregular expenditure, identified material irregularities and resourcing of primary healthcare facilities, which was a special audit.
Medico-legal claims
•The total amount of medico-legal claims as at 31 March 2023, is approximately R67*billion (4% less than the baseline in 2018). Unfortunately, over the years, the balance has increased significantly above the baseline, rather than the planned/intended reduction.
• The sector is currently at 372% of the target, with one year remaining in the MTSF.
• NDoH did not meet the targets relating to medico-legal claims set for the 2021/22 and 2022/23 reporting period. This could indicate that efforts to curb medico-legal claims are slow.
• Challenges relating to record-keeping were unfortunately still reported in some provinces, mainly due to inadequate filing processes.
• Information above indicates that although the claims balance is exorbitant, the actual cash outflow is 3.4%(2023), and 1% (2022) respectively. These could be attributed to the efforts by the sector to address medico-legal claims.
• The amounts paid are however, still high, with R2.65 bn, R926m and R1.75 bn paid in the past three years, respectively. Payment of these claims puts a strain on the financial viability of the sector.
• Added to this, the sector has been reporting significant balances of accruals (unpaid invoices) at year end for the past number of years. Accruals represent funds that would have been utilised in the current year for rendering services to the citizens, that were committed to settle prior year expenditure.
• This means the sector consistently borrows from their future allocations to fund current year expenses, forcing management to continuously choose which activities/service to prioritise and which to suspend, further disadvantaging citizens.
• Overall, these payments are affecting the budgets of public facilities and, in turn, will have a detrimental effect on the ability of the sector to deliver healthcare services.
*see presentation
Concluding recommendations
•The Committee must request regular feedback on the progress of the implementation of effective action plans to improve the internal control environment
•The Committee must request management to prioritise investigations into irregular expenditure incidents, and report back in sufficient detail on the consequence management processes implemented
•The Committee (in collaboration with the NCOP) must also closely monitor the areas contributing to the funds being depleted from the sector that could have otherwise been used to procure much-needed equipment, expand and maintain infrastructure, and increase capacity and skills in the sector. These include the areas of medico-legal claim, ICRM, fruitless and wasteful expenditure and better management of goods and services processes so that accruals are reduced.
•The oversight committees in the sector must request the sector leadership to provide plans for the measures the leadership will implement to not only improve the systems of internal controls relating to financial and performance management disciplines. This must be supported by proper governance processes.
•The Committee must invest in engaging the National Health Council (NHC), the sector executives etc., on how they plan to turn around the key service delivery matters for the sector, to ensure that it meets its key delivery targets (NHI, Vision 2030).

Responses
Ms Musisinyani said AGSA always aimed to improve how it communicated its messages and appreciated that the Committee found the presentations thought-provoking.
She said that recommendations and leadership are made to leadership and accounting officers and their teams within departments. The ecosystem is very specific about the role of senior managers and the role of officers supported by the internal committee. Senior management refers to the Director-General (DG) and his team, because the DG has the ultimate responsibility to ensure the development of implementation and a controlled environment that aligns with financial management. For every finding raised, it is communicated to the relevant Department and official. This also goes with recommendations.
When the AG responds, it indicates whether it agrees with the finding and what action plans are in place to correct it. Sometimes, it asks Departments to reassess action plans if they are not addressing the real root cause of the finding. Action plans need to be tracked as repeated findings could indicate that the core issues facing departments are not being addressed. Action plans could also not be adequate enough to address the real root causes. A request is then sent for information on a contract basis, but sometimes information is not sent to AGSA due to various reasons such as documents being destroyed in a fire. The situation depends on each province and the samples we collect. As an auditor, if certain documents are not received it is unable to determine whether proper procurement processes were followed.
The Office of the AGSA only does audits on a sample.
The Department would have a full list of delayed projects.
On the implications of the limitations in the Annual Performance Report,  AGSA’s role is to provide assurance to the Committee on whether balances disclosed are valid, accurate and complete. If the sector is reporting an amount and is working toward specific targets, AGSA cannot provide assurance on this number. Where there is no credible information, the sector is unable to respond to service delivery. Where there is no information on stock and at a time when the sector is about to render a service through giving a patient pharmaceuticals, the Departments would then find themselves without the medicines due to processes not being followed to close the gaps on some of the limitations. Looking at the implication of the contingent liability, the standards on how departments must account is very clear relating to contingent liabilities. At a given point in time, Departments do not know whether a claim will result in an actual payment, so the standard allows Departments to estimate it based on prior experiences. For example, if there is a trend that they get to pay only around 50% of the claim, the standard allows the Department to unpack all the claims it is receiving and only disclose the 50% in its financial statements.
The most important thing is to disclose the assumption of what they will use for that amount. When AGSA comes in, it audits the estimates and assumptions of the Department and when it is comfortable that it is supported by evidence, it is able to accept some of the findings disclosed. In the past years, the AGSA qualified the contingent liability balance because it was not satisfied with the process the sector undertook to ensure the amount of the contingent liability was reasonable. It has been seen that when the sector looked at historical trends, it was able to make adjustments to some amounts. The Eastern Cape struggled to get the estimated amounts and the impact of this is that the Department is unable to plan when it cannot estimate what it needs to pay. This can have a ripple effect from a budgeting point of view.
Whenever there is an investigation, a full employee process kicks in and the Department also has an investigation and the final decision on consequence management rests with the Department. Swift consequence management is encouraged. AGSA has received feedback that some employees have received warning letters. Sometimes, there are legal processes which is why it takes long. When some of these recruitments are not done on time, officials have to play a number of roles which is why there is an additional burden on hospitals and employees.
The effects of recommendations not being implemented is that the audit outcomes will remain as what has been presented. This also has a ripple effect on service delivery. Material irregularities can be raised and if we can recap on this, it can be used as an enforcement for the Accounting Officer to consider the recommendations. Instructions are issued if the Accounting Officer does not comply with this. The slides on material irregularities are examples of where AGSA had to use enforcement as the Accounting Officers did not address the matters. It took this a step further to ensure recommendations are implemented.
Regarding late payments to suppliers, the DoH had a lot of these, and material non-findings were reported. AGSA observed that many invoices were not settled on time which triggered the findings. The impact of the late payments becomes fruitless and wasteful expenditure. On the implication of the 90% for the available funds, there were funds allocated for a specific year, and the expectation is that the funds are available for the 2022/23 financial year. The process to be followed was that the sector would have submitted a request for a budget to National Treasury linking it to targets. Due to financial constraints in the sector, and when invoices are not settled by the end of the year, when the new year starts, service providers request the payment of their funds before service delivery. R17 billion of unpaid invoices is already placing the sector on the back foot. By 1 April, old invoices must be settled before any new allocations can be received. This means the available funds for the year are depleted. The difference is what remains for the sector and some services will have to be reprioritised. Some service delivery options will have to be set aside and this is what is meant by implications.

The presentation referred to a system problem where there were penalties to pay in terms of tax submissions and that this error was being worked on to correct it. She asked for the timeframe in which this would be addressed.
Ms Hicklin said on Programme One that there was a typo which referred to a R1697 surplus and which should have been a R197 million surplus. She was concerned about the deferred certification which has been pushed over to the next year. She asked why this happened. The certification has the ability to constantly deliver and provide products and services that meet customer, statutory and regulatory authority pilots. Surely, this would be fundamental to what SAHPRA does. Generic medicine was a concern in relation to what medical aids are willing to cover. This was a major concern, considering staff turnover. Is the scale attracting the barrier to the top calibre of people to work at SAHPRA? Has SAHPRA identified any other challenges it can share with the Committee, and what is being done to manage these? What strategy is being employed in terms of the high staff turnover of skilled people and what kind of litigation cases are being faced, if any? What is the number of litigation cases, in which provinces are they and what is the severity of it? What was the irregular expenditure for and what is being done to address this? The inability to collect revenue seems to be a problem in the South African government as a whole, but there also seems to be an aversion to collecting appropriate fees and how is this being addressed now and in the future?
Mr Xaba welcomed the presentation, asking what the limitations of the enterprise with the RAG committee were, what impact this would have on the registration programme, and the effectiveness of SAHPRA’s turnaround times. Has the entity recovered from the COVID-19 budget adjustments and what impact can further adjustments or implementation of cost containment have on the entity meeting its target of 80% registered generic medicines with the performance of 57% and 22% of the new GWNP and GWP-related licenses within 125 days?

Responses
Dr Semete said on the AMA Treaty that the conference of State parties would determine how much each member state would contribute. No dollar amount has been communicated as the Conference of State parties still needs to meet to determine this. South Africa will get this information once the treaty has been signed and ratified. She said there would be benefits for South Africa to sign and ratify the treaty. The impact of this would be for the country to export to the rest of Africa.
The AMA Board is being selected and there has been an indication of wanting South Africa represented on the Board. She believed that it would benefit South Africa to participate on this platform, particularly given the role of President Cyril Ramaphosa on some of these platforms such as those related to COVID-19 and local manufacturing. She thought it was fitting for South Africa to sign and ratify the treaty.
She said the entity largely dealt with generics compared to new chemical entities and delays were due to the entity not being able to apply reliance as by the time the applications are submitted, a lot of changes have taken place. Sometimes changes are made by the European Agency and as a result, SAHPRA cannot rely on them, because their assessment of the product could be outdated if they changed manufacturers. SAHPRA is also under-capacitated which can be seen in the turnover time for new chemical entities. It is really stretching the team and digitisation will assist, but there is a need to improve the current capacity.
The reason for a reduction from 73% to 72% is that some new KPIs were included for the specific financial year.
The staff turnover is not outside of the industry norm and some mechanisms during exit interviews are that salaries come up often and some staff members indicate that SAHPRAs environment is very demanding and staff have to deliver a lot with a limited capacity and staff are really stretched and would like to have a better work-life balance. SAHPRA  needs to ensure that it has adequate capacity.
SAHPRA believes it will do better in the current year on the workplace skills plan and it believes that it has put the right types of development areas and training programs within the plan. SAHPRA is in the process of raising funding to bring in a training and development specialist as it is a knowledge-intensive entity and sends its staff for a lot of training, so it needs to ensure that it has someone who can coordinate this and ensure that benefit is derived from sending staff for such training.
The ISO Certificate was deferred as SAHPRA was being assisted with the World Health Organisation (WHO) benchmarking. She was confident that the entity would deliver on this in the present year. The entity has already had its desktop audit and analysis of its policies and will receive an outcome by the end of the year.
The offers rejected relate to the salary that is offered. The entity is attracting staff from an industry that pays much higher than it is offering and much higher bonuses in the public sector. It is difficult for SAHPRA to match some of these offers. It really wants to attract top candidates as it operates in a global sphere and sometimes its assessors have to assist assessors from the WHO. It wants to keep this up, but also attract people who can train others. If there is an equally strong candidate, it will make a second offer, but there are instances where it would rather run the process from scratch.
On litigation, she did not have the exact numbers at hand, but would share the quarter two report on this with the Committee. Some of the cases still being dealt with by SAHPRA are COVID-19 related where it authorised the Pfizer vaccine for the pediatric population and this decision was challenged. This was challenged as it was said that SAHPRA was breaching the constitutional rights of children. Authorising a product does not mean it is mandatory. On the severity of cases, SAHPRA has been successful in some of them and has stuck to its mandate. A detailed report would be provided to the Committee at a later stage.
On the enterprise architecture, this was an administrative matter where SAHPRA could simply not get this to the Board in time, but this was subsequently approved in quarter one of the current year, and it has contracted a service provider to implement the regulatory system for it.
On the recovery from adjustments, SAHPRA is  a knowledge-intensive organisation and its biggest asset is its people, as it utilises their knowledge to review these dossiers and make decisions. Any cuts will impact employment, training and recruitment programs which will be seen in the long term. This is something that has been seen in SAHPRA, where it has stagnated by the 300 mark in terms of staff complement when it should be sitting at 500. This means SAHPRA is not growing as there is no adequate funding. This is a concern as the number of applications is growing and people are being replaced.
On the muffins which contained THC or CBD, she could not comment on this as it was not a medicine and could not speak with any level of authority on this as she only heard about this via the media. The important thing about cannabis regulation was that all stakeholders needed to do their part. There must be a clear national policy which states that when we relax some of these requirements, which entity will step in to ensure that matters of safety are addressed, such as the Department of Social Development (DSD), the Department of Justice (DoJ) or Agriculture being brought on board. This is a multi-stakeholder matter. SAHPRA focuses on things used for medicinal purposes as per its mandate.
The vacancy rate continues to be high due to financial reasons. SAHPRA is very circumspect in how it makes decisions which will have a financial impact. Where roles cannot be filled, a high vacancy rate will be challenging.
On the GNP and GWP, there are parts wherein SAHPRA is ready to do an audit, but the applicant would not be ready and would ask for this to be done in six months which throws SAHPRA off.
Mr Regardt Gouws, Chief Financial Officer, SAHPRA, said in terms of the utilisation of surpluses, a schedule three entity needs to obtain permission from National Treasury to retain the surpluses. It is difficult to commit to a long-term appointment due to this. National Treasury has approved a budget deficit for SAHPRA to utilise the surplus to retain earnings, but it would rather look at contracted positions.
As of 1 March, it had 79 unfunded positions, mostly technical staff costing around R80 million, which SAHPRA does not have for the coming financial year. It is busy with its recruitment plan, and quite a few vacancies still need to be filled in the current financial year, so revenue growth growing with the cost of employment has been a challenge.
Slide 37 shows the high level of control deficiencies and corrective measures.
On adjusted financial statements and performance information, SAHPRA wants to align its disclosure notes and there was additional information flagged by the AGSA. On non-compliance, a collection process was followed and some applicants have no intention of staying beyond five years. The main challenge is with medical devices. They apply for a tender, but are not interested in continuing with certain devices. The challenge is also contacting the applicants, as they would simply ignore collection efforts. SAHPRA is busy with a detailed action plan which can be presented to the Committee. It will make a final attempt to collect, and if this is not possible, it will follow a process of cancellation as licenses are gazetted. It is thinking of system enhancements and trying to manually upload this on the system. It will complete this before the end of the year. Note three indicates R6,6 million for license and retention fees.
System issues with PAYE were specific to the 2022 tax year, and SAHPRA  does not foresee a continuation of this challenge.
The one irregular transgression identified related to a procurement process for ports of entry where it needed office accommodation for border officials and the entity it engaged at that point was not tax compliant, as legislation required it to be compliant on the date of approval and the internal checks missed this. This will be dealt with in the same way as other transgressions.
With National Treasury’s recent cost containment and guidance, SAHPRA has submitted a plan for where cuts can be made. A tender was cut on communications, outreach, and radio campaigns.
There is a detailed action plan on the impact of control’s and this will be tracked moving forward.
Prof Rees said the Board had asked similar questions to the Committee. She wanted to highlight a few high-level things. At the highest level, there is huge interest in the African region in local manufacturing, vaccine manufacturing and pharmaceutical manufacturing. There is going to be huge competition between countries so it needs to ensure that it has a good regulatory authority. It is very difficult to do pharmacovigilance if there is no budget for this. She suggested that Members think about how much they wanted to comment on the budget cuts as SAHPRA was looking for supplementary funding streams. The AMA will become very important, and South Africa needs to be a part of it. This will set regional standards and will allow collective evaluations. If SAHPRA is not in, it is out, and that is not good news. A strategic planning meeting will be held with the Board in the coming week.
On collection, a public health issue needs to be considered, as well as the need for generics and the issue with medical aids. Prof Rees highlighted that it was not beneficial to have more generic paracetamol as once one reaches about seven generics, one ceases to lose the price advantage. Many generics were not meant to be brought to market in South Africa and some were possibly in the African Region. She said that SAHPRA needs to have a more analytic approach to generics. In some regulatory authorities, there is a limit to generics as there is no public health advantage. An incremental fee scale can be used when a number has been reached where there is no public health benefit. The entity is battling with the number of generics it has bought that have simply not been brought to market. The legal framework for SAHPRA is also important. It will consider the legal issues in its current framework and bring this to the attention of the Minister to be revised.
The Acting Chairperson made brief closing remarks. She said South Africa was looking forward to being a part of the AMA Treaty and thanked the entity for its good work.
Meeting adjourned.
