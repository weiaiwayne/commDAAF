2025/26 Annual Performance Plans
The Portfolio Committee convened to review the Department of Public Service and Administration’s (DPSA's) revised strategic plan for 2025–2030 and the annual performance plan (APP) for 2025/26.
The Chairperson opened the meeting by commending the Department’s ongoing efforts to professionalise the public service, combat maladministration, and enhance ethical governance. However, he also acknowledged persistent service delivery challenges in certain regions, urging the Department to intensify its interventions.
The Department outlined its key priorities, including the monitoring of appointments and tenure of Directors-General and Heads of Department, oversight of the national professionalisation framework, compliance with public administration norms, and the strengthening of disciplinary processes and lifestyle audits. Its focus also extended to implementing Batho Pele service delivery standards, improving productivity measurements, and enhancing information communication technology (ICT) governance and data architecture.
The budget overview indicated that the total allocation for 2025/26 was R564.72 million, rising to R680.65 million over the medium-term expenditure framework period. The largest share was dedicated to the administration programme, reflecting the Department’s labour-intensive nature and the need for robust support functions. Expenditure was primarily allocated to compensation of employees, goods and services, and transfers to the Centre for Public Service Innovation.
Committee Members' concerns and questions included the rationale behind budget allocations, the impact of inflation, ongoing service delivery failures in municipalities, and the adequacy of disciplinary management and lifestyle audits. They also queried the Department’s approach to artificial intelligence in the public service, mechanisms to prevent the redeployment of dismissed officials, and the management of ghost workers. Other issues discussed involved the effectiveness of collective bargaining, the high turnover of Director Generals, and the Department’s strategies for addressing skills gaps and ensuring the professionalisation of leadership positions.
In response, the Department emphasised the alignment of budget allocations with operational needs and strategic objectives, noting the constraints imposed by the current fiscal environment. It clarified that the Department’s mandate at the local government level was limited, but was expected to expand with pending legislative amendments. It reported ongoing efforts to monitor compliance, conduct lifestyle audits, and prevent the movement of officials with unresolved disciplinary matters. Skills audits, professionalisation frameworks, and digital learning initiatives were highlighted as key interventions to build capacity and address workforce challenges.
The Department also addressed the role of artificial intelligence, viewing it as both an opportunity and a challenge that required careful, strategic adoption to enhance productivity without compromising jobs or equity. It reaffirmed that there was no current intention to reduce posts, but rather to fill existing vacancies and improve workforce capability through targeted training, partnerships, and continuous learning.
Meeting report
Chairperson's opening remarks
The Chairperson said that the session would proceed without delay due to a congested parliamentary schedule, including upcoming debates throughout the week. He acknowledged the significant progress made by the Department of Public Service and Administration (DPSA) in professionalising the public service and fostering an ethical, developmental state. He commended the strides taken to address maladministration and corruption across spheres of government. He underscored the importance of ensuring that public resources deliver value for money by directly improving citizens' quality of life.
However, he also noted the persistent service delivery challenges in certain regions, where communities continued to face difficulties in accessing basic public services. He urged the Department to intensify its efforts in addressing these shortcomings, while maintaining its commitment to ethical governance and efficient administration.
He extended appreciation to Inkosi Mzamo Buthelezi, Minister of the DPSA, for his consistent availability and leadership in appearing before the Committee, and formally invited the Minister and the Department to proceed with the presentation of the revised strategic plan and annual performance plan (APP).
DPSA's strategic plan 2025–2030 and 2025/26 APP
Ms Linda Dludla, Deputy Director-General: Administration, presented the DPSA's revised strategic plan and APP. Key focus areas included:
Monitoring appointments of Directors-General (DGs) and Heads of Department (HoDs) in line with prescribed qualifications;
Tracking the tenure and mobility of accounting officers;
Oversight of the implementation of the national professionalisation framework;
Monitoring compliance with public administration norms and standards across all spheres of government;
Conducting lifestyle audits and reducing backlogs in disciplinary cases;
Strengthening the central register for disciplinary sanctions to prevent re-employment of dismissed officials across spheres.
Further updates included:
Implementation of Batho Pele revitalisation interventions through standardised service delivery frameworks;
Productivity measurements across service delivery departments to improve output;
Monitoring of the African Peer Review Mechanism national action plan across relevant departments;
Review and implementation of the Public Service Complaints and Compliments Framework (2025–2029).
Ms Dludla highlighted the key APP targets and priorities across the DPSA’s five programmes --Administration; Standards and Compliance; Labour Relations and Remuneration; Information Communication Technology (ICT) and e-Government; and Government Services Access and Improvement.
Budget overview
Mr Masilo Makhura, Chief Financial Officer (CFO), reported that the total budget for 2025/26 was R564.72 million, increasing to R680.65 million over the medium term expenditure framework (MTEF) period. The budget included R15 million allocated to the Centre for Public Service Innovation (CPSI).
Programme 1 (Administration) received the largest share (52%) due to centralised operational functions such as IT, finance, legal, and internal audit.
The breakdown by economic classification was:
54%: Compensation of employees (CoE);
35%: Goods and services;
10%: Transfers and subsidies (mainly CPSI);
1%: Capital assets (ICT tools, equipment).
Strategies
The Department’s strategic focus for the period included:
Monitoring qualifications and tenure of DGs and HoDs;
Supporting implementation of the Professionalisation Framework via binding directives;
Conducting departmental skills audits and closing identified skills gaps;
Ensuring timely finalisation of disciplinary cases and lifestyle audits;
Improving ICT governance, continuity, and data architecture;
Institutionalising Batho Pele service delivery standards.
Interventions and partnerships
Key interventions and collaborative efforts included:
Deployment of task teams to support skills audits in departments;
Partnership with the Auditor-General (AG) and internal audit structures to strengthen performance oversight;
Engagements with organised labour through ongoing collective bargaining processes;
Monitoring the African Peer Review Mechanism (APRM) national action plan alongside Treasury, Justice, Forestry and Environment, and Cooperative Governance,
Collaboration with departments to implement the revised Complaints and Compliments Management Framework.

DPSA's response
Mr Makhura responded to the questions relating to financial matters. He explained that the budget allocation classification, as presented, indicated that compensation of employees accounted for 54% of the budget, followed by 35% for goods and services, and 1% for capital assets. This distribution reflected the Department’s labour-intensive nature as a policy department, where deliverables such as the development of policies and regulations rely heavily on internal employees, rather than infrastructure projects. In contrast to infrastructure-heavy departments like Public Works, the Department’s outputs did not require large-scale capital investment. On the question of why Administration alone accounts for 52% of the budget, Mr Makhura clarified that Programme 1 was a support programme encompassing all units that support Programmes 2 to 4. This included the ministry (Minister and Deputy Minister), the Director-General’s office, HR, IT services, the CFO’s office, internal audit, international relations, and a significant allocation of approximately R74.595 million paid to the Department of Public Works and Infrastructure (DPWI) for office accommodation in Pretoria. He emphasised that this structure had been debated and approved through the MTEF process with National Treasury, and that the budget escalation also considered the consumer price index.
Regarding fruitless and wasteful expenditure, he said that this was managed internally as a target to monitor and prevent occurrences. The Department had a strong financial management record, with minimal instances of such expenditure, as reflected in previous audit reports and annual reports. In the past year, only two cases were recorded, amounting to R10 000, due to missed flights by two officials. These incidents were under investigation, and appropriate consequence management measures would follow, potentially including recovery of the funds.
On the question of revised allocations, Mr Makhura explained that the Department had already received its final allocation prior to the Minister of Finance’s third budget presentation, which had imposed no cuts on departments. Therefore, the allocation presented to Members was final and had been distributed across units to implement the approved strategic plan and APP. He assured Members that the allocation was sufficient to support the Department’s strategic objectives, including ICT programmes.
Mr Willie Vukela, Acting Director-General, DPSA, addressed the questions concerning service delivery failures in municipalities. He said that, as previously noted in the presentation, the Department had a second piece of legislation, currently being amended, with the bill now before the National Council of Provinces (NCOP). The intention of this law was to strengthen the Minister’s ability to intervene in and support the local government sphere. At present, the Department’s mandate extends only to the national and provincial spheres of government. However, once the amended law was passed, it would provide the necessary leverage for greater engagement with local government through the NCOP and its custodianship of local government matters. The Department already had Branch 5, which develops and applies instruments to assist local government. He stressed, however, that under the current legal framework, the Department’s mandate at local level remained limited. The new legislation would allow for significantly more interventions in the local government sphere.
Mr Vukela also responded to questions on consequence management and compliance. He explained that the Public Service Act empowered the Minister, under Section 16A, to engage other executives, compile compliance reports, and submit these to Cabinet and Parliament. This Committee, in turn, could request such reports. In addition, the Minister may engage colleagues at a moral and executive level to address non-compliance. Much of the Department’s work on compliance was channelled through these reports to Cabinet and Parliament.
Dr Anusha Naidoo, Acting Deputy Director-General (DDG): Human Resource Management and Development, responded to questions regarding skills development and workforce capability. She said the Department was undertaking intensive and impactful work with the Department of Cooperative Governance and Traditional Affairs (COGTA), particularly its cooperative governance component. Multiple engagements had already been held to explore how the performance management and development system -- specifically, the personal development plans of individuals at the local government level -- could be made more skills-oriented and better aligned to integrated development plans (IDPs). This included exploring how AI could be utilised to support work at the local government level, even as the Department awaited the NCOP’s approval of the amended legislation.
On addressing skills gaps at national and provincial levels, Dr Naidoo highlighted seven key components of the Department’s systematic, practical interventions.
First, she outlined the professionalisation framework and directive issued by the DPSA to enforce merit-based recruitment, competency assessments, and career pathing aligned to qualifications and experience. This also involved integrity testing, security vetting, and continuous development of managers and leaders.
Second, she noted the longstanding use of competency-based assessments, applying the DPSA framework for senior and middle management to guide appointments, development plans, and performance reviews. Ethics testing was a mandatory part of the interview process for all public service positions.
Third, she explained that compulsory induction and ongoing learning programmes, delivered by the National School of Government (NSG) -- including the widely recognised certificate -- were integral to professionalisation. Short courses, certifications, and executive development offerings, were also being expanded in collaboration with universities.
Fourth, she addressed the impact of artificial intelligence, noting that basic AI-related programmes were already being implemented through the NSG.
Fifth, she described ongoing sector-wide skills audits, workforce planning, and capability mapping, which guide human resource development strategies and inform where scarce or critical skills were needed.
Sixth, she spoke on youth and graduate pipelines, pointing to internships, learnerships, and graduate recruitment schemes as critical for building early career pathways. These were supported by partnerships with universities to prepare graduates for high-demand occupational categories at national and provincial levels.
Seventh, she discussed the expansion of digital capacity through digital learning platforms and the integration of ICT competencies into job profiles. This was reinforced through partnerships with centres of excellence, professional bodies, and international best practices.
Dr Naidoo concluded that skills gaps in the public service were structural and historical, but they were being systematically addressed through a coordinated approach centred on professionalising the sector, building workforce intelligence, and fostering continuous learning. She emphasised the need to move from a compliance-based culture to one focused on strategic capability development, aligned with the vision of a developmental, ethical, and capable state.
Mr Vukela commented on how the Department intended to manage additional commitments, given the constrained fiscal environment. He acknowledged that there were no additional funds available from government, and stressed that the Department must plan within its existing resources. He assured Members that financial and human resources would be maximised to meet commitments without creating unfunded liabilities. While the Treasury had indicated that departments may review their budgets where necessary, at present, the DPSA was focused on operating strictly within its allocated financial and human resources.
Ms Dludla responded to the question regarding a potential reduction in posts. She clarified that the Department had no intention of reducing posts at this stage. She explained that the Department’s current structure, approved in 2020 and now under review, already included approximately 67 posts that could not be filled due to reductions in the compensation budget. Rather than reducing posts, the Department was actively working to fill vacancies as resources allowed. She added that the ongoing review of the organisational structure would provide a clearer picture of whether any reduction was necessary in future, but for now, the priority remained addressing the high vacancy rate, which currently affected nearly 50% of approved posts.
Mr Mandla Ngcobo, Deputy Director-General (DDG): eGovernment and Chief Government Information Officer, responded to the question on the impact of AI in the public service. He characterised AI as both beneficial and potentially problematic, depending on how it was applied. On the positive side, AI could help address gaps in the public service workforce and enhance productivity, particularly as much of government’s work involves developing policies, guidelines, and providing services. AI could complement and improve service delivery -- for example, through the adoption of bots on websites to interact with citizens in a pre-programmed and efficient way. Full adoption of AI also compelled the public service to re-examine roles and skills requirements. For instance, HR, IT, and finance professionals would need to adapt their work methods to align with AI-enhanced processes.
Mr Ngcobo emphasised that the public service must focus on data literacy and evidence-based decision-making, requiring officials to develop skills in data analysis and interpretation to effectively leverage AI capabilities. He also noted challenges at a macro level, such as the fact that many large language models (LLMs) underpinning AI systems were English-centric and did not always cater adequately to South Africa’s linguistic diversity. He pointed out that some alternatives, such as Chinese-developed models, were emerging, but investments were needed to adapt such technologies to the South African context. Therefore, he cautioned that the public service must adopt AI carefully and strategically, weighing its benefits against potential impacts on jobs and equity. While tools like Microsoft’s AI-enabled applications were being piloted, the public service remained cautious about widespread adoption to ensure balanced, sustainable implementation.

Mr Makhura addressed the question regarding the analysis of the Department’s budget breakdown. He explained that the budget allocation was directly informed by the APP which the Department presented to Parliament, and was further detailed in an internal annual operational plan (AOP) that guided the achievement of APP targets. He noted that the budgeting process accounted for the human resources required to execute the planned work, as well as for special or once-off projects. These were costed and allocated accordingly, while ensuring adherence to the baseline budget allocated by National Treasury.
He emphasised that, given the current fiscal challenges in the country, departments no longer received additional funding beyond their allocated baselines. Therefore, the Department ensures strict alignment with its baseline allocation and reprioritises projects where necessary. If a need for additional funding arises, the Department identifies lower-priority projects to remove from the plan to make room for higher-priority initiatives. These decisions were made in consultation with National Treasury. Internally, the Department holds budget committee meetings chaired by the Director-General to review expenditure quarterly, while the CFO monitors expenditure monthly. These reviews help identify potential overspending on line items and ensure that remedial action is taken early.
Managers and project managers were engaged to assess spending patterns and adjust budgets within the baseline through virement -- shifting funds between programmes or branches within the limits permitted by the Public Finance Management Act (PFMA). He added that the Department’s audit committee also exercises oversight over both expenditure and performance on a quarterly basis, raising any red flags about overspending or mismanagement, which management then addresses.
In conclusion, Mr Makhura stressed that the Department continuously analyses and manages its budget and allocations carefully to remain within its appropriated funds and avoid unauthorised expenditure under the PFMA.
Mr Dumisani Hlophe, Acting DDG: Negotiations, Labour Relations and Remuneration Management (NLRRM), addressed questions relating to collective bargaining, wage agreements, and the issue of ghost workers.
On collective bargaining, he emphasised that it would be impossible for government not to honour salary agreements concluded through negotiations. He explained that the Department aligned the negotiations process with government planning and the budgeting cycle, working closely with National Treasury. Any agreement reached in the Public Service Coordinating Bargaining Council (PSCBC) must be confirmed by the Minister of Finance to ensure the funds are available. Thus, all wage negotiations are conducted within the fiscal constraints of the national budget. He noted that the most recent wage round had secured a multi-year, three-year agreement, meaning there were no wage negotiations in the current year. However, the Department continued to engage organised labour on other conditions of employment to maintain labour peace and improve productivity in the public service. Ongoing discussions at the PSCBC included issues such as the early retirement policy without penalties, and reforms to the occupational specific dispensations (OSDs), based on findings from the personnel expenditure review.
Regarding ghost workers, Mr Hlophe acknowledged the heightened public awareness of this issue following remarks by the Minister during the Budget Vote. He clarified that employment management was decentralised, with accounting officers and CFOs in each department responsible for verifying that employees exist and were properly remunerated. Two main mechanisms are currently used to mitigate the risk of ghost workers:
Physical verification, where line managers and CFOs check that all listed employees were present and performing their duties, with verification reports submitted to National Treasury; and
System-based verification through the personnel salary (PERSAL) payroll system, which requires at least three individuals to confirm each employee’s existence before payment.
He also noted that the DPSA had developed a joint strategy with National Treasury to investigate the scale and root causes of the phenomenon further. A progress report on this initiative was expected to be presented to the Portfolio Committee in the third quarter of the financial year.

Ms Naidoo addressed the question of why the monitoring of qualifications and experience focused specifically on DGs and HODs, rather than on staff at all levels. She explained that this focus was a strategic governance imperative, rooted in critical considerations of leadership, accountability, and the disproportionate impact that apex-level leaders had on state capability. DGs and HODs occupied key leadership positions directly responsible for policy implementation, organisational performance, resource allocation, institutional culture, and ultimately the achievement of national priorities.
Ms Naidoo elaborated that monitoring qualifications and experience at this level was intended to:
Ensure alignment with the Directive on Professionalising the Public Service (Volume 1), which defines the required qualifications and experience for these roles;
Eliminate irregular or politically motivated appointments;
Set a precedent for meritocratic recruitment at the highest levels and affirm ethical, capable leadership; and
Strengthen compliance with the Public Service Act, the Public Finance Management Act, and constitutional obligations under section 195.
She added that weaknesses at the executive level often correlated with institutional underperformance, audit disclaimers, and poor service delivery. Monitoring these variables, therefore, enabled early identification of skills gaps, succession risks, and the need for targeted executive development while fostering a culture of competence, ethics, and professionalism that cascades through the public service hierarchy. This approach was the first component of a two-pronged strategy. While current monitoring was targeted at DGs and HODs, the Department was also implementing broader monitoring of the professionalisation framework at other levels, with an 80% compliance target in the first year. This included variables such as qualifications, experience, ethics testing, and adherence to the norms and standards outlined in the directive. Over the medium-term period, the Department would extend monitoring progressively to all levels of the public service to support a capable, ethical, and developmental state.
On the question of staff in ministerial offices, she explained that such positions -- known as private offices -- were contract appointments tied to the term of office of the respective Minister or Executive Authority. Nonetheless, these staff were also required to comply with the norms and standards of the professionalisation framework, including appropriate qualifications, experience, and (for senior managers) completion of the certificate, among other requirements.
Ms Naidoo concluded by reiterating that the monitoring efforts were designed to both symbolically and systemically strengthen governance credibility, institutional performance, and public trust in the public service.
Ms Dludla addressed the question regarding why some DGs did not complete their full five-year contracts. Drawing on findings from a study conducted more than five years ago, she said that there were many reasons, as DGs were employees like any others and were therefore subject to similar career dynamics and disciplinary processes. Some DGs did not complete their term because they were dismissed following disciplinary proceedings, while others voluntarily resigned to pursue other opportunities, whether within another department through a transfer, or outside the public service entirely -- for example, moving to the private sector.
She noted that in some cases, DGs request transfers between departments, which results in them leaving their original post before the contract ends. In other instances, a DG may reach retirement age during the contract, resulting in a premature conclusion of their tenure. She emphasised that there was no single reason for DGs not completing their contracts -- the factors mirrored the kinds of transitions and exits experienced by employees at other levels of the public service. She concluded by stating that a fresh study on this issue was set to commence during the current year, and its findings -- including updated reasons for early departures -- would be reported to Parliament in due course.
Mr Hlophe contributed further on the DG turnover matter. He elaborated that the high turnover of DGs was receiving focused attention as part of the broader process of professionalising the public service. This issue was one of the key sub-themes under discussion in the context of building a professional synergy between executive authorities and heads of administration. He said the challenge of aligning and stabilising the relationship between political executives and DGs or HODs was not unique, as there could naturally be some level of attrition. However, Cabinet had requested a comprehensive review of government capacity and the capacity of the state more broadly. This review would examine what was required to enhance productivity and the effective delivery of public goods and services, with the relationship between political heads and DGs identified as a critical factor. He assured the Committee that through the professionalisation framework and the ongoing capacity of the state project, the Department was actively exploring mechanisms to strengthen and stabilise this relationship. This work was positioned as a key part of efforts to build a capable, ethical, and developmental state.
Mr Vukela addressed the remaining questions posed by the Chairperson and other Members. He assured the Committee that the Department had not closed the page on any of the issues raised, and had taken note of all submissions and requests for additional information. He confirmed, as also acknowledged earlier by the Deputy Minister, that the Department would compile and submit a consolidated report covering the outstanding matters raised by the Chairperson, including specific data on the progress of national audits and SCM issues. This information would be incorporated into the formal submission to the Committee.
Regarding the Chairperson’s final question regarding the employment of foreign nationals in universities and certain departments, despite the availability of similar skills locally, he described this as reflective of a recurring issue in the public space, and said the Department engages with this matter daily and is working on it actively. He confirmed that the Department was preparing a submission on this topic for the Presidency, as it fell within the broader mandate of the President’s office. He invited Ms Naidoo to respond to specific aspects of the question.
Ms Naidoo said the Department would be meeting later in the week with the South African National Security Secretariat to present data and discuss the current state of foreign national appointments. She said the DPSA manages this matter with a clear focus on constitutional compliance, national employment priorities, and legal consistency -- anchored in policy, legislation, and employment regulations.
She outlined the DPSA’s policy position, which restricts the appointment of foreign nationals to permanent positions in the public service. Foreign nationals may be employed only on a contractual basis, and only when no suitably qualified South African citizen is available to fill the post. This policy underscored the obligation to prioritise South African citizens, particularly given the high youth unemployment rate and ongoing transformation goals. She described the verification and monitoring processes in place:
Citizenship and work permit status of applicants must be verified at the outset of the recruitment process.
Personnel records must accurately reflect citizenship status.
Departments were required to demonstrate that no qualified South African was available before appointing a foreign national.
Improper appointments were addressed through legal processes, including the termination of contracts and referrals under the Immigration Act.
The DPSA also conducts audits and clean-up initiatives in collaboration with the Department of Home Affairs (DHA), the State Security Agency (SSA), the Department of Employment and Labour (DEL), the Department of International Relations and Cooperation (DIRCO), and other relevant entities. These efforts aimed to eliminate irregular appointments, prevent identity fraud, and enforce compliance with employment laws.
Ms Naidoo highlighted that the DPSA was strengthening its oversight and monitoring mechanisms, noting a recent workshop with key stakeholders to enhance these processes. As an example, she cited the Department of Basic Education’s reduction in the appointment of foreign nationals to only those with permanent residence status, who were recognised as citizens, except for voting rights. She emphasised that limiting foreign national appointments aligned with the DPSA’s professionalisation agenda and the broader national transformation imperative to address historical imbalances and improve access to employment for marginalised South Africans. She also noted reforms aimed at improving access for South African citizens, such as allowing matriculants to apply for entry-level public service jobs and increasing outreach through modern platforms, including a WhatsApp group that now reaches nearly 80 000 citizens with weekly vacancy updates.
In conclusion, she reiterated that while the DPSA recognises the value of international expertise, such appointments must remain exceptional and well-justified, with the priority firmly on serving national development objectives, legal compliance, and the South African people.
Mr Vukela assured the Committee that the Department had taken note of all outstanding matters requiring written submissions, which would be provided by the agreed date of 12 July. He reiterated the Department’s commitment to supporting the Committee and remaining at its service.
