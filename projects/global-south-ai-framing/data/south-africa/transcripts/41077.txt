2025/26 Annual Performance Plans
The Portfolio Committee on Public Works and Infrastructure convened a meeting to discuss the Annual Performance Plans (APPs) of four entities: the Independent Development Trust (IDT), Agrément South Africa, the Construction Industry Development Board (CIDB), and the Council for the Built Environment (CBE).
A significant portion of the meeting involved discussions around apologies for absent officials, with concerns raised about the lack of proper documentation and accountability for overseas travel. There was a strong call for adherence to proper procedures for submitting apologies and a demand for more transparency regarding the purpose and cost of official travel.
The committee also reviewed resolutions from previous meetings, including scheduling future meetings more efficiently, conducting lifestyle audits, finalizing blacklisting of non-performing contractors, quarterly updates on Audit Action Plan implementation, and engaging with National Treasury on payment issues.
The IDT highlighted financial sustainability, client service, and organisational capability for infrastructure delivery in its presentation. The committee expressed deep concerns about the IDT's history of financial mismanagement, persistent deficits, wasteful expenditure on litigation and travel, and its request for additional funding without clear justification for improved performance. Questions were raised about the collection rate of management fees, the lack of local resources, and the need for quarterly financial and delivery scorecards. The IDT clarified that its request was for projects to manage, rather than direct funding, to help achieve financial sustainability. The importance of accurate reporting and avoiding misleading Parliament was heavily emphasised.
Agrément South Africa highlighted its focus on eco-labelling and the need for more internal labs and testing centres to ensure sustainability and effective product assessment across various environmental conditions.
The CIDB outlined its strategic interventions for 2025-2030, including the B.U.I.L.D Programme, monitoring industry performance, supporting marginalised groups, promoting best practices, and digitalisation. The committee questioned sanctions for non-compliant municipalities and state-owned enterprises, support for small and emerging contractors to advance in grading, and the progression of graduates into the industry. Concerns were also raised regarding contractors with high grades failing to complete projects and the potential for corruption in the grading system. The CIDB indicated plans for new research to create a reporting framework for all project stakeholders to ensure accountability.
The CBE's mandate of transforming the Built Environment Sector, developing professionals, and empowering women, youth, and persons with disabilities was noted. Strategic risks included inconsistent policy application, insufficient funding for tracking systems, inadequate IT integration, and challenges in revenue diversification.
Overall, the meeting underscored the committee's focus on financial accountability, effective project delivery, and the crucial role of oversight in ensuring entities meet their mandates and contribute to national priorities.

Independent Development Trust
(
IDT) Presentation
Mr Christopher Mulaudzi, General Manager, Strategy, Policy and Corporate Performance, IDT, led the committee through the presentation.
The IDT's mission is to manage the delivery of infrastructure and social development programmes as a Built Environment Implementing Agency. Their vision is to be a competitive, successful, and commercially driven Built Environment Implementing Agency, with a brand promise of assured efficient infrastructure delivery.
The strategy is built upon three pillars: securing financial sustainability by growing revenue streams, adapting Supply Chain Management (SCM) processes, and accelerating program expenditure; refocusing and streamlining client service offerings by adopting a client-centric culture and developing a diverse portfolio of services; and building organizational capability to be agile and deliver projects on time, within budget, and to the right quality.
The IDT's value proposition includes delivering quality social infrastructure, acting as a one-stop centre for infrastructure planning and delivery management, providing construction project quality and delivery cost assessment, and serving as a centre of state capacity for built environment professional services. They also focus on creating job opportunities for women, youth, and people with disabilities, developing a skilled workforce in the construction sector, supporting emerging contractors, and stimulating local industrialisation.
The plan aligns with the Medium Term Strategic Framework (MTSF) priorities, aiming for inclusive growth and job creation, reduced poverty, and a capable, ethical, and developmental state. Key targets for 2025-2030 include growing the number of completed infrastructure facilities by an average of 50%, increasing annual program expenditure on designated groups by 12% annually, and growing work opportunities at an annual rate of 5%. The IDT also aims to achieve an operating surplus of at least 3% and maintain a high compliance ratio. The presentation concludes by recommending that the Portfolio Committee on Public Works and Infrastructure consider and note the IDT’s 2025/26 Annual Performance Plan and Five-Year Strategic Plan (2025-2030).

Responding to the question, he explained how the department and its entities interfaced during the planning processes.
He stated that when entities embarked on their strategic planning sessions, they did so in interaction with the department’s planning unit. Through this engagement, the department was able to guide the entities to ensure alignment between their plans and the department’s broader strategic outcomes. He added that the entities also included their boards in the planning process to ensure that all areas of the plans were properly considered and guided.
Once these internal planning processes were completed, the entities would submit their plans to their respective boards for ratification. The board-approved plans would then be submitted to the department, in line with the requirements of the Public Finance Management Act (PFMA). He noted that the department received these board-sanctioned plans and reviewed them accordingly. Ultimately, the Director-General and the Minister would sign off on the plans before the APPs and strategic plans were presented to Parliament.

He confirmed that the department had indeed reviewed the documents received by the committee. Specifically regarding the IDT, he added that the department had also signed a shareholder compact with the entity, as it does with all other entities. He explained that this compact is separate from the APPs and strategic plans and serves to clarify roles and responsibilities between the shareholder (the department) and the entity.

Agrément South Africa (ASA) Presentation
Mr Richard Somanje, Chief Executive Officer, and Mr Mulamuleli Rannzwa, Chief Financial Officer, from Agrément SA led the committee through the presentation.
ASA presented its five-year Strategic Plan for the 2025/26 to 2029/30 financial years and its 2025/26 APP. This plan aligns with the foundational principles and priorities of the 7th Administration of the government, as outlined in the
Medium-Term Development Plan
(MTDP) 2024-2029. Despite complex socio-economic and built environment challenges, ASA aims to realise its strategic intent and MTDP priorities.
ASA, a statutory body established by the ASA Act of 2015, focuses on providing assurance of fitness-for-purpose for non-standardised construction products and systems. Its core mandate includes supporting integrated socio-economic development, promoting certified non-standardised products in local and international markets, minimising risks associated with these products, and serving as an impartial, internationally acknowledged assessment centre.
The organisation's strategic focus is built on five pillars: optimising the organisation, regulation capacity and enforcement, customer focus, financial sustainability, and stakeholder management and collaboration. ASA operates through three main programs: Administration and Governance, Regulation and Certification Services, and Proactive and Relevant Built Environment Interventions. Key strategic initiatives include developing human capital management and financial sustainability strategies, enhancing staff training, implementing performance management, reviewing the ASA Act, and streamlining certification processes.
ASA faces challenges such as insufficient budget, lack of testing sites and SANAS-accredited laboratories, reliance on external technical experts, limited human resources, absence of an enforcement and regulation strategy due to gaps in the ASA Act, and a lack of digitised business processes. To address these, key strategic projects involve reviewing the ASA Act to make certification mandatory, acquiring testing sites and laboratories, implementing an Eco-label Scheme and a Government Sustainable Building Rating Tool, and developing a financial sustainability model. ASA aims for an optimised organisation, improved certification services, and a transformed built environment, with targets including 100% implementation of corporate services and financial sustainability strategies, amending the ASA Act, and increasing the number of certified innovative building products

Mr Mulamuleli Rannzwa, Chief Financial Officer, Agrément South Africa, explained that their current revenue generation strategy was largely based on what the Act allowed them to do. He acknowledged that the certification process was voluntary, which impacted their revenue model. They charged certification fees for the issuance of certificates, as well as annual fees once a certificate was granted to a holder. Additionally, they charged for validity reviews conducted every three years. If a certificate holder needed to maintain their certificate, they would go out to validate it, ensuring that nothing had changed since the certificate was issued. If there were changes, they would assess whether those changes still met the previously approved criteria or whether the new changes were acceptable. For these activities, they charged fees to cover the resources allocated to the project.
He also mentioned the annual quality reviews, where certificate holders were audited to confirm their compliance with the approved standards. The fees from these audits helped recover the cost of employees involved in the project, as well as travel costs related to the work. As for the cost of professionals required to complete certifications, they transferred that cost to the client, including the sourcing of the necessary professionals for the process. The same applied to test sites, where they would source the sites and pass on the costs to the client.
This revenue model, however, created challenges when it came to supporting the development of previously disadvantaged groups. They were looking at their budget and acknowledging limitations in covering the costs of the professionals required to validate certain applications. He explained that, as they moved into the medium-to-long-term framework for 2027/28, there would be some reallocation between programs, particularly between Programme 2 and Programme 3. Programme 3 focused on research and proactive development in the built environment, and they had decided to move more funds into this area.
In Programme 2, most of the costs were transferred to the clients, with invoicing covering the work done by external parties and the costs associated with employees involved in the projects based on their time allocations.
He then touched on the ongoing development of their revenue and sustainability strategy. They had already begun engagements with the board, and he expected the strategy to be finalised in the coming quarter. Part of the strategy would involve addressing concerns and issues related to the approval of the Act review. He mentioned that they hoped to introduce some amendments to the Act, which would allow them to charge certain fees and levies that would help raise additional revenue. These proposals would be presented as part of the review process for the Act, and they are intended to seek inputs on those proposed amendments to better support the organisation's financial sustainability.
Construction Industry Development Board (CIDB) Presentation
Mr Bongani Dlala, Chief Executive Officer, CIDB, led the committee through the presentation.
The CIDB's mandate, derived from the CIDB Act 38 of 2000, involves providing strategic leadership, promoting sustainable industry growth and emerging sector participation, improving performance and best practices, ensuring uniform policy application and ethical standards, and monitoring/regulating industry and stakeholder performance through project and contractor registration.
The CIDB's vision is "A dynamic construction industry for sustainability," and its mission is "To strategically lead the South African construction industry towards improved socio-economic development for a better quality of life for all". Their values include service excellence, being ethical, authoritative, collaborative, and committed.
A SWOT analysis identified strengths such as being a construction industry knowledge hub and having guiding instruments, while weaknesses include prescript enforcement and transformation impact evaluation. Opportunities include industry capacitation through the B.U.I.L.D Programme and reviewing the CIDB Act, with threats being increased non-compliance and government fiscal constraints.
As of Q4 2024, the Register of Contractors (RoC) had 21,331 active contractors in Grades 2-9, with most concentrated in Grades 2-4. Black-owned contractors accounted for 68% across Grades 2-9, and women-owned contractors constituted 36%. Youth-owned contractors made up 12% across Grades 2-9. Public infrastructure underspending has improved since the 2020/21 fiscal year, with a 12% year-on-year increase in total infrastructure expenditure. State-owned companies were the largest contributors to public infrastructure expenditure, spending 95% of their budget. However, compliance with the Register of Projects (RoP) has shown a growth in non-compliance, rising from 506 in 2023Q4 to 1246 in 2024Q3.
Over the next five years (2025-2030), the CIDB will focus on supporting a high-performing construction industry and ensuring the organisation's sustainability. Key strategic interventions include implementing the B.U.I.L.D Programme, monitoring industry performance and transformation, building an enabling environment for marginalised groups, promoting best practice standards, and digitalising operations. Performance targets for 2025/26 include resolving 80% of audit issues, conducting one board performance assessment, paying 100% of invoices within 30 days, achieving 98% system uptime, establishing two partnerships, filling 90% of funded positions, and achieving a 60% employee satisfaction rating. The CIDB's budget for 2025/26 totals R313,148,355, with the largest allocations to Administration and B.U.I.L.D programs. The CIDB plans to enhance its contribution to the construction industry through the CIDB Amendment Bill and will continue to focus on improving compliance and driving industry governance and regulation.

He suggested that the CIDB should look into partnerships with existing private training institutions that were already succeeding in what the government was struggling to do. He criticised the current model where taxpayers were continuously funding government training programs, despite their inefficiencies, and he called for a shift in focus toward incentivising creativity and collaboration. He urged the department to force the issue of partnerships and hold officials accountable by linking their performance to clear KPIs.
In sum, he was calling for both an internal crackdown on corruption within the CIDB and a more creative, results-oriented approach to training, one that could leverage successful private sector models and push government institutions to be more innovative and effective.
Ms Kobe followed up by referring to the George Report and mentioned that, based on her knowledge, some of the contractors involved in the report were not registered with the CIDB. She expressed concern over why such contractors were allowed to work on large-scale projects, particularly in the human settlements sector, without being properly registered with the CIDB. She requested that the CEO provide clarity on this matter, emphasising the importance of ensuring that only registered and compliant contractors were working on such significant projects.
Mr Bongani Dlala, CEO, CIDB, responded by addressing the concerns regarding contractors under investigation, assuring that they would be sharing the details of the concluded cases. He emphasised that the CIDB also looked into the employees involved in such cases over the past few years. He mentioned that he had personally signed at least three termination letters and pointed out that there had been instances where individuals resigned during an investigation. He further questioned whether CIDB should open criminal cases against these employees, given the gravity of the situation, as the corruption had far-reaching consequences, such as delays in project completion and the resulting negative impact on service delivery. He reiterated CIDB’s zero-tolerance policy toward such corruption and discussed the possibility of more stringent actions to deter these kinds of activities.
Regarding training, he clarified that the focus had been on training company owners, not graduates. He acknowledged the importance of partnerships with the private sector, recognising that half of the construction industry is driven by private players, and emphasised the need for their active involvement in development efforts.
On the issue of the George Report and the unregistered contractors, he explained that the contractors in question were registered with a different entity under Human Settlements, due to an exemption in the regulations. He noted that this exemption had allowed certain accommodation projects, which were registered with the National Home Builders Registration Council (NHBRC), to bypass CIDB registration. However, he conceded that this exemption might have been intended for smaller projects and not large-scale structures. As a result, CIDB had reached out to the NHBRC to discuss whether this exemption should continue or if regulatory changes were necessary, emphasising that the CIDB’s role focuses on risk mitigation, health and safety, and ensuring that contractors have the relevant experience for projects of similar complexity.
He concluded by mentioning that there were some remaining questions, and perhaps a representative from Public Works could assist in answering the remaining questions.
Mr Mahlangu explained that the process had progressed significantly, having already reached the stage of conducting interviews for candidates. After the interviews, a list of recommended candidates had been finalised, along with a reserve list. He added that the necessary documentation regarding qualifications and the steps followed in the process were also being processed. Currently, the process is at the stage where it is being finalised by the chair of the selection committee and routed to the minister for consideration. Following this, the process involves vetting, cabinet approval, and eventually the formal appointment of the members. He indicated that they aimed to have the appointments finalised by the end of July.
Council for the Built Environment (CBE) Presentation
Dr Msizi Myeza, Chief Executive Officer, and Mr Phuti Manamela, Manager: Strategic Planning, Monitoring and Evaluation, CBE, led the committee through the presentation.
The CBE, established under the Council for the Built Environment Act, 43 of 2000, is a Schedule 3A Public Entity. Its central mandate is transforming the Built Environment Sector, creating skilled Built Environment Professionals, and expediting the empowerment of women, youth, and persons with disabilities. The vision for 2035 is a sustainable, intelligent, inclusive, adaptive/agile, and thriving Built Environment. The mission is to advance a sustainable Built Environment by promoting public interest, professional growth, and best practices in health, safety, and governance.
The presentation also detailed strategic risks, including inconsistent policy application by CBEP, insufficient funding for professional development tracking systems, inadequate IT system integration, and failure in revenue diversification strategies. Furthermore, it addressed CBE's alignment with national priorities outlined in the Medium-Term Development Plan (MTDP) 2025-2029, focusing on inclusive growth and job creation, reducing poverty, and fostering a capable, ethical, and developmental state.
Key outcomes for CBE's Strategic Plan 2025-2030 include strengthening administrative excellence and financial sustainability, developing a capable Built Environment Sector, fostering skilled Built Environment Professionals, engaging in research, policy, advocacy and transformation, enhancing CBE's reputation and public engagement, and protecting and promoting public interest. The APP also outlines output indicators and annual targets across various programs such as Administration, Built Environment Sector Empowerment, Professional Skills and Capacity Development, Research and Knowledge Management, and Public Protection and Legislation. The presentation concluded with a recommendation for the Portfolio Committee to approve the CBE 2025/26 Annual Performance Plan.
