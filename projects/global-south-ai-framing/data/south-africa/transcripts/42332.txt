Meeting Summary
The Standing Committee on the Auditor-General (SCOAG) held its first meeting of the year to consider and adopt its Committee Strategic Plan (CSP), Annual Performance Plan (APP) for 2025/26, previous minutes, and its Term 1 Committee programme. The meeting also included a presentation on an international comparative study to inform a proposed study tour.
The Committee adopted the minutes of meetings held on 21 and 28 November 2025 without amendments. It then turned to the CSP (2025–2029) and APP (2025/26). Members noted that the CSP had previously been workshopped and tentatively adopted, with inputs incorporated from Members. Discussion focused on strengthening elements of the plan, particularly around deepening public and stakeholder engagement and ensuring that such engagement is measurable and reportable in the Committee’s Legacy Report. Members also raised the need for clearer timelines and a framework for reviewing and potentially realigning the Public Audit Act (PAA), especially in light of developments such as artificial intelligence, automation in auditing, and emerging governance challenges.
There was discussion on expanding the Committee’s focus to respond to current national developments, including allegations relating to policing and corruption, with suggestions that SCOAG work with the Auditor-General of South Africa (AGSA) to scrutinise financial management in implicated departments. The Chairperson also flagged upcoming institutional matters, including the end of the Deputy Auditor-General’s contract and the Auditor-General’s term in 2027, noting that SCOAG may play a role in future appointment processes. The CSP was adopted with the understanding that it can be reviewed and refined alongside the 2026/27 APP.
Members further emphasised the importance of SCOAG asserting its independence through its own oversight visits, while acknowledging the limits of its mandate. Concerns were also raised about ensuring responsible internal budgeting and avoiding disproportionate expenditure in the fourth quarter.
The Committee Researcher presented a comparative study examining funding models, independence, professional audit oversight, and real-time/automated auditing practices in jurisdictions including South Korea, Canada, Australia, Singapore, and the Seychelles. A central theme in the discussion was AGSA’s funding model and financial sustainability, with some Members expressing concern that the current model places undue emphasis on debt collection. Members agreed that the choice of study tour destination should be guided by a clear problem statement, particularly around funding, independence, and innovation in auditing. It was confirmed that, unlike South Africa, the other jurisdictions are funded directly from the public purse. Members agreed to submit their preferred study tour destinations via a WhatsApp poll by the end of the following week.
Finally, the Committee adopted its Term 1 Programme, including its next meeting scheduled for 13 March 2026. The meeting concluded with congratulations to AGSA for being recognised as a top employer and to several municipalities for achieving clean or unqualified audits.
Meeting report
The Chairperson welcomed everyone to the meeting, which marked the first meeting of the year. Members joined both physically and via virtual platform. Apologies were received from Mr E Marais (DA), Mr A Beesley (DA) and Mr S Mwali (MKP).
The Chairperson took Members through the agenda for the day and proceeded with the adoption of the previous meeting minutes.
Adoption of minutes
The Chairperson provided a high-level overview of the minutes for adoption, which included the meetings held on 21 November 2025 and 28 November 2025. No corrections were made; both minutes were duly adopted.
Committee Five-Year Strategic Plan and Annual Performance Plan 2025/26
The Chairperson reminded Members of their previous discussions on the Committee Strategic Plan (CSP). The Committee had adopted the draft version and elected to revisit the CSP. The Chairperson suggested going through the focus areas of the CSP and, where necessary, amendments be made. He invited proposals from the Members.
Mr S Vilakazi (DA) highlighted that the CSP was for 2025/26 and essentially the Committee was only left with a month and a half thereof. He had no issues with the CSP; however, he did not see much room for discussion, considering the 2025/26 term period was coming to an end.
The Chairperson clarified that the APP was for 2025/26, and the CSP was for 2025-2029. He further reminded Members that the Committee awaited the adoption of Parliament's Strategic Plan before the National Assembly before the respective committees proceeded with their own. This was a process which commenced last year, August, with the Committee having its own first draft in September.
Mr I Subrathie (ANC) began by complimenting the Committee Secretary, Ms Zubeida Arnold, for the exceptional drafting and detail in the Committee minutes. He said he sat in other committees, and Ms Arnold’s quality of work stood out among the rest and he appreciated the efficiency and detail thereof.
Regarding the CSP, he said the Standing Committee on the Auditor General (SCOAG) would have management (Manco), which would have applied itself and taken inputs from SCOAG meetings in drafting the CSP. He suggested that someone from Manco provide the Committee with a brief explanation on the approach taken in the drafting of the CSP. He added while Members would have looked at the CSP it would be remiss for someone from Manco not to provide a brief overview of the CSP.
The Chairperson reiterated Mr Subrathie’s sentiments on the excellent work of Ms Arnold and the Committee Secretariat. He appreciated the work done by the Committee support team. Regarding a representative from Manco, he said the Content Advisor, Mr Xolisile Mgxaji, would be joining the meeting shortly. He unfortunately went to the wrong venue but was on his way to the meeting.
Ms V Mente-Nkuna (EFF) requested the Committee consider in its CSP, the new developments in the country, especially issues relating to the Madlanga Commission and, more especially, the Ad Hoc Committee to Investigate Allegations made by Lieutenant General Nhlanhla Mkhwanazi. She suggested the Committee work together with the AG and look at the finances of the departments which were troublesome/implicated. Similarly to how the AG looked at the finances spent during the Covid period by the various departments and was able to formulate a position and curb a lot of corruption around the expenditure which occurred during the time.
She highlighted that the exorbitant spending on policing was already highlighted; however, there were no reasonable explanations. There were also tenders which took place in the police; she made an example of the tender, which was meant to be given to Mr Cat Matlala, but was then given to someone else. She said it was important for the Committee to look into this and get the necessary information. She emphasised it was important for SCOAG not to be left behind in giving the country confidence in how the finances of the country were spent in those transactions. She said the Committee should consider adding this to the CSP.
The Chairperson thanked Ms Mente-Nkuna and said this was an important point she raised, the CSP highlighted the importance of assisting and ensuring that the function of the AG also translated into curbing corruption within government whether it be inflated prices, corruption or otherwise. He said the CSP spoke to this very point, through researching other jurisdictions and looking at how to enhance real time audits and value of money.
The Content Advisor, Mr X Mgxaji joined the meeting, the Chairperson requested he provide guidance on the CSP and conduct a brief overview of the CSP highlighting the focus areas.
Mr Mgxaji said he misunderstood the objectives of the meeting today. He reminded the Committee that he had presented to them the CSP and the APP in the Committee's workshop. This was followed by inputs and additions made by Members which were subsequently included in the CSP. It was then tentatively adopted, and Members agreed that if there were additional items Members wished to add, this would then be done. He highlighted that he had not received any further inputs from Members and was under the impression that today’s meeting was for such further inputs/additions. The CSP remained the same as was tentatively adopted previously. If Members had any further inputs, he would provide further guidance thereon.
The Chairperson thanked Mr Mgxaji for the clarity provided and said there were a further two aspects to be considered, the first being that the Deputy Auditor-General’s (DAG) contract was coming to an end, and the current DAG was not available to take up the AG’s office. Therefore an appointment would need to be made, and process conducted where the AG appoints the DAG after consultation with other mechanisms such as SCOAG. He asked Members also to be reminded that the current AG’s term was coming to an end in 2027. Previously, Parliament would establish an ad hoc committee to make the appointment of the AG. This practice was adopted in line with the Constitution's guidance, which provides the National Assembly Speaker with the power to appoint the AG. The SCOAG thus had proportional representation powers to assist in such appointment. Therefore SCOAG would be in a position to conduct the interviews. He asked Members to have a discussion and consider whether the Committee was in the best position to conduct those interviews or not. There were arguments against this, arguing that SCOAG was too close to the current AG administration and so forth. He asked the Committee to have these discussions and said it was something that should be added as a focus point in the CSP.
He agreed with Mr Mgxaji that the Committee was briefed on the CSP, and inputs made during the workshop were included in the updated CSP. He asked Members for any further inputs.
Mr Subrathie appreciated the clarity provided and asked what inputs were added to the CSP in order for Members to understand the enhancements.
He had had two inputs, the first was regarding the CSP reference to public and stakeholder engagement. While the CSP made mention of such engagements, it was silent on how Members were to deepen public engagement. The mandate emphasised accountability and transparency however the plan did not provide enough detail on how this would be implemented or measured. How was this going to be done, he called on Members to engage on what such public engagement together with the AG would entail.
Secondly the CSP made mention of the need to evaluate and realign the Public Audit Act but it did not provide a timeline or framework on how this will be done. He requested that the Content Advisor provide clarity on these two points.
The Chairperson said regarding the inputs made to the CSP, there was a draft CSP circulated to Members in order for inputs to be made, and inputs made at the Committee workshop were included. Members were also invited to make written submissions, and inputs were received from the DA and ANC. No further additions were received thereafter. He requested Mr Mgxaji speak to the two other issues raised by Mr Subrathie.
Mr Mgxaji highlighted that in a previous meeting, where Members discussed the CSP, Members had discussed the importance of public engagement; however, no inputs were made on how this would be done. He emphasised it was important that it be measurable in order for it to be clear on how it would be achieved.
He said he would further need to consider the timeline for the reevaluation of the Public Audit Act (PAA) and how it should be done.
The Chairperson asked for further discussion on the public engagement point.
Mr Mgxaji emphasised extensive discussions were had in the last meeting with regard to this however it was not resolved how this would be measured. It was important for this to be done in a manner which was measurable because Members would need to account for all they have done in their Legacy Report once their term came to an end. The Legacy Report would measure the actions of the Committee against its outlined objectives. He would need to take Members views and incorporate same into the CSP.
The Chairperson expressed agreement and said it was important for Members to continuously keep the PAA at the back of their minds and highlight any irregularities should these arise. The Chairperson gave an example of the first certificate of debt which was issued last year. He said some irregularities might be seen therein. There might be actions happening in practice that require alignment with regulations to further streamline matters. The PAA might not be aligned with the new world with artificial intelligence and automation of audits and so forth. Therefore, these changes might necessitate an amendment to the PAA.
The Committee would further need to have discussions on the suggestions made by Ms Mente-Nkuna to ascertain a way forward to address corruption and irregularities.
Mr Vilakazi said he agreed with sentiments made, especially Mr Subrathie’s sentiments, regarding public engagement and how the Committee would go about this.
He explained his understanding was that the CSP was deliberately high-level and the detail thereof would be broken down in the APP. He requested Mr Mgxaji’s input on this.
The Chairperson said this was correct and that the same would be included in the 2026/27 APP, which the Committee would endeavour to complete as soon as possible.
Mr Mgxaji said he agreed with the Chairperson and added that the CSP was not set in stone and Members had the liberty to make changes and ensure it was aligned with the Committee APP. Members would have such a review opportunity around March 2026 in order to finalise the 2026/27 APP.
The Chairperson suggested the Committee adopt the CSP with such review in mind.
Mr Vilakazi added the need for the Committee to conduct its own independent oversight visits outside of the joint committee's oversight visits. He highlighted that the independence and identity of the Committee must be asserted. He strongly encouraged the plan in the CSP for the Committee to conduct its own oversight visits, looking at underperforming municipalities.
The Chairperson highlighted that there was already an oversight visit planned to the Free State to be conducted by the Committee. He further added that there would not be any joint oversight visits for this year. If there was an invitation for a joint oversight then Members would be alerted and a decision taken on whether or not to attend and which Members from the Committee would attend such oversight. The Chairperson agreed with sentiments made on the importance of the Committee conducting its own oversights; however, he reminded Members that the SCOAG’s mandate was limited and the joint oversights assisted in this regard to expose the Committee to more issues, considering its limited mandate, while not overreaching on the other C
committees' mandates.
Mr Vilakazi agreed and said that while the Committee's mandate was limited, it had key criteria which required general information before it could zone in on its core mandate and protection of the AG’s office.
Further, he cautioned Manco on the fourth quarter being where most of the Committees' spending was located. He cautioned against fiscal dumping and called for fair distribution of funds amongst the four quarters. He emphasised it was important that the Committee spread itself out.
The Chairperson said it was well noted and highlighted that there was a reason sometimes why some items only occurred in the fourth quarter; he agreed with the caution against fiscal dumping.
The Committee agreed to adopt the CSP for now and should amendments need to be made it would be reviewed and the necessary amendments applied thereto. The Chairperson invited the Committee Researcher to provide a high-level overview of the study titled International Comparative Study of Best Approaches in Financing Models for the Auditor-Generals, Immediate Audits, Professional Audit Oversight and Automated Auditing based on the study tour options with their respective funding models.
International Comparative Study of Best Approaches in Financing Models for the Auditor-Generals, Immediate Audits, Professional Audit Oversight and Automated Auditing
Mr Mbuyiselo Hlekiso, Committee Researcher
,
provided a high-level overview of the Comparative Study of the various countries, which include Singapore, Seychelles, Australia, Canada and South Korea. He highlighted that South Korea had the longest-standing funding model of all the other jurisdictions. Some jurisdictions, like South Africa, were only now using AI in their auditing. He requested that Members provide the Secretariat with two options each. Once options were finalised, these, together with supporting documents, would be submitted to the Chair of Chairs for approval.
The Chairperson thanked Mr Hlekiso and said the comparative study, regardless of the study tour, was great work which would assist Members in their overall work. He urged Members to read the document, which presented Members with a variety of ways in which the various jurisdictions did things and how to best approach and assist the AG in its work. He invited Members' input.
Mr Vilakazi began by stating he had not read the document and therefore was unable to comment on the substance thereof. He asked when the research team would like to have feedback from Members and asked that they provide the Committee with any guidance in making a decision.
Ms Nkuna-Mente also requested a turnaround time on when Members were required to make their recommendation. She further requested that the Secretary create a poll to share on the Committee WhatsApp Group in order for Members to vote for their preferred jurisdiction. In this way voting would also be more transparent.
Mr Subrathie welcomed the detailed document and research performed and said that the ANC study group had interrogated the report and further drafted a summarised table thereof, which he was happy to share with Members if Members would like to have sight thereof.
He added it was important for the Committee to determine what question or problem it was looking to solve for the AGSA in choosing to conduct such oversight. Given the various issues South Africa faced, one of which was the funding model of the AGSA, that is, its financial sustainability. Due to its funding model, he expressed concern that the AGSA was becoming more of a debt collector than a professional body. It spent a lot of its energy chasing payments. He was of the view that once this was fixed, the AG could address more pertinent issues such as service delivery and fighting corruption.
It was important to have a clear endgame of what the Committee was seeking to achieve. With that said, he asked which of the countries which had been researched were countries that were funded through pure budgets, i.e., National Treasury of those countries. In other words, they were not modelled in the way South Africa was, but were purely funded by government. He suggested these be the starting point, looking at where such funding model was working and not stifling innovation.
He understood that one of the risks of such model of government funding was the AG potentially being captured, however he argued for a model where the AG was centrally funded through a budget. He added that from the countries provided, Canada was one which stood out for him, it was funded by public funds, it operated independently from government, and it ticked boxes in terms of immediate orders, professional audit oversight and automated auditing. Second was Singapore which he felt was also very innovative however the problem was its population size compared to South Africa.
He implored Members to think about what the problem statement of the AGSA is that it wishes to solve, and then hunt for those countries that have resolved that problem.
The Chairperson reminded Members of the issues of the AGSA which were raised in its workshop, these include the issues highlighted by Mr Subrathie, such as independence, professional audit oversight, real-time automated auditing, as well as the professional audit oversight, who guards the guard. While the Committee was an oversight body, Members were not professional auditors. These were the four aspects which Members sought to address and hoped for one jurisdiction which would hopefully address these.
He said the document by the ANC would be welcomed, and he invited Mr Hlekiso to respond to the questions raised.
Mr Hlekiso said that with all the jurisdictions, the AG was funded by the public purse except in South Africa. However, he agreed that the question of independence does come into question, which is why the AGSA took on a different model, although it too had its own problems.
Regarding a timeline, he said this was all dependent on the parliamentary programme as to when the study tour should take place.
Mr Mgxaji interjected and said it was in fact not dependent on the parliamentary programme but rather on the Committee plans, which were scheduled for this fourth quarter. The plan was to have the big expenditure happen in this fourth term.
Mr Hlekiso added that it was important in its considerations for the Committee to consider whether the jurisdiction decided upon, whether its Parliament was open and not in recess, in order to also see the Parliamentary Committee which oversees the AG within that country for comprehensive engagement.
The Chairperson proposed that Members provide their votes before the end of next week in order to get the ball rolling on arrangements, etc., and further align with the Committee's programme and availability of Members.
Mr Vilakazi supported the proposal to submit their options by the end of next week considering the long processes of Parliament. He added the sooner the better in order for the Secretariat to set up an itinerary for the desired destinations and plan accordingly.
Ms M Mofokeng (ANC) seconded the suggestion for Members to make their vote on the WhatsApp poll before the end of next week.
The Chairperson proceeded to the final agenda item.
Consideration of draft Committee Programme focusing on Term 1
The Chairperson took Members through the draft Committee Programme, highlighting the next Committee meeting on 13 March 2026. He highlighted that Parliament was moving towards a more physical meeting approach. The Dome would be handed over by the Department of Public Works and Infrastructure later in the morning. He proceeded with a high level overview of the programme and requested Members adopt the programme.
Ms L Ligaraba (ANC) moved for the adoption of the programme, which was duly adopted.
He invited Members to make any final comments before closing the meeting.
Ms Ligaraba applauded the work done by the SCOAG Secretariat and further congratulated the AGSA for being awarded the top employer for both 2025 and 2026 by the Top Employer Institute. This marked the second year of such global recognition. She further extended her congratulations to municipalities which received a clean or unqualified audit, including eThekwini Metropolitan Municipality, uMhlathuze Local Municipality and Ugu District Municipality. She wished to applaud all the municipalities and departments for their commitment to sound governance and responsible stewardship of public resources.
Closing remarks
The Chairperson thanked Ms Ligaraba for bringing this to the Committee’s attention and thanked Members for their participation and contributions. Further information regarding the study tour or any changes to the Committee programme would be communicated to Members. He wished everyone well and said Members would see one another at the State of the Nation address. He wished Members a fantastic weekend.
Meeting adjourned
