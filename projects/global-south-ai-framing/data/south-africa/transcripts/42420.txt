Meeting Summary
Video
The Portfolio Committee on Water and Sanitation convened a meeting to consider the potential establishment of a parliamentary Water, Sanitation and Hygiene (WASH) Caucus and to learn from international experiences in strengthening parliamentary leadership in the sector. The session began with remarks emphasising that water and sanitation remained critical to national concerns affecting public health, dignity, economic development, and social stability. It was highlighted that Parliament had an important role in ensuring effective oversight, leadership, and accountability to improve service delivery. The meeting was therefore intended to provide Members with an opportunity to reflect on the current state of water and sanitation in South Africa and to explore whether a dedicated parliamentary caucus could enhance coordination and focus on WASH-related issues.
During the introductory session, representatives from international parliamentary bodies and civil society organisations outlined their involvement in water governance and WASH advocacy. It was explained that parliamentary WASH caucuses in several countries functioned as voluntary, non-partisan platforms through which legislators collaborated with civil society, development partners and technical experts to strengthen oversight, influence legislation and advocate for increased sector funding. These structures were described as complementary to formal parliamentary committees, providing a flexible space for Members of Parliament to engage with stakeholders, promote policy reforms and amplify the voices of communities affected by water and sanitation challenges.
A presentation was then delivered on the global and national state of water, sanitation and hygiene within the framework of Sustainable Development Goal 6, which aims to ensure universal access to water and sanitation by 2030. It was reported that although global access to basic services had improved in recent years, billions of people worldwide still lacked safely managed drinking water, sanitation, and hygiene services. Within the South African context, the presentation indicated that access to improved services had expanded over the past two decades; however, significant inequalities remained across provinces and between metropolitan and rural areas. Persistent challenges such as unreliable water supply, uneven sanitation improvements and gaps in hygiene practices were highlighted, and it was noted that South Africa remained off track to meet the SDG 6 targets by 2030 without accelerated investment, stronger governance and improved infrastructure resilience.
Representatives from Zambia, Uganda and Liberia shared their experiences in establishing parliamentary WASH caucuses. It was reported that these structures had been created to strengthen advocacy, improve legislative oversight, and mobilise greater political attention to water and sanitation issues. The experiences presented illustrated how caucuses had enabled Members of Parliament to influence budget allocations, sponsor legislation, conduct oversight and work collaboratively with civil society organisations and development partners. It was also noted that, in several cases, caucuses had contributed to increased national budget allocations for the sector and strengthened partnerships aimed at improving service delivery.
The role of civil society organisations in supporting parliamentary WASH caucuses was also discussed. It was explained that civil society groups often provided technical expertise, research support and coordination assistance to Members of Parliament, while also ensuring that the perspectives of communities and vulnerable groups were reflected in policy discussions. At the same time, it was emphasised that clear boundaries were necessary, with political leadership remaining the responsibility of parliamentarians while civil society organisations served primarily as knowledge brokers and facilitators of stakeholder engagement.
During the discussion session, Members expressed appreciation for the presentations and noted the value of learning from international experiences. Questions were raised regarding lessons learned in establishing caucuses, the relationship between a caucus and existing parliamentary committees, funding arrangements and mechanisms to ensure balanced representation of rural and urban communities. Responses indicated that caucuses generally complemented rather than duplicated the work of formal committees and were typically supported through modest administrative arrangements, parliamentary staff assistance or limited donor support. It was further emphasised that informal relationship-building among Members of Parliament and stakeholders was often crucial during the early stages of establishing such structures.
Members also discussed governance considerations, including the need to safeguard parliamentary processes and prevent potential misuse of caucus platforms. It was explained that these risks could be mitigated through clear constitutions, well-defined terms of reference, and diverse membership structures. The discussion further highlighted that parliamentary caucuses could function effectively as multi-party platforms, similar to other caucuses already operating within Parliament, provided that transparency and accountability were maintained.
In closing remarks, participants emphasised the importance of strengthening parliamentary engagement in the WASH sector to accelerate progress towards global and national water and sanitation goals. It was observed that many countries were still behind in achieving universal access to WASH services and that parliamentary caucuses could play a significant role in advocating for stronger oversight, improved policy implementation and increased domestic investment. Participants expressed appreciation for the opportunity to exchange experiences and indicated support for continued collaboration and dialogue on the matter.
The meeting concluded with an indication that further engagement would follow. It was noted that a report outlining the outcomes of the discussion and possible next steps would be prepared and shared with parliamentary leadership for consideration. The aim would be to determine whether the establishment of a Parliamentary WASH Caucus could strengthen Parliament’s role in addressing water and sanitation challenges in South Africa.
Following the departure of external participants, the Committee briefly discussed an internal procedural matter relating to the Water Services Amendment Act. It was reported that the Committee had received correspondence from the National Economic Development and Labour Council (NEDLAC) indicating that it had not been consulted during the legislative process and requesting that the matter be postponed for three months. Members were informed that legal advice had been requested to clarify whether consultation with NEDLAC was required in this instance and to determine the potential implications for the legislative process. It was agreed that the Committee would await the legal opinions from both the State Attorneys and Parliament’s legal services before deciding on the appropriate course of action. Once the advice had been received, a special meeting would be convened to consider the matter and determine the way forward.

Presentation: An Overview of the State of Water and Sanitation in South Africa
Mr Thomani Manungufala, Committee Content Advisor, delivered the presentation which outlined the global and national context of Water, Sanitation and Hygiene (WaSH) within the framework of the 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development. It was explained that the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), particularly Goal 6, sought to ensure the availability and sustainable management of water and sanitation for all. The presentation indicated that WaSH remained critical for preventing waterborne diseases, improving public health, promoting school attendance and learning, advancing dignity and gender equality, and supporting sustainable community development. However, it was noted that global progress towards the SDG 6 targets remained off track, partly due to persistent data gaps and challenges in monitoring implementation.
It was further reported that global access to water, sanitation, and hygiene services had improved between 2015 and 2024. The proportion of the global population using safely managed drinking water, sanitation, and basic hygiene services had increased steadily during this period. Despite these gains, billions of people remained without adequate services, including 2.2 billion without safely managed drinking water, 3.4 billion without safely managed sanitation, and 1.7 billion lacking basic hygiene services at home. The presentation also highlighted that access to WaSH services in schools remained insufficient, leaving hundreds of millions of children worldwide without reliable water, sanitation or hygiene facilities, with Sub-Saharan Africa identified as the region lagging furthest behind.
With regard to South Africa, the presentation provided an overview of the state of WaSH based largely on findings from the 2024 General Household Survey conducted by Statistics South Africa. It examined key indicators including access to drinking water, sanitation facilities, reliability of water supply, household perceptions of water quality, and hygiene practices. While it was noted that access to improved water and sanitation services had expanded over the past two decades, the presentation emphasised that significant disparities persisted between provinces, metropolitan and non-metropolitan areas and different socio-economic groups. It was also reported that challenges such as declining water access in certain provinces, frequent water supply interruptions, uneven sanitation improvements and gaps in hygiene behaviour continued to undermine progress.
In conclusion, it was indicated that although some progress had been achieved, major inequalities in WASH services remained across provinces. Higher levels of access were observed in provinces such as the Western Cape and Gauteng, while areas including Limpopo, Mpumalanga and KwaZulu-Natal remained comparatively underserved. The presentation further reported that widespread water interruptions, persistent sanitation challenges in rural areas, and declining hygiene practices posed risks to public health and environmental sustainability. It was therefore emphasised that South Africa remained off track to achieve SDG 6 by 2030 and would require accelerated investment, stronger governance and more resilient infrastructure to improve water, sanitation and hygiene outcomes.

Presentation: The Case of Zambia
Mr Twaambo Mutinta MP expressed appreciation for the presentation delivered by South Africa, indicating that it provided a detailed and insightful analysis of the water, sanitation and hygiene (WASH) sector. It was noted that the presentation highlighted important realities such as disparities in access between rural and urban communities, provincial inequalities, and concerns relating to water quality and service delivery. Through this structure, stakeholders were able to provide evidence-based policy briefs, engage in discussions on legislative reforms and contribute to sector budgeting processes. Mr Mutinta concluded by noting that the caucus had become one of the most active platforms within the Zambian Parliament and expressed openness to continued learning and cooperation with other parliaments working to advance the WASH agenda.

Presentation: WASH in Uganda
Mr Silas Aogon MP, the Chairperson of the Uganda Parliamentary Forum on Water, Sanitation and Hygiene (WASH), indicated that access to water, sanitation and hygiene was a fundamental right and an essential component of the Sustainable Development Goals, particularly SDG 6, which sought to ensure the availability and sustainable management of water and sanitation for all by 2030. It was explained that legislators had a critical responsibility to ensure that no one was left behind in accessing these services. Mr Aogon further reported that the Uganda Parliamentary Forum on WASH had been established in 2012, initially as an informal platform before later being formalised following research conducted by WaterAid Uganda. The forum was described as a voluntary, non-partisan platform that brought together Members of Parliament, civil society organisations, the private sector, development partners and technical experts to strengthen collaboration and improve WASH outcomes in Uganda.
It was further explained that the work of the forum was aligned with the four key constitutional roles of Members of Parliament: legislation, representation, oversight, and budget appropriation. Through these functions, Members influenced laws and policies related to WASH, represented the interests of communities, particularly vulnerable groups such as women, children, persons with disabilities and the girl child, and conducted oversight to ensure that government commitments translated into effective service delivery. The forum also advocated for increased and protected budget allocations for the WASH sector, organised national dialogues and annual symposia, and worked closely with civil society and other stakeholders to promote evidence-based advocacy. Mr Aogon concluded by noting that the forum had contributed to achievements in areas such as policy development, improved sanitation in schools, increased awareness and stronger sector partnerships aimed at advancing WASH goals in Uganda.
The Liberian case
Mr Goshua II MP briefly presented on the Liberian case. He greeted the meeting once more and reiterated that he served as a Member of the Liberian Legislature and Chairperson of the Liberian Legislative Caucus on Water, Sanitation and Hygiene (WASH), which comprised both Representatives and Senators. He explained that the caucus had been established in 2016 to promote the WASH policy agenda, strengthening budgetary support for the sector and exercising oversight to improve governance within the WASH sector in Liberia.
He noted that Liberia’s WASH sector remained heavily dependent on external assistance, with approximately 96 per cent of funding originating from external donors and only about 4.3 per cent contributed through national resources. He further highlighted the scale of service delivery challenges, indicating that around 25 per cent of the population did not have access to safe drinking water, approximately 18 per cent had access to improved sanitation services, and hygiene coverage remained extremely low. He added that more than 45 per cent of the population continued to practise open defecation across the country’s 15 counties.
Mr Goshua explained that the withdrawal of certain major donor partners had further drawn attention to the vulnerability of the sector and had prompted the caucus to call upon the Government of Liberia to intensify its efforts and prioritise domestic financing for WASH. He stressed that increased national investment was necessary to ensure sustainability and national ownership of WASH programmes.
He reported that the caucus had engaged its members and stakeholders, including civil society organisations, to highlight the sector’s challenges and mobilise support within Parliament and the executive branch. Through these engagements, the caucus had advocated for stronger allocations during the national budget preparation process for the 2026 financial year.
Mr Goshua indicated that these efforts had yielded some progress. The budget for the National Commission on WASH had increased from USD 600 000 to USD 1 million. The allocation for the Liberia Water and Sewer Corporation, a government service provider, had been increased to approximately USD 9.4 million. In addition, USD 500,000 had been allocated to the Accelerated Community Development Programme, a joint initiative between the Government of Liberia and the United Nations Development Programme aimed at expanding WASH services in rural areas. A further USD 700 000 had been included in the legislature's budget to support WASH initiatives across Liberia's 73 districts.
He also noted that, although the WASH caucus itself was not a formal parliamentary committee reporting to plenary, their advocacy efforts had led to the establishment of a formal standing committee on WASH within the legislature, with the approval of the Speaker and parliamentary leadership. This committee would provide direct oversight and work alongside the caucus in advancing the WASH agenda.
Mr Goshua concluded by stating that, although progress remained gradual, it was steady, and he expressed optimism that further gains would be realised as the budget was implemented. He added that he looked forward to learning from the experiences of other countries with established WASH caucuses and expressed hope for greater collaboration across Africa to strengthen the sector and improve the lives of citizens.
Remarks: Global Parliamentary Water Network
Mr John Oldfield, representing the Global Parliamentary Water Network, indicated that he wished to afford Honourable Goshua from Liberia the opportunity to speak before him. He clarified that he was not a Member of the United States Congress or Senate, but rather a representative of civil society. He stated that he would reserve most of his detailed remarks for later in the session but would briefly provide context regarding the Congressional Global Water Security Caucus in Washington, D.C., which he informally supported.
He explained that the United States Congress comprised two chambers: the House of Representatives and the Senate. The Congressional Global Water Security Caucus was currently situated only within the House of Representatives and had been established approximately six years prior. He described it as a formal yet informal structure—neither a statutory committee nor a commission, but rather a caucus or advocacy network, similar in nature to those described by representatives from Zambia and Uganda.
Mr Oldfield highlighted a key distinction between the United States caucus and those in Uganda, Liberia and Zambia. Whereas the latter focused primarily on domestic water, sanitation and hygiene (WASH) challenges, the U.S. caucus concentrated exclusively on global water security issues rather than domestic matters. Accordingly, it worked closely with the House Foreign Affairs Committee and relevant subcommittees of the House Appropriations Committee.
He outlined the caucus’s principal objectives, namely, to support the passage of legislation prioritising safe drinking water, sanitation and hygiene globally, and to advocate for increased appropriations for these issues. He noted that the caucus comprised approximately 14 Members and was co-chaired by representatives from the two major political parties, the Democratic and Republican parties. He emphasised that water security was a non-partisan, or post-partisan, issue that united Members across political divides, as well as bridging rural and urban constituencies.
Mr Oldfield further stated that the caucus enjoyed strong support from civil society organisations and maintained close working relationships with relevant technical departments, particularly the U.S. Department of State. He added that the caucus leadership also benefited from robust support from congressional staff.
He observed that the current context of foreign assistance in the United States presented challenges, making the work of the Global Water Security Caucus particularly important. He noted that when the United States Agency for International Development (USAID) had been operational, it had served as the caucus’s primary technical partner. Following reductions in foreign assistance and the closure of USAID, the Department of State had assumed that role. He reported that, through the caucus’s advocacy efforts, reductions to water and sanitation budgets had been mitigated, even though broader foreign assistance allocations had been significantly reduced or eliminated.
In concluding, Mr Oldfield outlined several next steps for the caucus, including continued advocacy for increased funding, enhanced congressional oversight of the executive branch and relevant federal agencies, and the organisation of further briefings on global water and sanitation security. He also suggested that the United States should consider establishing a national-level domestic water security caucus, similar to those in Liberia, Uganda and Zambia. He noted that water caucuses already existed at state level in certain states, including Texas, particularly where water security remained an ongoing challenge.
The Chairperson proposed that the Committee proceed by omitting agenda item 0.7 and instead move directly to the item concerning the role of civil society, after which the session would proceed to questions. He enquired whether this approach would be acceptable to Members.
Upon receiving confirmation from Members, he noted that there were no objections and confirmed that the revised order would stand. He then invited Ms Shozi to begin the discussion on the role of civil society.
Presentation: The Role of CSOs in South Africa
Ms Shozi explained the concept of a Parliamentary Water, Sanitation and Hygiene (WASH) Caucus and highlighted the role of civil society organisations (CSOs) within the South African context. It was indicated that a Parliamentary WASH Caucus served as a platform through which Members of Parliament could advance advocacy, oversight, and coordination on WASH-related issues. The presentation noted that such caucuses had been successfully implemented in other countries and had demonstrated their value in addressing water scarcity and sanitation challenges. It was further explained that a caucus differed from a portfolio committee in that it functioned more flexibly as a cross-party advocacy platform rather than a formal parliamentary oversight structure.
The presentation defined civil society organisations as a broad category that included non-governmental organisations, community groups, labour unions, faith-based organisations and advocacy groups working to promote the public good and influence policy. It was reported that CSOs played an important role because they were grounded in community realities and promoted inclusive participation, particularly for women, youth and vulnerable groups. Their involvement was also said to strengthen democratic accountability by ensuring that the perspectives and needs of communities were reflected in policy discussions and parliamentary processes.
It was further explained that CSOs could support Parliamentary WASH Caucuses in several ways, including providing secretariat and coordination support, supplying research and evidence to inform parliamentary engagement, convening stakeholders and advocating for sector priorities. Through these functions, CSOs helped Members of Parliament to ask more informed parliamentary questions, pursue actionable reforms, and strengthen oversight of government commitments. The presentation also highlighted the importance of capacity building for Members of Parliament, noting that CSOs could enhance their understanding of technical WASH issues and improve their ability to engage effectively in debates and legislative processes.
In conclusion, it was emphasised that the relationship between CSOs and Members of Parliament required clear boundaries, with political leadership remaining the responsibility of parliamentarians while CSOs provided technical support, coordination and community linkages. The presentation indicated that effective collaboration could strengthen policy development, influence budget allocations, improve coordination across government sectors, and elevate sanitation issues to the same level of political priority as water supply. It was also noted that CSOs should be adequately supported for their involvement, as their contributions were often funded through grants or donor support and required sustained effort and expertise.

Discussion
The Chairperson informed colleagues that the meeting would now proceed to the stage of asking questions for clarification. He explained that, should Parliament consider establishing such a structure, a formal process would need to be followed. This process would include discussions with the Chairperson of Chairs and would ultimately require a decision by the Speaker on whether such a body could be established.
He indicated that the purpose of the questions at that stage was therefore primarily to obtain clarity. He added that the Committee would thereafter deliberate and determine the appropriate way forward in addressing the matter.
Mr V Reddy (MKP) thanked the Chairperson and indicated that he did not have any questions at that stage. Instead, he expressed his appreciation to the presenters for their contributions and for the opportunity to engage with international counterparts.
He noted that the platform allowed Members to learn from the experiences of other countries facing similar challenges and emphasised that such exchanges could be beneficial for South Africa. Mr Reddy expressed his support for initiatives that strengthened collaboration and encouraged efforts to expedite the process of establishing such engagement platforms.
Ms M Kobe (Action SA) thanked the Chairperson and welcomed the presentations delivered by the various speakers representing different legislative bodies and organisations working in the WASH sector. She noted that the contributions had been encouraging and reflected positively on the work already undertaken by the Portfolio Committee over the past two years. In her view, the Committee appeared well-positioned to begin considering the establishment of a WASH caucus, particularly in light of the work conducted under the Chairperson’s leadership.
Ms Kobe raised a question regarding lessons learned from the experiences shared by previous speakers. She referred specifically to a point made by one of the presenters, who had indicated that it was beneficial to first establish informal relationships before formalising a structured caucus. Ms Kobe sought clarity on the challenges that had informed that assessment, asking what specific difficulties had been encountered that led to the recommendation of beginning with informal engagement before establishing a formal structure.
She further observed that the situation within their own context appeared somewhat different, as the Committee was already operating within a formal parliamentary structure and was now engaging more informally with stakeholders around the potential establishment of a WASH caucus. She therefore wished to understand whether there were particular lessons or challenges from other countries that the Committee should consider before moving to the next stage. She thanked the Chairperson.
Ms Muyovwe-Mumba explained that one of the key challenges they initially faced was that their engagement with legislators was largely limited to formal interactions, typically when they were invited to participate in meetings of parliamentary committees, such as portfolio committees within the National Assembly. She noted that this constituted the primary avenue through which they were able to interact with Members of Parliament on WASH-related issues.
Over time, however, through attendance at these meetings and observation of parliamentary debates, they began to identify certain Members of Parliament who demonstrated a genuine interest in water and sanitation matters. She indicated that some Members showed this interest by raising questions during parliamentary sittings or by engaging more actively on the issue.

If the Department had failed to follow the required consultation processes, he indicated that any corrective action would need to be carefully considered and agreed upon by the Committee.
He acknowledged that taking such steps could potentially delay the Committee’s legislative process but emphasised that the Committee should be guided by the legal advice before making any decision. Once the legal opinions from both the State Attorneys and Parliament’s legal advisers had been received, the Committee would be in a better position to take an informed decision on how to proceed.
The Chairperson concluded by indicating that the Committee would convene a special meeting as soon as the legal advice had been received. The legal opinions would be circulated to Members in advance so that they could review the information and discuss the matter collectively. He then thanked Members and indicated that he would see them later in the House.
The meeting was adjourned.
