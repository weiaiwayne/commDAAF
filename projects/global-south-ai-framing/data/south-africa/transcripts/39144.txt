Meeting Summary
Trade, Industry and Competition
Small Business Development
The Select Committee met to consider the annual performance plans (APPs) of the Department of Trade, Industry and Competition (DTIC) and the Department of Small Business Development (DSBD).
They were told that the DTIC's APP was responding to huge shifts within South Africa, such as the sluggish economy, the energy crisis and job creation imperatives. The DTIC had to respond to these and come up with strategic priorities and targets in its APP. There were 12 functional areas for the Department that included transformation, research and legislation, but the budget reductions would affect the Department's revenue and could require an adjustment of other APP priorities.
Members pointed out that in a transformative economy, where there was a need for job creation, it was important that all parties played their part. How much was the government regulating or keeping an eye on the private sector, so that they also played their role in creating jobs? They were concerned that with many senior officials in acting positions, this would present problems of continuity and stability within the Department. Other issues involved the need for more special economic zones, for incentives to be limited to start-ups for entrepreneurs, and for greater effort to be made to encourage investment.
The DSBD said the clear focus in their budget was on building and strengthening small to medium enterprises, in keeping with the National Development Plan. The reason for this focus was the fact that small, medium and micro enterprises were the core drivers of economic growth and the creation of employment. This was the crux of the DSBD's work, which was ultimately to reduce inequality, extreme unemployment and poverty. Government contributed 30% to the gross domestic product, while the private sector contributed the other 70%, so it was important that they engaged with the private sector to enable them to play their part within the ecosystem. They were also looking at ways to reduce red tape to make it easier for SMMEs to play their part.
Two of the DSBD's entities, the Small Enterprise Finance Agency (SEFA) and the Small Enterprise Development Agency presented their APPs. SEFA referred to the reduction in the budget from R9 billion to R7 billion, and said the lack of revenue presented constraints such as a loss of funding for youth and women's enterprise programmes. SEDA said that they focused on using technology to track, measure and monitor every programme to ensure that they were relevant.
A Member asked the Department to be more aggressive in removing red tape, because foreign individuals were taking over township economic activities, as they were not worried about the red tape, and money was being taken out of the community. There was also concern that the Department's programmes might be creating professional business grant recipients, instead of professional business people.
There was general consensus that not enough time had been allocated to discuss the presentations thoroughly.
Meeting report
Department of Trade Industry and Competition
Annual Performance Plan 2024/25
Mr Parks Tau, Minister of Trade, Industry and Competition (DTIC), introduced his two Deputy Ministers, Mr Zuko Godlimpi and Mr Andrew Whitfield, accompanied by Acting Director-General, Ms Malebo Mabitje-Thompson, and Ms Irene Ramafole, Chief Financial Officer (CFO).
The Minister remarked that there had been a Cabinet Lekgotla recently, and it had agreed on specific priorities that may not have found their way into the current 2024/25 annual performance plans (APPs). As a result, and also because of the medium-term development plans, the APPs as they stood may have to be revisited, but he would inform the Committee when that happens.
Briefing on Department of Trade Industry and Competition
Annual Performance Plan 2024/25
Ms Mabitje-Thompson described the presentation as a hybrid, as she wanted to contextualise the Department first, placing it within a legal framework and then presenting the APPs. The DTIC's overarching mission was to promote structural transformation towards a dynamic industrial and globally competitive economy, to provide a predictable, competitive, equitable and socially responsible environment which was conducive to investment, trade and enterprise development, and to broaden participation in the economy. The APP was responding to huge shifts within South Africa, such as the sluggish economy, the energy crisis and job creation imperatives. The DTIC had to respond to these and come up with strategic priorities and targets in the APP.
She said the APP sets out 45 departmental outputs in the 2024/25 reporting period, and that branches developed by the Department contributed to their delivery. All of the 45 outputs had defined branch indicators and targets. The Department had also prioritised legislation such as the Companies Amendment Bill, the Design Amendment Bill, the International Trade and Administration Act Amendment Bill, and the Trade Marks Amendment Bill. The work of the DTIC was closely aligned to the medium term strategic framework (MTSF) priorities 2 and 7, which cover economic transformation and job creation, as well as a better world and Africa.
Ms Ramafole focused on the impact of the budget allocation on the DTIC's strategic priorities and local economic development. She said there were 12 functional areas for the Department that included transformation, research and legislation amongst others, and the impact of the budget reduction would affect the Department's revenue and could require an adjustment of other APP priorities.
The budget allocation for 2024/25 was R9.6 bn:
35% was allocated for business incentives, such as the Automotive Incentive Scheme (AIS), Global Business Services (GBS), film and television, etc;
5% for infrastructure programmes, such as special economic zones (SEZs) and the Contractor Incubator Programme (CIP);
18% was to support various external programmes, namely the Clothing, Textiles, Footwear and Leather (CTFL) programme, and the Social Employment Fund (SEF) at the Industrial Development Corporation (IDC);
21% was for transfers to entities;
2% was for non-profit organisations (NPOs) such as Proudly South African, Intsimbi Future Production Technologies Initiative, and membership fees to foreign organisations such as the World Trade Organisation (WTO) and the World Intellectual Property Organisation (WIPO); and
19% was for operational expenditure, mainly for cost of employment (COE) and goods and services.
She said that the APP was a work in progress, and that they would adjust it after finalising the medium- term development plan (MTDP).

Discussion
Ms M Dlamini (ANC, Free State) pointed out that in a transformative economy where there was a need for job creation, it was important that all parties played their part. In this regard, she wanted to know how much the government was regulating or keeping an eye on the private sector so that they also played their role in creating jobs. She noted that the private sector was making huge profits in an environment created by this government, and therefore they must be impressed upon to also make contributions to job creation and “plough back”. She wanted to know what the Committee, together with the Ministry, could do to strengthen legislation that would ensure that the private sector played its part.
Mr N Pienaar (DA, Limpopo) congratulated the Minister and reminded him of the responsibility he carried and the work in front of him, mentioning that millions were unemployed and looking to the Minister for help. He pointed out that the Department’s structure showed nine programs within the Ministry that had heads in acting positions. He said that this presented problems of continuity and stability within the Department. He wanted to know what was to be done about that, especially in a country that desperately needed economic stability.
He appreciated the work of the DTIC regarding the SEZs but noted that Lephalele, his constituency, was supposed to have been designated as an SEZ almost five years ago, but that still had not happened. He pointed out that given the economic and energy potential such as coal availability and vast fertile lands for agriculture, the region had lost out on hundreds of millions of rands in investment because of the delay in designating Lephalele as an SEZ. He expressed some concerns about the competing interests of the district municipality and local municipalities which might lose some of their budgets to the other. He wanted to know what was being done to assure each sector.
Mr Pienaar wanted to know about the success rate of the investments of the Department into small business enterprises, specifically as to how many were paying back the loans and how many were still in operation after five years. He suggested that there had to be monitoring of those investments. He asked why the Department was struggling to form public-private sector partnerships in the area of technology, and what was being done to improve trade with South Africa's largest trading partners such as Europe and the United States of America. He also asked about practical measures taken to reduce red tape.
Ms M Kennedy (EFF, Limpopo) said that people now live and work in an age of disruptive technologies such as advanced robotics, artificial intelligence, and data mining. How did the technological advances impact on the operations of the Department?
Mr H Van den Berg (FF+, Northern Cape) used his background as an entrepreneur to illustrate the role of small businesses and entrepreneurs in creating jobs. He wanted to know what other incentives were given to entrepreneurs, such as a tax break he had got from the government in the early nineties. He said that government needed to give entrepreneurs challenges and see how they came up with innovative solutions that created jobs. He suggested that there needed to be more focus on locally produced goods such solar panels, especially now that they were into green energy. He said the country did not need advanced gadgets but tools that worked and were locally produced. He needed more information on the SEZs, especially the Namaqua area. He pointed out the decline in the textile and manufacturing sector in South Africa, and pleaded with the Department to address that by looking into local production.
Mr P Mabilo (ANC, Northern Cape) said that investments were the way to go, and that all avenues must be explored. He asked which South African companies were contributing as investors locally. He wanted to know specifically how much money was being invested into infrastructure in Kimberly. There must be a clear picture of how many black industrialists were being funded, and what the sources of that funding were -- as far as he knew, there were no industrialists, and no funding for them. He said industrial parks were not performing as expected, because infrastructure was damaged and buildings lay fallow, and he asked for an update on them. He raised the issue of scrap metal as a multi-billion sector mired in economic crime affecting municipal infrastructure. He wanted to know how the Department, working together with law enforcement, could use technology in this fight.
Mr B Farmer (PA, Western Cape) said slide 2 mentioned and amount of R9.6 billion, 25% of which went to another agency. He said that as a new Member, he was unfamiliar with everything, and therefore asked that the information be forwarded to him to not waste the Committee’s time. He said slide 24 referred to "Improving the capacity and responsiveness of the state and social partnerships." He said that he wanted to make clear that the Seventh Administration must use this term to make sure that this was not business as usual. He expressed concern that some government officials had voted to give themselves bonuses while their performance was not good and while the people were hungry, and he wanted to make it clear that he would oppose actions like that.
The Chairperson pointed out that the Department’s presentation mentioned that R164 million had been re-prioritised for electric vehicles, and she wanted a more detailed update on where they stood on these issues, especially since the money had been announced during the 2023 State of the Nation Address (SONA).
SEZs were important to her, as she came from Mpumalanga, an export corridor, with so many unemployed youths that could benefit from SEZs, but Mpumalanga did not have one. She wanted the Committee to consider engaging with the Mpumalanga Economic Growth Agency (MEGA). In the re-imagined industrial strategy, R3.3 billion was available -- what was the strategy to include the private sector?
She referred to the reported cyber-crime at the Department of Public Works and Infrastructure (DPWI), and wanted to know if measures were in place to prevent cyber-crime. She mentioned that the master plans that were often mentioned had been around since the dawn of democracy, but she was not sure where they stood at this time. She said some municipalities deemed as not financially viable were not allowed to buy electricity from independent power producers. She asserted that renewable energy should be available to municipalities to buy. She asked what the Department was doing to assist those municipalities that did not qualify to buy electricity so they could do so now.
DTIC's response
Minister Tau said that while he worked with the Department of Cooperative Governance and Traditional Affairs (COGTA), they had worked on programme to assist municipalities on the issue of energy and electricity. He could share the documents with the Committee. He recognised that there were questions that overlapped, and therefore he would allow his colleagues to respond. He pointed out that he was focused on stabilising the organisation to be able to ensure continuity and efficiency. He also wanted to highlight some budgetary constraints, as some of the positions were not funded and others were funded for just a year. However, there were engagements with the Treasury to fill some of the critical vacancies.
The Chairperson offered the Deputy Minister, Mr Andrew Whitfield, a chance to respond.
Deputy Minister Whitfield wanted to emphasise the importance of entrepreneurship and manufacturing in creating jobs. He also recognised the challenges facing entrepreneurs and manufacturers not just locally, but globally as well, especially after the pandemic and global economic shrinkage. He asked what they were learning from the entrepreneurs surviving in this environment. He cautioned against entrepreneurs depending on government incentives, as incentives were meant to assist the entrepreneur to get started, so that once they had found their feet, the incentives could be deployed to help another entrepreneur.
Deputy Minister Godlimpi wanted to emphasise that it was worrying that some were seeing incentives as an ongoing budgetary item. He said that incentives were meant to assist one until one could go to the capital markets and compete on one's own. The SEZs were a function of provinces' cooperation with local government. He recognised the importance of these within the local and provincial economies, and said they were now looking at other models. He wanted to ensure they would follow up with the MEGA, as Mpumalanga was important as an import/export corridor through Maputo.
He assured the Committee that despite global geopolitics, the Department and the South African government were talking to everyone in order to improve the country’s trade relations. He mentioned that the Minister of the Department of International Relations and Cooperation (DIRCO) was currently in London strengthening trade ties, the Minister of Trade Industry and Competition would be in the US next week and he, as Deputy Minister, would be in Russia talking with Brazil-Russia-India-China-SA (BRICS) bloc nations with the same objective of improving trade relations with whoever was interested in South African goods and services.
On the matter of disruptive technologies, the Deputy Minister described the situation as “shifting sands” and a balancing act of embracing technology while securing livelihoods. He made it clear that he wanted crime involving scrap metal gone, and said he believed a total ban on scrap metal was the only way to go. He argued that as long as scrap metal was allowed, it would be difficult to talk of building infrastructure for energy, water and roads.
Department of Small Business Development Annual Peformance Plan 2024/25
Ms Jane Sithole, Deputy Minister of Small Business Development (DSBD), began by congratulating the Chairperson on her election. She pointed out the importance of the Department in solving many of the country’s challenges. She recognised the importance of the National Council of Provinces (NCOP) and the Select Committee in the Department’s work.
She said they had presented their budget vote to the National Assembly, and their clear focus in that budget was on building and strengthening small to medium enterprises, in keeping with the National Development Plan. The reason for this focus was the fact that small, medium and micro enterprises (SMMEs) were the core drivers of economic growth and the creation of employment. She described this area as the crux of the DSBD's work, which was ultimately to reduce inequality, extreme unemployment and poverty.
She said the Department had developed the integrated funding framework in order to make sure that the approach was looking at the whole ecosystem. Government contributed 30% to the gross domestic product (GDP), while the private sector contributed the other 70%, so it was important that they engaged with the private sector to enable them to play their part within the ecosystem. She mentioned they were also looking at ways to reduce red tape to make it easier for SMMEs to play their part. The bills going through Parliament, such as the one on business licensing, were part of this overall strategy.
Deputy Minister Sithole also mentioned other challenges that concerned the communities, such as foreigners taking over township economies, and big businesses infiltrating the local economy. She emphasised that they had revisited some regulations because of these challenges. She made sure that SMMEs and corporations received pre-funding training to make sure that they were ready to effectively use the funding received and were enabled to make the desired contributions. These training programmes were an ongoing support mechanism for SMMEs after they had been funded, to ensure a value-added benefit.
She mentioned that every time the Department came to the Committee, they faced the issue of time constraints. She therefore requested that the Committee consider reserving two days for the presentations so that the Committee could be satisfied, and the Department could be more detailed in its presentation.
Ms Thulisile Manzini, Acting DG, DSBD, put the Department’s APP within the context of a changing local and global environment. She said that challenges such as load-shedding required that they streamline their interventions, and following the Minister’s mentioned priorities, they had focused on township and rural areas for development. The Department had identified some game changers that had informed the key priorities for the 2024/25 APP, in which they had focused on the representation of women as owners of SMMEs, with a target of 50%, and aimed to achieve 4% for people living with disabilities.
Ms Manzini listed other targets the Department planned to achieve, including an unqualified audit and reaching 30 000 rural and township entities with financial support to increase participation. The Department streamlined business interventions to increase the value chain to achieve these targets. She mentioned the need for an Ombudsman Office that would streamline and oversee the work of the Small Enterprise Development Agency (SEDA) and the Small Enterprise Finance Agency (SEFA), and expressed the hope that these initiatives and targets would be achieved soon.
SEFA
Ms Candice Williams, Acting Chief Financial Officer, SEFA, focused her presentation on the medium term expenditure framework and budget allocation, which she said was about R 7 billion. This budget provided R789 million for staff, including filling vacancies within the Department, and about R117 million for administration and some seed funding reserved for the Ombudsman Office mentioned by the Deputy Minister. She broke down the allocation according to specific programmes, and also the allocation for each province. She noted the reduction in the budget from R9 billion to R7 billion. She said the lack of revenue presented constraints such as a loss of funding for youth and women's enterprise programmes of the Department. She added that for the Department to achieve its goals, it would need more funding from Treasury.
The Department had made inroads on all its targets. Their plans and progress have been accepted by COGTA and the Department of Performance Monitoring and Evaluation (DPME). She expressed pride that the Minister and the Deputy Minister were activist district champions, which went a long way to ensuring implementation. She explained that the SEFA programmes were scattered throughout the provinces, including in rural areas, with 15 district directors and partnerships with municipalities to establish business establishments.
Mr Nkosikhona Mbatha, Acting CEO, SEFA, said they were an entity established in 2012 to provide financial support to enterprises in all nine provinces. They employed both direct lending and acting as guarantors to the enterprises. He noted that they had R15 million limits to SMMEs in general, and a limit of R1 million for SMMEs in rural and township areas, because the economies were structured differently. However, he pointed out that they had alternative products for partners who fund a lot of informal initiatives that SEFA cannot fund directly. In some cases, they provide guarantees so that others could fund and lend to informal enterprises. He mentioned that their entity also had properties they made available for use by enterprises.
SEDA
Ms Ntokozo Majola, Acting CEO, SEDA, said that they focused on using technology to track, measure and monitor every programme in order to ensure that they were relevant. They had 110 incubators in a variety of industries, including manufacturing, construction and technology which were funded by various entities, including municipalities and provinces. They also support the presidential awards and partner with other stakeholders to support old general dealers in rejuvenating the old townships and small town economies.

Discussion
The Chairperson reminded the Committee of time constraints, and advised that Members should submit their questions in writing for this part of the presentation. She allowed time for a few pertinent questions.
Mr Modise suggested that going forward, the Committee should consider alternating the meetings to fully engage all the stakeholders. He also wanted to remind the DSBD that when they presented their interventions, there should not just be numbers -- they should be specific about the location of such interventions so that when the Committee visited certain constituencies, they could visit such intervention sites. He asked the Minister to link success stories to specific areas and people, so that Members could see tangible results.
Mr Farmer congratulated the Minister and the DSBD for reaching the target for women's participation. He asked the Department to be more aggressive in removing red tape because the foreign individuals were taking over township economic activities, and they were not worried about the red tape, and the money was then taken out of the community.
Mr Mabilo asked what form of measurement was being used to measure progress.
Mr Van den Berg wanted the goals and outcomes to be made clear, because he was concerned that the Department might be creating professional business grant recipients, instead of professional business people. He expressed concern that the Department maybe spending too much money for diminished returns. He asked how much money was being spent to create how many jobs, because he believed that an entrepreneur should be funded once and, by their nature, they would want to thrive on their own, as that was the nature of entrepreneurship.
Ms Kennedy said that the SMME sector constantly suffered from high levels of failure. She wanted to know if the funded SMMEs were pre-checked to see if they had the infrastructure to utilise the funds for the intended goals. She asked if they were followed up to check and provided with support to ensure they were progressing well towards set goals. She said that SMMEs struggled with cash flow, especially when their creditors did not pay them on time. She wanted to know what legislative or policy initiatives the Department was taking to ensure this was resolved.
Mr Pienaar wanted to know the success rate of programmes such as SEDA and SEFA.
The Chairperson apologised for the time constraints, and gave an assurance that the next meetings would streamlined. She asked the Minister and the Department for written responses to the questions of the Committee. She then handed over to the Minister to close the meeting.
Deputy Minister Sithole commented on Programme 1 and said its outcome was clear to see -- Mr Farmer had commented on the Department reaching its target for women's participation. She said that funding towards this goal should be a priority, because it was a multiplier and its accountability was visible
Ms M Ndlangisa-Nodada (ANC, Eastern Cape) urged the Minister to push legislation and policy changes that would streamline the work of the Department. She realised this would require working with other departments, but she was confident that the Minister and his Department were equal to the task. She told the Committee that she had confidence in the Minister, and that the Committee would have a good working relationship with him.
Ms Stella Ndabeni Abrahams, Minister of Small Business Development, thanked the Committee for its invaluable contributions, and committed to written responses, as requested by the Committee. She said there had been progress and referred to the proposed Ombudsman, whom she said the President would sign off on soon, which would help ensure that corporations and SMMEs were paid on time.
She said foreigners were allowed to trade in South Africa, but only under specific conditions. However, it was true that there were problems and the DSBD was working with other departments to advance legislation that seeks to address some of the concerns regarding foreigners.
The Minister said that the Department was 50% towards achieving its goals, and that they had achieved a 66% success rate in company funding. She emphasised that the Department was focused on assisting professional business people who contributed by producing something or providing services, and was not in the business of providing grants. She pointed out that payment on the funding provided was delayed for six months, and 30% of the funded amount was retained for the sustainability of the business. She added that the targets and goals in the APP were clear and thorough, but time had not allowed the Department's delegation to make comprehensive presentations.
The meeting was adjourned.
