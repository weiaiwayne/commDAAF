Meeting Summary
Companies and Intellectual Property Commission (CIPC)
(Revised)
Companies Tribunal (CT)
Takeover Regulation Panel (TRP)
The Portfolio Committee on Trade, Industry, and Competition met to hear presentations from the Companies and Intellectual Property Commission (CIPC), the Companies Tribunal, and the Takeover Regulation Panel on their 2024/25 Annual Performance Plans.
The Commission projected stable growth and significant capital investments in intangible assets and property development.
The Committee raised several key points, including questions about the staff complement, CIPC’s climate change involvement, and ICT investment strategy. Members also expressed concerns about the ease of doing business and called for more educational outreach, especially for small businesses and cooperatives. Additionally, patent laws and their impact on healthcare affordability were discussed, with the committee urging the CIPC to prioritise reforms.
The Tribunal emphasised its clear mandate under the Companies Act and its commitment to transparency and timely dispute resolution. However, it faces challenges, including budget constraints and a low volume of cases.
Committee members inquired about the Tribunal’s dispute resolution process, asking for clarification on the steps involved and the timeline for resolving cases. They also raised concerns about the Tribunal’s relationship with the newly legislated Small Enterprise Ombuds Service, urging the Tribunal to collaborate to avoid overlaps in responsibilities. The Tribunal’s limited research capacity was addressed, with members suggesting that its partnerships with universities could help improve this. Additionally, the Tribunal acknowledged the need to enhance its marketing and outreach efforts to make more businesses aware of its services, particularly in underserved areas.
The Panel said it plans to maintain its focus on efficiency by continuing to issue timely rulings and decisions. Furthermore, transformation remains a key objective, focusing on staff retention and procurement policies that promote the empowerment of designated groups. Expanding stakeholder engagements and improving electronic document management systems to enhance ease of doing business are also critical goals for the coming years.
The Committee raised questions about penalties for non-compliance with takeover regulations, how the TRP ensures compliance and its involvement in international regulatory forums. Members also expressed concerns about foreign takeovers of local companies and asked how the panel protects South African businesses. The TRP explained that foreign companies involved in takeovers must comply with local laws, and the panel closely monitors these transactions. The Committee also asked about the qualifications required for panel members, with the TRP stating that expertise in corporate law, securities regulation, and takeover law is essential. The acting chairperson concluded the meeting by emphasising the importance of improving service delivery, regulatory oversight, and outreach efforts to strengthen South Africa’s business environment. The meeting adjourned with an invitation for further written responses from the presenting entities.

Companies and Intellectual Property Commission (CIPC) Presentation
Adv Rory Voller (Commissioner), Mr Muhammad Jasat (CFO) and Mr Lungile Dukwana (Chief Strategy Executive) delivered the presentation.
The presentation provided a comprehensive overview of the CIPC and its strategic direction. It began by contextualising the CIPC's operational environment, emphasising the organisation's response to global trends in digital transformation, the adoption of modern technologies, and adherence to progressive legislative frameworks. The presentation underscores the impact of the COVID-19 pandemic, which accelerated the integration of digital technologies and necessitated shifts in business models. As customers become more digitally savvy, the CIPC must modernise its services, focusing on strengthening online platforms, automating processes, and minimising transaction times. This digital shift aims to improve user experiences, maintain business continuity, and position the CIPC as a competitive, future-ready entity.
A significant focus of the CIPC's strategy is its modernisation efforts. The organisation is transitioning its services to modern platforms, which includes automating registration processes for companies and intellectual property (IP) and establishing Self-Service Centres and Partner Sites to improve accessibility. The CIPC also highlights its alignment with South Africa's 2018 IP Policy and ongoing legislative reforms, such as the amendment of the Patent Act and the implementation of the Substantive Search and Examination (SSE) system. These reforms aim to strengthen the patent system by enhancing compliance with the TRIPS (Trade-Related Aspects of Intellectual Property Rights) flexibilities, ensuring access to essential medicines, and preventing unwarranted monopolies. The SSE initiative is further bolstered by the Experiential Learning Programme (ELP), which aims to build the capacity of patent examiners and enforce IP laws effectively.
Internally, the CIPC is focused on building organisational competencies, emphasising human capital development and addressing staffing challenges. With 444 employees as of March 2024, the CIPC has made strides in meeting employment equity targets, especially regarding gender representation in senior management. However, it has not yet reached its goal of employing 2% of persons with disabilities, currently at 1.6%. The CIPC has implemented several initiatives to support its workforce, including reskilling programmes, bursaries, and developing Personal Development Plans (PDPs) for employees. The organisation also strongly emphasises governance and accountability, with a well-structured system of committees overseeing financial, risk, and IT management, ensuring alignment with its strategic goals.
Financially, the CIPC is operating within a stable framework, with its budget projected to increase at an average annual rate of 2.8% from R759 million in 2024/25 to R823 million by 2027/28. Expenditure is focused primarily on compensation for employees (56%), goods and services (36%), and capital investments (8%). The presentation strongly emphasises capital expenditure, with a projected investment of R277 million over the medium term, primarily in intangible assets and property development. These investments align with the CIPC's modernisation strategy, ensuring the organisation remains competitive and agile in a rapidly changing technological environment.
In terms of programmatic performance, the CIPC achieved 85% of its key performance indicator (KPI) targets for the 2023/24 financial year, marking a slight decline from the previous year. The organisation monitors its progress closely, with monthly reports to the Commissioner and a focus on integrated planning across budgeting, human resources, and risk management. The CIPC is committed to reducing red tape, enhancing state capacity, and improving the ease of doing business in South Africa, all of which contribute to greater investor confidence and economic growth.

Companies Tribunal Presentation
Judge Dennis Davis (Chairperson), Ms Irene Mathatho (CFO) and Mr Selby Magwasha (Registrar) delivered the presentation.
The presentation outlined the organisation's strengths, weaknesses, opportunities, and priorities for the 2024/25 fiscal year. Strengths of the CT include its clear mandate as established under the Companies Act, which allows it to adjudicate company-related disputes and facilitate alternative dispute resolution (ADR) processes. The Tribunal's structure is well-governed, with experienced members appointed by the Minister of Trade, Industry, and Competition and independent committees for auditing, risk management, human resources, and ICT oversight. The Tribunal operates transparently, ensuring fair and cost-free resolution of disputes for companies. Another notable strength is its commitment to timely decision-making, with specific targets for resolving disputes within 20-35 working days.
Weaknesses include budgetary constraints and a limited mandate, which restrict its scope and operational capacity. The Tribunal also reports a low volume of cases, possibly due to limited awareness or lack of engagement from companies. Additionally, the Tribunal's reliance on external bodies, such as universities, indicates that its in-house research capabilities may be underdeveloped.
Opportunities lie in the Tribunal’s ability to expand its influence through partnerships and outreach initiatives. The CT has signed memorandums of understanding (MoUs) with universities like the University of Pretoria and the University of KwaZulu-Natal to enhance its research capabilities. These partnerships provide opportunities for producing high-quality research that could inform policy and improve the corporate landscape. Additionally, the Tribunal has opportunities to increase engagement with underserved communities and businesses by conducting more outreach programmes and seminars, targeting townships, rural areas, and educational institutions.
Priorities for the Tribunal in 2024/25 focus on improving service efficiency and expanding its mandate to contribute more effectively to economic transformation. Key objectives include reducing red tape, improving the turnaround times for adjudicating cases, and increasing stakeholder engagement through outreach programmes. The Tribunal also aims to ensure high staff retention, procurement from designated groups, and the publication of a research report. Financial sustainability remains a priority, with continued emphasis on securing sufficient funding to meet its operational and developmental goals.

Mr Magwasha stated that once an application is filed, the administration staff, headed by the registrar, ensures that the application is enrolled. After the matter is filed, the next step is to allocate it to a member through the chairperson. The chairperson is responsible for allocating cases to the two members, with assistance from the registrar. At this point, the role of the members becomes relevant as they are allocated cases. For unopposed cases, the member must deliver a decision within 25 days. On the 25th day, the case must be approved, and once approved, it is taken to the court. He clarified that the 25-day period does not start from the receipt or date of filing but from when the matter is filed after the required regulations have been followed, allowing the respondent to file an answer and the applicant to reply. Once these steps are completed, the member handles the case and is paid accordingly. Members receive a capped daily fee of R5,000 for unopposed matters for research and decision writing, with the total payment amounting to R10,000. However, for opposed matters, the fee increases to R15,000, divided as follows: five days for preparation, five days for the hearing, and five days for decision writing. This outlines the process followed in handling applications.

The Takeover Regulation Panel (TRP) Presentation
Mr Zano Ndluli (Deputy Executive Director) delivered the presentation.
The presentation highlighted several strengths, weaknesses, opportunities, and priorities of the TRP. Strengths include the TRP's clear statutory mandate to ensure market integrity and fairness in regulated takeovers and its role in providing crucial information to shareholders. Its governance structure, including the presence of key stakeholders such as the JSE and CTSE, supports its impartiality and independence. The TRP's human capital is notable, with most female and black employees aligning with national transformation goals. Its commitment to efficiency is evidenced by its high compliance with targets, such as issuing rulings within five working days.
Weaknesses include the TRP’s limited research capabilities, which are confined to analysing data from affected transactions. The current vacancy in the Executive Director role could hinder leadership continuity and effectiveness. Additionally, the Panel’s revenue relies heavily on surcharge fees from listed companies, making it vulnerable to delistings from the JSE, which may negatively impact its financial sustainability.
Opportunities for the TRP include strengthening its research capabilities to provide more comprehensive data on market trends, which could better inform policymakers. The Panel’s internship and secondment programmes also offer a chance to build a pipeline of future professionals, especially in the Mergers & Acquisitions industry. By expanding its engagement with international regulatory bodies, the TRP can adopt global best practices and enhance its regulatory framework.
Priorities for the TRP moving forward include filling the vacant Executive Director position and ensuring that its strategic reserves remain robust to defend against litigation. The TRP also plans to maintain its focus on efficiency by continuing to issue timely rulings and decisions. Furthermore, transformation remains a key objective, focusing on staff retention and procurement policies that promote the empowerment of designated groups. Expanding stakeholder engagements and improving electronic document management systems to enhance ease of doing business are also critical goals for the coming years.

Discussion
Mr Subrathie began by acknowledging that the question on funding had already been answered and then asked about the factors considered when reviewing a takeover. He wanted to understand what exactly is looked for in such cases. Specifically, he asked what happens if a takeover takes place without the necessary approval and what the consequences are. He mentioned that he had read something about a penalty of 10% of the turnover and wanted clarity on whether that was true. He focused on what companies must avoid to prevent such penalties, seeking a clear explanation of the rules to ensure that companies understand how to comply.
He further inquired about international regulation forums, noting that much money is spent on these. He was interested in what he had learned from attending these forums and how that learning had been implemented locally.
In addition, he asked about surcharge fees, mentioning that the surcharge is 25% of the listing fee. He wanted to understand what happens when a company delists—whether these fees are reversal or adjusted in such cases.
He then shifted focus to audits, asking who audits the panel and whether the audit fees were up to date. He mentioned that he is involved in debt collection for the Standing Committee on Public Accounts (SCOPA) on behalf of the Auditor General. He noted that the Auditor General has a significant amount of outstanding fees. Given that the panel has a healthy cash surplus from its income, he hoped their audit fees were current.
Next, he sought clarification on the "admin fee" listed in the report, which was approximately R4 million annually. He wanted to know what specific elements are included under this fee, as there was also a line item for salaries and wages, and it was unclear what the administrative expenses encompassed.
Finally, he asked about foreign buyers and investors, especially with takeovers of local companies. His concern was that while attracting foreign investment is important, there should also be protection for local businesses, ensuring that skills and resources are shared rather than simply allowing foreign companies to buy up local assets. He wanted to know how the panel manages takeovers by foreign companies and whether safeguards are in place to ensure the protection and development of local businesses.
Mr Chance started by asking if any takeover automatically triggers something, such as an investigation or the need for an application. He wanted to understand whether all takeovers are subject to scrutiny and how this process unfolds.
He followed this by inquiring about the distinction between complex and uncomplex transactions—specifically, how the panel determines whether a takeover falls into either category and whether this influences the investigation or approval process.
Next, he asked about multiple exchanges. Since South Africa has four or five exchanges, he wanted to know whether companies listed on more than one exchange are charged fees for each listing. He was interested in how fees are applied in these cases.
Lastly, he asked about the qualification requirements for panel members. He wanted to know what kind of individuals the panel looks for when it calls for applications. Specifically, he asked what qualifications or CV attributes are ideal for panel members, indicating an interest in understanding the expertise required to serve on the panel.
The Acting Chairperson asked how long the individual has held their current position and sought to understand how they ensure that appropriate legal advice is provided, especially when dealing with law firms. He highlighted the importance of empowering corporate lawyers, particularly women and black lawyers, referring to concerns raised earlier by Judge Davis about the lack of diversity among corporate lawyers. This lack of diversity, he pointed out, can hinder their eligibility for judicial appointments, especially in cases involving corporate law.
Next, he addressed a concern regarding women being financially constrained by lenders. He described a situation where women may take out loans for tenders or contracts but, due to financial difficulties, cannot repay the loans in the specified time. As a result, the lenders take over the contracts or companies. He asked whether such matters fall under the purview of the panel or company and, if so, how these situations are handled.
Finally, he touched on BEE compliance, specifically questioning how level four BBBEE status is monitored. He expressed concern about companies merely "ticking the box" to meet compliance requirements and asked about the demographics of the 60% of companies that claim compliance. He seemed to want clarity on whether these companies are genuinely diverse or if compliance is superficial.
Prof Tshepo Mongalo (Panel Chairperson) clarified the role and mandate of the regulatory body under sections 197 to 199 of the Companies Act, which is to oversee transactions involving regulated companies and ensure compliance with rules surrounding affected transactions. These transactions include the disposal of assets, mergers, amalgamations, mandatory offers, compulsory acquisition transactions, and creep transactions, where a company gradually increases its stake by acquiring small percentages over time. He explained that such transactions must be reported and are closely monitored. The two key considerations that guide their oversight are ensuring that minority shareholders' rights are safeguarded, particularly in situations where they object to transactions such as mergers. For instance, if a company fails to go to court for approval as required or if minority shareholders aren't allowed to have their shares appraised and bought at a fair price, the regulatory body will not approve the transaction. They are tasked with ensuring that the broader market remains fair and transparent, free from manipulative or unfair practices that could harm its integrity. He emphasised that they do not evaluate a transaction's commercial benefits or downsides. They do not act as a "super board" that makes company business decisions. Instead, they focus on whether the transaction meets the regulatory requirements concerning minority shareholder protection and market integrity. If these conditions are not met, the transaction will not be approved, even if the transaction is seen as commercially beneficial by some parties. In this way, the regulatory body's role is strictly legal and regulatory, not to judge or influence the business merits of a transaction.
Mr Nduli said there was a question about what should be done to avoid a situation where people evade penalties for non-compliance. He explained that there is a regulated process through which the panel investigates transactions, and before imposing penalties, they thoroughly investigate to determine if compliance was achieved. He acknowledged that the rules they create are sophisticated and complex, making it common for people to believe they are compliant, only to find out later that they are not. Therefore, if non-compliance is discovered, their initial approach is not to impose penalties immediately. However, if someone repeatedly breaks the rules, they would address the issue and consider imposing harsher measures. He further explained that the panel does not impose penalties itself but issues compliance notices when non-compliance is found. If the party fails to comply with the notice, the panel can petition the court, arguing that the party disregards the law and requests a penalty. He emphasised that penalties are not directly imposed by the panel but are determined by the court. He also mentioned their efforts to learn from international conferences and bilateral engagements with other regulatory bodies. The mergers and acquisitions they regulate are part of global practice, with commercial principles being largely universal across different countries. The rules are designed to address the inherent information asymmetry in these transactions, ensuring that parties making commercial decisions have sufficient information. By engaging with international peers, they align their testing methods for disclosures and other compliance issues, ensuring consistency with global practices. He gave an example of their peers’ confidence in South Africa’s rules, mentioning that at a recent international takeover regulators’ conference, their interest in hosting the next event was met with positive feedback, demonstrating the respect their regulations receive.
He concluded by saying they take pride in their work and feel they exceed expectations in this space. Regarding audit fees, he mentioned that they have been audited since last year by an external auditing firm, SNG Grant Thornton, with the approval of the Auditor-General's office, and they do not owe any fees to the AG's office.
Prof Mongalo addressed the qualifications for panel members recruited to join the organisation. Generally, the organisation seeks skills related to corporate law, emphasising securities regulation and takeover laws, specifically those outlined in Chapter 5. He added that having transactional law skills, especially in mergers, takeovers, and schemes of arrangement, would be an advantage. They look for people with skills and experience in those areas, even if they are not practical skills but research expertise demonstrated through extensive writing on the subject. He shared a personal anecdote, recalling when he was appointed to the panel and told his mother he would deal with security regulations. She misunderstood the term "security," thinking it referred to physical security, while he had to explain that it actually referred to shares and investment-related security regulations. He stressed the need for individuals with knowledge of security regulation in takeover transactions. He also highlighted few specialists in South Africa in this field, as universities typically do not offer postgraduate degrees in securities regulation. Most institutions focus on corporate law, and while insider trading is sometimes taught at an undergraduate level, it is regulated by the FSCA, not their panel. However, understanding insider trading can help comprehend aspects of mergers and takeovers. He mentioned that his colleagues could provide more detail regarding the procurement of services from law firms. Still, their organisation considers not just the firm’s qualifications or BEE status but also the composition of its partners, specifically looking at the inclusion of women and youth. When engaging a law firm, they assess whether it has the capacity and expertise to assist in interpreting transactions and applying the law. He noted that some transactions they deal with may involve financial obligations related to a law firm being taken over by an affected company, but that is not their primary concern. They focus on ensuring that the requirements for the transaction, such as for a takeover, scheme of arrangement, or disposal, are met according to the law. They do not consider any particular case's commercial advantages or disadvantages, as legislation precludes them from doing so.
Ms Zoliswa Mkwetshana (Senior Associate: Mergers and Acquisition) noted a question regarding what happens when a company delists, particularly concerning surcharge fees. She explained that surcharge fees are only charged to listed companies since they are calculated as a percentage of the listing fees. Consequently, when a company delists, the panel loses that revenue. She mentioned that a previous Executive Director had written a paper highlighting this specific risk, as many companies are moving away from public listings for various reasons. In South Africa, she explained that companies delist primarily because remaining listed has little benefit if their market value falls below a certain threshold. Companies continue to pay regulatory fees and meet compliance obligations but do not experience the anticipated benefits, such as raising funds in the market. This has prompted the panel to explore alternative ways of generating revenue to address this risk, given that surcharge fees are lost when companies delist. She also addressed a related question about fees from other exchanges in South Africa, noting that the panel does receive surcharge fees from these exchanges, which amount to 25% of their listing fees. However, she pointed out that the two active exchanges in South Africa are the Johannesburg Stock Exchange and the Cape Town Stock Exchange.
Mr Subrathie said he wanted to expand on the panel's mandate to avoid confusing it with the Competition Commission. From what he understood, the panel’s focus is on listed companies. He expressed concern about what happens when companies want to merge without adhering to the two primary criteria used by the panel: protecting minority interests and maintaining market integrity.
He then questioned what would happen if a company wanted to abuse the market by engaging in takeovers and mergers without being listed. He asked where, within the Department of Trade, Industry, and Competition (DTIC) space, such activities would be regulated, as they could still affect market integrity and minority shareholders. He was trying to understand where this kind of regulation fits within the broader regulatory ecosystem.
Mr Nduli clarified that the panel's mandate extends beyond just listed companies—it includes all public companies. If the scenario described involved an unlisted public company, it would still fall under the panel's jurisdiction. Additionally, private companies whose shares have been transferred, with 10% or more of them being transferred, could also be captured. He further noted that changes would be introduced through the Company's Amendment Bill and the Second Amendment Bill, which will alter the criteria for affected companies. While a company may not be listed, it could still be subject to regulation if it falls within the panel's three categories: state-owned companies, all public companies, and certain private companies.
Ms Margaret Motsoahae (CFO) explained that administrative fees include costs related to operations, such as insurance, utilities (like electricity), photocopying, computer services, and IT expenses. These fees represent money going out, as they are expenditures and part of the organisation's overall operating costs.
Mr Nduli clarified that the executive director's position has been vacant since January 1st of this year.
Prof Mongalo explained that there was a question regarding how takeovers involving a foreign company are regulated. He clarified that a company listed on the South African market is regulated under the Companies Act. Therefore, even if it is a foreign company listed or involved in a merger or an affected transaction with another listed company in the South African market, it would be subject to the provisions of Chapter 5 of the Companies Act. He further noted that the foreign company would need to comply with the provisions of the local laws and the laws of the foreign market it operates in when merging with a South African company. This is how the regulatory framework functions. He emphasised that their tools are based on Chapter 5 of the Companies Act.
Mr Nduli addressed the question regarding the difference between complex and uncomplicated transactions. His simple answer was that knowing until the transaction is in progress is impossible. The same rules apply to all transactions, but the complexity arises from what happens during the process. Compliance issues, for instance, may turn a transaction into a complex one. However, at the outset, it's not always clear whether a transaction will be straightforward or complicated.
Prof Mongalo added an example to illustrate the complexity of regulating takeovers involving foreign companies, using the case of MultiChoice, which is publicly known. He explained that this case involved several regulatory frameworks, not just the Companies Act but also communications regulations. These regulations require that foreign companies investing in South African telecommunications companies not have more than 20% of voting rights. In this case, the foreign company involved held a 35% stake in MultiChoice, which triggered a mandatory offer under the Companies Act due to the size of the shareholding. However, the communications regulations limited the voting rights of any foreign company to 20%, even if they held more shares. This created a conflict between the regulatory frameworks, as the Companies Act technically required a managerial offer at 35%, but the communications regulations capped the voting rights at 20%. He posed the question of handling such a scenario, where one regulatory framework triggers a managerial offer while another imposes limitations. In this case, the complexity arises from the competition between the different regulations. If no other regulatory frameworks were involved, a 35% stake would clearly trigger a managerial offer under the Companies Act.
The Acting Chairperson thanked everyone for attending the meeting and the entities for their presentations.
The meeting was adjourned.
