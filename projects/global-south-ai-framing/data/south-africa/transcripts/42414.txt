Meeting Summary
Video
The Select Committee met with the Department of Higher Education and Training (DHET), the National Student Financial Aid Scheme (NSFAS) and Higher Health, to discuss the state of readiness for the 2026 academic year and other pressing issues.
Members of the Committee raised concerns about student funding, accommodation, safety, and institutional support, particularly for technical and vocational education and training (TVET) and community education and training (CET) colleges.
Key points coming to the fore during the Committee discussion were:
Concerns regarding outstanding payments to universities -- with approximately R14bn owed by NSFAS -- and private accommodation providers. NSFAS committed to addressing individual provider claims and improving communication.
The need for safe, accredited student accommodation, especially in rural areas. NSFAS confirmed that direct allowances were given where accredited beds were unavailable.
Concern over the underfunding of TVET colleges compared to universities, and high dropout rates, particularly at CET colleges, which were driven by socioeconomic factors. The DHET confirmed regular monitoring visits were conducted.
Meeting report
Department of Higher Education and Training Briefing: 2026 Academic Year readiness; Student Accommodation; Enrolments; Student Allowances; Funding of Missing-Middle
Ms Thandi Lewin, Chief Director: Institutional Governance and Management Support, Department of Higher Education and Training (DHET), said for the 2026 academic year, the Department was projecting enrolments of well over half a million students in the Technical and Vocational Education and Training (TVET) system -- approximately 526 000 students. This reflected significant growth compared to 2024 and 2025, and demonstrated steady progress towards expanding access in line with the Department's policy objectives. Many of these students were supported through National Student Financial Aid Scheme (NSFAS) funding.
Of this total, around 170 000 new learners were enrolled in ministerially funded programmes, specifically the National Certificate Vocational (NCV) and Report 191 National Technical Education (NATED) programmes. In addition, enrolments in the Centres of Specialisation continued to support priority trades and occupations aligned with industry demand.
The provincial breakdown of projected enrolments for 2026 reflected historical capacity, including the number of colleges and available spaces in each province. While growth was evident and aligned with the Department's strategic goals, expansion was not yet occurring at the pace they would ideally like to see.
Most Technical and Vocational Education and Training (TVET) colleges had already commenced the 2026 academic year. Her colleagues would provide an update on actual enrolment figures as registrations were finalised, but the system was operational and progressing as planned. The enrolment planning process for 2026 had been completed, and the approved figures were reflected in the final planning submissions.
Community Education and Training
Ms Thembisa Futshane, Acting Director-General (ADG): Community Education and Training (CET),
Department DHET, said CET colleges had opened on 12 January for administration, and student registrations had commenced on 14 January, continuing until the end of February. Classes had begun across all community colleges, and the start of the academic year had been relatively calm. The only significant disruption had occurred in Limpopo, where storms at the beginning of the year had affected 13 centres, preventing them from opening on schedule. These centres had since reopened, and damage-related matters were being addressed.
Enrolments for 2026
The targeted enrolment for the 2026 academic year was 147 163 students. Historically, enrolments were highest in provinces such as KwaZulu-Natal (KZN) and lowest in the Northern Cape. They were monitoring enrolments weekly to track progress against their targets.
The Department had also assessed available new spaces, particularly in light of the 2025 matric cohort. CET colleges played an important role in accommodating students who wished to improve their National Senior Certificate (NSC) results. In collaboration with the Department of Basic Education (DBE), they admit students aged 20 and above who either did not pass matric or wish to improve their results.
In addition to academic programmes, they offered short skills programmes at entry level, including welding, sewing, plumbing, cell phone repairs and related trades. The figures presented reflected the number of new students colleges had indicated they could accommodate in 2026.
Infrastructure challenges
She said infrastructure remained a significant constraint. There were nine CET colleges, one per province, operating across 1 791 centres. However, 96% of these centres operated from DBE school premises, often using a platooning system. This arrangement limited flexibility and access. To address this, the Department had secured a R1 billion infrastructure allocation from internal funds to establish fit-for-purpose, standalone CET centres and reduce reliance on DBE schools.
In addition, under the leadership of the Minister, they were developing a collaboration framework to enable CET colleges to operate increasingly from TVET college campuses, rather than DBE schools. This would support seamless articulation pathways and better integration within the post-school system. Some CET colleges were already operating from TVET campuses, although not yet at the desired scale.
Ms Futshane said the DHET faced challenges relating to ICT infrastructure, including limited access to devices and poor internet connectivity. While some centres had connectivity, it was minimal and often inadequate. A newly appointed Chief Information Officer (CIO) for the post-school system was currently reviewing information communication technology (ICT) infrastructure to strengthen digital capacity across the sector, including CET colleges.
Staffing and lecturer development
In terms of staffing, the Department had adequate personnel overall. However, shortages existed in certain subject areas, particularly mathematics and physical sciences. They also required more lecturers able to teach in Afrikaans.
A key strategic shift was the repositioning of CET colleges so that they did not focus solely on academic programmes. The Department was retraining and reskilling lecturers to deliver occupational and short skills programmes. Currently, there were 46
Quality Council for Trades and Occupations
(QCTO)-accredited skills programmes, and the Department was equipping its staff to deliver these effectively.
The CET academic calendar was aligned with that of the DBE, largely because the majority of its centres operate from DBE school facilities.
Student support and retention challenges
The Department relied heavily on partnerships to expand access and improve student support.
One of the most serious challenges facing the sector was food insecurity, which significantly affected student retention. Dropout rates remained high, partly due to hunger and poverty. Many students also struggled with transport costs.
Within the post-school system, the National Student Financial Aid Scheme (NSFAS) primarily supported university and TVET students, and CET students did not receive the same level of financial support. However, interventions were underway. Under the leadership of Dr Mimmy Gondwe, the Deputy Minister of Higher Education, partnerships had been initiated with organisations such as Pick n Pay, Shoprite and Tiger Brands, to support food security initiatives, including the establishment of food banks at vulnerable centres.
Safety and student well-being
Ms Futshane said regrettably, since the start of the academic year, the sector had experienced two deeply concerning incidents.
At Ekurhuleni West TVET College, one student tragically died by suicide. While the incident was not directly related to college matters, it reflected the broader challenges facing young people. Higher Health had provided counselling support to the family and students.
In a separate and extremely distressing incident last week, a young female student had been raped during orientation at one of their centres. The incident had occurred on college premises. Higher Health had again provided counselling support. The student had been hospitalised after attempting suicide and was currently receiving care. Both she and the college principal had been in contact with the family. The three alleged perpetrators, who were also students at the same centre, remained at large. The Department was working closely with the South African Police Service (SAPS) to ensure they were apprehended.
These incidents were deeply regrettable and painful. However, aside from these tragic events, the sector remained stable. Teaching and learning had commenced, funds had been disbursed to colleges, and the Department was supporting institutions to ensure readiness and stability for the 2026 academic year.
Fee regulatory framework
Ms Lewin said the Department was currently working on the development of a fee regulatory framework that would guide and regulate tuition fee increases at universities.
Since 2016, the sector has operated under a social compact aimed at moderating fee increases. The Department was now in the tenth or eleventh year of implementing this approach. However, the Department was moving toward a more structured, long-term framework that would provide greater predictability and transparency regarding tuition costs.
The objectives of this framework were threefold:
To improve transparency around how tuition fees were determined;
To address disparities in fee levels across institutions; and
To ensure that tuition fees remain affordable and, where possible, become more affordable for families and for the state.
She said this was particularly important, given that the state, primarily through the NSFAS, now supported well over 60% of undergraduate students. Rising tuition fees had direct fiscal implications.
At present, universities remain highly dependent on tuition fee income, and this reliance is likely to continue in the foreseeable future. The proposed regulatory framework therefore seeks to balance institutional sustainability with affordability and equity. This work was ongoing, and the Department would continue refining the framework during the course of this year as it moves toward establishing a longer-term regulatory approach for the system.

2026 NSFAS application and funding timelines
Mr Waseem Carrim, Acting Chief Executive Officer (CEO), NSFAS, said that for the 2026 academic year, it had followed a strict and disciplined timetable. Applications had opened on 15 September 2025 and closed on 15 November 2025, after a two-month window. The objective was to improve efficiency and reduce the anxiety and uncertainty experienced by both first-time entering and continuing students.
For continuing students:
In the university sector, institutions submitted academic results by 15 December, and NSFAS communicated progression decisions by 31 December 2025.
In the TVET sector, results were received by 15 January, and progression outcomes were communicated by the end of January.
For first-time entering students, interim funding decisions were finalised by 31 December 2025, and funding lists were released to institutions by 2 January 2026. The intention was to ensure that students knew their funding status before the release of matric results. Once results were published and universities issued offer letters, institutions already had NSFAS funding lists, enabling smoother admission and registration processes.
NFSAS had noted that many students still applied to TVET colleges only after not securing university placement. This remained a systemic issue they aimed to address in future cycles.
Upfront payments and disbursements
NSFAS had made an upfront payment of approximately R3.6 billion to universities on 2 February, enabling institutions to release student allowances early in the academic year. Early disbursement was critical, as the majority of NSFAS beneficiaries came from vulnerable and working-class households. Upfront tuition payments were also made to TVET colleges to support operational stability.
In terms of allowances:
A first TVET payment run took place on 13 February, covering approximately 52 000 students.
A second TVET payment run was scheduled to cover the remaining students.
A second upfront university payment would be processed to maintain academic stability.
Institutions had until 31 March to upload registration data to the NSFAS portal, which would inform ongoing disbursements for the remainder of the year.
Application outcomes
Mr Carrim said that for 2026, NSFAS had received approximately 890 000 applications. Of these, about 670 000 students had been approved for funding. However, approval did not guarantee placement. Across universities and TVET colleges, there were approximately 350 000 first-time entering spaces, meaning many approved applicants would not secure institutional admission.
On academic progression:
Approximately 426 000 university students had met progression criteria (over 80%).
Around 136 000 TVET students had met progression requirements.
This demonstrated strong performance among NSFAS-funded students across both sectors.
Institutional support and decentralisation
During registration, NSFAS had deployed full-time teams to universities and TVET colleges to provide in-person support. Although the system was primarily online, students often required assistance with document uploads, appeals and administrative queries.
This was an interim measure as NSFAS transitions to a decentralised model, placing permanent staff at all institutions. This two-year project was expected to be consolidated by the end of 2027, with major implementation occurring in 2026.
Student accommodation
Mr Carrim said student accommodation remained one of the most complex areas within the NSFAS ecosystem. Prior to 2023, accommodation was managed independently by institutions. A previous NSFAS administration had decided to assume a more direct role in managing accommodation and had introduced a national accommodation cap to regulate costs. In addition, the qualifying distance for accommodation eligibility was reduced from 40 km to 10 km from campus. This had significant budgetary and availability implications.
The current NSFAS Board had identified that when this function was assumed, there may have been insufficient feasibility analysis, risk assessment, governance frameworks and property management capacity within NSFAS. Student accommodation was effectively a property management function requiring specialised expertise.
In 2025, a mandatory Student Accommodation Protocol was introduced and released for public comment. After receiving substantial feedback, revisions were made. Institutions had requested delayed implementation, and a second version would be released for further consultation, with finalisation targeted for mid-2026.
A full legal review of the accommodation function had also been conducted, and its findings would be presented to the Portfolio Committee. For 2026, institutions already participating in the accommodation project would remain within it, while those not previously included would remain outside for this academic year.
Operational improvements for 2026
To improve accommodation management, NSFAS had:
Aligned accommodation timelines strictly with the DHET academic calendar;
Introduced clearer communication protocols among students, institutions and NSFAS;
Implemented real-time onboarding systems;
Enhanced student support and feedback mechanisms;
Used 2025 data to forecast 2026 accommodation demand more accurately; and
Strengthened data-driven reporting to ensure timely payments to accommodation providers.
Mr Carrim said a key outcome of the legal review was the decision that NSFAS should no longer use third-party payment providers for private accommodation. From 2026 onward, NSFAS would pay accommodation providers directly, increasing accountability and improving payment reliability.
National audit and accreditation
Concerns had been identified regarding accommodation accreditations granted in previous years, as some may not have reflected the actual condition of facilities. NSFAS had commenced a national audit of student accommodation accreditations. Where accreditation had been incorrectly granted, or standards had not been maintained, consequence management would be implemented. This was a large-scale process expected to take at least 12 months. In the interim, student bodies and oversight structures had been requested to report unsafe or uninhabitable conditions for immediate intervention.
He said NFSAS was also aware of a complaint referred via the National Council of Provinces (NCOP) relating to accommodation in KwaZulu-Natal (KZN). That matter had been received, and was being addressed as a priority.
Ms Lewin said the focus on student accommodation issues was particularly informative for Members.
She briefly outlined the functioning of the War Room, to which the Minister had already referred. She said the War Room mechanism had proved to be highly effective. A weekly coordination meeting took place every Thursday morning, bringing together key stakeholders across the post-school education and training (PSET) sector. These engagements included the Minister, Deputy Ministers, Deputy Directors-General, institutional representatives and sector organisations. The purpose was to provide a central platform where emerging challenges could be raised, discussed and resolved quickly.
In addition, a daily operational War Room addresses institutional matters on an ongoing basis. This structure enables rapid resolution of operational issues as they arise. During the period between the release of matric results and the start of the academic year, challenges tend to be numerous and time-sensitive. The War Room therefore plays a critical role in stabilising the system during what is traditionally a high-pressure period for students, institutions and the Department.
She said the Department also participates in National Joint Operations and Intelligence Structure (NATJOINTS) structures, through which it receives regular updates on safety and security matters, and engages closely with the South African Police Service (SAPS) where necessary.
Ms Lewin highlighted that a Memorandum of Understanding (MoU) between SAPS and the Department of Higher Education and Training had been concluded to strengthen cooperation in responding to incidents at institutions. Importantly, the long-term objective of this agreement was preventative -- to identify risks early, address vulnerabilities, and mitigate potential disruptions before they escalate into major institutional crises. Through this collaboration, the Department was able to work proactively with law enforcement to anticipate and manage risks affecting campuses.
Higher Health
Prof Ramneek Ahluwalia, Chief Executive Officer: Higher Health, indicated that Higher Health’s state of readiness focused on five critical areas:
Psychosocial support
Campus safety
Early detection mechanisms
Crisis response protocols
Institutional accountability, particularly in relation to gender-based violence (GBV) and other social and health epidemics.
He acknowledged that the Acting DG was correct in highlighting recent serious incidents, including cases of rape, suicide and attempted suicide across community colleges, TVET colleges and universities. These were ongoing challenges that required routine, systemic intervention, and systems were being strengthened to improve readiness and response.
Campus safety audit protocol
A central pillar of Higher Health’s readiness plan was the implementation of a campus safety audit protocol. A technical task team, led by the Deputy Minister, would meet at the end of March to finalise the updated draft protocol. This would require institutions to implement and account for comprehensive safety measures, including:
Adequate lighting;
Policing and campus security;
Regular patrols; and
Vetting of security personnel
Prof Ahluwalia noted a current rape case at a community college allegedly involving a security official, underscoring the urgent need for strict vetting and strong preventative systems to protect students and ensure justice for survivors.
Implementation and capacity building
He said that from 1 April, Higher Health would roll out national roadshows and large-scale capacity-building workshops at institutions. The focus was on training frontline responders, not only management. These included security and residence staff, student representative councils (SRCs), student support services, campus health services and counselling units. The aim was to ensure that those who were first on the scene were properly trained and equipped to respond.
Peer education and SAPS collaboration
With support from the SAPS, Higher Health was strengthening its peer education programme, which included approximately 10 000 peer educators. These peer educators were being trained to support campus policing forums, promote accountability on safety measures (lighting, patrols, security), and encourage peer-to-peer reporting and prevention. This approach empowered students to hold institutions accountable, while supporting campus safety structures.
24-hour Crisis Helpline
Prof Ahluwalia said Higher Health operates a 24-hour toll-free crisis helpline, available in all South African languages, providing support for gender-based violence (GBV) and mental health crises. The helpline was supported by psychologists, social workers and counsellors. A national awareness campaign was underway to increase visibility and accessibility. He emphasised that while media often highlighted tragedies, many lives had been saved through this service.
Disability support programme
A dedicated 24-hour support system for students living with disabilities was being strengthened. This initiative covered physical, learning and psychosocial disabilities. Higher Health was working closely with the Departments of Health and Social Development to improve screening, assessment and referral systems. The objective was to build a comprehensive national disability support framework across universities, TVET colleges and community colleges.
Accredited civic and soft skills programme (NQF Level 5)
Prof Ahluwalia said Higher Health had introduced an accredited National Qualifications Framework (NQF) Level 5 short course in civic education and soft skills, accredited by the Quality Council for Trades and Occupations (QCTO) and supported by the United Nations Educational, Scientific and Cultural Organisation (UNESCO). It was the first accredited programme of its kind in Africa.
Eligibility was available to anyone with NQF Level 1 (Grade 9), including students in community education colleges, TVET colleges and universities.
The programme was mobile, accessible and self-paced, and offered in several South African languages, including sign language. It was peer-driven, and covered civic education, the Constitution, anti-racism, civic values, democracy, gender, mental health, disability awareness, sexual and reproductive health, substance abuse, gambling addiction, climate change, financial literacy and social entrepreneurship. It also includes a strong focus on indigenous African perspectives on climate change.
He said that Higher Health was also developing accredited programmes in digital literacy and artificial intelligence for NQF Level 1 participants.
In partnership with the Independent Electoral Commission (IEC), civic education training was also being extended in preparation for the local government elections, including outreach through community colleges.
Early detection and risk screening
Prof Ahluwalia said Higher Health had implemented a digital early detection mechanism for multiple risk areas, including:
Gender-based violence and intimate partner violence;
Mental health (stress, anxiety);
HIV and TB;
Substance abuse;
Gambling addiction; and
Pregnancy and contraception.
Through a quick response (QR) code system, students could complete a two-minute risk screening on their phones. They would receive automated feedback, risk categorisation and referral pathways to services in their area. The target was to reach one million young South Africans this year through early screening interventions. The evidence indicated that early screening and early support significantly improved outcomes in GBV, HIV and mental health.
GBV protocols and criminal justice alignment
Higher Health’s technical task team had released 10 GBV protocols. In partnership with the National Prosecuting Authority (NPA), and in light of strengthened domestic violence legislation, a critical shift was being implemented:
Young women under 25 in post-school education were recognised as vulnerable;
All sexual violence cases must immediately enter the criminal justice system;
Survivors must be taken to a designated health facility (e.g. accredited care centres within 10–20 km of campuses) for medical, forensic and legal processes; and
Evidence must be secured before institutional disciplinary processes proceed.
He said this ensured reduced internal interference, improved evidence protection and stronger justice outcomes. Institutions were required to align their internal disciplinary systems with criminal justice processes.
The GBV technical task team included the Department of Health, SAPS, the NPA, the Commission for Gender Equality (CGE), survivor representatives and civil society organisations. This multi-sectoral approach recognises that the post-school sector is a microcosm of broader societal GBV challenges.
A dedicated crisis psychosocial response team, comprising psychologists and social workers, was deployed to campuses following incidents such as suicide, rape and serious accidents. Prof Ahluwalia referred to a recent incident in which five TVET staff members had died in a road accident, requiring sustained psychosocial intervention across the campus community.
Institutional accountability
Finally, Prof Ahluwalia stressed that institutional leadership -- vice-chancellors, principals and executive management -- must be held accountable. A strengthened monitoring and evaluation (M&E) system was being implemented to ensure compliance with safety audits, proper GBV protocol implementation, financial and governance accountability, and the protection of students and staff

Department's response
Ms Lewin indicated that she would respond to matters relating to universities before handing over to colleagues responsible for TVET colleges, community colleges, NSFAS, and Higher Health.
CPUT student protest near Parliament
She clarified that the students who gathered near Parliament were from the Cape Peninsula University of Technology (CPUT), specifically from the District Six campus, which was located close to the parliamentary precinct. The Minister had engaged with the students on the evening following the State of the Nation Address (SONA).
The protest was related to challenges in placing students in appropriate accommodation at both the Bellville and inner-city campuses. These accommodation challenges had predated the protest, and many students had already been placed. As of the previous week, the number of students still awaiting placement was reportedly relatively small.
Students had also raised concerns about receiving only 50% of their book allowances. The university had resolved this by ensuring students received their full allowances, while discussions with NSFAS were ongoing.
Student accommodation challenges
Ms Lewin acknowledged that student accommodation was a complex, systemic issue affecting universities and colleges. Key challenges included availability and safety of accommodation, affordability, appropriate location, proper accreditation and cost regulation of providers, and preventing student exploitation.
She noted the importance of ensuring adequate funding for NSFAS-qualifying students, and strengthening oversight over accommodation providers. She also referred to long-term planning initiatives, including the Student Housing Infrastructure Programme and major infrastructure projects in both the university and TVET sectors. She undertook to provide more detailed information to the Committee.
Safety and security
Responding to concerns raised about safety, including social media risks and the cancel culture, she acknowledged their importance. She indicated that a colleague would provide an update on the MOU with SAPS. She suggested that issues of online safety may be addressed through civic skills programmes and mental health support initiatives.
Academic support and earmarked grants
She assured Members that the Department supports both rural and urban universities through earmarked grants and various cross-cutting academic support interventions.
Private higher education growth
Ms Lewin said that the private higher education sector was growing rapidly to meet demand, and that the Minister and Department would undertake a “size and shape” review of the post-school education and training (PSET) system in 2026 to assess long-term access needs.
Comprehensive student funding model and the "missing middle"
Ms Lewin indicated that a "missing middle" loan scheme had been initiated and was housed at NSFAS. A more detailed update would be provided.
Historical student debt
She acknowledged that historical student debt in universities remained high. Most NSFAS-qualifying student debt was managed through reconciliations between institutions and NSFAS, though gaps occasionally arose. The Department was currently working with universities to:
Quantify the total student debt;
Identify who holds the debt;
Determine how many affected students were still in the system;
Assess whether students had completed their studies; and
Examine cases where students exceeded the N+1 funding rule
She commented that resolving debt for self-funded students was unlikely, but the Department was seeking a systemic response. She also highlighted that historical debt posed financial risk to universities, which often had to write off unpaid amounts.
Monitoring and visits to TVET Colleges
Mr Sabelo Buthelezi, TVET Branch, DHET, confirmed that regular monitoring of TVET colleges was part of the branch’s functions. Recently, he and the Deputy Minister had conducted a “state of readiness” visit to Mpumalanga, including Ehlanzeni and Mpumalanga University, along with the Acting Director-General. Within a few weeks, they had visited approximately 13 institutions. He emphasised that ongoing monitoring ensured that institutions were prepared, particularly for the transition to occupational programs.
Projected capacity for TVET colleges
Mr Buthelezi said he could not provide figures on the projected capacity of TVET colleges for 2026 offhand, as he had not seen the earlier presentation. He requested that the Committee be provided with the official figures in writing, or as a formal submission, to avoid inaccuracies.
High dropout rates and retention
He acknowledged concerns regarding high dropout rates in some TVET programs. However, he noted that other programs, particularly those with occupational training and workplace components, had high retention rates. He suggested that a detailed, consolidated response would be prepared to differentiate between programs with high dropout rates and those with high retention, highlighting risk areas and reasons for student attrition. This would enable targeted engagement with institutions to address challenges.
Ms Lewin thanked Mr Buthelezi, and apologised to Members for any delays or missed responses, noting that the oversight season was still ongoing and some officials, including Deputy Minister Dr Nomusa Dube-Ncube, were currently on site at the University of KwaZulu-Natal (UKZN). She indicated that registration-related oversight activities would likely conclude after this week.
Regarding the question about NSFAS students using their funds for other purposes, she acknowledged the importance of the concern. She said many students came from low-income or no-income households, and often sent portions of their funds home, which could sometimes create challenges such as hunger on campus. Addressing these issues involved financial literacy and student support programs, including gambling prevention initiatives, which Prof Ahluwalia could elaborate on. She confirmed that Higher Health and the education institutions themselves were actively involved in supporting students in these areas.
Ms Futshane addressed the recent incident at Tsakane Community Learning Centre (CLC), a health community college operating from a school facility. The incident had occurred near the toilets, and the lone security guard on site had been unable to intervene or witness the event. The student involved was still hospitalised and unable to assist in identifying the perpetrators, who were also new students at the college. The police had been informed, engaged with the family, and were conducting an ongoing investigation. She emphasised that the surrounding community should allow law enforcement to lead the investigation. The college had since appointed an additional security guard to conduct regular patrols. She acknowledged the broader vulnerability of community colleges, most of which lacked sufficient security and resources. She said the Department would seek assistance from the Safety and Security Sector Education and Training Authority (SASSETA) to support security across multiple centres.
Ms Futshane stressed the importance of parallel interventions targeting male students to prevent such incidents at the source. She highlighted the Higher Health “Mentalities” program aimed at addressing behavioural issues among male students and promoting safety for female students.
ICT infrastructure
Community colleges currently have 77 ICT laboratories supported through partnerships with various ICT structures for connectivity. She emphasised that community colleges had very limited resources, with 92.3% of their budgets going toward employee compensation. Operational expenses, including ICT upgrades, relied heavily on partnerships and external funding.
Student dropout rates
She noted that community colleges experienced the highest dropout rates among post-secondary institutions. Causes included socioeconomic challenges, inadequate infrastructure, student-headed households, employment-related dropout (e.g., seasonal work), lack of financial support, and food insecurity.
Ms Lewin noted that further comprehensive responses were needed regarding, the skills programs at TVET colleges, the allegations at Central Johannesburg College, and issues of underfunding. However, these matters required the presence of the Deputy Director-General (DDG) for full clarity.
Mr Carrim addressed several questions raised by Members
Unpaid accommodation providers
In 2025, NSFAS’s accommodation team had conducted provincial and regional visits to meet providers and reconcile outstanding claims from 2024/25, resulting in approximately R200 million in additional payments. Some providers had admitted students without confirming their NSFAS funding, contrary to policy. Any accommodation provider still experiencing unpaid claims has been invited to contact NSFAS directly.
He apologised for any unanswered communications from the Committee or NCOP, and committed to responding by the end of the week.
University of the Free State accommodation
The UFS manages accommodation independently. At the Qwaqwa campus, there was insufficient accredited accommodation. NSFAS provided direct accommodation allowances to students to secure their own safe housing. Long-term solutions were supported through the infrastructure program, though shortages of approximately 100 000 beds remained nationwide.
Student engagement and support
Students were encouraged to use the online portal first, followed by the contact centre; in-person support was reserved for complex cases, particularly for rural students. NSFAS staff were building stronger relationships with both students and institutions as part of a fully regionalised and decentralised model.
Outstanding claims
During the academic year, institutions submit claims for tuition, accommodation, and allowances, which NSFAS settles. Reconciliation occurs post-academic year through the “closeout project” (initiated in 2021). As of the last financial statement, NSFAS owed institutions approximately R14 billion, while institutions owed NSFAS R7 billion -- a net amount of R7 billion owed by NSFAS.
Allegations at Central Johannesburg College
Reports of accommodation providers offering bribes to students, student representative councils (SRCs) or NSFAS staff. had been referred to the NSFAS forensic unit. Providers engaging in such behaviour faced suspension from NSFAS platforms.
Student allowances
In 2025, allowances were dispersed consistently, with no missed payments. Instances of “defunding” were limited to cases where students materially misrepresented information, or attempted to register for a second qualification while already holding one.
Funding stability and the "missing middle"
State allocations had not kept pace with inflation, while more students qualified for NSFAS due to better education outcomes and the rising cost of living. The Cabinet had approved consultation on a comprehensive, sustainable funding model in 2026, in collaboration with the DHET. The "missing middle" loan fund existed, but required greater awareness and marketing to boost uptake.
Student accommodation safety
Private accommodation providers had to meet safety, security and amenity standards, including transport to and from campuses. Students using unaccredited accommodation faced higher risks, and law enforcement and campus security provided additional support.
Comprehensive student funding model
NSFAS was undertaking policy and consultation work to develop a sustainable model of student support.
Ms Lewin thanked CEO Carrim for his responses. She noted that this year had been calmer, attributing improvements to NSFAS staff being on the ground, working closely with university colleagues, and sharing information at the campus level with potential students and institutions. She emphasised the Department’s commitment to collaborating with NSFAS on long-term policy and sustainability issues, acknowledging that Mr Carrim had accurately highlighted the challenges that required policy-level solutions.
Higher Health's student support initiatives
Prof Ahluwalia provided an overview of Higher Health’s mental health and student support initiatives. He began by outlining the toll-free 24-hour mental health crisis helpline, which logs roughly 13 000 cases annually. Each crisis typically took three working days to stabilise, after which the case was handed over to the counselling or psychosocial team -- a process that could take up to nine months due to the severity of the impact. Last year, 12 828 cases were handled through the crisis helpline, with a total of 35 211 mental health cases receiving follow-up therapy. These figures aligned with the World Health Organisation (WHO) report showing that one in four students in higher education experiences mental health challenges.
Prof Ahluwalia then highlighted GBV statistics for the 2024/25 academic year. There had been 311 reported cases, with 192 on campus and 118 off campus. On-site counselling had been provided to 689 young women, while 54 had declined counselling. Most cases had involved partner violence, and while psychosocial support was prioritised, connectivity to the criminal justice system was actively encouraged.
On bullying and social media, he noted that the civic and soft skills program, launched last year, had already trained 12 000 young South Africans at National Qualifications Framework (NQF) Level 5. Each participant had been required to teach ten peers, creating a multiplier effect that reached an estimated 120 000 individuals, raising awareness on mental health, GBV, and gambling issues.
Prof Ahluwalia also highlighted the expansion of on-campus social worker support. He said 150 social workers were now deployed across rural and urban TVET and university campuses, with 30 more in community colleges. These on-site professionals enabled faster reporting, counselling and care, while also creating jobs in rural areas. Funding was being sought to increase these numbers to meet the growing demand.
Regarding gambling addiction, Higher Health was developing a national framework and peer-to-peer programs, supported by NFSAS and sector education and training authorities (SETAs). Early risk assessments and interventions, modelled after substance abuse programs, were being implemented to provide timely social care.
On transforming mentalities, all institutions were establishing men’s forums to engage male students as peer educators and identify potential perpetrators of misconduct. South Africa was the first African country, and second globally, to adopt UNESCO’s Transforming Mentalities framework, reflecting the seriousness of this initiative.
He concluded by emphasising that while these interventions were impactful, sustained effort was required to fully address mental health, GBV, bullying, and gambling challenges across the post-school education sector.
Closing remarks
The Chairperson expressed appreciation for the engagement, but highlighted several gaps and concerns. He commented that Members had identified serious gaps in the presentations, and were dissatisfied with how some information had been delivered. However, to ensure progress, they had engaged constructively rather than rejecting the presentations or the meeting. He requested formal written responses to all issues raised to address the gaps, emphasising that responses must be clear, direct, and timely.
He pointed out that the DG was not present, and that communication should be improved so that information from one person could be passed to others, avoiding situations where responses were delayed or unavailable. He highlighted that some correspondence, including a case sent to the CEO’s office, had not been responded to. He stressed that this was not an isolated issue, and that the public relied on timely responses to have their concerns addressed.
The Chairperson requested that all returned responses be provided within seven days, stressing that delays hindered the Committee’s ability to perform its constitutional and political mandate effectively. Without timely feedback, oversight visits and interventions became meaningless to constituents. He emphasised that issues raised must be taken seriously, not treated as a formality or a box-ticking exercise. He said the Committee would follow up with the Minister where necessary to ensure commitments were fulfilled.
The meeting was adjourned.
