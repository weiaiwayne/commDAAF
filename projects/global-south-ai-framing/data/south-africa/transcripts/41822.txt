Annual Reports 2024/25
The Portfolio Committee on Science, Technology and Innovation met to receive briefings from the Council for Scientific and Industrial Research (CSIR) and the South African Council for Natural Scientific Professions (SACNASP) on their 2024/25 Annual Reports. The Chairperson opened the meeting by welcoming attendees, including the Deputy Minister of Science, Technology and Innovation, and explained that the session would include presentations followed by discussions. The CSIR was the first to present, reporting that it had achieved or exceeded 29 of its 31 performance indicators, marking its sixth year of implementing the 2019–2025 strategy. It reaffirmed its mandate to advance scientific and industrial development while addressing national challenges such as poverty, unemployment and inequality. Highlights included innovations in sustainable packaging, artificial intelligence for food security, and literacy enhancement through the “Ngiyaqonda!” app. The CSIR also exceeded its small, medium and micro enterprise (SMME) support targets and expanded infrastructure projects worth R505 million. Financially, the organisation recorded total income of R3.543 billion, an 11% increase from the previous year, and achieved a net profit of R40.62 million against a projected loss. The Auditor-General issued an unqualified audit opinion, confirming strong financial governance and compliance.
The South African Council for Natural Scientific Professions (SACNASP) reported that it had continued to execute its mandate as the registration and regulatory body for natural science professionals in South Africa. It achieved progress in professional registration, ethics, and public engagement, having completed 85% of a major policy advisory report on future scientific skills. Registrations increased to 3,141, exceeding targets, while retention stood at 84%. The organisation also advanced transformation through programmes involving women and youth, although its lifelong learning participation rate was slightly below target. SACNASP implemented flagship initiatives such as the SheSTEAM project, benefiting over 350 girls from disadvantaged communities, and expanded its Continuing Professional Development (CPD) and Candidate Mentoring Phase (CMP) programmes in collaboration with municipalities and government departments. Financially, SACNASP’s income remained stable at R33.13 million, with a 9% increase in revenue from registered scientists and a 24% rise in investment income. The entity reported a 4% decline in operating expenditure, a 26% decrease in bad debts, and an increased surplus of R1.82 million. It maintained an unqualified audit outcome, confirming sound financial management and sustainability.
During the discussion, Members raised concerns about SACNASP's accumulated surplus of R8.4 million, suggesting that these funds should be used for transformation initiatives rather than retained. Questions were also asked about the decline in operational expenditure, the measurable outcomes of SACNASP's international science diplomacy activities, and the equity of its partnerships, which appeared to favour metropolitan municipalities over rural areas. Members sought clarification on SACNASP's qualitative performance indicators, legislative amendments, and compliance measures for unregistered practitioners. The CSIR faced questions on financial independence, private sector engagement, energy and infrastructure research contributions, and its role in mitigating corruption and cybersecurity threats. Members also discussed how the CSIR's innovations could support rural communities, the expansion of literacy initiatives, and efforts to maintain financial stability amid global challenges.
In conclusion, the Deputy Minister commended both entities for their work and reaffirmed the importance of collaboration, financial accountability, and inclusivity in science. The Chairperson expressed the Committee's satisfaction with the presentations and responses, thanked all participants for their engagement, and encouraged continued transparency and cooperation between the Department, its entities, and Parliament.

CSIR Annual Report 2024/25
Led by the Chief Executive Officer (CEO), Dr Thulani Dlamini, the CSIR presentation reported that during the 2024/25 financial year, it had achieved or exceeded 29 of its 31 key performance indicators, marking the sixth year of implementing its 2019–2025 strategy. The organisation reaffirmed its mandate to drive scientific and industrial development in South Africa and highlighted its role in addressing national challenges such as poverty, unemployment, and inequality. Performance results showed a 22% increase in government and state-owned enterprise projects, a 17% rise in joint industry initiatives, and an 11% increase in localised technologies. Significant innovations included compostable packaging, an AI-based food security tool, sustainable fungal protein production, and the Ngiyaqonda! literacy app to improve early reading in home languages.
The CSIR continued to strengthen its support for small, medium and micro enterprises (SMMEs), exceeding its target by supporting 175 enterprises. Efforts to enhance human capital development were evident through increased representation of black (73%) and female (40%) staff in science, engineering, and technology roles, as well as new leadership and skills development programmes. Infrastructure investments reached R505 million, supporting the establishment of new facilities such as the FuturePharma manufacturing plant, a transport safety laboratory, and an encapsulation facility for advanced research applications.
Financially, the CSIR reported total income of R3.543 billion, surpassing its target by R422 million and representing an 11% increase from the previous year. Local public sector income accounted for 64% of total revenue, while private and international income represented 7% and 10% respectively. The entity achieved a net profit of R40.62 million against a projected loss of R67.6 million, reflecting robust cost management and efficiency measures. It maintained a healthy current ratio of 1.40 and continued to diversify its income through large-scale infrastructure projects and strategic partnerships aligned with national priorities.
The Auditor-General of South Africa issued an unqualified audit opinion, confirming sound financial governance and compliance. The CSIR also maintained a Level 1 B-BBEE rating and recorded a low incident rate of 0.12, reflecting its strong commitment to safety and ethical conduct. Despite facing challenges such as declining public funding, skills shortages, and global economic pressures, the organisation reaffirmed its financial sustainability and strategic relevance as a leading driver of research, development and innovation in South Africa.

South African Council Natural Science Professionals (SACNASP) Annual Report 2024/25
Led by the CEO, Dr Nompumelelo Obokoh, the SACNASP presentation reported that during the 2024/25 financial year, it continued to execute its mandate as the registration and regulatory body for natural science professionals in South Africa. Its performance reflected steady progress across its strategic objectives, particularly in advancing professional registration, ethical standards, and public engagement. SACNASP completed 85% of its major policy advisory report on the skills and competencies required for future natural scientists, with publication expected in November 2025. Registration of professionals rose significantly to 3,141, exceeding the annual target of 2,800, while retention stood at 84%. The Council also achieved its outreach and transformation goals through increased participation of women and youth in its mentorship and student enrolment programmes. Although the lifelong learning participation rate fell slightly short at 53% against a 60% target, several interventions were implemented to address this gap.
Operationally, SACNASP delivered a number of flagship initiatives aimed at promoting inclusivity and professionalisation. The SheSTEAM programme benefited over 350 girls in underprivileged Western Cape communities, integrating learning in robotics, coding, and hydrogen technologies. The Council expanded partnerships with municipalities and national departments to roll out Continuing Professional Development (CPD) and Candidate Mentoring Phase (CMP) programmes, supporting over 2,000 public sector scientists. It also played a key role in international cooperation through participation in the World Science and Technology Development Forum in Beijing, fostering bilateral science diplomacy between South Africa and China.
Financially, SACNASP recorded stable performance with total income of R33.13 million, largely unchanged from the prior year. Revenue from registered scientists increased by 9% to R20.9 million, while other income rose by 24% due to higher investment returns. DSTI project income, however, declined by 18% as a result of underspending on some project activities. Operating expenditure decreased by 4% to R31.31 million, reflecting cost-saving measures and reductions in project expenditure and bad debts. Salaries rose by 20% due to inflation and staff expansion, while bad debts were reduced by 26% to R2.34 million. The entity achieved a surplus of R1.82 million, a 23.5% increase from the previous year, with accumulated reserves rising to R8.42 million.
In terms of governance, SACNASP maintained an unqualified audit outcome for the year, with no fruitless or wasteful expenditure reported. The Council stated that its financial position remained strong and sustainable, with adequate resources to continue operations into the foreseeable future. Looking ahead, SACNASP planned to strengthen its regulatory function through the pending NSP Amendment Bill, enhance professionalisation within the natural sciences, expand CPD offerings, and secure additional funding to support its long-term strategic objectives.

CSIR Responses
Income
Dr Dlamini began by thanking the Committee for its questions and comments. He responded to Mr Mjadu’s question by confirming that 64% of the CSIR’s income had come from the public sector. He explained that 20% of the organisation’s revenue had derived from the grant received from the Department, while the remainder had come from contract research undertaken with national, provincial, and local government.
Independence from Contract Work and the State
Dr Dlamini stated that achieving complete independence from state and contract work was an unrealistic expectation. He reminded the Committee that the CSIR had been established by the state to serve and support it; therefore, dependence on state investment and collaboration would always remain essential. He illustrated this point by referring to the COVID-19 pandemic, when the government had relied heavily on the national system of innovation, science councils, and universities to devise critical solutions such as ventilators. He stressed that this had been possible only because of the state’s sustained investment in skills development within these institutions. He explained that the CSIR’s role was to intervene continuously to enable the state to deliver services effectively, and that there was no vision for the organisation to operate independently of government. He concluded that the CSIR’s existence was intrinsically linked to its partnership with the state.
Private Sector Distrust
The CEO reported that the CSIR had been intentional about diversifying its income base to reduce reliance on the public sector. He stated that, over the previous six years, private sector income had increased by more than 40%, while income from international projects had more than doubled. He said these figures disproved any perception of private sector distrust, demonstrating instead that collaboration with industry partners had strengthened. He affirmed that the CSIR would continue to position itself as a valuable and credible partner for private enterprises, contributing to the country’s competitiveness and sustainability.
Reaching Transformation Targets
Addressing concerns about unmet transformation targets among chief executives and researchers, Dr Dlamini disagreed with that sentiment. He reported that the target of 28% black chief researchers had been surpassed, with the organisation achieving 29%. Similarly, the target of 28% female researchers had nearly been met, with 24% achieved in the last year, largely due to promotions within the organisation. He cautioned against misinterpreting these outcomes as gender bias, explaining that the CSIR’s promotion process was self-managed and based on individual readiness and performance. It had simply occurred that more male researchers had met the criteria for promotion in the past year, but he expressed confidence that female researchers would reach parity in future. He added that, among principal researchers, the target of 37% black representation had been exceeded at 41%, and the goal of 24% female representation had nearly been met at 23%. He stated that various mechanisms were in place to support researchers’ growth and development, expressing satisfaction that the organisation was on the right track.
Job Creation Examples
Dr Dlamini linked his next response to the queries raised by Mr Mjadu and Mr Nkosi, emphasising that the CSIR’s contribution to job creation was indirect. He explained that the organisation’s role was to support partners and enable them to fulfil their own mandates in sustaining the economy. He noted that an impact assessment was underway to measure the CSIR’s long-term economic influence. Since job creation was not a direct function of the organisation, there were no performance indicators specific to employment. However, he believed that through its partnerships with small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) and technological support initiatives, the CSIR contributed significantly to job sustainability. He cited that the organisation had supported more than 770 small enterprises, each of which created employment opportunities and strengthened the economy, even though these jobs were not directly created by the CSIR itself.
Financial Stability Issues in the Geopolitical Climate
The CEO thanked Ms Mazzone for her positive remarks about the Global Sustainable Technology and Innovation Conference (GSTIC). He acknowledged that the prevailing geopolitical climate posed financial risks to some ongoing CSIR projects. He explained that an assessment had been undertaken, coordinated by the Minister, to evaluate the impact of global political developments on South Africa’s science and innovation communities, a matter on which the Deputy Director-General would elaborate further.
He illustrated the challenge with an example involving a Turkish enterprise with which the CSIR had collaborated. He reported that payments for completed work had been delayed because the Turkish company’s board chairperson and certain members had been sanctioned due to collaborations with states deemed unfavourable by Western nations. Consequently, South African banks had blocked access to these payments, resulting in R400 million being frozen. He explained that although the CSIR had successfully expanded its international business, such restrictions directly affected its operational capabilities. He stressed the need for government support to prevent future disruptions of this nature, even when the countries involved maintained positive diplomatic relations with South Africa.
Risk Management Against External Influences
Dr Dlamini stated that while the CSIR maintained comprehensive governance systems, no process was entirely foolproof. He affirmed that risk management was treated with utmost seriousness and that the organisation employed a combined assurance model alongside robust internal audit function. These ensured that the CSIR avoided compromising situations and upheld its core values of integrity, which were consistently communicated to staff.
In terms of safety mechanisms, he expressed confidence that existing systems were effective and that there was no cause for concern at present. He hoped that this positive record would continue and that the risk management measures in place would remain sufficient to protect the organisation.
Issues of Cyber Security
The CSIR CEO agreed that cybersecurity had become a serious national concern. He informed the Committee that the CSIR had established a Cyber Security Research Centre as part of a new strategic initiative. The centre, he said, was highly active and involved in multiple projects at national, provincial, and metropolitan levels, aimed at strengthening South Africa’s cyber resilience. He emphasised the need for greater investment in cyber skills to ensure adequate capacity and urged for increased resource allocation to sustain and expand the CSIR’s capabilities in this area.
Legislation Update
Dr Dlamini expressed appreciation for the suggestion regarding legislative review. He explained that the CSIR had held discussions with its Board to determine how the organisation could be better positioned to deliver on its mandate, as referenced by the Deputy Director-General in opening remarks. He acknowledged that, despite the organisation’s strong capabilities, certain legal constraints hindered its operations. Responding to Dr Boshoff’s earlier query, he noted that an analysis had been conducted into contracts that could not be concluded because of unclear engagement models between the CSIR and public entities. He reported that over R6 million worth of potential contracts had been lost due to such uncertainty, as public sector partners were reluctant to proceed where procedural risks were perceived. He asserted that greater clarity in public procurement processes was needed and that legislative adjustments could empower the CSIR to collaborate more effectively with other state entities.
Loadshedding Research
Dr Dlamini acknowledged awareness of the challenges surrounding loadshedding, noting that many of the solutions implemented were maintenance-based rather than technological. He explained that the CSIR’s contribution included assisting Eskom with steam turbine repairs using laser-based systems that eliminated the need to remove parts for overseas repairs, thereby saving time and costs. He described this as a cost-effective intervention that allowed domestic maintenance capacity. He also referred to several programmes run by the CSIR’s Energy Centre focused on load management and performance monitoring. Additionally, he confirmed that the CSIR had contributed to the Integrated Resource Plan, providing technical input and drafting reports submitted to both the Minister of Electricity and the Minister responsible for the Committee. He concluded that continuous collaboration among the CSIR, Eskom, and the Department had been vital to improving South Africa’s energy system.
Financial Loss and Non-Grant Income
Dr Dlamini reported that the CSIR had achieved 8.7% growth in contractual income over the past year, describing this as evidence of success. He explained that both private sector and international income streams had expanded significantly and that the organisation had recorded a profit of R400 million, indicating financial stability. He clarified that while certain divisions occasionally experienced challenges, the organisation continuously reviewed its operations to ensure long-term sustainability.
Entrepreneurship Increase
Addressing entrepreneurship initiatives, Dr Dlamini acknowledged that the CSIR faced constraints due to limited funding, despite receiving over 400 applications for its entrepreneurship programmes. He suggested that the Committee could assist by helping to secure additional funding, as the demand for such programmes was high and their impact substantial.
Offices in Rural Areas
Dr Dlamini acknowledged that the suggestion to establish offices in rural areas was valuable but explained that it was not currently feasible due to resource limitations. He noted that expanding the organisation’s footprint would increase overhead costs. He emphasised that the main CSIR campus was in Pretoria but that regional offices in Durban, Gqeberha, Stellenbosch, and Cape Town helped ensure visibility and a broader geographic reach. While these locations did not cover the entire country, he reiterated that financial constraints made further expansion impractical for the time being.
Monitoring of Environmental Management Practices
Dr Dlamini stated that the Deputy Director-General would elaborate further on environmental matters but affirmed that the CSIR was active in this area even if it did not regularly report such details to the Committee.
Advocate Esmé Kennedy, the CSIR Group Executive: Legal, Compliance and Business Enablement, explained that the organisation had implemented a biodiversity plan covering all operational sites and neighbouring communities. He stated that this plan had been developed through several baseline studies to ensure compliance with environmental legislation. National biodiversity goals had been set to strengthen environmental stewardship, including integrated waste management, species conservation, and greenhouse gas monitoring. He reported that the CSIR tracked its carbon emissions to establish its carbon footprint and had developed reduction strategies accordingly. Although current emissions remained below regulatory thresholds, the organisation continued to monitor and set new reduction targets as part of its biodiversity and environmental management plan.
Collaborations with the Department of Basic Education
Dr Sandile Malinga, the CSIR Group Executive: Smart Society, reported that the CSIR had not yet engaged the national Department of Basic Education regarding the Ndiyaqonda! Literacy programme. He explained that the initiative had originated in Gauteng, specifically in Mamelodi and Soweto, and that the current collaboration was limited to the Gauteng Department of Education. Once the programme had been refined and tested, he said, the CSIR would seek engagement with the national department to explore nationwide implementation.
Dr Dlamini concluded by stating that all questions had been addressed. The Deputy Minister thanked him and his team for their responses and invited SACNASP to present their replies next.
SACNASP Responses
Science Diplomacy Engagement
Dr Obokoh began by expressing gratitude for the engagement and feedback, which she said would help strengthen the organisation’s mandate. She explained that, in evaluating SACNASP’s role as a regulatory body for scientists, science diplomacy should be used to expand opportunities holistically, particularly for registered scientists. She added that this approach would create pathways for collaboration and align South African scientists with advanced international practices.
She elaborated that the partnership with the China Association of Science and Technology focused on skills development, technology transfer, and collaborative research initiatives designed to invest in the broader scientific community. This included training exchanges, job search programmes, and institutional partnerships, which had generated strong interest among young scientists. She noted that the call for participation had been sent to the 20,000 registered scientists in SACNASP’s database, with proposals assessed based on their potential to build sustainable partnerships and return to South Africa to apply the acquired knowledge. The Memorandum of Understanding had been signed in 2024, and impact tracking would follow to determine whether participants fulfilled the agreed objectives. The findings would be used to assess how beneficiaries had leveraged the partnership to expand their research and professional work.
Professionalisation Drive
Dr Obokoh stated that the key objective of the partnership with the City of Cape Town was to advance professionalisation at provincial and municipal levels. She expressed appreciation to the Chairperson for recognising the importance of this initiative. She explained that the City of Cape Town had approached SACNASP after encountering challenges with other professional entities, particularly in cases where scientists were not adequately represented. The aim of the collaboration, she said, was to understand and strengthen the existing professionalisation strategy, assist with the registration of scientists, and use the partnership as a model for all municipalities.
She further explained that this collaboration would benefit municipalities with limited capacity. SACNASP was also forming a partnership with the National Institute for the Humanities and Social Sciences (NIHSS) to expand the initiative to under-resourced municipalities that required scientific and technical expertise. She stressed that it was crucial for the organisation to use the strategy to build capacity in areas where it was most needed.
Legislative Change
The CEO informed the Committee that SACNASP was already in the process of amending the Natural Scientific Professions Act (NSPF), as mentioned in the presentation. She stated that this process had been initiated by the Department of Science, Technology and Innovation (DSTI) and that the timeline for its completion was not an issue. SACNASP was currently preparing for submission to Cabinet, having already engaged with the cluster of Directors-General on the matter.
Duplication of Efforts
Dr Obokoh clarified that SACNASP operated under the NSP Act, which empowered it to regulate the natural sciences. She explained that SACNASP was the only entity within the Department that focused exclusively on scientists, functioning as a statutory regulatory body responsible for enforcing ethical standards to protect public interests. This, she said, lay at the core of the organisation’s legislative mandate. She added that the revision of the NSP Bill would further strengthen this mandate and that SACNASP would engage with the Committee once the amendments were finalised. She expressed appreciation for the Committee’s willingness to assist in this process.
Affordability and Access to Registration
The CEO reported that SACNASP had received numerous requests from young scientists seeking registration support, with some staff members personally contributing funds to help individuals who could not afford registration fees. She cited the Engineering Council of South Africa (ECSA) as an example of how financial support mechanisms could be used to assist disadvantaged applicants. SACNASP, she said, planned to collaborate with industry sectors to achieve a zero-rated registration fee model, with assistance from the new Council. Discussions were also underway with the Department to identify ways to support early-career scientists, taking into account their socioeconomic backgrounds and related challenges.
She emphasised that the organisation wanted its scientists to thrive and contribute to the country’s economic development, noting that SACNASP maintained a strong youth focus. She added that the development of the professionalisation drive, candidate mentorship programme, and Continuing Professional Development (CPD) initiatives reflected a commitment to inclusivity. The organisation was also intervening in rural areas through partnerships with the National System of Innovation (NSI) to ensure alignment and avoid duplication of mandates.
Consequences for Failure to Register
Dr Magezi Mabaso, Council Member, explained that enforcing compliance with registration requirements formed part of SACNASP’s legislative mandate. He stated that the organisation would ensure accountability for scientists who failed to register or renew their annual membership. He said the enforcement strategy would prevent unregistered individuals from practising within the natural sciences.
He added that SACNASP planned to collaborate with the Engineering Professions of South Africa (EPSA) to establish a bilateral agreement requiring all government departments employing scientists to verify valid registration before allowing them to practise. He warned that failing to do so could have serious financial and legal implications for government. For example, he said, if an unregistered scientist signed an official report, it could be invalidated in court, potentially causing financial losses to the state. The aim, therefore, was to hold unregistered practitioners accountable and safeguard professional integrity.
Accumulated Surplus and Utilisation of Revenue
Mr Terrence Mangalana, the Chief Financial Officer (CFO), SACNASP, explained that the reported growth of R8.4 million should be understood within the context of prudent financial management and adherence to the Public Finance Management Act (PFMA). He clarified that the accumulated surplus represented funds built up over several years and reflected SACNASP’s consistent record of efficient resource management and financial stability. He confirmed that these funds would be used to strengthen the Council’s mandate and contribute to strategic priorities, rather than remaining idle.
Decline in Operational Expenditure
Mr Mangalana explained that the reduction in operational expenditure was the result of cost-containment measures, improved efficiency, and a transition to digital operations following the COVID-19 pandemic. He stressed that the decline did not indicate a withdrawal from service delivery. He stated that activities such as physical outreach, travel, and in-person events were now conducted in collaboration with strategic partners to minimise duplication and maximise value for money. He added that the reduction in bad debt had also contributed to the lower expenditure levels. This approach, he said, enabled SACNASP to channel resources towards core priorities such as strengthening registration systems, enhancing governance capacity, and promoting professional development.
Negative Operating Activities
Mr Mangalana clarified that the negative cash flow recorded for the year was primarily due to delays in receivables and the timing of annual fee invoicing. He explained that invoicing typically occurred between January and February, but during the financial year under review, the process had been delayed pending a presidential announcement that affected the timeline. Consequently, invoices had been issued later than usual, leaving insufficient time for settlements before the financial year-end. He confirmed that SACNASP had extended the payment deadline to March to allow scientists adequate time to settle their accounts.
Improvement of Expenditure
The CFO reported that SACNASP had already implemented a financial recovery plan aimed at improving expenditure management and accelerating project implementation. He said the plan focused on expediting delayed initiatives, streamlining internal processes, and enhancing project execution capacity to ensure timely delivery. He added that quarterly performance reviews had been introduced, along with stronger coordination between the finance and operations divisions, to improve spending efficiency.
He further emphasised that SACNASP typically received its funding from the DSTI late in the financial year, between January and March, which limited implementation time. He explained that earlier release of funds would significantly improve expenditure performance. Engagements with the DSTI were already underway to secure earlier transfers, ideally at the start of the financial year in April, to ensure uninterrupted project implementation.
Closing Remarks
The Deputy Minister then invited the Deputy Director-General to deliver closing remarks before concluding the meeting. The Deputy Director-General thanked both entities for their presentations and comprehensive responses, as well as the Committee for its engagement. He concluded by stating that any outstanding questions from Members would be addressed through written responses submitted by both entities in due course.
Deputy Minister’s Response
Deputy Minister Gina thanked the Portfolio Committee and remarked that its engagements were always insightful and constructively challenging to both the Department and its entities. She emphasised that science was a field that thrived on collaboration rather than isolation. She stated that there was a pressing need for international partnerships to enable the exchange of skills and the sharing of cultural and scientific knowledge. She added that Parliament would continue to observe and learn from the processes and experiences of other countries, working closely with the Department and its entities in doing so.
The Deputy Minister highlighted the importance of ensuring that public funds were spent responsibly and with measurable impact. She explained that the overarching goal was to expand South Africa’s scientific expertise by learning from international best practice and adapting it locally, thereby strengthening both the entities and the broader government system. She reiterated that, although all activities must account for the use of public funds, this focus reinforced the commitment to delivering services that benefited the citizens of the country.
She further noted that collaboration also created opportunities for marketing and exposure, particularly for disadvantaged scientists who lacked access to such platforms. She described this as central to making science a tool for community development and local engagement. In conclusion, she reaffirmed the Department’s commitment to expanding opportunities through collaboration, expressing confidence that with the continued support of the Committee, more disadvantaged individuals would gain access and exposure within the scientific community.
Closing Remarks
The Chairperson stated that the Committee was satisfied with the responses provided by the entities. She thanked the Deputy Minister for attending, commenting that her presence always elevated the quality and spirit of the discussions. She also expressed appreciation to the Deputy Director-General for his participation.
She extended gratitude to both entities for their presentations and conveyed special appreciation to the CSIR for the positive experience shared during the GSTIC. She acknowledged the growing capacity within the Department. She expressed optimism that the President would, in future, present the CSIR's work at Cabinet level, thereby giving wider exposure to the institution's achievements. She described this prospect as exciting and beneficial for the country, noting that the Committee's oversight visits had deepened its understanding of the CSIR's operations.
The Chairperson stressed that it was part of the Committee’s duty to communicate the Department’s projects and initiatives to the public to promote transparency and national development. She said she was hopeful that further collaboration would follow, particularly when entities were invited to present to the Committee, as the working relationship continued to strengthen. She congratulated the entities on their new responsibilities and affirmed that while the Committee remained supportive, it would also continue to apply critical oversight to their initiatives.
In closing, she expressed her wish that, with increased funding and collaboration, more projects and initiatives would emerge from the entities and the Department. She concluded by thanking all participants for their engagement and contributions.
The meeting was adjourned.
