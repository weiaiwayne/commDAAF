Strategic Plan
The Portfolio Committee met virtually to receive the 2025/26 Annual Performance Plans and budget briefings from the Department of Water and Sanitation (DWS), the Trans-Caledon Tunnel Authority (TCTA), and the Water Research Commission (WRC). The meeting aimed to assess the institutional readiness, policy alignment, and implementation capacity across the water and sanitation sector.
Deputy Minister David Mahlobo acknowledged the sector’s failure to meet the targets of the National Development Plan. He stated that deteriorating infrastructure, institutional weaknesses, and external pressures, such as climate change and population growth, had resulted in 105 out of 144 water services authorities being classified as dysfunctional. He outlined a reform programme focused on stabilisation, improved governance, enforcement, and collaborative oversight with the National Treasury and the Auditor-General.
The Deputy Minister cautioned that messaging about water conservation must be carefully framed to avoid penalising communities already lacking access to water.
On the issue of municipal debt and the perceived disparity between service delivery and the salaries of municipal officials, the Deputy Minister clarified that the Department could not dictate local government salary structures, as collective agreements governed these matters. The Department was cautious not to undermine such agreements. However, he stressed that performance needed to be demanded. As part of its reforms, the Department aimed to introduce performance-based contracts for officials working in water utilities, whether municipally or privately owned.
Deputy Minister Mahlobo stressed that the Department's central mission was to improve water reliability. Over the next five years, the reliability target needed to increase from 68% to over 80%, while access must rise from 90% to 95%. At the same time, non-revenue water had to be reduced from 47% to 32%. He urged all stakeholders to collaborate in understanding the interconnection of these various indicators.
The Department’s plan prioritised infrastructure development, improved regulatory compliance, sector transformation, and legislative reform. The Water and Sanitation Master Plan and Sustainable Development Goals were referenced as guiding frameworks. The Department also committed to strengthening monitoring and support to municipalities.
The Trans-Caledon Tunnel Authority highlighted progress on major infrastructure projects such as the Lesotho Highlands Water Project Phase II. Officials cited delays due to site challenges and inflation-linked cost escalations. They reported plans to raise capital through bond markets, pending guarantees from the National Treasury.
The Water Research Commission presentation focused on research, innovation, and sector capacity-building. The Commission reported on succession planning, gender transformation, and collaborative partnerships across academia and government.
Committee Members raised serious concerns about the widespread collapse of municipal wastewater treatment, frequent sewage spills, and the deterioration of water quality in towns and rivers. They criticised the Department for its continued reliance on consultants, lack of internal capacity, and failure to enforce accountability. Some Members questioned the sustainability of TCTA’s funding model and the feasibility of proposed timelines, particularly in light of past project delays. Others expressed disappointment at the limited translation of WRC research into practical solutions at the community level. Concerns were also raised about youth development, intergovernmental coordination, and the absence of clear, measurable outcomes.
Officials responded by outlining recovery efforts, ongoing litigation where necessary, enforcement mechanisms, and plans to bolster technical capacity. The Committee resolved to strengthen oversight of all three entities, emphasising the need for practical impact and accountability in service delivery.

Deputy Minister’s Opening Remarks
Introduction and Delegation Details
Mr David Mahlobo, Deputy Minister, DWS, extended his well wishes following the Passover and Easter weekend. He informed the Committee that he would be joined by Mr Isaac Seitlholo, Deputy Minister, DWS, in leading the delegation from the Department.
Deputy Minister Mahlobo indicated that the departmental delegation was administratively led by Mr Frans Moatshe, Chief Financial Officer (CFO), who was acting on behalf of Dr Sean Phillips, the Director-General. The delegation also included Ms Nthabiseng Fundakubi, Deputy Director-General (DDG) for Corporate Support Services; Dr Risimati Mathye, DDG for Water Services Management; Mr Xolani Zwane, DDG for Regulation, Compliance, and Enforcement; Mr Livhuwani Mabuda, Acting DDG for Water Resource Infrastructure Management; and Dr Moloko Matlala, Chief Director for Water Information Management.
The delegation was further accompanied by the Special Advisor to the Minister, Mr China Dodovu.
Deputy Minister Mahlobo reported that the delegation from the TCTA was led by the Chairperson of the Board, Ms Precious Sibiya, supported by the Chief Executive Officer (CEO), Mr Percy Sechemane, and the Chief Financial Officer, Ms Andisa Zinja.
The Water Research Commission (WRC) delegation was led by Dr Rethabile Melamu, the WRC Board Chairperson, supported by Dr Jennifer Molwantwa, CEO, and other representatives.
Structure of the Presentations
Deputy Minister Mahlobo explained that the meeting would receive three presentations: first from the DWS, followed by TCTA, and concluding with the WRC. He indicated that, in line with established guidelines, each entity’s Board Chairperson would introduce their respective input, after which the accounting officers would lead the high-level presentations.
He confirmed that the presentations would focus on the entities’ Strategic Plans and Annual Performance Plans (APPs). He acknowledged the Portfolio Committee’s ongoing support in assisting the Department to advance its mandate of ensuring water security and universal access to water and sanitation services across South Africa.
Alignment with National Plans and Strategic Context
Deputy Minister Mahlobo indicated that the Department was entering the final stages of implementation of the National Development Plan (NDP) 2030 targets, alongside the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs). He noted that while South Africa might not meet all the targets, significant progress had been achieved. He attributed the increased demand for water services to economic growth, population expansion, and urbanisation, particularly in metropolitan centres such as Johannesburg, Cape Town, and Durban.
He highlighted that climate change and international obligations continued to impact national water security. He confirmed that the Department’s presentation would outline investment plans to support major bulk water resource infrastructure. While acknowledging project delays, he noted that recovery efforts had been undertaken, including work monitored during the Committee’s oversight visit to Lesotho, where the Minister of Water and Sanitation, Ms Pemmy Majodina, had intervened to realign delivery efforts.
Service Delivery Challenges and Trust Deficit
Deputy Minister Mahlobo acknowledged that many communities continued to experience failures in water and sanitation service delivery. He expressed concern regarding institutional non-responsiveness, particularly by municipalities. He noted that, although the country demonstrated overall water balance, some areas continued to face acute water insecurity, with either no water infrastructure or intermittent supply.
He reported that in some regions, communities endured extended periods without water, while others had no supply at all. He added that the Department’s planning processes were informed by multiple reports, including those by the Auditor-General of South Africa (AGSA), National Treasury experts, and internal departmental assessments. These reports collectively identified 105 of the 144 water services authorities as facing a service delivery crisis.
Deputy Minister Mahlobo underscored a growing trust deficit, noting that many communities no longer had confidence in their municipalities. He attributed this to the municipalities’ inability to meet their constitutional obligations, particularly in operating and maintaining infrastructure such as water and wastewater treatment works.
Reform Agenda and Sectoral Transformation
Deputy Minister Mahlobo clarified that the Department could not address all challenges in isolation and called for local government leadership to implement turnaround strategies agreed upon for their municipalities. He confirmed that the Department was proceeding with a comprehensive reform agenda to ensure that no community would be left behind.
He stated that the reform initiative aimed to change the water sector’s delivery model, which had been in place for the last 30 years. He noted that the Department’s new approach responded to the crisis in 105 municipalities and prioritised increased investment, alternative financing, strengthened technical capacity, and improved efficiency. He added that the plan would also promote water-sensitive communities and address criminality and corruption within the sector.
Conclusion and Presentation Handover
Deputy Minister Mahlobo concluded that these priorities would be shared with the Committee during the session. He confirmed that the President had directed the Department to develop a turnaround plan for the water sector, emphasising that water was the country’s top priority. He then invited Mr Frans Moatshe to deliver the Department’s high-level presentation on the Strategic Plan and APP, which had been circulated to the Committee.

Water Research Commission Presentation
Dr Rethablie Melawu, Chairperson, WRC, alongside Dr Jennifer Molwantwa, CEO, presented the WRC’s Annual Performance Plan for 2025/26.
Strategic Focus
The WRC's presentation outlined its strategic focus, which includes a stakeholder-centric research agenda, prioritisation of funding for water and sanitation, and an emphasis on knowledge generation and dissemination. The WRC also focuses on skills development and capacity building. Additionally, there is a strong emphasis on human capital development, intending to increase the WRC's footprint through the Centre of Excellence. This aligns with the need for Research, Development, and Innovation (RDI) solutions that support evidence-based decision-making, creating a national, regional, continental, and global RDI impact.
External and Internal Factors of Influence
The presentation highlighted both external and internal factors that affect the WRC's ability to deliver on its mandate. External factors include climate change, digital transformation, the state of South Africa’s water research enterprise, the condition of the country's water resources and services, and the broader economic environment. Internally, the WRC faces challenges related to its core competencies, organisational culture, employee satisfaction, the digitisation of business processes, and the need for greater brand awareness and visibility.
Institutional Programmes
The WRC is organised into three institutional programmes based on its optimal operating model:
Administration Programme: This programme provides strategic direction and leadership to the organisation through Planning, Monitoring, and Evaluation (PME), Risk and Compliance Management, ICT Governance, Finance, and Corporate Services units. The outcomes of this programme aim to ensure an agile and financially sustainable organisation.
Research, Development, and Innovation (RDI) Programme: The focus of this programme is on generating new knowledge and innovation, supported by continuous human capital development. This programme aims to foster an informed, innovative, and transformed water sector that contributes to sustainable water management.
Stakeholder and Communication Programme: This programme is centred around building collaborative partnerships and engaging with stakeholders both locally and internationally. The goal is to extend the WRC’s footprint and enhance the impact of its knowledge and innovation products. The desired outcomes are a more informed water society and a financially sustainable organisation.

Discussion
Mr V Reddy (MKP) referred to page 15 of the DWS APP and 5-year Strategic Plan presentation, which reflected that only 46% of water systems met microbiological quality standards. He contended that this statistic implied that nearly half of South Africans were consuming water that was unsafe and posed a health risk. He asked why this situation had not been declared a national health emergency.
He turned to the issue of non-revenue water, as presented on page 20, which currently stood at 47%. He remarked that this figure demonstrated that nearly half of the country’s water supply was being lost before reaching consumers. Although the Department had committed to reducing this figure to 32% by 2030, Mr Reddy argued that this still amounted to a third of South Africa’s water being lost over the long term. He suggested that the Department appeared to have accepted this as a norm while communities continued to experience water shedding.
Mr Reddy asked whether the Department was leveraging the Water Research Commission and other entities under the Department’s portfolio to fund more urgent and innovative strategies to reduce water losses, beyond what was currently planned.
He further referred to page 16 of the presentation, which revealed that municipalities owed water boards approximately R25 billion. He expressed concern that these were the same municipalities where officials were receiving high salaries, yet rural communities remained without a basic water supply. He questioned what concrete action the Department was taking to address this situation, adding that the Committee was not seeing results from what he characterised as ongoing reform discussions and policy dialogues.
Lastly, Mr Reddy referred to page 11 of the presentation, which noted that South Africans consumed an average of 218 litres of water per person daily—far exceeding the international average of 173 litres per person per day—despite South Africa being classified among the 30 most water-scarce countries globally. He queried whether this overconsumption reflected inefficiencies, waste, and a lack of accountability, and called on the Department to outline short-term enforcement measures to address these challenges.
Deputy Minister Mahlobo thanked Mr Reddy for his contribution and explained that the Department’s introductory remarks were intended to illustrate the terrain and the complexity of the challenges it faced. He emphasised that the Department could not resolve these issues alone, which was why a whole-of-society approach was necessary. The DWS was the first in the Seventh Administration under the Government of National Unity (GNU) to actively call on all stakeholders to assist in addressing these systemic problems.
Responding to the question on water quality, Deputy Minister Mahlobo referred to slide 12 of the DWS presentation, which outlined findings from drop reports. He noted that the AGSA's report supported these, National Treasury reports, and other expert sources, including the Department’s own analysis. He explained that, based on this collective evidence, the Minister of Water and Sanitation had identified 105 of the 144 water services authorities as being in crisis due to their failure to meet service delivery responsibilities.
These municipalities were discussed in the Water Task Team meeting chaired by the Deputy President and attended by the Minister of Cooperative Governance and Traditional Affairs, the Minister of Human Settlements, and others. As a result of that engagement, each of the 105 municipalities was instructed to submit turnaround plans or corrective measures. While these plans had been submitted, the Deputy Minister acknowledged the ongoing risk of non-implementation. He emphasised the importance of local government leadership assuming responsibility, with oversight from Parliament and municipal councils.
On the matter of non-revenue water, Deputy Minister Mahlobo cautioned against losing sight of its root causes, which stem from infrastructure asset degradation and poor technical management. He affirmed that the goal of reducing non-revenue water from 47% to 32% was realistic. This reduction would include addressing both metered and unmetered losses, including illegal connections. Achieving this 15% decrease would result in an improvement in the reliability of supply from 68% to 80%.
He highlighted that many municipalities lacked basic infrastructure records, such as asset maps. This necessitated the use of technologies like artificial intelligence and digital tools to improve planning and decision-making. Once infrastructure assets reach the end of their lifespans, they must be replaced or renewed. For instance, he cited Johannesburg, which has over 800 kilometres of ageing pipelines, and Witbank, with more than 1 400 kilometres in need of replacement. In areas like Mfuleni, non-revenue water losses reach 72%, which cannot realistically be reversed in a five-year timeframe without significant support. The Deputy Minister underscored that these realities must be properly contextualised.
He added that while interventions like the War on Leaks programme could involve youth, asset failure required skilled intervention. Once the infrastructure was restored or renewed, trained personnel could be deployed to manage it using tools such as pressure-reducing valves, leak detectors, and flow restrictors.
Addressing the issue of high daily per capita water consumption, Deputy Minister Mahlobo stressed the importance of sensitivity. Some communities had consistent access to water and were prone to wastage, while others had no access at all. The Department had learned from the City of Cape Town’s successful introduction of tariffs targeting high-consumption households. However, he cautioned that messaging about water conservation must be carefully framed to avoid penalising communities already lacking access to water.
On the issue of municipal debt and the perceived disparity between service delivery and the salaries of municipal officials, Deputy Minister Mahlobo clarified that the Department could not dictate local government salary structures, as collective agreements governed these matters. The Department was cautious not to undermine such agreements. However, he stressed that performance needed to be demanded. As part of its reforms, the Department aimed to introduce performance-based contracts for officials working in water utilities, whether municipally or privately owned.
Deputy Minister Mahlobo concluded that the Department's central mission was to improve water reliability. Over the next five years, the reliability target needed to increase from 68% to over 80%, while access must rise from 90% to 95%. At the same time, non-revenue water had to be reduced from 47% to 32%. He called on all stakeholders to support one another in understanding how these various indicators were interconnected.
Mr Reddy remarked on the difficulty of receiving a clear response from Deputy Minister Mahlobo due to his extensive knowledge of the water sector. He nonetheless acknowledged the Deputy Minister’s explanation, although he felt it did not address the lived realities of citizens. Speaking on behalf of the MK Party, he stated that the party would consider its position on the matter in due course.
Mr Moore noted that he had several questions and therefore intended to speak holistically. He expressed concern that the budgets of the DWS and its entities were not necessarily aligned with the urgent priorities facing South Africa. He cited the deterioration in microbiological water quality compliance over the past decade—from 5% in 2014 to 46% in 2025—as evidence that the situation was worsening rather than improving. He further noted that nearly half of municipal water was lost before reaching households.
Mr Moore acknowledged the Department’s goal to increase reliable water access from 68% in 2022 to 80% by 2030 but felt this was overly optimistic in light of the water crisis in Gauteng. He criticised continued investment in traditional bulk water projects, arguing instead for a shift in focus towards more immediate interventions.
He began his questions by asking whether the Department would consider reallocating R1–R2 billion directly toward an intervention model akin to a “Marshall Plan” for municipalities to address water leaks and non-revenue water. Mr Moore stated that while addressing water loss was technically a function of the Department of Cooperative Governance and Traditional Affairs (CoGTA), the DWS should not continue investing in bulk infrastructure while municipalities were losing half of the country's water supply. He emphasised that the Department’s mandate was to ensure sustainable, equitable, and safe access to water and sanitation, and this required innovative, responsive planning.
His second question related to the reliability of water supply. Noting that one in three South Africans experienced water interruptions in 2022, he asked whether it was acceptable that millions would continue to have unreliable access to water even five years into the Department’s current planning cycle.
Lastly, Mr Moore raised the issue of sewage spills. He referred to the Green Drop Report, which showed that 334 wastewater treatment works were in critical condition. He explained that this contributed to sewage pollution in rivers nationwide, posing a health risk and increasing the threat of waterborne diseases such as cholera. He questioned why there was no dedicated budget line for urgent wastewater repairs or upgrades. He asked whether the Department would consider adjusting its budget to implement a targeted, measurable programme to address the immediate decay of critical infrastructure.
Deputy Minister Mahlobo stated that all South Africans were affected by the failures of municipalities in fulfilling their duties and responsibilities. However, he stressed that the Committee must not lose sight of the fact that constitutional delineations of responsibility limited the Department’s support and intervention. He confirmed that Mr Moore was correct in stating that the Department’s main role was to ensure water security, which is why the Minister of Water and Sanitation had been working tirelessly on institutional reform, including the establishment of the National Water Resources Infrastructure Agency (NWRIA).
He explained that this reform was necessary to ensure that a water-scarce country such as South Africa would be in a stronger position beyond 2030. This would support economic prosperity, socio-economic transformation, environmental sustainability, and national security. He added that all the major work undertaken by the Department and the TCTA had been focused on bringing bulk infrastructure projects online. These efforts were designed to ensure that South Africa did not run out of water and to improve the national water mix.
Deputy Minister Mahlobo acknowledged that the most significant problems facing the Department were water supply, provision, and reliability—matters primarily falling under municipal jurisdiction. He stated that some of these concerns had already been identified during the budget process. He cited poor leadership and negligence, particularly regarding financial management in the water sector, as major challenges.
He recalled that during the Water and Sanitation Indaba, stakeholders had agreed that increased investment and new financing options were necessary for the sector's financial sustainability. Traditional financing—primarily grant funding—was available, but there had been a lack of discipline and prudence in its management.
He outlined various funding sources, such as the RBIG and the WSIG, which had helped with infrastructure projects across the provinces. He also mentioned funding streams outside the Department’s control, including the Municipal Infrastructure Grant (50% of which must be ring-fenced for water and sanitation) and the Human Settlements Development Grant (with 30% dedicated to water and sanitation projects outside metropolitan areas and cities).
Deputy Minister Mahlobo stated that funds, including those under the Urban Settlement Development Grant, had been returned to the National Treasury due to underuse. He emphasised that 33% of these funds must be set aside for water and sanitation projects. He also noted the existence of the Informal Urban Settlement Development Grant (IUSDG). He underscored that all these grants were tied to the Department, including money from the equitable share allocated to poor households—R860 per household for free basic water and R560 for sanitation.
He warned that despite these ring-fenced allocations, over R60 billion in grant funding had not been utilised diligently in the previous financial year. He called for money from trading entities to be ring-fenced as well, as ongoing wastefulness hindered the sector’s efficiency.
Deputy Minister Mahlobo highlighted infrastructure collapse in Johannesburg and elsewhere, attributing this to geological issues, outdated technologies, illegal connections, and faulty meters. He cautioned that if municipalities failed to use available funding prudently, challenges would remain unaddressed. He also noted that funding for water infrastructure had never been sufficient.
To address this, the Department had adopted blended financing strategies—combining grants with market-based funding mechanisms in collaboration with Infrastructure South Africa and the Water Partnerships Office. He referred to projects such as the Ebenezer Water Scheme in Polokwane as examples, emphasising the importance of regular meetings by the Budget Facility for Infrastructure (BFI), guided by comprehensive and bankable plans.
The Deputy Minister added that private sector funding was being mobilised through the TCTA, which the government established to attract market-based finance for socio-economically viable projects. This funding model relied on the guarantee of future water payments from consumers.
He acknowledged that many rural areas and villages still lacked access to water. The Department was working with the mining sector to address these challenges, citing the Olifants Management Model in Limpopo as an example. He noted that both financial and leadership issues were to blame for the lack of progress in these communities.
On the issue of sewage spills and pollution, Deputy Minister Mahlobo invited Deputy Minister Seitlholo to contribute. He stated that further announcements on support measures would be made during the upcoming Budget Vote and reiterated the call for improved leadership and discipline at the local government level.
Deputy Minister Seitlholo described the situation at wastewater treatment works as dysfunctional and severely polluting water resources, particularly those under the jurisdiction of municipal water services authorities. He supported Deputy Minister Mahlobo’s position that the establishment of special-purpose vehicles, such as in Mfuleni, could help address these systemic issues.
He referred to discussions at the Water and Sanitation Indaba, where the Department had considered the state of South Africa’s raw water quality and possible ways to manage sludge from wastewater treatment works more effectively. He explained that the Department was exploring partnerships with the private sector, particularly in areas where key wastewater treatment works were polluting major rivers like the Vaal and Hartbeespoort.
Deputy Minister Seitlholo confirmed ongoing discussions with partners to explore innovative approaches to tackling upstream pollution in Gauteng, particularly where it affects downstream resources, such as the Hartbeespoort River. He said these efforts would require coordination with municipalities, even though the Department was sympathetic to their difficulties.
He emphasised the importance of identifying and addressing the sources of pollution and reiterated that collaboration with other national departments, including Cooperative Governance and Traditional Affairs (CoGTA), was essential. He expressed hope that the outcomes of the Water and Sanitation Indaba would ensure the effectiveness of water services authorities and that the Department could expedite the use of special-purpose vehicles to resolve key challenges.
He concluded by expressing optimism that the Water Research Commission could be leveraged to drive innovation and address the infrastructure collapse contributing to Gauteng’s pollution crisis.
Deputy Minister Mahlobo then invited Dr Mathye to provide details on how many projects under the RBIG and Water Services Infrastructure Grant (WSIG) were currently being supported by the Department during the current year and the medium-term expenditure framework. He stressed that the Department’s ability to intervene was limited and that municipalities ultimately remained responsible for service delivery.
Dr Mathye acknowledged that the Department’s presentation had clarified the number and scope of projects funded through the RBIG and WSIG, which were multi-year in nature. On average, he reported, the Department managed approximately 500 projects across both grants, all of which were tied to the budget allocations mentioned earlier by the CFO.
He stressed that merely utilising the allocated budget was insufficient to meet the Department’s infrastructure needs. He suggested to Deputy Minister Mahlobo that the Committee should be aware of insights shared during the Department’s engagement with the Water Task Team, which the Deputy President led.
Dr Mathye reported that, according to the Department’s analysis, an estimated R400 billion would be required solely to address the backlog in infrastructure upgrades and replacements for water and wastewater systems. He confirmed that the current budget allocation would not be enough to reverse the situation in the short term.
He affirmed that Deputy Minister Mahlobo’s earlier remarks were critical to understanding the broader context, and that turning the situation around would require a joint, multi-stakeholder effort. He added that this was why targets had been set in advance, enabling the Department to align its skills and resources within the limitations of the current fiscal framework.
The Chairperson told the Deputy Ministers that the Portfolio Committee wished to see a clear prioritisation of the most critical problems. Specifically, the Committee wanted to receive a timeframe indicating when the Department would intervene in ten municipalities over the next 24 months. The Chairperson explained that, even if it required removing infrastructure from the municipalities and transferring it to a water board or another institution, this action must be considered to resolve entrenched issues.
The Chairperson reiterated that the Portfolio Committee was seeking decisive action because the financial figures presented, such as R400 billion across 106 municipalities, were unmanageable unless approached incrementally. He remarked that if the Committee did not “eat the elephant piece by piece,” meaningful progress would remain elusive. He noted that this concern also underpinned the line of questioning from Mr Moore, who sought clarity on where to begin and how to monitor tangible results.
The Chairperson concluded by expressing dissatisfaction with the current status quo, noting that although plans existed, implementation remained absent.
Mr Moore thanked the Chairperson and agreed with his concluding remarks. He appreciated the contributions of the Deputy Ministers and acknowledged the Department’s position in light of systemic challenges, including failures attributed to the COGTA and other institutions.
However, Mr Moore pointed out that despite these broader constraints, the Portfolio Committee was currently reviewing the budget of the Department of Water and Sanitation, not those of Cogta, Human Settlements, or others. He stated that the key question was whether the Department was deploying its budget in a manner that responded swiftly and effectively to urgent national issues. In his view, this was not happening, particularly because the Department had not identified any direct deliverables.
He commented that it would be commendable if the Department could report that it was actively intervening in the ten worst-affected municipalities and could provide progress updates to the Committee. This, he noted, would be in line with the Department’s Constitutional responsibility to address issues such as wastewater management.
Mr Moore concluded by stating that he remained unsatisfied with the responses received thus far. He criticised the Department for focusing on what other stakeholders ought to do, rather than reflecting on what it could do proactively. He urged the Department to shift from long-term planning to immediate, creative interventions, using the current budget more responsively.
Deputy Minister Mahlobo assured Mr Moore that the Department would share information on all 105 municipalities and the intervention plan applicable to each. He clarified that intervention did not necessarily equate to a financial injection; in some cases, the Department deployed personnel and requested that they develop support plans tailored to specific needs.
He acknowledged that Johannesburg was of particular concern to Mr Moore and confirmed that the Department had an intervention plan for the city. A working group focused on water had also been established for the area.
Deputy Minister Mahlobo stated that the Department welcomed individual requests for further information, whether submitted via the Portfolio Committee, the Chairperson, or Members in their personal capacity. He added that the Director-General and Dr Mathye would, on behalf of the Minister, share detailed information on the 105 municipalities, including their associated challenges and plans, with the Committee.
He explained that although the DWS had undertaken thorough preparatory work, it was not operating in isolation. The Department of Human Settlements (DHS), which also held funding for water-related infrastructure, would contribute to the interventions. Additionally, funds were allocated to the COGTA, and support was available from the Municipal Infrastructure Support Agency (MISA), water boards, and other implementing agents, each deployed according to the needs of the area.
Deputy Minister Mahlobo reiterated that this was a high-level presentation and thus did not engage with each municipality in detail. He welcomed guidance from the Portfolio Committee and indicated that the Department would remain responsive to Members’ concluding remarks.
He reported that the Department had reached the point of active intervention, having decided not to wait for further public complaints. However, he noted that challenges remained. In some areas, resistance had been encountered, including legal action taken against the Department under Section 63 of the Water Services Act. These issues, he said, highlighted the need for legislative reform, which would be addressed in future discussions with the Committee.
Deputy Minister Mahlobo concluded by stating that interventions and allocations would require consultation with Members of Parliament, including deliberations on the distribution of funds to each province and district. He confirmed that of the more than 600 projects under the Department’s purview—including wastewater treatment works, water treatment plants, and bulk infrastructure—the relevant details were available for Committee scrutiny.
Mr N Nxumalo (MKP) raised several questions relating to the Department's APPs. Mr Percy Sechemane, Chief Executive Officer (CEO), TCTA, began by addressing the broader funding landscape. He explained that the TCTA worked with various funders, both local and international. These funders paid close attention to the economic conditions in South Africa, and such factors influenced their willingness to commit funding.
He noted that the Committee was likely aware of ongoing issues that influenced funder confidence, including the current state of international relations between South Africa and the United States. Funders were cautious and primarily interested in protecting their investments. He added that, within the water sector, long-term funding—preferably for twenty years or more—was considered ideal. This allowed the TCTA to plan better and secure the necessary skills and resources.
However, given the current economic conditions, many funders had become more conservative, preferring shorter-term commitments of between five and seven years. He explained that some funders expressed willingness to provide funding but hesitated to make long-term commitments due to the uncertainty of working with a public entity. Instead, they opted for shorter durations, despite the TCTA’s preference for twenty-year funding.
Mr Sechemane explained that while the TCTA could secure funds in the short term, the Domestic Medium-Term Note (DMTN) Programme, listed on the JSE, was intended to provide the TCTA with quicker access to capital when required. He offered an example: the organisation might secure a seven-year loan with the potential to switch or refinance it before the term expired.
He reported that the TCTA continued to search for longer-term funding instruments, as these were more effective in keeping water tariffs affordable. The DMTN Programme enabled the organisation to “swap” or restructure debt where necessary. He stressed the need for predictability to prevent water costs from escalating due to abrupt repayment demands, for instance, if a R20 billion loan had to be repaid in just five years.
This approach, he explained, allowed the TCTA to bridge financial gaps and repay loans in the short term while continuing its work. Infrastructure development required long-term planning and time-consuming processes, from project conceptualisation to implementation. He noted that this extended timeline was part of the challenge.

He stressed that those responsible should be treated as having committed financial crimes.
The Chairperson thanked Mr Nxumalo but suggested that his concern about tankers stemmed from having missed the recent Water and Sanitation Indaba. He used a metaphor involving a broken car to explain that the Department of Water and Sanitation could not be expected to resolve tanker issues, as these were the responsibility of local government. He suggested that either CoGTA take action or that national regulations be introduced to require municipalities to procure their own tankers.
Mr Nxumalo clarified that he understood the Department was not responsible for managing water tankers and was aware of the separation of duties. He apologised if it appeared as though he was directing his comments at Deputy Minister Mahlobo. He explained that he was making a broader point about the absence of a coherent plan, given how long the issue had persisted. He concluded by asking whether the Deputy Minister would like to respond.
Deputy Minister Mahlobo responded that the Portfolio Committee needed to "find each other" on the application of the department’s roles and responsibilities. He emphasized that the Department supported local government as required by law, specifically in terms of Section 154 of the Constitution. He highlighted several support mechanisms such as training, team deployment, and implementation support, including invoking Section 63 of the Water Services Act—though this could only be done by the Chairperson when Section 139 was already in effect. He added that the Department was working collaboratively with the Inter-Ministerial Committee on Local Government.
The Deputy Minister acknowledged that many interventions, including those under Section 139, had not delivered the desired results. He stated that the Ministries of Cooperative Governance and Traditional Affairs (CoGTA) and Water and Sanitation were cooperating, particularly in reviewing the white paper. Existing documentation, he said, facilitated more effective intervention and support.
Deputy Minister Mahlobo reminded the Committee that local government constituted a separate sphere of government and was not subordinate to the national sphere. He stressed that municipalities had their own rights and responsibilities, which shaped how the Department engaged with them.
While agreeing that corruption was "an ugly thing," the Deputy Minister stated that the primary issue was leadership. He argued that leaders often failed to take decisive action, even when solutions were available.
He reported that stakeholders had previously agreed under one roof on a minimum program, and now it was time to hold each other accountable for actions and commitments. Responding to the Chairperson, he reaffirmed his earlier comments on water tankers.
Deputy Minister Mahlobo reiterated that tanker supply should only be a temporary measure. He warned that some municipalities were treating tankering as permanent, which was problematic given the risk of water pollution and unreliable delivery. He stressed that citizens deserved a consistent water supply directly from taps.
The Deputy Minister clarified that municipalities could not charge residents for tanker water, as this fell under the category of non-revenue water. In response to questions about planning, he insisted that the issue was not the absence of a plan.
He stated that municipalities were expected to pursue insourcing, citing eThekwini Municipality as an example. The Deputy Minister described the situation in the metro—where tankering had become common—as an embarrassment. He instructed the municipality to insource tanker operations temporarily, until reliable tap water could be restored.
Deputy Minister Mahlobo further recalled that depot managers had lost their lives in eThekwini. He informed the Chairperson that the matter had been referred to the Special Investigating Unit (SIU), as the Department could not allow the killing of staff members to continue unchecked.
Referring to the emergence of a black market in water, the Deputy Minister stated that the Chairperson had joined the Department in addressing this issue. A programme was developed to tackle broader criminality related to water access and theft.
Addressing Ms Khawula, the Deputy Minister described sewage spillages as inhumane. He noted that sewage systems were municipal assets, not owned by the department. He reiterated that these spillages were unacceptable and had to be eradicated.
He referenced the situation in eMfuleni, where water and sewer spillages persisted for decades, flowing through the streets and into people’s homes. He recounted that some children had grown up under such conditions and only realized the abnormality of their environment upon leaving for university.
The Deputy Minister stated that Dr Mathye had previously described a failed intervention involving soldiers. Eventually, the Department brought in a capable groundwater institution to manage the situation, which was largely due to broken pipes and clogged drains. He linked this problem to the findings of the Green Drop report, indicating that although Mfuleni had improved, the issue had not been fully resolved.
He reported that the Department continued to support eMfuleni through a special purpose vehicle designed to provide sustainable assistance.
Deputy Minister Mahlobo also highlighted challenges in Standerton, Mpumalanga, where businesses nearly left due to sewer overflows affecting schools and homes. He said the Department of Human Settlements had failed to reticulate certain areas. The Department eventually fixed the issue, which led to the municipality being placed under the Minister of Finance’s administration by court order.
He reiterated the Department's aversion to sewage in the streets and emphasised the importance of local government functionality. He reminded the Portfolio Committee that while the Department offered support, its ability to intervene was limited by budget and resource constraints.
Mr Nxumalo expressed dissatisfaction with the Department's response, particularly around its method of accountability. He questioned whether the Department merely issued warnings to mayors and municipal managers, despite acknowledging the unacceptable state of sewage spillages.
In response, the Deputy Minister stated that the Department had taken multiple municipalities to court. He cited cases in his home province of Mpumalanga, where municipalities such as Emalahleni were currently on trial, facing potential charges of up to R600 million. He added that another municipality had been fined over R300 million, and that the Department had also acted against Victor Khanye Local Municipality. He emphasised that simply writing to municipalities was often ineffective.
In closing, Deputy Minister Mahlobo clarified that while the Department did engage in enforcement and regulation, it also extended support when necessary. He offered to provide Mr Nxumalo with a list of municipalities that had received notices, directives, or had been taken to court. He committed to sharing this list with the Chairperson and the Portfolio Committee upon request, affirming that the Department was not idle.
Chairperson's closing remarks
The Chairperson accepted the Deputy Minister’s offer to supply the requested information. He remarked that the meeting had run almost two hours longer than scheduled and expressed gratitude to Members and attendees.
The Chairperson noted that two questions remained unanswered in the Zoom chat and asked that responses be provided in writing to the respective Committee members.
He concluded by thanking attendees and reminded them of the next in-person meeting scheduled for 6 May 2025 in Cape Town. He then adjourned the meeting.
The meeting was adjourned.
