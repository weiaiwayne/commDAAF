Title: Committee Legacy Report
Date: 27 Mar 2024
Committee:

Meeting Summary
Video
The Committee considered and adopted the Legacy Report and the majority of its recommendations were adopted. However, Members disagreed with the recommendation that the Committee be split into a Portfolio and Standing Committee on Finance in the Seventh Parliament. Members proposed alternative solutions on how to manage the large workload of the committee such as having a subcommittee to deal with certain responsibilities. The recommendation the Committee agreed to after discussion was that this matter be looked at in the Seventh Parliament.
Members from each political party thanked the Chairperson for his impartiality in chairing the meetings in the Sixth Parliament. They wished each other well for the upcoming elections. The Chairperson thanked Members for their co-operation in not disrupting meetings and treating each other with respect. The Committee support staff were appreciated especially as Parliament transitioned to virtual meetings in 2020.
Meeting report
The Chairperson noted this would be the last Committee meeting of the Sixth Term unless there were extraordinary circumstances which required the Committee to meet. After the National Assembly plenary session, Members would be campaigning for the upcoming elections.
Committee Legacy Report: briefing
Dr Zakhele Hlophe, Committee Content Advisor, asked how proceed with the report, if they should go through the entire report or skip straight to the recommendations.
The Chairperson took it that Members had read the report beforehand and thanked them for doing so. This was a busy day for Members and suggested that they skip to the issues.
Dr Hlophe stated that if Members had read the lengthy report, he would skip straight through to the recommendations, otherwise he would browse through the contents of the report.
The Chairperson stated that the protocol followed when they processed reports was based on the assumption that upon receipt, Members had read through the contents of the report. They would zoom into the last section which dealt with the recommendations and observations. He asked Dr Hlophe to proceed straight to the recommendations and if any issues arose this would be dealt with. The meeting would specifically focus on the recommendations so that their time would be productive.
Mr Hlophe then took Member through the recommendations as outlined in Section 7 of the Report.
Mr Hlophe said that the Legacy Report did not cover all the issues and he advised that the report be supplemented by the detailed observations and recommendations found in the Fiscal Framework reports and Budget Review and Recommendations Reports of the Committee between July 2019 and March 2024. The Legacy Report served as a guideline. Dr Hlophe made comments on some of the recommendations:
- On recommendation 7.2, there was a recurring concern also raised in the previous Committee Legacy Report about the Committee’s dual mandate. According to the Money Bills Amendment and Related Matters Act in section 4(4)(1) the Committee is required to deal with the Fiscal Framework and fulfil the responsibilities of a Portfolio Committee. This Committee was the only Committee in Parliament which was also a Standing Committee, so this brought challenges. The Money Bills Act authorised the Standing Committee to deal with the Fiscal Framework, the MTBPS and the Tax Bills. In dealing with both fiscal and the portfolio responsibilities, this resulted in the Committee not overseeing all the entities that they were required to do, because they were so busy with the Fiscal Framework. Members needed to consider what to recommend to prevent this in future.
- On recommendation 7.4, Dr Hlophe asked why the adoption of minutes been added to the meeting agenda.
- On recommendation 7.5, Committee members were not invited to the relevant conferences and workshops mentioned. Often reflections were made on their behalf, it did not feel as though they were allowed to speak for themselves; they were labelled as ‘inexperienced’ compared to Treasury, so recommendation 7.5 was deemed important.
- On recommendation 7.6, before a Private Member Bill is given to the committee, the socio-economic impact study should have already been conducted. Parliament should maintain a proactive approach.
Dr D George (DA) asked at what point the Committee could have a discussion on the items. The recommendation to split the Committee was significant. He served on the Portfolio Committee before it became a Standing Committee where they handled all the current responsibilities and were very busy and might not have covered everything. The way they were proceeding was almost as if there was an agreement that the Committee should split, but to do this would require a lot of discussion and consideration. He did not think they could take it as a given that they were agreeing to the split.
The Chairperson replied that Dr Hlophe was summarising the recommendations and then Members would need to go through each recommendations individually, take a position and then finally adopt the report. He requested Dr Hlophe to ‘gallop’ as he went through the recommendations.
Dr Hlophe then summarised the remaining recommendations of the Standing Committee.
- On recommendation 7.8, he noted how Bills were amended that Members had never seen in their original form despite the amendment of these Bills each year. He emphasized the importance of consultation as there have been consistent complaints about insufficient time allocated to dealing with the Fiscal Framework.
- On the Portfolio Committee recommendations, Dr Hlophe noted how there were only three oversight visits so they should consider increasing the frequency of these visits.
- Engagement with AGSA was emphasized.
- Public hearings were conducted only when processing the Fiscal Framework but when the budget framework and BRRR were processed, stakeholders were not invited to make input.
Deliberations
Recommendation 7.1 was agreed to by the Committee.
On Recommendation 7.2 Dr George stated that as he had previously mentioned, he served on the Portfolio Committee before it became the Standing Committee and that Committee was very busy as they did all the work that they do now. The Money Bills Amendment and Related Matters Act enabled Parliament to amend the budget, a capability that they were previously not allowed to do. This amendment would imply that they would be tasked with more work as previously they were just required to process the budget. The law was amended to facilitate this, but amending the budget has not been done yet.
He had consulted with his party deputy chief on the matter and it was true that the workload was high and this needed consideration but he was unsure if he supported the recommendation that the Committee should be split. He was mindful of the fact that something needed to be done and they needed to consider this. At this point, he could not support the split as perhaps there was something else that could be done. The Committee has never discussed this before. This was the first time this was being raised now in the report.
Mr G Skosana (ANC) said it was true that the Committee was overloaded to the extent that some of their responsibilities were neglected, such as oversight. He reinforced the point that there were only three oversight visits in the Sixth Term.
To split the Committee would result in serious confusion to the public. There could be instances where people intended to write to the Portfolio Committee but wrote to the Standing Committee instead and vice versa due to misunderstanding which Committee dealt with what.
The establishment of two Finance Committees would probably result in confusion both for Parliament and the public. A better solution would be to establish a subcommittee which would focus on overseeing National Treasury. This way the Committee would be able to focus on micro economic policy, bills etc. This was ‘doable’ as the Portfolio Committee of Justice and Correctional Services had established a subcommittee that dealt with Correctional Services, as justice matters had become the main focus. This would be a better solution as the split would cause too much confusion.
Ms N Abraham (ANC) agreed with the comments made by previous Members, including Dr George’s suggestion. Instead of proposing two Committees, they should have an investigation to determine how the Committee would function best. She did not believe that some of the things that were not done, were because of a lack of time.
Her preferred approach was that the Seventh Parliament commenced with a strategic workshop to familiarize Members of the Committee’s objectives, past accomplishments and future plans. This would provide insight into the intended goals that were not completed within the intended timeframe and this would inform Parliament of the Committee’s shortcomings. She was not convinced that time was the issue, as there was time for tasks to be completed but they remained unfinished.
Recommendation 7.2 was not acceded to by the Committee.
The Chairperson said they should bear in mind that this was not the only finance committee, in terms of the rules of Parliament there were almost three Committees. What they were recommending instead was that this matter stand over and be looked at in the Seventh Parliament.
Ms Abraham then chaired the meeting as Mr Maswanganyi had another matter in Parliament to attend to.
Recommendations 7.3 and 7.4 were agreed to.
Mr Skosana agreed on the importance of prioritizing the adoption of meeting minutes while they were still fresh to ensure the accurate recollection of meeting proceedings. However, he expressed uncertainty on the process for addressing standing items in the minutes. He questioned the practicality of addressing Wednesday meeting minutes at the following Tuesday meeting. He proposed that Tuesday meeting minutes focus on the adoption of the minutes from the previous Tuesday meeting, while the Wednesday meetings could handle the minutes from the prior Wednesday. He was unsure if the previous meeting’s minutes would be ready by the next meeting.
Dr Hlophe stated that typically two meetings were held each week on Tuesdays and Wednesdays and the minutes from the previous week meetings were then adopted. But this practice of adoption of minutes has not been done for the past two years. He constantly reminded secretaries for this to be done and for the minutes to be ready within three days of the meeting. He proposed that this be a standing item in every meeting agenda.
Mr Skosana agreed.
Recommendations 7.5 and 7.6 were agreed to.
On recommendation 7.6 Dr Hlophe highlighted the specialized nature of socio-economic studies, emphasizing the need for expertise in this area. He mentioned sending a request to the Parliamentary Budget Office on the SARS Bill, where they did not receive a response. This was one of the Bills that was not finalised due to lack of capacity within Parliament to conduct socio-economic impact studies.
The Chairperson proposed that this recommendation apply to Parliament broadly.
Dr George agreed with the suggestion to bring the matter before Parliament broadly, but noted that Members were to bear in mind that the Parliamentary Budget Office was established for a purpose. According to the Money Bills Amendment and Related Matters Act, when the PBO was written into law this was for the purpose that it offer advice and in the case of Private Member’s Bills, it provide support which in his opinion included social impact assessments.
The Reserve Bank Bill was not processed and the reason was because the social impact assessment was never completed, which he found problematic and surprising. The recommendation should be framed broadly in a way that did not lose sight of PBO which he believed was not being effectively utilized.
The Chairperson then included the phrase ‘effective utilization of the Parliamentary Budget Office’.
Recommendations 7.7 to 7.12 were agreed to.
On Recommendation 7.13 the Chairperson asked about handling greylisting matters and suggested that they comment on how they collaborated with other committees and in discussing their interactions with other committees, also ensured timely alignment with expectations. Was this relevant to the recommendation?
Dr Hlophe said it was relevant, and should be added.
The Chairperson said incoming Members should be aware of their functions in the different committees.
Recommendations 7.14 to 7.21 were agreed to.
The Chairperson asked if Itala Bank was referred to?
Dr Hlophe replied that the main report explained this in detail.
Recommendations 7.22 to 7.27 were agreed to.
Committee Legacy Report: adoption
The Committee adopted the report with the amendments.
Closing comments
The Chairperson thanked all Members and wished them the best as they ‘go to war’ on the 29 May elections.
Dr George extended his thanks to the Chair for the fair and consistent manner in which he chaired the Standing Committee. He felt that he always had an opportunity to say what needed to be said. While Members had not always seen eye to eye, there has been a shared desire for South Africa’s success, for alleviation of poverty and for employment opportunities and to serve the people who have sent them to Parliament. Members in this Committee have attempted to do so to the best of their ability. There was still a lot that needed to be done but all Members remained committed to the tasks at hand. On behalf of the DA he wished everyone well for the upcoming elections and that it would be safe and everyone would return focused to serve the interests of the country’s people.
It was noted that Mr Manyi was not in the meeting and hence no contribution by the EFF.
Ms Abraham on behalf of the ANC confirmed that the Chairperson has been such a balanced chair. He was objective in the meetings which was really appreciated by the Members. She thanked him for his wisdom and even when they faced the worst, he kept his cool. When Members were out of order, he corrected them without making them feel small or awkward. She thanked Committee Members for the sterling work that they have done which was not taken lightly. Everyone was looking for a better life for the people of South Africa, development, growth, and value for the life of the people, which was just done through different angles. The debates were always enriched by the contributions of the Members, so she thanked Members for this. She also thanked the support staff who have always shown their ability to serve because to lead they needed to serve. They had always responded to Members' needs. She thanked the Department in their absence for the work that they have done. This could not be underestimated.
The Chairperson thanked Ms Abraham for holding the fort. He thanked Members for sticking together for the past five years. As much as they were from different political parties, on issues of national interest they were able to work together and be united in their diversity. The Committee had to operate within tight schedules and pressures, particularly when processing the General Laws Amendment Bill yet they remained united in their diversity. The robust debates were both constructive and enjoyable. He could not recall any disruptions during meetings over the past five years, emphasizing that no one’s views were suppressed either. He thanked Members for the mutual respect demonstrated amongst themselves.
It was an honour and a privilege to serve as the Chairperson. He had to do things in a manner that demonstrated impartiality, objectivity and not conduct affairs in a manner that disadvantaged those who did not come from his political party. He thanked Members for their co-operation. He acknowledged his mistakes and thanked Members for tolerating him.
The Chairperson thanked the support staff for their assistance and support of Members as politicians and not being partisan. When they came to the Committee, they were professional. With the transition to virtual meetings in 2020 this was not easy and was new. They were used to receiving hard copies and then everything had to be done through artificial intelligence. He thanked the staff for their assistance. If had powers, he would recommend these staff get bonuses.
He thanked the legal team, Adv Jenkins and van der Merwe, Communications team, the Treasury entities who had been helpful. He also thanked the stakeholders who made their lives easy by bringing knowledge on many issues because participatory democracy was the cornerstone of democracy. He thanked the Minister and all the National Treasury officials. He wished Members the best for the elections campaign, to campaign fiscally and consider national interests and do things in a manner that they were able to build the confidence of the international community. Members would meet later that afternoon in the National Assembly and out in the field campaigning.
The meeting was adjourned.
