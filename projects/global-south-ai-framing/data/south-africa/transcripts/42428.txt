Title: Technology Innovation Agency 2.0 strategy, audit action plans and related matters; with Minister
Date: 24 Feb 2026
Committee: 
URL: https://pmg.org.za/committee-meeting/42428/
============================================================

Meeting Summary
Video
The Committee received a presentation from the Technology Innovation Agency (TIA) on its TIA 2.0 strategy, on measures taken to stabilise its executive leadership, the appointment of a new Chief Executive Officer, and progress made in addressing audit findings for the 2023/24 and 2024/25 financial years.
The Committee questions spanned matters on governance, transformation, legislative alignment, digital readiness, risk management, operational capacity, private‑sector mobilisation, and the viability of the TIA’s proposed ecosystem orchestration model under the TIA 2.0 strategy.
The TIA, the DSTI, and the Minister jointly acknowledged historical governance deficiencies but expressed confidence that the TIA 2.0 strategy turnaround programme, once fully implemented, would reposition the TIA as South Africa's premier innovation catalyst.
The Committee resolved that additional questions would be submitted in writing.
Meeting report
Acting Chairperson elected
Ms Shanaaz Isaacs, Committee Secretary, opened the session, welcomed participants and called for nominations for an Acting Chairperson in the absence of Mr T Shiviti (ANC) who resigned.
The first nomination by Mr T Ramongalo (DA) for a Member who declined was withdrawn.
Mr V Nkosi (ANC) nominated Ms T Mchunu (ANC).
Ms Mchunu accepted and the nomination was seconded by Ms L Sapo (ANC).
Ms Mchunu took over to serve as the Acting Chairperson for the meeting.
Minister's remarks
Prof Blade Nzimande, Minister of Science, Technology and Innovation, welcomed and thanked the Acting Chairperson for inviting him to make some opening remarks before the main presentation by the Technology Innovation Agency (TIA). He went on to recognise the presence of his colleagues from the Department of Science, Technology and Innovation (DSTI), Dr Nomalungelo Gina, Deputy Minister of Science, Technology and Innovation, and Dr Mlungisi Cele, Director-General, DSTI, who would engage with the Committee on arising matters.
The Minister commenced by locating the work of TIA as contextualised within “seismic shifts” in the global technology landscape and the intentions for South Africa to move from a linear model of innovation to an agile, technology‑driven economic development. He highlighted strategic technology domains central to this cause as artificial intelligence and machine learning, next‑generation computing and connectivity, biotechnology and health, energy and materials, and robotics and autonomous systems inter alia.
The Minister stressed that these domains drove technological sovereignty and digital self‑determination for South Africa. The stance for technological sovereignty and digital self-determination, he said, was demonstrated by America, which, through the presidents of Joe Biden and Donald Trump, had blocked the Chinese government from accessing Google and Nvidia microchips, respectively, to maintain digital sovereignty. He was reminding the Committee, as such, of the importance of technological sovereignty and digital self-determination, even for a relatively small country such as South Africa, to avoid being extensions of other countries.
He added that there was a growing focus towards innovation, with countries like China pioneering the movement. China was reported to be in the pipeline, as one of the first countries that would award PhDs for innovation. While the Minister said this was still to be verified and debatable in terms of its veracity, he made his point as that of a focus by China to steer differently from the traditional thesis-based PhDs to promote innovation within their sectors. This initiative was in line with one of the key pillars of the country’s decadal plans (2022-2032) for science, technology, innovation, and the need to enable innovation. Emulating President Xi Jinping of China, he stated that science and technology should thus be at the centre of production, hence enabling economic growth and production. He was proud that South Africa already had the foundational capabilities to employ these strategies towards the nation’s economic development.
The Minister recalled that the TIA had been established to bridge the innovation chasm between research and commercialisation, and stated that the TIA 2.0 strategy was a turnaround designed to restore the TIA’s catalytic role in the National System of Innovation and to advance innovation‑led industrialisation.
Part of the TIA’s mandate was to work around closing the gap between research output and commercial uptake, by providing risk funding from proof‑of‑concept to pre‑commercialisation, enhancing local capabilities, reducing dependency on imported know‑how, and delivering technologies that improve quality of life. While acknowledging governance and performance difficulties in recent years, he described the TIA as a catalytic actor and introduced “TIA 2.0” as a long‑term turnaround and repositioning effort intended to make the TIA the “oxygen” of South Africa’s innovation ecosystem.
He closed by requesting the TIA to give a concise presentation to leave ample time for Member engagement.
In reiteration, Dr Cele, who said the Minister had already covered the essentials in his opening remarks, advised the TIA likewise, to condense its presentation to the essentials, inviting the TIA Board and management to proceed.
Presentation by the Technology Innovation Agency
Mr Loyiso Tyira, Chairperson: Board, TIA opened by explaining that the TIA 2.0 strategy was a ten‑year transformation intended to reposition the TIA from a reactive, transactional funder to a proactive curator of innovation pipelines. It was to be implemented in three phases, namely, consolidate, grow and scale. The phases were anchored in a quadruple helix approach that drew together government, industry, academia and civil society. He emphasised that the strategy responded to fragmentation and duplication identified in external reviews, that partnership‑building (including with Sector Education and Training Authorities (SETAs) and the Department of Small Business Development) was central, and that a fund of funds would be used to crowd‑in private capital for early‑stage and scale‑up investment.
He also noted that the Board had strengthened oversight of investment exits to protect value following lessons from legacy transactions. In terms of that funding, he disclosed a major development in a legacy investment dispute, following litigation, that approximately R1.1 billion had been awarded and deposited with the TIA’s attorneys, subject to confidentiality undertakings and the finalisation of legal cost issues. The Board was working with the DSTI to ensure an appropriate process for reinvesting these funds into programmes, while also tightening internal capabilities for exit evaluation so that the TIA captured fair value when it leaves investments.
Mr Ismail Abdoola, Acting Chief Executive Officer (CEO), TIA, set out the execution architecture of the TIA 2.0 strategy. He outlined three implementation pathways. The first, “innovation curator”, focused on creating pipelines and improving commercialisation outcomes through technical guidance, milestone‑based funding, market access partnerships and venture development. The second, “ecosystem orchestrator”, concentrated on coordination, partnerships, policy influence and resource mobilisation to reduce duplication and strengthen throughput. The third, “capable and sustainable TIA”, invested in leadership, skills development, digital transformation, culture, reputation and financial sustainability to underpin delivery. Accompanying these shifts, the business model centred on three pillars: direct early‑stage funding; a fund‑of‑funds approach to crowd‑in private capital (with examples of recent matching in a seed vehicle); and ecosystem curation aligned to national priorities. The TIA would measure success by shifting from activity counts to outcomes, i.e., increased innovation throughput, higher commercialisation success, greater private‑capital participation, and stronger coordination across the system.
Under the decadal transformation plan, sequenced in three phases, consolidate, grow, and scale, the TIA was set to move from a transactional funder to a proactive curator and orchestrator of the innovation ecosystem. The TIA 2.0 strategy, in the consolidate phase, prioritised governance strengthening, operating‑model redesign, portfolio processes, and commercialisation enablement, supported by implementing partners to maintain delivery while internal capacity was rebuilt. Under the growth phase, the TIA would expand thematic portfolios, intensify orchestration across the ecosystem, and raise visibility. The scale phase focused on national impact and global competitiveness, with performance shifting from activity counts to impact metrics, notably improved commercialisation rates, increased private‑capital participation in early‑stage investment, and stronger coordination across the National System of Innovation. The fund of funds would further support emerging (including black South Africans and women) fund managers, furthering transformation in the venture‑capital landscape.
Ms Valentine Malevu, Executive: Corporate Services, TIA, reported that the TIA was operating at approximately 75% staffing capacity, owing to attrition and budget constraints, but had recently filled several critical posts, established a Project Management Office (PMO) to manage the transformation, and launched a customer‑relationship management portal to track queries and drive turnaround‑time reductions. Crucially, she confirmed the appointment of a new CEO, Dr Zwanani Mathe, and an experienced energy executive who holds a PhD in Chemical Engineering from the University of Cape Town, effective 1 April 2026. This move was meant to stabilise executive leadership, and outlined a one‑year digital‑maturity assessment to underpin information and communication technology (ICT) modernisation in support of the strategy.
Digitally, the TIA had launched a customer relationship management portal to track queries and turnaround times, appointed a public relations agency to strengthen visibility and trust, and commissioned a third‑party work study and digital‑maturity assessment to inform process re‑engineering and data‑driven management. As a flagship convening, the TIA had announced the inaugural South African Innovation Week for March, aimed at positioning the TIA as a national thought leader and ecosystem convener.
Mr Lwazi Koyana, Chair: Audit and Risk Committee, TIA, recorded that the TIA had sustained a run of unqualified audit outcomes, albeit with findings that management was addressing through tightened consequence‑management timeframes, year‑end procedural controls and supply‑chain oversight. He noted one item relating to an off‑site backup contract with the Council for Scientific and Industrial Research (CSIR) that was still being confirmed as to whether it constituted irregular expenditure.
He reiterated the Audit and Risk Committee’s zero‑tolerance stance on repeat findings. In specific terms, about a fifth (“22%”) of the prior year’s findings remained open; mainly year‑end procedural matters expected to close with the audit cycle, and consequence‑management delays had been corrected. The CSIR might be classified as irregular expenditure. If so, it would be the sole item of that nature in the next audit.
Ms Rekha Govender, Acting Chief Financial Officer (CFO), TIA, presented the audit action plan, stating that a significant proportion of findings across 2023/24 and 2024/25 had been resolved, with the remainder in progress and tracked through revised standard operating procedures (SOPs), targeted training, reinforced record‑keeping, and technical support for GRAP 104 implementation, with preventive measures to avoid recurrences of technical inefficiencies. The Board’s new approach was not only cure based, but preventative.
Concluding the presentation, Mr Abdoola and Mr Patrick Krappie, Executive: Innovation Enabling, TIA, described the orchestration metrics by which progress would be assessed – co‑funding leveraged, the number and depth of formalised partnerships, the strength and conversion of the deal pipeline (spin‑outs, licences, start‑up survivability), and socio‑economic outcomes (revenue, jobs, export orientation) – and indicated a multi‑year ambition of approximately 600 innovations reaching commercialisation over the planning horizon.

Discussion
Dr W Boshoff (FF+) began by asking for an update on Capra (sp) Bio Systems, enquiring whether the TIA had secured any recovery and whether the matter had concluded.
Mr Ramongalo questioned the rationale for the TIA’s repositioning, asking how the TIA would credibly serve as a national curator rather than a transactional funder in a system that already included the CSIR, National Research Foundation (NRF) and universities. He asked for assurances on whether institutional capabilities, finance, programme management, risk, and monitoring and evaluation, were sufficient to sustain the proposed interventionist model. He requested information on the governance reforms envisaged (board composition, committee mandates, performance reporting), the international platforms to be used to position South Africa as an innovation hub, and any regulatory or legislative barriers requiring Parliamentary attention.
Dr S Thembekwayo (EFF) focused on programme‑level risk management, asking what frameworks the TIA had to prevent delays and failures in large or multi‑year innovations, how project termination and recovery of funds would be handled when milestones were not met, the timeline for reducing the vacancy rate (especially at executive level), and how staff complaints, including those concerning executives, would be managed without bias. She asked how many technology‑driven start‑ups the seed fund of funds would support to strengthen the venture ecosystem.
Mr T Mjadu (MKP) raised concerns regarding the TIA’s legal footing to assume a system‑wide orchestration role without amending the Technology Innovation Agency Act 26 of 2008. He queried the proposal for a special purpose vehicle (SPV) with Public Finance Management Act (PFMA) 1 of 1999 exemptions to hold state equity investments, asking for quantified fiscal‑risk exposure, loss‑tolerance thresholds, and oversight mechanisms, and whether National Treasury had approved such an arrangement.
He asked the TIA to commit to time‑bound, quantifiable targets to 2030, including commercialisation rates, job creation, crowd‑in ratios and turnaround‑time reductions, and to specify the consequences should these not be met.
Mr Nkosi cautioned that frontier technologies risked excluding smaller actors and asked how affordability and accessibility for smallholders would be ensured. He asked for specific strategies for collaborating with the private sector that the TIA would implement. What was the timeline for the digital maturity assessment plans, and how did they maintain alignment with technology-driven innovation?
Responses
The TIA’s leadership reiterated that the Carpa Bio Systems case had been successfully concluded and that the recovered proceeds were in hand pending the completion of legal‑cost and approval formalities. The Board had accordingly strengthened exit‑valuation and related governance to protect public value.
Mr Abdoola described the TIA 2.0 strategy architecture, phasing and resourcing, confirmed the use of milestone‑based contracting with provisions for termination and recovery, and explained the fund of funds model, initially supporting five fund managers with an indicative pipeline of 25 or more start‑ups, expandable as additional private capital is leveraged.
Ms Malevu addressed leadership stabilisation (including the incoming CEO on 1 April), vacancy reduction, culture change and whistle‑blowing arrangements, the PMO, and a one‑year digital‑maturity assessment to guide ICT modernisation.
Mr Koyana explained the audit action plan, the mix of resolved and work‑in‑progress findings, reinforced SOPs and consequence management (including GRAP 104 support), and the intention to eliminate repeat findings through stronger assurance.
Mr Krappie set out orchestration metrics, co‑funding leverages, partnership density, pipeline conversion and socio‑economic outcomes. He reiterated the medium‑term ambition of around 600 commercialised innovations as a measure of improved throughput.
Responding to Dr Boshoff, Mr Tyira confirmed that litigation had been finalised in the TIA’s favour and that the recovered proceeds of approximately R1.1 billion, arising from an original R24 million investment, had been received and were being handled by attorneys pending completion of remaining legal‑cost processes and approvals. He added that the Board had strengthened exit‑evaluation controls to prevent value leakage in future disposals.
Mr Abdoola responded to Mr Ramongalo that repeated system reviews had identified fragmentation and duplication, hence the shift to a curated, programme‑oriented model with stronger commercialisation enablement, a redesigned operating model and implementing‑partner delivery while rebuilding internal capacity.
He added that performance would be benchmarked against outcome‑level indicators in the annual performance plan (APP) and Strategic Plan. He explained that the TIA 2.0 strategy had emerged from persistent structural challenges, such as fragmentation of funding instruments across the ecosystem, thin early‑stage capital, particularly in high‑risk innovation funding, and weak commercial translation of research outputs. The TIA’s shift toward curation and ecosystem orchestration, he stressed, was not duplication of other agencies’ mandates but rather an effort to integrate the ecosystem and unlock efficiencies through coordinated programmes, partnerships, and pipeline‑based interventions.
Mr Tyira reinforced this position, noting that consultations across the innovation system had revealed widespread support for the TIA taking up a more integrative leadership role, provided it remained collaborative rather than competitive.
Dr Cele, in his concluding remarks, explained that the DSTI was initiating a process to develop overarching national innovation legislation, which would clarify institutional architecture, address grey areas of overlapping mandates, and ensure systemic coherence. This legislation would formally map out the roles of the TIA, CSIR, NRF, and other actors along the innovation value chain.
On concerns about whether the TIA had the institutional capacity to implement such an ambitious shift, Mr Abdoola acknowledged that capability constraints were real, but outlined several measures being implemented. These measures included: prioritised recruitment to fill strategic vacancies; greater use of implementing partners (such as universities, Small Enterprise Finance Agency [SEFA], and other sector bodies) to decentralise operational load; governance and process modernisation, including updated SOPs, decision‑making frameworks, and portfolio‑management tools.
Ms Malevu added detail on internal readiness by stating that: staffing levels sat at approximately 75%; multiple acting roles were being formalised; a PMO had been established to manage strategic‑plan execution; and a full culture‑transformation programme had begun, addressing staff morale and performance expectations.
On workplace climate, a question raised by Dr Thembekwayo, Mr Tyira confirmed that staff complaints, particularly those submitted through whistle‑blowing channels, were receiving confidential handling under HR/RemCo oversight. He also announced an all‑staff engagement for 27 February, where these issues would be openly addressed. He expressed the Board’s intent to cultivate a modern, ethical, high‑performance culture across the TIA. He asked the other Board members to clarify on other issues.
Mr Abdoola pointed to the draft strategic plan, which included a pipeline target of approximately 600 commercialised innovations over five years, compared to a baseline of 216. He described the shift from activity‑based metrics (such as number of projects funded) to impact‑based metrics, including: number of structured partnerships; investment leveraged through fund‑of‑funds and catalytic programmes; number of thought leadership outputs; university spin‑outs and start‑up performance indicators; and measurable ecosystem collaborations.
This issue was further explained by the Minister who added that the APPs, which would be tabled before the Committee in the normal cycle, would contain precise, auditable indicators, enabling the Committee to interrogate progress rigorously.
Responding to Mr Mjadu, Mr Abdoola clarified that matters concerning the SPV concept were not an immediate step, but rather an outer phase initiative within the scale phase of the TIA 2.0 strategy. Because the TIA was a PFMA 3A entity, retaining surpluses or holding equity would require: standing PFMA exemptions; National Treasury engagements; and a formal governance, oversight and risk‑management framework co‑developed with the DSTI. The Director General would confirm that these structures would be evaluated within the broader legislative reform underway.
On recovery of funds where obligations are not met, Mr Abdoola explained that the TIA used milestone‑based funding, in compliance with the PFMA. If a beneficiary breached contractual obligations; funds were suspended; contractual remedies were activated; and where possible, asset‑based or liquidation‑based recovery was pursued. He cited the example of AgriProtein, where the TIA had successfully recovered value through liquidation processes after the project failed.
He explained the TIA’s position on the question of digital maturity and ICT modernisation posed by Mr Nkosi. The TIA had launched a year‑long assessment and re‑engineering programme focusing on: business‑process redesign; digital‑maturity assessment; data‑architecture improvements; portfolio‑visibility systems; and integration of the new customer relationship management (CRM) portal for tracking query throughput and client satisfaction. This programme was intended to underpin the intelligence‑led, system‑thinking strategy of the TIA 2.0 strategy.
Mr Krappie added that the TIA was scaling and focusing on industry‑led centres of competence, strategic innovation programmes that united companies, universities, and government, incentives for research industry partnerships, and models that encouraged private‑sector research and development (R&D). On the private‑sector (demand‑side) dimension, he highlighted the Small Business Research & Innovation Programme (SBRIP) built on international best practice. This programme enabled government departments and municipalities to issue problem‑driven innovation calls and procure pre‑commercial solutions. He reported that 14 projects had recently been awarded under this mechanism, reflecting strong early uptake. He emphasised on incentivising private sector, R&D institutions, and government through funding as one of the main strategies of encouraging cross sectoral collaborations. These sectors had to be in the forefront of research and solution finding missions as they were the ones who better understood their challenges.
Dr Cele stated that the TIA mandate was broad enough to accommodate the TIA 2.0 approach, while targeted regulatory refinements could be introduced if necessary. Based on four points, one of which was the broadness of the TIA’s mandate, he reported that the DSTI was drafting an overarching legislation to codify the governance architecture of the National System of Innovation and to clarify institutional roles, thereby preventing mandate overlaps. Thirdly, he encouraged the broader collaboration, coordination, alignment and coherence among institutions of concern. Finally, he stated that the DSTI continued to engage the TIA on its corporate strategy 2.0, to ensure clarity of the elements by all role players.
He further confirmed that significant recoveries (including Carpa Bio Systems) were subject to ministerial and National Treasury approvals, in accordance with the PFMA.
The Minister closed the discussion by reaffirming the policy direction towards innovation‑led industrialisation, stating that the TIA’s repositioning was intended to close the innovation chasm, and indicating that APPs would codify the time‑bound, quantifiable targets against which the Committee may hold the TIA to account. He referred Members to two reports he had commissioned, explaining that they had done two reports because the first was negatively leading to one outcome, which was to dissolve the TIA. The follow up report was righting those wrongs and ensuring that the TIA served its purpose.
Co
mmittee Business
Following the departure of the Minister, the DSTI and the TIA, the Committee turned to its internal business.
Members converted the meetings scheduled for 10 March and 24 March to virtual format and discussed complications regarding a study‑tour approval in the wake of the former Chairperson’s resignation.
The Committee mandated the Acting Chairperson and the Committee Secretariat to engage the House Chairperson urgently to resolve the administrative impasse and requested that Members submit any further questions in writing for referral to the TIA and the DSTI. Other matters were concerning parliamentary administrative procedures.
The meeting was adjourned.
