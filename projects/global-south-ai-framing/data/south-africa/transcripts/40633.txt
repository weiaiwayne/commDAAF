Meeting Summary
Video
The Portfolio Committee on Agriculture received an update from the Minister and Department of Agriculture (DoA) on the status of Foot-and-Mouth Disease (FMD) in South Africa and the interventions measures to control its spread. It reported on the outbreaks from 2021 to 2025, current outbreaks per province, outbreak control strategies and new proposed interventions as well as emerging challenges. The Minister emphasised that promulgating the Animal Health Act of 2002 as well as the new interventions proposed will greatly help the Department in curbing the spread of FMD.
Key issues raised by Committee members:
- Would South Africa ever regain its FMD-free status.
- Shortage of veterinary services and resources to employ enough veterinary officers to monitor all livestock auctions in a bid to fight FMD outbreaks.
- Insufficient penalties for those who contravene FMD regulations. - Declaring the FMD outbreak as a national disaster.
- Multi-Sectoral Cooperation: The importance of cooperation from the whole of government such as provincial departments and especially SAPS in enforcement of regulations.
- The efficacy of the vaccine for SAT1, SAT2 & SAT3 stereotypes of the virus which are currently in circulation in South Africa.
- Lack of fencing to control animal movement.
- South Africa cannot keep buying the vaccine internationally; ARC needs to double up capacity.
- Vaccine production: challenges and progress in establishing an FMD vaccine facility.
Meeting report

Minister of Agriculture, Mr John Steenhuisen, said FMD is a very serious concern for South Africa which is why they as the Department have initiated a variety of measures to try and get on top of it. However, it is going to require a whole-of-government approach to resolve the matter and therefore cooperation from provinces is required, including provincial traffic departments and the South African Police Service (SAPS). It is essential that South Africa gets on top of FMD because it is the single biggest loss of opportunity for us in the agriculture space because it blocks access to particularly lucrative markets like the European Union.
Mr Serage will lead the presentation and give updates on the latest outbreaks and outline the initiatives to get on top of FMD. The words that Mr Serage will use will be “we can’t do business as usual, we have to do something different” and he will outline what those are. However, we do require this to be a whole of society and whole-of-government approach to get on top of dealing with FMD.
It is obviously very difficult because we have some auctioneers and some speculators who simply refuse to play by the rules and thus have put the industry at significant risk. We therefore need to take a far stronger line against them and to that effect investigations are ongoing into two auctions that we believe have the potential to have caused some of the problems that we are now having to go and clean up.
Foot-and-mouth disease update & interventions
Mr Dipepeneneng Serage, DoA Deputy Director-General: Agriculture, Biosecurity and Natural Resources Management, and the Chief Veterinary Officer, Dr Bothle Modisane, presented on reported outbreaks from 2021 to 2025, current outbreaks per province, outbreak control strategies as well as FMD emerging challenges.
Dr Modisane noted that before 2019 South Africa had a FMD free zone status without vaccination. South Africa had actually regionalised FMD only to areas very close to the Kruger National Park and a little portion in the north of KwaZulu Natal (KZN) that were regarded as high risk and they were being vaccinated. Then in January 2019 there was an outbreak in Limpopo that resulted in South Africa losing its international FMD-free zone status. In 2021 the outbreak started in KZN province and this outbreak is still continuing, then in 2022 there were multi-province outbreaks in Limpopo, North West, Free State, Gauteng and Mpumalanga. The last of these cases was reported in December 2022 and these provinces were able to successfully control and resolve the outbreaks with the help of the World Organisation for Animal Health (WOAH) in October 2024. The 2024 outbreak in Eastern Cape involving mainly the dairy industry was controlled in 2024 and the province is still in the process of closing the outbreaks. During 2024 - 2025 there were new outbreaks in KZN which spread to Mpumalanga and Gauteng in April 2025 due to the auction in Utrecht.
Control Zones
The eastern side of Limpopo and the eastern side of Mpumalanga is the highly infected zone (red zone). Buffalos are the main culprits in carrying the disease and harbouring it. Buffalos are mainly found in the Kruger National Park which is why the disease then moves from the National Park to the protection zone or the high suburban zone (yellow zone) where we do not expect to see disease, but measures are taken to try to a large extent to control the spread of the disease from going deep into South Africa. The black zone/dots are outbreaks that have happened in the past and were ultimately resolved (closed cases). The triangles are the cases that were reported in the Eastern Cape and have since been controlled.
Current FMD outbreak in KZN
KZN outbreaks have been ‘simmering’ since 2021 but remained contained. In 2025 the infection moved west to more commercial areas; there was a significant spread to several farms via a single auction in Utrecht. When it was noticed that this auction had taken place in February 2025 and that there was disease, DoA started doing trace forwards for the animals sold from that auction.
Current FMD outbreak in Mpumalanga
From the trace forwards the outbreak was found to be contained in one farm in Mpumalanga and there is no indication of a spread to other farms in the area. There are also no clinical signs but virus circulation is confirmed on the farm.
Current FMD outbreak in Gauteng
Just as the outbreak was confirmed in Mpumalanga there was a request for sample testing because animals were brought in reportedly from the Heidelberg auction and they were showing clinical signs in Mogale City, Gauteng. The tests were positive for FMD. Gauteng is busy doing trace forwards but it has been concluded that the Heidelberg auction is not the culprit for causing the FMD outbreak in Gauteng. Investigations are still underway to determine the origin and linked locations.
Other Provinces
Northern Cape and Western Cape have not had any outbreak and remain FMD free.
Gauteng had an outbreak in 2023 that was resolved on 13 March 2024.
Limpopo’s 2023 outbreak was resolved on 29 August 2023.
In Mpumalanga the 2023 outbreak was resolved on 26 June 2023.
North West had an outbreak in 2023 and it was resolved on 5 March 2024.
Free State’s 2023 outbreak was resolved on 18 October 2024.
Eastern Cape is still in the process of resolving its outbreak and the last reported outbreak was on 05 September 2024.
KwaZulu Natal, Mpumalanga and Gauteng still have current outbreaks.
There were reports of buffalos escaping from the Kruger National Park and were spotted in Vhembe. Limpopo province has been requested to be on the lookout so that the disease can be caught as soon as possible. The eight buffalos that were spotted have been shot and disposed of.
Outbreak control strategies used by the Department of Agriculture
In terms of the Animal Diseases Act, FMD is a controlled animal disease so special action is taken once there is a suspected case of FMD outbreak. FMD has a significant impact on the export market, outbreaks may affect other commodities such as the export of amarula products. Once outbreak suspicions are confirmed the premises/farm, as well as linked location, are placed under quarantine pending clinical exam and further testing. If necessary the Minister then declares that area a Disease Management Area, like the ones in Eastern Cape and KZN. The aim is to control the movement of animals which is the most effective way to curb the spread of the disease.
Vaccination of affected herds helps to decrease the viral load and animal identification of vaccinated herds (with an F brandmark) is key to effective movement control. Pre-emptive vaccination of high risk areas is only done with the Director of Animal Health’s permission. Often if the feedlots or commercial herds are affected DoA also does a ‘controlled slaughter’ to ensure all the animals that may be harbouring the disease are disposed of.
FMD Emerging Challenges
- There was been a change in the behaviour of the virus; some animals no longer show the “classical” symptoms and some display a complete absence of symptoms. This complicates clinical diagnoses and early warning of the presence of disease at farm level.
- There is a spread of the disease in commercial industry through auctions and speculators. Even though there is an agreement with the Agricultural Produce Agents Council (APAC) in terms of biosecurity rules, the rules sometimes are not adhered to because there are still animals that are going to the auctions and spreading the disease. There is poor recording and traceability of animals and the 28 day post movement requirement is not complied with especially in rural communities.
- There is a lack of resources at national and provincial level; there is non-visibility of technical staff or veterinarians that are actually servicing farmers particularly the rural communities.
- Inability to procure the vaccine due to financial/supply chain management issues is also a challenge.
- Lack of individual animal identification and traceability makes it very difficult to trace back from known outbreaks and timeously find the source of infection, because tags are easily removed by farmers when taking animals to the auctions.
Way forward
So far all the measures DoA has unleashed as a response to the outbreak of FMD are proving not to be effective. The Department has therefore started a process that will at some point affect trade in order to get this matter adequately addressed. The Minister will issue a directive with new measures for control of animal disease, to auctioneers and livestock conveyancing companies to ensure that all auctions are registered, to individually identify all animals that are sold at auctions, and to disinfect vehicles entering and leaving auctions. DoA aims to allocate Animal Health Technicians (AHT) at every auction, supported by the Veterinary Officer in the area, to authorise movements at auctions and ensure that directives issued by the Minister are adhered to. The aim is to create permanent AHT positions to service and audit auctions, despite the lack of funding. DoA also aims to develop regulations to direct auctions and livestock movement in South Africa and to strengthen the regulations already in place, so that eventually it is enforced that there will be no cattle movement done in South Africa without permission.
Next steps include:
- Consulting Provincial Departments of Agriculture and Provincial Vet Services on implementation of the new measures and also meet with auctioneers, APAC and the livestock industry to discuss the new measures as well.
- Prioritise funds to procure the vaccine and related consumables. The ARC currently cannot produce vaccines to a large scale hence the vaccine is purchased from Botswana. DoA aims to increase funding to ARC to increase capacity and therefore double up on producing more vaccines. As part of the public-private-partnerships (PPPs) that the Minister always encourages, some farmers have come forward to indicate that they can buy the vaccine on their own and DoA will allow it under its guidance and supervision. More farmers that can afford to buy the vaccine for themselves are encouraged to come forward.
- Investigate practicality and science towards preventing buffalo shedding virus in the Kruger National Park (KNP) and Hluhluwi Umfolozi Park (HUP). Investigate practicality of culling FMD positive buffalo in HUP.

Department response
Mr Serage thanked the Committee for the insightful and helpful questions that improve their actions as a Department. The questions also serve as an additional light onto what could be the Department’s blind spot areas.
Mr Serage was emphatic that South Africa will regain its FMD-free status. The Department is going to employ these extraordinary steps that they would not like to implement but they have seen that the outbreak continues and is going beyond what they expected. The targets it has set up for itself are achievable because once the movement of animals is controlled, once the abattoirs are controlled, once speculators are controlled as well as the education of farmers, the Department will be able to stop the spread of FMD. Unfortunately, they may not be able to give a time-frame at the moment but they hope to be able to close these outbreaks by the end of this year and get back to where we were as a country before 2019.
The areas prone to be affected by the disease share borders or have easier routes to the KZN, Mpumalanga and Limpopo production zones. In North West, if measures are done well then ordinarily it should not have outbreaks. In Northern Cape there is no outbreak. Free State has outbreaks because it is in the centre of South Africa and shares borders with Eastern Cape and Gauteng. The specific areas will be submitted to the Committee.
On movement control, Mr Serage said that this links to enforcement. The Minister did mention that the Director-General of the Department of Agriculture and his counterpart at SAPS are planning a meeting that will look into drafting a Memorandum of Agreement (MOA) between the Department of Agriculture and the South African Police Service so that enforcement of agricultural regulations becomes part of SAPS day to day activities.
Mr Serage agreed that unless people know what to comply with, they will not be able to comply. The MOA aims to, amongst other things, control stock theft and control the movement of animals. Especially once the Minister has issued the directive, it will have to be enforced by SAPS.
On the shortage of Veterinary Officers, the Department is aware of this. It should be able to a greater degree control the FMD outbreak with the current available capacity. This boils down to the allocation of funds that the Department has and a case can be made to National Treasury with the assistance of the Portfolio Committee, because when National Treasury does employee budget cuts across government, it forgets that agriculture cannot be practised by using artificial intelligence. Agriculture still requires boots on the ground. We cannot use drones to vaccinate animals for example. The Department explains to National Treasury that if they see more police visibility as an effective way of controlling crime in South Africa then they must also see that deploying more veterinary officers on the ground would be one way of preventing disease outbreaks.
However, given the Department’s current allocation of funds, its projection is that it may be able to have Animal Health Technicians allocated to all auctions, but again they are calling on the private sector to play its part to subdue the problem. In the long-term DoA would like to have a system where the auctioneers and the speculators know what to comply with.
The Department acknowledges that it does have a shortage of Extension Officers and Veterinary Services and they are working on solving the matter. On the placement of Compulsory Community Service Veterinarians, Mr Serage said that currently they are not being placed at auctions. It is a matter that he will ask his team to look into on how best they can have these officials rotating at the auctions to assist with controlling the disease outbreak. Every year the Department enrols about 100 Compulsory Community Service Veterinarians for a period of 12 months, so maybe that is the area they can look into going forward.
Mr Serage noted that the implementation of individual animal identification has to be enforced, however if it is controlled at the state level or auction space then everybody would know that their animals have to be individually identified. The good question that the Committee is asking is that people still do not have animal brand marks years later. The Department is busy procuring vehicles/mobile offices in 2025/26 with the intention to have these mobile offices to go around especially in villages to allow livestock owners to register their livestock. This is a measure to try and bring government to the people. This is planned as part of the Department rolling-out the individual animal identification regulations.
Mr Serage agreed that FMD is a disaster but when you go further into the definition of disaster, it does not accommodate FMD as a disaster. Since the Disaster Management Act is not implemented by DoA but is the competency of the Department of Cooperative Governance and Traditional Affairs (COGTA), DoA has had engagements in the past with COGTA on declaring FMD as a disaster so that they can access disaster funding. However they have not followed-up on those discussions and it is a matter that he will take up as well. There was willingness from COGTA to assist but the challenge is that to declare a disaster is a very lengthy process and sometimes by the time a disease is declared as a disaster the outbreak might have already been contained. The law is just cumbersome for a disaster to be declared. The Committee might have noticed that even in the case of veld fires and locusts, the disaster was contained by the time funding was released. Perhaps this is a matter that Committee members can take up with COGTA because the process is just too long. The Department also undertakes to take up this matter with its counterparts at the National Disaster Management Centre and COGTA to see how best this can be explored.
Mr Serage noted Mr Trollip’s statements on self-regulation and adhering to the law and regulations are designed to be complied with and not necessarily to be enforced. In the event however where there is poor adherence to the law then enforcement happens.
Self-regulation is what the Department supports. That is why one of the key stakeholders they are meeting with this week is the livestock industry, namely the Red Meat Industry Services (RMIS) which is a representative body of the livestock owners. Self-regulation has proven to work because when the outbreak was contained in Humansdorp, it was a multi-faceted stakeholder engagement with the Eastern Cape Farmers Association and everybody in that area who worked together under the leadership of the Department and the RMIS. Self-regulation is an encouraged practice where members of the industry themselves are self-regulating. It happens a lot in the food industry where farmers know what not to do and they also police each other.
Mr Serage said that it is correct to say that the fences we used to have pre-1994 are no longer there and it is a challenge. For FMD control, South Africa needs about 2 000kms length of adequate fencing. Unfortunately fences get tampered with and vandalised so it is a serious challenge that South Africa faces but it is a matter that is worth exploring.
The Department agrees that there should be enforcement of the regulations. The intention is to have those that must adhere to the law know what the law says, what they need to do to comply with the law and what is the benefit for them to adhere to the law. They will then be able to get others to also adhere to the law.
Mr Serage said a trial was done at ARC to produce 20 000 vaccine doses in a cycle and the process is ongoing. He has tasked ARC to submit to the Committee in writing the numbers of how many vaccine doses they might have and by when. Also as a way forward they should indicate how many vaccine doses ARC will be able to produce, since the presentation mentioned they will add capacity to ARC to enable them to double-up on what they are currently able to do.
Mr Serage stated that the Animal Diseases Act is a 1984 law and most of laws designed at that time had very low penalties. The intention with new laws is to enforce penalties that would serve as a deterrent to people. His team looked into it and found that the emphasis of the Plant Pest Act and the Animal Diseases Act of 1984 was not on penalties but on self-regulation and an emphasis on people knowing what the law is and what needs to be done. The penalties prescribed in these laws are minimal and they would not serve as a deterrent.
Mr Serage said that the aim is to replace the Animal Diseases Act with the Animal Health Act of 2002, which embraces the current Constitution and the formation of government into local, provincial and national spheres. It also separates the functions of the different government spheres from national and provincial. Once that Act is brought into operation with regulations, we will be able to have penalties that would serve as a deterrent.
On employing unemployed graduates, Mr Serage indicated that DoA has a lot of them as there are many Animal Health Technicians that are unemployed, but the issue they have is budget constraints. If the Committee can assist, DoA will be able to appoint them because they know where they need capacity. If they have funds, they will be able to appoint these unemployed graduates.
Mr Serage explained that the Northern Cape and Western Cape have remained FMD-free because they are just careful and also because they are assisted by the geography of South Africa, especially the Western Cape. One then might ask why geography is not assisting KZN as it also has an ocean border. This is not the case for KZN because of the proximity of Hluhluwi Umfolozi Park. However the good governance strategies that are being employed in the Northern Cape and Western Cape are not being minimised.
Mr Serage stated that he could not capture Ms Ndalane’s question and asked Dr Modisane to respond to it if he managed to get it.
Livestock sold in open spaces in towns should not pose a threat if there is awareness and those goats and sheep that need to be vaccinated have been vaccinated. That is however an area that the Department needs to get closer to and assist the farmers that they do not get in touch with infected animals. If those animals are bought from controlled auctions where animals sold have been tested to be free from disease, then that practice should not be a problem. The major problem is usually at the source, that is, at the auctions, and if there is control in that space then the FMD outbreak would be addressed.
Mr Serage agreed that something has to be done about the fencing. As mentioned earlier for FMD to be controlled the Department needs about 2 000kms of good quality fencing that will be able to control movement of animals. Sometimes rains and floods would push some fences to the ground but DoA does need to revamp the current livestock control fencing that we have in South Africa.
Mr Serage noted that post-1994 there were good things that were also done away with and dipping tanks was one of the good practices done away with since the start of democracy. As a department they have to find a way to bring dipping tanks back especially in communal areas. Dipping tanks were able to control external parasites and diseases and the department was able to see how many farmers there are. Vaccinations were also done at the dipping tanks if and when required. This is a matter that the department did not pay sufficient attention to, because with the advent of democracy everybody started accessing livestock remedies for themselves and this coupled with the dwindling capacity in government and the fragmentation in the provincial and national department there just was not enough follow-through from DoA. South Africa still does have dipping tanks though but their management can be improved upon.
On creating awareness in farmers, DoA will have to ensure that awareness is created so that people know what is it that they need to adhere to and comply with.
Mr Serage stated that DoA did not present on the number of vaccinated livestock because it wanted to give an overview of the disease right from the beginning of when the outbreak started. However, they do have the number of the livestock that was vaccinated, but to be honest since November/December 2024, DoA ran out of vaccines. This was not due to funding constraints but because there were complications within DoA, that is, DoA has not had a Chief Financial Officer for some time now and the Acting CFO had no understanding of the urgency of the situation and eventually they could not get the vaccines on time. This is why the presentation could not indicate the number of animals vaccinated recently. The procurement process has been started, however, and the hope is to have the vaccines by the next week.
Mr Serage commented that the efficacy of the vaccines is so far very good; the vaccines do work. This is seen from the animals that have been vaccinated such as in the Humansdorp area where there was a huge outbreak, but after vaccination the outbreak was contained.
Mr Serage spoke to the challenges of producing South Africa’s own vaccine doses. The establishment of an FMD facility is a long-term goal of DoA, the plan and design is already there but the funding is not enough for the project to start. In the meantime DoA is relying on the medium-scale plan of producing vaccines from ARC while they are waiting to construct a fit-for-purpose FMD facility.
Dr Modisane noted that the Director of Animal Health, Dr Mpho Maja, and Deputy Director: Disease Control, Dr Marietta Swanepoel were also in attendance at the meeting.
Dr Modisane wanted to add that particularly in KZN dipping tanks are still functional and they are being utilised as inspection points for the animals. The dipping material that KZN purchases also serves as an incentive for the farmers to bring their animals there and the animals get to be inspected for diseases such as FMD. It is important to note that in the north-western part of KZN there is not only one disease that DoA is concerned about but there are also challenges of another disease known as Corridor Disease.
Dr Modisane commented on how SAT2 was introduced to KZN. Speculation is that the virus causing SAT2 is associated with a virus occurring in the northern part of South Africa in Makhado in Limpopo. DoA believes that perhaps the virus came from the goats referred to earlier, that are being sold by speculators around the uMtubatuba area. Therefore, speculators are definitely playing a role in the spread of viruses.
On farmers being disciplined for contravening regulations, Dr Modisane indicated that one such example is the person who caused the 2019 outbreak in Limpopo. He was held accountable and taken to court. Unfortunately having seen the magnitude of the allegations against him, the person ended up shooting himself and Dr Modisane was not sure if he is still alive or not. The perpetrator was deliberately buying cattle from the FMD control zones and going to sell them inside South Africa. That is the only case DoA can cite at the moment.
Dr Modisane explained the proliferation of red dots in the northern part of KZN. One reason there are so many outbreaks in that area is because of the lack of fences to keep the animals in and animals therefore graze communally. Animals can come from one village to graze at another village and, if they are sick, they then leave the disease there. Another reason is that it is trust land so fencing may be prohibited in that area.
The Department mainly uses fences as a barrier to control the spread of FMD but sometimes natural barriers such as mountains and rivers are used. The problem with rivers is that they sometimes dry up or over-flood. There is a very limited number of mountains, however, in that northern part of KZN to actually prevent the movement of cattle from one village to the next. Hence why there are so many FMD outbreak cases in that area.
Dr Modisane explained that the red dots on the map mean it is an FMD infected zone where there are buffalos and mainly it is in the KNP. The yellow part of the map indicates the FMD protection zone. The black dots are actually reported cases that have been resolved/closed. There is an outbreak in East London and Port Elizabeth that is in the process of being closed as well.
Dr Modisane emphasised that the vaccine is effective but the vaccine does not necessarily prevent the disease but assists in managing the disease and the shedding of the virus. The most important interventions are the quarantines and the control of movement. The intention of DoA is to get the outbreaks under control and then they will start the process of applying for FMD-free status again. The only challenge is that there are still new cases being reported at the moment.
Follow-up questions
Mr Montwedi said after listening to the DoA response from DoA, resource mobilisation seems to be one of the biggest problems affecting South Africa in dealing effectively with FMD. Has DoA actually reached out to National Treasury to seek more funds and plead its case based on the severity of the disease? It can cite that not having a FMD-free status has major financial implications for South Africa’s economy.
Mr Mngxitama commented that it is really disturbing to hear that the legislation does not have updated penalties which could be effective in addressing the current FMD problem. It seems as if DoA is facilitating these transgressions by farmers because it simply says there is old order legislation that is insufficiently punitive and therefore it is not going to be preventative and effective. The onus is therefore on DoA to assist this Committee by addressing this. He requested that DoA furnish the Committee with the proposed amendments so that they can see if the suggested punitive measures are actually going to address the matter. We are sitting with a problem that could have been addressed a long time ago because if farmers are not being punished for their transgressions then that is an incentive to continue to do what they are doing. It would be beneficial for DoA to submit the proposed amendments so the Committee can see if the matter is being addressed or not.
Department response
Mr Serage replied that DoA has had meetings with National Treasury especially during the Sixth Administration. He confirmed that DoA has not had an opportunity now in the Seventh Administration to meet with the new National Treasury Director General. However this is a normal government process where DoA submits its financial needs but National Treasury does not seem to understand. DoA is going to try again because now that it is a split department maybe its voice was not audible enough when it was part of the bigger department. Hopefully now things will be easier because it is a focused department.
In 2017/18 the Department met with National Treasury and it was allocated additional funds for biosecurity purposes and it used this for FMD and other disease control measures. In the previous financial year DoA also received additional funds but the funds were allocated very late so they could not be used. Mr Serage believed that if the discussions can be escalated to be held Minister to Minister then that perhaps would help because DoA cannot afford not to have boots on the ground when it comes to the livestock and farming business.
Mr Serage agreed with Mr Mngxitama about the current outdated legislation. The Department is still busy finalising the regulations of the new Animal Health Act of 2002. They have started the process of promulgating the Animal Health Act so it can replace the current Animal Diseases Act of 1984. Once the regulations are done, they will be shared with the Committee. In the new regulations, DoA will see if it can factor in deterrent penalties as well. He agreed that the current framework does not make provision for real deterrent measures compared to laws in other countries. For example, DoA cannot charge a farmer with a spot fine upon discovering they are harbouring controlled products. The current law says you must go to the magistrate court and it is a tedious process that does not help in enforcing agricultural laws.
Minister final remarks
Minister Steenhuisen thanked the Committee for their interaction and Mr Serage and Dr Modisane for their inputs. He hopes the Committee can see FMD is something that DoA is taking seriously given its impact on South Africa.
Firstly, the lack of deterrent measures for farmers has been a concern for him ever since he became Minister. That is one of the reasons DoA is currently reviewing all agricultural sector legislation and some of these Acts date as far back as 1940. We need to ensure that we have a legislative environment that is fit-for-purpose to handle all this.
In terms of the new Animal Health Act of 2002, the penalties and offences are set out in section 29 and it states that “any person found guilty of an offence under this Act is: (i) in the case of a first conviction is liable to a fine or imprisonment for a period not exceeding 2 years, or to both such fine and imprisonment. (ii) in the case of a second or subsequent conviction, if the same or any other offence referred to, is liable to a fine or imprisonment for a period not exceeding 4 years, or to both such fine and imprisonment.”
It is clear that the sooner we can get the new Act regulations done and get a commencement date for the Act from the President, DoA will be in a firmer position because the new Act does take into consideration deterrent measures.
Secondly, the Minister responded to why DoA has not declared a disaster. If we want to isolate it to provincial level this function falls to the Provincial Executive in each of the cases. At a national level there are very strict criteria that need to be met before a national disaster is declared. This was seen clearly in the recent attempts by COGTA to try and declare a national disaster on the food poisoning crisis. The advice it got from the State Attorneys was that the food poisoning crisis did not meet the requirements. He thinks that DOA will also face the same fate if we try declaring the FMD outbreak as a national disaster.
The Minister stated that we also need to be mindful that the measures and implementation strategies discussed today have consequences for the industry and the agricultural sector as a whole. It must be noted that while FMD deals with cattle, goats and sheep, the disease also affects the world’s trade and a variety of other industries. So the last thing we want to do is create a major scare for the international markets and trading partners when they see that now suddenly there is a national disaster and then shut down all of the sectors, rather than us dealing with the outbreaks and isolating declaration as a notifiable disease. We then inform trading partners when the outbreaks happen. He believes that is a more responsible way to approach the matter because if we notify the export market it will drive down prices, with loss of market and excess of product and thus negatively affect the local market. We saw this previously when the world was banned from China as a result of FMD outbreaks; luckily we now have protocols in place there. So if we were to declare a national disaster even China will be forced to move away from their compartmentalised approach and then put a national ban on all our products as a country.
The Minister said the third item we have to grapple with is the constitutional contradictions that make it incredibly complex to navigate veterinary services. Yes, we do have a national shortage of veterinarians in South Africa, which is why Mr Serage and his team have been working tirelessly to remove the red tape that makes it difficult for DoA to deploy people who want to come and work as veterinarians in South Africa. We also lose a lot of veterinarians to other countries. That is one of the reasons DoA has the Compulsory Community Service model so that if we are spending money on training veterinary practitioners and veterinarians, then at least we do get something back as a country.
However, the Minister noted that there is an anomaly in the Constitution. Animal health is a functional area of national and provincial competence but veterinary services as set out in Schedule 5 Part A of the Constitution is a provincial legislative competence so the real power of veterinary services actually lies with the provinces and not with national. The Constitution reads “the provinces have exclusive provincial legislative competence over veterinary services excluding the regulation of the profession”. This indicates that the job of the National Department is to regulate the profession but it is up to provinces to grant deployment. The National Department however cannot walk away from veterinary services given the issues being discussed today but this is an issue we are going to have to grapple with in the coming years on how we are going to clarify the constitutional roles, because if we start to regulate veterinary services at national level there could be push back and challenges on why DoA is legislating and making regulations on these matters when it is in fact an exclusively provincial legislative competence. It is something the Committee and DoA needs to keep in mind as we push forward in the agricultural space, particularly when it comes to veterinary services.
Chairperson remarks

She thanked the Minister for assisting in clarifying issues like the process involved in declaring a disaster and the legislation around it. It benefited the Committee to note the impact it would have if they were to follow that route, that instead of helping resolve the FMD challenge, it would cause a negative image for SA.
The Chairperson indicated that DoA had presented its Annual Performance Plan and Strategic Plan two weeks ago. However, it looks like they will have to start afresh so they will request DoA to present the budget process again. She noted that two of the DoA Entities did not appear before the Committee because the Minister had sent them back to finalise certain areas of their presentation. The Committee hopes that when the meeting is called again all the entities will be ready and present along with DoA.
The Minister and Department were excused from the meeting.
