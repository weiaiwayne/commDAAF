Meeting Summary
Video
AnchorThe Portfolio Committee on
Trade, Industry and Competition met in Parliament for a briefing on the outcomes of a Master Plans evaluation by Trade and Industrial Policy Strategies (TIPS), a South African independent, non-profit, economic research institution.
TIPS informed the Committee that the DTIC had funded the research to assess the success of the Master Plans programme five years after introducing the Master Plan approach. The evaluation was intended to help identify new opportunities and areas for improvement in the Master Plans programme, which had arisen from the concept of a reimagined industrial strategy. TIPS had decided to undertake a review of the overall Master Plans programme and an analysis of the main mode of implementation of individual plans. Four plans were taken as case studies and reviewed in more depth.
The main problem was the lack of a policy document which would have shown the intention of the Master Plans. However, there had been close collaboration and engagement with industry stakeholders. The Master Plans had not evaluated the impact of tariffs and local procurement on prices, costs, and government services, nor had they opened new opportunities for small businesses on a large scale. TIPS addressed the aims and prioritisation of the Master Plans, analysed the strategies and measures and examined some of the outcomes. TIPS assessed and interpreted the impact on socio-economic groups and presented some proposals that the Department might wish to consider. The analysis concluded by noting the implications that the Master Plans had for the country’s industrial policy.
Members were fully engaged in the presentation, which came after detailed discussions with the Department of Trade, Industry, and Competition on Master Plans the previous day. Questions were specific to TIPS's research, but Members almost asked for the views of the senior economist who had made the presentation.
Why was the Industrial Policy Plan (IPAP) and all its iterations replaced when seemingly there was no plan in its place? What were the structural causes of low self-employment levels in South Africa? What was preferable: higher employment levels or higher self-employment levels? What would be the ideal ratio between the two if the economy were functioning well? Was it correct to use the terms industrial policy and industrial strategy interchangeably? Were they the same thing, and if not, what were the differences? Was industrial policy part of the industrial strategy, or was industrial strategy part of the policy? Had labour unions, as a sector, raised the need for an emphasis on job creation, job growth, the quality of the jobs and the sustainability of jobs created on behalf of their members? How were the broader, unemployed, working-class interests represented in each sector?
Members were also concerned about the evolution and fast-forwarding of artificial intelligence, the digitisation of the economy, and the emergence of the digital economy. How did that map out the country's industrialisation? How did it address the issues of insecurity amongst working-class people?
Members asked for some thoughts on the country's sunrise industry. What were the two or three things that, if the country did them well, would result in large-scale job opportunities for the masses? What was going to anchor South Africa's industrial policy?
Meeting report
Opening Remarks
The Chairperson opened the meeting a little late but acknowledged that the Committee members did not want to extend it that day as they were all preparing for the afternoon’s budget speech. He noted that the documents on Master Plans had been thoroughly interrogated the previous day and asked the Department of Trade, Industry and Competition (the dtic) to commence immediately.
Evaluation of the master plans by TIPS
Dr Tebogo Makube, Chief Director: Industrial Procurement at the Department of Trade, Industry and Competition (DTIC) introduced his colleagues, Ms Ncumisa Mcata-Mhlauli, Chief Director: Agro-processing (Sugar, Poultry, Furniture and Cannabis Master Plans) and Ms Nyakallo Dlambulo: Director for the Steel and Metals Master Plan. He was accompanied by the Chief Economist at TIPS, Dr Neva Makgetla, who would brief the Committee on the evaluation of the DTIC Master Plans that the non-profit organisation had undertaken.
Dr Makgetla explained that
TIPS was an independent research organisation largely funded by the DTIC, with whom TIPS worked closely. TIPS was asked to evaluate the Master Plan project by the DPME (Department of Planning, Monitoring and Evaluation) and the DTIC. TIPS had finished its work a few months previously, and there had been some developments since then that were not captured in the documentation
The Master Plans had arisen from the concept of a reimagined industrial strategy. TIPS decided to tackle the research as a project into Master Plans generally and not simply assess individual plans. The main problem was the lack of a policy document that would show the intention of the Master Plan and so on. Neither a white paper nor a green paper contained the actual policy. However, there had been close collaboration and engagement with industry stakeholders, i.e. established business and organised labour, on various platforms, and that was more important than a published document.
She presented the strategies and measures of the Master Plans. Master Plans did not evaluate the impact of tariffs and local procurement on prices and government services, nor did they open new opportunities for small businesses on a large scale. Industry-level platforms were unable to deal with crises at Eskom and Transnet. As a group, the Master Plan industries saw a slower increase in import intensity than other industries, but only historic commodity exports climbed significantly (mostly due to higher world prices).
The presentation showed the implications for the industrial policy, which had to meet South Africa’s unique needs and specify long-term goals to help ensure the process remained on the right track. The programmes should be scaled up to create opportunities and empower people through innovative programmes, including products, ownership, financing and intermediaries. Established producers should not be protected from competition but encouraged to become more competitive. It was key for the state to prioritise a few key aims and programmes as the basis for alignment across the country; the Department of Trade, Industry and Competition should reallocate capacity within the Department; the risks of structural change had to be managed.

Dr Makgetla said that she would combine some questions in her response. In terms of low self-employment, the structural cause was apartheid. Compared to other developing countries, people did not have their own productive assets. They did not have families that they could learn from in most cases. They did not have suppliers' financing and market systems in place on which they could build. So because of what happened in apartheid, people had to start fresh if they wanted to open a business, and that was very hard. That inheritance in material, cultural, and social competencies was an issue. What should the government do? The problem was that self-employment was risky, but if the country could get it right, people could do much better than in a wage job. But it also meant that the government was forcing individuals to take on a lot of risk, often without resources to make it easier.
She said that one has to have networks, assets, security in one’s life, and the competency and experience to succeed. To push people into it without that was risky. South Africa should have more small businesses, but how much of the employment problem should be addressed through small businesses and how much through employment schemes? How much is the social economy through public sector schemes and subsidised projects? Big businesses should expand in labour-intensive areas. Industrial policy was not a science, so one had to look at all the possibilities and see what would work. That could not be determined in advance, but it was not realistic to say that everybody who was unemployed should become self-employed, not without a huge amount of support.
Dr Makgetla argued that the real problem was joblessness, not long-term objectives. The government needed to grow employment and self-employment, but the question was how to do it. The Master Plan story suggested that there should be more focus on where the country could grow output, where to grow employment and the state's role in subsidising some of that because of the social benefits of employment. To be blunt, resourcing was a trade-off with social grants. Given the limited resources, it was much less risky for National Treasury to put the money into social grants. People got social grants and many big social benefits from social grants, but that squeezed the rest of the budget. She would probably be doing social grants if she were in the Treasury. However, the long-term solution, socially and economically, required structural change, which was hard. The link between strategy and policy was what it was all about. South Africa had industry-level policies but no clear strategy, which was the problem. Part of the reason for the lack of a clear strategy was that the society was very deeply divided. South Africa had a political democracy but was one of the most unequal societies in terms of the economy, which made it extremely hard to come up with agreements on anything. Hence, the fudging. Standing up to big and established businesses was hard, yet one needed to get the votes. Research shows that the more integrated and equitable a society, the faster it grows. Deeply unequal societies tended to grow more slowly, with the contestation over ownership and policy being a key reason. It was easy to talk about the need for priorities as long as they were one’s own priorities.
She declared that very high export prices of the mineral energy complex had saved the country in the aftermath of the COVID-19 downturn. Now, there was a conflicted approach to MEC: when prices were low, there was talk of reconstructing the economy, but there was always a hope that prices would go up. The solution was more about diversifying away from mining.
She noted that there were many questions. What to diversify into? What made sense? What could be done? South Africa could not just copy Asia and go into manufacturing because it was a latecomer. Could the country benchmark to see if there was diversification? What would diversification look like in terms of those targets? Being more dependent on mining exports than other countries limited employment because it was capital-intensive. The issue was more about what kind of industries could create employment or self-employment, what were realistic targets to get there and how to measure getting there and being able to change policies if targets were not being attained. The coal value chain was the basis because coal production for energy had made the mines possible, Transnet possible, etc.
Dr Makgetla explained that part of the question was about closing off opportunities and how to change that. It was necessary to sustain mining but to consider if there were things to do that would help move beyond mining. The government needed to be more creative about off-budget resources during financial austerity. During COVID-19, the use of UIF funds reinforced what could be done. There was talk about it, but nothing more; being careful was essential. Treasury had said it would do big cost-cutting and hold down remuneration and social grants, but that became politically impossible, and then the budget was busted. Treasury cut things slightly, at random. The government needed to engage in reprioritisation genuinely and not just do impossible things for political reasons. Part of the problem was the unrealistic budgets that were politically not viable.
She believed South Africa was generally overly restrictive on fiscal and monetary policy. Without structural change, there would be more inflation, slower growth, etc., so that was not the core of the problem.
The unions always raised the claim of job creation, but Dr Makgetla did not think it was fair to expect the unions to deal with those problems and to represent the rest of society; it was the role of the democratic state to represent people who otherwise would not have a voice. It was a problem because it put a huge burden on the mandating process and on non-officials who were being asked to think about innovative solutions. The inherited systems were not set up to enable them to do that. TIPS found that every country with a successful industrial policy had some type of think tank to help because it was very hard to implement a policy, keep an eye on the sector and engage with stakeholders to develop innovative ideas. In such a scenario, the political leadership had to determine the mandate. The next question was how to resource the mandate. There were no systems in place to deal with that, and it was annoying to walk into a job summit where the government guys asked what the unions could give as labour. The response from labour was to ask what the government wanted. People often wanted labour to be less militant but did not have the guts to say that. Wages in South Africa were not lower than in other countries, but given the unequal societies, a low-wage strategy would not work. People wanted less workplace conflict but did not have the guts to say that. Management would need to change to create a less conflict-ridden society. Labour often could not develop specific proposals, which was not their job. Who represented the working class? It should be a democratic government.
Dr Makgetla considered what TIPS could do to assist. Her boss would tell them about the resourcing issue. Saul Levin, the chief executive director, had to keep an eye on the money, and although TIPS would love to volunteer for more things, it had limited resources. However, TIPS could help with the numbers. The Committee was getting outputs and outcomes, but it needed to look at the impacts, and that was hard because things happened in two ways. Firstly, the outputs could be good, but jobs were lost somewhere else. How did one see that? Over the longer run, as opposed to a quarterly report, if employment was not going up, then it did not matter what the outputs were. The policy was not working. TIPS could help by looking at some of the impact figures. If the Department said it was supporting 200 000 jobs in an industry, and by supporting, it meant there were 200 000 people involved in an industry with a Master Plan, one was getting into the definition of what was meant by support. In terms of technological change, which also applies to the MEC, one has to consider the unemployment problem. Other countries did not have unemployment; they had people employed at low productivity self-employment, i.e. on subsistence farms and in little retail shops, producing things in a fairly premium way. In South Africa, there was flat-out unemployment, so the government had to come up with growth that generated employment opportunities for people to engage with the economy and with society to earn some kind of income that might not be cutting-edge by anybody's standards because they would be going from producing nothing to producing something. People moved from agriculture to manufacturing services to increase productivity in other countries. She believed South Africa could not chase higher productivity across the board, although the country needed some higher productivity sectors. The government had to think about how that would work.
Dr Makgetla suggested that much labour displacement was anticipated from things like AI and robotics and the fourth IR generalism. (She suggested the Committee should talk to Saul Levin about that.) But that was much more relevant to the global North, where wages were much higher. However, South Africa adopted the same technology to supply its wage levels. International companies had two strategies for dealing with workers' wages: they either mechanised them out of existence or moved to the global south. And that was still happening, but the real problem was that South Africa copied their technologies. She believed there would be a lot less displacement of labour in South Africa because of AI than people talked about in other parts of the world and that there were areas where South Africa had real strengths, like in software. The trouble was reflected in the inequalities in education that could increase inequalities in the country if people were not ready for that. That was her simple answer. The government had to think about the just transition issues as well. Still, the bigger issue was to ask how to grow employment in less advanced areas, which would add value to society and give people an income. That might or might not require some subsidies but would enable people to be part of society and generate income for themselves. The government also had to figure out how to grow competitiveness in the higher end of industries. Those things could be contradictory, but they had to be managed.
Moving on to why IPAP was replaced, she suggested that people felt the country needed to have a few industries and make them work. IPAP was seen as a collection of industry strategies, but people did not think about the fact that it also provided a cross-cutting framework, even if it was not always well-articulated as a cross-cutting framework. The effort was more about enforcing priorities. However, they did not think about what they were losing in the process or what happened to sector strategies for industries that were not priorities but required some kind of sector-level response. That was the result of people genuinely not thinking. She believed the Green Interim Plan might help because it provided a framework for thinking about a cross-cutting strategy instead of just industry-level policies. The government also needed to care about what it was offering to industries that did not have a national plan.
Regarding incentives for the auto industry, Dr Makgetla stated they were linked to tax policy, i.e., the tax breaks they received from tariffs. The tariffs on the imports of autos were maintained as long as the local auto industry exported vehicles. That was what the Master Plan originally intended to change because it meant that the domestic market, particularly for smaller cars, was met by imports. South Africa had almost a trade balance in autos most years, with a very slight export surplus. The net balance was small, and the plan had been to look for a way to protect the domestic market for local producers, but that was never implemented. The domestic market required attention to get to a million cars, but if the government were not prepared to do that, she would argue for a small, high-quality, world-class assembly industry for cars. Asking what the country got out of it and how to maximise the benefits was like saying that one could not criticise a horse for not being a unicorn.
She explained that there was very little justification for building a car from scratch in South Africa or having a South African model. They should be asking what personal transport needs were unique to South Africa and how to respond to that. That might provide a platform for going to other countries that also need such vehicles. That was the Brazilian model: it had developed Embraer jets in response to the argument that they needed short-haul jets and nobody was producing them. Having 313 million people, Brazil had a much bigger market than South Africa, but they focused on their needs, and it turned out that many other people found that a useful thing, too, and nobody else was producing them. If South Africa were just producing a car that India could produce, it would start from scratch with a much smaller scale market and would not be able to compete.
Dr Makgetla’s personal opinion was that South Africa should be looking at e-bikes and motorcycles because people did not have personal transport but could not afford a car. If South Africa produced motorcycles for all of Africa, it would improve people's living standards as they were relatively cheap and made a big difference in people's lives. Producing a car that met South Africa’s unique needs would not be viable. However, the government needed to determine what was wanted from the viable auto industry if the country was not going to protect the domestic market the way the Master Plan had originally proposed.
Because TIPS had been doing a lot of work on steel, Dr Makgetla made a few points on that issue. The steel industry, historically the MEC, was competitive because the government held down the price of iron ore and coal to the domestic steel producers, and they held down the price of steel to people downstream. The logic behind the steel industry in South Africa was that there were very small deposits of extremely high-quality iron ore and some decent coal: that was the basis for the steel industry. Once the industry charged market-related prices for iron ore and coal, it was hard to justify an integrated mill, so the Committee should get out of the fight. Instead of raising the price of scrap metal and putting the mini-mills out of business, South Africa should ask how to bring down the price of other inputs because that would capture benefits for the country. However, the government had to ensure that ArcelorMittal did not just raise its downstream prices. South Africa had never been willing to do that. ArcelorMittal had downsized by 60% since ArcelorMittal South Africa took over the steel mills, and that told one that it was a long-term crisis. Shutting down the mini-mills would not help resolve that crisis.

The government needed to use policy instruments to deal with structural ownership and the prices accruing to a few people, as that did not make the economy competitive.
Dr Makube said that, from an institutional point of view, what was becoming clear was that the implementation of some industrial policies was stagnant, and some of the Master Plans were based on the performance of other government agencies. So, how could they use competition in some of the sectors to open up the economy? Competition also improved prices. How did they drive the transformation to create opportunities in the regions? Those were the issues the government would have to deal with while driving economic transformation. He added that the DTIC was doing other work besides the Master Plans, as seen in the APP. He agreed that DTIC should not lose focus on the economic structural challenges that need to be resolved.
Dr Makgetla agreed that there was work being done on an industrial policy framework, which she believed was precise because there was a definition and because the baby had been thrown out of the bathtub with IPAP. The Committee might also want to engage in the drafts of the industrial policy early on rather than just waiting for the final document. The new Master Plans indicated priority projects more than industrial policy development. A critical question was how to make industrial policy more than the whole government project, given the inherited structures and the huge differences in an unequal society.
She said that asking about the long-term aims and measuring progress against them might mean changing specific policies, but that would be useful. Another question was about the institutional structures that could ensure alignment: what was the presidency's and Treasury's role? What was the role of the cluster? If the government were serious about alignment, those things would have to be restructured, requiring more resources than the country's. Having a more consistent approach towards business with clearer mandates would be helpful. What happened was that the government was not clear on what it wanted from established business, so the tendency was to either give them whatever they wanted or to tell them how much the government hated them, which made them crazy. The government had to be clear about what it wanted and promise to do everything it could to cut business costs. In return, the government had to set expectations regarding investments and transformation costs, which it would not walk away from. In return for such cooperation, the government would, for example, make sure Eskom worked. Those were the kind of trade-offs needed for things to get better. That also applied to what the Committee had said about needing a clear industrial strategy that was implemented according to well-defined policies. Again, the government had thrown out some of its strengths in that area, but people were trying to resurrect them.
About Asia, Dr Makgetla said that Asian countries were in a very different context. Firstly, they competed with the Global North. South African wages were about the same as China’s; the difference was that South Africans competed with the U.S., and South Africans competed with China. When building its economy, China had to grow back, but now South Africa was competing with China, which was much harder. South Africa could not just copy China, which had agricultural societies rather than societies with massive joblessness. The Chinese government could afford to tell people to stay on the farms until there was a job, and then they could take it. South Africa was under much more pressure to create jobs quickly. Thirdly, Asia was in the middle of the Cold War, which meant there was lots and lots of American military spending all over the region, which certainly helped. It also meant that regional infrastructure was built in ways impossible in South Africa. So if undersea cables or ports or whatever were needed in South Africa, getting that done was all on South Africa’s shoulders. South Africa could not look to China to compete with Japan and South Korea to compete with Japan. South Africa’s possibilities were limited and different.
Concerning new energy, Dr Makgetla said it was a huge advantage for the country to have cheap renewable energy. It was an advantage in two ways: firstly, cheaper electricity in the long run, particularly as coal was forced to internalise the cost of pollution; secondly, 20% of South Africa’s imports were nuclear fuel. So it would be much better to shift to renewable energy. The base load was not such a big issue because the base load was more critical in a highly centralised electricity system and less critical in a de-centralised system. If energy were a more decentralised system, the different sources could be played against each other. It did not mean there was no need for a base load, but there were more options. Technology was so much better than it used to be that the long-term benefits were there; it was the transition costs that the country had to manage. The long-term benefits were pretty poor. Saving 20% of imports on nuclear fuel would be great and would make a huge difference.
Creative destruction was when new technologies were rising, and existing companies had to sell off assets ahead of time. Dr Makgetla declared that the government was not good at dealing with creative destruction. ArcelorMittal, the mini-mills and a lot of those technologies that were established businesses lobbied the government to protect them, and it did so rather than going with the new technology that would bring about change.
Finally, Dr Makgetla turned to poverty and joblessness. TIPS was doing some work on the issue. She argued that the critical thing to consider was how to recreate that ecosystem for small businesses and self-employment destroyed under apartheid in many ways. It was not just about giving some funding to small businesses or giving people land. It was the whole thing: all of the services people needed to use the land, the inputs they needed to use the funds, the skills and that kind of support. The government was not good at that, so it had to consider which intermediaries would work, whether it was contracting out agriculture, franchise operations in retail and food or other services. Even government services were becoming more labour-intensive, and bringing in people at lower skill levels was possible. The country had to be much more innovative about those kinds of projects, and the really big challenges had always been the risk and the money, and those two things were related. Being innovative was very hard; under current government financial rules, paying an intermediary to do that kind of work was hard.
The issue was that departments were good at dealing with established businesses, but it was unclear who was responsible for creating new employment and opportunities on a mass scale. Was it the DTIC? Was it the Department of Agriculture? Was it the Department of Social Development? Was it municipalities? It was very hard to allocate those responsibilities until clear impact targets indicated what was needed. How did the government manage the risk so that it felt comfortable funding the process? She asserted that those were the two really big innovations that had to be corrected.
The Chairperson thanked Dr Makgetla. He wished that all committee members had attended the meeting. He still questioned to what extent TIPS could help sharpen the Committee so that Members could ask the right questions about the new APP. Much work had been done ideologically to converse the views between the Committee and the Department, and they seemed to be finding each other. Now, an instrument was needed to make sure that they could hold each other accountable.
The meeting was adjourned.
