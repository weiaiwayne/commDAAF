Meeting Summary
Video
The Portfolio Committee on Women, Youth and Persons with Disabilities met to engage with stakeholders ahead of a planned workshop. The Chairperson acknowledged that the Committee had not adequately addressed matters affecting young people and persons with disabilities. In response, the Committee would host a workshop the following week to identify key focus areas and set priorities. The purpose of the current meeting was to prepare for that workshop. The meeting focused on matters related to the Committee's mandate, with input from various experts to assist in determining the priority areas for the upcoming workshop.
The Committee received a presentation on youth well-being, which highlighted persistent socioeconomic exclusion, weak coordination across departments, and a lack of targeted support for young people. Another presentation focused on barriers to education for children with disabilities, including inadequate infrastructure, poor implementation of inclusive policies, and long waiting lists. A briefing on technology and disability rights emphasised the failure of departments to comply with digital accessibility standards and called for enforceable legislation.
The Committee also heard a historical reflection on the Commission for Gender Equality, its mandate, and the institutional challenges it continues to face. A detailed analysis of the National Strategic Plan (NSP) on Gender-Based Violence and Femicide (GBVF) outlined budget cuts, data discrepancies, service gaps, and poor interdepartmental coordination. A final presentation addressed community-based responses to GBV, the gendered nature of violence, and the importance of trauma-informed, grassroots interventions.
Members expressed concern about high youth unemployment, weak service access in rural areas, and limited NYDA outreach. They called for stronger enforcement of inclusive education and questioned the state's ability to uphold constitutional rights amid budget constraints. The high levels of unemployment among persons with disabilities were condemned, with Members noting the private sector's poor compliance. They raised concerns about inaccessible infrastructure, outdated legislation, and the failure to monitor gender-based violence involving persons with disabilities.
Stakeholders urged better coordination across departments, stronger oversight, and improved outreach. They identified data gaps, institutional weaknesses, and the lack of legislative follow-through as key obstacles. They also emphasised that government underspending undermines services meant to support vulnerable groups.
The Committee criticised the return of unspent funds to Treasury and questioned whether the current oversight model of the GBVF plan was sufficient. It committed to further engagements and confirmed that unresolved matters would be pursued at the next physical meeting.

Briefing by SALDRU on Youth well-being in South Africa
Prof Ariane de Lannoy, Researcher: Southern Africa Labour and Development Research Unit (SALDRU), University of Cape Town (UCT), briefed the Committee on the structural and multi-dimensional barriers faced by young people in South Africa. She framed youth well-being across the life course, outlining the social wage package that supports individuals from early childhood through to adulthood. These include early learning interventions, school nutrition programmes, the Child Support Grant, and later, youth-focused active labour market programmes (ALMPs) and the Social Relief of Distress (SRD) Grant.
Challenges facing young people
She stressed that adolescence and young adulthood remain critical phases where socioeconomic exclusion is most deeply entrenched. A lack of coordinated and targeted interventions during these periods could lead to social exclusion, mental ill-health, and long-term social drift. Prof de Lannoy highlighted that without meaningful support, deprivation experienced by youth becomes cyclical and persistent.
Proposed approach to youth support
The presentation advocated for a comprehensive and coherent youth policy framework that includes better tracking of youth deprivation at sub-national levels, improved accountability across departments, and streamlined service delivery. Prof de Lannoy emphasised that support strategies should be tailored to individual profiles and local context, noting the importance of community cohesion, proximity to labour markets, and multifaceted needs. A potential intervention, the "Basic Package of Support for Young People," was cited as a promising example of an integrated approach.
Use of data and evidence
She presented the Youth Explorer tool (
https://www.youthexplorer.org.za/
) as a valuable resource for evidence-based policymaking. This tool maps youth deprivation and service access at the local level and can support strategic planning and targeted intervention.

The Chairperson welcomed the presentation and expressed agreement with its contents, stating that it aligned closely with the Committee’s mandate. She supported the recommendation for cross-sectoral collaboration and emphasised the value of the Youth Explorer tool in informing the Committee’s work. The Chairperson reiterated the Committee’s concern regarding the accessibility of the National Youth Development Agency (NYDA) in rural areas and stressed the need for the agency to improve outreach efforts. Briefing by CCL on access to education for children with disabilities
Ms Karabo Ozah, Director: Centre for Child Law (CCL), University of Pretoria (UP), briefed the Committee on the structural and systemic barriers to education for children with disabilities (CWDs) in South Africa. She noted that, despite constitutional guarantees, between 500 000 and 600 000 children with disabilities remained out of school.
Fragmented legislative and policy framework
She highlighted that the existing legal and policy frameworks remained fragmented, outdated, and inadequately responsive to the needs of CWDs. Planning, implementation, and monitoring were inconsistent, and there was little evidence of meaningful inclusion by the DBE.
Inequality across school types
Education for CWDs occurred through three channels: ordinary public schools, full-service schools, and special schools. Ms Ozah stated that the implementation of White Paper 6 was insufficient, with the target of 500 full-service schools falling short of national needs. In addition, many schools lacked infrastructure, trained personnel, assistive technology, and funding to function inclusively.
Persistent access barriers
Ms Ozah said that more than 11 500 children remained on long waiting lists, often for years, and in some cases ageing out of the system without ever receiving formal education. Underspending, a shortage of special schools—especially in rural areas—and the failure to meet the goals of the 20-year White Paper 6 plan were flagged as key concerns. She also noted that the last census data from 2011 was outdated and not useful for planning.
International obligations and legislative gaps
Ms Ozah expressed concern about the limited inclusion of children’s issues in the draft legislation aimed at domesticating the UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (UNCRPD). She noted that Articles 7 and 24 had not been adequately incorporated and that legal and policy mechanisms were still misaligned with the Convention’s obligations.
[See attached presentation for further details]
The Chairperson appreciated the presentation and agreed that the Committee could do more to review existing policy and legislation. She recalled visiting a school for learners with visual impairment in KwaZulu-Natal during a previous oversight, which she described as being in a disastrous state. She stated that the Committee had regularly engaged with the Portfolio Committee on Basic Education on matters relating to learners with disabilities. The ongoing reports of children being placed on long waiting lists and lacking adequate access to education were deeply concerning to her, and she suggested that this issue should be prioritised as a key focus area. She then introduced the next speaker.
Presentation: Technology and disability rights
Ms Chelsea Williamson, a disability rights advocate, briefed the Committee on the intersection of technological advancement and disability inclusion. She began by outlining how assistive technologies have evolved from siloed, often inaccessible tools to increasingly integrated features in mainstream technology. Inclusive design by leading tech companies was now enabling greater dignity, independence, and social participation for persons with disabilities.
Legislative context and accessibility frameworks
Ms Williamson presented the existing domestic, regional, and international legal instruments underpinning disability rights and access to technology. These included the Constitution, the Promotion of Equality and Prevention of Unfair Discrimination Act (PEPUDA), the 2016 White Paper on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities, the African Disability Protocol, the UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (UNCRPD), and the OECD Principles on Artificial Intelligence.
Emerging technologies and inclusive potential
She explored how Artificial Intelligence (AI) and Extended Reality (XR) could be leveraged to support persons with disabilities through real-time captioning, South African Sign Language (SASL) interpretation, and the simulation of accessible educational or work environments. She cautioned, however, that without intentional inclusion, innovation could lead to exclusion by design.
Call to action and legal reform
Ms Williamson called on policymakers to ensure legislation evolves to match technological progress. She advocated for enforceable compliance standards, requirements for accessibility in public digital services, incentives for inclusive design, and the protection of personal data for persons with disabilities.
[See attached presentation for further details]
The Chairperson agreed that legislation should evolve to keep pace with advances in technology. She committed to following up on the issue of accessibility of government websites for persons with disabilities. The low employment rate among persons with disabilities remained a serious concern, and she stated that more should be done to address this.

Responses
Youth accessibility and services
Prof de Lannoy responded to concerns about accessibility for youth in rural and township areas. She agreed that the NYDA could not achieve meaningful access on its own and stated that other Departments needed to be involved. She emphasised the importance of oversight in determining the resources being utilised, their channels, and the potential outcomes. She raised the issue of "service deserts," and said that the Youth Explorer platform was helpful in this regard, particularly where young people had limited access to services.
She stated that the greatest failure was the ongoing challenge of access. Young people, she said, should not be rejected but supported. Citing global studies, she noted that being responsive to youth had a positive impact and argued that efforts should be intensified to reach more young people. She acknowledged that many youth were discouraged and added that part of the problem was the lack of awareness about existing platforms and services. For this reason, she said outreach was essential. Service points, she explained, functioned as referral hubs, and if one could not assist a young person, that individual should be directed elsewhere. This intervention would ensure that fewer young people were abandoned by the system.
On the issue of skills provision, Prof de Lannoy said that the DBE played a role in providing a conducive learning environment and in equipping youth with basic skills. While there were universities and training colleges available, she maintained that the quality of education and training remained inadequate for preparing young people for the labour market. She observed that those with tertiary education transitioned more easily into employment, and noted that university graduates experienced the lowest unemployment rates among all youth. However, she was uncertain whether Technical and Vocational Education and Training (TVET) colleges adequately prepared students for work in the private sector.
She added that job opportunities for young people were scarce and often poorly remunerated. She stressed that youth required more support, especially in entrepreneurial ventures. Some youth, she said, entered small business with little backing, and when such ventures failed, they were left in debt. She reiterated that support should be available when young people seek help, particularly given the mental health challenges they face. She observed that stigmatisation of mental illness remained high, while support was limited.
Legal and Infrastructural Challenges Affecting Children
Ms Ozah provided an update on the legal matter referenced in her earlier presentation. She informed the Committee that the 2010 court order did not include timelines for compliance, prompting her to conduct research on developments since then. Based on her findings, she intended to plan for litigation and advocacy.
She reported that the school in the Eastern Cape, where children had previously been forced to sleep on concrete floors, had since been closed. Funds had been allocated for renovations, but delays and contractor disputes had hindered progress. The school had drawn media attention, particularly due to the lack of use of allocated funds. As a result, learners were relocated to other schools, some up to 200km from their original district.
Turning to the Constitution and resource allocation, Ms Ozah suggested that the Committee engage with the issue of large sums being returned to the National Treasury despite ongoing deprivation among children. She recalled that a timeline for eradicating poor infrastructure had once been set but was later withdrawn, which had undermined her work and broken promises made to affected children.
On children with disabilities confined to their homes, she pointed out that the matter was multi-sectoral but placed particular emphasis on the role of the Department of Social Development. She questioned whether officials were conducting outreach to communities where such children were excluded from schooling. She noted that some were ineligible for the Care Dependency Grant due to not meeting specified criteria and said the Department could provide guidance on the application process.
She raised concern over the practice of grouping children with disabilities of different ages and learning levels in one class. She stated that inclusive schools were under pressure in this regard. She called for greater intervention and proposed that solutions focus on both skill development and safety for the affected learners.
Advocacy for person-first language and inclusive education
Ms Williamson addressed the issue of Person-first Language and informed the Committee that its current terminology was outdated. She encouraged the Committee to adopt Person-first Language to avoid alienating persons with disabilities. Examples of outdated terms included “impaired/impairment,” “special needs,” and “able-bodied.”
She argued that children with disabilities should be considered in terms of their rights and not be isolated from society. She supported the need for intervention to improve access for children with disabilities and stressed that digital literacy was crucial, as most employment opportunities now required it.
Ms Williamson said that 70% of children with disabilities were not being admitted into schools. She recalled working with a child who had autism and low vision, who was rejected by several schools due to her disabilities. She proposed a transition to a more inclusive education system to move away from discrimination and added that attitudinal barriers remained a major obstacle.
She further asserted that despite possessing the necessary skills, persons with disabilities were often excluded from the labour market. She explained that Broad-Based Black Economic Empowerment (B-BBEE) impacted the employment of persons with disabilities. In her view, the 3% hiring target largely benefited African individuals, inadvertently excluding others and reinforcing discrimination. She observed that even those who were hired were often not supported in the workplace and were employed to fulfil quotas rather than to develop meaningful careers. She added that existing policies on workplace accessibility were not being effectively implemented.
She called for greater regulation of South African Sign Language (SASL) and expressed concern that qualified interpreters were not being employed, which negatively affected the Deaf community.

Briefing by Prof Gertrude Fester
Prof Gertrude Fester, Researcher and Writer, Feminist Forum, thanked the Committee for the opportunity to share her experiences as a Commissioner on gender equality. She explained that when she requested the briefing, she was informed that some Members might not know what the Commission on Gender Equality (CGE) is, and that she could provide some explanation. However, she asked if she could instead give a brief presentation covering the context and purpose of the Commission, which was accepted. Her presentation was structured in two parts, starting with a five-minute overview of the struggles since the 1980s, followed by an account of the Commission’s establishment and role. She noted that she would not be using PowerPoint but rather telling the (herstory) story.
Early activism and context
Prof Fester recounted her involvement with the United Nations Organisation in Cape Town in 1980, where she attended a meeting featuring a tape and slide presentation on women in Mozambique and their role in anti-colonial struggles. She highlighted how, after Mozambique's 1974 revolution, women were pushed back into domestic roles, a situation that political women activists in South Africa were determined to prevent in their own country. She emphasised the importance of ensuring women's participation at all decision-making levels, noting the significance of the Committee's work.
Intersectionality and challenges
She discussed her participation in the
Joint Monitoring Committee
on the quality of life and status of women and acknowledged the enormous scope of the Committee’s portfolio. Prof Fester explained the adoption of an intersectional approach considering race, class, gender, disability, and urban-rural divides. She expressed solidarity with the Committee’s challenges and offered her availability for ongoing interaction.
Women’s organisations and historical struggles
Prof Fester described her role as educational and training portfolio holder in the United Women’s Organisation. She reflected on the organisation’s historical roots in the 1950s, citing certain prominent figures and the racially inclusive nature of the movement. She noted the wide reach of the United Women’s Organisation, which had 6 000 members across numerous branches despite operating during apartheid. She also described collaboration among various women’s groups nationwide, including The Federation of South African Women (FEDSAW) formed in 1954, which united different racial and political organisations with a shared women’s charter.
Formation of alliances and coalitions
She explained the formation of the Women’s Alliance in 1991 following the unbanning of the ANC and other political organisations and the start of formal negotiations with the National Party government. She noted that the alliance sought to increase women’s representation in political discussions, given that early meetings had very few women participants. Prof Fester described the establishment of the Women’s National Coalition in 1992, which united women across diverse backgrounds and parties despite ongoing political violence, particularly in KwaZulu-Natal. She emphasised the challenge of maintaining unity among women amid such conflict.
Contributing to the Constitution
Prof Fester outlined the process of consulting grassroots women across churches, unions, and communities to determine what they wanted included in the new Constitution. She recalled how difficult it was initially to get women’s and human rights issues on the constitutional agenda. She observed that even some National Party women acknowledged the shift in language towards inclusivity during negotiations. She affirmed that the CGE, as one of the Chapter 9 institutions, emerged from these struggles.
Gender mainstreaming and monitoring challenges
She discussed the evolution of gender mainstreaming policies, explaining that instead of a women’s ministry, gender focal points were established in departments to monitor implementation. However, she found that many departments lacked these focal points or active engagement with gender issues. Prof Fester shared her experiences monitoring government departments, including difficulties in obtaining cooperation from local government officials, which sometimes required escalating matters to provincial premiers. She described a common pattern of male-dominated meetings and limited female participation beyond administrative roles, reflecting broader challenges within government structures.
Commission mandate and initial obstacles
Prof Fester detailed her term as a Commissioner from 2001 to 2006, explaining that the Commission faced significant challenges from the outset, including a one million Rand lawsuit and leadership disputes. She noted that the Gender Equality Act of 1996 was not fully aligned with the final Constitution, complicating the Commission’s work. She also described tensions with the Office on the Status of Women within the Presidency, arising from unclear division of responsibilities.
Notable work and impact
Prof Fester praised the early Commission’s work, highlighting a national conference on witchcraft that addressed rural women’s persecution under accusations of witchcraft and related property disputes. She explained how this conference contributed to research, legislation, and raising awareness about women’s rights. She reiterated the Commission’s dual national and provincial monitoring roles and her collaboration with other Commissioners to advance gender equality across levels of government.
The Chairperson thanked Prof Fester for sharing her insights. She remarked that it is important sometimes to look back in order to move forward, noting that many of the challenges Prof Fester had highlighted remain the same challenges currently faced by the CGE. She thanked Prof Fester and said the Committee would continue engaging with her.
Additional reflections on the National Strategic Plan (NSP) by SCIS
Ms Lisa Vetten, Research Associate, Southern Centre for Inequality Studies (Wits), structured her presentation around three key pillars of the NSP, focusing on the criminal justice system, response and support services, and women’s economic empowerment.
Data discrepancies and their implications
Ms Vetten highlighted significant discrepancies in femicide statistics that undermine efforts to address gender-based violence. She noted inconsistencies between police data and South African Medical Research Council (SAMRC) figures. The police annual report for 2022/23 indicated 3 914 women murdered, while quarterly reports suggested 4 169 cases, revealing a 45% discrepancy amounting to 1 739 unaccounted cases. The MRC’s mortuary data showed that 60% of femicides from 2020 to 2022 involved intimate partners, with concentrations in Gauteng and the Eastern Cape. She cautioned that such inconsistent data reporting hampers the ability to detect trends, including the disturbing rise in femicide rates since 2017 despite declines in male homicide rates.
Prosecution challenges and system performance
Ms Vetten discussed prosecution and conviction rates for sexual offences, observing a decline in cases set down for trial by the National Prosecuting Authority (NPA) from 8 173 in 2013/14 to 5 270 in 2023/24, despite fewer reported cases. Conviction rates improved modestly from 67% to 72% over the same period, but the reduced number of cases reaching trial raised concerns. She recommended shifting performance measures away from conviction rates alone towards tracking cases brought to trial, to avoid perverse incentives that might discourage prosecuting weaker cases.
Barriers to protection order processes
The presentation underscored systemic barriers in the protection order process. Ms Vetten cited Department of Justice and Constitutional Development (DOJCD) data from 2015/16 showing only 33% of 275 000 applications resulted in final orders. She attributed this to understaffed courts, budget austerity measures, and a lack of costed service standards, which collectively hamper access to justice for survivors.
Budget constraints and service delivery
Ms Vetten examined budget trends affecting shelter services. She noted a sharp decline in Department of Social Development (DSD) allocations, with shelter funding dropping from R55 million in 2021/22 to R23 million projected for 2024/25. Furthermore, R230 million was returned to the Treasury in 2023/24, including funds earmarked for victim-friendly rooms and Thuthuzela Care Centres (TCC). She warned of the damaging effects of underspending on essential support infrastructure.
Municipal bylaws and administrative harm
Focusing on Gauteng, Ms Vetten described how retroactive enforcement of municipal building bylaws led to shelter closures, displacing vulnerable women. Compliance costs reportedly reached up to R500 000, with administrative processes prioritising bureaucratic requirements over survivor needs. She termed this “administered harm” and called for more consistent and rights-centred application of regulations, including flexibility in business plan submissions.
Interdepartmental coordination and policy gaps
Ms Vetten emphasised the need for improved coordination across key government departments. She highlighted delays in implementing the Special Housing Needs Policy by the Department of Human Settlements (DHS), which affects survivors leaving shelters. The health sector’s lack of domestic violence response directives, as required by the Domestic Violence Act, was also noted. She further urged Cooperative Governance and Traditional Affairs (COGTA) to harmonise municipal bylaws to prevent arbitrary service denials.
Recommendations for reform
Ms Vetten recommended a thorough audit of police crime statistics to align data with MRC methodologies, thereby improving data integrity. She stressed the importance of budgeting reforms, including costing service delivery frameworks such as sexual offences courts, to facilitate accurate budget requests. She proposed enhanced oversight actions, including summoning the Public Protector to review compliance with gender-based violence service norms and investigating underspending in DSD and NPA programmes. Lastly, she called for interdepartmental task teams to address conflicting laws, such as municipal bylaws that conflict with the Children's Act.
Addressing administered harm
Ms Vetten concluded by reframing GBV as a governance crisis. She highlighted how bureaucratic rigidity (“administered harm”)—from retroactive bylaws to austerity cuts—denies rights and perpetuates vulnerability. She called for systemic reforms to align policies with the NSP’s objectives, stressing that data-driven oversight and interdepartmental coordination are critical to reversing femicide trends and economic precarity.

Briefing by the Centre for the Study of Violence and Reconciliation (CSVR)
Mr Athini Magodla, Gender Assistant & Community Facilitator, Centre for the Study of Violence and Reconciliation (CSVR), emphasised the critical importance of community engagement and relations within their work, noting that such engagement forms the foundation for most of their interventions and research. CSVR prides itself on having an extensive footprint in community relations across South Africa.
Overview
Mr Magodla provided a brief overview of CSVR, describing it as an independent non-governmental organisation established in 1989 with a mandate to understand and prevent violence, heal its effects, and build sustainable peace at both community and national levels. The organisation adopts a multidisciplinary approach incorporating research, advocacy, and psychosocial support, all informed by a gender lens.
He stressed that CSVR acknowledges the gendered nature of violence, recognising that experiences of violence differ significantly between men and women. This gender perspective guides the development of interventions tailored specifically to the needs of communities and individuals.
Research and programming focus
CSVR’s work spans research, advocacy, and psychosocial support. Its gender programme is responsible for mainstreaming a gender perspective throughout its activities. Research topics have included reproductive health, sexual violence, social cohesion, xenophobia, and studies on drivers of violence. Geographically, the organisation also engages in transitional justice efforts in the northern parts of Africa.
Recently, CSVR has focused on issues related to boys and masculinities, highlighting the need to address emerging themes such as toxic masculinities, which have become increasingly significant in conversations around gender-based violence (GBV) prevention and gender equality. The organisation recognises the importance of engaging men as allies and promoting positive masculinity.
An emerging area of interest for CSVR is technology-facilitated gender-based violence, which manifests within digital spaces and is difficult to address due to the intangible nature of online abuse and the anonymity of perpetrators.
Community-informed research and lived experiences
Mr Magodla emphasised that CSVR’s research is deeply informed by lived experiences within communities, which often provide insights that statistical data cannot capture. The organisation values its close relationships with community members and stakeholders, facilitating a reciprocal learning process.
In 2017, CSVR was among the first organisations to describe violence against women in South Africa as a crisis, which sparked public discussion. In 2022, it published a report entitled
Lived Experiences and Responses to Sexual and Gender-Based Violence (SGBV)
,
based on interviews with eight high-risk communities. The study highlighted the root causes of violence and the importance of community-centred interventions. It found that gender-based violence is frequently a symptom of broader structural violence.
Psychosocial support and healing trauma
CSVR’s psychosocial support work focuses on healing trauma through both individual and group interventions. One notable initiative is an intergenerational dialogue programme, which brings together women from different generations to share experiences and foster learning. This approach also contributes to building social cohesion, which strengthens community capacity to respond to and prevent violence.
The organisation also provides capacity training to grassroots organisations, including NGOs and police officers, to help them manage secondary or vicarious trauma. This support ensures that frontline workers can effectively assist communities without becoming overwhelmed.
Advocacy and policy work
CSVR’s advocacy efforts aim to influence laws and policies on gender-based violence and make these accessible to communities. The organisation works to simplify complex legislation into language and formats that community members can understand and use. This addresses the disconnect often found between national laws and their implementation at local levels.
Mr Magodla described CSVR’s trauma-informed healing approach as central to all its programmes. It recognises the historical and ongoing trauma shaping people’s lives, including how trauma can perpetuate cycles of violence unknowingly.
Community engagement and prevention strategies
Community engagement is fundamental to CSVR’s research and prevention work. The organisation works with communities to confront social and cultural norms that perpetuate violence, a process which is often sensitive and challenging.
Mr Magodla stressed that prevention work involves education, empowerment, and recognising the complexity of violence in communities. Violence has multiple causes and intersects with unemployment, hunger, poverty, lack of social services, entrenched inequality, and economic stress. Structural violence is often so normalised that community members struggle to make sense of it.
CSVR adopts an intersectional approach, acknowledging that violence is experienced differently depending on gender, race, and economic status. The organisation recognises there is no single solution to the multifaceted nature of violence.
Confronting cultural norms and harmful practices
One of the most difficult aspects of CSVR’s work is addressing cultural and social norms that enable violence. Mr Magodla gave the example of women in the Eastern Cape reporting how lobola (bride price) is weaponised against them, including in the context of marital rape and domestic abuse.
CSVR seeks to empower communities to question and change these harmful norms while respecting cultural contexts. The organisation supports communities in developing locally owned prevention strategies that reflect their realities and sensitivities.

Discussion
Ms Chauke-Adonis welcomed the presentations and acknowledged their focus on critical areas, particularly the lack of infrastructure and resources to support victims of GBV. She sought clarity on the sector-specific research referred to in the presentations. She stressed the importance of data collection and enquired about the number of gender-based violence and femicide (GBVF) cases involving persons with disabilities, noting that such cases were often under-reported.
She recalled that statistics released the previous year had indicated instances of GBVF against persons with disabilities, but she found those figures to be insufficient and inaccurate. She asked what research had been conducted on these cases and what interventions had been proposed. She also enquired how the Committee could support and amplify ongoing research.
She hoped educational initiatives around GBVF would reach men, women, and children. She emphasised the importance of monitoring National Treasury to ensure sufficient funds and resources were allocated. She added that the Department should exercise a stronger oversight role and suggested that the Committee could assist in fast-tracking the establishment of the Gender-Based Violence Council.
Dr Letlape raised concerns regarding the CGE and the challenges faced by women, particularly in relation to language barriers within national organisations. He questioned whether it was time to move away from political correctness and adopt a more pragmatic approach by using English as the universal language of instruction. He argued that English proficiency could unlock both social and economic opportunities, suggesting that the country should aim for bilingualism with English as a shared medium. He described English as being prevalent across sectors and stated that it was a human right to be proficient in the language.
Turning to the issue of unspent funds, Dr Letlape questioned whether non-profit organisations (NPOs) and non-governmental organisations (NGOs) would be better equipped if they possessed stronger business and safety management skills. He proposed line-item budgeting as a way to improve accountability and eliminate underspending. He stated that there should be no leniency for late applicants and that all organisations should comply with prescribed deadlines. He further asserted that NGO and NPO leaders needed to be appropriately qualified to manage government funds. Upholding the law, he said, was essential.
On intergenerational dialogues, he maintained that national laws should inform and reform institutions. He noted that although the Constitution and the Bill of Rights formed the foundation of South African democracy, the government continued to fund institutions that violated these principles. He cited the COGTA as an example of an institution that was in line with the Constitution and, therefore, deserved support. Conversely, he argued that institutions not aligned with constitutional values should no longer receive public funding and should be reformed.
Dr Letlape expressed concern about the traditional practice of
ukuthwala
,
which he described as patriarchal and harmful. He stated that such practices, which treated women as the property of men, should be condemned and eliminated, and not funded by the state. He also highlighted complaints from women that the
lobola (
Nguni dowry
)
system was being used against them. These customs, he believed, perpetuated GBV and should be reformed.
He sought advice from the presenters on the prevalence of demeaning language towards women and referred to a recent incident of Mr Macgyver “MacG” Mukwevho stating that it should be offensive to all women. He condemned the behaviour and stated that an apology should be issued to all women and to society as a whole. He said such conduct was unacceptable in a democratic society and that toxic masculinity was deeply embedded in certain cultural practices. This, he argued, was not compatible with the values of democracy.
Ms Rweqana commented on the CGE and reflected on its feminist origins, which had focused on securing successes such as gender-responsive budgeting and the Commission’s Chapter 9 mandate. However, she acknowledged the persistent challenges rooted in structural sexism and tensions. She pointed to ongoing concerns, including unclear roles of the Office on the Status of Women (OSW), misalignment between the CGE Act 39 of 1996 and the Constitution, and limited resources. She questioned what timelines were in place to ensure the alignment of the amended Act with the Constitution and the clarification of OSW’s mandate.
She asked how National Treasury planned to address CGE’s chronic funding shortfalls and the salary parity gap. She sought information on how many of CGE’s recommendations had led to legislative amendments and what follow-up mechanisms were in place. She also asked what disciplinary processes existed for addressing concerns about Commissioners’ political loyalties, which she said compromised their oversight role. She enquired how the Department of Women, Youth and Persons with Disabilities (DWPYD) would measure the CGE’s impact in reducing GBV. She clarified that she was raising these questions as a Member of the Opposition and not as part of the Government of National Unity (GNU) and asserted her right to ask questions freely.
The Chairperson responded that Ms Rweqana was entitled to ask questions but reminded Members that they shared collective responsibility for advocating on behalf of women, youth, and persons with disabilities. She noted that, in a previous meeting, Ms Vetten stated that changes had been implemented and that there were no payment issues facing NGOs. However, the Chairperson questioned the accuracy of this assertion in light of the half a billion that had been returned to National Treasury. She accused Ms Vetten of misleading Parliament and criticised her dismissive attitude towards the Committee’s concerns about challenges facing NPOs and NGOs.
She recalled that during a prior engagement with the Department, a question had been raised regarding accountability for departments failing to meet the targets of the NSP. The response, she recalled, was that departments were not holding one another accountable. She asked whether departments were fulfilling their roles and whether the NSP was a well-designed plan capable of addressing GBV for the remainder of the current term. Referring to statistics from the justice system, she asked whether the NSP would lead to improved prosecution of rapists. She directed a question to Prof Fester regarding the CGE, asking whether it was meeting its targets and fulfilling its mandate. If not, she asked whether the problem lay in funding or in leadership.
Response
Data gaps and disaggregation challenges
Prof Fester said she had learnt a great deal from the presentations and reiterated concerns about the lack of disaggregated data, particularly on persons with disabilities in rural and township settings. She noted that African data was often skewed, minimal, or inaccessible and called for stronger partnerships between the SAMRC, Statistics South Africa (Stats SA), and universities. She stressed that how funding was used was critical, despite its availability, and encouraged greater institutional collaboration for effective data sharing.
Language, inclusion, and cultural balance
On language, Prof Fester warned against the marginalisation of South Africa's official and unofficial languages. She suggested scholarships for writing in indigenous languages could help foster linguistic diversity and proposed a balanced, pragmatic approach to language and culture in education and policymaking.
Service delivery failures and unspent funds
Prof Fester attributed unspent funds to both departments and NGOs. She recalled R600 million being returned by the KwaZulu-Natal Department of Education (KZN DoE), despite extreme need. She called for improved service delivery, accountability, and the withdrawal of funding from institutions failing to meet constitutional obligations. She also advocated for community dialogues to address toxic masculinity.
CGE challenges and engagement with communities
Referring to the amended CGE Act of 2013, Prof Fester noted unresolved salary parity issues and acknowledged the institution's ongoing difficulties. She confirmed that submissions and sexual harassment cases had been escalated to Parliament without resolution. She praised CGE's use of community radio in indigenous languages and recommended continued media engagement.
Disability, violence and the justice system
Ms Vetten confirmed that data on women with disabilities remained inadequate and that studies often failed to differentiate between disability types. She highlighted a programme by Cape Mental Health that supported court readiness for women with intellectual disabilities. She stressed that many rape cases involving persons with disabilities did not proceed due to assumptions about witness reliability.
Underspending and mismanagement
Ms Vetten clarified that unspent funds came from departments, not NGOs, citing R500 million returned to Treasury in Gauteng. She detailed late payments, contract delays, and rigid Departmental expectations. She criticised classifying NGO proposals as business plans and opposed costly compliance standards not yet embedded in law.
National Strategic Plan (NSP) limitations
Responding to a prompt from the Chairperson, Ms Vetten argued that the NSP's success was hampered by limited administrative capacity, poor infrastructure, and weak interdepartmental coordination. She called for data-informed, context-specific targets and a shift toward addressing socioeconomic drivers of violence rather than solely focusing on gender norms. She also urged departments to make better use of the Intergovernmental Relations Framework and supported more effective use of the CGE's subpoena powers.
Reflections on GBV Council and CGE
Ms Vetten said the GBV Council lacked full statutory authority and functioned mainly as a board, which limited its impact. The Chairperson echoed concerns, stating that a stronger CGE was required, particularly if the Council remained underfunded.
Context-specific provincial responses
Mr Magodla agreed with much of the discussion and stressed that provinces required tailored approaches. He proposed provincial-level engagements and pointed to mistrust in SAPS, especially among women discouraged from reporting GBV due to systemic failures. He questioned what role correctional services played in rehabilitation and highlighted patriarchy as a continuing barrier. He called on government structures to stay meaningfully engaged with communities.
Closing remarks
The Chairperson said it was disheartening to hear that many women were unable to access justice, which could discourage them from reporting GBV cases. She stated that the previous Parliament had erred in assigning NSP oversight to only one Portfolio Committee and suggested this matter be revisited in the following week’s meeting.
She thanked all presenters, stakeholders, and support staff, and said the Committee had gained valuable insights. She confirmed that the next meeting would take place physically and that the issues raised would be further considered, as significant work remained.
The meeting was adjourned.
