2025/26 Annual Performance Plans
The Committee met to receive a briefing from the Department of Employment and Labour on its Strategic Plan for 2025-2030 and Annual Performance Plan for 2025/26. The Deputy Minister was present for the engagement.
The Department highlighted its extensive service delivery footprint across all nine provinces and detailed its plans to address the significant challenge of unemployment, which stood at an official rate of 32.9 percent and an expanded rate of 43 percent. It outlined its Vision 2030, aiming for 3.5 percent GDP growth and a reduction in unemployment to 27.5 percent, with a target of creating two million work opportunities through its Public Employment Services.
The presentation also covered the Department's alignment with national development priorities and its seven strategic interventions across various programmes, including Public Employment Services, Inspection and Enforcement, Labour Policy and Industrial Relations, and Administration. Key targets for the upcoming financial year were presented for each programme, alongside details on planned infrastructure projects.
The Committee expressed considerable concern regarding the persistent dysfunction of the Unemployment Insurance Fund and the Compensation Fund, noting a lack of detailed turnaround strategies and questioning the removal of critical performance indicators from the Annual Performance Plan, which members felt could limit parliamentary oversight. There were also questions about the impact of digitisation on job losses in the Department, the effectiveness of the national minimum wage in the context of rising unemployment, and the Department's enforcement of employer compliance with UIF regulations.
Members sought clarity on the provincial breakdown of job creation figures, the sustainability of employment opportunities, and the Department's approach to filling specialist vacancies and promoting internal staff. Concerns were also raised about the sluggish economy, the high cost of living, and the need for more inspectors to combat non-compliance with labour laws. Some members questioned the continued use of race-based empowerment policies, considering the Government of National Unity's stated commitment to merit and inclusivity.
In response, the Department acknowledged the challenges, including the exodus of skilled individuals and the need for improved systems. It committed to providing detailed job placement figures and affirmed that the private sector was the largest employer, with ongoing labour law reforms aimed at simplifying business and balancing interests.
The Department stated that digitisation would focus on reskilling workers and that the national minimum wage, aligned with international standards, had not caused job losses. It noted that there were provisions for exemptions. It highlighted the prioritisation of women, youth, and persons with disabilities across all programmes. It acknowledged system challenges in rural areas, particularly in the Eastern Cape, with new systems being rolled out.
Accountability was stressed as non-negotiable, with a focus on filling vacancies and addressing internal progression.
The Department welcomed a recent national discourse on employment data and confirmed that labour market reforms were being pursued to attract investment and support business sustainability through public-private partnerships. It also announced a "Project 2 000" initiative to recruit inspector interns in collaboration with the International Labour Organisation to bolster enforcement capacity.
The Deputy Minister acknowledged the concerns regarding the Unemployment Insurance Fund and Compensation Fund. He said significant work was underway to unbundle these entities by 2027 so that they could operate more efficiently. He committed to providing a detailed presentation on the progress, including claims data.
The Committee acknowledged the Department's vision but cautioned about a growing credibility gap between promises and delivery. It emphasised the expectation of measurable, monitored, and credible implementation, with accountability for failures. The Committee appreciated the comprehensive responses provided by the Deputy Minister, noting the depth of the engagement.

Deputy Minister’s opening remarks
Deputy Minister Sibiya began by acknowledging that the unemployment statistics reflected real lives and human suffering, not just numbers. He agreed with the Chairperson that the figures must be approached with empathy and seriousness.
He noted that the meeting took place in the aftermath of devastating floods in the Eastern Cape, particularly in the town of Mthatha. He stressed the need for all levels of government and society to begin addressing the broader and worsening impacts of climate change, especially as inclement weather increasingly damages infrastructure and economic stability across provinces like the Western Cape, Eastern Cape, and KwaZulu-Natal.
In solidarity, the Minister of Employment and Labour had visited affected communities to provide support, not just a symbolic presence. The Deputy Minister urged that such tragedies should not be ignored and highlighted the need to build infrastructure that is resilient to climate change.
He then addressed recent public debate following comments by the CEO of Capitec Bank, who had questioned the credibility of South Africa's unemployment statistics. The Deputy Minister welcomed such debates as a sign of healthy democracy and critical reflection, but clarified that the current metrics were based on International Labour Organisation (ILO) methodologies and used globally. However, he acknowledged their limitations, especially in economies like South Africa's with high informality and discouraged job seekers.
He emphasised that the ILO itself recognised these flaws, and South Africa must strive for a fuller and more nuanced understanding of its unemployment landscape.
The Deputy Minister noted that this public discourse coincided with the 2025 International Labour Conference (ILC) in Geneva, where South Africa played a visible leadership role. He said this was deliberate and rooted in constitutional and developmental values, with South Africa sharing its experiences in labour market transitions and public employment initiatives.
Turning to the automotive sector, he expressed concern about the announcement by Goodyear to close its operations in the Eastern Cape after 70 years. He said the Department was aware of the profound impact on workers and families, citing past examples like Ford and VW retrenchments that left many destitute.
In response, the DEL was engaging with provincial teams and social partners to explore immediate interventions while also working on long-term strategies for industrial revitalisation. The Department remained committed to job preservation and economic sustainability.
He warned of potential relocations of major players such as Audi and Mercedes-Benz, citing increasing global competition, particularly from Chinese manufacturers. He urged the country to consider whether it should revise its industrial policies, including localisation and trade protection, to secure South Africa’s industrial base and protect workers.
The Deputy Minister then outlined the Department's alignment with the seventh administration's principles of ethical leadership, unity, participatory democracy, and social justice. He listed seven strategic departmental priorities:
Strengthening labour market regulation and fairness.
Enhancing coordination of employment interventions across government.
Promoting ethical leadership and governance within the Department and its entities.
Improving citizen-centred service delivery.
Investing in institutional capacity and performance management.
Marketing the Department's services to improve accessibility.
Repositioning the Department as a central player in job creation and inclusive economic growth.
He acknowledged operational challenges within the UIF and Compensation Fund but assured the Committee that the Department was making progress and was actively reforming these entities.
He detailed how the Department had developed a process plan aligned with the 2025 State of the Nation Address, focusing on three core priorities: inclusive economic growth and job creation, poverty reduction and cost of living relief, and building a capable and ethical developmental state.
The National Employment Policy, a flagship intervention, was nearing completion. It aimed to ensure that economic growth led to job creation, social inclusion, and labour absorption, rather than mere capital accumulation.
He highlighted the National Pathway Management Network (NPMN), coordinated by the Presidency and run operationally by Harambee, which had registered over 5 million young people. The programme had placed 1.57 million youth into earning opportunities, 70 percent of whom were women. He stressed the Department's focus on deliberately uplifting women, given their historical disadvantage.
He reiterated the Department's goal of creating 2 million jobs by 2030, broken down as follows:
650 000 jobs through collaboration between the Industrial Development Corporation (IDC), NPMN, and Public Employment Services (PES).
350 000 jobs through PES alone.
1 million jobs via the UIF, including 140 000 education assistant jobs and 860,000 through labour activation programmes. A national strategic framework for public employment programmes was being developed in partnership with academics and the ILO, with pilot implementation expected in 2026.
He reiterated that the Department's vision was grounded in decent work and inclusive economic growth. He emphasised the need for ethical, accountable public service and better implementation of policies so workers could see tangible benefits, such as fairer wages, safer workplaces, and more stability.
The briefing, he noted, came at a time of mounting socio-economic pressure, including persistently high unemployment, inequality, and cost-of-living concerns. Yet, he expressed optimism that these challenges could be turned into opportunities for reform and stronger state capacity.
In conclusion, the Deputy Minister said the Department had developed a comprehensive strategic plan responsive to these crises and committed to bold, practical interventions. He stated that this was not just about programmes or numbers, but about rebuilding the dignity of work and advancing a

DEL Strategic Plan 2025-2030 and Annual Performance Plan 2025/26
The DEL presented its strategic and annual plans, outlining efforts to address unemployment and contribute to national development goals. Key highlights of the presentation were:
Service delivery footprint
The DEL maintained a wide service delivery footprint across all nine provinces of South Africa. This included provincial offices, labour centres, satellite offices, Thusong Service Centres, visiting points, self-help kiosks, and mobile employment centres or buses, ensuring broad access to services for employers, employees, the unemployed, and other beneficiaries. A cross-cutting focus was placed on women, youth, and persons with disabilities.
Major challenges: Unemployment
A significant challenge highlighted was persistent unemployment:
Provincial unemployment rates (official and expanded) showed considerable variation.
The North West province recorded the highest expanded unemployment rate at 51.5 percent in Q3:2024.
Causal factors for youth unemployment included:
Pathways: Difficulties for young people to access the labour market due to lack of work experience.
Sectors: Low growth in sectors that traditionally absorbed a large workforce.
Skills: Poor education outcomes and a mismatch between available skills and industry demand.
SMEs: Limited opportunities for self-employment and small and medium enterprise growth.
Despite economic growth over the past 16 years, fewer young people were employed in 2024 than in 2008. Only 40 percent of new labour market entrants found steady employment, with the remainder joining the long-term unemployed.
Vision 2030: Inclusive growth and job creation
The DEL's Vision for 2030 aimed for a thriving South African economy with inclusive job opportunities and sustainable growth.
Targets included achieving 3.5 percent GDP growth and reducing the unemployment rate to 27.5 percent by 2030.
The focus would be on economic diversification and preparing the workforce for future industries, including digital and green economy skills.
The DEL specifically targeted creating 2 million work opportunities by 2030 through its public employment services.
The outlook for South Africa's GDP growth in 2025 was broadly positive, with predictions ranging from 1.2 percent to 2.2 percent.
Alignment with Medium Term Development Plan (MTDP) priorities
The DEL's plans aligned with three key MTDP Priorities for 2025-2030:
Priority 1: Drive Inclusive growth and job creation.
Priority 2: Reduce Poverty and tackle the high cost of living.
Priority 3: Build a capable, ethical, and developmental state.
The Department had identified seven strategic priorities to guide its interventions:
Strengthen regulatory capabilities of the Department.
Coordination of employment interventions within government through collaborations, partnerships, and cooperations.
Good corporate governance.
Improved service delivery.
Strengthen the institutional capacity of the Department.
Massify the marketing of the departmental services and programmes.
Advance the new employment mandate of the Department.
Programme 3: Public employment services (PES)
This programme focused on creating sustainable jobs, particularly for youth.
Annual Performance Plan (APP) 2025/26 targets included:
1 000 000 work-seekers registered on Employment Services of South Africa.
120 000 employment opportunities registered.
270 000 registered work-seekers provided with employment counselling.
70 000 registered employment opportunities filled by registered work-seekers.
Nine jobs or career fairs conducted in provinces.
A National Employment Policy (NEP) and Employment Services Amendment Bill (ESAB) were being finalised for approval.
Programme 2: Inspection and enforcement services (IES)
This programme aimed to reduce poverty and improve livelihoods by protecting workers' rights.
APP 2025/26 targets included:
298 104 employers or workplaces inspected to determine compliance with employment law.
95 percent of non-compliant employers served with notices within 14 calendar days.
65 percent of non-compliant employers referred for prosecution within 30 working days if non-compliant after notice.
Six formal advocacy sessions conducted to increase awareness of employment law.
Programme 4: Labour policy and industrial relations
This program focused on reducing poverty, improving livelihoods, and mainstreaming gender, youth, and people with disabilities empowerment.
APP 2025/26 targets included:
Review and approval of the National Minimum Wage by 1 March each year.
Publication of annual employment equity (EE) reports and public registers.
Submission of progress reports on bilateral cooperation and multilateral obligations.
100 percent of labour organisations' applications for registration approved or refused within 90 working days.
Production of research reports on the impact of labour legislation and employment creation programs.
Production of labour market trend reports.
Programme 1: Administration
This programme focused on building a capable, ethical, and developmental state, and ensuring digital transformation and a secured cyberspace.
Key APP 2025/26 targets included:
80 percent implementation of the departmental project plan for digital transformation.
80 percent implementation of the Centre of Internet Security (CIS) controls project plan for cybersecurity.
90 percent of funded vacancies filled within eight months.
80 percent of consequence management cases related to misconduct finalised within 90 days.
100 percent of invoices paid within 30 days of receipt.
Procurement targets for goods and services from women (40 percent), youth (30 percent), and persons with disabilities (7.0 percent).
40 communication activities aimed at increasing visibility and awareness of DEL services.
Infrastructure projects
The presentation outlined various infrastructure projects with estimated costs, including:
Refurbishment and construction of Labour Centres.
Repair and renovation of Supported Employment Enterprises (SEE) factories.
Installation of water tanks and generators across different provinces.

Deputy Minister's responses
Deputy Minister Sibiya thanked the Chairperson and Members for the robust engagement, guidance, and strategic input. He emphasised the Department's appreciation for the seriousness with which the Committee approached its work and assured Members that the Department did not take its interactions with the Committee lightly. The Department ensured it came well-prepared with credible and practical content for presentations.
Responding to Mr Pienaar, the Deputy Minister acknowledged the point raised about the exodus of skills from South Africa, which he said was not unique to South Africa but part of a global phenomenon affecting many countries. Migration of skilled labour, particularly in the context of global labour markets and international laws, remained a challenge. However, while the country was losing some skilled individuals to other countries, the situation was not at a crisis level. He pointed out that South Africa retained a strong internal capacity to develop skills and that it was important to continue creating employment opportunities domestically to retain those skills.
In relation to the request for placement figures in the public and private sectors, he noted that such data was available but that the Department would prefer to submit a written response to ensure accuracy rather than provide estimates or "thumbsuck" figures during the meeting. He confirmed that significant work had been done in this area.
The Deputy Minister addressed a broader concern raised by several Members, stating that while the government was the most visible employer, it was in fact not the largest contributor to employment in the country. That role was played by the private sector, which was why the Department was actively engaging in partnerships with the private sector to stimulate employment.
Part of this effort included labour law reforms, as presented by the Deputy Director-General (DDG), Mr Thembinkosi Mkalipi. These reforms aimed to simplify doing business in South Africa and encourage profitability and expansion in the private sector, thereby enabling job creation. At the same time, the Department remained committed to protecting workers and balancing the interests of employers and employees. These reforms had progressed through Cabinet and would be tabled in Parliament shortly. He encouraged Committee Members to actively participate in shaping them.
Deputy Minister Sibiya stated that the Department was engaged in comprehensive labour law reforms, covering the Basic Conditions of Employment Act, Labour Relations Act, Occupational Health and Safety Act, and migration policy. These reforms aimed to address the core issues raised by the Committee – making laws that eased business investment and growth, while simultaneously protecting workers.
He noted that many laws were enacted between 1994 and 2002, and global changes since then required updating the legal framework to fit new realities. The Department was working closely with organised labour, business, and civil society in developing these reforms, which were due to be presented to Parliament soon, with an invitation for Committee input.
On the topic of digitalisation and job losses, the Deputy Minister acknowledged the concern, but emphasised that technological advancement was unavoidable given the ongoing fourth industrial revolution and current developments in artificial intelligence. However, he assured the Committee that the Department did not anticipate job losses due to digitisation. Instead, there would be a focus on reskilling workers to protect employment.
He highlighted Productivity SA, an entity within the Department, which collaborated with companies to help avoid job losses and improve profitability. The Department also coordinated with the UIF and the Commission for Conciliation, Mediation and Arbitration (CCMA) to protect jobs. It recognised the challenges in sectors such as automotive, but was striving to mitigate losses.
Regarding the national minimum wage, the Deputy Minister explained that it was a policy aligned with international labour standards, particularly those set by the ILO, aimed at protecting the most vulnerable workers at the lower end of the labour market. He clarified that the minimum wage legislation included a provision for exemptions where employers could apply to pay less than the minimum wage under valid, credible circumstances. This flexibility was designed to strike a balance between protecting workers and enabling businesses to survive.
He emphasised that exploitation of workers was unacceptable and unprofitable in the long term. The minimum wage had helped improve the standard of living for many South Africans, and investigations indicated it had not caused job losses. The Deputy Minister promised that further data on the positive impact of the minimum wage would be provided by DDG Mkalipi.
In response to Ms Ndlangisa-Nodada, the Deputy Minister acknowledged the prioritisation of inclusive growth and job creation, particularly for women, youth, and people with disabilities. These groups were strategically targeted across all sectors and reflected in specific programmes such as the entity, Supported Employment Enterprises (SEE), which exclusively employed people with disabilities. He assured the Committee that these targets were not mere numbers but part of a strong commitment.
Deputy Minister Sibiya emphasised the potential of SEE, which comprised state-owned manufacturing entities under the DEL that provided employment specifically for people with disabilities. SEE operated in eight provinces, with efforts underway to establish operations in Mpumalanga.
These entities manufactured goods such as school desks, office furniture, hospital linen, hotel linen, and uniforms. The Deputy Minister called on the Committee to assist in advocating for all government departments – national, provincial, and municipal – to procure from SEE, particularly in sectors like education and health. Such procurement would allow for the creation of sustainable and permanent employment opportunities for people with disabilities. He appealed directly to the Chairperson and Members, highlighting that a government-wide commitment to local procurement from SEE could significantly reduce unemployment among persons with disabilities.
Regarding women and youth, the Deputy Minister stated that all departmental programmes prioritised their inclusion. He referenced the Labour Activation Programme (LAP), which focused on partnerships with private companies for the purposes of permanent employment creation, skills development and training. He assured Members that women, youth, and persons with disabilities were at the top of the Department's strategic priorities across all interventions.
He acknowledged the feedback on the Bethlehem office. He reiterated that workers who approached labour offices were often in distress, seeking access to UIF and Compensation Fund benefits, or other essential services. Therefore, it was critical that all offices operated at optimal capacity. He noted that the Department was working hard to ensure full functionality across all provinces, although some areas – particularly in the Eastern Cape – still faced infrastructural and systems compatibility issues.
He had recently conducted an inspection in Lusikisiki and confirmed that rural areas in the Eastern Cape faced the most significant system challenges, although urban centres were faring better. The Department was investing resources into resolving these issues and had introduced a new system across all offices, which had already improved service delivery. The Department was conducting active oversight to ensure responsiveness and resolve challenges without delay.
On the question of UIF enforcement, he confirmed that the Inspection and Enforcement Services (IES) branch played a central role in ensuring compliance with all labour legislation. He admitted that non-compliance remained a serious challenge across the country, noting a general culture of disregarding laws, even among employers with the capacity to comply.
Despite this, the Department was responding firmly. Inspectors were conducting daily inspections across various sectors, including farming, iron and steel, hospitality, and others. He added that he personally conducted inspections every Friday to reinforce the Department's seriousness about enforcement.
He reiterated the Department's commitment to enforcing all labour laws, including the UIF Act, Compensation for Occupational Injuries and Diseases Act, Basic Conditions of Employment Act, National Minimum Wage Act, and Occupational Health and Safety Act.
The Deputy Minister acknowledged that the UIF system faced internal challenges that hampered enforcement. However, system upgrades and reforms were underway to ensure greater agility and efficiency in future enforcement efforts.
He echoed earlier sentiments expressed by the Chairperson, emphasising that if employment creation and economic reform were not addressed urgently, future generations would suffer the consequences. He described the present generation as being duty-bound to arrest and resolve current socio-economic challenges and reiterated the Department's commitment to this responsibility.
He acknowledged a Member’s request for provincial breakdowns of job creation figures and apologised for their omission. He assured the Committee that future engagements would include data disaggregated by province.
Regarding vacancies, the Deputy Minister stated that the Department had advertised 298 posts which would all be filled without re-advertising. He undertook to investigate concerns raised by Mr Modise, particularly regarding the Deputy Director: Labour Centre post and internal upward mobility, stating that qualified internal staff should be afforded opportunities for progression, thereby opening lower-level posts for others.
He addressed Mr Kennedy's input, agreeing that the state could not be the only driver of employment. The Department was creating a conducive labour market and business environment to encourage investment and job creation, both externally and domestically. The goal was to ensure local companies could also grow, thrive, and employ, thereby reducing poverty and dependency on social grants and redirecting funds toward service delivery and infrastructure.
He admitted the Department's technology systems were outdated and inefficient, but confirmed ongoing efforts to revamp, reimagine, and modernise digital infrastructure. The new systems would improve service delivery turnaround times; enhance auditability; and strengthen anti-corruption capabilities.
He offered to present the Department's technology plans to the Committee and stated the goal is to enable job seekers to register remotely, reducing the need to visit labour centres.
On the issue of accountability, the Deputy Minister was unequivocal: "It can't be business as usual." He noted that everyone in the Department was expected to account for their actions and decisions. Consequence management was non-negotiable. Accountability started with the Minister, Deputy Minister, and Director General.
He addressed Mr Farmer’s concerns regarding the similarities in strategic plans presented previously. He acknowledged this but noted there were substantive differences across plans.
On concerns about labour reforms potentially deterring investment, he reassured Members that the Department was striking a careful balance between protecting workers, creating a business-friendly environment, and fostering inclusive economic growth.
Deputy Minister Sibiya stressed that reforms were being developed in consultation with organised labour, business, and community stakeholders, and were not being imposed unilaterally. He affirmed that the labour law reforms underway were necessary and would support economic progress and inclusivity.
Responding to Mr Farmer’s remarks on the Capitec CEO's recent comments regarding employment data, the Deputy Minister described it as a necessary national discourse. While noting that Statistics South Africa remained the custodian of national employment statistics, he welcomed the debate as healthy and essential, particularly when new perspectives were introduced. He acknowledged that:
The Capitec CEO's comments stemmed from deep familiarity with South Africa’s informal labour market, given Capitec’s large client base at the lower end of the income spectrum.
There were valid critiques of existing statistical approaches, including those endorsed by the ILO, which themselves acknowledged inherent limitations.
The Department was monitoring the discourse closely and was open to reform and improvement if such discussions led to practical policy shifts.
On Mr Mabilo's concern about a sluggish economy and critical sector stagnation, the Deputy Minister confirmed this assessment and reaffirmed that this was the reason for the Department's pursuit of labour market and policy reforms. These reforms aimed to attract investment; support business sustainability; ensure worker protections; and facilitate growth through public-private partnerships (PPPs)
He emphasised that PPPs were a central component of the government's economic strategy, in line with the President’s directive, noting that these reforms were being developed in coordination with key departments such as Trade, Industry and Competition.
On the sustainability of created jobs, the Deputy Minister said temporary, government-initiated employment – such as the Expanded Public Works Programme (EPWP) and Public Employment Programmes (PEPs) – served as stopgap measures during high unemployment periods. There were ongoing efforts to develop exit strategies for EPWP beneficiaries to avoid situations where individuals completed multi-year stints in temporary roles without acquiring marketable skills or access to permanent employment.
Reforms were being implemented to ensure clear exit pathways; linkages to skills development and entrepreneurial opportunities; and redirection from passive workfare to active job creation and employability enhancement.
On skills development, the Deputy Minister stated that the Department focused on practical, employment-oriented training, with a move away from training for certificates alone. This included support for entrepreneurship development; work-readiness training; and job-creating skill sets, particularly through the Labour Activation Programme (LAP)
The Deputy Minister admitted that the Department did not have enough inspectors to enforce compliance with labour laws effectively. South Africa had millions of businesses but only about 2 000 inspectors. Financial constraints hindered the hiring of additional inspectors. Inspectors had to be highly skilled, including legal and engineering professionals. They required approximately two years of training before being fully competent due to the legal sensitivity and complexity of the work. He urged the Committee to assist by advocating increased National Treasury funding to expand the national inspectorate.
On a positive note, the Deputy Minister announced the DEL’s Project 20,000 initiative:
The Department was in the process of recruiting 20 000 inspector interns, split across two years, 10 000 in the current financial year and 10 000 in the next. These interns would bolster compliance monitoring and enforcement capacities across sectors. The initiative was being implemented in collaboration with the ILO and aligned with international labour standards.
The Deputy Minister acknowledged the Chairperson's concerns about the performance of the UIF and the Compensation Fund (CF), noting that the Department shared these concerns. He said significant work was currently underway to reform these two entities. The Department was in the process of unbundling the UIF and CF to operate as more independent and agile structures, in the way that the SA Revenue Service (SARS) functioned. While they would remain entities of the Department, the intention was to allow them to function more efficiently under a semi-autonomous model. These reforms were being driven by the DEL's Project Management Office and were expected to be completed by 2027.
The Deputy Minister emphasised that this unbundling process would enable the two entities to deliver services more efficiently and with greater flexibility, regulated under a framework that facilitated operational improvement. He committed the Department to returning to the Select Committee with a detailed presentation outlining the unbundling progress, including timelines for each phase and consultations with relevant stakeholders. This would cover when the proposals were expected to be submitted to Parliament and Cabinet for approval.
He further stressed that the funds held by the UIF and CF did not belong to the Department but to workers. Therefore, there was a duty to ensure that those funds were invested in a way that improved service delivery and access for beneficiaries. Rather than prioritising external investment returns through vehicles like the Public Investment Corporation (PIC), the Department was now deliberately investing internally to enhance the efficiency and responsiveness of the UIF and CF systems. The goal was to ensure that workers could access their benefits promptly, without unnecessary delays or distress.
On the matter of delayed claims, the Deputy Minister noted that the Department was not able to provide the proportion of claims older than six months during the current session. However, he committed to formally submitting a breakdown of claims data to the Committee, starting from 14 days, 30 days, and upwards. He declined to give an estimate offhand, citing the risk of providing inaccurate figures and emphasising the Department's commitment to maintaining ethical standards and accountability.
In his closing remarks, Deputy Minister Sibiya expressed appreciation to the Chairperson and Members of the Select Committee for their guidance and oversight. He noted that the inputs received during the engagement were valuable, offering alternative perspectives that assisted the Department in reassessing its approach and identifying additional possibilities for improvement. He reiterated that the Department did not see itself as the sole source of wisdom on labour matters, and that parliamentary input played a vital role in shaping and strengthening the Department's policies and operations.
He concluded by inviting his team to contribute further insights, if necessary.
DEL responses
Ms Aggy Moiloa, Deputy Director-General: Inspection and Enforcement Services, DEL, elaborated on the Department's role in enforcing compliance with UIF regulations. She explained that inspections focused on ensuring employers were registered with the UIF and were submitting monthly declarations as required by law. These declarations must indicate the number of employees and the corresponding contributions made to the fund. This process ensured that employers remained accountable, and that workers' rights were protected.
From the perspective of workers, Ms Moiloa noted that the most important step was confirming registration with the UIF. The simplest method for doing so was to check their payslips for UIF deductions. She emphasised that, by law, even if an employer failed to pay over those deductions to the UIF, workers would not be penalised. The presence of a UIF deduction on a payslip was considered sufficient proof of registration, thereby safeguarding the worker's entitlement to UIF benefits.
In addition, Ms Moiloa addressed the issue of inspector capacity. She highlighted that inspector training was an ongoing and evolving process, particularly as labour market conditions changed and the Department introduced new service delivery models. Inspectors were regularly updated through a combination of mentoring, coaching, workshops, and conferences. These capacity-building initiatives ensured that inspectors were adequately prepared to respond to emerging trends and legal reforms in the labour market. She concluded her input by aligning with the Deputy Minister's emphasis on the importance of legal reforms.
Mr Thembinkosi Mkalipi, Deputy Director-General: Labour Policy and Industrial Relations, addressed two key issues for clarification. Firstly, regarding the impact of the national minimum wage on employment levels, he emphasised that the research referenced by the Deputy Minister was not conducted by the Department itself, but by the Development Policy Research Unit, a reputable research institute affiliated with the University of Cape Town. This body had conducted ongoing research over the past six years, consistently finding no evidence that the national minimum wage had had a negative impact on employment. Mr Mkalipi offered to make this research available to the Committee, should they wish to be briefed on the findings in greater detail.
Secondly, he responded to concerns about whether South Africa’s transformation laws, particularly those related to employment equity, amounted to discrimination. Mr Mkalipi clarified that, in terms of the Employment Equity Act, the first and most fundamental requirement for any job applicant was whether they were suitably qualified for the position. This assessment was entirely independent of race or gender. He stressed that no provision in the law required employers to hire individuals who did not meet the necessary qualifications. Transformation measures might be applied thereafter, but merit remained the first and foremost consideration in recruitment.
He concluded by noting that the Deputy Minister had already comprehensively addressed most of the other issues raised.
Chairperson’s closing remarks
The Chairperson thanked the Deputy Minister and his delegation for the comprehensive responses provided during the engagement. The Chairperson noted with appreciation that it was the first time in their experience that a Deputy Minister had responded to all questions posed by the Committee, a gesture that was acknowledged and valued by all Members present. The Deputy Minister and his team were excused as the Committee proceeded to address internal matters.
Other business
The Committee then turned its attention to the adoption of eight sets of minutes. However, given the absence of several Members, including Mr Farmer, and acknowledging that some Members had not attended all the meetings, Ms Ndlangisa-Nodada proposed that the adoption of the minutes be deferred to a future meeting. She further suggested that future meetings begin promptly at 0900 to allow time for such administrative matters. The Chairperson agreed and requested the Committee Secretariat to confirm the scheduling and ensure there were no clashes with other meetings. She confirmed that adjustments could be made, provided there were no conflicts with existing Committee engagements.
The Chairperson concluded by thanking Members for their robust engagement and reminded them to enjoy the upcoming question session with the President.
The meeting was adjourned.
