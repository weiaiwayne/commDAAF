Meeting Summary
Video
The Portfolio Committee on Tourism met to consider and adopt its 2025-29 Strategic Plan, 2025/26 Annual Performance Plan (APP), and the Draft Third Term Programme (October 2025 – March 2026).
The Committee Content Advisor outlined how the revised Strategic Plan and APP incorporated Member inputs from the previous week. Priorities included strengthening oversight of municipal infrastructure grants for tourism, expanding oversight visits and stakeholder engagement, supporting niche markets such as agro-, sports, cruise, and wellness tourism, and promoting digital transformation and monitoring tools. Members were cautioned about governance risks at South African Tourism (SAT) following reports that the Tourism Business Council of South Africa (TBCSA) intended to withhold R500 million from the TOMSA levy.
The Committee Researcher presented options for international study tours, recommending Kenya, France, Germany, and India as priority destinations. Kenya was identified as South Africa’s continental competitor, France and Germany as long-standing source markets, and India as an emerging BRICS partner. Key global trends included infrastructure investment, simplified visa processes, expanded airlift agreements, and the development of niche products.
The Committee Secretary presented the draft Third Term Programme, which proposed oversight in Gauteng, Limpopo, Mpumalanga, and the Western Cape, joint meetings with other committees, and an international study tour in January 2026.
In discussions, Members called for the Strategic Plan to be explicitly linked to the United Nations Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), and for rail infrastructure to be included alongside roads. They stressed the importance of continued quarterly reports from SAT on tourist tracking and raised concerns about SAT's governance and qualified audits. Members also requested clarity on the MoU governing the TOMSA levy.
The Secretariat confirmed that all amendments would be incorporated before submission by 19 September 2025 and that a legal opinion on the dissolved SAT Board was pending. The Committee adopted the Strategic Plan, APP, and Draft Programme.
Meeting report
Chairperson's Opening Remarks
The Chairperson opened the meeting by greeting Members, departmental officials, and any delegations from the Minister’s office. She noted that the Ministers were not present, and that the Committee Secretary would confirm the names of those in attendance.
She reminded Members that the meeting was a continuation of the previous week’s session, during which the Committee had received presentations on its Strategic Plan (2025–2029) and Annual Performance Plan (2025/26). Members had been given time to study the documents and make submissions both during the meeting and in writing. She expressed the hope that all submissions had been forwarded to the Committee Secretary and incorporated into the current meeting.
The Chairperson stressed that the Committee’s priorities should be fully reflected in the plans, as these would guide the Committee in carrying out its oversight role and holding the executive accountable. Before handing over, she informed Members that she had just returned from a doctor's appointment, was feeling unwell, and would therefore not be able to continue chairing the meeting. She asked Ms A Hlongo (ANC) to take over as Acting Chairperson.
Committee Strategic and Annual Performance Plans
Dr Sibusiso Khuzwayo, Content Advisor to the Committee, briefed members on two documents requiring adoption: the five-year Strategic Plan and the Annual Performance Plan (APP). He explained that the APP captured the same priorities as the Strategic Plan and had been amended to incorporate Members’ earlier submissions.
Key adjustments included:
Tourism as a concurrent function: highlighting the need for ring-fenced municipal infrastructure grants to support tourism.
Committee priorities: expanded provisions for oversight visits, stakeholder engagement, unannounced inspections, and cost-benefit analysis of projects.
Niche tourism areas: inclusion of agritourism, sports, cruise, health, heritage, astro-tourism, cuisine, and paleo-tourism. These sectors were identified as important for job creation and rural economic growth.
Digital transformation: emphasis on integrating artificial intelligence, online marketing, and connectivity at tourist sites.
Monitoring tools: proposals for a tourism “dashboard” to track arrivals, expenditure, and economic contribution.
Dr Khuzwayo further noted that South African Tourism (SAT) faced financial risks following reports that the Tourism Business Council of South Africa (TBCSA) intended to withhold approximately R500 million from the TOMSA levy due to governance concerns. He stressed that all members’ inputs would be consolidated into the APP and highlighted in the upcoming Budget Review and Recommendations Report (BRRR).
Countries to Consider for International Study Tours
Ms Sisanda Loni, Committee Researcher, presented an analysis of possible destinations for international study tours. The proposals were based on members’ suggestions and prioritised countries where South African Tourism (SAT) had offices, to enable both learning from global best practice and oversight of SAT’s international operations.
Key Observations
Many of the recommended countries were ranked among the world’s top tourism economies, including the United States, China, Germany, Japan, the United Kingdom, France, Italy, and India.
Common trends across these destinations included:
Significant investment in tourism infrastructure.
Strong integration of domestic and international tourism.
Simplified visa processes (such as e-visas).
Expanded airlift agreements.
Development of niche markets (wellness tourism, eco-tourism, cultural and spiritual tourism, medical tourism).
Recommendations
Four countries were highlighted as priority options: Kenya – South Africa’s continental competitor, France and Germany, all established European source markets that had successfully diversified their products, and India, an emerging market and BRICS partner.
The Committee could either focus on a single destination or combine neighbouring countries to maximise value.
Draft 2025 Third Term Programme of the Portfolio Committee on Tourism
Mr Disang Mocumi, Committee Secretary, presented the draft programme of the Portfolio Committee on Tourism for October 2025 – March 2026. The programme outlined oversight visits, joint meetings, stakeholder engagements, and international study tours.
Key Activities
October 2025: Oversight in Gauteng, adoption of the BRRR, and a joint meeting with the Portfolio Committee on Employment and Labour.
November 2025: Oversight in Limpopo and Mpumalanga, briefings on transformation, domestic tourism, and quarterly reports.
December 2025: Committee week in the Western Cape, including a colloquium on tourism Small, Medium, and Micro Enterprises (SMMEs).
January 2026: Proposed international study tour to Kenya, Germany, India, and France.
Feb–Mar 2026: Briefings on tour operator licence backlogs, tourism grading, the sharing economy, and SAT’s Road development strategy.
He explained that the programme was intended to balance oversight, stakeholder engagement, and policy development until March 2026.
Discussion
Ms H Ismael (DA) suggested that the Committee’s work on transformation should be explicitly guided by South Africa’s commitment to the United Nations Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs). In particular, she recommended that the Committee integrate SDG 1 (poverty reduction), SDG 5 (gender equality), SDG 8 (decent work and economic growth), and SDG 10 (reduced inequality) into its oversight and policy functions. These goals, she said, provided a platform for transformation and sustainable development in the tourism sector.
On international benchmarking, Ms Ismael expressed concern about focusing on countries such as France and Italy, which were among the largest tourism destinations globally but not comparable to South Africa’s context. She suggested that comparisons should rather be drawn with countries that offered similar natural beauty, such as Australia, other African countries, or Brazil. However, she noted that many of South Africa’s international hubs were already linked to France and other European countries, and therefore benchmarking in those markets could still be useful.
She also raised concerns about the TOMSA levy, pointing to a media statement that the TBCSA was withholding R500 million from South African Tourism (SAT) due to dissatisfaction with the handling of the dissolved Board saga. She added that she had heard there was a Memorandum of Understanding (MoU) signed between TBCSA, SAT, and the Department, and suggested that the Committee request a copy of this MoU to clarify the basis on which the TOMSA levy was being transferred. She noted that SAT had received qualified audits, and TBCSA had reportedly set certain requirements in response. She stressed that the Committee should scrutinise these conditions and the MoU as part of its oversight.
Ms E Linde (DA) welcomed the Strategic Plan and congratulated the Committee and Secretariat on its development. She said if the goals outlined were achieved over the next five years, the Committee would leave a lasting legacy by elevating the tourism sector in South Africa. She acknowledged that the final draft of the plan had been received late, which limited the time Members had to study it. Nevertheless, she expressed satisfaction with the outcome. She commended the inclusion of the Youth in the Tourism Programme, which she believed would support economic growth, job creation, and opportunities for rural communities.
Ms Linde proposed that the Strategic Plan should also explicitly reference rail infrastructure. She suggested that section 10.2, which referred to roads, be amended to read “roads and rail infrastructure” to ensure rail was not overlooked. With this addition, she said she would be satisfied with the report. She also thanked Ms Loni for her work on the international tourism benchmarking report, noting that the research appeared thorough. She said she would study the document further and humorously remarked that the next challenge would be to identify funding for visits to all the countries highlighted in the report.
Ms L Ligaraba (ANC) thanked Dr Khuzwayo and the Secretariat for the work done on the Strategic Plan. She stressed the importance of continuing to track tourist arrivals and requested that SAT maintain its practice of providing quarterly reports to the Committee.
Ms N Buthelezi (MKP) referred to the programme presented by the Committee Secretary. She asked about the dissolution of the SAT Board, and whether the Committee would be following up on this matter with the Minister. She said she could not see where such follow-up had been reflected in the plan, unless she had overlooked it.
The Acting Chairperson, Ms Hlongo (ANC), responded to Ms Buthelezi’s concern. She reminded Members that when the matter of the dissolved SAT Board had previously been discussed, it was noted that once the Board had taken the matter to court, there was little the Committee could do from outside that process. However, she stressed that this did not prevent Members from questioning the Minister of Tourism on the issue when the Minister appeared before the Committee.
Dr Khuzwayo thanked the Chairperson and Members for their contributions. He said all inputs raised during the meeting would be incorporated into the documents before submission. The administration had reminded the Committee that the Strategic Plan and Annual Performance Plan had to be submitted by Friday, 19 September 2025. He emphasised that the documents belonged to Members, and the role of the Content Advisor and staff was to make sense of members’ proposals and reflect them in the final drafts.
On the issue of the TOMSA levy and the reported withholding of funds by the TBCSA, Dr Khuzwayo said the Committee would have an opportunity to raise this with the Minister during the upcoming Annual Report processes. If the MoU had indeed been signed between TBCSA and SAT, it would remain binding on the organisation regardless of changes in leadership. A possible breach of contract was therefore a matter for the executive to pursue. These issues would be flagged for discussion when the Committee engaged the Minister.
He added that all other inputs, including those highlighted by Hon. Ismael, would be reflected in the documents. The only outstanding issue was the budget, which had already been discussed with Members the previous week. A budget table had been populated for the current financial year, and it would be inserted into the documents as agreed. He concluded by noting that the documents were ready, and that the amendments from both last week and the present meeting would be captured for review by members before submission.
On the issue of the dissolution of the SAT board, Mr Mocumi confirmed that the Committee had already submitted a request for a legal opinion from the Parliamentary Legal Services unit. The request was made on the basis that the matter concerning the dissolution of the SAT Board was now sub judice as it was before the courts. He explained that once the legal advice had been received, the Committee would be able to decide what further steps it could take in addressing the matter.
The Five-Year Strategic Plan, the Annual Performance Plan and the proposed programme were adopted by the Committee.
Way Forward
Dr Khuzwayo thanked the Committee for its work on the Strategic and Annual Performance Plan. He noted that, despite limited time compared to other committees, Members had engaged thoroughly with the documents and made substantive submissions. He confirmed that the Secretariat would incorporate the changes agreed upon during the meeting and, through the Office of the Chairperson, ensure that the plans were submitted by the deadline of 19 September 2025.
On the study tour, he reminded Members that they had approved it in principle with suggested destinations including Kenya and three other international countries. He explained that Parliament required at least three months’ lead time to process applications, including for Members who needed to update their passports. He therefore requested guidance on when the Committee would finalise the list of countries to be visited. Since this was the Committee’s last meeting of the term, he suggested that a round-robin process through the Office of the Chairperson could be used to confirm the final destinations.
He added that Members would also have another opportunity to engage on matters related to the SAT Board when the Committee met with SAT and the Minister of Tourism in October. However, the Committee was still awaiting the legal opinion on the dissolution of the Board.
The Acting Chairperson thanked Dr Khuzwayo for his input. She noted that Members had already deliberated on the proposed study tour countries and adopted them as recommended. The matter now rested with the Office of the Chairperson, which would provide final guidance, likely later that same day. She assured the Secretariat that there would be no delay in processing the matter further.
She then thanked Members for their participation and for allowing her to chair the meeting in the absence of the Chairperson. The meeting was officially adjourned.
