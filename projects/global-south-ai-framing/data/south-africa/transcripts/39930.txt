Meeting Summary
Video
The Special Investigation Unit (SIU), South African Police Service (Saps), Hawks, and National Prosecuting Authority (NPA) briefed the committee on the investigations and prosecutions related to the alleged corruption and maladministration at the National Lotteries Commission (NLC).
In November 2020, President Cyril Ramaphosa signed a proclamation authorising the SIU to investigate the NLC and recover any losses.
The SIU highlighted systemic corruption within the NLC, including diverting funds meant for community development to private accounts and luxury expenditures. It emphasised the need for enhanced governance and systemic reforms. The NPA provided an update on prosecutorial actions, noting significant challenges such as delays in securing witnesses and obtaining documents. The Hawks focused on active cases involving large-scale fraud, money laundering, and theft, highlighting collaboration with other agencies to trace assets and prosecute offenders.
Key concerns raised by committee members included the slow progress in resolving cases, discrepancies in reported figures regarding funds misappropriated and recovered, and the need for stronger inter-agency coordination. Members also emphasised the importance of addressing systemic governance failures at the NLC, improving the transparency of grant distributions, and enhancing compliance measures for non-profit organisations.
The Deputy Minister acknowledged the systemic lapses and stressed the need to design rational systems to combat corruption and ensure equitable distribution of funds. He also addressed concerns about lacking a centralised big data system to streamline processes and detect irregularities. The NLC Commissioner outlined ongoing reforms, including implementing a robust online application system with artificial intelligence capabilities, enhanced compliance measures, and forensic investigations to address historical issues.
Discussions also touched on resource constraints faced by the SIU, NPA, and Hawks, with members urging for improved capacity and funding to expedite investigations and prosecutions. Proposals included leveraging artificial intelligence for fraud detection, revising governance frameworks, and introducing legislative amendments to strengthen accountability.
The meeting concluded with all entities committing to enhance their efforts in addressing corruption within the NLC.

Special Investigating Unit (SIU) Presentation
Adv Andy Mothibi, Head, SIU, with the assistance of his colleagues, presented.
The presentation provides a comprehensive update on the status of the SIU’s investigations into the National Lotteries Commission (NLC), as mandated by
Proclamation R32 of 2020
. It opens with an outline of the SIU’s constitutional and legislative framework, highlighting its role in investigating corruption, maladministration, and malpractice while ensuring transparency, fairness, and legal compliance. The presentation emphasises the SIU's powers to subpoena, search, and interrogate and its limitations, such as the inability to prosecute or discipline, requiring collaboration with other agencies.
The investigation, covering activities from January 2014 to November 2020, focuses on the misuse of funds by NPOs and NPCs. Specific examples include misappropriating millions of rands meant for community projects, construction, and events, with funds diverted to private accounts, luxury purchases, or unrelated entities. Cases of hijacked organisations, fraudulent reporting, and collusion with NLC officials are detailed. The SIU has made significant progress, including preserving properties and funds, initiating civil litigation, and preparing criminal referrals.
The presentation concludes with systemic recommendations to prevent future abuses, such as enhanced transparency, stricter oversight, and more robust anti-corruption frameworks. The SIU also underscores the importance of inter-agency cooperation to ensure accountability and the recovery of misused funds.

National Prosecuting Authority (NPA) Presentation
Adv Rodney De Kock, Deputy National Director of Public Prosecutions, NPA
,
presented, with the assistance of his colleagues.
The presentation provides an update on the NPA’s progress regarding referrals from the SIU related to the NLC. It opens with an overview of the NPA's mandate under Section 179 of the South African Constitution, emphasising its role in prosecuting crimes, recovering stolen funds, and ensuring justice for victims. It highlights the collaboration between the NPA, SIU, DPCI (Hawks), and other partners in addressing corruption and maladministration, particularly within the NLC.
The SIU's referrals stem from Proclamation 32 of 2020, which authorised investigations into the misuse of lottery funds between 2014 and 2020. These referrals detail cases involving fraudulent applications, misappropriation of funds, and systemic corruption by NLC officials and associated NPOs. Examples include cases like Denzhe Primary Care, where millions allocated for a rehabilitation centre were diverted for personal expenses, and Life for Impact in the 21st Century, where funds intended for youth awards were misused. The presentation identifies the critical role of forensic accounting, witness affidavits, and the integration of civil asset recovery through the Asset Forfeiture Unit (AFU) in securing justice.
Challenges include the delay between offences and investigations, the unavailability of witnesses and documents, and legal disputes over the proclamation's validity. Despite these obstacles, the NPA has preserved over R94 million through asset forfeiture and is actively pursuing legal proceedings. The presentation concludes with the NPA's commitment to finalising these cases through a dedicated team and ongoing inter-agency collaboration, aiming for accountability and justice.

Directorate for Priority Crime Investigation (DPCI) Presentation
Major General M Makinyane, Component Head: Serious Commercial Crime Investigation, DPCI, presented with the assistance of his colleagues.
The DPCI, known as the Hawks, presented an update on their investigations into corruption and mismanagement involving the NLC. Tasked under Section 17D of the South African Police Service Act, the Hawks are mandated to investigate serious crimes, including organised crime, corruption, and economic offences that require specialised expertise. The presentation highlighted the scale of corruption in the NLC, with 19 active cases involving fraud, theft, and money laundering. These cases, which represent losses exceeding R15 billion, underscore systemic failures and the need for accountability.
Key cases include the Kgatelopele Foundation, where fraudulent applications led to the misappropriation of R500,000, and a R135 million case involving funds for the Commonwealth Games and borehole drilling that were never used for their intended purposes. Other examples involve a library and museum project that diverted R20 million and a R3.1 million sports facility project that was never implemented. These cases span multiple provinces, with Gauteng handling the majority, and involve complex money-laundering schemes and falsified applications.
Investigations have required collaboration with the SIU, NPA, and other agencies and extensive evidence collection, including 479 witness statements to date. Challenges include obtaining original documentation, tracing funds through sophisticated laundering schemes, and engaging uncooperative witnesses. The Hawks emphasised their commitment to conducting investigations thoroughly and without bias, ensuring that those responsible are held accountable and that misappropriated public funds are recovered. The presentation concluded with a commitment to enforcing the law and restoring public trust in governance and resource management.

Mr Zuko Godlimpi, Deputy Minister of Trade, Industry and Competition, greeted the members of the committee and the representatives of the state institutions. He expressed gratitude for the cooperation from the SIU and the NPA in their efforts to uncover the issues under investigation. He emphasised that the work done by the NLC in funding charitable causes was vital to the broader state agenda. He described the NLC as an arm of public service that ideally helps address societal social deprivations. He further noted that the integrity of the NLC and its work had significant implications for the credibility of the state project as a whole.
He highlighted that the current investigation revealed the risks of failing to build rational, predictable systems capable of combating nefarious intent. He pointed out that the discovery of amounts as large as 15 billion rands being funnelled out through the back door demonstrated the absence of such systems from the outset.
He also mentioned the SIU's recommendations to the committee, noting that the new NLC board and administration had already begun implementing some of these recommendations in collaboration with the relevant authorities.
As a national entity, the NLC is expected to distribute grants with a geographic spread that reflects its national mandate. However, he observed a spatial concentration of distribution, where significant amounts flowed into one or two provinces instead of being distributed across the entire country. He noted that this highlighted a systemic design problem in managing funding for charitable causes.
He further explained that discussions with the NLC had addressed the looseness of the definition of "charitable causes." He emphasized the need for predictable parameters to solve nationally recognised social problems. Using an example, he pointed out the irrationality of requesting R30 million for a tree-planting initiative without a clear basis for the amount or its specific geographic focus. He stressed the importance of aligning funding amounts with the purpose, citing a soccer tournament requiring R4.5 million as an example, where the scale of the project should determine the amount allocated.
He mentioned that part of rebuilding the NLC’s business processes involved designing rational systems, including predictive algorithms. These systems would detect glaring issues, such as a single ID number being associated with multiple NPOs applying to the same institution. He noted that such obvious irregularities should not be overlooked and highlighted the ongoing efforts since his office took over five months ago to address these issues.
He outlined the importance of the NLC grant distribution system in addressing urgent social challenges. He cited examples of recurring crises in South Africa, such as flooding during December and January and informal settlement fires in the Western Cape around June and July. He emphasised the need for a state system capable of rapid response in such scenarios, supported by credible NPOs. Without such a system, he warned, there might be a temptation to dismiss the current arrangement altogether despite its potential to address pressing issues.
He concluded by acknowledging that systemic failures had led to the current problems but emphasised the efforts being made to resolve them. He assured the committee that the DTIC and the NLC would return after the recess to provide a status update on reforms to create a more robust system. He expressed confidence that significant progress would be made and committed to presenting their findings to the committee. With those remarks, he ended his address.

The CRO developed an end-to-end risk assessment process by mapping forensic and fraud reports from the SIU and their forensic team. She added that they were launching their NLC fraud prevention plan, which incorporated ISO standards such as anti-bribery and corruption (37001) and records management. She also noted their ambitious target of obtaining 37001 certification, which typically takes two to three years.
She elaborated that the system aimed to reduce risk, enhance organisational integrity, and prevent the hijacking of directors and NPOs. They had developed APIs linked to entities like CIPC, SARS, Home Affairs, the Department of Social Development (DSD), and the South African Fraud Prevention Services. Additionally, one of the major banks had shown interest in partnering with the system to ensure proper bank account management and reduce fraud. She highlighted that the system could detect blacklisted individuals and facilitate compliance by notifying relevant authorities and offering assistance.
She acknowledged that out of the 2,784 applications received, only 115 were compliant, a figure corroborated by research conducted by the University of Pretoria, which found that only 25% of the NPO sector was compliant. She explained that the system immediately identified non-compliance areas, provided forms for resolution, and notified relevant partners like SARS and DSD for prioritisation. Memorandums of Agreement (MOAs) had been signed with these partners, enabling responses within specific timeframes: seven days for DSD, one to three days for CIPC, and five days for SARS.
She also addressed the challenges of addressing historical issues within the organisation, noting that individuals implicated in misconduct were suspended within two days of referrals from the SIU. However, she acknowledged tactics employed by some to delay processes through legal challenges, with one individual remaining on suspension for two years due to such interventions.
Regarding the system's transformative goals, she explained their approach to enabling compliance among the 2,600 non-compliant applicants. Service-level agreements were in place to expedite resolution processes. The system was designed to assist applicants in identifying and addressing compliance issues, with forms and notifications streamlined to relevant authorities.
She mentioned their collaboration with the University of Pretoria and reliance on external research to structure their application processes. For instance, priorities in the charity sector included early childhood development centres, protection of the vulnerable, gender-based violence, food security, and animal welfare. Funding allocations were aligned with these priorities.
She further explained their control environment, incorporating preventative and detective controls. Preventative measures included on-site project verification, while detective measures focused on monitoring and evaluation, which now covered 100% of budgets compared to 20% previously. The board had adopted a zero-tolerance approach to fraud and corruption, with all projects subject to monitoring and evaluation. She also highlighted concerns around cybersecurity, ensuring protective measures were in place.
She detailed their incorporation of ISO standards into the system, including anti-bribery and records management protocols, ensuring processes could not be bypassed. They also engaged master’s and honours students to contribute to rapid system development. Cases flagged by the SIU were shared with the DTIC, and they currently had four DA members adjudicating applications. Lifestyle audits were also conducted for 56 individuals, and 157 cases were referred to SAPS. Forensic investigations were ongoing, with 208 projects under review in 2022-23 and 99 cases in the current financial year.
She emphasised their focus on capacity building and development for grantees, helping them understand and implement financial controls. Monitoring and evaluation were conducted after six months to ensure adherence. Automation was prioritised to streamline forensic case management and allow more focus on grantee support. Finally, she outlined their developmental approach to non-compliance, distinguishing between fraud-related issues and administrative oversights to facilitate compliance and effectively support grantees.
Closing Remarks
The Chairperson expressed gratitude to the members present and thanked them for their engagement. He also thanked all the stakeholders in attendance and for their presentations. Lastly, he requested that members with copies of the presentations keep them securely and refrain from distributing them.
The meeting was adjourned
