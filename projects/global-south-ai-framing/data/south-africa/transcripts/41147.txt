Meeting Summary
2025/26 Annual Performance Plans
The Select Committee held a meeting with the Independent Police Investigative Directorate (IPID) and the Deputy Ministers of Police on the Directorate's 2025/26 Annual Performance Plan.
The Chairperson commended IPID’s work on high-profile cases and welcomed improvements in budget allocations, strategic planning, and a shift toward victim-centred services. She called for stronger inclusion of oversight bodies in departmental frameworks and better recognition of Parliament’s role.
Members raised concerns about leadership diversity, case backlogs, information communication technology (ICT) modernisation, staff shortages, witness protection, and forensic capacity. They emphasised the need for clear provincial data, improved investigator retention, and inclusive service for vulnerable groups, including the LGBTQIA+ community and persons with disabilities.
In response, IPID reported progress in conviction rates, the recruitment of retired detectives, and efforts to strengthen internal systems. The Department committed to providing requested documents, including a full organogram, an ICT plan, and a backlog strategy. It also outlined ongoing transformation efforts, including internal promotions, wellness support, and youth absorption.
The Deputy Ministers reaffirmed their support for IPID’s independence, and highlighted plans to address structural and resource challenges.
Meeting report
The Chairperson opened the meeting and expressed appreciation to the Department and Deputy Ministers (DMs) for their consistent presence and commitment to the Select Committee’s work. She emphasised the importance of executive accountability and the role it played in enabling Parliament to perform its oversight function effectively.
She acknowledged the Independent Police Investigative Directorate (IPID)’s recent work, especially in high-profile investigations like the Gugulethu shootout and other incidents involving police misconduct, abductions, and shootings in the Western Cape and the Free State. IPID’s role in ensuring independent oversight of police conduct, promoting consequence management, and restoring public trust in law enforcement was highlighted as crucial, particularly in the current climate of high crime, corruption, and gender-based violence (GBV).
The Chairperson welcomed the reported budget increases and IPID’s strategic shifts, including the creation of new units and efforts to address backlogs. She noted concerns about low investigator numbers in provinces like Gauteng, and urged IPID to develop responsive strategies.
She also called for greater recognition of the Select and Portfolio Committees in the Department's strategic frameworks, as their input shapes Departmental planning and accountability. Lastly, she urged the Deputy Ministers (DMs) to ensure that oversight bodies were explicitly referred to in official frameworks developed by the Department of Planning, Monitoring and Evaluation (DPME).
Ministers' input
Ms Polly Boshielo, Deputy Minister of Police, acknowledged the Chairperson's opening remarks, and affirmed that the Department took the matters raised by the Select and Portfolio Committees very seriously. She introduced the delegation from IPID.
Mr Cassel Mathale, Deputy Minister of Police, expressed appreciation for the opportunity to engage. He emphasised that these interactions were valuable and not taken for granted, as they helped the Department grow through the questions and feedback from the Committee. He invited Members to raise issues so the Department could provide complete and accurate information. He acknowledged the importance of keeping the Committee well-informed to support their engagement with constituencies and provinces.
IPID 2025-2030 Strategic Plan, and 2025/26 Annual Performance Plan and budget
The briefing aimed to outline IPID’s key policy priorities, strategic focus areas, and budget allocations. The Department emphasised its commitment to enhancing service delivery, accountability and governance, in line with government priorities. Notably, IPID had received an additional R126.3 million over the medium term expenditure framework (MTEF) period, which would support, among other things, an increase in personnel from 387 in 2025/26 to 421 in 2027/28.
A major feature of the presentation was the newly approved budget programme structure, effective from April 2025. The revised structure consolidates IPID’s work into three programmes: Administration, Investigations and Provincial Coordination, and Legal Compliance, Information, and Stakeholder Management. The restructuring aims to optimise resource use and reduce functional duplication.
Performance-wise, IPID reported a rise in registered cases from 5 136 to 5 497, and an increase in completed investigations from 4 669 to 5 310 in the last financial year. A total of 14 463 active cases had been carried over into 2025/26, with 72% of the completed cases consisting of backlog matters.
Key strategic interventions included a backlog reduction strategy supported by the recruitment of retired detectives and the procurement of dedicated vehicles. Ten detectives had already been recruited, and another 15 would be appointed over the coming years. Gender-based violence and femicide (GBV&F) remained a top priority, with dedicated training for investigators and system enhancements to improve monitoring. In 2024/25, IPID completed 92 rape investigations, resulting in both departmental and criminal convictions.
IPID also outlined its continued advocacy for the IPID expansion strategy, which was critical for enhancing public access and improving response to community needs. Modernisation efforts included the development of a new case management system (CMS), improved integration with the justice sector, and the establishment of a dedicated call centre. The Department’s rural safety strategy had led to more integrated outreach in remote areas, combining investigations, community engagement, and station inspections.
The mandate of IPID had been significantly revised following the enactment of the IPID Amendment Act, No. 15 of 2024. The Act expands IPID’s authority to include investigating deaths caused by police actions regardless of duty status, torture, corruption and serious assaults. It also repeals responsibilities for investigating some lesser offences. This aligns with IPID’s aim of being an effective and independent oversight body that promotes accountable policing.
Strategic outcomes for 2030 include a 30% reduction in legal claims, filling 90% of vacancies within six months, and increasing convictions in GBV&F, corruption, and other misconduct cases. The APP for 2025/26 sets out clear targets across three programmes: improving internal governance; strengthening provincial coordination and investigations; and enhancing legal compliance and stakeholder engagement.
Several challenges were acknowledged, including limited investigator capacity in provinces like Gauteng, underspending in some regions, and low compliance levels by SAPS with IPID’s recommendations. Measures to address these issues included recruitment, enhanced training, vehicle provisioning, and improved coordination. While no new legislation was planned for 2025/26, IPID would operationalise the newly passed IPID Regulations, which support the implementation of the 2024 Amendment Act.
In conclusion, IPID reaffirmed its dedication to strengthening police oversight and service delivery, stressing that the feedback and oversight role of the Select and Portfolio Committees were central to its planning and accountability processes.

Discussion
The Chairperson thanked IPID for providing a detailed and extensive presentation, noting it would serve the Select Committee well throughout the next financial year. She said that due to time constraints and the broad scope of issues the Committee handles, future engagements would focus on specific topics to ensure effectiveness. She emphasised the importance of the oversight mandate, and expressed hope that IPID would meet its planned targets.
Lastly, she raised a concern regarding how outcomes related to disability and MTSF priorities were structured in the presentation, advising the Department to review how these outcomes were categorised to avoid unnecessary fragmentation of priorities.
Mr V Gericke (EFF, Western Cape) commended IPID for a comprehensive and informative presentation, especially the clarity on budget matters. Reflecting on the origins of IPID and the Civilian Secretariat, he highlighted how both had been established to offer the public credible avenues to report police misconduct, contrasting it with the inaccessibility of justice during apartheid.
He emphasised that IPID was a powerful oversight body that was expected to act independently and serve the public with dignity. However, he raised concerns about the lack of visible diversity in IPID’s top leadership structure, warning that an all-black executive may unintentionally send a non-inclusive message to the public, despite the competence of black professionals. He stressed the need for transformation and language accessibility to reflect South Africa’s diversity.
On performance, he questioned why only around 1 300 convictions had been secured from nearly 6 000 cases under investigation. He also raised concerns about recent media allegations of political interference in investigations, especially in KwaZulu-Natal, where former staff reportedly faced victimisation for doing their work thoroughly.
Mr Gericke acknowledged IPID’s heavy workload, and supported hiring more investigators, but cautioned against relying too heavily on former police detectives, suggesting that it may raise perceptions of bias. He proposed that IPID consider training new investigators from scratch to instil its values and culture.
Lastly, he expressed deep concern over the high number of deaths resulting from police action, which reflected a troubling culture within SAPS. He asked what recommendations could be made to help shift this violent culture towards a more service-oriented approach, in line with SAPS's transformation from a force to a service intended to treat the public as clients.
Ms A Mashobeni (EFF, Eastern Cape) requested a detailed breakdown of completed cases, carried-over cases, and backlogs by province to track progress, especially since her province had a high backlog.
She agreed with the suggestion of establishing a call centre to help address backlogs, but sought clarification on whether victims or reporting offices would lodge cases there. She welcomed the priority given to clearing backlogs, emphasising that long delays caused victims to lose hope.
Lastly, she asked for clearer information regarding the number of rape investigations mentioned, as the report was unclear.
Mr M Billy (DA, KwaZulu-Natal) raised concerns about IPID’s structure and functioning, noting it was underfunded and understaffed, which hampered its ability to hold the police accountable. He suggested the institution’s design may need reconsideration. He criticised the low performance targets set by the IPID, highlighting that they did not reflect the severity of police-related killings, especially in KwaZulu-Natal (KZN).
Mr Billy warned that accepting these minimal targets made oversight ineffective. He urged all stakeholders to reflect on the purpose and effectiveness of their work, given IPID’s challenges, emphasising the need for serious reconsideration.
Mr O Mokae (DA, Northern Cape) emphasised his practice of tracking the Committee’s progress through notes from past meetings, commenting that current information builds on previous sessions. He focused first on the backlog issue, recalling that last year, IPID had planned to contract 15 workers to reduce backlogs and was now seeing progress with the retired detectives hired. He sought an update on the impact and results of this intervention, and whether the strategy would continue.
He addressed the improvement of ICT infrastructure, mentioning the R8 million allocated for information technology (IT) modernisation and digital transformation. He requested more details on how this investment supported digitalisation, including artificial intelligence (AI), and asked for a timeline for expanding these systems.
Regarding the Northern Cape office, he noted that a house in Upington had been identified to become an office, and asked for updates on public engagement and verification processes. He appreciated the acknowledgement of the Northern Cape's unique demographics and logistical challenges, emphasising the need to consider these in planning.
He requested a provincial organogram for future meetings, showing provincial heads and their teams, including regions or sub-offices, to better understand the structure across provinces.
Lastly, he raised concerns about cases involving the LGBTQIA+ community, asking how IPID addresses such incidents, which may be categorised generally as assault, but reflect cultural issues within the SAPS.
Ms O Medupe (ANC, North West) acknowledged the progress made since last year, particularly highlighting the Ministry's support in securing increased funding. This funding had helped the Department to start overcoming previous challenges. She commended both the political and administrative components for their efforts in fighting for this support.
She requested clarity on the main ICT projects planned for 2024/25 and 2025/26, asking how these initiatives would support departmental efficiency and digital transformation, how progress toward the 80% target would be tracked, and what risks existed for underperformance.
She expressed concern about recruitment challenges, and called for a clear retention strategy. She noted that investigators might be leaving not only due to retirement, but also for better opportunities elsewhere, and wanted to know what the Department was doing to retain skilled staff.
She asked what measures were in place to protect witnesses, and suggested that insufficient protection might be contributing to case backlogs because witnesses were reluctant to come forward.
Ms Medupe inquired about the integrated case management system's current status and whether it was being piloted across all provinces or only in one.
Lastly, she highlighted concerns about forensic laboratory services, specifically citing an incident in the North West province where a delayed forensic response was attributed to only one staff member being available. She mentioned that the focus appeared to be on high-crime areas like Potchefstroom, and she intended to follow up with formal questions on this matter.
The Chairperson aligned with Mr Gericke's consistent stance on the transformation agenda. She stressed that as leadership addresses past and present imbalances, it was essential not to perpetuate forms of exclusion such as tribalism, racism, or sexism. She emphasised the need for intentional inclusion rather than mere accommodation, especially in hiring practices, and supported the idea that advertisements should clearly state the demographic groups being targeted to ensure diversity and inclusion.
She acknowledged the sensitive nature of these discussions, and appreciated that the meeting was not public or media-covered, allowing for open and honest reflection. She urged the Department’s leadership to seriously consider transformation deliberately. She also drew attention to the importance of representing the LGBTQIA+ community in demographic reporting. She expressed concern about the Department's insufficient progress in employing persons with disabilities, pointing out that the 2% employment rate suggested a lack of real commitment.
She welcomed the proposed strategy on youth development, but questioned whether it was backed by a formal institutional policy, noting that a strategy without policy support lacked authority and sustainability. Similarly, she inquired about the gender focal point, whether it was a designated unit or just a single coordinator, and requested full details, suggesting the Department should provide a written submission that also includes the organogram previously requested by Mr Mokae. She emphasised the value of an organogram to show the Department’s structure and appetite for addressing identified shortcomings.
Regarding the Department’s provincial footprint, she welcomed the increase in its presence, but asked for more details. She referred to another department -- the Civilian Secretariat for Police Service (CSPS) that listed provincial offices but lacked a presence in certain areas, making the community feel excluded. She requested a detailed submission that identified physical locations, ensuring Committee Members could verify local presence and accessibility, especially for constituency work.
The Chairperson expressed support for the ICT strategy, and noted its importance in strengthening core functions. However, she cautioned against investing in systems that were not aligned with the Department’s mandate -- for example, adopting solutions designed for other sectors like health. She called for a detailed breakdown of what was included in the ICT strategy to ensure it supports the Department’s core work, such as investigations and oversight.
She concluded by asking whether the Department collaborates with the State Security Agency (SSA) academy for training investigators and detectives. She emphasised that addressing complex investigations and corruption cases required skilled personnel, and noted that other institutions had benefited from the SSA’s expertise.
Lastly, she asked for more information on the Department’s backlog strategy. While the presentation referred to one, she requested a detailed submission, similar to the Government Printing Works' (GPW’s) strategy involving overtime and overnight work, so the Committee could monitor and assess whether it was fit for purpose and aligned with budget allocations.
IPID's response
Ms Suzan Letlape, Director: Strategy, IPID, addressed the Department’s target for employing persons with disabilities. She said the Department wanted to improve representation, but had struggled to attract suitable candidates in the past years. Due to the Department’s small size, even a 1% increase was difficult to achieve. She emphasised that while transformation targets were considered during recruitment, they must also ensure that selected candidates are competent and meet job requirements. Human resource (HR) advisors provide equity reports during the hiring process, and the Department follows recruitment regulations.
Mr Patrick Setshedi, Chief Financial Officer (CFO), IPID, in response to Ms Mashobeni's question regarding the budget allocation per province, confirmed that the Department would provide a detailed breakdown of investigation cases per province. This would allow Members to compare the number of cases handled with the resources allocated. He specifically noted that the Eastern Cape and KZN were considered major provinces and received similar allocations due to their high case volumes.
In response to Mr Mokae’s question about office space in Upington, he confirmed that the Department had engaged with the Department of Public Works (DPW) last year, and had been promised a house to be used as a district office. However, after requesting access for inspection and renovation, the DPW had informed them that the house was no longer available. The Department currently lacked the resources to rent office space externally, but was still pursuing the matter through the DPW at the national level to find a solution.
Regarding the ICT implementation plan, the CFO confirmed that the plan consisted of 14 projects. Due to its detailed nature, he requested permission to submit the full ICT implementation plan as part of the additional documentation, to allow Members to study it thoroughly over the MTEF period.
Dr Hlengani Bila, Head of Investigations, began by addressing the concerns about political interference in IPID’s operations. He stated firmly that there was no such interference, assuring Members that he was personally involved in investigations and was leading from the front. This should give the Committee confidence in the Department’s independence and commitment to its mandate.
He highlighted significant improvements in IPID’s performance, particularly in its conviction rate, which had risen from 43% to 76%. This increase was a positive reflection of the Department’s work and its commitment to justice. In addition, there had been a notable rise in the number of completed cases -- an increase of 641 from the previous financial year. He further indicated that over 1 000 matters were currently being actively managed, showing a healthy workflow and steady progress.
In response to suggestions about addressing the case backlog, Dr Bila spoke about the recruitment of retired SAPS detectives. He assured Members that ethical considerations were central to this process, and all retired recruits underwent assessments to ensure they would uphold high standards. IPID planned to employ 25 retired detectives, each expected to finalise around 10 cases per month, translating to about 3 000 resolved cases annually. This formed part of their broader backlog strategy, which also considered ideas like overtime work.
He clarified that any case not completed within 90 days was classified as part of the backlog. The current backlog rate was around 3.5%, but through increased staffing and efficient processes, the Department aimed to significantly reduce this number. The strategy was data-driven and included specific performance targets.
Responding to the request for more detailed statistics per province, he referred Members to slides 28 and 34 of the presentation, which detailed completed and carried-over cases respectively. For example, 2 474 cases were carried over in the Eastern Cape. He committed to continuously improving the way case data was broken down for transparency and better oversight.
Regarding demographic representation, he addressed concerns raised by several Members about equity and diversity in leadership. He mentioned that the Department was already making progress, pointing out that KZN was led by an Indian official, Free State by a Coloured official, and Mpumalanga by a White official. He acknowledged the need for more detailed data, and promised to submit a full provincial organogram for the Committee’s review.
Explaining how cases were handled, he described the life cycle of a docket from receipt, investigation and completion, to referral to the National Prosecuting Authority (NPA). If a case was viable, it was placed on the court roll, and outcomes could include either acquittal or conviction. If a case lacked sufficient evidence or witnesses, it may be deemed unfounded.
On witness protection, Dr Bila clarified that once someone became a witness in an IPID case, they were considered a state witness. The Department worked in collaboration with the NPA and other departments to provide protection where necessary, following proper legal protocols. He assured Members that these systems were in place and were activated when needed.
Ms Phalatse (sp), Director: Human Resource Management, responded to questions regarding staff recruitment and the youth development strategy. Acknowledging the high turnover of investigators, she outlined various retention strategies designed to position IPID as an employer of choice. One such initiative was the annual Investigator Performance Awards (INDABA), which had been running for two years. This program, supported by sponsors due to government restrictions on direct financial rewards, motivated investigators through recognition and monetary prizes for top performers. It had reportedly fostered healthy competition among provinces and improved performance.
To further motivate staff, the Department had created a WhatsApp group to share and celebrate convictions, offering real-time recognition of individual investigators’ achievements. This practice has helped boost morale and performance. In addition, the Executive Director had introduced an Investigators Task Team to enhance communication between operational staff and leadership. Quarterly meetings were held between investigators’ provincial representatives and management, supported by another WhatsApp platform, to address operational issues promptly and avoid unnecessary backlogs.
The Department also implemented internal advertisements for promotional posts, allowing investigators to advance without competing externally. This move had helped address concerns about limited upward mobility, and enabled the Department to focus on upskilling internal staff. Moreover, the Department was aligned with the Association of Certified Fraud Examiners (ACFE) and other professional bodies to promote the professionalisation of investigators, in line with public service frameworks.
In addressing salary disparities, the Department implemented Section 23 to achieve salary parity with SAPS detectives. Investigators now received equal pay and allowances similar to those in SAPS, such as overtime and special allowances. Going forward, IPID aimed to establish its salary dispensation for investigators through organised labour chambers to better reflect their competencies and roles.
Another key component of the Department’s retention strategy was employee wellness. Due to the traumatic nature of investigative work, investigators received regular trauma debriefing sessions conducted by independent psychologists. These take place monthly and quarterly, and in the past year alone, 54 referrals have been made for employees requiring further psychological support.
Lastly, Ms Phalatse acknowledged the Chairperson’s call for a supporting policy. While a formal policy was still being considered, the Department did have an implementation plan that was monitored monthly. As part of this strategy, IPID has successfully absorbed 41 interns into permanent positions between 1 April 2024 and the present. This approach not only addressed youth unemployment, but also ensured continuity by retaining trained and mentored individuals within the Department.
Mr Stephens Ramafoko, Chief Director: Legal Services, confirmed that he had reviewed all the questions posed during the meeting, and believed all had been adequately addressed. He had no further comments to add.
Ms Jennifer Ntlaseng, Executive Director, acknowledged that most questions had been addressed, except for a few that were more observational. One such issue was the shortage of investigators in Gauteng, which was currently being addressed through a reprioritisation of staff, especially in hotspot provinces like Gauteng, KZN, the Western Cape and Eastern Cape. The Department was moving resources from less-affected provinces to support these high-need areas.
The recruitment of retired detectives had proved effective in reducing backlogs and transferring skills to younger investigators. The Department received monthly progress updates and remained committed to helping victims, even making the Executive Director accessible to them directly. However, heavy workloads remained a concern, with some investigators handling close to 280 dockets, increasing the risk of rushed or compromised investigations.
To address quality concerns, a quality assurance unit had been established to review case files before finalisation. The Department was focused on improving conviction rates rather than simply clearing cases. In addition, they were considering revisiting withdrawn cases with assistance from internal review units to improve outcomes.
Ms Ntlaseng confirmed that a new call centre would allow victims, witnesses, and even anonymous callers to report cases. Many anonymous tips had already proved helpful. The Department would provide written responses to any outstanding Committee questions and submit additional requested data, such as provincial footprints and satellite office details.
On inclusivity, the Department was committed to prioritising LGBTQIA+ cases as part of its broader transformational agenda, ensuring all victims were treated equally. The gender focal point was a dedicated individual within a transformation unit, working closely with the Department of Women, Youth, and Persons with Disabilities, and the Employee Wellness Programme.
The Department collaborates with the State Security Agency (SSA) on employee vetting and security matters, and also partners with the Department of Intelligence. Finally, while some staff were working overtime to address case backlogs, challenges persisted because courts operate only on weekdays, limiting how fast progress could be made.
Overall, the Department was striving to balance case volume with quality, to improve turnaround time, and maintain public confidence through transparency and strategic collaboration.
The Chairperson thanked the Department for its responsiveness, and confirmed there were no outstanding responses from previous meetings. However, she reminded the Department to submit all newly requested information within 14 days. The Executive Director was asked to provide a designated contact person, including their details, to ensure Members could reach the Department quickly when issues arise. This should include provincial contact points to improve communication and accountability across regions.
Deputy Ministers' closing remarks
DM Boshielo thanked the IPID team for their responsiveness, and acknowledged the Chairperson’s guidance on improving accountability. She praised IPID for starting their presentation by responding to prior questions, and encouraged all entities, including SAPS, to adopt this approach. She emphasised that while issues of transformation and inclusion, especially regarding race, disability, and gender, must be addressed, they should be approached systematically and constitutionally.
She noted that the current leadership had been in office for less than a year, and was therefore taking time to understand the institution before implementing drastic changes. From the 2025/26 financial year, they planned to address structural challenges more decisively, while avoiding political interference in operational work.
She urged Members of Parliament to reach out to her or Deputy Minister Mathale directly via WhatsApp if concerns arose. She stressed the urgency of focusing on hotspot provinces -Gauteng, KZN, the Western Cape and Eastern Cape- because they account for the bulk of criminal activity. If crime was reduced in these areas, national crime statistics would improve significantly.
She acknowledged the challenges faced by IPID due to budget constraints, and emphasised that the Department could not rely on SAPS for independence or functionality. She proposed a conversation about the future structure and resourcing of IPID, including whether it should remain dependent on SAPS for budget and staffing. She also called for shared recruitment pools across agencies to address the shortage of qualified detectives.
Deputy Minister Boshielo encouraged collaboration across government departments and entities, particularly in the use of technology and skills development. She criticised a passive mindset about budget limitations, and encouraged proactive, innovative thinking. She reiterated that while IPID was underfunded, it had competent leadership and should not be demoralised.
She concluded with heartfelt reflections on the emotional toll of GBV cases, referring to specific tragedies to highlight the brutality investigators encounter. She supported the integration of social workers into police stations to address the root social causes of crime. Regarding forensic capacity, she noted the need to expand laboratory facilities to all provinces, while cautioning against promising what could not be funded.
Despite the challenges, she reaffirmed the Department’s commitment to serving the public, stating, “We are here to serve, and we will continue to serve our people.”
DM Mathale said that progress had been made in securing a building for the KZN forensic lab through engagements with the Minister of Public Works. Negotiations with the landlord were ongoing, and acquisition was the next step.
He then addressed earlier concerns about political interference, clarifying that the Executive Director’s comments may have unintentionally suggested pressure from higher-ups, but that was not the case. He emphasised that IPID operates independently and the relationship between the leadership -- such as the National Police Commissioner and the Executive Director -- was collaborative, not adversarial.
He highlighted that IPID, along with SAPS, the Civilian Secretariat, and the Directorate for Priority Crime Investigation (DPCI), were interrelated institutions meant to uphold constitutional policing. He reflected on South Africa's historical transition from apartheid to democracy, noting that this transformation required a long-term process, not overnight change.
He stressed that IPID was established not only to police the police, but also to guide and reorient them toward constitutional and ethical conduct. He envisioned a future where IPID would become obsolete because the police would have fully internalised professional conduct, suggesting that IPID’s ultimate success lay in making itself unnecessary.
He reiterated the Ministry’s support for IPID’s independent mandate, and affirmed that all entities must operate without fear or favour, including investigating high-ranking officials if needed.
In closing, he expressed appreciation to the Members for their probing and constructive questions, especially those that provided context and challenged the Department to improve. He thanked them for their oversight, and affirmed the importance of their continued engagement.
Chairperson's closing comments
The Chairperson thanked the Deputy Ministers, acknowledging their consistent cooperation and professionalism, and shared positive feedback received from Members. She emphasised unity across the Department, stating that all officials must be aligned and operate as one team within the same institution.
She reflected that the Committee had had a productive and fruitful engagement. She reiterated that they would closely monitor the commitments made by the Department until the end of the 2025/26 financial year. The Committee particularly appreciated the Department's shift towards a victim-centred approach, aligning with the National Council of Provinces' (NCOP's) stance that services must be community-focused.
She also welcomed the introduction of lifestyle audits as a performance and accountability measure, requesting specific feedback on the outcomes, particularly how many senior managers had failed the audit, and what actions would be taken against those who may pose a conflict of interest. She emphasised that this cleanup was critical for restoring trust and integrity in the system.
She acknowledged the Department’s confidence in obtaining a clean audit again this financial year. She reminded everyone that Parliament assesses departments through tools like the Auditor-General of South Africa (AGSA) reports and the implementation of their recommendations. She also cited Section 76 of the Constitution, which allows MPs to raise concerns freely in these forums.
The meeting was adjourned.
