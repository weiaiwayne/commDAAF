Meeting Summary
Video
The Portfolio Committee on Water and Sanitation met with National Treasury and the Auditor-General of South Africa (AGSA) to discuss the financial and operational challenges facing Johannesburg Water. The Committee's primary concern was the entity's sustainability as the water provider for South Africa's main economic hub.
AGSA reported that Johannesburg Water was financially stable on paper, with positive net assets and a recorded cash balance of R4 billion. The Committee challenged this, noting that the R4 billion represented a loan account entry on the balance sheet rather than accessible cash, as funds were swept daily into the City of Johannesburg’s (CoJ) central account. National Treasury attributed the entity’s long-term decline to weak municipal governance, poor capital budgeting, and a failure to treat Johannesburg Water as a self-sustaining business, which had led to chronic under-investment in infrastructure.
A significant focus of discussion was the sweeping arrangement. AGSA stated that its audit found that sweeping contributed to 30% of late payments to suppliers, while 70% resulted from internal inefficiencies within Johannesburg Water. Committee Members contested this, citing oversight visits that indicated sweeping was a major cause of delays, hindering the entity’s ability to pay contractors and maintain infrastructure. Members highlighted the disparity between internal financial issues and outstanding debts to major contractors, emphasising that sweeping had significant practical consequences for service delivery.
Both AGSA and National Treasury underscored the importance of ring-fencing Johannesburg Water’s revenue and establishing clear service-level agreements with CoJ as part of critical reforms. National Treasury outlined measures to develop a service compact by the start of the next financial year, granting Johannesburg Water greater operational autonomy. Committee Members pressed for urgent intervention, stricter regulation of sweeping, and consequences for the City providing misleading information. They stressed that Johannesburg’s water security was a matter of national importance, requiring that revenue collected by Johannesburg Water be reinvested into the service and that infrastructure funding not remain locked in loan accounts.
The Committee emphasised the need for robust internal controls, improved financial management, and accountability for poor decisions. Members noted that modern management tools, including technology innovations, should be employed to support the utility, given the size of Johannesburg’s population and its critical economic role.
Deputy Minister David Mahlobo concluded by expressing the Department’s support for the Committee’s oversight and commitment to reforms that would ensure revenue was reinvested into services, while highlighting ongoing collaboration with Rand Water and initiatives to unlock major infrastructure investments. The Committee acknowledged the responses as satisfactory but reiterated the urgent need for systemic improvements to safeguard Johannesburg Water’s functionality and South Africa’s broader water security.
Meeting report
Opening remarks
The Chairperson opened the meeting by greeting all attendees and thanking them for joining the virtual engagement concerning Johannesburg Water, which included presentations from National Treasury and the Auditor-General of South Africa (AGSA).
He confirmed apologies from Ms Pemmy Majodina, Minister of Water and Sanitation, who was on sick leave, as well as from Mr M Dlelanga (ANC), Deputy Minister Isaac Seitlholo, and Ms M Kobe (ActionSA). He noted that Deputy Minister Mahlobo had indicated that he would need to be released from the meeting early.
The Chairperson expressed appreciation for the comprehensive reports from National Treasury and AGSA. He emphasised that the Committee's focus was not on Johannesburg Water's finances broadly, but specifically on the entity's sweeping arrangements, water losses, infrastructure crisis, and the way forward, as these were of serious concern for ensuring a reliable water supply for residents. He asked the presenters not to refer to other entities and to confine their presentations strictly to Johannesburg Water. He noted that the AGSA report stated that R4 billion was secured if needed, but in his view, this money existed only on the books and not in the bank and indicated this issue would be revisited during the discussions.
Department of Water and Sanitation’s (DWS) overview
Mr David Mahlobo, Deputy Minister of Water and Sanitation, explained that the Department was working jointly with National Treasury as part of the metro trading services reform process. He noted that Mr Enoch Godongwana, Minister of Finance, had convened a meeting with all metros on reforms relating to water, sanitation, and electricity, attended by Mr Ashor Sarupen, Deputy Minister of Finance, and Mr Isaac Seitlholo, Deputy Minister of Water and Sanitation.
He said mayors had agreed that the support programme, reinforced by the incentive introduced by National Treasury, created a significant opportunity to stabilise municipal utilities. Deputy Minister Mahlobo emphasised that trading services were essential for institutional autonomy, governance, and sound financial and asset management. He argued that resources should be ring-fenced to safeguard reinvestment into water services and highlighted planned amendments to the Water Services Act that would professionalise water utilities, introduce performance-based contracts, and strengthen licensing under Sections 19 and 20.
He stated that implementation models used by municipalities such as the City of Johannesburg (CoJ) were inefficient and outdated, and that water services should operate as business entities. Johannesburg’s infrastructure had deteriorated due to chronic under-investment, and current allocations remained insufficient. He referred to work underway between the Department, the World Bank, the Development Bank of Southern Africa (DBSA), and the Presidential Working Group. While some investments had begun, he cautioned that they remained inadequate. A consolidated report from the Department, AGSA, and technical experts was being finalised.
He added that CoJ continued to use a sweeping arrangement that disrupted access to Johannesburg Water’s revenue streams, and he stressed that full ring-fencing should begin early in the next financial year. He also reported that electricity remained the principal revenue engine for municipalities. He affirmed that Minister Godongwana had set deadlines to conclude turnaround plans and that the Department would continue supporting metros including eThekwini. He thanked the Committee for its oversight and expressed confidence that coordinated work between legislators, communities, experts, NGOs, and government could reverse the decline.
Dr Sean Phillips, Director-General, noted that he co-chaired the Water and Sanitation Workstream of the Presidential Johannesburg Working Group. The Workstream had been meeting regularly with civil-society organisations and business representatives to monitor progress on Johannesburg Water’s turnaround strategy. Johannesburg Water continued to struggle to pay contractors responsible for critical capital projects, including reservoir upgrades and pumping-station improvements. Some contractors had halted work due to non-payment, which Johannesburg Water attributed to the sweeping arrangement that limited access to its own funds.
Briefing by AGSA on Johannesburg Water Financial Management and other key matters
Ms Thabelo Musisinyani, Business Unit Leader, AGSA, reminded Members of the structure of the water sector, which included national entities, water boards, and municipalities. She explained that she and her colleague were mainly responsible for the water boards, while AGSA's provincial offices were responsible for municipal audits. She introduced Mr Mavundla and Ms Sindisiwe Zama, Senior Manager, who would lead the discussion.
Mr Msizi Mavundla, Deputy Business Unit Leader: Gauteng, confirmed his role and stated that AGSA had been auditing Johannesburg Water for some time and had noted the issues raised by the Committee. He explained that AGSA had raised findings where relevant, particularly regarding compliance with applicable prescripts. He acknowledged the infrastructure challenges and confirmed that various findings had been issued on these matters. He further confirmed that AGSA would address its understanding of the sweeping account arrangements. He then handed over to Ms Sindisiwe Zama to deliver the formal presentation.
AGSA reported that Johannesburg Water retained positive net assets of R15.6 billion and had delivered consistent surpluses over three years. Its overall cash balance was R4.044 billion, although this was held largely within the City’s sweeping account. AGSA explained that the entity’s underlying financial position remained stable, but operational indicators were deteriorating significantly. Debt-impairment levels rose to 90%, debtor-collection periods averaged 74 days, and creditors were being paid in 87 days. These trends created material risks for operational continuity.
AGSA stated that Johannesburg Water continued to breach the MFMA requirement to pay suppliers within 30 days. Internal inefficiencies accounted for about 70% of late payments, while the sweeping arrangement contributed 30%. Because the sweeping mechanism cleared Johannesburg Water’s accounts daily, the entity depended on the City to release its operational funds; the City did not always release these funds timeously. This caused delayed payments to contractors, slowed infrastructure projects, and increased the severity of water outages.
Infrastructure deterioration and material irregularities
AGSA described the state of Goudkoppies Wastewater Treatment Works as severely degraded. Rusted pipes, cracked concrete structures, silt-filled tanks, and failing mechanical equipment weakened treatment capacity. Effluent quality did not comply with the National Environmental Management Act or the National Water Act.
Johannesburg Water’s network infrastructure was deteriorating at an accelerating pace. Pipe bursts increased from 20 729 in 2023/24 to 33 436 in 2024/25. Johannesburg Water restored only 67% of bursts within the 48-hour target against an 85% target. In 2023/24, emergency water-tankering services cost R30.48 million, a direct result of infrastructure failures.
Water losses remained high at R2.8 billion, representing 32.94% of system input. Physical losses accounted for 23.72%, while commercial losses (illegal connections, metering errors, and data-transfer issues) accounted for 9.22%. AGSA warned of a deepening water and infrastructure crisis driven by ageing assets, stagnant maintenance, and delayed capital projects. While Johannesburg Water was not at immediate risk of being a going concern, operational sustainability was under acute strain.

Briefing by National Treasury on the financial position of the City of Johannesburg and implications for water security
Mr Willem Voigt, Director: Local Government Budget Analysis, National Treasury, and Mr Sydney Serogole outlined National Treasury's view that the City of Johannesburg's decline was the result of long-term, self-reinforcing weaknesses in governance, financial decision-making, human-resource capability, and internal support systems. Utility entities such as Johannesburg Water and City Power were not treated as trading services capable of generating surpluses for capital investment, and Council budget decisions had entrenched under-investment despite warnings from Group Finance.
External pressures — the pandemic, loadshedding, and rising costs — further weakened utilities. Non-revenue water remained between 34% and 45%. The provision for bad debt had risen to three times Johannesburg Water’s annual capital expenditure, severely limiting investment. Collection rates remained below benchmarks, and capital-expenditure levels continued to decline.
National Treasury emphasised that distribution losses — 72% physical and 28% commercial — significantly undermined sustainability. The City's own financial performance deepened the challenge. CoJ reported an overall deficit in 2022/23. Several municipal entities, including utilities, posted losses. This placed pressure on Johannesburg Water to subsidise operations across the municipal group.
Governance challenges and the sweeping arrangement
National Treasury said the sweeping arrangement was contributing to operational instability. While the practice was lawful and intended to optimise cash management, in Johannesburg it was associated with substantial delays in returning entity-generated revenue. Entities capable of generating surpluses were unable to pay contractors or advance capital projects. Transparent revenue-sharing arrangements and clear service-level agreements did not exist, and the absence of such frameworks weakened financial governance.
CoJ had allocated R1.7 billion in the capital budget to water and sanitation, which signalled commitment to maintaining investment levels, but systemic weaknesses limited the impact of such allocations.
Reform requirements and path forward
National Treasury argued that grant funding alone would not fix Johannesburg Water's structural problems. Long-term under-investment — estimated at more than R25 billion — inefficiencies, split responsibilities, and weak internal integration prevented Johannesburg Water from managing large infrastructure systems effectively.
Ring-fencing was identified as essential to improving financial discipline and operational sustainability. Reform programmes under the Division of Revenue Act required metros to configure trading services into viable business units to access future performance-based incentives. Johannesburg had begun organisational restructuring and capacity development to support this shift.
National Treasury concluded that infrastructure, financial performance, and governance would remain under pressure unless reform elements were implemented in an integrated manner.

Discussion
The Chairperson referred to page 6 of the AGSA's report, where it stated that there was enough cash held by Johannesburg Water. The Chairperson explained that this was misleading because the amount referred to was in fact a loan account. He reported that the Committee had engaged with Johannesburg Water. Although the entity initially denied the existence of the R4 billion, it ultimately conceded that the amount existed as a loan account.
The Chairperson stated that the Committee had asked whether this R4 billion could immediately be transferred to Johannesburg Water, and Johannesburg Water confirmed that it could not.
The Chairperson requested that the AGSA clarify why the report presented the amount as available cash when it existed only as a balance-sheet loan and not as real, accessible funds. He added that he was also concerned about page 5 of the AGSA’s presentation, particularly the sixth bullet point, which indicated that the sweeping arrangement contributed only 30% to the delays.
He noted that, based on the Committee’s engagements, sweeping appeared to contribute to more than 90% of the challenges. He described how Johannesburg Water reportedly had to “go on its knees” to request that funds be transferred from the CoJ account before contractors could be paid. The Chairperson asked the AGSA to provide clarity on these matters.
He then addressed a second question to National Treasury. He said that the Committee agreed with National Treasury's view that Johannesburg Water was being run as a department rather than as a business. He asked whether there was any mechanism to short-circuit the current situation so that the entity could be run more professionally, in line with a business model. He noted that during the oversight visit to Johannesburg Water, it became clear that operational decisions depended on Council processes that had no defined timelines. He asked whether National Treasury could take steps to accelerate the reform process so that Johannesburg Water could operate effectively as a business unit.
Ms Zama clarified that when AGSA referred to cash being available, it was based on the end-of-year position reflecting a positive cash balance of R4 billion within the sweeping account. She confirmed that this amount was not physically held in Johannesburg Water’s bank account but was managed by CoJ. Johannesburg Water had to request funds from CoJ on a daily basis when it needed to pay suppliers. She acknowledged that although CoJ generally released funds, the transfers were not always made when requested and were at times delayed or insufficient.
She stated that the indication that the sweeping arrangement accounted for only 30% of late supplier payments was derived from a sample-based audit. AGSA had examined the root causes of late payments in the previous financial year and found that 30% were attributable to the sweeping arrangement, while the remaining 70% resulted from Johannesburg Water’s internal deficiencies. She emphasised that the 30% figure related to a sample and not the full population, and she confirmed that Johannesburg Water’s management had agreed with the findings. She added that the same finding had been issued in the current year, and AGSA was still assessing the precise percentage impact for the current period.
Mr Serogole said that CoJ should work collectively with stakeholders to ensure that, by the next financial year, Johannesburg Water could operate fully as an independent entity. He noted that key requirements included strengthening the quality of the board and finalising a service compact. He explained that service compacts were negotiation instruments that needed to set out clear revenue-sharing arrangements and the defined roles of the board and management. He emphasised that the service compact had to be agreed upon by both CoJ and Johannesburg Water.
He stated that the drafting of the service compact was expected to begin in early December and that it needed to be adopted before the start of the new financial year. He said that the service compact was critical to ensuring that Johannesburg Water became a fully functional entity.
Mr S Dithebe (ANC) thanked both AGSA and National Treasury. He asked the National Treasury why the 20-year period studied (2002–2022) was limited to those years, noting that much had changed between 2022 and 2025. He said this was important in light of the introduction of metro trading services, which would result in entities such as Johannesburg Water operating as utilities based on a business model. He requested clarity on why the data had not been extended beyond 2022 and whether there had been significant developments after that period, given the reforms presented, including the Presidency’s intervention involving multiple stakeholders, such as business.
Mr Dithebe noted that Johannesburg Water had reported surpluses, unlike City Power, Pikitup, and other entities. He asked for clarity on whether the R4 billion swept into CoJ's main account was derived entirely from revenue collected by Johannesburg Water. He linked this question to AGSA's earlier response on the R1.8 billion surplus versus the loan amount. He said this question could be answered by either AGSA or National Treasury.
He asked a final question regarding the sweeping arrangement. He observed that the introduction of trading services appeared likely to bring a major turnaround and asked what AGSA and National Treasury recommended should be done about the sweeping system, particularly as CoJ had stated during the oversight visit that the arrangement had existed since 2002. He asked what could be done to prevent harmful effects on entities, especially now that they were expected to operate as utilities under a business model.
Ms Zama explained that there were currently no direct recommendations to change the sweeping arrangement. She noted that the system had been in place for many years and that the R4 billion was disclosed as a loan. She highlighted that CoJ subsidised other entities through the same arrangement, particularly those operating at a deficit. She added that while this approach helped CoJ provide services across the City, delays in transferring funds negatively affected Johannesburg Water by disrupting payments to suppliers and slowing projects. She emphasised that the CoJ needed to determine an arrangement that best supported service delivery for residents. She clarified that AGSA could not prescribe whether funds should remain with Johannesburg Water or be pooled under the City, but stressed that the chosen system must serve citizens effectively.
Mr Serogole observed that the reforms had been triggered by sector-wide challenges. He noted that 20 years ago, Johannesburg Water had operated efficiently under a management contract and had been considered a leading utility model. He reported that over time, standards had declined, with similar collapses occurring in other municipalities, such as the City of Ekurhuleni and City of Tshwane, which had dismantled previously well-functioning business units. He argued that the 20-year trend illustrated declining performance, which necessitated the current reforms. He added that Treasury, in collaboration with sector departments, had diagnosed the problem and agreed on intervention measures to halt the decline.
Mr Dithebe clarified that his earlier question had been posed in the context previously outlined. He acknowledged the usefulness of the 20-year analysis but argued that the last two to three years were equally significant, as recent reforms appeared to address the decline. He reported that earlier that day, the Committee had engaged with the Department, water boards, and Rand Water, which he described as a model of good governance. He noted that Rand Water supplied 76% of Johannesburg’s water in collaboration with the City of Ekurhuleni and the City of Tshwane, and that it had indicated using artificial intelligence (AI) to assist in financial management. He suggested that if Johannesburg Water had operated efficiently two decades ago without such technology, modern innovations like AI should certainly be considered today. He stressed that Johannesburg served a very large population and required modern management tools.
Mr Dithebe agreed with Deputy Minister Mahlobo that the Committee should continue to monitor developments. He acknowledged the challenges facing Johannesburg but argued that political leadership demonstrated an interest in improvement. He proposed that maintaining pressure on leadership would strengthen progress and concluded by thanking the Chairperson.
Mr S Moore (DA) referred to earlier engagements with DWS and highlighted that Johannesburg was home to 10% of South Africa’s population, produced half of Gauteng’s GDP, and that Gauteng accounted for approximately one-third of the country’s GDP. He explained that one in every six rands generated in South Africa came from Johannesburg, describing it as the country’s economic hub. He stated that Johannesburg’s success was critical but noted that presentations indicated it was currently failing. He added that while this was not the Department of Water and Sanitation’s fault, the situation affected the country as a whole. Therefore Johannesburg required closer scrutiny, although similar challenges existed in other municipalities.
Mr Moore directed his first question to AGSA, asking whether CoJ truly held Johannesburg Water’s R4 billion. He clarified that, while similar to a question previously asked by the Chairperson, his inquiry was intended to focus more specifically. He contended that Johannesburg Water did not have the funds and that they had been swept to the City. He highlighted major challenges in accessing the cash. He asked AGSA to confirm whether it was confident that CoJ was indeed holding at least R4 billion in cash and not merely reflecting it as a credit entry.
His second question to AGSA concerned unpaid invoices. He noted that AGSA had reported 50% to 70% non-payment levels in its sample tests. He asked whether AGSA differentiated between small and large invoices, observing that there was a significant difference between, for example, a Johannesburg Roads Agency invoice for pothole repairs and multimillion-rand invoices for major infrastructure projects. He cited a site in Brixton, which the Committee had previously visited, and noted that it had again not been completed due to non-payment when he revisited it at the start of the month. He reported that contractors remained unpaid and that project completion had been further delayed.
Mr Moore addressed National Treasury, asking whether the sweeping arrangement used by CoJ aligned with Treasury policy or guidelines. He referenced Treasury's earlier remark that the system served residents' interests but stated that, as a Johannesburg resident, the sweeping of revenue for water and electricity resulted in outages, which did not appear to be in residents' best interests.
Mr Voigt clarified that the R4 billion had originally been transferred to Johannesburg Water but was moved back to CoJ on 1 July. He explained that the funds were therefore unavailable to pay creditors or support Johannesburg Water at that point. On the sweeping arrangement, he stated that no specific national guidelines currently governed how sweeping should be carried out. He emphasised the importance of ring-fencing surpluses but noted that there was no policy determining what portion should remain with the City for subsidising general services versus trading entities such as Johannesburg Water. He reported that Treasury was working with the City to develop such a guideline and to establish service-level agreements with all entities. He added that under the trading services reform, the amount ring-fenced for entities would need to increase over time, eventually resulting in the full amount being retained by those entities.
Ms Zama stated that Treasury had already addressed the issue of the R4 billion. She confirmed that AGSA differentiated between small and large invoices, explaining that the audit approach was risk-based and focused more heavily on larger invoices. She added that AGSA assessed the causes of non-payment and that engagements in the current audit cycle were ongoing to determine whether delays on long-term projects were driven by the sweeping arrangement. She reported that management had cited cash-flow challenges as the primary issue from suppliers’ perspectives but that AGSA would continue to assess whether the sweeping system contributed further to delays.
The Chairperson noted that during the Committee’s oversight visit, it had become clear that Johannesburg Water and the Metro were setting emerging contractors up for failure by not paying them, despite intentions to support new contractors in building businesses. He asked National Treasury whether there was any way to prevent this, even if it was not technically within Treasury’s mandate, and whether it could suggest measures to ensure emerging contractors were not harmed by payment failures. He requested the presenters to respond and allowed Mr Moore to continue if his questions remained unanswered.
Mr Moore stated that he was willing to wait for the response to the Chairperson’s question but indicated that he still had further questions. The Chairperson asked whether he was satisfied with the answers already provided. Mr Moore replied that he was not and expressed concern about the responses. The Chairperson acknowledged this and requested that Mr Moore’s concerns be addressed.
Mr Moore reported that both National Treasury and AGSA had confirmed that the R4 billion had been swept back into CoJ’s account on 1 July. He explained that he and the Chairperson were familiar with the issue due to a prior information request concerning Johannesburg Water, following similar claims from the City. He noted that the earlier request showed that no such amount had been taken out or returned on 30 June or 1 July, suggesting a possible error by the Speaker, but emphasised that AGSA had confirmed the same information, which warranted concern.
He stressed that the repeated focus on the R4 billion reflected a significant and genuine problem. He highlighted that financial documents on overdue payments to service providers showed that outstanding debt owed to major contractors was more than ten times larger than smaller internal financial issues at Johannesburg Water. He argued that presenting the cause of late payments as 30% due to sweeping and 70% due to internal problems at Johannesburg Water was misleading, as internal issues represented a much smaller absolute financial value compared to major contracts. He expressed discomfort with the responses provided by National Treasury and AGSA, noting that civil society organisations had obtained information raising red flags.
Mr Moore added that National Treasury had confirmed there were no regulations on sweeping arrangements. He expressed concern that CoJ’s stated intention to move away from sweeping appeared uncertain, as previous meetings indicated officials would still negotiate with Treasury. He observed from his public service experience that “negotiate” implied a desire to continue with the sweeping arrangement for as long as possible. He concluded by stating that he was not satisfied with the responses and expected National Treasury and AGSA to take a firmer stance.
Mr Mavundla acknowledged Mr Moore’s concerns and explained that sweeping was a long-standing practice linked to Treasury processes, which the City of Johannesburg continued to use for that reason. He clarified that the key question under consideration was whether CoJ currently held the R4 billion in cash. He reported that AGSA’s audit up to the end of June showed the City’s cash balance was lower than R4 billion. However, from an audit perspective, the amount was correctly accounted for, as CoJ had recorded the amount owed to Johannesburg Water, and Johannesburg Water had recorded the corresponding amount owed by the City. AGSA therefore considered the financial statements accurate because both entities acknowledged the debt.
He stated that Johannesburg Water had sufficient positive cash flow to sustain operations and that the books were properly presented. Addressing the issue of late payments, he explained that AGSA’s responsibility was to verify whether Johannesburg Water paid suppliers within 30 days. The audit sample showed that the entity faced challenges. After investigating the causes, AGSA found that only 30% of delayed payments were due to the sweeping arrangement, as Johannesburg Water first had to request funding from CoJ before paying suppliers. The remaining 70% of delays were due to inefficiencies within Johannesburg Water’s own internal processes, unrelated to the sweeping system. He concluded by stating that AGSA was satisfied with its audit findings.
The Chairperson acknowledged AGSA’s responses but noted that while the matter might be a bookkeeping issue for auditors, National Treasury had indicated that the money had been paid in, whereas Mr Moore’s earlier application had shown it had not. The Chairperson requested further comment from Treasury.
Mr Serogole agreed with AGSA’s position and clarified that when Treasury referred to the money, it did not mean that the amount had been paid into Johannesburg Water’s bank account, but that it was reflected in CoJ’s financial records. He emphasised that the money remained within the broader municipal structure. He reminded the Committee that Section 65(2)(e) of the Municipal Finance Management Act (MFMA) required municipalities to pay suppliers within 30 days, and failure to do so constituted a violation of the law.
The Chairperson concluded that the R4 billion existed only as a financial record and was not available as cash in the bank.
Ms B Kegakilwe (ANC) stated that she would focus on the issue of sweeping arrangements. She explained that, according to best practice, sweeping should act in the interests of the group, the institution, and residents, with funds belonging to an entity returned to that entity’s account. Based on AGSA’s presentation, she observed that this did not appear to be happening in the City of Johannesburg. She highlighted major disparities in cash flow among entities, citing City Power as an example, which at times operated with a large negative balance. She argued that the City was not performing as expected.
She asked what actions the Department of Water and Sanitation and National Treasury planned to take regarding this conduct. She emphasised the government’s responsibility to ensure sweeping was correctly applied and monitored, regardless of the system used. She recalled that during a previous Committee visit, questions about the R4 billion had been met with excuses and that the requested Johannesburg Water bank statement could not be retrieved. She asserted that the recent presentation showed that the City had misrepresented the status of the funds and insisted that the City must account for this behaviour, emphasising that municipal entities should operate independently and only be intervened in when there were challenges or service failures.
Ms Kegakilwe asked why the City had misrepresented the matter and what actions would be taken, particularly given that the sweeping arrangement appeared not to be working. She inquired whether there was an intention to change the model or grant entities more autonomy. She raised a second question regarding the lack of proper service-level agreements between the City and its entities, including clear revenue-sharing rules, highlighting that this gap required urgent attention. Her final question concerned unauthorised, irregular, fruitless, and wasteful expenditure at Johannesburg Water. She noted that investigations had reportedly been conducted over the past three years. Yet, no report had been released, and she asked what the outcomes were and what consequence management had been implemented.
The Chairperson noted that Johannesburg Water was not present to respond to these questions and requested that the questions be forwarded, inviting AGSA to respond.
Mr Mavundla stated that he had noted the comments, particularly regarding sweeping and the lack of a proper service-level agreement, which Treasury had addressed in its presentation. He added that the issue of irregular expenditure and consequence management was a matter for the entity.
Mr Voigt reported that the City had submitted proposals to address the sweeping issue and that a final recommendation was expected for implementation in December. He expressed dissatisfaction with how the City had handled unauthorised, irregular, fruitless, and wasteful expenditure, particularly the reduction of amounts. He confirmed that Treasury had submitted section 6 and 216 documentation requesting information, which it would review. He emphasised concerns over the credibility of information, giving an example where the City reported R25 billion in outstanding creditors at the end of June but later reported only R11 billion by September, implying an incorrect R14 billion payment. He noted that Treasury expected improvements in cash-flow management, contractor management, and internal control systems.
The Chairperson asked Treasury what consequence management would follow when incorrect information was provided and who was responsible for implementing it. Mr Voigt stated that Treasury would continue to uphold equitable share allocations but that there was a threat of December allocations being withheld. He reported misconduct concerns involving the Accounting Officer, CFO, and management. He noted that a report from the Ministry of Finance had been submitted to the Council, though no feedback had been received. He concluded that mismanagement and non-compliance with the Municipal Finance Management Act persisted, and the Council had failed to address the issues.
The Chairperson noted that it was unclear whether the report had ever been served to the Council and asked Ms Kegakilwe if she was satisfied with the responses. She replied that she was satisfied and requested, through the Chairperson, that the Department ensure a service-level agreement was implemented between the City and its entities.
Positive developments and recommendations
Mr K Ramaila (ANC) noted that presentations indicated a positive direction for CoJ and Johannesburg Water, with officials previously transferred to the City now returned to the entity. He emphasised that Johannesburg Water’s solvency and ability to generate a surplus should be acknowledged and encouraged.
He discussed sweeping arrangements, noting that Treasury and AGSA had explained that sweeping was being used for cross-subsidisation. He observed that certain entities would never generate profits and would always require subsidisation, recommending that sweeping arrangements be governed by specific ratios to safeguard liquid entities. He noted that factual information showed that 70% of delayed payments were caused by internal weaknesses at Johannesburg Water, and only 30% by sweeping, demonstrating the need to improve internal financial management.
Mr Ramaila recommended that sweeping should not be open-ended, that specific ratios be applied to protect liquidity, and that the Capital Replacement Reserve (CRR) be cash-backed and held by Johannesburg Water to enable continuous infrastructure replacement. He also advocated for stronger ring-fencing of income to ensure liquidity remained protected and sweeping did not remove all available funds from the entity.
Mr Serogole agreed with these principles, emphasising that municipal funds generated through trading services should be reinvested to maintain infrastructure and ensure service quality. He warned that failure to invest would lead to service deterioration, negatively affecting residents.
Mr Mavundla reiterated that AGSA’s mandate was limited to auditing and that it would not take a position beyond its audit role.
Mr Moore thanked the panel and posed three questions. First, he asked whether reforms included measures to impose consequences for poor decisions. Second, he asked if sweeping large amounts of money, particularly when CoJ did not have R4 billion in cash, violated MFMA provisions requiring council approval for budget changes. Third, he requested a breakdown from AGSA of the value of invoices delayed due to sweeping versus Johannesburg Water’s internal weaknesses.
Mr Mavundla replied that AGSA had not found sweeping arrangements to violate the MFMA but would verify if any provisions directly addressed sweeping and report back. He noted that the invoice breakdown was based on the 2023/24 audit, and the 2024/25 audit was still finalising results.
Mr Serogole stated that under new reforms, there would be significant consequences for poor decisions, with underperforming municipalities losing access to grants and funding, enforcing accountability across management structures.
Mr Moore thanked the Chairperson, noting the importance of confirming that the R4 billion existed only on the books and not as cash, and highlighted Johannesburg’s role as the country’s economic hub. He expressed satisfaction with the responses.
Closing remarks
The Chairperson thanked participants and noted that no further questions had been raised. He emphasised that the meeting’s purpose was to ensure South Africa’s economic stability, highlighting that Johannesburg’s failure would have national consequences. He clarified that the R4 billion was only on a loan account and not fully available, and called for collaboration between National Treasury, Parliament, and Johannesburg Water to implement financial ratios ensuring cross-subsidisation without undermining liquidity. He warned that failure to address these issues could place Johannesburg Water, like City Power, in a severe crisis, and stressed the importance of water and sanitation as essential services.
Deputy Minister Mahlobo thanked the Chairperson and expressed satisfaction with the conclusions reached. He stated that Johannesburg Water could not be allowed to fail and noted that reforms would ensure revenue collected by the entity was reinvested into services. He added that Rand Water would collaborate more closely with Johannesburg Water. He highlighted the need to unlock major investments, including developments in Lanseria, as part of addressing water infrastructure challenges. On behalf of the Minister, he conveyed appreciation to all Committee members and colleagues and wished participants a pleasant afternoon.
The meeting was adjourned.
