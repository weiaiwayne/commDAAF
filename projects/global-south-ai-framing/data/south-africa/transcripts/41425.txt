Meeting Summary
Video
The Committee met to deliberate on Budget Vote 14 (Local Government) in the 2025 Western Cape Appropriation Bill. The opening remarks from the Provincial Minister highlighted the contrasting performance of Western Cape municipalities compared to the rest of South Africa. While 157 of the country's 257 municipalities were reported to be in distress, the Western Cape's 30 municipalities showed stronger governance, with 20 receiving clean audits.
Key challenges discussed included water security amid climate change, disaster preparedness, and the need for infrastructure investment.
The Minister emphasised the province's low per capita water usage and the importance of maintaining service delivery standards despite population growth and budgetary constraints.
The Head of Department (HOD) presented the 2025/26 budget of R440.022 million, outlining allocations for the Cost-of-Living Adjustment (CoLA), disaster risk mitigation, and infrastructure projects such as the Hessequa Energy Project, aimed at reducing electricity costs.
Members expressed concern over staff being required to deliver high performance despite constrained budgets, emphasising the importance of employee welfare. Questions were raised on the financial implications of upcoming local government elections, coalition governance challenges, and balancing agricultural land needs with other development demands. Clarification was sought on energy security projects, particularly affordability benefits, maintenance responsibilities, and funding for securing municipal infrastructure. Other concerns included disproportionate funding favouring the City of Cape Town, inadequate rural municipality support, and the effectiveness of capacity-building programmes.
Members also discussed disaster management funding, oversight, and measurable impact. The Chairperson stressed proactive water resilience considering below-average rainfall forecasts. Attention was given to the Climate Change Act’s mandate for municipal climate strategies, with questions on their integration into planning. Members examined the balance between municipal revenue-raising and affordability, the Department’s role in infrastructure funding beyond national allocations, and the risks of increasing municipal debt for water and energy projects. They called for clearer departmental programme alignment and urged executive-level engagement to strengthen economic development and public-private partnerships (PPPs) for sustainable municipal finance.
Following deliberations, the Committee voted in favour of the budget, with one Member registering opposition.
Plans were finalised for public hearings on the Support to Municipalities Bill and the Independent Municipal Demarcation Bill, scheduled for late April across three locations. The meeting concluded with an emphasis on inclusive participation and forward-looking strategies to address municipal challenges, particularly in rural and underserved areas.
Meeting report
The Chairperson welcomed everyone present at the meeting and allowed Members to introduce themselves. He stated that they would then proceed to the main item on the agenda, which was the deliberation on Budget Vote 14: Local Government in the 2025 Western Cape Appropriation
Bill
. However, before doing so, he asked Mr Anton Bredell, Western Cape Provincial Minister of Local Government, Environmental Affairs and Development Planning, to give some opening remarks. He added that they would then hand over to Mr Graham Paulse, Head of Department (HOD), Western Cape Department of Local Government, to do the same.
Minister’s Remarks
Minister Bredell stated that it was important to consider the South African local government context when engaging with the Department. There were 257 local governments in the country, of which 157 were in distress, 96 had adopted unfunded budgets, and 41 were under administration.
In contrast, he said, the Western Cape had 30 local governments, 20 of which had received clean audits with no findings. The City of Cape Town was the best-performing metro in the country, and struggling municipalities such as Knysna, Beaufort West, and Kannaland were receiving support from the Department and the Treasury to improve their status. Several studies, he noted, indicated that Western Cape municipalities outperformed the rest of the country.
He thanked Mr Paulse and his team for their hard work, stating that significant effort had gone into reaching the current standards, and that maintaining them would require even greater effort.
Regarding the State of the Nation Address, Minister Bredell said that President Cyril Ramaphosa had prioritised efficient local government and water infrastructure. He supported these priorities and noted that the
White Paper
for Local Government was under review to determine whether legislation should be amended to assist municipalities. He cautioned that, in the Western Cape, the current legislation was functioning well, and changes should not be made for the wrong reasons.
He emphasised that water was essential for sustaining communities and economic growth. The Western Cape, already a water-scarce region, faced further challenges from climate change, with hotter and drier conditions predicted. Over the past decade, the population had grown by 19.6 percent, and a further 1.2 million residents were expected in the next ten years. He urged the Committee to consider the impact of this growth on services such as water.
Minister Bredell said that improving water management, reducing leakages, increasing water reuse, and investing in infrastructure would be key focus areas. The province’s water use, 144 litres per person, was the lowest in the country, attributing this to successful public campaigns, which he said should continue.
Regarding disaster management, he stated that climate change had linked drought and wildfires in summer with flooding in winter. Preparations for the coming winter were necessary. He added that effective disaster management required skilled personnel and proper equipment, which were costly but essential to avoid far greater expenses in the long term.
Presentation by the Western Cape Department of Local Government on MTEF Infographics
Mr Paulse took the Committee through a presentation on the Department of Local Government’s Medium Term Expenditure Framework (MTEF).
The Department’s 2025/26 budget totalled R440.022 million, reflecting an increase of R6.331 million (1.46 percent) from the 2024/25 revised estimate of R433.691 million. This growth was mainly driven by an additional R2.345 million for the CoLA, effective 1 April 2025, along with earmarked funding for the Fire and Rescue Capacity Grant to strengthen municipal disaster preparedness and response. Over the MTEF, allocations decreased to R353.019 million in 2026/27 and R358.787 million in 2027/28. The budget aligned with the principles guiding the main budget process, focusing on supporting municipalities in fulfilling their developmental and service delivery mandates. Strategic priorities were maintained, safeguarding projects linked to provincial and departmental priorities, and ensuring compliance with the Municipal Structures and Municipal Systems Acts. The Department had also reprioritised spending to absorb the continued impact of the CoLA implemented in April 2024 and to adjust for prior budget cuts.
Over the 2024 MTEF, the Department received earmarked funding for two key initiatives. An allocation of R10 million in 2025/26 and R3.318 million in 2026/27 would support dedicated project management for the successful rollout of the Sustainable Infrastructure Development and Finance Facility (SIDAFF) Programme. Additionally, R8.711 million in 2025/26 and R1.5 million in 2026/27 had been allocated for proactive disaster risk mitigation, including the deployment of artificial intelligence systems and technology for anticipatory disaster risk management.

Discussion
Mr N Masipa (DA) inquired about the impact on employees, noting that they were being expected to achieve near-impossible tasks with a limited budget. Regarding staff welfare, he acknowledged the resource constraints but emphasised that the effect on staff also required attention. He asked whether Members could offer greater support from a resolution perspective, considering that limited resources demanded maximum performance from staff.
Ms N Nkondlo (ANC), referring to page 748, asked about the upcoming local government elections and the issue of coalition governments. She enquired about the fiscal impact of this development at the local government level and what mitigation measures, if any, the Department had implemented in response to what appeared to be becoming the norm in the country and municipalities.
Referring to page 751, she asked about the legislation guiding the Department’s work, particularly the Spatial Planning and Land Use Management Act (SPLUMA). She noted that, in the previous financial year, almost 555 applications for subdivision and rezoning had been received from municipalities, and questioned how land use was being managed to balance the needs of agriculture, the province’s largest industry, with other demands. She also asked whether the Department had tools or mechanisms to address these competing requirements.
On page 755, under the strategic priority of inclusive growth, she sought clarity on strategic priority number one, regarding energy security and the Just Energy Transition. She referred to the recent role of the United States President in promoting the just energy approach and related funding. She asked what impact these developments might have on provincial energy projects and whether the Department had considered such implications.
Mr G Marais (FF+) asked, regarding the concluded Hessequa Energy Project, whether its purpose was solely to ensure energy supply during load-shedding or to also assist with the affordability or subsidisation of electricity costs. He noted that electricity affordability was a growing concern and asked if this was an objective in any future projects.
He further asked whether maintenance costs for the Hessequa infrastructure would be borne by the municipality or the province. Referring to page 778, he sought clarification on whether any of the R48.5 million allocated for municipal infrastructure was designated specifically for securing the infrastructure, rather than solely for installation.
Mr N Constable (PA) agreed with Mr Masipa regarding the lack of funding in light of growing service demands and environmental changes in the Western Cape. He emphasised the need to support the Department in securing additional funding, as it was responsible for assisting and monitoring municipalities.
Demographic spending appeared to favour the City of Cape Town, which received a disproportionately large share of provincial resources, leaving rural municipalities underfunded despite their greater need for support. He highlighted the importance of aligning budgets with rural municipalities facing capacity challenges.
The Department’s mission included monitoring municipal compliance and enhancing disaster response. However, he questioned whether current expenditures were achieving tangible outcomes. He enquired whether there was demonstrable improvement in service delivery efficiency and whether disaster management was adequately resourced to reduce future risks. He urged a forward-looking approach, suggesting existing assessments could inform better budgeting.
He asked how effective the proposed capacity-building initiatives were in enabling municipalities to meet their mandates, particularly in high-need areas. He also sought assurance that budgeted resources and grants would translate into increased local government capabilities.
Regarding the R10.2 million allocated for disaster mitigation, Mr Constable inquired about the specific measures and oversight in place to ensure effective utilisation, and what metrics the Department would use to assess the impact of disaster management spending on reducing the effects of natural disasters in municipalities.
The Chairperson noted that, for interest, the South African Weather Service (SAWS) had a briefing the previous Thursday regarding the likelihood of below-average rainfall in the Western Cape that year and its potential impact on water resources. Although the previous winter had been very wet, the same was not expected, underscoring the importance of the Department staying ahead with water resilience strategies.
Responses
Minister Bredell responded to Mr Masipa’s question by stating that limited resources were a long-term reality. The overall budget was fixed, and resources had to be shared among departments such as education and health, meaning there was no additional funding available. This placed considerable pressure on employees, who strived to perform at their best despite the challenges.
He expressed concern about the Department’s difficulty in competing with larger municipalities and the private sector regarding salary scales, noting that this led to the loss of skilled officials. While this turnover was not entirely negative, allowing new talent to enter and contributing to the broader national agenda, it required a careful balance between developing existing staff and recruiting new personnel.
Minister Bredell highlighted the need to provide employees with adequate tools and support, noting that Department engineers often travelled extensively between municipalities and that hands-on assistance was essential, as opposed to remote communication. Political stability within municipalities was crucial, as instability contributed to staff attrition. Responsibility for capacity retention lies with both the Department and local municipalities.
Mr Paulse stated that the Minister had addressed the issue of capacity and assured that they were trying their utmost. He explained that, beyond legislative mandates, there were implied and incidental mandates; for example, water and energy were not strictly provincial competencies but still required attention, adding pressure on staff.
He acknowledged the challenge with engineers, noting that although they appointed engineers who often moved to municipalities within a few months, the existing staff were tasked with additional responsibilities. He said the Department was urging the Premier to fill posts urgently to increase capacity. The Department was also collaborating with national and provincial departments, including water and sanitation, the South African Local Government Association (SALGA), and district and local municipalities, to alleviate pressure on its staff.
He explained that during a recent five-year planning session, academic experts and local government specialists projected an increase in coalition governments in local municipalities. He noted that while most coalition governments in the province were stable, some were unstable, leading to motions to remove mayors or speakers. The Department provided extensive support to municipalities affected by coalitions, recognising the financial impact, including costs related to the dismissal of senior managers following coalition changes. He emphasised the importance of monitoring these municipalities closely and providing on-site support, as coalition governance was becoming the new norm.
Regarding spatial planning, Mr Paulse stated that the Department worked closely with the Department of Environmental Affairs, which held primary responsibility for the SPLUMA. The Department facilitated collaboration with municipalities during Integrated Development Planning and infrastructure projects to ensure compliance with the Act. This cooperation also involved the Department of Infrastructure and Human Settlements to strengthen spatial planning efforts.
On the Hessequa Energy Project, he highlighted significant cooperation between the Department, the Department of the Premier, and the municipality. He commended Mr Marius Brand, Director: Municipal Infrastructure, and his engineers for their support of the municipality and noted that the project would reduce Hessequa’s demand on ESKOM, thereby lowering electricity costs for residents. He explained that the municipality’s billing from ESKOM would decrease and that electricity drawn from the municipality’s battery energy storage system would improve affordability.
Some municipal funding was allocated to securing infrastructure, such as fencing, boreholes and installing cameras to prevent vandalism. He explained that vandalism of infrastructure, including pumps and cables, was a significant problem, diverting funds away from other priorities such as housing. To address this, the Department had launched an anti-vandalism campaign to raise public awareness of the consequences.
Responding to Mr Constable’s concerns about demographic spending, he clarified that most of the Department’s general expenditure was based in the metro area, where the head office was located. However, grant funding was primarily directed towards rural municipalities, particularly for water and energy infrastructure, with a focus on areas experiencing capacity challenges. He acknowledged questions about investing in better-performing municipalities but maintained that prioritising rural areas was necessary.
He described capacity-building initiatives, including quarterly reporting requirements from municipalities to monitor grant performance. Engineers conducted site visits to verify that infrastructure was completed and functional before authorising payments, while disaster management staff assessed compliance with grant conditions.
Mr Paulse also reported an improved disaster management response in the province, especially regarding fires, thanks to investments by the Western Cape Provincial Government. These investments had increased capacity in district and local municipalities, enabling most fires to be addressed within the first hour, demonstrating effective municipal support.
Mr Kamal Makan, Director: Municipal Governance, responded to questions regarding coalition governments and their management, acknowledging that due to the number of hung councils in the province, changes in coalition compositions were inevitable. He explained that during transition periods, the Department supported municipalities by deploying designated persons when the Speaker was unavailable. He referred to Section 291A of the Structures Act, which empowered the Member of the Executive Council (MEC) to designate an official to ensure a smooth transition amid coalition changes and the resulting political consequences.
He added that the Department continuously provided capacity training, focusing especially on municipalities that had undergone coalition changes. This included broader adult training in specific fields, with additional emphasis placed on municipalities experiencing political transitions.
The Committee was aware of amendments currently being introduced in the Municipal Structures Amendment Act aimed at addressing and countering the impact of coalition governments.
Regarding preparations for the upcoming local government elections, he stated that the province had consistently been a leader. The MEC had published the determination of the number of councillors in the Provincial Gazette, and boundary determinations had also been published. The Department was working closely with the Demarcation Board on the ward delimitation process. Once this was finalised, the Establishment Amendment Notices for the affected municipalities, reflecting changes in councillor numbers, ward boundaries, or municipal boundaries, would be published accordingly.
Mr Brand responded to the question regarding the impact of the withdrawal of United States Agency for International Development (USAID) funding on the Just Energy Programme. He acknowledged that it had affected the broader provincial strategic rollout but explained that, at the municipal level, the SIDAFF programme and new service providers were specifically identifying and prioritising electricity and energy resilience projects. He confirmed that these municipal efforts would not be affected by the withdrawal. The Department was seeking donor financing institutions to assist with project preparation and planned to use a blended financing mechanism to attract private sector investment in the Energy Resilience Programme.
Regarding the Hessequa project, Mr Brand clarified that it would not only benefit by making the town free from load-shedding but would also advantage the community and users financially. He explained that municipalities were currently budgeting on the assumption of a 12% increase in tariffs, but by cross-subsidising the reduced costs resulting from lower ESKOM demand, they aimed to maintain or reduce tariffs compared to previous rates.
On operation and maintenance costs, he confirmed that these would be the responsibility of the municipality, as it would become the asset owner, rather than the Western Cape Government.
Finally, in response to the question on security, Mr Brand stated that security measures had been included in the project costs and that the Department was committed to ensuring the security of the facility.
Dr Sandra Greyling, Director: Municipal Support and Capacity Building, responded regarding the increased capabilities and the utilisation of grants. She explained that while most grant-funded projects specifically addressed challenges within municipalities, not all were focused solely on increasing municipal capabilities. The majority of the Department’s projects aimed at building capacity within various municipal sectors.
She acknowledged the difficulty in measuring increases in capability, noting that this was challenging because trained personnel sometimes left the municipalities, requiring capacity-building efforts to be repeated.
Ms Sonja Chinnian, Director: Disaster Operations, stated that the Department ensured risks were highlighted and, as the Head of Department had indicated, various risk assessments were conducted relating to fires and other hazards. All planning was directed towards reducing these risks.
She explained that lessons learned from debriefings were used to identify investment priorities. For example, the hazard flood model supported winter readiness by mapping flooding risks within the province.
Regarding fire services, Ms Chinnian said the Department implemented several capacity-building initiatives to strengthen municipal capabilities. These included training, equipment provision, and financial support, all aimed at improving response during the critical first hour. Research had shown that responding within this timeframe could significantly reduce the risk and impact of fires.
Ms A Supra-Vertue, Acting Deputy Director: Fire and Rescue Services, reported that from 1 November 2024 until the end of February 2025, there had been a total of 60 callouts for aerial resources involving the one-hour extended attack strategy. She stated that all incidents had been successfully managed. The budget for aerial attacks was R19 million. She explained that the one-hour response window was allocated to prevent further escalation of incidents.
Further Discussion
The Chairperson stated that he had recently seen a statement from the National Minister of Forestry, Fisheries and Environment regarding the implementation of the new Climate Change Act. He explained that the Act imposed requirements on local municipalities, including the development of localised climate strategies. Having worked on the legislation during a previous term in the National Assembly, he enquired whether this requirement was being or would be factored into plans. He acknowledged that many Western Cape municipalities were already ahead in this regard, which was commendable. He asked if the local government's perspective and support were being considered alongside these developments.
Ms Nkondlo sought clarification on where the issue of municipalities’ revenue-raising ability versus the affordability for citizens fell within government responsibilities. She enquired whether this matter was within the Department’s remit, and if so, which unit handled it, as opposed to what was managed by the Provincial Treasury. She raised this in the context of the earlier question regarding the Hessequa Energy Project and referenced the State of the Province Address visit to a water treatment plant. While acknowledging the plant’s quality, she noted the continuing challenge posed by high municipal costs in financially distressed municipalities and questioned how such costs aligned with public demand.
Ms Nkondlo further sought to understand the Department’s role in municipal infrastructure funding beyond allocations from the National Treasury, both grants and equitable share, as well as funding from the Provincial Revenue Fund or reserve funds. She mentioned recent public disclosures by municipalities such as Witzenberg, which had announced loans for infrastructure projects, and noted that the City of Cape Town was reportedly on its second loan.
Expressing concern over municipalities’ increasing recourse to debt markets for infrastructure financing, particularly for water and energy projects, she highlighted potential future liabilities for both citizens and municipalities. She requested an explanation of the Department’s role in understanding and managing the balance between revenue raising and affordability.
She asked for clarification regarding the apparent overlap between the two Department programmes. She noted that municipal performance monitoring, reporting, and evaluation fell under Programme Two, while municipal infrastructure was a sub-programme within Programme Three. Not being an expert or familiar with departmental structures, she requested clarification on the rationale and interface between these sub-programmes, especially regarding the budgeting and placement of certain facilities.
Mr Constable directed his remarks to Minister Bredell, responding to his earlier comments to Mr Masipa regarding limited resources. He expressed that this issue was a significant concern for him. While acknowledging that economic development fell under the Agriculture Department, he referenced Ms Nkondlo’s point about municipalities’ revenue bases. He observed that some municipalities lacked the revenue capacity to adequately deliver services.
It was time for the Department and its Economic Development counterpart to engage in discussions on how to enhance economic development projects within local government. He warned that, at some point, grants alone would no longer suffice to support municipalities effectively.
He proposed that conversations should take place at the executive level to explore ways to establish or improve economic development in the municipal sphere. Noting that there was no funding allocated for public-private partnerships (PPPs) in the current budget, he advocated for PPPs to support municipalities through economic development initiatives. This, he argued, would help create jobs and enable citizens to pay their municipal bills.
Mr Constable concluded by putting this suggestion forward for consideration and encouraging the relevant MECs to convene and discuss the matter.
Minister Bredell responded to Mr Constable’s question by clarifying that he did not mean people should simply get used to limited resources, but acknowledged that this had been the reality for the past ten years. He explained that the overall budget was limited. If more resources were allocated to one sector, such as local government, then others like health or education would receive less, as it was impossible to create more money. He stressed the need for economic growth to resolve this issue sustainably.
He agreed with Mr Constable that very few municipalities had successfully managed local economic development over the years. He mentioned that the Department had experts focusing on the unique opportunities of various towns, citing a small project on honeybush tea involving Ms Nozuko Zamxaka, Chief Director: Integrated Service Delivery, and her team. Although the project could be criticised given competing needs, he believed it could help rural communities professionalise and access broader markets.
Further responses
Minister Bredell also highlighted the importance of aligning skills development programmes, such as the Expanded Public Works Programme (EPWP) and the Community Work Programme (CWP), with local economic development. The aim was to ensure participants did not remain in such programmes indefinitely but gained skills to become self-sufficient within six months, allowing new participants to enter the cycle. He emphasised the need for ongoing debate and learning from both successes and failures in economic development projects.
Regarding Ms Nkondlo’s question, he acknowledged the challenge of balancing revenue raising with the affordability of essential services such as water and electricity. He referred to a survey conducted in collaboration with the University of Stellenbosch, aimed at defining the services required for a dignified life. Whereas the Constitution set minimum levels, for example, six kilolitres of water and 50 units of electricity, the Department’s most recent study from 2022 suggested a higher benchmark, 10 kilolitres of water and 150 units of electricity.
He posed the question of how to provide 150 units of electricity to a poor household without triggering unsustainable price increases or making services unaffordable for the middle class. He expressed optimism that it was possible, provided the benefits, such as reduced crime and poverty alleviation, were clearly understood and measured against the costs.
Minister Bredell explained that the Department continuously monitored municipal finances, encouraging municipalities to keep service costs reasonable. He highlighted concerns about rising tariffs influenced by factors such as Eskom’s price increases and municipalities’ use of availability fees to compensate for revenue losses. He cautioned that availability fees should not become an additional form of taxation.
He concluded by stressing the importance of ongoing debate and collaboration with the Standing Committee, recognising their oversight role and the value of learning from their experiences.
Mr Paulse explained that regarding the Climate Change Act, the Department worked closely with the HOD, Mr Gerhard Gerber, in the Department of Environmental Affairs and Development Planning, which was primarily responsible for driving and implementing the Act in the province. Their department provided a platform for this collaboration and worked in concert with Environmental Affairs.
He said their responsibility centred on understanding climate change data and information to better manage natural disasters, with an emphasis on mitigation and preparedness rather than just response and recovery. Disaster management was one key aspect of the climate change strategy led by his department, alongside water resilience initiatives in the province. Given the consequences of climate change, particularly for water security and disaster risk, the Department had received additional funding to develop initiatives to improve preparedness.
Mr Paulse noted that Minister Bredell had extensively discussed the “basket of services,” which aimed to determine the true cost of municipal services to citizens. The Department regularly conducted validation assessments to review municipal charges for rates and taxes, monitoring affordability and flagging where municipalities had become too expensive. They also performed indigent assessments to inform the Minister accordingly.
He mentioned ongoing calls for a revised financial model for municipalities, which was being considered by National Treasury, Cooperative Governance and Traditional Affairs (CoGTA), and SALGA, with everyone agreeing that the current model failed to adequately address fiscal pressures.
The Department had also undertaken revenue enhancement work in certain municipalities, identifying gaps in billing and levying rates and taxes on all properties. They had funded selected municipalities to support this revenue improvement effort.
Regarding municipal borrowing, Mr Paulse explained that the Provincial Treasury played a central role in reviewing loan applications, consulting with his Department to ensure municipalities did not overburden future generations and maintained sustainable debt levels. This process adhered to MFMA regulations requiring consultation with the Provincial Treasury and Finance Minister.
He acknowledged the need to connect with the Minister of Economic Development, noting that while that Department had previously assisted municipalities with economic development plans and initiatives, this support was currently inactive. He urged Economic Development to re-engage and help municipalities both develop plans and implement initiatives.
Meanwhile, his Department's Integrated Service Delivery directorate had identified small-scale economic projects in municipalities. By engaging with local communities at Thusong Centres and other venues, they identified groups with needs. They helped initiate projects such as sewing cooperatives or hairdressing businesses, thereby fostering grassroots economic activity.
Mr Brand explained that given the current fiscal realities and constraints impacting municipalities, particularly the diminishing fiscal flows for municipal infrastructure, the SIDAFF programme was established. Consultants were appointed to work directly with municipalities, assisting them within their existing financial realities by developing long-term financial plans and assessing gearing ratios to determine the best funding approaches for infrastructure projects.
He noted that, due to the day-to-day operational challenges municipalities faced, this team added capacity by helping move projects from feasibility to bankability. This was crucial because, under the new borrowing framework set by National Treasury, there was a growing reliance on attracting private sector investment. The focus was on engaging pension funds, insurance companies, and other asset managers to co-finance municipal infrastructure.
To achieve this, projects should be sufficiently de-risked to appeal to private investors. Loans would differ from conventional short-term municipal loans; instead, they would cover the full lifecycle cost of the asset, lower interest rates and reduce the financial burden on citizens, as these costs would not be directly passed on via increased rates and taxes.
Regarding economic development, Mr Brand highlighted their infrastructure-led economic development programme. This work was integrated within the JDMA subcommittee, which combined economic development and infrastructure planning. They identified catalytic infrastructure projects that could stimulate economic growth in towns, thereby increasing the local revenue base. He confirmed that the Department was actively involved in this JDMA approach.
Committee Vote
The Chairperson said that they were going to move on to discuss whether the Committee supported the budget vote. After that, they would proceed to the resolutions stage, at which point officials not involved in the resolutions would be excused.
He stated that he would put the question to the Committee on whether there was agreement to support the budget vote. He reminded Members that, as mentioned in their previous meeting on Friday, political parties had the opportunity to voice their opposition to the budget. However, to be thorough, they would go through the matter, Member by Member, to ensure no one felt excluded.
Mr P Johnson (DA), Mr D Wessels (DA), Mr Masipa, Mr Marais, Mr Constable and the Chairperson were in support of the budget vote.
Ms Nkondlo stated on behalf of the ANC that she registered her vote not to support.
The Chairperson informed her that she did not have a vote, but thanked her for her point. However, due to Ms Stoffel not being present due to connectivity issues, Ms Nkondlo was now an Alternate Member, and her vote would be considered. He indicated that the Committee has therefore agreed to support the vote.
Finalisation of Public Participation and Public Hearings
The Chairperson said they could move on to the outstanding item from last week, which was the finalisation of public participation and hearings for two pieces of legislation: the Support to Municipalities
Bill
, a Provincial Bill, and the Independent Municipal Demarcation
Bil
l, which had come through the NCOP.
He explained the proposal to hold three public hearings, in Vredendal, George, and Cape Town, to gather input on both bills, considering the tight timeframes, especially for the NCOP. The aim was to ensure rigorous public participation rather than a mere tick-box exercise.
The Chairperson said they had proposed those three locations and now needed to give Mr Johannes Coetzee, Committee Procedural Officer, the go-ahead to begin advertising the hearings. He then asked Mr Coetzee to confirm the proposed dates for the public hearings.
Mr Coetzee said the public hearings were scheduled for the 23rd, 29th, and 30th of April. He added that he had submitted the dates to the Chief Whip and the Programming Authority and hoped to receive approval by the next day to begin preparations and advertising.
He also mentioned that they would write to SALGA and all municipalities, asking them to facilitate communication with their ward councillors and encourage them to provide further input on the legislation.
Mr Constable expressed his disappointment that Central Karoo was not included in the programme. He requested that every effort be made to ensure that the voices of the Central Karoo people were heard, whether through comments or other means, so they can be fully involved in the programme.
The Chairperson responded that this concern applied across the province, and they would do their best to reach out and facilitate the process within the limits of their capacity and budget. He then confirmed that this was agreed upon by the Committee.
The meeting was adjourned.
