Title: Induction Workshop: Transport sector; DoT targets and major projects; Committee staff role
Date: 20 Aug 2024
Committee: 
URL: https://pmg.org.za/committee-meeting/39266/
============================================================

Meeting Summary
The Portfolio Committee's meeting reviewed the transport sector's five-year progress and the Department of Transport's 2024/25-2026/27 targets.
Committee Members raised concerns about the Department's oversight and management of underperforming entities, and stressed the need for realistic targets. They also discussed audit and reporting issues, noting that delayed Auditor-General engagements and late financial statement submissions contributed to a lack of accountability. The situation at the Passenger Rail Agency of South Africa was highlighted, with attention drawn to the long-standing suspension of a Deputy Director-General, and the need for detailed reporting on suspended officials.
Progress on achieving the objectives of the National Development Plan over the next six years was discussed, and concerns were raised over the underutilisation of local resources for licence machines and the need for better coordination on scholar transport issues. The Committee called for detailed reporting on the status of suspended entity officials. Another issue was setting unrealistic targets, where reports were created merely for compliance. The Committee Chairperson emphasised that targets should be meaningful, and easy-to-achieve goals should not be presented as major accomplishments.
Meeting report
Discussion on Committee programme
The Chairperson said the first presentation, by the Committee researcher, would guide the Committee through the five-year review of the transport sector in terms of key perspectives and oversight.
However, Dr C Hunsinger (DA) expressed concern that the Committee Week, which had been blocked out in the parliamentary calendar for engaging with 14 entities and to provide an in-depth opportunity for new Members to engage on the Committee's work, had not happened. He asked the Chairperson to explain why, in particular, the Auditor-General (AG) had failed to brief the Committee on the financial situation of these entities.
He said the week had been planned as an ideal start to the term, but this had been a missed opportunity due to the programme's rejection without explanation. He asked for clarification on the sudden change in plans, noting that the lack of engagement and communication had resulted in a meeting with very little content, compared to what could have been.
The Chairperson responded that he had planned to address this later, but he would do so now as it was an important concern. He explained that the Chair of Chairs and programming teams had advised them on potential developments, and an exemption had been requested but not granted due to the complexity of internal arrangements involving multiple entities. He assured the Members that everything possible would be done to ensure this Committee was given the necessary time and flexibility, even outside the regular parliamentary schedule.
He added that there was an agreement that required all Members to be in Parliament this week for workshops, and any exemption requests would be considered with other political parties to prevent conflicts between committees. He apologised that the Members had not received an explanation in the email that had been sent out.
Department of Transport five-year review
Dr Sifiso Ngesi, the Committee researcher, introduced himself and presented the five-year review of the transport sector, focusing on key perspectives and oversight.
Adv Alma Nel, the Committee Content Advisor, introduced herself and presented the targets and major projects planned by the Department of Transport (DoT) entities as per their 2024/25-2026/27 corporate and 2024/25 annual performance plans (APPs).

Discussion
The Chairperson first responded to Dr Hunsinger's question about the AG. Ideally, they would have preferred to meet with the AG first, receive the report, and then engage with the entities. Unfortunately, the AG had indicated that they were signing off reports that day, so they were unable to schedule them for this meeting. However, they would be meeting with them on 27 August. This would fully equip the Members when they met with the entities.
He said it was important to cross-check and verify some of the information provided. They should be able to sample and identify specific issues to see if they function as reported. He believed that the report presented had given a good perspective and insight, helping to bridge the gap left by not following the programme as originally planned. He then opened the floor for Members to discuss the presentation.
Mr M Gana (Rise Mzansi) said that the key area they needed to focus on was the Department's ability to provide oversight and leadership over the entities. He noted that some entities were not submitting their reports, and others were without chief executive officers (CEOs). This was crucial, especially considering that the Department had demonstrated its capacity to provide leadership and oversight with entities like Transnet and South African Airways (SAA). However, the entities currently under their wing were not meeting these standards.
The second point he raised tied back to the previous discussion the Committee had had about allocating time to the Committee. He said that when reviewing these reports, they needed to identify the critical areas the DoT should focus on, beyond the targets they had set for themselves. The Department had indicated that many of the targets mentioned in the presentation were merely for compliance. He urged the Department to distinguish which targets were genuinely important so they could be prioritised and achieved, and which ones were red flags. Setting numerous targets and trying to focus on all of them at once was not effective.
He also suggested adding a comments column to the presentation to indicate if the Department was likely to achieve each target, or where challenges might arise. This addition would help the Committee to track trends and outcomes as annual reports were submitted.
Mr T Mabena (DA) said he wanted to address the issue raised in the report on the fire at OR Tambo International Airport, which involved a fraudulent contract later challenged in court. The CEO had mentioned that they were still investigating the incident. However, this investigation dates back to the 6th Parliament, and they were now in the 7th. The focus should have been on investigating the company involved, which had been established only a few days before the incident, and had then changed its name.
He referred to the Passenger Railway Agency of South Africa (Prasa) and the expenditure of R30 million, where the agency had to rehire Zolani Matthews as Group Chief Executive Officer, resulting in a period with two acting CEOs. He commented that while the issue had recently been resolved, it had come at a significant cost. The Prasa board, which had made this decision, needed to answer to the Committee about how this situation had been handled and who would be responsible for settling the bill. He requested that the DoT provide clear answers on this matter.
Another issue was related to the Driving Licence Card Account (DLCA), where an official had lost a laptop in 2023 on which all contract-related data was saved, resulting in the entity having to restart the entire contract process and re-advertise the tender. He described this situation as indicative of high-level corruption.
Concerning the licence machines, he pointed out that South Africa had many institutions, such as the Council for Scientific and Industrial Research (CSIR) and the Artificial Intelligence Institute of South Africa (AIISA), which could potentially design a driving licence card and the machines to manufacture them. He questioned why these institutions were not being utilised for this purpose, especially since the current machines were serviced in Germany and there were plans to switch to France, which seemed to be creating more problems. He requested information on the potential costs involved in this process.
At Air Traffic and Navigation Services (ATNS), two corrupt board members were refusing to repay money owed, and the state had indicated that recovering the funds would cost more than the amount owed, so they had decided not to pursue recovery. The Road Accident Fund (RAF) had proposed to reduce their legal fees by 80%. He questioned how the proposed 80% reduction in legal fees would be implemented and resolved, as it seemed ambitious.
Mr Mabena said the Department had not had a clean audit because in 2023, they had struggled to pay their service providers within the required 30-day period due to office closures by officials. He asked for an update from the Director General (DG) on the situation, and the consequences faced by the officials involved.
He said the R537 road upgrade had been problematic. The project had involved extending the road from Limpopo into Mpumalanga, but progress had stalled in Mpumalanga and Gauteng. Initially, there were suggestions to either build a new road or a new railway, but it had been decided to proceed with upgrading the existing road. He wanted to know the costs of building a new road versus a new railway, and the timeline for each of these projects.
The last point he raised concerned the Road Traffic Management Corporation (RTMC). He had noted that vehicles were parked outside in the sun. He asked why it had been difficult to house these vehicles, given that they had been exposed to the weather elements for a very long time, despite numerous promises of a solution.
Mr S Mwali (MK) noted from the report that there were issues with entities submitting their financial statements on time. He wanted to know the reasons for these delays, as they allowed entities to potentially avoid accountability for financial issues that had occurred during the year. He pointed out that such delays could lead to problems being identified only later, which might result in the government having to cover up or compensate for lost finances.
He also highlighted issues with the South African Maritime Safety Authority (SAMSA), Prasa, and the Road Accident Fund (RAF). He noted that both Prasa and the RAF were aiming for a clean audit as part of their recovery from insolvency. He mentioned that the RAF had changed their accounting policies, which had shifted their status from insolvent to solvent. He expressed concern that these entities might pursue their targets by any means, potentially using incorrect methods. Concerning the Department's targets, he questioned whether they could achieve the targets they had set for themselves.
The report mentioned that the Department had underspent the public transport budget by R98.2 million. There were still issues with unreceived COVID-19 relief funds, yet the Department was reporting significant underspending, which was a concern. He proposed that a meeting with the AG and the entities be held to address these issues and clarify the findings in the report.
Ms N Nolutshungu (EFF) noted that the National Development Plan (NDP), which was set to expire in 2030, could have been used for oversight. She suggested that the Department should have reviewed the progress made in the past 12 years, and assessed what remained to be achieved in the next six years. Prioritising these achievements and targets would have helped focus on what needed to be accomplished.
Regarding the Zolani Matthews issue, Ms Nolutshungu proposed that all entities provide a comprehensive list of individuals suspended, both with and without pay, for a thorough examination.
She said that the Department's documents had not addressed scholar transport. She questioned whether scholar transport fell under the Department of Education or the Department of Transport, as no other entities had mentioned it. She highlighted that there seemed to be a lack of accountability for this sector.
The last point she addressed was about SAA and Transnet. She asked who these transport entities were accountable to, and proposed that a detailed report, similar to the one presented to the Committee that day, should have been provided for these two entities.
Mr S Subrathie (ANC) suggested that after hearing about the challenges and various action plans, a management tool should be used to address ongoing issues, some of which had remained unresolved since 2019. He recommended the Eisenhower Matrix, a prioritisation tool that categorises tasks into four quadrants: urgent and important (to be tackled immediately); important but not urgent; less urgent, and important (to be delegated); and not important. He believed this strategy would help to manage a large number of items and focus priority on what was important efficiently.
Ms N Nkopane (ANC) said that, based on the five-year trend review presented, there had been improvements, particularly in road development in rural areas. However, the report also indicated a decrease in performance since 2019. The audit opinions for the Department and the entities, which showed adverse and disclaimer opinions, indicated that management had not implemented key drivers for improved audit outcomes. She wanted to know the reasons behind this
She said that the five-year trend review presented had indicated that there had been improvements, particularly in road development in rural areas. However, the report also indicated a decrease in performance since 2019.
She added that after the AG's letter to a department or entity, senior management was supposed to draw up an audit improvement plan, which the Committee then oversaw. She questioned the whereabouts of those plans and whether their absence had contributed to the ineffective implementation of key drivers for improvement. She wanted to know what was happening with these plans.
She inquired about the current status of the precautionary suspension of the CEO and other senior employees. She wanted the Committee to be briefed on their current positions to assess whether these suspensions contributed to the Department's situation. She raised concerns about internal control deficiencies mentioned in the reports, questioning if these issues stemmed from the suspensions or from the appointment of non-qualified personnel. She also sought clarity on the ongoing and unacceptable situation with the CEOs at Prasa.
Mr C Malematja (ANC) said that Members needed to acknowledge their purpose, reminding the Committee that their goal was to strive for quality and affordable transport for all, to stimulate innovation in the transport sector, and to uphold transparency, accountability, and accessibility. He emphasised that these principles should have guided their work.
He noted that the report mentioned several factors that would equip the Committee to fulfil its responsibilities in representing the people of South Africa. He emphasised that consequence management would be a key focus, questioning where things had gone wrong and highlighting concerns about underspending, which the report had clearly outlined.
He pointed out that the report lacked detail on the challenges facing the taxi recapitalisation programme, which needed reinforcement. He stressed the importance of acknowledging progress when improvements were made, and sought clarity on the regression in performance. Despite these concerns, he expressed satisfaction with the presentation, and felt it had covered the necessary points.
Chairperson's comments
The Chairperson thanked the Members for their input and commended the Department of Transport for providing their reports. He said the Committee shared a unified approach and common expectations for the entities, which played a crucial role in the performance of the economy and in improving the lives of South Africans. As long as the Committee remained aligned with these goals, they should be able to engage with anyone impartially. He advised the Members to avoid overburdening the Department with questions, and instead to focus on what needed to be addressed immediately.
The Chairperson said that managing numerous entities presented a challenge, and stressed the need for effective prioritisation. However, excessive focus on one entity could lead to regression in others. Therefore, the Committee must find a balance to oversee all entities, giving special attention to those underperforming while still keeping track of those performing well.
Another issue was the unrealistic targets, where reports were created merely for compliance. The Chairperson emphasised that targets should be meaningful, and easy-to-achieve goals should not be presented as major accomplishments. He specifically highlighted the RAF targets as problematic and questionable, noting that some had been assessed only at the end of the financial year instead of being tracked quarterly. This lack of ongoing monitoring contributed to some of the issues faced.
Regarding the Prasa incident, he commented that there was also a Deputy Director-General (DDG) who had been suspended with pay for two years. He suggested that when writing to these entities, there should be a request for a list of all suspended officials, the total amount they had been paid during their suspensions, the duration of their suspensions, and the number of officials who had been dismissed. This information should be included as an addendum to the report they present.
Referring to the National Development Plan, which still had six years to run, he wanted to know how far they had progressed, and which areas needed the Committee's attention. This would help the Committee monitor and focus their efforts.
On the issue of licence machines, he questioned why internal resources were not being used. He wondered if there was a lack of confidence in the country's capabilities, and suggested raising this point appropriately at the right time. Regarding scholar transport, he had requested all bilateral joint committees to come together to identify and address joint issues.
Ms Nolutshungu raised the issue of documents being handed out on the morning of the discussion, noting that this practice hindered Members' ability to prepare adequately for Committee meetings. She requested that all documents be distributed on time to ensure proper preparation.
The Chairperson agreed with Ms Nolutshungu, stating that there would be no meetings if Members did not receive the necessary documents on time. He emphasised the importance of providing all the required documents well in advance.
He thanked the Members for their input, and assured them that all raised points would be addressed.
The meeting was adjourned.
