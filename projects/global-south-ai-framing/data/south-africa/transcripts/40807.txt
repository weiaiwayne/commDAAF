Meeting Summary
Video
The Portfolio Committee on Higher Education convened to receive a comprehensive progress report from the Local Government Sector Education and Training Authority (LGSETA). This followed a whistleblower-triggered forensic investigation into allegations of systemic corruption, maladministration, and governance failures within the entity. Key findings from the investigation revealed significant procurement irregularities, including a R2.3 billion tender that showed non-compliance, the irregular appointment of training providers, discretionary grant allocations made without board approval, and instances of financial bias and overpayment. The report also highlighted conflicts of interest involving individuals serving on multiple procurement committees. Although a criminal case had been opened with the Hawks, LGSETA was unable to publicly disclose names due to ongoing legal proceedings. Despite governance challenges, the LGSETA reported a notable improvement in its performance, rising from 66% in 2020/21 to 80% in 2023/24. The Committee was also informed about the LGSETA's partnership with China, which includes a training programme for 500 unemployed youth in areas such as mechatronics, artificial intelligence, and e-commerce. The programme, although fully funded, was said to have received a low application response, which then prompted a re-advertisement. The entity took precautions to ensure that there would be no political interference in the process by administering bursaries through an online portal. The total bursary target for the current financial year was said to be 1 640 students, including 700 new enrolments. Further discussions addressed the LGSETA's role in supporting local government service delivery.
Committee Members raised serious concerns regarding an unexplained reversal of a qualified audit opinion by the Auditor-General of South Africa (AGSA), which occurred via a phone call rather than formal communication. This raised criticism from Members, who questioned the integrity of audit processes and accountability within the entity. Members also questioned the LGSETA’s financial management, particularly the R39.8 million in fruitless and wasteful expenditure in 2023/24. Concerns were noted around high travel and accommodation costs, with expenses rising from R5 million to R15 million. The Committee emphasised the persistent lack of clean audits over three financial years and ongoing organisational instability.
Members questioned the LGSETA’s outreach and accessibility, mostly in rural and remote areas where internet connectivity remains an issue. While the LGSETA installed internet towers in rural municipalities, Members requested a detailed list of locations and called for the programme’s expansion. The Committee also expressed concern over the limited awareness and reach of bursary opportunities and international programmes, especially in rural communities. Members raised concerns about whether these bursaries truly reach “missing middle” students and questioned the lack of institutional oversight when bursaries are routed through universities.
Members were encouraged by reports of programmes targeting water reticulation, road maintenance, and local economic development, though they emphasised the need for measurable impact and sustainable job creation. Questions were raised about the validity of certain recruitment practices at the municipal level and the potential for nepotism in beneficiary selection. The Committee urged LGSETA to ensure transparency and impartial access to offered opportunities.
Structural concerns were also discussed, particularly the absence of a functional board since March 2025. For the time being, the Minister authorised the Chief Executive Officer to act as the accounting authority, a decision some Members viewed as problematic, given potential conflicts of interest and governance risks. Members called for urgent board appointments and also called for collaborations among SETAs to increase programme reach, especially for youth training in townships, and to address critical service delivery backlogs.

The unemployment rate among the youth stood at 62%, with over 3.8 million young people not in employment or training. The Minister stated that it was the Department's responsibility to come up with interventions to address this crisis. Local Government SETA’s briefing
Mr Ineeleng Molete, Chief Executive Officer, Local Government Sector Education and Training Authority (LGSETA), presented the entity’s report which touched on corruption and maladministration, exorbitant costs of the CEO’s security detail, the increase in legal fees and travel, unfair labour practices and staff victimisation, and an update on the suspended executives. He said that the LGSETA received a whistle-blower report through the former board chairperson. The entity then commissioned a forensic investigation and appointed a suitably qualified, and experienced service provider to conduct this investigation. The final report was received on 29 September 2022 addressing various allegations, including irregularity in the appointment process of a panel of training service providers, learning programmes department tender for five years for R2.3 billion, irregular allocation of training to accredited training service providers [no list of accredited service providers (database of training providers)], and irregular dissemination of discretionary grant funding by the LGSETA (without board approval/resolution), among others.

Discussion
Ms M Maseko (DA) asked what determined the sensitivity of some of the information or the list that could not be provided.
Mr Molete replied that an inquiry with the Hawks had been registered. A criminal case had been registered against the alleged service providers and officials, and the entity was, therefore, informed that it could not divulge the details of the matter.
Ms Maseko said that the management must clarify which aspects of the report are considered sensitive, as the details address issues related to maladministration, governance failures, and corruption. Consequently, some questions will pertain to officials who were involved and whether there were associated costs. The absence of in-depth information would hinder the Members’ ability to engage effectively. She inquired about what questions the team could and could not respond to.
Mr Molete replied that the names at this stage would be tricky to divulge. He requested not to get into the details involving officials' names, and some of the matters that were still being processed through the disciplinary and criminal proceedings, however, the entity could submit a detailed report to the Committee.
Ms Maseko asked for details of the Auditor-General of South Africa’s (AGSA’s) opinion that was reversed.
Mr Molete replied that was a mystery to this day. He unpacked the process of how the management report and audit report are processed in a Schedule 3 entity, indicating that these have an independent audit, risk and finance committees that must be recommended to the board of AFS, annual performance report and the audit opinion. All these processes, he said, were adopted on a Friday meeting, and the board made peace that they received a qualified audit opinion. The following Sunday, the chairperson of the board received a call from the senior manager, informing him that the AGSA had reversed the audit opinion from a qualification to unqualified. The AGSA report indicated that the auditors were not present, but he was informed that the audit opinion remained the same after the dispute resolution process happened. Mr Molete said that the report could be submitted to the Committee as well.
Ms Maseko asked what documents were shared with the CEO that the audit opinion was reversed.
Mr Molete replied that he was referring to the 2021/22 audit opinion, not the 2022/23. He indicated that a special board meeting had to be called because the minutes of the previous board meeting (Friday) indicated that the entity received a qualified audit opinion. A resolution was required for the reversal.
Ms Maseko interjected and said this was a unique scenario and asked what empowered the official to inform the CEO of the reversal.
Mr Molete replied that it was the AGSA’s senior manager, not an LGSETA official.
Ms Maseko felt this did not make sense. Furthermore, it was indicated that there was a lot of travelling involved, and in addressing some of the issues emanating from the report she highlighted the increased amount from R5 million to R15 million. She asked what was unique about these issues, that the cost of travel and accommodation escalated so much.
Mr Molete replied that the comparison made was from zero, which was during the COVID-19 pandemic, and he added that he had not prepared reports for the period before COVID-19. He stated that the entity runs projects in remote and rural areas and international partnerships in China with various universities.
Ms Maseko said for the 2023/24 financial year, the LGSETA incurred fruitless and wasteful expenditure amounting to R39.8 million and asked if a portion of this amount was due to the escalated accommodation and travelling costs.
Mr Kwena Mokgokong, Acting Chief Financial Officer, LGSETA, replied to the issue related to contracts that expired at year-end and explained that when a contract expires at year-end, it must be cancelled, as per the regulations. Measures would then be undertaken to reinstate the contract due to activities rolling on the ground. The only issue may be that the service level agreement (SLA) would have expired at year-end, and the entity would have already paid monies to the contracts even though the SLA had expired. This results in the AGSA classifying such expenditure as fruitless. Mr Mokgokong said that SETA argued that when the monies were paid, these were based on deliverables met at that point and although the contracts had expired, the benefits would have been realised. SETA accepted the matter but eventually reversed the finding. He added that the projects have been reinstated.
Ms Maseko refuted that the R39.8 million did not only constitute amounts for contracts.
Mr Mokgokong said that for the inclusion of travel and accommodation, the amount was about R172 000, which related to instances where travellers bought items for that trip; thus, officials could claim incidental costs associated with their trips. He added that there are Uber costs involved for navigating between different meetings, laundry fees, etc. The entity had explained to the AGSA that these were incidental costs and part of the travelling and had prepared evidence for the incurred incidental costs.
The Chairperson noted the response and instructed that a detailed breakdown must be provided to the Committee, which would include the R38.9 million breakdown. He asked for clarification of the reversal of the audit opinion and the AGSA’s reasoning for the reversal. He felt the explanation provided to Ms Maseko was still unclear.
Mr Molete said part of the reason the board included this matter in the scope of the investigation was to get to the bottom of the issue. In that Friday meeting, the board painstakingly engaged with AGSA regarding the qualification. However, the board eventually accepted the qualification, committing to the development of an action plan to address it. On a Sunday afternoon, he received a call from the board chairperson, who informed him that a senior manager at AGSA had communicated that the audit opinion had been reversed. The chairperson told Mr Molete that the call was abrupt and did not provide any explanation. As a result, in his capacity as CEO, Mr Molete proposed convening a special board meeting on Monday to hear directly from AGSA the reasons for the reversal. Noting AGSA’s audit methodology, there had been no changes to the internal control findings and deficiencies, yet the audit opinion had changed. He then suggested that the entity engage independent experts to assist in understanding how the reversal occurred without any formal explanation. The investigators later sent a letter indicating they would not participate in the investigation. Mr Molete noted that the letter could be shared with the Committee. At the Monday special board meeting, he stated that the board resolved to adopt the reversed audit opinion, while highlighting that the incident occurred in the 2020/21 financial year. He added that, to this day, they still do not know why the audit opinion was reversed.
Ms D Van Der Walt (DA) expressed concern that the Committee continued to observe SETAs questioning their own mandates. She asked what the mandate of the LGSETA is and whether it is being effectively implemented. She also questioned the monitoring processes in place to ensure that appointed individuals are properly qualified for their positions.
Mr Molete stated that the mandate of the SETA is to skill and upskill the underprivileged, and its goal is to provide hope to those in despair and to open opportunities for communities. SETA also aims to create a bridge to the workplace while supporting Small, Medium, and Micro Enterprises (SMMEs). The LGSETA's theory of change focuses on improving municipalities by ensuring there are capable leaders at both the political and administrative levels. This is achieved through the Councillor Development Strategy developed by the Department of Cooperate Governance and Traditional Affairs (CoGTA) in 2021. He added that LGSETA wants to ensure that by the time councillors complete their five-year term, they have received sufficient upskilling. The entity offers occupationally directed qualifications for councillors, covering areas such as governance, finance, meeting management, community engagement, project management, and community analysis.
Most importantly, the emphasis is on the importance of creating oversight. Mr Molete said that LGSETA also prioritises financial prudence. When training councillors in oversight, municipal finance qualifications are provided, enabling councillors to transition into employees of the municipality rather than remaining as consultants after their term. In 2021, LGSETA partnered with the South African Local Government Association (SALGA) to train over 900 councillors as facilitators for inducting new councillors. He included that bursaries are also offered. Each year, the entity is required to produce a sector skills plan, which serves as a form of labour market intelligence, directly linking its spending to SETA’s priorities. This plan outlines the five priorities along with information about bursaries.
Further, LGSETA supports learners from grades 10 to 12 by providing extra classes in mathematics, accounting, English, and technology. Each learner receives a minimum of 62 hours and is awarded bursaries upon entering university. Due to limited access to universities in the country, SETA has partnered with the Republic of China and 11 universities. In September, 500 students are planned to enrol in various fields such as electric vehicles, mechatronics, electrical engineering, and the Internet of Things. Training for shop stewards is also provided to enhance their leadership skills. For community development, 270 young people have been engaged, connecting them with approximately 13 municipalities in China. This initiative seeks to link them with Chinese companies operating in South Africa, ensuring that when they return from their training, they have access to e-commerce opportunities.
Additionally, the entity funds candidacy programmes in partnership with the Engineering Council of South Africa, the Institute of Internal Government Auditors, and the Association for Certified Auditors, among others. There also have been collaborations with a municipality in South Pasadena, California, to train firefighters and metro police in the latest technology and drone operations. Mr Molete continued to share that LGSETA’s theory of change envisions the realisation of Chapter Seven of the Constitution, which includes stable local governance, access to water and energy security, and the maintenance of infrastructure and roads. The organisation’s performance indicates that it is beginning to understand and fulfil its mandate effectively.
Ms Van Der Walt asked how successes and value for money are monitored and asked for the percentage spent on salaries vis-à-vis their budget.
Mr Molete explained that the grant regulations require 10.5% of the organisation’s total payroll, and the total cost to operate the SETA exceeds R1.16 billion. Of this total, 49.5% is allocated to discretionary grants, which include bursaries and other projects. Additionally, 20% is designated for the NSF, and another 20% is set aside for mandatory grants. The remaining 10.5% is left for salaries and other operational costs.
Mr Mokgokong stated that the LGSETA’s salary bill amounted to R83 million as of 2024, which is roughly about 70% of the administration's income, and added that the funding for administration remains inadequate. He said that LGSETA generates its income to supplement the levies that are not adequate and that the consensus is to increase the 10.5% because it was not adequate for administration.
The Chairperson advised that when the entity trains municipalities, it must be for service delivery agents, not the politicians. Additionally, he stated that there must be effort in training electrical engineers of municipalities.
Mr S Ngubane (MKP) emphasised the importance of addressing major service delivery concerns and inefficiencies at the local government level. He pointed out that some local councillors cannot read financial statements. In light of this, he requested that the Chief Financial Officer (CFO) prepare a report detailing how the organisation supports and upskills councillors.
He raised questions regarding the relationship between LGSETA, the South African Local Government Association (SALGA), the Municipal Infrastructure Services Agent (MISA), and CoGTA. Mr Ngubane pointed out that the CEO had referenced individuals implicated in the report regarding the procurement process and other functions, as well as the issues surrounding the five-year rental contract. He requested the CEO to clarify what measures have been put in place to address the gaps that contributed to the maladministration and corruption at SETA and to explain how the matter of the five-year rental contract was resolved.
Mr Ngubane further probed into what has been done to improve the relationship between management and the organised labour union as there is often a back-and-forth between the two stakeholders throughout the sector. Lastly, he asked how LGSETA attempted to minimise the exorbitant display of security detail and convoys for councillors in meetings.
Mr Molete stated that a strategy for the training of councillors at the local government level had to be developed. This strategy is divided into three components, which include a matric equivalent upon completion of the programme. He mentioned that after local government elections in one province, an assessment revealed that over 300 councillors lacked a matric qualification or its equivalent. The organisation is, therefore, using this as an opportunity to collaborate with CoGTA to create a local government qualification that will empower councillors. To ensure effective oversight, Mr Molete indicated that councillors need to be empowered. The LGSETA has been facilitating this training through the Institute of Directors in Southern Africa (IODSA) and has begun with a governance module to help councillors understand the distinction between management and oversight. Since the beginning of this administration, LGSETA has trained over 9 000 councillors in partnership with SALGA. This training focuses on financial oversight, governance, and understanding large infrastructure projects as it was identified that there is a need for committees to enhance monitoring and oversight of construction projects. Mr Molete stated that LGSETA has an existing relationship with SALGA through a Memorandum of Understanding (MoU) to address councillor development. Recently, he was in Silicon Valley with SALGA, where they were working on an international development programme for CFOs and municipal managers in collaboration with a certain university. This is a partnership characterised by an interest in improving the local government sector, he said.
Mr Molete stated that there is also an additional programme run with MISA, and that CoGTA has a representative on the LGSETA board. In the past five years, there was no representative from CoGTA but the relation between the two entities has been since resuscitated, together with the Department of Traditional Affairs. He mentioned that the organisation must ensure the development of traditional leaders as per its theory of change. There is currently an approved traditional leader’s strategy, which was a collaborative effort between LGSETA and the Department of Traditional Affairs. Mr Molete indicated this was now a matter of bringing an actual change into the sector.
He mentioned that he had informed the board that taking the company to court would result in a lengthy process to obtain an order. The organisation also weighed the legal costs against how quickly it could secure another building. Recently, the board approved a management plan. As part of this, reviewing the type of building required was discussed, and the building needed to be one that is accessible to communities. Mr Molete emphasised that the entity did not want to rely on booking boardrooms when Parliament visits. He indicated that the board approved the proposed layout and specified the types of services wanted, including shared services and access for other SETAs. After assessing the building space, it was discovered that there was more than 1 000 square metres which was not needed. The entity is now prioritising securing a value-for-money space.
Mr Molete mentioned that the public service sector is about power struggles and that the reality of being an executive in the public sector borders on whether you are going to be pushed around or stick to your guns. These both have negative connotations, but one must choose. As a former shop steward, Mr Molete said that he understands that things change once you assume a position in administration. He indicated that things should be addressed in the boardroom, not in other forums.
Mr Molete stated that he holds quarterly meetings with the unions and has met with them on Monday for the interviews. He noted that the challenge lies in the fact that the union leadership no longer formally exists. Individuals only tend to emerge when someone is charged, doing so without any conference or adherence to the union's constitution. However, because they have a point to prove, they position themselves as the face of anti-executive sentiment. He urged Members to ask themselves why people are fighting when the allocated funds are intended for bursaries and stipends for young people.
He indicated that the issue of protection for councillors is a sad reality. In the 2023 strategic plan, there was an executive who attended a meeting and got shot two weeks later with their bodyguard. Mr Molete voiced that this is the reality they live with.
The Chairperson noted that while there were conflicting views on where to focus LGSETA resources, it was critical to ensure that when empowering service delivery agents, councillors also need to be considered. He stated that some of the councillors approve budgets without understanding financial statements. He advised that SETA, in its partnership with the South African Institute of Chartered Accountants (SAICA), could develop a programme as municipalities relied on outsourcing a lot of services because councillors sit and agree to proposals to allocate R300 million in hiring waste removal trucks for three years. He stated that if these municipalities instead bought trucks, it would be cheaper, and people could be employed for longer periods. The Chairperson said that these municipalities make these deals because there is no deeper understanding of these matters. He advised the LGSETA to establish MoUs with other stakeholders to help develop a document that will be easily teachable to councillors to understand the objective.
Lastly, he mentioned that stakeholder relationship management with organised labour must be investigated. He stated that he had received a letter from the National Education, Health and Allied Workers' Union (NEHAWU) at LGSETA. The letter raised concerns about a restructuring process taking place at the SETA, alleging that proper consultation had not occurred. NEHAWU expressed the belief that management was attempting to ensure that some employees would lose their jobs. They further alleged that the SETA was interviewing candidates or advertising for positions that were already filled. While he acknowledged management’s efforts to engage with organised labour, he encouraged them to do more.

He mentioned that the Minister is also expected to provide feedback on issues related to the University of Limpopo on the Department’s progress in implementing the recommendations of the forensic report.
[The meeting was adjourned.]
