Meeting Summary
Video
The Portfolio Committee on Science and Innovation conducted a virtual session to receive presentations on the 2025–2030 Strategic Plans and the 2025/26 Annual Performance Plans (APPs) of the Department of Science, Technology and Innovation (DSTI), the National Advisory Council on Innovation (NACI), the Technology Innovation Agency (TIA), and the National Research Foundation (NRF).
The DSTI focused on several pressing challenges, with particular concern around the possible loss of about R2 billion in research funding, largely due to shifting foreign policies. To respond to this challenge, the Department indicated that there is a new ministerial advisory body being set up to assess the long-term effects. Concerns were also raised over the continued underrepresentation of women and black professionals in the science, technology, engineering, and mathematics (STEM) fields, as well as the limited use of innovation in vital sectors like mining, manufacturing, agriculture, and health. Looking ahead, the Department's five-year strategy aims to enhance spending on research and development (R&D) from 0.61% to 1.5% of GDP by 2029. Plans include building stronger partnerships between the public and private sectors, supporting impactful research in key economic areas, and using science, technology, and innovation (STI) to drive inclusive growth and institutional development.
The NACI outlined its role in advising on policy. Its focus is on monitoring the system's long-term performance, tracking transformation, and helping to anticipate future trends. Key outputs include an annual set of STI indicators, a national investment framework, and stronger collaboration at home and abroad. Equity and inclusion remain a priority, with ongoing efforts to improve participation by women and historically disadvantaged groups in science.
TIA introduced its revamped strategy under "TIA 2.0," which marks a shift from simply funding projects to actively shaping innovation ecosystems. The Agency now places more emphasis on commercialising innovation, supporting inclusive growth, and aligning efforts across government, academia, and industry. TIA also reported on steps taken to fix governance issues, improve how funds are allocated, and increase support for black-owned businesses, especially in ICT and health. There's also a focused push to serve underserved regions through innovation hubs and startup support.
The NRF presented its new direction based on the “TIES for Impact” framework—Transformation, Innovation, Excellence, and Sustainability. The entity shared successes from the previous strategy, such as exceeding equity targets in postgraduate funding and strengthening infrastructure like the Square Kilometre Array (SKA). Going forward, the NRF plans to widen its funding base, deepen partnerships, and strengthen research at historically disadvantaged institutions. It also revealed a new framework to better assess how research contributes to real-world social and economic benefits.
While Committee Members welcomed the strategic focus shown in the presentations, they voiced serious concerns about several issues. These included the sustainability of financial plans in light of declining foreign funding, a high vacancy rate in the Department, heavy reliance on US partnerships, and weak private sector involvement in R&D. Members also felt more clarity was needed on how innovation would directly tackle unemployment, poverty, and inequality. Additional questions were raised about the regulatory space for startups, support for local patents, and the need to improve foundational STEM education.
Members stressed that progress must be supported by clear, measurable results. Members also highlighted the importance of building a more resilient and self-reliant system, less dependent on foreign funding.

Minister’s Opening Remarks
Minister Nzimande addressed the Portfolio Committee on Science, Technology and Innovation, emphasising the importance of engaging with and briefing the Committee on the work of the Department. He began by briefly outlining the global and national challenges South Africa faces, including deep structural changes in the global economy, the growing impact of disruptive technologies such as artificial intelligence, robotics, and automation on labour markets, and the challenge of climate change. He highlighted the multiplicity of sources from which major pressures on South Africa, particularly on the research side, have arisen recently.
The Minister noted that globally, the actions taken by the United States to cut research funds are posing some challenges to South Africa. He informed the Portfolio Committee that he is establishing a Ministerial Advisory Committee as a matter of urgency to assess the full impact of these actions. According to the preliminary report from the Department, which Minister Nzimande felt required further work, South African institutions, including universities, could potentially lose approximately two billion rands in research funds originating mainly from the United States. The Minister expressed his dissatisfaction with the depth of the current assessment and explained that the committee, to be led by Dr Swartz, a science expert, would provide a more in-depth report by the end of May. He committed to returning to the Portfolio Committee soon after receiving this report.
Minister Nzimande highlighted the ongoing nature of the challenges emanating from the United States, citing new developments and a barrage of executive orders impacting various interventions, not just those targeted at South Africa. He expressed uncertainty about the potential impact of funding cuts to US universities on scholarships for South Africans and emphasised the need to address this situation proactively. He reported that he is assembling a committee including vice-chancellors from universities, representatives from science councils, and private sector experts in research and innovation to gain a comprehensive briefing.
Addressing domestic concerns, the Minister stated that South Africa's NSI is also faced with some challenges. He emphasised the need to invest in ground-breaking innovations with the potential to create new jobs and sources of livelihood, enabling the building of a capable, ethical, and developmental state. He highlighted the Department's newly adopted mantra of placing science, technology, and innovation at the centre of government, education, industry, and society, to serve as a compass for the next five to ten years. The Minister recognised that urgent work needs to be done to deepen and diversify the networks linking the NSI to crucial industries such as manufacturing, mining, and agriculture. Despite having the largest science system in Africa, it is not yet of a sufficient scale to make the desired impact on economic development and state capacity.
The Minister emphasised the need to significantly increase gross investment in research, development, and innovation, noting that in 2022 to 2023, this investment stood at only 0.61% of the gross domestic product (GDP), which he described as woefully inadequate. He also raised concerns about declining levels of private sector investment in research, development, and innovation. Furthermore, despite commendable advances in social transformation of the NSI, Minister Nzimande expressed deep concern about the insufficient number of black and women scientists and science leaders in key areas. He noted that the inaugural transformation summit was held recently to address this issue.
The Minister informed the Committee of the President's recent visit to the Council for Scientific and Industrial Research (CSIR) to familiarise himself with the organisation's work and needs. This visit was seen as a promising sign of the President's active interest in the NSI and its potential role in government capacity building and innovation advancement. The President commented that many of the CSIR's capabilities could be utilised by the government and admitted that the government was not fully leveraging the capacity of its science entities. The President has requested the Minister to present the work of the science entities, starting with the CSIR, to the Cabinet.
Looking ahead, Minister Nzimande outlined the strategic priorities for the next five to ten years. These include better aligning the innovation landscape to national efforts to address pressing challenges and forging more effective strategic partnerships with key science, technology, and innovation-intensive departments, agencies, and state-owned enterprises to drive large-scale innovations that can lower costs and stimulate inclusive growth. The Minister mentioned that the Department has already engaged the departments of basic and higher education as part of this approach. There is a focus on energising and scaling public-private sector partnerships with high prospects for innovation-led growth. Effective ways of harnessing critical agenda-setting instruments, such as the Science, Technology and Innovation (STI) Presidential Plenary and the Inter-Ministerial Committee on STI, will be emphasised to reset the STI system as a critical investment across key sectors.
The Minister emphasised the importance of greater coherence within the system and stressed the need for improved synergies among the entities. He tasked himself with improving these synergies, noting that there are often overlaps in the work of the entities. He used the example of the CSIR and the Technology Innovation Agency (TIA), noting that they should work more closely together. Minister Nzimande urged the Portfolio Committee to monitor the extent of synergy amongst the entities, acknowledging the importance of each entity's distinctive contributions. He stated that developing an ecosystem for science, technology, and innovation requires improving the interaction of departments and entities with industry and academia. He mentioned that efforts will continue to support targeted research and the development of technologies in key economic sectors such as energy, agriculture, mining, health, and digital economies, with a special focus on areas like artificial intelligence. The Minister expressed his strong focus on artificial intelligence, emphasising its importance as a key driver not just for the STI system but for the entire country, government, and society. He requested that the Committee empower itself to engage in the area of artificial intelligence. He noted that while the Department of Communications and Digital Technologies leads in this area within the government, his Department plays a crucial role in research and capacity building.
Minister Nzimande confirmed continued efforts to support the government in developing credible pandemic preparedness capacity by supporting local vaccine manufacturing. Focus will also remain on space science, with the South African National Space Agency (SANSA) playing a critical role, and on supporting municipalities to better prepare for and respond to natural disasters. Upgrading and developing new science infrastructure and the production of critical and high-end skills, including through the Presidential PhD Programme, will also remain a priority. The DSTI will continue to mobilise additional funding and resources and use existing resources innovatively due to the constrained fiscal environment.
Finally, the Minister addressed the issue of governance at the Technology Innovation Agency (TIA). He emphasised his legal obligation to ensure good governance and that entities fulfil their mandate in line with the priorities of the DSTI and the government. He indicated that TIA had been experiencing sustained governance instability for several years, negatively impacting its performance, key programmes, and staff morale. Minister Nzimande indicated that he had formally raised his concerns with the TIA board and the former chairperson on multiple occasions. These concerns were raised against the backdrop of two extensive reviews of TIA's functionality and relevance. He mentioned raising concerns with the board and chairperson about the failure to appoint a permanent chief executive officer (CEO) and the turnover of staff. After numerous engagements, the Minister made the decision to remove the former chairperson of the TIA board, citing her admission of contravening the TIA Act. He explained that he had asked the TIA board to present a strategy to implement the recommendations from the reviews, which led to the request for a "TIA 2.0" strategy. When the Minister received a response that was unsatisfactory, he met with the TIA board and discovered that the board had not received the letter he had written to them via the chairperson, nor were they aware of the chairperson's reply to him. This, the Minister deemed a serious transgression of governance rules and an act of deception.
Department of Science and Innovation (DSI) presentation
Ms Gugulethu Zwane, Acting Director-General, Department of Science and Innovation (DSI), presented the DSI's strategy for the next five years and the APP for the current financial year. Ms Zwane began by outlining the presentation's structure, which included the government-level role of science, technology, and innovation (STI) in the Medium Term Development Plan (MTDP), specific DSI commitments, a detailed look at the DSTI strategic plan response, highlighted risks and mitigating actions, and programme-level commitments for the next 11 months, excluding April. She indicated that the chief financial officer (CFO) would later address the budget aspects.
In terms of strategic priorities, Ms Zwane stated that the DSI aligns with the government's three strategic priorities at the MTDP level: inclusive growth and job creation, reducing poverty and tackling the high cost of living, and a capable, ethical, and developmental state. The DSI contributes to strategic priority one through a dynamic STI ecosystem for growth, and reporting to the economic cluster. She emphasised the strategic role of STI throughout policy documents, including the National Development Plan (NDP) and the White Paper, highlighting the importance of innovation in economic and social development, boosting productivity, improving service delivery, and fostering technological advancements. The White Paper emphasises a whole-of-government approach, integrating innovation into key policy areas through strategic partnerships, and the DSTI decadal plan builds on this by focusing on systematic capabilities and enablers over a 10-year period. All of this will be supported by structured monitoring and evaluation, focusing on lessons learned for evidence-based decision-making.
Ms Zwane noted that innovation comes from various sources, including research, industry, and government agencies, and reaches rural areas, requiring collaboration with non-governmental organisations (NGOs). She added that improved coordination and public-private partnerships are essential for leveraging innovation for commercialisation. She emphasised supporting micro, small, and medium enterprises to participate in emerging sectors, focusing on increasing investment in research and commercialisation of innovation to drive new economic sectors. The target is to reach 1.5% of South Africa's Research and Development expenditure as a percentage of GDP by 2029, with specific departmental responsibilities to achieve this goal by involving other stakeholders. Another goal is for public sector investment in private-public research to grow from 300 million to a billion.
In terms of economic policy integration with science, technology, and innovation, the DSI is working with the President, the Department of Planning, Monitoring and Evaluation (DPME), and National Treasury to prioritise budget coordination and integrate STI into microeconomic trade and industrial policies. She stated that STI must be seen as a catalyst for a just energy transition, supporting South Africa's move towards renewable energy and green technologies. The digital economy and technology adoption emphasises e-commerce and digital platforms with the aim to increase the percentage of internet users, hoping to reach 50% by 2029 from the current 24%. There are plans to expand artificial intelligence, blockchain, and fintech applications as part of the broader digital transformation.
Regarding inclusive education and Science, Technology, Engineering, and Mathematics (STEM) development, she stated that the focus is on promoting STEM education, particularly among women and youth, and skills development in emerging industries to create a more competitive workforce. Innovation policies will prioritise equitable access for women, youth, and persons with disabilities, addressing the transformation of the sector and looking at economic transformation. Economic transformation, in this respect, is seen as integral to inclusive economic growth, with interventions aimed at reducing inequality and enabling economic participation for marginalised communities. Funding mechanisms for women and youth-owned enterprises will be expanded through competition settlements and innovation grants, stemming from resolutions of the recent transformation summit.
Ms Zwane explained the three intervention indicators in the MTDP which are an increased share of the national budget allocated to developing science technology and innovation capabilities, human resources and research infrastructure, increased investment by the private sector into research development and innovation, and the Rand value of private sector investment in public research and innovation partnerships, noting the baselines for these indicators.
Concerning the cross-cutting contribution to government priorities, the DSI is working by integrating science, technology, and innovation into key government programmes across sectors like health, energy, education, and digital transformation, supporting policy infrastructure skills and innovation tools that drive inclusive and sustainable development. Ms Zwane stated that the DSI sees itself as an enabler of a capable, ethical, and developmental state. The impact statement is to position science, technology, and innovation at the centre of government, education, society, and industry, as the cornerstone of South Africa's transformation for economic competitiveness, social justice, state capability, and intergenerational well-being.
A source summary has been conducted, looking at the strengths, opportunities, desired results, and aspirations, highlighting the strength that the DSI is the custodian of the NSI with a clear mandate and leadership in the international community, overseeing key assets like the CSIR, National Research Foundation (NRF), TIA, and South African Maritime Safety Authority (SAMSA). Opportunities include the decadal plan adoption, investment in Indigenous Knowledge Systems (IKS) and bio-innovation, global partnerships, agro-innovation programmes, and enhanced public-private partnerships and R&D coordination. Aspirations include building a science-literate society and increasing youth innovation hubs in communities. The desired results would be high public trust in science and then STI-driven jobs and economic growth.
In terms of the transformation agenda, the strategic plan adopts a systems thinking approach, recognising the interdependencies between economic development, scientific progress, and policy coordination, and constantly reviewing it as needed. This is grounded in South Africa's unique context, leveraging science, technology, and innovation to unlock system capabilities that can advance development, shaped in collaboration with players across the NSI. The transformation summit supported the approach of equity transformation and inclusion, accelerating STI-driven development, fostering inclusive innovation, and strengthening partnerships for systematic change, committing to demographic transformation, including historically disadvantaged institutions, in conjunction with the Gender-Responsive Planning, Budgeting, Monitoring, Evaluation and Auditing (GRPBMEA) framework.
Looking at the theory of change, the strategic approach integrates key drivers of transformation while looking at macroeconomic priorities, environmental imperatives, social drivers, and technological advancement, embedded into concrete programme-level actions and measurable impact, particularly for the current financial year. The Department has seven outcomes, which have been classified under the different strategic priorities. Under strategic priority one, the outcomes are:
To expand and transform STI capabilities, human resources, and research infrastructure for South Africa.
Innovation and commercialisation support provided to the NSI, including grassroots innovation support, ensuring that innovation is found regardless of where it sits in the country.
Increased STI investments and increased support for R&D led to enhancements in the competitiveness of existing and new industries.
Under strategic priority two, the outcome is improved availability of innovative technologies in relevant sectors, such as health, water and sanitation, human settlements and energy. Under strategic priority three, the outcomes are improved NSI governance and coordination, as well as increased integration of STI-based evidence into government decision making, to assist in making decisions based on evidence and being able to integrate those results moving forward. The enablers are governance and institutional strengthening, and research and development investment.

National Advisory Council on Innovation (NACI)
presentation
Mr Tilson Manyoni, Council Chairperson, National Advisory Council on Innovation (NACI), commenced the session, expressed gratitude for the opportunity to present the plans, and acknowledged the Minister, DSTI executive team, and NACI executive members present. He then passed the floor to Dr Cele to lead the detailed presentation.
Dr Mlungisi Cele, Chief Executive Officer, National Advisory Council on Innovation, stated that the presentation would cover NACI's mandate, vision, mission, outcomes, key tasks, and targets. The Chief Financial Officer (CFO) and Head of Human Resources (HR) would address resource-related questions, with the presentation concluding thereafter.
Dr Cele acknowledged the context already provided by the Minister and emphasised the impact of post-COVID conditions and the necessity of continued work in preparation for future pandemics. NACI's strategic plan aims to contextualise work, referencing the STI policy trajectory since the 1996 White Paper, the adoption of the Decade Plan by Cabinet, and the establishment of the new Department of Science, Technology, and Innovation. He stressed the focus on utilising the NSI to accelerate socio-economic development and environmental sustainability and contribute towards a capable state.
Dr Cele highlighted the long-term STI vision articulated in the White Paper and the central theme of the Decade Plan. He emphasised that the Decade Plan incorporates distinct impact pathways leading to improvements in the economy, environment, and society. Monitoring, evaluation, and policy learning are central tasks assigned to NACI by the White Paper and the Decade Plan, underscoring NACI's key role.
He detailed the construction of NACI’s strategic plan, guided by the Department’s Monitoring and Evaluation Guidelines, and rooted in the constitution, policy mandates, and institutional policies and strategies. He indicated that no court rulings were directly applicable to NACI's work. The strategic focus encompasses NACI’s vision, mission, and key values. The strategic plan also includes a detailed situational analysis (internal and external components), outcome measurements, indicators, baselines, five-year targets, and strategic risk assessments with corresponding mitigation factors.
Dr Cele differentiated between the Strategic Plan (outcomes-focused) and the APP (outputs-focused). He stated that the APP articulates NACI's mandate, analysis, vision, mission, and values, and sets out output indicators tied to available resources, with the goal of generating outputs and reporting on them through annual and quarterly targets. This plan also identifies key risks and mitigation strategies, alongside technical indicator descriptions.
Dr Cele outlined four key NACI outcomes, linked to their founding legislation:
Improving the efficacy of policy advice to the Minister of Science, Technology, and Innovation and to Cabinet, ensuring that advice is evidence-based, evidence-informed, confidential, and timely.
Contributing to the building of the NSI, monitoring, evaluation, and learning capability, to assess the system's health and its contribution to sustainable and inclusive development.
Contributing to a well-coordinated, responsive, and effective NSI by exploring and proposing solutions to long-standing policy questions, such as better coordination, prioritisation, financing, investment in research, development, and innovation, as well as addressing human resource questions around knowledge production and diffusion.
Transforming NACI into a smart and efficient learning organisation, recognising the importance of people as a critical asset, offering training, improving internal operational inefficiencies, enhancing work quality and turnaround times, communicating work effectively, and exploiting the benefits of digitisation and digitalisation.
Dr Cele also summarised strategic actions emanating from the White Paper, which NACI has to undertake over and above its founding legislation. These include working with the Department in developing a public STI investment framework, implementing knowledge management systems, and collaborating with other entities to ensure NACI acts as a national STI monitoring and evaluation institution, harnessing existing resources and creating interfaces. He emphasised NACI's strategic role at the presidential plenary and the inter-ministerial committee, providing analytical, technical, and potentially administrative support. Dr Cele added that NACI would continue to conduct long-term foresight studies to improve the system's ability to prioritise, think ahead, and conduct expert studies. He reminded the Committee that NACI was established in 1997, deriving its mandate from that period. While work is underway to amend the legislative framework, the Minister is leading that process. Dr Cele reiterated that NACI’s mandate is to advise the Minister, and through the Minister, the Minister's committee (now the Inter-ministerial Committee) and Cabinet, on the role and contribution of science, mathematics, innovation, and technology, including indigenous technologies, in promoting and achieving national objectives. NACI advises on the coordination and stimulation of the NSI and the identification of research and development priorities.
He detailed NACI's advisory functions, including strategies for promoting the dissemination and accessibility of scientific knowledge and technology, as well as promoting public understanding of science and technology and their supportive role in innovation for development and progress. He highlighted the promotion of mathematics, natural sciences, and technology in the education sector, interpreting this as encompassing the entire education value chain, including basic education. Finally, Dr Cele mentioned that NACI is obliged to provide advice on any other STI and mathematics matters referred to it by the Minister.
He stated that NACI's mission is to be a leading advisory body to the government on STI within a well-coordinated, responsive, and functioning NSI. NACI seeks to provide evidence-based advice to the Minister (and through the Minister to Cabinet), relying on existing research expertise and mobilising stakeholders through roundtable discussions before finalising technical, analytical, or research reports and converting them into advice to the Minister. He stressed the promotion of professionalism, integrity, innovation, knowledge-sharing, transparency, and accountability. NACI's immediate outputs include continuing to provide advice to the Minister, producing monitoring and evaluation reports (including the status of NSI transformation), contributing towards systemic planning, coordination, and agenda setting (STI plenary, Inter-ministerial Committee (IMC), and working with the Department on budget coordination), revamping the national science, technology, and innovation information portal, and developing a knowledge management system.
Dr Cele described NACI's commitment to improving internal corporate governance, skills development, communication and branding, and promoting partnerships. He mentioned that the chairperson and council members play a role in stimulating private sector engagement and that the NACI is mandated to institutionalise foresight capability and has plans to implement and generate these outputs.
He summarised medium-term tasks, which include council-initiated and ministerial-directed advice, Monitoring and Evaluation (M&E) activities, and producing annual STI indicators reports. NACI seeks to raise its strategic profile and policy importance by establishing itself as an independent entity and contributing to the plenary and IMC, with effective communication, branding, and public engagement. He also described contributions to the transformation of the NSI, particularly by monitoring the implementation of Transformation Summit resolutions, developing the NSI Transformation Measurement Framework, and continuously producing reports on women's participation in STEM. NACI will also promote private sector participation and engagement.
Dr Cele noted the upcoming hosting of the G20 Chief Scientific Advisors, with experts meeting in April and the main event in September. The outcomes of this engagement may feed into ministerial and heads of state discussions on STI. NACI remains committed to deepening its participation and contribution at the regional and continental levels, promoting south-to-south partnerships, and continuing to work with existing strategic partners.

Technology Innovation Agency
(TIA) Presentation
Mr Loyiso Tyira, Chairperson, Technology Innovation Agency, mentioned that the Minister had already painted a picture of the current context, and emphasised that TIA is repositioning itself to address the challenges the Minister raised, including the outcomes of reviews and the decadal plan. Referencing the TIA Act and its objectives, he noted that economic growth and unemployment have been persistent issues in the country. Mr Tyira stressed that ensuring the innovation system works is crucial for addressing these challenges, especially in light of the global supply chain issues. He stated that TIA has been central to bridging the gap between research and the commercialisation of intellectual property. He introduced TIA 2.0, stating its promise to enhance the NSI to significantly impact innovation output. Mr Tyira then passed the presentation to the CEO for further details, indicating he would provide additional input later.
Mr Ismail Abdoola, Acting CEO, Technology Innovation Agency, mentioned revisiting TIA's vision, mission, and values as part of the strategic planning process, influenced by the TIA ministerial review process. He introduced TIA 2.0, explaining it as a strategic repositioning to establish TIA as a curator and thought leader within the NSI. He then highlighted the importance of customer-centeredness within TIA's values. Mr Abdoola described TIA's repositioning from primarily de-risking and funding projects to a curatorship role focused on optimisation. This optimisation involves collaboration with ecosystem partners, harmonising funding efforts, driving entrepreneurship, and providing thought leadership. He also stated that this strategy is underpinned by a revised business model aligned with thematic areas from the decadal plan and delivered through a three-tier model. He then went on to explain the three-tier business model, which includes the innovation curator role, driving commercialisation, and a funder of funds model. Mr Abdoola noted that this model will be implemented over ten years in three phases, namely, consolidation, growth, and scale. He emphasised the need for agility and re-evaluation throughout the strategy's implementation. He stated that the strategic plan aligns with national priorities such as inclusive growth, job creation, poverty reduction, and a capable state. He also noted that TIA aims to adopt an inclusive approach, serving underserved areas and supporting strategic development. Mr Abdoola reiterated that the decadal plan serves as a framework for structuring interventions.
He addressed macro challenges, including global sanctions affecting partners dependent on USAID, and committed to providing additional support, particularly in HIV/AIDS work. He also pointed out challenges within the NSI, such as the low translation of knowledge into goods and services and the need to develop the venture capital industry. Mr Abdoola detailed TIA's internal strategic context, including a R420 million budget, and noted efforts to direct the majority of funding towards investment-related activities while maintaining an efficiency ratio of 15% or below. He highlighted TIA's success in leveraging additional funding, raising R680 million in the last year against a R3 million target.
Mr Abdoola mentioned that the organisation has achieved an unqualified audit and maintains ISO 9001 standards. He identified turnaround times as a challenge and discussed ongoing efforts to improve efficiencies. In addition, he stated that stakeholder engagement is conducted through surveys, with a focus on continuous improvement. Mr Abdoola concluded by indicating that TIA conducts impact evaluation studies to learn and improve strategy implementation. He further explained that the implementation of the decadal plan involves deploying interventions and collaborating with relevant entities within the ecosystem, utilising existing programmes and infrastructure. He noted that TIA has identified specific objectives and focus areas within these thematic areas.

National Research Foundation (NRF) Presentation
Prior to the commencement by the NRF, the Chairperson expressed gratitude to the previous presenters and announced the transition to the National Research Foundation (NRF) presentation. She informed Members that the NRF board chairperson was scheduled to join at 2 pm and queried whether another board representative was present to lead the presentation.
Dr Fulufhelo Nelwamondo, Chief Executive Officer, National Research Foundation, confirmed that the chairperson was not yet online. He suggested that, with the Committee’s permission, Dr Mutshekwane stand in for the chairperson if available. He also noted the presence of another board member, Ms Khumalo, but deferred to the Chairperson for further guidance.
The Chairperson reiterated her request for any board member to represent the chairperson. She clarified that if no board member was prepared to assume this responsibility, Dr Nelwamondo would be permitted to proceed.
At this point, Ms Sapo (ANC) intervened, advising the Committee not to delay proceedings in search of a board representative. She stated that if the chairperson was unavailable, Dr Nelwamondo should proceed, and any related issues could be addressed later.
The Chairperson thanked Ms Sapo and explained that the NRF board chairperson’s absence was due to a scheduling misunderstanding, as the chairperson had been informed to join at 2 pm. She confirmed that since no board member was prepared to take responsibility, Dr Nelwamondo would proceed with the presentation.
Dr Nelwamondo apologised for the absence of board members, attributing this to the agreed schedule. He outlined that his presentation would cover the NRF Strategic Plan for 2030, the APP for 2025-2026, a review of the mandate, key highlights from the 2023-2025 Strategic Plan, the transition to the 2025-2030 Strategic Plan, and a future outlook. He reminded Members that the NRF’s mandate is anchored on four pillars. The first pillar is to support, promote, and advance research and human capacity development through funding and the provision of research infrastructure, to facilitate knowledge creation, innovation, and development in all fields of science and technology. The second pillar involves developing, supporting, and maintaining national research facilities, including large-scale infrastructures such as the Square Kilometre Array (SKA), which Members of the Committee had previously visited. The third pillar concerns supporting and promoting public awareness of, and engagement with, science, ensuring that scientific advances are communicated to and benefit the public. The fourth pillar is the promotion, development, and maintenance of the national science system, aligning with government priorities and supporting the NSI.
Dr Nelwamondo then provided key highlights from the implementation of the NRF Strategy 2025, noting that targets were set from a low base to enable rapid growth. He reported that the NRF had focused on the transformation of the science system, particularly the demographic profile and success rates of NRF-funded postgraduate students. The five-year target was 80% black and 55% women among graduates, and he was pleased to report that actual performance exceeded these targets, reaching 86% black and 61% women. Regarding the profile of NRF-funded researchers producing research outputs, the initial targets were 48% women and 42% black, both of which were surpassed.
He highlighted that the NRF had also prioritised enhancing the impact of the research enterprise by entrenching knowledge and societal impacts in the research it supports. The NRF developed and began implementing a research impact framework, approved in 2022-2023, with ongoing execution. Dr Nelwamondo emphasised that the NRF had established a strong baseline for inclusive support across all domains of science and was preparing a detailed report, to be presented with the annual report, that would illustrate the performance by scientific field and identify areas needing further attention.
He further reported on the completion of the research development information platform and the full implementation of the engaged research framework, which supports enhanced science engagement. Internally, the NRF focused on organisational transformation, aiming to create an institution that lives its values and culture. He acknowledged challenges in increasing the representation of women in fields such as astronomy and nuclear science, where a target of 38% was set but only 32% was achieved, although this represented progress from a lower base. Dr Nelwamondo assured Members that interventions were being implemented to address these shortcomings and would continue under the new strategy.
Transitioning to the NRF Strategic Plan 2030, Dr Nelwamondo reiterated the NRF’s mission to enable and facilitate the contribution of scientific research and knowledge to national development, with the overarching vision of "research for a better society". He explained that the NRF aims to position itself as a transformed and agile knowledge organisation, shaping and influencing all aspects of the research and knowledge enterprise with unwavering commitment to transformation, innovation, excellence, and sustainability, directed towards achieving societal impact.
He introduced the "TIES for Impact" framework, which stands for Transformation, Innovation, Excellence, and Sustainability. Transformation, he explained, goes beyond demographic change to encompass systemic transformation of the knowledge enterprise. Innovation focuses on enhancing the translation of research outputs into products and solutions that address national challenges such as poverty and unemployment, necessitating greater coordination with other science councils. Excellence is about striving for international competitiveness in research, students, and infrastructure. Sustainability includes the sustainability of the knowledge enterprise, the environment, and the resources needed to ensure continuity. Dr Nelwamondo emphasised that these pillars are essential for achieving impact on society, the economy, and the environment. He stressed that proper resourcing, infrastructure, a skilled workforce, effective science-society engagement, and both local and international collaboration are required to realise these goals. The 2030 strategy, he said, is designed to build on these four pillars to ensure greater impact. To achieve these objectives, Dr Nelwamondo outlined several strategic levers. The first is thought leadership, which involves promoting evidence-based policymaking and placing science at the centre of government decision-making. The NRF also aims to foster global and local knowledge leadership and to shape research agendas at the continental level. The second lever is the translation of research to innovation, ensuring that scientific research leads to tangible outcomes without compromising fundamental science. This includes optimising funding for national priorities, increasing the competitiveness of grant mechanisms, and aligning research outcomes with societal needs. The third lever is the creation of a research and data analytics hub, which will build capacity in data science and analytics, promote open access, and inform decision-making, especially in the context of artificial intelligence. The final lever is transformative resource mobilisation and national coordination, emphasising the importance of coordinated efforts across the science engagement landscape.
Dr Nelwamondo concluded by stating that proper coordination and implementation of these strategies are critical for the NRF’s future impact and alignment with national priorities. He reaffirmed the NRF’s commitment to transformation, innovation, excellence, and sustainability, and expressed confidence that the organisation is well-positioned to contribute meaningfully to national development through science and technology.

He explained that, as curator of the NSI, TIA’s perspective was to consider the entire innovation value chain. He illustrated this with an example: if the NRF funds a researcher and IP is registered, TIA might then fund the development of a prototype. However, innovators often struggle to commercialise their products domestically and are forced to seek overseas markets due to a lack of local market access. This, he said, is even more pronounced in government procurement. Mr Tyira argued that the government should use its significant procurement power to support and purchase local, sovereign technologies, thereby creating incentives for investment in South African innovation. He highlighted that investors, including venture capitalists, are primarily motivated by the prospect of returns, and that a supportive local market would encourage more investment in home-grown technologies.
Addressing questions from Mr Nkosi and Ms Mchunu about regulatory frameworks for startups, Mr Tyira stated that, even within the current regulatory environment, there was significant potential to support startups. He observed that bureaucratic hurdles, such as the overuse of strict procurement rules, often made it difficult for government officials to support innovative startups. He pointed out that the new procurement bill could help address these challenges and urged the Committee to assist in ensuring its effective implementation.
Turning to a question from Mr Ramangalo about stakeholder engagement, Mr Tyira explained that TIA’s newly approved strategy included the development of a comprehensive stakeholder engagement framework. The first step, he said, would be to engage with the Department to align policies and coordinate efforts. He emphasised that TIA’s stakeholders extended beyond the Department and the science and innovation sector to include companies, especially multinationals, that invest heavily in research and development. The TIA aimed to encourage these companies to leverage existing local IP and integrate it into their operations, rather than duplicating research efforts. He also highlighted TIA’s adoption of the “quadruple helix” model, which brings together government, academia, industry, and civil society to foster innovation and identify market opportunities.
On the issue of inclusivity and black participation, Mr Tyira assured the Committee that TIA was actively monitoring and setting targets for inclusivity and black participation in its funding portfolio. He noted that, for the first time, TIA had established numerical targets and was tracking progress closely. The board was committed to transparency and was willing to provide detailed lists of funded beneficiaries, including universities and individual innovators, to the Committee.
Regarding risk mitigation, Mr Tyira confirmed that TIA had identified a range of risks in its strategic plan, each with corresponding mitigation measures. He highlighted the importance of stakeholder buy-in and system functionality as key areas of focus for the board and indicated that the CEO would provide further details on these matters.
Mr Tyira then handed over to Mr Abdoola to address specific questions in greater detail.
Mr Abdoola began by thanking the Chairperson and Committee Members for their questions, noting that the board chairperson had already covered many of the key points. Responding to Dr Tembekwayo’s question about how TIA measures success in its funded projects, Mr Abdoola explained that TIA’s mandate is to support and promote the quality translation of R&D into commercialisation. He said that TIA’s strategic plan includes clear outcomes for measuring success, with the overarching goal of creating and supporting new economic sectors and driving the growth of existing sectors. TIA’s work, he explained, is intended to help shift South Africa from a resource-based economy to a knowledge-based economy, with intellectual property, technology, and human capital as key drivers of growth.
Mr Abdoola elaborated that TIA's new strategy positions the Agency as a curator of innovation, providing thought leadership and helping to position South Africa as an attractive investment destination. He summarised that TIA's impact would be measured by its ability to drive innovation and competitiveness within the global economy.
Addressing the question about eligibility criteria for funding, Mr Abdoola stated that requirements vary depending on the specific funding instrument, but generally, TIA funds projects with unique intellectual property, except in the case of grassroots innovation programmes that address local problems. He clarified that eligible applicants must be South African citizens or registered juristic persons (companies) that are compliant with the Companies and Intellectual Property Commission (CIPC) and the South African Revenue Service (SARS). He added that the TIA does not fund certain sectors, and its funding priorities are aligned with government priorities and thematic areas.
Responding to Mr Nkosi’s question about regulatory frameworks for startups, Mr Abdoola emphasised that policy advocacy is a key element of TIA’s new strategy. He said TIA is committed to pushing for regulations and laws that support innovation, technology development, and startup growth. He described TIA’s efforts to engage with regulators to develop “regulatory sandboxes” in areas like FinTech, AgriTech, and HealthTech, allowing startups to test innovations under lighter regulatory conditions.
Mr Abdoola also addressed questions about support for underserved provinces, explaining that TIA targets support for the Northern Cape, Limpopo, Free State, Eastern Cape, North West, and Mpumalanga, as well as specific districts and metropolitan municipalities such as the Ugu District, Zululand, and Ekurhuleni.
On the topic of transformation and inclusivity, particularly within the ICT sector, Mr Abdoola reported that TIA had funded 51 ICT projects to the value of approximately R283 million, with 64% of that funding directed to black-owned initiatives. In the venture capital space, TIA had historically supported three emerging fund managers, and since 2023, had committed to two more, with plans to onboard seven new black or women-owned fund managers.
In response to a question about healthcare sector funding, Mr Abdoola indicated that TIA had been increasing its support for black-owned healthcare projects to address historical imbalances and portfolio concentration. He undertook to provide detailed data on this to the Committee in writing.
With these responses, the Chairperson thanked TIA and all participants for their engagement and contributions. She reiterated the Committee’s commitment to advocating for adequate funding and supporting innovation in South Africa.
Meeting adjourned
