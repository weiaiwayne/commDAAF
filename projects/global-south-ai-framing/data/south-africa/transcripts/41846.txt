Meeting Summary
2024/25 Annual Reports
The Committee met to consider Statistics SA’s 2024/25 Annual Report.
Stats SA achieved 96.9% of its targets (380 of 392). The organisation received an unqualified audit opinion with findings. Concerns included unauthorised expenditure (R1.19 billion) and irregular expenditure (R850 million), much of it linked to the 2022 Census. Stats SA’s vacancy rate stood at 21.8% (720 posts) due to funding shortfalls. Persistent budget cuts and underfunding threaten operational sustainability. The Deputy Minister indicated that Stats SA hopes the Medium Term Budget Policy Statement (MTBPS) will address the shortfall.
Members expressed concern over the loss of tablets (worth ~R102 million) from the 2022 Census. They demanded stricter controls, accountability, and recovery efforts. Members criticised Stats SA’s R1.19 billion overdraft, urging the organisation to “live within its means” and prioritise essential outputs. Questions were raised about employment equity targets and the rationale for citing funding limitations as a barrier to diversity goals. Members flagged delays in publishing key reports, stressing the need for real-time statistics to inform policy decisions. Members asked how Stats SA was using AI, digital tools, and data portals to improve accessibility and efficiency. Members agreed to engage National Treasury directly regarding funding, recognising data as a national asset.
Meeting report
Chairperson's Opening Remarks
The Chairperson welcomed everyone to the meeting and acknowledged that an agenda had been circulated to all Members and colleagues. She noted that the main item for discussion was a presentation from Statistics South Africa (Stats SA).
She highlighted the critical role that Stats SA plays in the country’s development, expressing the Portfolio Committee’s appreciation for the institution’s work in providing data-driven insights that inform policy decisions, drive economic growth, and promote social progress. In a rapidly changing world, she emphasised, accurate and reliable data is more important than ever, as it helps identify challenges and opportunities and measure progress. Stats SA’s dedication to producing high-quality statistics, she noted, is central to these efforts.
Acknowledging that Stats SA has consistently demonstrated its commitment to excellence, she also recognised the challenges highlighted in the Auditor-General's report presented to the Portfolio Committee. As they discussed Stats SA’s performance, she stressed several priority areas for the Committee: ensuring data reliability and trustworthiness, promoting accessibility of data in user-friendly formats, developing the skills and expertise required for high-quality statistics, and fostering collaboration and partnerships to leverage resources and expertise.
She emphasised that these areas are vital to Stats SA's success and its ability to fulfil its mandate. The Portfolio Committee, she added, is committed to providing oversight and support to ensure the institution has the resources and guidance necessary to excel.
The Chairperson also noted apologies that had been received. Following this, Mr M Manyi (MKP) moved for the adoption of the agenda, which was seconded by Ms A Kumbaca (ANC).
Remarks from the Deputy Minister
Ms Nonceba Mhlauli, Deputy Minister in the Presidency, welcomed everyone to the meeting and noted that the purpose was to present Statistics South Africa's (Stats SA) annual reports for the 2024/25 financial year. She explained that this year marked the final year of implementing the organisation's five-year strategic plan. She highlighted that, despite financial and human resource constraints, Stats SA had demonstrated remarkable resilience, achieving over 90% of its targets since the start of the strategic planning cycle in 2020–2021.
She emphasised that this performance reflected not only operational efficiency but also Stats SA’s pivotal role, as the Chairperson had noted, in shaping evidence-based policy and supporting developmental outcomes across the country. The Committee was therefore being presented with an overview of Stats SA's financial and organisational performance.
The Deputy Minister reported that the Acting Statistician-General would highlight key achievements against the strategic outcomes of the five-year plan. For the year under review, Stats SA received a financially unqualified audit report with findings, which had been discussed previously with the Portfolio Committee, including during the budget vote earlier in the year.
She acknowledged ongoing financial challenges, particularly the rising cost of employment and services, which had affected Stats SA's vacancy rate and its ability to meet employment targets. Despite these constraints, the organisation successfully delivered timely, relevant, and accurate information on the economy and society, publishing over 290 statistical releases during the year. She cautioned, however, that the sustainability of these efforts remained at risk due to resource limitations that Stats SA had consistently raised.
The Deputy Minister expressed hope that the upcoming Medium Term Budget Policy Statement (MTBPS) process, expected to be tabled in Parliament in the coming weeks, will address funding issues, as indicated by the Minister of Finance during Budget 3.0.
She concluded with positive news that the new Statistics Act had been signed into law by the President in December 2024. The Act is intended to enhance statistical production and coordination by strengthening partnerships among data producers. She added that Stats SA continues to collaborate with government departments to improve data quality and achieve official statistics standards.
Stats SA Annual Report 2024/25
Stats SA's vision is to "Improve lives through data ecosystems", and its mission is to "transform the production, coordination and use of statistics through optimisation, innovation and partnerships." Over the 2024/25 financial year, the organisation focused on delivering key strategic outcomes, including generating insightful data, maintaining an agile operating model, developing interconnected statistical systems, and transforming organisational capabilities. The year marked the final phase of the five-year strategic plan, with priorities centred on driving transformation and change, disseminating the results of Census 2022, sustaining the quality of national indicators, and progressing the Statistics Amendment Bill through Parliament.
Organisational performance remained strong, with a 96.9% achievement rate, as Stats SA successfully completed 380 of its 392 planned targets. The 12 targets that were not met were primarily affected by funding and human resource constraints, dependency on stakeholders, and reprioritisation of activities. Despite these challenges, the organisation continued to provide valuable statistical insight through more than 290 annual publications, covering the broad themes of People, Living Conditions, the Economy, and the Natural Environment. These outputs continue to inform evidence-based policymaking and provide critical data for national development planning.
Human resources remained a key area of focus, with a vacancy rate of 21.8% as of 31 March 2025, amounting to 720 unfilled posts out of a total of 3 301. Employment equity targets are contingent on additional funding allocations, and current HR indicators show that 55.4% of staff are female and 89.6% are Black African.
On the financial front, Stats SA received an unqualified audit opinion from the Auditor-General, with a matter of emphasis noted, and one material misstatement in the performance audit was corrected. Irregular expenditure totalling R850.258 million remains under review by National Treasury, while total unauthorised expenditure amounted to R1,191.750 million, largely attributable to R807.987 million in 2022/23 for the Census 2022 extension of data collection. In 2024/25, unauthorised expenditure was reported at R83.303 million, resulting from budget reductions and limitations on virement rules.
Stats SA continues to face significant strategic risks and operational challenges, which are categorised under demand, the business operating model, supply, and resources. Resource constraints, particularly insufficient funding for the cost of employment, a high vacancy rate, and inadequate ICT infrastructure, have hindered the organisation’s ability to fill posts, maintain a fully skilled data collection workforce, and sustain the quality of statistical indicators.
Key projects, including the Continuous Population Survey, have experienced delays as a result. Regarding legislative developments, it was recommended that the Statistics Amendment Bill be fast-tracked, with Presidential assent targeted for December 2024 and proclamation in September 2025. Strengthening collaboration with the private sector has been an important focus, resulting in Memoranda of Understanding with over 20 National Statistical System entities. Stats SA has also requested additional funding from the National Treasury to address shortfalls and protect the quality of statistical indicators, although requests for ICT investment were not approved.

Mr D Bergman (DA) said that it was very hard to move from the Auditor-General’s report and then look at the Stats SA report, because the challenges were well known, especially those related to budget constraints. He stated that the Chief Financial Officer (CFO) had dealt with what he believed was the biggest problem, the issue of wasteful and fruitless expenditure, with the highlight being the R102 million loss. He explained that he could understand when someone said it was R102 million, and when one considered the amount of equipment, weighed up the options, and compared the cost of recovering R60 million to R102 million against spending R4.5 million, the latter seemed minor in the greater scheme of things.
He added that, as the CFO had mentioned, the logistics of driving around, fetching, and contacting everyone involved were a challenge. However, he said he would have thought that the internal controls of an institution like Stats SA, with many Deputy Directors-General (DDGs) and Directors-General (DGs) responsible for oversight, would have been strong enough to manage their equipment. He noted that, given the Deputy Minister’s pride in the introduction of the new amendment bill, he would have expected that Stats SA would have mechanisms in place to monitor and control its devices such as cell phones, tablets, and other equipment issued to field staff.
He emphasised that, as the organisation improved its use of technology, it would be handing out more equipment. If, however, R102 million worth of equipment was being lost every year and effectively given away as disbursements or “gifts” to field workers, the Auditor-General would continue to reprimand the department annually. He asserted that there had to be accountability, whether from the Statistician-General (SG), the CFO, or someone else, because someone needed to take responsibility.
He remarked that there were apps on tablets and cell phones that allowed users to trace their devices, noting that even ten-year-olds knew how to use tracking features like “Find My Phone” or “Find My Tablet.” He added humorously that one could even buy a tracking tag for R40 or R50 at the parliamentary shop to trace their belongings. He stressed that accountability was essential and that one could not simply “throw their hands up in the air.” Instead, they needed to find solutions and ensure proper asset management.
He stated that if Stats SA was going to introduce more technology and request more funding, it needed to demonstrate that it could safeguard its assets. He added that assets were not only physical equipment but also human resources, and that the organisation had a major challenge in this regard. Referring to their recent engagement with the Auditor-General, he said that there were long-serving staff members, some with decades of experience. Yet, there was a "missing middle" between these veterans and the new recruits. He observed that young graduates were attracted to work at Stats SA but often did not stay long, leading to a loss of institutional capacity.
He illustrated this by referring to the case of Calvin Molongoana, the Director-General (DG), asking why someone who had been with the institution for decades would leave a DG position to take up a Deputy Director-General (DDG) role in Limpopo. He said that this was a clear example of how valuable human resources were being lost cheaply, much like the organisation's tablets and cell phones. He therefore questioned who would be held accountable for the missing equipment, stating that he did not accept the explanation that the organisation had simply "lost it" or that it was unwilling to pay R4.5 million to trace it.
He said he believed that every tablet had a serial number and could be traced. He suggested that similar to Parliament's approach when a Member leaves, Stats SA could allow departing staff to purchase their devices at a discounted rate as an incentive or cost-recovery measure.
He then turned to the issue of target setting, urging honesty and self-reflection. He said that when an organisation achieved 98% of its targets despite lacking sufficient budget and facing significant operational challenges, it raised questions about whether the targets were realistic. He argued that Stats SA might be setting the bar too low, making it easy to achieve. Referring to a colleague's analogy, he said the organisation was using a "chip and pat" approach — merely patting over the bar rather than jumping over it.
He criticised the organisation's performance dashboard, saying that if it only produced positive feedback without highlighting real issues, it was not an effective management tool but rather a "yes-men" dashboard. He said such a dashboard failed to provide the kind of warning indicators that leaders needed, such as alerts about unpreparedness for a census year or upcoming budget challenges. He concluded by saying he would prefer to see Stats SA set ambitious, realistic targets — even if it meant occasionally failing, rather than producing overly optimistic reports. He emphasised that while the organisation might present all "green lights," the Auditor-General's report still showed R102 million in missing equipment and a situation where senior officials like DGs were leaving for lower positions in other provinces.

As an example, she said the IMF standard required that population estimates be published at least once a year, meaning that their statistical publications followed international guidelines. She said they welcomed the proposal made by Members of the Committee for the Portfolio Committee to play a role in engaging with the Appropriations Committee during budget processes. She also thanked members for their suggestions, including the one regarding the definition of "African" versus "black," which the team would revisit to ensure proper legal alignment. She acknowledged all other inputs from members and assured that her team would look into them accordingly.
Further Discussion
Mr Manyi said that he was not going to debate with the Deputy Minister, but he would like to make a few points. Firstly, he stated that he would send a report from Stats SA that clearly shows how the NDP targets have been missed. He recalled that this presentation had been done by the Statistician-General himself and expressed surprise that the Deputy Minister was not aware of it. He reiterated that he would share that presentation with the Deputy Minister.
Secondly, he welcomed the Deputy Minister’s commitment, along with her team, to review the issue regarding the distinction between “black” and “African.” He further noted that there was a public service application form, known as the Z83, which should serve as a clear reference point on the matter, since the form does not use the term “black African.”
He then raised another concern, saying that he sensed there was a misunderstanding about what constitutes an establishment within a department. He explained that when applying for a budget, the department's organogram must be aligned with funded posts. He emphasised that unfunded posts should not be regarded as part of the establishment, as they do not exist until funding is allocated.
He pointed out that it was problematic for a department to claim it was unable to perform certain functions due to vacancies when those vacancies were actually unfunded. He stressed that a legitimate vacancy only exists when a post is funded but cannot be filled due to a lack of suitable skills. Therefore, it was incorrect to attribute performance challenges to vacancies caused by lack of funds, as that simply reflects a “wish list” rather than an actual establishment.
He advised the department to re-examine its establishment and ensure that references to vacancies are limited to funded posts. He concluded by saying that it was absurd to hear departments claiming they had vacancies simply because they did not have the money to fill them, as those posts technically did not exist.
Ms Aphiri said that, for knowledge and interest’s sake, she wanted to raise a question. She noted that South Africa’s population currently stands at about 63 million and mentioned that, when Stats SA collects data, some of the households they visit are occupied by foreign nationals. She then asked whether it could be said with certainty that the country knows exactly how many foreigners are residing within its borders.
She added that this information is important because there are instances where government departments, such as the Department of Education, report insufficient funding based on the estimated population and number of learners they have on record. However, in reality, schools and hospitals often accommodate more people than planned for, many of whom may be foreign nationals. She therefore requested clarity on whether Stats SA has accurate data on the number of foreigners currently living in the country.
Mr Solly Molayi, Acting DDG: Population and Social Statistics, responded and said that, to be brief, when one looks at the census, as he had already mentioned, they were collecting data in all areas. He explained that the census was not a survey, as they went to all areas to collect information from everyone within the borders of South Africa. He stated that they had figures from the census regarding those who were foreign-born, and at that time, the number was around 2.4 million. He added that in the population estimates, they were counting everyone within the borders, using the many records available to them. He emphasised that they were talking about individuals, including those coming from their own surveys conducted across different areas. He further explained that the mid-year population was an estimate based on all available data sources. He concluded by saying that they could share with the Committee the information they had on foreign-born individuals from their surveys and other related programs.
Dr Nzimande-Mbele responded and said that they always appreciated the opportunity to come and speak to the Portfolio Committee. However, more importantly, she mentioned that they had not previously had the opportunity to explain how the Council actually worked in relation to Stats SA. She explained that their role was to play an oversight function in guarding official statistics, and in doing so, they had four committees that worked with different sectors dealing with various products, including population and population statistics, economics, overall strategy, and methods.
She stated that they had worked closely in reviewing the output produced by Stats SA, as well as assessing its achievements and identifying some of the gaps that had been noted during the previous financial year. She said they highly commended Stats SA for the quality and success it continued to produce, particularly in light of the Amendment Act that had been passed.
She emphasised that they were very concerned, and continued to be concerned, about the issue of budget cuts. To be more specific, she said she would outline two areas that had already been mentioned. When discussing vacancies, she explained that the Council listened to different departments and sections about what they were expected to produce versus the actual implications on human resources, especially in terms of production capacity. She acknowledged that members from Stats SA had indicated that they were currently operating at a level where they were producing statistical outputs that were critical for the country and essential for planning ahead.
She stated that when vacancies remained unfilled, it meant that the ability to produce what was required became significantly compromised. Moreover, when one considered the Amendment Act and the additional responsibilities that had been placed on Stats SA, including the development and safeguarding of the national statistical system, it was clear that these responsibilities were not simply “nice to have,” but rather essential to the country’s needs.
She therefore stressed that there should be discussions on how to ensure that Stats SA received adequate financial support. She explained that with such support, the planning processes across various organs of state would become much easier, as they would have better access to data and be able to input data more effectively for planning purposes.
She concluded by saying that, for her, it was about appreciating the opportunity to engage, acknowledging what they were able to accomplish, and recognising the support that the Committee was providing in terms of funding. She added that they continued to have such discussions regularly and remained hopeful for positive progress moving forward.
Chairperson’s Concluding Remarks
The Chairperson thanked all Members of the Portfolio Committee for their participation and engagement. On behalf of the Committee, she also extended appreciation to the Deputy Minister, Professor Nzimande, and the team from Stats SA for their contributions. She noted that the Committee had recently met with the Department of Planning, Monitoring and Evaluation, and that this marked their second engagement with Stats SA.
She explained that the Portfolio Committee was in the process of consolidating its report, drawing on key findings and observations from all the annual reports, and that the Committee would present its recommendations the following week. While she did not expect the Committee's recommendations for Stats SA to differ significantly from those submitted to Parliament the previous year, she emphasised that the issues largely remained the same, particularly concerns regarding financial support, which continued to require attention.
The Chairperson suggested that, for the next quarter's program, the Committee should consider including an item to facilitate joint meetings with the Appropriations Committee. She concluded by stressing that unless the Committee continued to emphasise the importance of adequate financial support, it risked returning repeatedly to the same discussions and revisiting the same unresolved issues.
The meeting was adjourned.
