Meeting Summary
2024/25 Annual Reports
The Portfolio Committee on Science, Technology and Innovation met to consider the 2024/25 annual reports of the Human Sciences Research Council (HSRC) and the Academy of Science of South Africa (ASSAf). The HSRC reported that its work remained guided by its statutory mandate to advance human sciences research, inform policy development, and build capacity across Africa and beyond. It achieved most of its targets, producing 413 research outputs, including 135 journal articles, 13 books, and 25 policy briefs. The entity highlighted studies such as the 30 Years of Electoral Democracy survey and the HIV prevalence survey as examples of its contribution to evidence-based policymaking. It maintained an unqualified audit opinion, though it recorded a deficit of R57.8 million due to the accounting treatment of prior surpluses.
ASSAf reported that it continued to serve as South Africa's official national Academy of science, with its work guided by the principles of excellence, inclusivity, and independence. Its activities were structured around three pillars, namely Science for Science, Science for Policy, and Science for Society. The Academy presented research outputs including consensus studies on biosafety, gene therapy, pesticide poisoning, and health governance, while also addressing issues such as gender-based violence and air pollution. ASSAf noted ongoing work to strengthen scholarly publishing and digital transformation and acknowledged challenges related to limited funding, transformation, and low uptake of recommendations by policymakers. The Academy also emphasised its efforts to promote diversity, particularly among women in science, technology, engineering, and mathematics (STEM), and to expand participation from underrepresented universities.
Committee Members raised several questions regarding financial management, the entities’ engagement with parliamentary committees, and the practical impact of their research. Concerns were expressed about the HSRC’s financial deficit, staff retention, and whether its research had translated into measurable policy change. Members also asked about ASSAf’s information technology (IT) controls, publication output, transformation record, and international travel expenditure. Further questions covered the influence of artificial intelligence on research, the entities’ collaboration with government departments, and the need for stronger mechanisms to ensure policy uptake. Members commended both institutions for their clean audit outcomes but encouraged them to improve financial sustainability, expand revenue diversification, and enhance their societal relevance.

HSRC 2024/25 Annual Report
The Human Sciences Research Council (HSRC), through Prof Sarah Mosoetsa, Chief Executive Officer (CEO), HSRC reported that its work during the 2024/25 financial year was guided by the mandate set out in the HSRC Act 17 of 2008, which required the institution to conduct human sciences research relevant to South Africa, Africa and beyond, inform policy development, and build research capacity. The Council also sought to promote collaboration, debate, and data dissemination to address development challenges and support evidence-based decision-making.
It was stated that the HSRC achieved an exceptional performance outcome, meeting or exceeding nearly all its targets. The organisation produced 413 research outputs, including 135 journal articles, 13 books, 65 book chapters, and 25 policy briefs. These outputs reflected the HSRC’s continued leadership in knowledge production and policy influence. Requests for HSRC research came mainly from academic and government institutions both locally and internationally, with significant engagement from the United Kingdom and the United States (US).
The presentation highlighted several major research achievements, such as the 30 Years of Electoral Democracy in South Africa survey, the Sixth South African National HIV, Behavioural and Health Survey (SABSSM VI HIV) prevalence survey, and the launch of the Independent Electoral Commission (IEC) Electronic Voting Study. Other important initiatives included the Comparative Study on the Agrarian Rural Household Economy, the National Gender-Based Violence Study, the African Early Stage Investor Summit (AESIS) Network Conference, and the African Young Graduates and Scholars Conference. These projects underscored the HSRC’s contribution to shaping national policy, advancing research excellence, and strengthening continental collaboration.
In terms of governance and financial performance, it was reported that the HSRC maintained an unqualified audit opinion with no material findings, reflecting sound financial management and compliance. Although a deficit of R57.8 million was recorded, this was primarily attributed to increased spending from the approved surplus budget allocation by National Treasury (NT). The HSRC’s Human Resources division focused on organisational development, salary parity, and cultural transformation initiatives to enhance staff engagement and performance.

ASSAf 2024/25 Annual Report
The ASSAf, through Prof Himla Soodyall, Executive Officer, ASSAf, reported that it was inaugurated in 1996, with Nelson Mandela as its patron, and formally established under the Academy of Science Act, 67 of 2001. As the official national Academy, its vision was to be the apex organisation for science and scholarship in South Africa, internationally respected and connected. The Academy's mandate was to honour distinguished scholars through election to membership and to provide independent, evidence-based advice to government and other stakeholders.
It was stated that ASSAf’s mission had been expanded to include catalysing innovation that promoted sustainable development, inclusivity, and socioeconomic growth, while aligning with both national and global priorities. The presentation outlined the Academy’s focus on transformation, equity redress, and inclusivity, with particular emphasis on advancing opportunities for women, youth, and persons with disabilities. ASSAf recognised ongoing challenges related to representation in academia, noting that in 2022, only 35% of full professors were black and 31% were female.
The presentation highlighted that ASSAf’s work was structured around three guiding pillars:
Science for Science
Science for Policy
Science for Society.
Programmes included science advisory work, scholarly publishing, and national and international liaison. The Academy produced consensus studies on issues such as biosafety and biosecurity in the Southern African Development Community (SADC) region, ethical and legal aspects of gene therapy, and food-related pesticide poisoning. It also conducted research and convened discussions on governance in the health system, gender-based violence in schools, air pollution, and social policy.
ASSAf reported ongoing efforts to enhance scholarly publishing through the South African Journal of Science, the Scientific Electronic Library Online (SciELO) platform, and initiatives promoting open science and digital transformation. The Academy continued to face challenges, particularly limited funding, insufficient member engagement, and slow uptake of study recommendations. Despite these constraints, it maintained its commitment to providing authoritative scientific advice, supporting transformation within the research community, and strengthening the role of science in addressing South Africa’s societal challenges.

Responses from the HSRC
Prof Mosoetsa thanked the Chairperson and Members for their questions and said she would provide some opening remarks before allowing the executive team to respond in more detail. She reminded members that the HSRC's core mandate was to influence policy through research rather than to draft or implement policy directly. It was explained that the organisation contributed by providing evidence-based insights, participating in advisory forums, and supporting government departments at both national and international levels. Researchers from the HSRC served on key structures, such as the National Human Resources Council and the National Health Insurance Council, to ensure research evidence-informed decision-making.
Addressing the question on artificial intelligence (AI), the CEO said the HSRC recognised both the opportunities and challenges posed by AI and was actively implementing a digital transformation strategy that included research on AI itself. This work aimed to inform national policy and contribute to public understanding of emerging technologies. She also responded to questions about engagement with parliamentary committees, confirming that the HSRC regularly collaborated with various departments, including the Department of Agriculture and the Department of Women, particularly on projects such as the agrarian and land study, and the national gender-based violence research. She welcomed further guidance from Members on additional committees the organisation should engage with.
On matters relating to staff well-being, Dr Mosoetsa clarified that the HSRC maintained an open and healthy working environment supported by regular engagement with the staff union and a consultative forum. She said staff movement was expected in any organisation and that many interns left early as secured permanent employment elsewhere had been found, which the HSRC viewed positively.
Responding to concerns about the scope of research, she emphasised that the HSRC conducted ethical and independent studies, including work related to state capture through its contributions to the Judicial Commission of Inquiry into State Capture, Corruption and Fraud in the Public Sector, as well as ongoing research on public attitudes, political participation, and governance. She addressed questions on the IEC electronic voting study, confirming that it was a national and representative project commissioned by the IEC and still in progress. It was stated that the study captured diverse perspectives, including those from rural communities, and reflected public concerns about connectivity and digital inclusion, noting that the HSRC would be happy to provide a detailed briefing to the Committee once the study was complete.
Prof Sharlene Swartz, Divisional Executive: Equitable Education and Economies, HSRC, responded to a question regarding the impact of the organisation’s policy briefs. It was explained that the HSRC had prepared a detailed briefing for the Minister, which could also be shared with the Portfolio Committee, outlining how 18 recent policy briefs had contributed to shaping government policy.
As an example, she noted that one of the HSRC’s policy briefs had informed the drafting of the National Policy on Women’s Empowerment and Gender Equality for South Africa. The same brief had also influenced the revised National Policy on Human Settlements, specifically the sections addressing empowerment, transformation, and gender mainstreaming. This work had been carried out in close collaboration with the Department of Women, Youth, and Persons with Disabilities.
A second example related to the HSRC’s e-Participation and Policy Modelling Platform for South Africa, which had been incorporated into the draft of a new public participation policy for one of the metropolitan municipalities. The HSRC hoped that this approach would later be adopted by other municipalities.
In conclusion, Prof Swartz announced that the HSRC would be hosting a conference on engaged policy research the following week. The event aimed to demonstrate how the organisation’s research had been translated into practical policy influence, and the forthcoming report from the conference would be shared with the Committee, as it would provide further insight into the HSRC’s efforts to link research and policymaking.
In response to Ms Mazzone’s question regarding AI, Dr Lucky Ditaunyane, Chief Operating Officer (COO), HSRC, informed the Committee that the organisation had completed the drafting of a Digital Transformation Framework to guide its digital development trajectory. The framework covered all aspects of digital transformation, including the integration and governance of AI technologies.
He explained that, from a human resources perspective, the framework also addressed the skills and capabilities required to support AI adoption, particularly in relation to future recruitment and workforce planning. In addition to this, the HSRC had drafted a dedicated AI Policy to complement the framework.
Both the Digital Transformation Framework and the AI Policy would be submitted through internal governance structures for Board approval. The COO concluded that these initiatives were designed to ensure that the HSRC remained well-positioned to adapt to ongoing advances in digital and AI technologies.
Ms Jacomien Rousseau, Chief Financial Officer (CFO), HSRC, responded to questions concerning the organisation’s income and financial position. She explained that the entity’s revenue had fluctuated mainly due to a decline in international funding, particularly from the US. In the 2022/23 financial year, international income accounted for around 64% of total revenue, but this had dropped to 37% in the current year. The decline was attributed to geopolitical risks and the timing of international surveys determined by funders. As a result, the HSRC was seeking to diversify its funding base by engaging with alternative international partners to reduce dependency on US funding.
Addressing the reported deficit of R57 million, the CFO clarified that public entities classified under Schedule 3A of the PFMA could not plan surpluses or deficits. Any surplus funds had to be surrendered to the NT unless permission was granted to retain the funds. Previous surpluses from 2021/22 and 2022/23 had been surrendered, which affected the financial statements and cash flow position. Despite this, the HSRC remained financially stable, with a liquidity ratio of 1.1 at the end of the financial year, improving slightly to 1.12 by the end of September.
The CFO reported that the HSRC had implemented several cost-containment measures to ensure prudent spending and sustainability. The organisation was focusing on cost recovery and time-billing models, ensuring that research projects covered operational costs. Lessons from recent financial cycles had reinforced the need for the entity to live within its means and maintain strong financial discipline.
Regarding internal audit findings, the CFO stated that the internal controls had been assessed as “improvement needed,” which was the second-highest rating below “satisfactory.” Areas for improvement included updating policies, refining controls, and strengthening oversight. The CFO added that the HSRC’s improved Broad-Based Black Economic Empowerment score would enhance competitiveness in government tender processes, noting that the organisation was not automatically awarded research contracts and had to compete in open public tenders.
The CEO then addressed concerns raised about the reported financial deficit, clarifying that it was a technical accounting deficit resulting from how surpluses were treated in the financial statements rather than an indication of financial mismanagement. She assured Members that the situation was entirely different from that of three years prior, when the organisation had faced issues related to fruitless and wasteful expenditure. The current deficit, she emphasised, did not warrant accountability measures against management, as it was simply a reflection of the accounting treatment of previously surrendered surpluses.
The CEO thanked the Chairperson and Members for the continued engagement and support, noting that such interactions encouraged the HSRC to remain committed to achieving meaningful impact through its work. She stated that the organisation constantly strived to ensure its research remained relevant to South Africa’s developmental priorities and informed public policy.
In closing, the CEO indicated that going forward, the HSRC would aim to provide clearer, more concise presentations to the Committee, including a snapshot of key projects and their immediate impact, while also highlighting research areas expected to yield long-term policy influence in line with the country’s policy development processes.
ASSAf responses
Prof Julian May, Council Member, thanked the Members for the questions and explained that the key issues would be addressed before handing over to the CEO and management team. His focus would be on three main areas, beginning with the influence of scientific evidence on policy. It was stated that research by the HSRC on food security and nutrition, conducted in 2023, had directly informed the revision of South Africa’s Food and Nutrition Security Strategy in 2024 and 2025. He further mentioned that evidence from the HSRC and related institutions had shaped advisories submitted to the Minister of Planning, Monitoring and Evaluation. He emphasised that the use of evidence in policymaking was a gradual process requiring persistence and coordination, citing the lengthy adoption of the revised Statistics Amendment Act 29 of 2024 as an example.
Prof May further highlighted that the Academy of Science had established a forum on the responsible use of AI in research and innovation. He acknowledged the potential risks of AI, such as plagiarism and academic dishonesty, but stressed its value in enhancing innovation, synthesis, and communication. The Academy had already produced a document on the ethical use of AI in universities, which was being shared across higher education institutions to guide policy development.
In addressing the Academy’s capacity for transformation, he noted that it operated with a limited annual budget and relied heavily on voluntary contributions from its members. Despite its size, the Academy aimed to use its resources strategically to foster a research community that tackled societal challenges, rather than functioning merely as a discussion platform.
Finally, Prof May discussed the Academy’s commitment to equity and diversity. While it was not required to maintain an employment equity plan for its membership, it had developed strategies to broaden representation across universities and promote inclusivity. Efforts were being made to attract nominations from a wider range of institutions, particularly those historically underrepresented, and the Academy had made notable progress in increasing the participation of women academics in STEM fields.
Prof Soodyall added to the discussion by emphasising that ASSAf worked strictly within its mandate, as outlined in its presentation. She reminded the Committee that the Academy had been founded under the patronage of the late President Nelson Mandela, following a deliberate effort to establish an institution that would support and elevate academic interests within the national system of innovation in a democratic, post-apartheid context. She expressed disappointment at the perception that the Academy merely engaged in internal discussion, stating that it worked actively to address national challenges.
Prof Soodyall highlighted the Academy’s community engagement initiatives, noting that staff had personally contributed funds and materials to support an adopted old-age home, including repairing roofs and providing food for residents, many of whom were elderly or living with disabilities. The Academy had also adopted a school, where members with scientific backgrounds assisted teachers and learners with laboratory work and practical demonstrations to enhance science education. She stressed that such initiatives reflected the Academy’s commitment to social responsibility and development, even within its limited resources.
In response to queries about publications, it was clarified that producing books was not the Academy’s primary mandate. However, it had published a public-oriented volume during the COVID-19 pandemic to clarify misinformation, featuring multidisciplinary contributions from experts. She explained that the Academy’s main outputs were consensus studies and proceedings reports, all accessible through its public repository.
Finally, Prof Soodyall discussed the Academy’s consensus studies in the health sector, noting that these were part of a long-standing series addressing governance and administrative issues in hospitals. It was stated that while the Academy could not directly fix failing institutions such as hospitals or schools, it continued to engage with government departments and local municipalities, contributing academic expertise and support in alignment with national development initiatives such as the District Development Model (DDM).
Ms Susan Veldsman, Director: Scholarly Publication Programme, ASSAf, explained that two audit interventions, one internal and one external, had been conducted in relation to the Academy’s IT systems. Following the internal audit, the Academy reviewed and updated its IT controls and standard operating procedures to ensure secure and reliable networks for staff and stakeholders. The subsequent external audit called for further refinement and detail in the IT policies, which were revised accordingly. The updated policies were due to be presented to the Audit and Risk Committee for approval, after which these would be submitted to the Council for ratification.
Turning to AI, she confirmed that the organisation was proactively engaging with the issue through quarterly AI Lekgotla discussions addressing emerging challenges and opportunities. The upcoming session, titled
It’s a Jungle Out There: AI Policies in Universities
, would explore the varied approaches taken by higher education institutions to manage AI in teaching and research. She stressed that while AI posed risks, it also offered significant benefits, and the Academy aimed to provide broad frameworks to guide institutions in using it responsibly. A dedicated AI forum was being established to strengthen collaboration and develop informed strategies within the sector.
Ms Veldsman then discussed the distribution of the
Quest
science magazine, explaining that the collapse of the national postal system had made it impossible to send copies directly to schools. Instead, the Academy had shifted distribution to 57 science centres across both urban and rural communities, as well as national libraries. About 13,000 hard copies were produced annually to address limited internet access in rural areas.
In relation to employment and skills development, it was stated that five of the 11 staff members in the scholarly publishing programme were interns from the HSRC internship scheme, many of whom had been absorbed into permanent roles. The Academy continued to make small but meaningful contributions to job creation and capacity building.
Finally, Ms Veldsman elaborated on the Academy’s community outreach projects. These included support for an Old Age Home in Hammanskraal, where staff assisted with water quality improvements and planned basic care training with nursing experts. The Academy had also adopted Dr A.T. Moresuela High School in Soshanguve, hosting science fairs in collaboration with partner organisations to promote science education and engagement among learners.
Dr Melusi Thwala, Manager: Science Advisory Programme and Strategic Partnerships, ASSAf, responded to a question regarding the Academy’s recent work on gender-based violence (GBV), explaining that the focus in this area had been informed both by national statistics and by findings from a 2023–24 study funded by the International Development Research Centre (IDRC). The study, which examined barriers to women's participation in science across selected African universities, revealed that GBV was a significant factor limiting women's involvement, particularly in the male-dominated fields of science, engineering, and technology. It was found that issues such as underreporting and confidentiality continued to hinder effective intervention.
Further internal discussions within the Academy’s STEM and Humanities Standing Committees had identified that vulnerability to GBV could be even more pronounced at the basic education level, where relationships between learners and teachers might expose young girls to greater risk compared to university students or professionals, who generally have higher awareness of GBV-related issues.
Dr Thwala stated that the Academy’s current initiative aimed to collaborate with the Department of Basic Education and the Department of Women, Youth, and Persons with Disabilities to strengthen awareness and prevention measures within schools. The goal was to improve knowledge among learners and reduce their vulnerability to GBV. Additionally, the Humanities Standing Committee had undertaken to initiate a formal consensus study to further examine the issue and provide evidence-based recommendations for addressing GBV in educational contexts.
Prof Soodyall then addressed a question concerning international travel and its value to ordinary South Africans. She assured the Committee that both local and international travel had been significantly curtailed, with trips only authorised when deemed absolutely essential. Most international travel expenditure was linked to partnerships with the DSTI, particularly in supporting scholars' participation at events such as the Lindau meetings in Germany, BRICS-related engagements, and activities involving the World Academy of Sciences (TWAS), which included hosting meetings for young scholars.
Prof Soodyall emphasised that management seldom undertook travel and only did so when strictly necessary. She expressed an awareness of the privilege of working with public funds and assured the Committee that these resources were used responsibly. Regarding financial matters, she noted that at the forthcoming Council meeting scheduled for 31 October, the current President intended to lead a discussion on identifying additional sources of funding for the organisation. She concluded by stating that an update on these deliberations would be provided at a future meeting.
Mr Morakeng Chiloane, Manager: Finance, ASSAf, expanded on the discussion regarding international travel, confirming that participation in the Lindau project accounted for most of the organisation’s travel-related activities. It was explained that those who travelled under this programme were emerging scientists rather than Academy employees, and that related costs were classified as programmatic expenditure. The total amount spent on international travel was approximately R672 000, primarily supporting South African scientists who attended the Lindau meetings in Germany to engage with global leaders in science. He reiterated that international travel by staff was minimal.
Mr Chiloane also responded to a question about overdue debts, stating that the organisation adhered to Generally Recognised Accounting Practice. In accordance with this framework, provisions were made for doubtful debts older than 120 days. However, practical steps were also taken to recover outstanding amounts whenever possible.
He further clarified the decision to abolish membership fees for Academy members. It was explained that, in previous years, members had been required to pay such fees, but Council later resolved to discontinue this practice. The decision was based on the recognition that members already contributed significant time and expertise voluntarily, far exceeding the monetary value of any fees. Consequently, unpaid membership fees were written off as non-recoverable.
Lastly, Mr Chiloane addressed the issue of funding diversification. He noted that, according to the latest audited financial statements, the Academy had successfully secured about 26% of its budget from sources outside its baseline allocation. This demonstrated continued progress toward financial sustainability. He emphasised that diversifying income streams remained a standing priority for the organisation and would continue to receive attention in future meetings.
Prof Soodyall concluded the responses by addressing an earlier question about how to encourage scientists to increase their research publications. She explained that most academics were based within universities, where publishing formed part of their institutional responsibilities. When the Academy engaged its members, contributions were typically in the form of studies, consensus reports, and other official publications. Therefore, while members contributed to the Academy’s work through these outputs, it was not a requirement for them to publish under the Academy’s name.
Closing remarks by the Chairperson
The Committee Chairperson expressed appreciation to the representatives of the HSRC and the ASSAf for the attendance, presentations, and comprehensive responses to Members’ questions. However, she also raised concern regarding the nature of some community outreach initiatives. She stated that, in her view, scientific institutions should ensure that outreach efforts remain aligned with science-related objectives rather than focusing solely on humanitarian gestures such as providing goods or services unrelated to research.
The Chairperson suggested that engagements with communities, including the elderly, should incorporate scientific or research elements that bring people closer to science and encourage public participation in scientific activity. She emphasised that the Committee’s role was to oversee science-related work and that all associated entities should reflect this mandate in their programmes. She concluded by thanking the presenters once again for their dedication and urged them to continue promoting science and making it more visible and appealing across the country.
The meeting was adjourned.
