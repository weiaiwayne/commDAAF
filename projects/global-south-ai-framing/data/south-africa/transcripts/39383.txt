Meeting Summary
SA National Road Agency Limited (SANRAL)
In the meeting of the Portfolio Committee on Transport, SANRAL presented its 2024/25 annual performance plan (APP), emphasising its business pillars of road development, safety, stakeholder engagement and economic transformation. It discussed its major projects along key national routes, its funding sources, and goals for creating jobs and improving road conditions.
Committee Members raised concerns about project delays, funding, road safety and irregular expenditure. SANRAL responded by highlighting ongoing efforts to address these issues, including plans to investigate all unethical behaviour and manage road transfers effectively.
The Department of Transport also presented the Merchant Shipping Bill, aimed at modernising South Africa’s maritime laws to align with international standards. The Bill was well received, with the Committee passing a motion of desirability to proceed with it.
Meeting report
SANRAL Annual Performance Plan 2024/25
The annual performance plan (APP) of the South African National Roads Agency Ltd (SANRAL) was presented by Mr Reginald Demana, Chief Executive Officer (CEO), Mr Dumisani Maluleke, Acting Chief Financial Officer (CFO), Ms Lehlohonolo Memeza (Chief Corporate Affairs Officer), and Mr Themba Mhambi (Chairperson).
The presentation emphasised SANRAL’s business pillars, including road development, safety, stakeholder engagement, and economic transformation, as well as its major projects along key national routes such as the N1, N2 and N3. The organisation highlighted its funding sources, which include government grants, toll levies and borrowings, and detailed its three-year project pipeline aimed at improving infrastructure and creating thousands of jobs.
SANRAL also outlined its specific goals for 2024/25, including maintaining road condition standards, increasing participation of black-owned businesses, and creating 14 000 jobs. The budget for the period was discussed, with significant allocations for both operational and capital expenditures. SANRAL also addressed key challenges, such as securing funding for road transfers, managing road-to-rail freight transitions, and addressing project disruptions

Discussion
Dr C Hunsinger (DA) noted that SANRAL had projects split between the northern, eastern, western and southern regions, and asked why certain projects were allocated to the head office instead of specific regions. Regarding road transfer requests and SANRAL’s position that roads must have an R1 or R2 classification to be considered, he asked for clarity on the exclusion of other roads. He suggested that the classification system should be interrogated, as it may be outdated. He asked why the goal for bridge condition exposure was 90%, compared to 95% for other projects in SANRAL’s economic transformation and job creation plan. He asked for an explanation of what nanotechnology was in SANRAL’s context.
SANRAL had outlined 12 specific areas to improve pedestrian safety, but Dr Hunsinger suggested that they should cast a wider net because the scale of pedestrian danger was all roads. He criticised their goal of investigating 75% of unethical cases, suggesting that the aim should be to address all unethical behaviour. In the same vein, he criticised the goal of reducing cases of irregular expenditure by 50%. Lastly, he asked whether they had budgeted for reduced income in the coming year.
Mr M Gana (Rise Mzansi) raised concerns about the time it takes to build roads. He made an example of the N2 towards Richards Bay, and asked if SANRAL had strategies to reduce these delays because they had a cost. There could also be extortion related to contracting, so it was important to do these projects as quickly as possible. He raised concerns about the N1, saying that the N1 north’s heavy traffic and road works could increase accidents. Did they have plans in place to reduce these accidents, as the intention of the road expansion was to reduce accidents?
Mr T Mabhena (DA) wanted to speak about SANRAL’s audit report from the Auditor-General (AG). However, the Chairperson said a separate meeting would discuss the annual report. Mr Mabhena criticised the entity's inclusion of a commitment to answer all questions posed by Parliament, since he felt that SANRAL must answer them regardless. During Minister Mbalula’s tenure as Minister of Transport, had there been progress on those transport projects.
Mr N Maduna (ANC) commended the board’s leadership structure and how it promoted accountability by having an audit executive. He agreed with Dr Hunsinger that all unethical behaviour - or at least more than 75% -- should be investigated. He also said reducing irregular spending by 75% instead of 50% would be more appropriate. He acknowledged that it was difficult to eradicate irregular spending, as some projects do go over budget for reasons that did not involve negligence. He asked what types of jobs were being created at SANRAL, and whether they were full-time employment or internships. He asked about the flagship programmes and what their direct economic benefits were. He also noted that Mr Maluleke was acting CFO, and asked if the entity could comment on when a full-time appointment would be made.
Mr T Khubeka (MK) started by acknowledging the SANRAL team’s efforts, especially in the difficult conditions they work under. Regarding the number of small, medium and micro enterprises (SMMEs) working for SANRAL, he asked if the Minister or Deputy Minister could look into the working conditions of workers in Johannesburg, and why they did not receive benefits at the same level as their counterparts in Cape Town. He noted that SANRAL had contracted digital marketing companies owned by South Africans but allegedly operated by foreign nationals. He asked if they could appoint a South African-based and operated company to run such a major campaign.
Mr P van Staden (FF+) asked for details about the goal of reducing irregular expenditure by 50%. He asked SANRAL to provide a more comprehensive report on the state of the national roads. What were the timeframes for the completion of SANRAL’s projects? He questioned whether SANRAL had the capacity to take on roads that had been transferred to their jurisdiction by municipalities and what the long-term plan was to build their capacity. He asked for an update on the Vala Zonke pothole programme. He also wanted to know about their plans regarding extortion groups, and their disruption of SANRAL operations.
Ms N Nkopane (ANC) asked whether the model of the toll roads, non-toll roads, and concessions was working, and what advantages and disadvantages it had. She asked what the impact of heavy-duty trucks was on the status of roads, and whether weigh-bridges functioned. She also asked for an update on tolled roads specifically. She questioned why municipal and other local roads were transferred to SANRAL, and whether municipalities would be able to build their own road maintenance capacity if it was a limiting factor.
A Member commented that SANRAL must improve its performance from 75% to 100%. He asked what plans SANRAL had in place to make industrial workers in the sector feel job security, and not perceive artificial intelligence as a threat. What strategies did it have to eradicate corruption in the tender process and fruitless and irregular expenditure?
The Chairperson commended SANRAL and the office of the Minister for their continuous reporting to the Committee. He also commended their intention to roll out the contractor development programme. He said that the Committee would be monitoring the programme, as it would open up the industry to new players. Regarding the industrialist programme, he asked how the Minister planned to advertise contracts and ensure that the positions were filled and how they were managing transformation in the sector.
Responses
Minister of Transport
Ms Barbara Creecy, Minister of Transport, thanked the Committee for its questions and mentioned they were still amending the APP to align with the Department's new mandate.
She said that irregular expenditure was not an optional investigation and that there was necessary consequence management -- the issue was evaluating allegations of irregular spending and deciding whether the evidence available necessitated an investigation.
She said that SANRAL's leadership structure needed to include the stakeholders too. SANRAL should not bear the load of fixing road safety alone, and the Department of Transport was also involved.
She emphasised that the behaviour of drivers and pedestrians needed to change for better road safety.
The Minister stressed the importance of reorientating the system of road management from filling potholes to regular, preventative road maintenance, since it was much cheaper and more effective that way. However, the capacity of municipalities and other stakeholders would still need to be tested for this change to happen.
Regarding the transfer of roads to SANRAL, a major factor was the capacity of municipalities to maintain roads. The long-term goal was for fewer roads to be transferred to SANRAL, and for provinces and municipalities to have the capacity to manage roads.
SANRAL
Mr Mhambi, SANRAL Chairperson, said he had noted the questions and concerns the Committee had for SANRAL, and they would be sure to revise their targets accordingly. He reassured the Committee that they had provided adequate funding for the chief audit investigator and that they were independent and empowered as a matter of organisational accountability. They would investigate 100% of unethical cases. He noted that the new provincial SANRAL offices would focus on the organisation's core business, which was the road network maintenance.
Mr Demana, SANRAL CEO, referred to the procurement of tenders and said that SANRAL would have to align its processes with the new Public Procurement Act. Therefore, some changes would be implemented after that had been reviewed and they had consulted with stakeholders.
Regarding tenders that had been awarded without bids and must be cancelled, Mr Demana said that tenders were abandoned only after all avenues had been exhausted.
Regarding the speed of road construction, SANRAL was working at nearly full capacity, so it was difficult to speed it up, but they did recognise the need for it. The lack of borrowing capacity for SANRAL meant that much of their revenue went to basic road maintenance instead of full maintenance activities such as resurfacing. As a result, many roads had deteriorated and had to be renovated through the CAPEX scheme.
He responded that nanotechnology referred to the chemical makeup of the resins used in the resealing of roads.
Ms Memeza, Chief Corporate Affairs Officer, said that SANRAL had developed a framework for the Black Investment Scheme, and was still in consultation with financial institutions for funding. They had sent out requests for information to identify various materials that would be the most appropriate for the programme. They intend to start implementing it in the fourth quarter of 2024.
The Chair called for a close to the questions, and then handed over to the Minister for a parting word.
The Minister requested permission to leave the meeting, as the agenda moved to the Merchant Shipping Bill, which was to be handled by the Deputy Minister,
Mr Mkhuleko Hlengwa
.
Merchant Shipping
Bill
[B22-2023]
The Department of Transport briefed the Committee on the Merchant Shipping Bill, which aims to modernise South Africa’s maritime laws. It seeks to replace the Merchant Shipping Act of 1951, which has been amended more than 32 times. Its purpose is to align South Africa’s maritime legal framework with international standards, particularly those of the International Maritime Organisation (IMO), ensuring the country’s compliance with international maritime agreements.
The Bill's objectives include promoting domestic ship ownership, supporting coastal shipping, improving port governance, and enhancing safety at sea. It also introduces provisions for regulating marine traffic, registering and licensing ships, applying labour laws to seafarers, and addressing crimes and casualties at sea. Moreover, the Bill incorporates the International Ship and Port Facility Security (ISPS) code to strengthen security in ports and ships, a measure that was absent in the previous act.
The Bill was developed following consultations with various stakeholders, including government departments, and received Cabinet approval in 2022 before being introduced to the National Assembly in May 2023. The Portfolio Committee had been asked to approve the Bill for further processing.

Discussion
The Chairperson noted that this Bill had been brought before the Committee before, and so it was familiar to the Committee.
Dr Hunsinger reiterated that this had been brought up in the 6th Parliament, but had to be revived because it had lapsed. He said that the Committee accepted the Bill in its current form, and put forward a motion of desirability.
Mr Maduna asked what the timeframe for the IMO audit was. He wanted to know so that they would not be caught off guard in the future.
Mr Khubeka said that the motion for a bill like this must be considered urgently, because many issues in South Africa’s maritime space, such as ports, need improvement.
Ms Nkopane was impressed that the Committee was overhauling this old act. She wanted to see how this new Bill would be in line with government priorities in the maritime sector, and seconded the motion of desirability.
Response
Mr Mthunzi Madiya, Deputy Director-General: Maritime, DoT, said the IMO conducted a Department evaluation last year. They wanted to establish if the port's authority was compliant with the needs of the country, whether policies and legislation were up to date, the capacity of workers, and the coordination of the different departments in the maritime sector. The IMO had developed a preliminary report from this audit, but the DoT had not yet received the outcomes.
In response to Mr Maduna, he said they had received a few areas of concern - especially concerning coordination, and search and rescue, for example. They had raised the issue of the government not having a link that coordinates between departments for search and rescue cases. They had begun to work on the recommendations they had received in the interim, but they were still waiting on the full report.
Deputy Minister Hlengwa added that the audits were infrequent and had no set intervals, so compliance should be checked regularly. When the report is released, the Department would report back to the Portfolio Committee. He said that search and rescue operations were major operations that included inland areas with dams. Lastly, he asked if Parliament could generally be more agile about acting on the agreements into which they had entered.
The Chairperson affirmed that the Bill’s motion of desirability had been passed, and that they would publish the Bill for submissions to be considered from 17 September to 25 October.
Dr Hunsinger added that it would be helpful for the Committee to get a pipeline of bills and amendments that they wanted to be considered in this term.
The Chairperson agreed, and said the Committee's support team would get on that.
Adoption of minutes
Committee minutes were proposed for adoption by Mr Mabena, seconded by Mr Maduna, and were adopted.
The Chairperson outlined the upcoming meetings for the Committee.
The meeting was adjourned.
