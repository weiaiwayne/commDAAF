SCOPA Oversight Enquiry
The Standing Committee on Public Accounts (SCOPA) continued its enquiry into the Road Accident Fund (RAF). PwC appeared to give account of its role in the development of the new Integrated Claims Management System (ICMS) at RAF. Ms Hannelie Gilmour and Ms Thaaniya Isaacs, PwC Consulting Partners, gave testimony assessing the progress, challenges, and financial implications of implementing the Integrated Claims Management System (ICMS). PwC outlined its mandate, explaining that it had been responsible for developing, configuring and supporting the core ICMS components – Guidewire, Updraft, and the Intelligent Digital Platform –while the RAF remained accountable for user acceptance testing, data quality, operational changes, and overall project governance. PwC emphasised that it had not advised the RAF on stop-gap operational measures to bridge gaps in functionality.
RAF Executive and Senior Management answered questions about the ICMS implementation:
Ms Mpho Manyasha, suspended RAF Head of CEO Office
Ms Bernice Potgieter, RAF Chief Financial Officer
Mr Rodney Mkhabela, RAF Acting Strategy and Transformation Officer
Mr Gcina Kubheka, RAF Internal Control Specialist
Ms Boitumelo Mabusela, former RAF Acting CFO
The Committee sought clarity on PwC’s role, the functionality of the system, the causes of delays, and the rising project costs. Committee members also scrutinised the RAF’s capacity, leadership stability, data integrity, and continued reliance on manual processes despite significant investment in automation.
The Committee questioned the persistent delays, especially the year-long shift in Release 1 timelines and the late start of Release 2. PwC attributed the setbacks to misalignment on the definition of the minimum sensible product, disputes over handling funeral claims, challenges involving the RAF 1 Form, and the limited availability of RAF personnel. Members expressed concern that the RAF’s internal instability, characterised by frequent changes in project sponsors and senior managers, continued to undermine progress. They stated that the RAF appeared disorganised, poorly coordinated, and overly dependent on contractors to compensate for weak internal systems and capacity.
A major point of contention was the system’s performance. Although over 101 000 claims had been processed through the ICMS by October 2025, throughput remained significantly lower than expected. PwC and the RAF attributed this to poor adoption, resistance to stricter controls, resource shortages, and unresolved SAP integration issues that affected payment processing. Members pressed the RAF to explain why thousands of processed claims still awaited approval or payment. They were also troubled that the RAF continued operating two parallel systems, its legacy platform and the new ICMS, raising questions about data integrity, duplication, and staff workload.
The Committee interrogated the escalating project costs. PwC explained that the contract was structured as an all-inclusive five-year agreement valued at R841.9 million, with variations permitted only for specific conditions such as exchange-rate fluctuations on Guidewire licences or additional deliverables formally requested by the RAF. Adjustments had increased the contract value to R881 million. Members pressed for assurance that no further increases would occur and questioned if the RAF had sufficiently safeguarded itself against unnecessary expenditure.
The Committee also sought clearer accountability for system defects, data migration challenges and the slow pace of enhancements. RAF representatives insisted that they had strengthened oversight mechanisms, but Members remained unconvinced, noting that many of the project’s sticking points – user readiness, staffing constraints, misaligned priorities and delayed decision-making – were internal RAF failures rather than technical obstacles.
Throughout the meeting, Members stressed that the ICMS was not simply a technological upgrade but a critical intervention meant to fix the RAF’s longstanding operational dysfunction. They insisted that unless the RAF improved project leadership, stabilised its internal structures and drove user adoption with greater discipline, the system would not deliver the efficiencies and cost savings it was designed for.

PwC presentation on Integrated Claims Management System at RAF
Ms Gilmour and Isaacs briefed the Committee on the PwC’s role in developing and implementing the Integrated Claims Management System (ICMS) for the RAF. The firm explained that the ICMS was introduced to modernise the RAF’s fragmented, manual and paper-heavy claims environment, which struggled with long processing times, rising legal costs, limited reporting capabilities and exposure to fraud. PwC’s mandate focused on business requirements, configuration of the Guidewire, Updraft and Intelligent Digital Platform (IDP) systems, data migration, integrations, training and programme management. The RAF retained responsibility for management decisions, user acceptance testing, data quality, staffing and operational readiness.
The ICMS is built around three integrated components: Guidewire as the core claims engine, Updraft as the legal and liability assessment tool and the IDP as the analytics platform. The system supports the entire claims lifecycle from onboarding and pre-assessment to merits evaluation, medical or supplier verification, quantum calculations, offer negotiation and preparation of a payment request for SAP.
PwC clarified that it did not advise the RAF on operational stop-gap or transitional measures. Development followed an agile, iterative approach. Release 1 delivered the minimum sensible product, which allowed RAF to start processing and paying claims through the digital system. Release 2 focuses on enhancements such as bank verifications, Home Affairs integrations, automated mortality checks and expanded client and vendor portals. Release 2 consists of four iterations, but the final one is paused due to changes in RAF project leadership.
The project has faced several delays. Release 1 was pushed back by a year due to decisions on environments, inconsistent understanding of agile processes, stakeholder availability, disputes over funeral claims and questions about the RAF 1 Form. Release 2 started five months late because the RAF needed more time to embed Release 1 and stabilise operations. Adoption challenges persist as staff adjust to stricter controls and new workflows while still managing a large legacy backlog. Throughput remains low due to resource shortages, SAP payment integration issues and cumbersome supplier-claim processes.
Financially, the ICMS contract is a five-year, all-inclusive agreement valued at R841.9 million, subject to defined variations. Adjustments have been made for exchange-rate impacts on Guidewire licences and for infrastructure provisioning. To date, around R589.9 million has been invoiced. The project runs until October 2027 and is currently in Release 2, iteration 3.

Morar, on 13 April, the next day, withdrew this. Somewhere thereabouts, there is another RFQ issued, which then occasions PwC's involvement, where they then submit their report to you. The board meeting was to be on 28 April. It also happened to be the date on which the PwC submitted their report. The Audit and Risk Committee (ARC) meets to the best of my recollection of PwC's presentation on 13 April. The board then meets on the 28th, takes a decision, but on the 28th, there is no alternative advice available to the board because we know that Morar have withdrawn theirs. PwC have not yet submitted because it is the same day they are submitting their response to the RFQ. Hon Maduna is raising an issue of a report that belongs to internal audit that in a conversation between yourself and Ms Potgieter, for some reason, they are asked to already withdraw the technical advice they provided. I am nearly certain that when the ARC meets the next day or a couple of days later, it does not have [the] benefit of any technical advice. And the document on which the CAE would have relied for that conversation has been withdrawn in the sense that after that conversation with the two of you, they either agreed to or were asked to volunteer to withdraw. If we take all of these versions to be true, that is what we sit with at the moment and we are going to take it further. I would like to ask the colleagues to comment on this timeline and this sequence of events because it gives rise to a number of governance questions.
Ms Mabusela: How I wish I could have accounted for this when my memory was still strong on the matter. Let me clarify something. Firstly, I had not seen the RFQ because it would have been from the CAE. I am recalling that the first time I met this particular document, because this one I know, was when I was called in the office of Mr Letsoalo, indicating that there is this Morar report from the CAE. And then he also indicated that the work that Morar was supposed to do is obviously not on the change in accounting policy, which I would not leave it to him because I also had to apply my mind, knowing that change in accounting policy was done by the management. And when I read the scope, it indicates there that we are asking them to assist in developing and applying the policy for the social benefit disclosure because now we have selected the standard. Mr Maduna, I do not know if you have seen that the RFQ and this (the other document) do not read the same. The RFQ does not say social benefit disclosure, it is just saying applying accounting policies that are fit for purpose. I then got a hold of the CAE. I do not think Ms Potigeter was in the meeting. If she was in the meeting, chances are she was not the one participating. We discussed this without Morar and I was trying to seek clarity on understanding why Morar has come up with a technical opinion on the changing of the accounting policy. I am not the one who organised a meeting with Morar. Hon Maduna is correct. This is not my project. It is the project of the CAE. The CAE did not bring to my attention that ‘Look CFO, I have requested Morar to look at this.’ He definitely agrees that we meet Morar. My recollection is that myself and the CAE met and addressed Morar together. There is a part that makes me so uncomfortable. Had he given me an indication that we did ask Morar for a technical opinion, I could have said to him that ‘Come let us go to the CEO because the CEO is perplexed by this particular report.’
Chairperson: Do not worry about the recollection. We are going to assist you. When did you become CFO, Ms Mabusela?
Ms Mabusela: 3rd of June, 2020.
Chairperson: Replacing Mr Songelwa?
Ms Mabusela: Yes.
Chairperson: Who initiated the process of considering a change in accounting policy?
Ms Mabusela: Mr Letsoalo – but not saying we are changing the accounting policy – indicating his concern around the RAF being a social benefit fund or an insurance and requesting me to go and check whether the standards that are applicable are relevant to the RAF. I cannot say he is the one who triggered the change in accounting policy.
Chairperson: Mr Modiba, you were on the Exco at the time, correct?
Mr Modiba: I think I joined the Exco as a full-time invitee around August 2020.
Chairperson: When you joined the Exco is this conversation live?
Mr Modiba: It is live.
Chairperson: Who drives it?
Mr Modiba: My recollection is that Ms Mabusela would have been given a task to research on the best standard for reporting purposes on RAF’s liabilities.
Chairperson: Does that assist you, Ms Mabusela, at all in terms of the recollection?
Ms Mabusela: It is correct.
Chairperson: I am not saying it began with Mr Modiba's recollection, but when he joins the Exco somewhere thereabouts, at the very least on his first Exco meeting after, then this becomes something that is discussed. Are you able to recall at which point the CAE is given the task of sourcing some kind of technical advice?
Ms Mabusela: No, because there has not been a request of that nature at Exco. If the request happened, it would not have been in Exco because I was present in all of those Exco meetings.
Chairperson: I think we know that the sequence of events begins on 24 February. It takes us to 8 April when Morar submit their technical opinion. On 12 April, they withdraw that technical opinion. Soon thereafter there is an ARC. I think if you compare the PwC RFQ and the Morar process, I do not suppose that the Morar report would have served before the ARC because it had now been withdrawn.
Ms Mabusela: Correct.
Chairperson: The discussion appears to have already been going on or about August of the previous year in the Exco. Ms Mabusela could not recall at which point the CAE would have become involved. What we are left with is that the Audit Planning Memorandum, Review of Annual Financial Statements for the Year ended 31 March 2021, happens between the closing of the RFQ and the dispatch of the report by Morar on 8 April because this is dated 31 March 2021. The only concern I have is that, and this is something we will discuss with the Board, you would expect an organisation that properly considers decisions, that the CAE and the ARC have a process that assists the ARC to make the best recommendation to the Board. The Chair of the ARC, I would assume, becomes the one who owns the discussion in the Board meeting about the change in accounting policy. Part of what needs to have occurred at least had the Morar report not been withdrawn, is that their conclusion that further legal advice was needed. That would also have gone into the pack of the Board, assuming it was missed in the ARC.
Mr Maduna: What is very important is that the technical was issued on 8 April. They withdraw it on 16 April. You are correct that the audit committee never received this opinion. That is where the problem was. The bigger question is how did the CEO get a hold of this technical opinion if it had not yet been tabled in the committee? He was not supposed to have. What I am establishing now is that the project by Morar and the project by PwC are from two different departments.
Chairperson: Ms Mabusela, so, PwC get commissioned by whom for the work that they did? Who owns that RFQ that results in PwC?
Ms Mabusela: I would prefer to get the RFQ and rely on the document.
Chairperson: Let me ask the question differently. Were you the executive responsible for the PwC report? It happens while you are still there.
Ms Mabusela: I do not think so.
Chairperson: When did you leave the RAF?
Ms Mabusela: 07 May.
Chairperson: 2021?
Ms Mabusela: 2021.
Chairperson: No, then the RFQ happens while you are still CFO.
Ms Mabusela: I would assume so. In your timeline, you mentioned the month of August.
Chairperson: I might have misspoken. Ms Potgieter, how is your recollection of this whole thing?
Ms Potgieter: For the RFQ that results in the appointment of PwC, we did use internal audit’s panel, but it was within the office of the CFO.
Ms Mabusela: It rings a bell, Chairperson.
Mr Maduna: That is what I am saying. There is a difference between the advisor when it's from internal audit and the way in which PwC prepared their report. That is how I established that the two reports were commissioned from two different departments. Now, I think it further complicates it. We know need to have the CAE, because we need to establish if the Morar technical opinion get to the ARC. If it did not, it then absorbed the ARC because it did not have the advice from internal audit. It means then they would have changed an accounting policy which had a material impact in their financial statement without an internal audit opinion.
Chairperson: I agree. I do not know if there is any value to carrying on with this any longer. I think there is certain additional information that we will need from other people. Ms Hlami Mathye, when she was here she told us that the MediaMix360 process came about because the CEO had felt that the Government Communication and Information System (GCIS) was not the right agency to use for media placement. They then went and tested the market and GCIS came tops. They were nonetheless instructed to go out on tender. The whole process was issued. She then says they present to the bid adjudication committee (BAC). You are in the BAC, Ms Potgieter. There had been the option to use GCIS and it was the most cost competitive process. Was there discussion around whether this is the cheapest option? I asked her (Ms Mathye) a specific question. I said, ‘When the bids came, was GCIS more expensive than some?’ And she said, ‘They were still the most competitive.’ We have got the minutes of the other meetings. Something seems to have occurred off the books. I need to understand if your recollection of that discussion involves whether this should proceed at all or not. From then on, it is recommended and then the expenditure begins. And the expenditure has been at a terrific speed.
Ms Potgieter: I do remember us discussing GCIS. I must be honest, I cannot recall the cost-benefit analysis. At some point, there is a recommendation made by the BAC. But the comments that came back from the CEO was not about them not being cost-effective. I believe he said something about them not having the capacity and capability of what is required. If I can get access to the minutes and the submissions, I will be able to submit them to the Committee. But I do not want to hang myself on something that I am not 100% sure on.
Chairperson: That is fine. We will have a look at the documents you have.
Mr Maduna: Two final questions. One, I want to check those claims that you usually do not capture because they are missing documents. Do you include some of them in your provisions? Mr Modiba, I see in the statement by the Minister that you have made a profit. I see that she speaks of economic inflow from investments. Did you have any investments?
Mr Modiba: No, we did not. When I spoke about it the other time when I was here, I was saying that we have a call account that we put monies in. We did not actively get into the investment market.
Chairperson: When we write our report, you will be sent a draft so that you may comment on the parts that mention you. If you have any recommendations of things we should consider in our report that will make the RAF work efficiently, we welcome those. The last thing I want to say is that I had a look at the RAF’s HR report immediately after Covid and this year. In this report, mental health is by far the most significant issue. The nearest to that is relationship issues, and then the workplace issues, personal development, et cetera. As a person who by training is a communications professional, it is always my preference that an organisation stays off the headlines. I really want to say I wish for three things for the RAF: One, for it to be an institution that facilitates the claims process in a painless way if you can achieve it at the lowest possible cost. The second thing that I really wish for is that mental health issue really disappears into one of the smaller issues, which is an issue of organisational environment, organisational culture, and so on. And I think one of the ways in which that will happen is if you also have permanent leadership. There have been too many actors for too long at the RAF. That on its own can create instability. And the final point is that I really am also looking for, and I am sure the Members are the same, where we only see you once a year when you are presenting your AFS. I know there is a lot of turmoil in the institution. There has been for quite some time but we will get over this and the RAF will be fine. We can adjourn.
The meeting was adjourned.
