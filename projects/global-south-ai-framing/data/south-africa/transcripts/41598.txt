Meeting Summary
Video
In this virtual meeting, the Portfolio Committee on Communications and Digital Technologies and the Portfolio Committee on Basic Education were briefed by the Department of Communications and Digital Technologies (DCDT) and the Department of Basic Education (DBE) on the status and progress of school connectivity.
The DBE and DCDT reported on school connectivity and digital transformation in the education sector. Improved collaboration was reported, but stressed that limited funding, device obsolescence, and rural infrastructure gaps remained major challenges. The outdated 2004 White Paper on e-Education was being revised, and a new digital education strategy had been developed, pending political endorsement by the Council of Education Ministers and eventual submission to Parliament for oversight. The cost of full information communication technology (ICT) rollout was estimated at R30.6 billion, far beyond National Treasury’s (NT) capacity, leaving provinces to use their equitable share to fund ICT initiatives.
Between 2022 and 2024, over 545 000 learner devices, 30 800 teacher devices, and 10 500 classrooms were equipped with ICT resources, while more than half a million learners and nearly 60 000 teachers gained connectivity. However, devices often became obsolete shortly after delivery. Broadband rollout was supported by the Telkom/Openserve project, targeting 1 224 schools, and the SA Connect Phase 1 project, which connected 597 schools. In rural areas, provinces relied on offline solutions such as preloaded tablets and laptops. Special schools also benefited from ICT and assistive devices, though rollout delays persisted with Cell C.
Digital platforms such as DBE TV, Matric Live, and Tswelopele provided learners with free educational content, while 69% of high-enrolment subject textbooks were digitised. Teacher training programmes were expanded through partnerships with universities, the private sector, and online certification providers. Public-private partnerships with mobile network operators and companies like Anglo American and African Rainbow Minerals contributed further resources, connectivity and training. Despite financial and operational constraints, the DBE and DCDT affirmed their commitment to ensuring that schools are connected as a central step towards universal digital access and bridging the digital divide, preparing learners for future opportunities in an increasingly digital world.
Major concerns about school connectivity, particularly in rural and special needs schools that remained neglected due to poor infrastructure, unreliable data, and limited funding, were voiced. Members questioned whether the targets truly reflected needs, stressing the importance of transparent baselines, accountability for service providers, and stronger monitoring. While collaboration between the DBE and DCDT was welcomed, gaps remained in teacher training, infrastructure security, and equitable rollout across provinces. Operation Phakisa’s delays, the outdated White Paper on e-Education, and unmet commitments were criticised. Members called for urgent timelines, sustainable funding models, and stronger alignment across departments to prevent rural learners from being left behind in the digital transition.
Members criticised the DBE and DCDT for over-reporting successes while schools were forced to fund their own internet or deal with obsolete technology. Issues included slow SA Connect rollout, lack of security for digital devices, unaddressed universal service obligations, and minimal progress in special needs schools. Calls were made for transparent monitoring, enforcement of service provider obligations, realistic action plans, better coordination among departments, and urgent attention to equity, sustainability, and capacity-building to ensure that learners received meaningful digital access and educational support.

The DBE and the DCDT briefed the JC regarding the progress made on the schools' connectivity on 20 February 2025. The JC raised numerous questions regarding the state of digital transformation in the basic education sector. The questions covered the following areas: policy environment, school connectivity, ICT implementation plan, budget, ICT devices, digital platforms, and partnerships with the private sector.
Policy environment
Some of the policy gaps included the emerging technologies trends in the White Paper on e-Education, no broadband connectivity in rural areas, and expensive ICT devices and solutions. Some of the mitigating strategies included reviewing and updating the White Paper, prioritising satellite connectivity policies and establishing a contract with education-specific devices.
White Paper
The DBE would review and update the White Paper on e-Education that was developed in 2004. There were gaps with the current policy because the ICT space had evolved drastically since 2004. The envisaged policy document would be more flexible and resilient to the ever-changing digital environment. Further, the DBE has already developed a digital education strategy that was aimed at reimagining the basic education sector. The consultative workshops had been held with various provincial education departments as well as national departments. The strategy would provide the basic education sector with a roadmap on how the digital transformation in the basic education sector would be achieved.
Digital education strategy
The digital education strategy was presented to the Heads of Education Departments Committee on 2 September 2025. The next phase involved seeking political endorsement from the Council of Education Ministers (CEM). Following endorsement by the CEM, the strategy would be presented to the Portfolio Committee on Basic Education in Parliament when requested. This stage introduces legislative oversight and democratic accountability. The Portfolio Committee would have an opportunity to scrutinise the strategy's contents.

Operation Phakisa
There was no feasibility study conducted to ensure the rollout of ICT in schools. The government adopted the big fast results problem-solving methodology in 2015, which was originally developed and implemented by the government of Malaysia. The big, fast, results-driven approach was adapted to the South African context. To highlight the urgency of delivery, the approach was renamed to Operation Phakisa (
Phakisa
meaning
Hurry Up
in Sesotho). Furthermore, the government, together with relevant stakeholders, conducted Operation Phakisa Lab in 2015, which led to a detailed, costed ICT implementation plan for the basic education sector. However, the plan was not funded due to other competing priorities by the government.
ICT implementation plan
The DBE developed a comprehensive plan and indicated that it would adopt a phased-in approach for the accelerated deployment of digital learning and teaching support material through ICTs at all levels of the basic education sector. The implementation of the plan was disrupted in 2020 due to COVID19, leading to a revised approach tailored to the pandemic’s challenges, during and after.
Estimated budget
The estimated cost for the implementation of the State of the Nation Address (SONA) commitment was R30.6 billion. However, the required amount by the basic education sector was more than what the NT could afford. The provincial education departments were using their equitable share to provide learners, educators, administrators and schools with ICT resources and connectivity. The allocated budgets were not adequate to provide all learners and teachers with ICT resources, thus widening the digital divide. As such, the rollout was not in tandem with the implementation plan.
Over the past three financial years, 2022/23, 2023/24, and 2024/25, the provincial education departments have made significant financial investments in the rollout of ICT resources, connectivity, and teacher training. These allocations were not only a reflection of growing digital demands in education but also an indication of efforts to bridge the digital divide and ensure equitable access to quality education for all learners.
ICT devices
A total of 545 938 learner ICT devices were procured for learning during the 2022 to 2024 financial years. The textbooks and digital resources were loaded on ICT device
s
that were provided to teachers and learners. A total of 30 818 teacher ICT devices were procured for teaching during the 2022 to 2024 financial years. A total of 10 588 classrooms were equipped with ICT resources for teaching and learning. A total of 535 633 learners received connectivity for learning. Further, 59 991 teachers received connectivity for content delivery purposes.
Digital platforms
The basic education sector, in collaboration with various partners, has developed online digital platforms. Various resources have been uploaded on these platforms, such as DBE TV, Matric Live and Tswelopele. The DBE and the provincial education departments had established various e-learning platforms. Further, MTN, Vodacom, and Telkom have implemented their own e-learning platforms, which were zero-rated for users on their network. The provincial education department’s e-learning portals and platforms provided learners with free access to educational content resources such as video lessons, apps, e-books, and interactive revision material.
The DBE has developed digital educational resources that are distributed to schools using various channels, such as online and offline platforms. A total of 152 titles (69%) of state-owned textbooks for high-enrolment subjects had been developed and digitised into PDF & e-Pub file formats.
Project management
Provincial governments have established structured project management systems for ICT initiatives in education. Each province had designated dedicated officials to oversee the planning, procurement, and execution of ICT projects. These teams were responsible for managing all aspects of implementation, ensuring alignment with national education objectives. Provinces were required to submit regular progress reports to the DBE every quarter, maintaining accountability and enabling national monitoring of ICT rollout across schools. The system demonstrated a coordinated approach where provincial implementation teams handled operational delivery, while maintaining reporting lines to the national department. This framework ensured both provincial autonomy in execution and centralised oversight through mandatory quarterly progress updates, creating a balance between decentralised implementation and national quality control.
Connectivity-rural villages
In rural provinces where internet connectivity remained a challenge, innovative offline digital solutions were being implemented to ensure equitable access to education. For example, provinces were deploying tablets loaded with educational applications and laptops preloaded with curriculum-aligned content, sometimes through the C3 server system.
Status of school connectivity-teaching and learning
South African schools remained unconnected to broadband due to a mix of infrastructure gaps, financial constraints and operational challenges. Many rural and township schools lacked fibre or mobile network coverage, while high installation and maintenance costs hindered expansion. Further, ongoing expenses like data costs and a lack of ICT equipment in many of the rural schools. Vandalism and theft further disrupt connectivity, making it difficult to sustain reliable internet access. Without addressing these systemic issues, achieving universal school broadband remained a significant challenge.
Telkom/Openserve broadband project
As part of the collaboration between DBE, DCDT and Openserve, a total of 1 224 schools were earmarked to be provided with broadband connectivity. These schools were selected because they were on the Openserve footprint. The selected schools would receive free internet services for a period of five years.
Connectivity to public schools: South Africa Connect phase one
A total of 597 schools had access to this broadband connectivity as part of phase one of the South Africa (SA) Connect policy. DCDT requested that DBE take over the connectivity and services from January 2023. In the absence of that takeover by DBE, DCDT was still paying for the connectivity (ten Mbps) and services (uncapped internet) by Broadband Infraco (BBI) and the State Information Technology Agency (SITA). DCDT continued to support this programme, subject to budget allocations from the NT. Currently, the DCDT plans to support these sites during the medium-term expenditure framework. The 209 non-operational sites were currently being reconnected by BBI and SITA. During 2014 to 2019, a total of 115 944 learner devices were provided to public schools. The ICT equipment provided to these schools as part of the obligations was obsolete.
Special schools
Since 2014, as part of ICASA’s obligations on mobile network operators, 406 out of 461 (88%) public special schools received ICT equipment, connectivity and assistive devices appropriate for the needs of each school. A total of 12 180 devices were distributed under this initiative. However, Cell C was lagging, having provided solutions to only 36 public special schools. ICASA was in the process of dealing with the non-compliance by Cell C. Cell C had submitted a full rollout plan to ICASA for the remaining 51 special needs schools. As part of the obligations, the special schools were provided with 30 computers/laptops, a charging trolley, 3G/LTE connectivity, assistive devices and educational software loaded on the server that were relevant to the needs of each school. The solution was not individualised but was shared by all learners within the school.
Teacher training
The DBE, in collaboration with various stakeholders, were offering various teacher training programs to support digital transformation in education, including government initiatives, university courses, private-sector partnerships, and free online certifications.
Public-private partnerships
The mobile network operators, Vodacom, MTN, Telkom and Cell C, were providing schools with ICT resources, connectivity and digital content as part of the corporate social investment. The schools were provided with a computer laboratory, connectivity and digital content loaded on a server that could be accessed online and offline. Furthermore, teachers were provided with training on how to infuse ICT into teaching and learning. Anglo American provided schools with ICT resources and connectivity. African Rainbow Minerals provided 13 schools with ICT resources as part of the rural upliftment trust project.

If there was a problem, this technology would come in for operators to consider beyond the usual terrestrial connectivity. The DBE and the DCDT would continue to collaborate. Cooperation progress has been made, and weekly meetings are being held with the industry.
Mr Mweli would try to respond to all of the comments and questions. If not, written responses would be provided within seven days. Ms Magagula questioned if the national and provincial government roles were lineated, with a second question on standards. The roles between a national and provincial department are delineated. Section 4 of the Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, 108 of 1996 addresses the concurrent functions of government, including those that were specific to provinces. The role of a national department was to develop policy, support and monitoring. The delivery of devices and connectivity was, by law, a responsibility located within provinces. The national department's role was to support and monitor. The support meant supporting provincial departments in delivering their constitutional and legislative mandate. The DBE was doing that in many ways, such as Operation Phakisa by developing polices, and reviewing the White Paper. This was part of the concurrent function, a responsibility of a national department.
The responsibility was not delegated downwards. It was the responsibility of the DBE to carry out. The provinces were doing their best to meet the required standards based on the funding that was available. The provinces were being monitored. All 75 districts were visited each year. Over 700 schools in the country were visited. He shared some aspects that were noticed. There was a team that specialised in these areas, but from an administrative point, he would go out, sit down with the district, receive presentations and then interrogate them. He looked at the plans, areas of improvement and the areas that required attention. So, when he met with the area during the next year, there was an expectation that improvements would be found. The copies of the engagement with the 75 district had been submitted, including ICT matters. The DBE’s position can be supported by evidence.
Mr Mweli responded to Mr Nchabaleng’s point of view in that the DBE would not add information in the presentation that was a misrepresentation or did not exist. The DBE would be in trouble if that were done. He made it abundantly clear that the DBE was not suggesting that every learner or teacher had a device, or that every school in the country was connected. It was not true that 24 000 schools had been connected in the country. However, what was provided in the presentation did exist. He saw this during his annual visits to over 700 schools and 75 districts.
Interviews were held with Grade 12 learners who indicated that the tablets had made a huge difference as the material was available. The content was locally developed. The South African curriculum, developed in the country, was added to the devices. The textbooks were added as well as supplementary materials, necessary for teaching and learning. These interviews may be provided to the JC. Provinces were encouraged to ensure that at least Grade 12 learners had exposure to using these devices and connectivity. This would ensure that these learners had some practice and familiarised themselves with the utilisation of devices before enrolling in higher education institutions. Even if provinces were battling to provide these devices to all learners in the system, the Grade 12 learners should be prioritised. It was not all schools, but provinces had significantly moved in the last three years, within the limited available resources.
Government did not appropriate funds for the rollout of devices and connectivity to schools. The dedicated budget was not available. The provinces utilised their own allocations to meet the need. Learners were still carrying school bags, whether they had devices or not. There had been visits to first-world countries. The advice had always been to digitise teaching and learning but keep the conventional way side-by-side. The conventional way should not be completely removed, otherwise it would usher in a disaster. This was especially so if the connectivity was not working. First-world countries were using a hybrid approach. So, while learners were carrying books in their school bags, some were carrying devices. Some learners do not take out a pen from their school bags but a tablet. He had seen this in schools. If he gave learners five tips on how to prepare for matric or how to be successful in their studies, they would take out their tablets and not pens and paper. Some students use their cell phones if they do not have a tablet. This was his experience when meeting with learners every year.
Mr Shongwe's statement around Engotini Primary School and the problems around pit toilets had been noted. The DBE would follow up with schools where challenges had been pointed out. Electricity was a problem in the country. However, it was improving in some areas.
The concerns raised around no connection in Khayelitsha in the Western Cape will be investigated and how this was affecting teaching and learning. The presentation provided a table of the areas that would be prioritised, such as the special needs schools that benefited from the partnership with the network operators. A list with the details of the schools could be provided for the JC to look at whether these were lies or not. Some of the schools were not connected, but some were. The DBE could not say that nothing was happening when it was clear that progress had been made in some schools.
An agreement was reached with Mr Komane that a need existed to forge intergovernmental relations. This was emphasised by both Chairpersons. Vandalism and theft occurred in schools. It was a major drawback to the efforts that the government was making. It was a challenge. The issue of resources was another challenge. It was his understanding that the appropriation of funds was an activity of the national and provincial legislatures. This was put under challenge as it would seem that the DBE abdicated their responsibility if they were given money to roll out devices and connectivity and then failed to do so. The calculations had been made, and R30 billion was required to roll out devices to every learner and educator. Various cost-saving options were explored, but no positive response was received from the NT and the government as a whole. The response was that there was no money. This was the reason provinces tried to find ways to deliver.
The DBE did not necessarily train the school governing bodies on e-learning because teaching was not conducted by the DBE. Some programmes addressed the issues of provision of materials, as part of the school governing bodies. Many schools in Thembisa still use chalkboards. This was the case throughout the country. Not all schools were using smartboards. He disagreed with Mr Nchabaleng; smartboards were not solely used for broadcasting. These were also used for teaching but could be also be used for streaming. There was no dedicated budget to provide 500 schools with tablets. Some schools used their own budget to make provision for this. From an administrative point of view, every school had to have a computer with connectivity. The area that was lacking was teaching and learning. He agreed with Ms Mngadi that the progress had been inadequate.
There were variances between the provinces due to the lack of a dedicated budget and was based on what the provinces could afford. At some point, Gauteng and the Western Cape had focused on ICT. The phrase 'received connectivity for learning' meant that computers/devices could be used for teaching and learning for the delivery of the curriculum.
Every school should be able to run its administration, to provide data, to respond to queries, and to respond to requests from the district, province, or national level. At least every school should have a laptop/desktop and be connected through 3G. However, 2G and 3G were being phased out and higher speed is the new norm. The South African Broadcasting Corporation (SABC) was contributing through its learning programmes. The DBE had its own TV for teaching and learning. The DBE did have an arrangement with the SABC, to run programmes for different grades and subjects. The SABC was helping a great deal. The DBE embraced Co-Chairperson Maimela’s proposal to submit a plan on how to deal with the identified gaps. The DBE could share the purchasing of devices, how the costing was done and the plans for the roll out of devices. A list of the schools connected per district would be provided. The JC would be able to see if this was feasible or not. He emphasised that Grade 12 learners were prioritised for devices to ensure that they leave school by being exposed to the utilisation of devices and interacting with digital materials. If resources were made available, a PMO could be established to coordinate and manage all of the activities. He recollected that many of the devices were manufactured outside of the country.
Mr Tlhabane said that Gauteng and the Western Cape were exceeding the standard. They were exceeding in terms of the speed of the broadband by providing more. It was more than the SA Connect project. It would be interesting to see where the 6% of teachers being trained came from. It could have been a misunderstanding. On slide 57, there was an indication that 388 000 teachers had been trained, with a population of just under 450 000 teachers in the country. It could surely not be 6%. The number was cumulative because of the emerging nature of technology. Some of the teachers have been trained more than once. So, it must not be understood as a headcount, but a considerable number of teachers had been trained on ICT. It was surely not 6% but more than 80%.
He noted Mr Shongwe's statement around an interactive map. All schools not connected would reflect in green. This was not what Mr Tlhabane had meant. He explained that it was not a live map. The DBE was mapping schools onto the application so that each one of them could be geolocated. The DBE was currently adding every school in the country to the map. Once each school had installed the map, that was where the live report would come into play. Mr Shongwe's comment was noted, but it was based on insufficient information provided.
Mr Mweli said that there were times that the DBE made the Chairpersons so angry, but not to the point where the Chairpersons would say that everything that was said was a lie. He committed to responding to all other questions in writing within seven days. He would be working with the DG from the DCDT to ensure that this happened. He thanked the JC for their guidance and leadership.
Closing remarks
Chairperson Sangoni thanked everyone for the responses. The JC was heartened by what is perceived as progress. The challenges were daunting. There was still a long way to go. However, she believed that the DBE and the DCDT were getting somewhere due to the spirit in which the issues were being dealt with. This included the fragmentation and duplication. There were a number of direct recommendations, which the JC hoped that the DBE and the DCDT would take note of. There had to be a structure in the fact-finding process. A Member had recommended doing oversight on rural communities. The JC would accept the invitation to conduct oversight at some of these schools. The issue of needs-based assessment and the financing model was raised. This was something that the JC would take up. However, what came to Parliament was a result of what was agreed upon in government. While there may be priorities, it was difficult to change that.
There would be an engagement with the NT and the original equipment manufacturers. The Portfolio Committee on Communication and Digital Technologies had a long outstanding meeting with the original equipment manufacturers, where some of these issues would be raised. It had to look at how to leverage some of the Corporate Social Investment programmes coordinating school programmes. There was a request to publish the list of schools that were on the baseline. An action plan on the identified gaps should be submitted. This would help the JC to see if progress was being made.
The JC was not angry with the DBE but with the DCDT as no one from the top leadership was present. It was time for the DCDT to take education seriously, especially regarding this matter. It was unacceptable, but the JC appreciated Mr Sono being present and sharing his knowledge and wisdom. It was unfortunate that the Minister and Deputy Minister of Communications and Digital Technologies could not be present.
Members should treat each other with respect and not raise temperatures and create unnecessary divisions. She understood that everyone was representing different political parties. At times, there would be some temptation to take a dig at another party. In meetings where there were such inflammatory issues, she requested Members to manage these. There would always be an insult to her President, but she did not gag anyone. This should be managed, especially if the responsible persons who could respond were not on the platform, such as in this meeting. She appreciated everyone for continuing to engage robustly, and the right to do so would always be protected.
The meeting was adjourned.
