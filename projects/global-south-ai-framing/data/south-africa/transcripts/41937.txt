Western Cape Government Departments Annual Reports 2024/25
The Standing Committee on Finance (WCPP) convened to deliberate on the annual reports of the Provincial Treasury and the Western Cape Gambling and Racing Board (WCGRB) for the 2024/25 financial year. The Provincial Treasury reported achieving its twelfth consecutive clean audit, underscoring its focus on long-term financial sustainability guided by principles of fairness, value for money, innovation, and trust. Key achievements included the publication of three major research outputs: the Provincial Economic Review and Outlook (PERO), the Municipal Economic Review and Outlook (MERO), and the inaugural Local Government Budget Performance Review. The Treasury's data-driven approach was bolstered by tools like the Fiscal Futures model, which supported lobbying National Treasury for an updated provincial equitable share formula. Further, the Department continued its interventions in financially distressed municipalities, including Beaufort West, Theewaterskloof, and Knysna, through Financial Recovery Plans (FRPs), with the goal of moving them from stabilisation to sustainability.
Members sought detailed progress updates on these municipal interventions and questioned the increase in municipalities with unfunded budgets, which rose from five to six.
The Treasury clarified that 24 municipalities had ultimately adopted funded budgets and detailed its support mechanisms, such as the Financial Management Capability Grant (FMCG) and the Municipal Financial Recovery Services Grant (MFRSG).
Concerns were also raised about provincial underspending, particularly on the Compensation of Employees (COE) budget and disaster relief grants received late in the fiscal year.
The Treasury provided a breakdown, noting that a significant portion of the R1.5 billion underspend was attributable to these specific grants. Other topics included the Treasury's role in assessing risks for Public-Private Partnerships (PPPs) and its comprehensive strategy to address high vacancy rates through workforce planning, staff development, and well-being initiatives.
The WCGRB also presented a clean audit outcome, reporting a significant increase in revenue collection, with approximately R1.7 billion in gambling taxes accrued. The Board highlighted its enhanced focus on responsible gambling, including public awareness campaigns and corporate social investment (CSI) programmes. In response to Member's questions, the Board detailed the existing regulatory framework for gambling advertisements and acknowledged ongoing national discussions to strengthen these rules. A significant challenge identified was the entity's capacity, with 41 funded posts remaining vacant due to uncompetitive remuneration, leading to operational strain. The Board also explained the rationale behind the approved relocation of a casino from Caledon to Somerset West, citing economic benefits and job creation for the new host community. Enforcement against illegal gambling, particularly the complex challenge of online operations and the Board's necessary reliance on the South African Police Service (SAPS), was also discussed.
The Committee concluded by resolving to request several follow-up reports. These included outcomes of the equitable share formula review, feedback on the national moratorium on the Integrated Financial Management System (IFMS), a progress report on filling the WCGRB's vacant posts, and clarity on job creation from the casino relocation. The Committee further requested timelines for the WCGRB's office refurbishment, a report on CSI spending trends, and progress reports with potential oversight visits to the municipalities under financial recovery. The meeting was adjourned after the adoption of minutes from previous sessions.

Minister’s Remarks
Ms Deidré Baartman, Western Cape Minister of Finance, expressed that she was pleased to be present for the finalisation of the annual report sessions for the 2024/25 fiscal year for Vote 3. She stated that the Provincial Treasury had achieved a great deal during the year under review and had continued to build on that foundation in subsequent periods.
She reported that the Treasury had focused on long‑term financial sustainability, guided by priorities of fairness, value for money, innovation, and trust. During the year, three major research publications were released: the Municipal Economic Review and Outlook (MERO), the Provincial Economic Review and Outlook (PERO), and, for the first time, the Local Government Budget Performance Review, which assessed all 30 municipalities and was well received.
The Minister explained that data‑driven tools had been used to strengthen fiscal planning, including the Fiscal Futures model, which supported lobbying efforts with National Treasury for updates to the provincial equitable share. She noted that two budgets had been tabled—the main and adjustment budgets—with the adjustment budget prioritising disaster relief and frontline services, while the main budget focused on growth and job creation.
She further reported that interventions had continued in municipalities: Beaufort West had progressed from Stage one to Stage two intervention, work had begun in Theewaterskloof (which subsequently entered financial recovery), and a support and recovery plan had been initiated in Knysna. She emphasised that the Treasury was proud to have achieved its twelfth consecutive clean audit.
Additional achievements included assisting departments to secure infrastructure funding from national government through Budget Facility for Infrastructure (BFI) applications. The Minister acknowledged that the year had been challenging due to changes in the national budget process and the audit cycle but expressed pride in the resilience of Vote 3. She thanked the Treasury team for their dedication.
Remarks by the Head of the Western Cape Department of Provincial Treasury
Ms Julinda Gantana, HOD, reminded the Committee that the Western Cape Treasury remained the smallest provincial Treasury in the country. She reported that, despite challenges during the year under review, the team had remained steadfast in building a solid governance foundation.

After that, their role shifts to monitoring expenditure, while the implementing Department or entity monitors project delivery.
Briefing by the Western Cape Government
Mr Steven Kenyon, Chief Director for Local Government: Public Finance, outlined the two primary grant instruments administered by Provincial Treasury: The Western Cape Financial Management Capability Grant (FMCG) and the Municipal Financial Recovery Services Grant (MFRSG).
He explained that the application process was a combination of municipal applications and proactive identification by Provincial Treasury through in-year monitoring processes, such as the Section 71 monthly statements and Section 52 quarterly statements, after which municipalities were encouraged to apply.
He detailed the five primary allocation criteria for the FMCG:
- To credibly reduce a municipality's vulnerability.
- To support long-term economic growth and development, which are the two pillars of the Municipal Finance Management Act (MFMA) strategy.
- To demonstrate replicability, meaning the project is a best practice that could benefit multiple municipalities, a point especially relevant for district municipalities.
- To involve co-funding, with a guideline of approximately 20% contribution from the municipality.
- Specifically for the bursary programme sub-component, to demonstrate internal capacity and skills, based on the principle of temporary support aimed at enabling municipalities to eventually perform these functions independently.
Regarding the MFRSG, Mr Kenyon stated that it was associated with formal interventions. The basic allocation criterion was that a municipality must credibly demonstrate that the grant would support the objectives of its budget funding plans and financial recovery plans.
Ms Nadia Ebrahim, Acting Chief Director: Asset Management, addressed the concern about the elevated vacancy rate, acknowledging it as a significant issue for the Department. She attributed this primarily to structured financing, noting that, as the HOD had indicated, 28% of approved posts were unfunded, which led to a reduction in headcount.
She outlined the Department's forward-looking strategy to address this, which included:
- The strategic prioritisation of critical posts, adjudicated by the Compensation of Employees (COE) Committee.
- Increased planning of training to retain capable staff, particularly those aged between 25 and 36, who were found to be the most mobile.
- The use of an external bursary programme to facilitate career progression in critical fields.
- The development of an alternative non-remunerative awards policy to boost staff morale in the absence of performance rewards.
Ms Ebrahim detailed the three pillars of the Department's COE management strategy:
Capacity: This involved a five-year workforce plan that included retirement risk planning and developmental programmes like bursaries and the Chartered Accountants Academy to feed the talent pipeline for the provincial government, municipalities, and national departments.
Capability: The Information and Communication Technology (ICT) team was exploring the digitisation of opportunities and leveraging Artificial Intelligence (AI) to optimise processes and reduce administrative burdens.
Well-being: This focused on promoting a work-life rhythm, conducting policy reviews, using employee engagement surveys, enhancing the employee health and wellness programme, and allowing staff to utilise flexible working arrangements.
One official provided further detail on provincial underspending for the 2024/25 financial year. She reported that the province spent 98.2% of its budget, resulting in an under-expenditure of 1.8%, which amounted to R1.5 billion.
She broke down the R1.5 billion underspend as follows:
- R829 million was for disaster funding received in the 2024 adjustments budget for the Comprehensive Agricultural Support Programme (CASP) and the roads conditional grant in the Department of Agriculture.
- R300 million was an allocation that was not received by the Department of Infrastructure for human settlements, as the allocation was reduced.
- The remaining underspending for the province was therefore approximately R400 million, or 0.26% of the budget.
She concluded by noting that some of this remaining underspending was on COE across all departments, and the impact of the DPSA Circular on Provincial Treasury's COE spending had been felt province-wide.
Another official elaborated on the two public-private partnership (PPP) inquiries, explaining that the first round focused on risk identification and the second on processes and oversight.
He outlined that PPPs have six phases and typically pass through four control points, with the Provincial Treasury providing input at each point. He noted that the Treasury typically assessed three metrics: affordability, value for money, and appropriate risk transfer.
The official listed the four control points as: a review of the feasibility study; the procurement documentation; the bids received; and finally, the contract documentation.
For the period under review, he reported that two projects were assessed: the parking development project, sponsored by the Stellenbosch Municipality, and the office block, sponsored by the Department of Infrastructure (DOI).
Regarding identified risks, the official stated that for the parking project, a point was made around the revenue share, indicating that negotiations between the public and private partner were necessary. For the second project, a risk was identified concerning value for money. He concluded by reporting that, on the back of this finding, the project had since been deregistered, as it was established during the feasibility study that value for money would not be realised.

Mr Kenyon described the Treasury’s close work with municipalities on budget funding plans, which involve customised engagement rhythms, monthly progress reporting, and reviews of evidence submitted by municipalities. He reported that many of these municipalities had moved to a funded position in the new financial year.

She cited a past example from the Health Department where limited bidding was essential to acquire life-saving equipment unavailable from any local supplier.
Mr Isaac Smith, Acting DDG: Governance and Asset Management, elaborated that open and competitive bidding was the default in the Western Cape, even for procurements under R1 million, through the e-Procurement system. Mr Robin Bennett, HOD: Regulatory Compliance, WCGRB, detailed the Western Cape’s specific advertising rules, which require board pre-approval for adverts by locally licensed operators. He highlighted the creation of a dedicated Responsible Gambling Manager position and a licence condition requiring operators to contribute 0.1% of gross gambling revenue to the South African Responsible Gambling Trust for education and treatment.
Mr Bassuday responded to the question on staff retention, confirming that remuneration levels were uncompetitive.
