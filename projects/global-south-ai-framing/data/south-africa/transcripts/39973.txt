Meeting Summary
Video
The Standing Committee on Finance in the Western Cape Provincial Parliament met with the Provincial Treasury for a briefing on the Municipal Economic Review and Outlook, the Western Cape Adjustments Appropriation Bill, the Medium-Term Budget Policy Statement and the Quarterly Performance. It was the Minister of Finance’s first Budget Policy Statement. She attended the meeting, leading a large team of officials from the Provincial Treasury and the Office of the Premier.
The first order of business was the Municipal Economic Review and Outlook. The review and outlook for the Cape Town City Metro and each district in the Western Cape were presented in separate booklets rich with information on the macro-economic performance and outlook, the economic growth and job creation, and safety and well-being. Those booklets, together with the Western Cape Provincial Economic Review and Outlook, formed a portfolio of evidence that had to be considered as a whole. The Review looked at the common thread of the impact of infrastructure investment within local government. The current year included the Financial Sustainability and Good Governance Index, a key lever for infrastructure development, and insights from Ratings Africa, the financial sustainability index. More details on environmental risk and vulnerability data were included as the Department intended to track climate change concerns and the impact of climate change within the province. New studies and updates on the housing markets in different districts and the growth potential of town study insights were considered. Insights into Gender-Based Violence (GBV) prevalence was a key thread. The key message was that economic growth in the Western Cape was tracking above the national average, and the Department expected that it would continue on that trajectory. The inflation level was subdued at 2.8%, which was below the 3% - 6% band set by the Reserve Bank, and a 0.25 base point decrease in interest rates was announced the previous week. The tourism industry had recovered following the COVID-19 pandemic and was showing a 20% year-on-year increase.
Members queried the regional tourism performance statistics as the Overberg had recorded 5.7 million bed nights, which was higher than the Cape Metro's 3.7 million bed nights. The spending proportion concerning the number of bed nights in various regions was queried. Members asked for the employment numbers in tourism, which was also a measure to understand the tourism industry's performance. The highest levels of food poverty were in Saldanha, but the income inequality on the West Coast had gone down between 2021 and 2023. What did the data mean? What did the inequality number indicate or indicate a more equal distribution? How did the Department, when interpreting data, determine what led to the conclusion that income distribution was being equalised?
Why was mining and quarrying 0.0% across the province? Could Members get a breakdown of the contribution of agriculture, forestry and fishing, as those were quite different sectors? How many jobs in the Western Cape were permanent, and how many were government jobs and Expanded Public Works Programme (EPWP) opportunities? Why could permanent jobs not be created on the West Coast, the Central Karoo and the Garden Route? Why was the potential for development in rural areas not being prioritised?
Did the Department have the exact number of increases in teenage pregnancy in the various municipalities? Did any of the ten municipal areas red flag an area for immediate intervention? Was there a relationship between serious crime and drug use? Was drug use, as well as drug manufacturing, increasing in rural areas? How were dwellings classified as informal? How were they calculated? Did the Department know what percentage of the 83% of Matric pass learners was being absorbed in higher educational institutions? Did the Department have a database that tracked young people post-Matric? Could Members be given detailed statistics, especially in the farming community, that showed that grievous bodily harm in the Western Cape was the highest in the country?
The Provincial Treasury presented the 2024 Medium Term Budget Policy Statement, the Second Quarter Performance for 2024/25 and the 2024 Adjustment Budget. Generally, the adjustment budget formed the basis for the next MTEF. However, in the current year, the Provincial Treasury was still finalising the Private Sector Participation (PSP) 2025-2030 owing to changes in regulations. Departments were still finalising their departmental strategic plans, and the Department was still looking at the first year of implementation. Whereas in other years Provincial Treasury had more certainty, the current presentation demonstrated the need to find a way to craft the budget to provide the basis for the 2025 MTEF, whilst still in the middle of a planning process. Looking at the current economic context, the global economy had remained resilient, but it had not reached the levels of pre-COVID-19 global growth, largely as a result of continued global or geopolitical tensions, high borrowing costs, as well as the impact and lingering impact of COVID-19. South Africa's GDP outlook in 2024 was expected to grow at a rate of 0.9%, increasing to 2% in 2025. The economic growth rate was still relatively low due to overall structural challenges and issues relating to inequality and unemployment. On the positive side, the repo rate had declined marginally over time, and the inflation rate had stayed between the overall range of 3% to 6%. One could see the potential for disposable income increasing, but looking at the population growth, there had been an 8.8% decline from 2014 to 2023 in terms of GDP per capita.
The Western Cape government remained committed to aligning the budget with the 2025-2030 provincial strategic plan, aiming to drive growth and expand fiscal capacity through a five-year approach. The focus was on delivering a budget to weather storms and prepare the province to face economic uncertainties, natural disasters, and other unforeseen challenges. The Provincial Treasury emphasised looking at fiscal resilience, protecting basic services and exploring alternative and blended financing for infrastructure investment to drive growth. The fiscal framework did not show sufficient growth to deal with the ask and increased demand for services. Important issues concerned budget reform and strengthening budget management and fiscal discipline, emphasising improved efficacy, efficiency, transparency and accountability in public sector operations. Between the 2021 and the 2024 Medium-Term Expenditure Frameworks, the province had seen a real budget cut of R8.4 billion.
Members asked if capital expenditure was being delayed to deal with shortfalls in departmental budgets. Members asked about the Department of Infrastructure's poor spending pattern and the impact of extortion on its work. Did the Adjustment Budget address infrastructure in the case of disaster in the informal settlement areas, such as Khayelitsha? Why was the Department giving such a big slice of funding to Infrastructure even though that Department had some risk of under-expenditure? What was the R25 million for the integrated transport hub? The majority of work by the Mobility Department was happening on the roads, so why was the Provincial Treasury shifting money to other departments? What was going to happen in January with enrolments at schools? What would happen to crime when all the tourists and visitors came to Cape Town? Should Members focus their anger and infuriation on the in-year or the outer year? Why was the Provincial Parliament having to deal with the cashflow problems of Freeport Saldanha? What was the percentage of direct intervention in job creation by provincial departments and their entities versus any other?
Why was R14 million allocated to the energy when there was no longer any load shedding in the country? Was the Western Cape Provincial Parliament putting money into public entities operating at a loss? How would the province be able to spend the R0.5 billion it had received in the Adjustment Budget in November 2024 to compensate for disasters from December 2023 to July 2024 by the end of the current financial year?

The Chairperson welcomed Members, the Minister and officials to the Budget Committee meeting. He congratulated Minister Baartman on tabling her first Medium Term Budget Policy Statement (MTBPS). Minister Baartman stated that she had been speaking for the past hour and 45 minutes and would like to hand over to the Committee. She added that she had tabled what she considered world-class publications. A lot of work had gone into them, so it would be wonderful if the Committee could lead by indicating what more they want to know.
The Chairperson informed Members that the Provincial Treasury had indicated that they would start with the Municipal Economic Review and Outlook (MERO).
Municipal Economic Review and Outlook
Ms Julinda Gantana, Head of Department of the Provincial Treasury, informed the Committee that the team would take Members through the MERO, but she was glad to see that the Members had copies of the books. There were six publications, consisting of all districts and the Metro; each booklet had the details of the municipalities within the districts as well, so it was rich with information. There were three sections: the first section dealt with the macroeconomic performance and outlook, section B dealt with economic growth and job creation, and section C dealt with safety and well-being. When she had presented the Western Cape Provincial Economic Review and Outlook
(PERO), she had indicated that she and her team would be bringing the MERO, and it was important that, as a portfolio of evidence, those two publications be looked at in their entirety and as a whole. She handed it over to Ms Nadia Rinquest, Project Coordinator for the MERO, to take the committee through the presentation.
Ms Rinquest stated that the theme for the MERO was infrastructure as a pathway to progress and that the common thread of the impact of infrastructure investment within local government should be looked at. Every year, the department likes to innovate within the MERO. The current year included the financial sustainability and good governance index, which was a key lever for infrastructure development, and it looked at the insights from Ratings Africa, the financial sustainability index. More details on environmental risk and vulnerability data were included as the Department wanted to begin tracking climate change concerns and their impact within the province. Housing market studies were included, as well as new studies and updates on the housing markets in different districts and the growth potential of town study insights. Insights into Gender-Based Violence (GBV) prevalence from the Victim Empowerment Programme were included.
Ms Rinquest added that she was excited about a health and education infrastructure analysis, which was new to the 2024/25 MERO. The Department had collaborated with the Children's Commission, where the MERO was broken down into insights that young people could understand. The key message of the 2024/25 MERO was that economic growth was tracking above the national average, which was great for the Western Cape, and the Department expected that it would continue on that trajectory. The inflation level was subdued, sitting at 2.8%, which was below the 3% - 6% band that was set by the Reserve Bank. There was a 0.25 base point decrease in interest rates the previous week. The tourism industry had recovered following the COVID-19 pandemic and was showing a 20% year-on-year increase.
She said that unemployment levels across the province had improved, driven by robust growth in the agriculture, tourism, transport, and logistics sectors. Given the disasters, flooding, and fires, the effects of climate change were becoming increasingly evident, causing a downside risk to the agricultural sector. Well-being indicators, however, showed a general improvement, although the Department still had concerns about safety metrics.
Minister Baartman added two things. In every MERO book, one would find the photo that the children monitors had drawn as to how they interpreted the respective MEROs. Members should note that the national crime statistics had been published the previous day, but the MERO books had gone to the printers before those new statistics were published. That had to be considered as some of the crime statistics had changed since publication.

Mr D Bryant (DA) referred to figures 9 and 10 on page 22 in the Cape Metro booklet, which were about sectoral employment contributions. Figure 9 specifically contained two things that jumped out at him. The first was 0.0% in mining and quarrying across the province. Effectively, it said that no mining or quarrying was happening anywhere in the province, which was not right. Could he receive an explanation as to why it was 0.0% across the board ? Could he get a breakdown of the contribution of agriculture, forestry and fishing, as those were quite different sectors? The province had over 50% of fish stocks, so it should have contributed significantly more to the GDP.
Minister Baartman responded to the question on mining and the quarries, explaining that it was the result of rounding down when adding all the districts, so it was not that there was not any mining and quarrying in the province; it was just that it rounded off either to zero or up to one. On the rounding up to one of the Gini coefficients, she said that the Department did not round up to one because one could only put the Gini coefficient between zero and one. Unlike other statistics where, say, 59% out of 100 or 80% out of 100, one would have the least income distribution inequality and the most equal income distribution would be zero. Any region, province or country would only be between zero and one.
Ms Rinquest said she would double-check the Overberg district bed night numbers, which are higher than the Cape Metro's. Bed nights were usually different in certain districts because of the time people spent travelling. If one counted domestic tourists, say from the Cape Metro, who might spend bed nights in the Overberg, that would impact the figures, but she would check and provide confirmation.
She explained to Ms Nkondlo that tourism was a sub-sector of a bigger sector: wholesale, retail, trade, accommodation, and tourism. The City of Cape Town had a much larger economy than, for instance, the Overberg. The figures indicated that spending and the proportion of the tourism sub-sector contribute to the larger economy. In the city, tourism would reflect a much smaller proportion because there were much larger sectors operating within the Metro compared to the Overberg, where tourism made up a 16% contribution. After all, tourism made up a huge part of the Overberg economy.
With regards to the poverty levels and the correlation, Ms Rinquest told Ms Nkondlo that in areas where there were high levels of semi-gration or high migration, such as in Saldanha with the larger economies, there might be a high population of wealthier constituents or citizens. However, there was a huge proportion of poor people within that area. There were poorer municipalities, such as Beaufort West and Prince Albert, with very high poverty levels. Still, people flocked to large economies like Saldanha and George because they thought they would find jobs as there was a perception that there was work there. People migrated into those municipalities and were absorbed into the poverty level of the municipality. Saldanha, Overstrand and George were such examples. In addition, there were municipalities with high poverty levels, such as Beaufort West and Prince Albert, where the data reflected the high poverty levels. Regarding income inequality and the Gini coefficient, South Africa had the highest income inequality globally at 0.60. One did not go beyond that, and it did not change, year on year, regardless of how many interventions there were. The wealthier people became wealthier, and the poorer got poorer across the country. Only high-level catalytic economic interventions would affect economic growth, jobs, poverty alleviation and crime. A holistic approach was necessary to see inequality start to shrink. She stated that when the economy did recover and grow so that inequality moved down, it would be evident in the decimals, e.g. not 0.59 but 0.59872 - that was where the difference would be evident. One would need long-term sustainable economic interventions to affect a Gini coefficient. It would only decrease substantially over a very long period.
Regarding the Cape Metro and the mining question, Ms Rinquest informed Mr Bryant that it could be explained by the HQ (or the headquarters) effect. A lot of mining was happening in Cape Town, such as quarrying and sand mining, but the SARS data, the spatial tax data used for the statistics, depended on where the business was based or headquartered. Depending on where the business was registered, that was where its contribution to the economy would be registered. According to data, molluscs seemed to be mined in the central Karoo, and fish were stored there, but there was no ocean – that was the headquarters effect as those activities had their headquarters in the Central Karoo. She admitted that there was an issue with the available data sources that no one in the country had been able to resolve, i.e., how to allocate where the activity was happening. The only way to track the economic contribution was through the spatial tax data, which considered where one lived and what one earned. Knowing the job and sector one worked in, statisticians could disaggregate it to a particular town level. However, the data did not exist where it could be pulled down regarding the headquarter effect.
Ms Rinquest stated that the Department had the data broken down into sub-sectors such as tourism, agriculture, forestry, fishing, etc. She would share that data.

For sectors where various indicators were available, the Department was able to piece together the story of where the employment was. Still, the contribution to the mining economy would be counted in Johannesburg. The Department used what it could to forecast and make predictions based on the available data, so it would not be consistent across all sectors regarding employment contribution because the spatial tax data was utilised. The available information included where a person lived and worked without the person's name, what a person earned, in what sector one worked, and where one contributed to the economy, as taken from the South Africa Revenue Service (SARS) data.

For example, a remote worker might not be registered as making a contribution in SA if the person were on holiday in SA. Nevertheless, she would ask SARS about it.

Ms Stoffel asked why a department would be given additional funds whilst the previous funds had not been spent. Dr Mbombo had asked the same question. The second question related to slide 19: Socioeconomic picture through outcome and key performance indicators. The HIV-positive rate of 15 to 24-year-olds for the second quarter was 1.3%. That 1.3% was fine, but she did not know whether it was for those at school or where because the age range indicated it might be for children at school. At that age, children were supposed to be at school. She suggested that the Department should develop new strategies when the target percentage was reached.
Dr Mbombo had an additional question regarding the provincial funding for Energy, which was R14.7 million in terms of the adjustment allocations (slide 30). The other issue concerns the Parliament's decrease of R68 000 (slide 28). On slide 30, the presentation discussed the specifics for Energy being R4.7 for the Green Hydrogen Master Plan. She was not sure where the rest of the funds went. Could that be explained? She was interested because the government had been very much into Energy during the load shedding, but there had not been any load shedding recently. Understandably, it was about moving away from fossil fuels towards green energy. However, when one looked at robbing Paul to pay Pauline, ​​​​​​​why would R14 million be allocated to Energy?
Could she be given a more rational motivation for that? On slide 29, R10.4 million would train peace officers, etc. Was that money going to Cape Town City, or was it also going to the other municipalities? Her constituency was George, so she always had to stand up for the rural areas with needs. Why was there a decrease in the budget for the Provincial Parliament? It might be minimal, but why was there a decrease and, likewise, why was there a decrease in Social Development?
She asked why an Adjustment Budget would be used to decrease funding for Social Development and Parliament. Still, it put aside funds for Freeport Saldanha's cash flow and gave more money to Energy at a time when there was no load shedding. It did not make sense.
The Chairperson requested the Minister to respond.
Minister Baartman said she would first deal with the net decrease for the Western Cape Provincial Parliament(WCPP) and Social Development. The Provincial Treasury had not decreased the WCPP budget; the WCPP was realigning its budget, and COE expenditures had not been completely concluded. That vote would be addressed in detail by the relevant oversight committee. Treasury was also not decreasing the Social Development budget. The net decrease was shown because Social Development needed Infrastructure to do some infrastructural things for the Department. That budget was moved between votes so that the Infrastructure Department could do the work for Social Development. The budget for Social Development was being increased because, not only in the Budget speech but also in the book, one would see that Social Development was being given humanitarian relief and nutritional support funding. After all, it was difficult to deal with disasters, especially having to wait for national government departments to provide disaster funding. So those two budgets, in accounting terms, might look like a decrease in the book, but it was about moving the funding to where it would be implemented. The Adjustment Budget actually decreased the energy reserve to reprioritise other matters.
The Minister explained that shifting money from the Mobility Department was about the Department's mandates. If everything infrastructure were going to be done by the Infrastructure Department in terms of roads, then the money for roads would go to the Infrastructure Department. Provincial Treasury had to ask where a particular mandate lay. If the mandate changes, then the money will follow the mandate. Regarding the split in disaster funding, the minister explained that when there was a disaster, the respective departments had to assess the National Treasury and COGTA (Department of Cooperative Governance and Traditional Affairs). That assessment of damages went to the National Treasury which did its own assessment while COGTA did its assessment. They would then decide whether or not they would give the province the money for the disaster. The money for disasters was from December 2023 to July 2024. Some of that money was spent during the disaster, and some adjustment money was used to make payments. The province had not received all the disaster money it applied for. Even when it came to BFI, it had to be remembered that the rapid builds had started building. The National Treasury had said in the main budget allocation that it was provisionally allocating R2.5 billion because the technical work had not been done then. As the building had started, the BFI funding was confirmed. Again, it was essentially a repayment.
She said the province could not go back to the National Treasury and say it did not want to spend the disaster money on a particular item because those were the projects sent in when the flood affected the building. The province needed money to fix it. When a province receives money for disasters, it cannot spend it on a different building. So, if the province fixed that building using its own money, it was a repayment for the money already spent on the building affected by the flood. That was why that money was coming through those particular grants essentially in response to those disaster applications. The Western Cape tried its best to coordinate disaster applications through both DLG (Department of Local Government) and the Environmental Affairs and Development Planning, as well as making sure that everyone kept the Provincial Treasury abreast so it could keep track of all the payments.
An important question for all departments and entities was whether the department or entity had the ability to spend the money. Sometimes, there were amazing ideas, and that was awesome as the provincial government wanted people to have innovative ideas. Departments and entities had to come to Parliament to account for their ability to spend the money given. They could not get upset when the Provincial Treasury took back some of the money if it were not being spent. The government had to work as a team. Treasury had to provide the tools, but the department or entity had to build the boat. That was what teamwork meant. What Treasury had done with the PACE (prioritise, ambitious, clarity and empowered) formula was obviously a preliminary allocation. WCPP will see the PACE phased over three years in the preliminary allocation.
Even though the PACE data was updated with the 2024 mid-year population estimates and the GDP estimates with departmental estimates across the departments, the money WCPP was supposed to get immediately was going to get over three years - 2025, 2026 and 2027 financial years. The last tranche of money was actually 2024 data that was only going to be provided in the 2027-28 financial year. In 2025/26, WCPP was getting R65 million, R252 million in the next year and in the final year, R356 million.
But there were problems because if the Treasury kept doing the three-year phase, it was never going to get to the situation where it was getting all the money needed right now—the money that was being phased in was needed immediately, but it was only going to be provided later. So, it created a situation where one could not budget for the whole R600 million plus immediately. The province had to focus on immediate priorities.
The other consideration was whether the data would change over the next few years, or if there were reviews of the PACE formula, would the province still get the money? There was a likelihood that things might change, and WCPP might end up getting less in the outer years than expected. In terms of upcoming pressures, she tried her best to reiterate to departments that it would be a journey, and they had to report on upcoming pressures so that the Provincial Treasury could do its own assessment. Provincial Treasury and the Department of the Premier had to approach the Budget Committee and collectively provide the information regarding what the departments are reporting in terms of their pressures specifically. That pressure is based on historical spending, current spending, and what the two departments believed would happen in the upcoming months. Departments also had a duty to report their pressure. If not, the assumption was that the department was fine, growing and growing, and everything was okay. When reporting pressure, a department had to provide the data and evidence to support the reported pressure, which resulted in trust and transparency. If evidence were not submitted, there could have been an incorrect calculation.
In response to Dr Mbombo's query about making a noise, Minister Baartman agreed that she should make a noise. She could not underestimate the fact that provincial envelopes remained the same. However, when the committees told departments to spend more money on particular matters, not one department would say it did not need more money. If the pie remained the same, difficult decisions would have to be made. Provinces needed to stand together to make sure that the envelope increased. She agreed that departments had to be able to explain their spending capacity, their ability to implement, their capacity to execute, and their ability and capacity to spend.
Ms Stoffel had asked about the Department of Health and Wellness and where the HIV-positive rate items were. The Minister was unsure whether her team would know that, but perhaps the Health and Wellness Department might; she would have to ask that Department.
Regarding the energy reserve, the minister stated that money was being taken away from it to reprioritise it for other matters. But in the Energy Council, now called the Energy and Water Council, Eskom sometimes made presentations to the Council and recently indicated that there was a real possibility that, based on their projections, there might be load shedding in the following year (2025) and it might be worse than any seen so far. She warned that just because there was no load shedding right now, it did not necessarily mean there would not be load shedding in the future. The Provincial Treasury had to ensure it budgeted for energy security in the province. People did not necessarily care whether local, provincial, or national governments provided the service. People expect a response from the nearest government authority when the electricity goes off. Energy security was a real risk in the province, as was water security. The Energy and Water Council was changing its priorities to include water and energy, and WCPP was requested to change the reserve to Energy and Water. That prioritisation would come in the main budget. However, in the meantime, WCPP was preparing the reserve for them so that if they wished to reprioritise water security, then the possibility was there.
She added that there was a list of projects in the energy allocations on page 99 of the MTPPS policy statement book, which takes the full energy plan and shows how much per year per project was budgeted for, the energy reserve and the total.
Ms Gantana responded to the question on the Freeport Saldanha cash flow requirement. On slide 29, it was raised as an in-year fiscal risk. The entity indicated a risk in their cash flow, which amounted to R25 million, specifically for project and investment support. The proposal was not to fund the R25 million from the fiscus, but there was some reprioritisation. Part of it is related to the parent department, the Department of Economic Development and Tourism, contributing; part of it would be because of the alignment of issues one would need to address if WCPP talked about investor support. An allocation also came from Wesgro. The R4.7 million for Green Hydrogen would be redirected towards the project and investor support request made by Freeport Saldanha. There was also an amount from the Provincial Revenue Fund, but the bulk was a combination of contributions towards the entity. Other things were happening within the space, but there was definitely an investor pipeline, and meetings with investors were taking place in the current week to ensure that they received the necessary support in terms of the funding that WCPP was providing, but also so that they could pull the investors in.
Ms Analiese Pick, Chief Director: of Provincial Government Public Finance, responded to the question on the R10.4 million in terms of police oversight and community safety. The R10.4 million included additional support to Neighbourhood Watch Programmes of R1.4 million, an expansion of the peace officer training to increase law enforcement of R3.6 million and youth resilience programs, and an expansion of work opportunities of R5.4 million. Of that, R10.4 million, R3.6 million went to the City of Cape Town, and R1 million went to various municipalities to provide increased support for the canine units outside the city. That was all she had about portions of the funding going to municipalities.
Referring to the funding received from the National Treasury for disaster funding and the question asking why the funding was going to the Infrastructure Department, she reminded Members that in the period December 2023 to July 2024, the Western Cape had three to four severe weather occurrences and the funding applied for was much more than the R947 million received. Secondly, the disaster funding came through conditional grants, so a province did not have the discretion of where to place the funds. The R947 571 was going to infrastructure because it came from the Roads Maintenance Grant. The WCPP application had been for the disasters that impacted the roads, but included in the application was a request from Cape Nature because their roads were also washed away, and there was no grant for the environmental sector. That was why the amount for Cape Nature via Environmental Affairs and Development Planning for roads came through the roads grant. It seemed a big amount of R571 million, but it was split between them.
Ms Pick said that Provincial Treasury had engaged in conversations with the Department of Infrastructure about its ability to spend not only from a perspective of what was happening on the roads but also the ability to spend the funding coming through for Cape Nature. The provincial Treasury had written to the National Treasury that R947 million was a lot of money to get in November of a year, and the province's understanding of that allocation was for a minimum of two financial years. That would give the departments time to spend it. It would have been ideal if the National Treasury had provided the funding from 1 April 2024 when the province was doing the main budget, but they would now need to consider the timing of the funding. The Infrastructure Department indicated that they were accelerating their projects now that they knew the funding package available to them. Planning had been accelerated their planning, and they would be working throughout the summer, December and January to increase implementation. R300 million was received for agriculture regarding river protection and to respond to the damage where that was concerned. However, the repairs from the floods had actually cost about R500 million.
Ms Gantana said that the WCPP allocation letter for 2025 indicated that WCPP was receiving an additional R647 million over the next three years. It was nowhere near the province’s service delivery demands and risks, but that was the outlook for 2025 for all departments. Where they could, they should make some savings, perhaps on COE (cost of employees), but because they now knew the pressures they would be facing in the next year, they would be able to, e.g. reduce COE in the current financial year, which also took the pressure off the following year. The Western Cape Provincial Parliament was reducing COE by about R1.064 million and utilising that for laptop refresh. WCPP also reduced its goods and services to deal with pressures the following year. One of the new pressures facing the Western Cape Provincial Parliament was the unintended consequences of fiscal consolidation of other departments. For example, the Police Service had been paying for certain security services for WCPP but would no longer be paying because the SAPS budget had been cut. WCPP had asked the Provincial Treasury to reduce its current year's baseline by R800 000 to pay for those unbudgeted pressures for the next two years.
Ms Gantana concluded that most departments faced this sort of thing as the unintended consequence of fiscal consolidation of national departments or their national partners, so they could no longer pick up costs as they had been doing.
Dr Naadia Ismail Director: of Strategic and Operational Management Support, responded to the question about the visualisation of the graph. The average was still there on the current graph but was depicted slightly differently because she had changed the depiction. But she could change again. It was done to share with Members and allow them to compare quarter-on-quarter. The quarter-on-quarter comparison was on slides 15 and 16; the bar chart's last column was average. However, the graph did not show the line. She could insert the line going forward, and to maintain the quarter-on-quarter comparison, the graphs would show a Quarter One line, a Quarter Two line, a Quarter Three line and a Quarter Four line with each line of a different colour. As indicated, the average for government departments was 84%, and DOTP was above the average. Six departments were below average: DOI, the Department of Cultural Affairs and Sport, the Mobility Department, the Education Department, the Department of Health and Wellness and the Department of Environmental Affairs and Development Planning. Similarly, one could see entities above and below average on slide 16. She cautioned that one needed to look at the number of indicators because some entities had fewer indicators in their Annual Performance Plan.
She agreed that one could assess and present the data differently about jobs. It would be good to do a proper analysis or a much more detailed analysis after six months. Looking at slide 17, one could easily say Treasury and DOTP were working together. The Department of Police Oversight and Community Safety was supporting 236 youth at the Chrysalis Academy, an intervention that stood out and was really contributing to the number of jobs created. One could look at the interventions, determine the nature of the jobs created across the departments, and categorise those jobs according to the type of jobs, which population was being targeted, or even the spatial disaggregation. That would require much more analysis, and one would need to look at the administrative data and the job profiling of the beneficiaries.
Looking at the data on HIV AIDS, Dr Ismail said that at 1.34% in Quarter One, HIV had decreased, and the target of 1.5% was achieved. The Department of Health and Wellness (DOHW) could provide the variables relating to HIV, and DOHW's current Annual Performance Plan provides a good depiction of HIV/AIDS in figures 5, 6, 7, and 8 in the book. Figure 7 spoke about the HIV tests and the HIV test positive rates; Figure 8 spoke about the total clients that started on treatment, and Figure 9 indicated the total clients remaining on ARTs.
Mr Faez Salie, Director of Local Government Accounting, added that on pages 11 and 19, the Treasury had put the quarter against the average line. He could add that graph in the future because it had been removed only to streamline the presentation. He asked Members to remember that HIV testing was not compulsory, so the result was only for those who had opted to have an HIV test at their local health facility. It also did not include private-sector testing.
The Chairperson called for a final round of questions.
Dr Mbombo had a follow-up question. She needed to understand the presentation fully because she served on the Budget Committee full-time. Her questions were selective, focusing on the Committees where she served full-time to take it further with the relevant Minister when on that Committee. Regarding infrastructure. She asked if Infrastructure would receive almost R0.5 billion that would be funding from now until 31 March. It was long overdue because it addressed some of the monies due to the disasters. Was Treasury content with that? She had heard that the provincial Treasury had written to ask that it could be spread over at least two budget years, which meant that the Minister agreed that there was no way they could spend the money in the current financial year. Despite the contractors' holidays, which normally started on 15 December, the Department of Infrastructure had said it would spend the money. Had it been agreed that there would not be a contractors' holiday? Was the Minister reassuring the Committee that there would not be a situation in January, February, March, or April when the money would be returned to the National Treasury because it was not spent? It was late, and everyone knew there was no way any department could spend R1 billion within that period, even though the department was not personally delivering the service. The Department depended on the contractors. Had the Minister said the contractors had agreed not to take a holiday. Was the Minister saying that it was doable or not doable? Because it was not doable, was the money going to be returned? What was the Minister's thinking around that?
Dr Mbombo partly covered Mr Masipa because his question was about the R288 017 allocated to the Department of Agriculture (page 28). What was the nett of the adjustment of R288 017 as some funds had already been spent? Was the funding intended to fill the hole that had just been created? Concerning the R25 million allocation to Freeport Saldanha, he saw R40 million had been taken from Wesgro. Was Wesgro going to be able to continue with its activities without that R40 million, or what measures were in place to ensure that Wesgro was not adversely affected by the reprioritisation?
Mr Petrus asked if ​​​​​​​WCPP was
investing money in public entities operating at a loss. When would they count that loss?
The Chairperson called on the Minister and the Department.
The Minister explained that part of the ability to spend was things like whether a contract was already in place, had the money, or a portion of it, had already been spent. Was it a repayment or would the department have to put a contract in place? Did they have an arrangement to build through the holidays or were they going to lose the summer season? If building, what was the timeline? It is important to plan so that if it were foreseen that they were not going to be able to spend the money, the Provincial Treasury would engage in a conversation with National Treasury. The funding for the Department of Agriculture was not for agriculture alone; Agriculture and Infrastructure had applied for the funding to help alleviate the pressure on other departments. Some of the additional money, even through the Provincial Revenue Fund, would go to DOHW and the Department of Education (WCED). That was the equitable share specifically.
The Minister asked Mr Petrus if he had additional information regarding his question.
Mr Petrus said that he could make one example, like the GoGeorge.
The Minister stated that GoGeorge was not an entity of the provincial government, but there was a contract for the GoGeorge services.
Ms Gantana indicated that the Wesgro contribution to the Saldanha Freeport was R5 million and was in terms of the investment programme because there was alignment of the mandate of both WesGro, what WesGro needed to achieve, but also what the Freeport Saldanha was trying to achieve in bringing in investors. When Freeport Saldanha raised the risk in terms of the operating expenditure amounting to R25 million, the Provincial Treasury had considered areas that could be reprioritised just to get them to the end of the financial year. The plan the entity had put in place gave sufficient evidence that they could be on a better footing for the 2025 MTEF. They were currently meeting with investors, so Treasury was quite confident that they could pull it through. The Minister of Agriculture monitored it closely and attached certain conditions to the adjustment. There was evidence that the entity was turning things around.
Ms Gantana informed Dr Mbombo that the Division of Revenue made provision for a province to show commitment against any allocation. Then, there was an almost automatic approval for rollover. The Department of Infrastructure had been requested to enter into a contractual commitment so that, although the work might not be done, the obligation would be there. That should provide sufficient grounds to apply for a rollover for the Department of Infrastructure specifically. The GoGeorge question was a tough one, and she thought that it would be fair that the Department of Mobility provided the Committee with further details in that regard, partly because part of the problem was that the George municipality was not in a position to come to the party in terms of funding so the whole project was being funded from the provincial side. That was the challenge.
The Chairperson thanked the Minister, the HOD, and the entire department for their patience and for answering all the questions. It had been a very long day.
The Minister thanked the Chairperson and the Committee Members. Often, the questions assisted the Provincial Treasury in refining its work and understanding how it could better present information and simplify procedures or how things were done. She was very passionate about the simplification of language. Given how technical it was, it was not very easy within the finance sector, especially within the Finance Department. Members would have seen the artwork done with the Office of the Children's Commissioner, and also, from the media statement that day, they would have been using artificial intelligence to create an MTBPS budget for Gen Z youth. Gen Z youth did not like reading an hour-long speech but would read a two-page summary, so they used artificial intelligence to put some technical terms into friendly language. She also thanked the team because some reforms recently implemented in the budget process were difficult. After all, it had been an institutionalisation of teamwork and an institutionalisation of trust. It had been a different type of budget procedure given that, usually, in a new term, one simply followed the drafting of the new PSP or the PSP to be followed.
She said it had not been an easy journey, but they were passionate about solving issues with departments. It was not a department knocking on the Provincial Treasury's door, nor the Provincial Treasury knocking on the door of a department. It was about working together to prevent, mitigate, or fix the problem. It came back to the quote of giving the tools to build the boat, but the department had to build it. If the Treasury built the boat, the department had to sail that boat. Problems had to be fixed together to make sure that they worked towards the future of diversifying the provincial revenue. There was a lot of work to do in that regard but that was for future Budget Committee meetings. She thanked the team from the Mayor of Cape Town and her team in the Ministry for their long hours and hard work in assisting her in the process.
The HOD also expressed her gratitude to all involved.
