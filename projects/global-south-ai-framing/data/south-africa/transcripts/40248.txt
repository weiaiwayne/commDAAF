Meeting Summary
Video
The Committee met with the Witwatersrand (Wits) University and related stakeholders to discuss the institution’s readiness for the current academic year and other related matters.
The presentations highlighted Wits’ commitment to academic excellence, financial stability, and student success while addressing key challenges.
The Vice-Chancellor and Council Chair emphasised rising student enrolments, financial aid efforts, and private-sector partnerships to support the "missing middle." The university remained financially stable, with a budget of R11.76 billion, but student debt and the National Student Financial Aid Scheme's (NSFAS's) inefficiencies remained concerns. Student services had expanded to support mental health, crisis assistance, and food security. Accommodation challenges persist, despite adding private-sector beds. Safety, gender-based harm protection, and leadership development were priorities. Research and innovation continued to grow, with investments in digital learning and artificial intelligence (AI)-driven education.
The Wits SRC raised issues of financial exclusion, housing shortages, and digital access, calling for expanded financial aid, rent regulation, and mental health services. Student protests reflected frustrations over financial struggles and the criminalisation of activism.
The university's unions acknowledged constructive engagement with management, but raised concerns about budget cuts, AI in teaching, and institutional transparency. They called for better staff support, fair employment practices, and improved working conditions.
The discussion focused on persistent challenges at the university, particularly regarding student debt, financial aid, accommodation, transformation, and stakeholder engagement. Concerns were raised about the institution's lack of proactive measures to address these issues, with Members questioning why certain problems recurred annually. There were inquiries about the accreditation of student housing, the financial exclusion of students due to fee blocks, and the rationale behind NSFAS defunding decisions. The dropout rate and student throughput were also highlighted as critical areas needing attention.
The Committee asked for a more detailed breakdown of student and staff demographics, especially regarding their distribution across faculties and senior management. Members were concerned about foreign academics holding key positions, and whether their qualifications were being adequately verified. They also questioned the university’s financial management, including the handling of R1.42 billion in student debt, the allocation of NSFAS funding, and the affordability of application and tuition fees. The issue of curriculum relevance was raised, with suggestions to assess whether certain degrees had become redundant in the evolving job market.
Further discussions touched on mental health services, stakeholder engagement, and labour relations, with calls for improved communication between the university and unions. The Committee questioned whether the institution had a transformation policy and how it dealt with bullying. The issue of the "missing middle" was a key focus, with Members questioning whether these students had been identified and if efforts were being made to secure alternative funding for them.
In response, the university leadership provided updates on student registration statistics, NSFAS funding challenges, and financial aid initiatives. It was confirmed that of the 13 executive team members, only one was not South African, and that there were currently no vacancies at the executive level. The Vice-Chancellor committed to meeting with faculty deans to discuss an extension of the registration deadline and to further engage with students on their concerns. The meeting concluded with a directive from the Chairperson for university leadership to take concrete steps to address these ongoing issues.
Meeting report
Opening comments
The Chairperson said that the Committee had sent the Wits University Council a letter inviting them to today’s meeting. In response, Wits management asked about the purpose of the meeting and said that the information requested was related to the internal operations of the university. The Chairperson did not appreciate the tone of the letter, which was signed by the Registrar. He said that this was part of the Committee's oversight. He had invited other stakeholders to corroborate what management was saying, and to ensure that all stakeholders were on the same page.
Dr Nobuhle Nkabane, Minister of Higher Education and Training (DHET), commented that there should be strong relationships between all university stakeholders.
She said that the National Student Financial Aid Scheme (NSFAS) was a major concern, causing instability, and the Department was trying to stabilise the institution. She said that on 18 February, a new board had been gazetted, and went on to introduce the new members of the board.
She said that the Act required that there be representation from student movements. She said there were no students that met the criteria, as the minimum requirement was a Master's degree.
The critical issue at Wits was student debt, which was R1.42 billion. The debt was accumulating interest, and she argued that the university should not be paying interest on that money.
The Chairperson noted that NSFAS was not at the meeting, and he was concerned that they had not availed themselves. He said that NSFAS could often have a working session with students in these forums.
Dr Nkabane explained that there was a plan to roll out an outreach programme, where NSFAS could engage with students at universities. She said that Deputy Minister Buti Manamela would be going to Wits on Monday to engage with the university and students regarding funding.
Wits University presentation
The presentation by the University of Witwatersrand (Wits) was presented by Mr Isaac Shongwe, Chair of Council; Prof Zeblon Vilakazi, Vice-Chancellor; Ms Carol Crosley, Registrar; Ms Maureen Manyama, Chief Financial Officer (CFO); and Mr Jerome September, Dean: Student Affairs.
Overview of Wits University
Ranked in the top 1% globally, positioning itself as a world-class institution.
Dedicated to nurturing talent, solving global problems, and driving social change.
Focused on research, innovation, governance, and student success to maintain its status as a leading university.
Teaching and learning
High-quality education that develops critical skills for societal impact.
Adoption of smart classrooms, artificial intelligence (AI)-driven learning, and blended teaching methods.
2025–2029 learning and teaching plan introduces multinational programmes, lifelong learning opportunities, and future-focused pedagogies.
Commitment to expanding access and success through technology and student-centred learning.
Governance and financial stability
Governed by the Higher Education Act, University Statute, and King IV Corporate Governance Code.
Clear decision-making structures ensure accountability and institutional integrity.
Financially stable, with an unmodified audit opinion for 2023/24, proving sound fiscal management.
Income grew to R11.76 billion (2023) from R10.67 billion (2022), with state subsidies and tuition fees each contributing 40%.
Student debt addressed through settlement discounts, discretionary funding, and structured payment options.
Enrolment and registration (2025)
Strong demand for higher education, with a structured and phased registration system.
Online registration supported by in-person services, ensuring accessibility for all students.
18 096 students utilised support services, including financial aid, enrolment verification, and accommodation assistance.
Student support and well-being
Comprehensive student success framework covering academic support, wellness, and personal development.
Mental health and career services, 24/7 crisis support, and external partnerships for well-being initiatives.
Food security programmes provide 1 000 meals daily and 3 500 meal packs monthly.
'End Period Poverty' initiative benefits 1 050 female students by providing sanitary products.
Accommodation and accessibility
5 758 on-campus beds, with 50% reserved for first years.
Partnerships with private providers had added 24 000 accredited beds, making housing more accessible.
Residence fees were frozen for 2025, except for Wits Junction (1.5% increase).
Budget meal option introduced at R30, to help students manage living costs.
Safety and security
24/7 campus security, including access control, surveillance, and after-hours escort services.
Collaboration with Braamfontein Improvement District to improve off-campus safety.
Anti-discrimination, transformation, and employment equity policies support a safe and inclusive campus.
Gender-based harm and protection
Dedicated Gender Equity Office (GEO) handling gender-based misconduct cases.
Clear policies prohibiting staff-student relationships, to prevent power imbalances.
Leading national efforts against gender-based violence (GBV), contributing to a national register of perpetrators and implementing recommendations from the Ministerial Task Team (MTT) report.
Strong external collaborations with the South African Police Service (SAPS), non-governmental organisations (NGOs) and government departments, to ensure a safer learning environment.
Student engagement and sport
"Gateway to Success" orientation programme integrates academics, wellness, and social activities.
First-year mentorship, student leadership training, and community-building initiatives.
New Wits sports complex set to open in 2026, expanding sports and recreation facilities.
The Wits Spirit game fosters a sense of university pride and student community engagement.
Research and innovation
Wits remains at the forefront of research tackling inequality, migration, and diversity.
Investments in healthcare research, homegrown energy solutions, and technological advancements.
The Wits Anglo American Digital Dome would be a digital research and innovation hub.
Key takeaways
Wits continues to be a global leader in education and research, ranked among the top 1% of universities.
A commitment to financial stability, with sound fiscal management and student debt relief initiatives.
Holistic student support systems, ensuring access to academic, financial, and wellness services.
Innovative learning approaches, including smart classrooms, AI integration, and blended learning.
Expanded access to affordable accommodation and food security initiatives.
Strong governance and gender equity policies, making Wits a safer and more inclusive institution.
Major investments in research, sport, and campus infrastructure, enhancing the student experience.
Conclusion
Wits remains dedicated to academic excellence, innovation, financial sustainability, and student success. By prioritising inclusivity, gender equity, research, and governance, the university continues to shape higher education in South Africa and beyond.

University Forum presentation
Mr Abe Pieterse, Head: Examinations and Graduation Office, Wits, presented a summary of the University Forum (UF) presentation to the Committee.
UF's role and functions
The UF advises the Council on key university matters, including:
Implementation of the Higher Education Act and national policy.
Race and gender equity policies.
Selection of senior management candidates.
Codes of conduct, mediation, and dispute resolution.
Fostering a culture of tolerance, respect, and academic excellence.
UF also provides written recommendations to the Council, with a system in place to review Council feedback.
The University Forum structure includes representatives from:
University leadership (Vice-Chancellor, Deputy Vice-Chancellors, Deans).
Academic and non-academic employees.
Students -- Students Representative Council (SRC) and other representative bodies.
Historically marginalised groups.
Donors, convocation, labour associations, and transformation advocates.
Thematic areas for reporting and engagement
The UF agenda focuses on key strategic areas:
Teaching and Learning.
Research, Innovation, and Postgraduate Affairs.
People Development and Institutional Culture.
Social Justice Initiatives.
Student Life and Experience.
Climate, Infrastructure, and Sustainability.
Readiness for the 2025 academic year
"Gateway to Success 2025" programme supports new students.
Postgraduate orientation programme to enhance academic transition.
Five-year learning and teaching plan ensures long-term academic excellence.
Wits Plus Pty (Ltd) supports flexible learning options.
Recommendations and future outlook
Strengthen engagement on strategic issues such as climate change and sustainability.
Enhance structured reporting and stakeholder feedback.
Prepare recommendations for future engagement with the Parliamentary Portfolio Committee.
Conclusion
The University Forum plays a crucial role in shaping policy, governance, and institutional development at Wits. Its structured approach ensures that key stakeholders are represented, and that the university remains responsive to national education priorities, transformation efforts, and student needs.

Wits SRC presentation
Ms Nombulelo Chiya, President: Student Representative Council (SRC), Wits presented on behalf of the student population. The Wits SRC report highlighted critical challenges facing students, including financial exclusion, accommodation struggles, digital access barriers, and mental health concerns. The report advocated expanded financial aid, policy reforms, and stronger collaboration between universities, government, and private stakeholders to create a more inclusive and equitable learning environment. She noted that many students were on a hunger strike to protest the lack of accommodation, as well as financial and registration blocks.
Key challenges identified
Financial challenges
NSFAS inefficiencies: Mid-year defunding, administrative delays, and debt preventing students from registering.
'Missing middle' exclusion: Students who do not qualify for NSFAS but cannot afford tuition face severe financial hardship.
Sponsorship gaps: Lack of sustained corporate funding for student bursaries.
Policy transformation
Financial exclusion thresholds: Students who were over R10 000 in debt were unable to register, leading to exclusion.
Expanding funding models: Advocating for tiered financial aid systems and low-interest student loans.
Tuition fee transparency: Need for predictable fee structures and affordability measures.
Student protests and brutality
Police violence: Excessive force used against student activists.
Criminalisation of activism: Disciplinary action and expulsions for protesting students.
Need for legal support: Establishing student advocacy protection frameworks.
Curriculum Transformation
Decolonising education: Inclusion of African knowledge and multilingual education.
Practical learning: Work-integrated learning, entrepreneurship education, and digital literacy.
Mental health awareness in curriculum: Stress management and resilience-building initiatives.
Accommodation and living costs
Housing shortage: Overcrowding, unaffordable rent, and student homelessness.
Regulating private rentals: Introducing price caps and tenant protections.
Emergency housing assistance: University support for displaced students.
Digital divide
Lack of devices: Many students cannot afford laptops or internet access.
Expensive data costs: Need for subsidised data bundles and university-funded connectivity.
Digital literacy training: Helping students to effectively use online learning platforms.
Social development and well-being
Student mentorship and career support: Expanding guidance services and employability programmes.
Mental health services: Free counselling, peer support groups, and mental health emergency funds.
Cultural inclusion: Expanding diversity programmes and social justice initiatives.
Recommendations
Expand NSFAS and 'Missing Middle' support through tiered financial aid and student-friendly loans.
Increase student housing options via public-private partnerships (PPPs) and stricter rental regulations.
Enhance mental health services with free counselling, stress management programmes, and crisis intervention funds.
Bridge the digital divide by providing subsidised laptops, Wi-Fi, and digital literacy training.
Protect student activism through legal frameworks, ensuring fair treatment and protest rights.
Transform curriculum to emphasise African knowledge, practical learning, and multilingual education.
Conclusion
The Wits SRC calls for urgent reforms to ensure accessible, inclusive, and financially sustainable education. Addressing financial barriers, housing insecurity, digital inequalities, and mental health needs was essential for student success and social transformation. The SRC urges collaboration between students, universities, government, and the private sector to create a higher education system that leaves no student behind.

Wits unions' presentation
The presentation was delivered by the Administration, Library and Technical Staff Association (ALTSA), the Academic Staff Association of Wits University (ASAWU), and the National Education, Health and Allied Workers' Union (NEHAWU). The presenters were Mr Marco Janse van Vuuren, Communications Officer, ASAWU; Ms Precious Biyela, President, ASAWU; Mr Billy Cebekhulu, President, ALTSA; Mr Tholane Khiba, Secretary, ALTSA; and Mr Molefi Makola, Acting Chairperson, NEHAWU.
The presentation focused on Wits University’s preparedness for the 2025 academic year and the challenges faced by its workers. It addressed institutional issues, union concerns, and recommendations to improve staff welfare and academic integrity.
Key points from the unions’ perspective were:
Union presence at Wits
Wits had 5 674 full-time and part-time employees.
8% of employees were unionised, represented by NEHAWU, ALTSA, and ASAWU.
Generally, there was positive engagement between management and unions, as well as good historical relations with student leadership.
ASAWU concerns:
Lack of guidelines on integrating AI (GenAI) in teaching.
Budget cuts affecting faculties and academic programmes.
No transparency on how student protests were handled, increasing tensions.
Institutional culture Issues:
Bullying and weak institutional support for staff.
Slow transformation, and prioritisation of subsidies over academic integrity.
Recommendations:
Commit to genuine transformation.
Reconsider the strategy that sidelines the humanities.
Promote an ethics-of-care approach in management.
NEHAWU concerns:
2000 members, about half of all professional, administrative and support staff.
Lack of protection from line managers for union representatives balancing jobs and union duties.
Employee Relations (ER) office functions like a "divorce court" rather than resolving staff-employer disputes.
Social Justice Office focuses on workplace bullying, but disproportionately targets junior black managers, leading to unfair dismissals.
Recruitment issues: Lack of succession planning and non-compliance with employment equity.
Leadership demographics: Overrepresentation of foreign nationals in senior positions, with only 11% black South African leadership.
Staff wellness issues:
High mortality rate among lower-grade employees (~2 funerals per month).
No structured substance abuse intervention for staff.
Mental health services available only for students, not staff.
Financial mismanagement:
Money wasted on hiring lawyers for internal disputes.
Fees Must Fall insourced workers being replaced by external contractors.
Suspicious contracting processes, with allegations of a "construction mafia."
Demands:
Unions should be involved in recruitment.
Investigate high project and maintenance costs.
Implement structured funeral and wellness support.
Reduce legal costs by requiring managers to represent the university at the Commission for Conciliation, Mediation and Arbitration (CCMA).
ALTSA concerns:
Readiness for 2025 depends on student funding (NSFAS and 'missing middle').
Labour peace and salary negotiations were crucial for institutional stability.
Need for transparency and trust between management and organised labour.
Challenges for workers:
Limited career development and upskilling opportunities.
Inconsistencies in labour relations and disciplinary procedures.
Recommendations:
Ensure consistency in employment law enforcement.
Foster trust between labour and management through transparency.
Address socio-economic challenges faced by workers to create a fair and inclusive academic environment.
Conclusion
The unions emphasised that Wits University’s ability to function effectively in 2025 depended on addressing these structural and operational challenges. They called for a commitment to transformation, fair labour practices, and improved staff welfare to build a more inclusive and ethical academic environment.

Discussion
Ms D van der Walt (DA) said this was not the first year these issues had arisen. The university should have been proactive, and not waited until registration closed before raising concerns.
She asked if the accommodation with available beds was accredited; inquired about the most important vacancies that needed to be filled; asked NSFAS what their motivation was for defunding students; and requested information on the dropout rate and throughput of the university.
Mr S Ngubane (MK) referred to slide 8 on student and staff demographics, and requested a more thorough breakdown of the demographics and their distribution across different levels and degrees.
He asked why Wits' financial year ended on 31 December, and had not been aligned with the financial year-end.
He expressed disappointment in the presentation by the institutional forum, stating that they had not addressed the challenges they were dealing with, or how they were assisting the university in its transformation efforts. He argued that the institution was failing in stakeholder engagement and needed to engage with stakeholders to close policy gaps.
He wanted to know how many foreigners held high academic positions, and said that there was an issue with the proper verification of foreign qualifications.
Ms K Khakhau (DA) noted that of the R1.42 billion in student debt, R32 million was outstanding from NSFAS. She wanted to know whether this amount was for the 2024 accrual period, or if it was for an extended accrual period, and if so, what that period was.
She asked how the university was dealing with the debt -- whether they were writing it off, or creating payment plans. She requested a breakdown of how many students who owed money had been financially excluded or allowed to graduate, along with a breakdown of their financial brackets.
She inquired whether the university had conducted a comprehensive analysis of where the 588 unregistered NSFAS students were, and the reasons they had not registered or returned. She asked that the Registrar provide the Committee with updated statistics, as the presentation contained statistics from 9 February.
She wanted to know how many students were not returning due to discrepancies between the credit bureau and the ITA34. She asked if there were plans to develop more student accommodation in the coming years, and if funds had been earmarked for this purpose. She praised the university for its approach to student safety and suggested collaboration with the city and metro police. She asked the Vice-Chancellor if he had explored ways of expanding and innovating digital education, noting that this could reduce education costs and the increasing need for accommodation.
Ms M Maseko (DA) asked how much money the university owed NSFAS, and what that money had originally been earmarked for. She noted a steady increase in student numbers, and asked how the university planned to accommodate the growing intake. She inquired whether Wits had engaged with private student accommodations.
She remarked on the evolving job market, and asked if there were redundant degrees. Had the university informed students about degrees with low employability prospects?
Regarding the 'missing middle,' she inquired whether the university could identify students falling into this category and engage with NSFAS to assist them.
Mr G Kgabo (ANC) expressed concern about the letter the Registrar had sent to the Committee, stating that he sensed an attitude in its tone. He believed it suggested resistance to transformation. He argued that the Health and Science Faculties made it difficult for black students to be admitted. He asked for a breakdown of the demographics of students admitted to these faculties.
He requested clarification on sources of university income beyond tuition fees. He asked how much students were charged for application fees, how much the university collected from this fee, and whether it was reinvested.
He requested a breakdown of the senior management demographics, and wanted to know if the university had a retirement policy.
He inquired whether the university had a transformation policy and how it handled bullying within the institution; what the rationale was behind requiring students to settle 50% of their historical debt before graduating; and what the university's strategy was to address classes not taking place.
Mr S Lonzi (EFF) argued that DHET needed to assist institutions, as these problems existed nationwide. He said that the Department was not holding universities accountable.
He asked for NSFAS's justification for defunding a student in the middle of the year; inquired how many students were unregistered due to fee blocks, stating that academically deserving students should be able to complete their studies; asked if beds were available and, if so, why students were not receiving them; and wondered if the university could create transient accommodation to house students until registration and funding were finalised.
He argued that the R100 application fee was unnecessary, and asked if the university could use technology to reduce human capital costs and waive the fee.
The Chairperson confirmed that according to slide 51 of the Wits presentation, there were 300 beds available.
Mr S Louw (ANC) also expressed dissatisfaction with the letter from the Registrar, stating that if the university had done everything correctly, student protests would not be happening.
He questioned the university's progress with its transformation plan and requested a demographic breakdown.
He asked how the university planned to address the 'missing middle' issue. He said that financial aid policies did not cater to students' needs and that the university needed to engage with stakeholders to address financial exclusion. What measures was the university taking to assist students at risk of being defunded by NSFAS, and how would they help students secure alternative funding?
He emphasised the need for mental health services and asked what plans the university had to improve them. He stressed the need for curriculum transformation and relevance, requesting a timeline for implementing curriculum changes. He highlighted housing challenges, and asked about Wits' strategies to increase affordable student housing and whether regulations would be introduced to protect students from exploitation. He inquired about the university's approach to improving relations and communication with unions, and whether the university had a labour forum.
The Chairperson commented that the Higher Education Act was inadequate for holding institutions accountable, and that amendments were being considered.
He criticised the Department for allowing the University of Limpopo to break its statute by permitting its chair to serve three consecutive terms.
He expressed concern about NSFAS representatives arriving late and missing the SRC presentation.
He inquired how much of Wits University's funding came from NSFAS.
He asked if the university could provide temporary accommodation to students awaiting registration, questioning Wits' accreditation process for accommodation and why student housing was so expensive.
University's response
Prof Vilakazi said of the 13 members of the executive team, only one was not South African, and there were no vacancies on the executive team. He explained that the VC and Registrar could serve two terms, with one term being five years long.
He said that every student who fell into the missing middle and was getting an accounting degree was covered because of a private donor. The same was true for engineering.
There were two hospitals that the university worked with, and that the Wits Health Consortium had been established to assist with reducing financial pressure. However, that money had been cut and the university now needed to find other forms of funding due to geopolitical issues.
He said that the university tried to keep residences close to the university.
Regarding student safety, he said that two years ago, the university had got the Johannesburg Metropolitan Police Department (JMPD) a permanent station near Wits. He said that JMPD could not deal with certain crimes, but it was in talks with the SAPS.
He said that two years ago the university had had to spend R80 million on diesel because of load-shedding, which was an unbudgeted cost.
Ms Manyama said that one of the challenges was receiving the defunded list. Ideally, they should get it in January. She said that 302 NSFAS students had not yet registered, with 8 657 being registered.
She explained that the other revenue stream from which the university received money was short courses. The university established a short course company in August last year, and therefore the revenue would be reduced. Student and staff accommodation also contributed to the university’s revenue.
She said they would respond to the questions on the debt threshold in writing.
She referred to the question of the R1.4 billion in student debt, and said the interest on that was about R10 million. They were looking to reverse the interest on a case-by-case basis. They did write off debt in line with the student fee policy, and only did it for accounting purposes.
On the interest earned from R450 million, she said that on their financial statements they made provisions for liabilities. Within these liabilities, they make a provision for NSFAS and earn interest on that. She would provide the Committee with a breakdown of where the interest was used.
The Chairperson asked the CFO when she would be meeting with the SRC. He said the CFO’s office must meet with the students by the end of the week.
Mr September said Wits had an accommodation policy linked to the DHET’s norms and standards. The rates were regulated, with NSFAS students paying the NSFAS rate. He said that if a student chose a more expensive room, there could be issues. He noted that the university did not increase its accommodation fees that year. He said that they had a mental health strategy which he would share with the Committee.
Ms Crosley emphasised that the registrar position was a contract one, and that a registrar could serve for two five-year terms. The chair of the Council could serve for three terms.
She said 37 894 students had been registered, but this could change in the next two weeks. She would send a breakdown of the trends from the last five years in terms of student registrations and dropouts to the Committee in writing.
She explained that the semester began on 10 February, and students were given two weeks to register. She would need to consult with the deans to decide if registration could be extended.
The Chairperson instructed the VC to meet with the deans to discuss late registration and extending the deadline.
Mr Vilakazi said he would meet with the deans the next day, and then engage with the students.
Mr Cebekhulu said there was corruption at the institution, and that when there were investigations, the outcomes were not shared.
Mr Makola explained that issues were brought to Parliament because the university was not engaging with Nehawu when they brought up issues. He said there was a mismanagement of funds.
A NSFAS representative apologised for their late arrival at the meeting. He said that the funded list was shared, and that it had been available to download from 12 January.
Mr Siphosethu Ndyumbu, Secretary General, Wits SRC, said communications between the university and the students had shut down. Management was not engaging with students on the ground, and would not discuss concessions.
He said that there was an issue with loan recipients. They received allowances and were told they had a bursary, but the funding was withdrawn in November.
He said that while fees had not gone up, students still faced high levels of debt, as accommodation was often more than the funding students received.
He referred to the security situation at the university, and said that students faced suspension when they protested.
Ms Chiya emphasised that there were a few issues that needed to be addressed. The first was allowing students who were academically deserving, to register. She asked that the concession be raised from R15 000 to R30 000 for students who were eligible to graduate. She said that the R15 000 cap was not enough, as most students had more debt.
Finally, she said that NSFAS needed to continue to fund a student while they were investigating whether they were eligible for a bursary.
Ms Nolwazi Gasa, DDG: Planning, Policy and Strategy, DHET, agreed that the Higher Education Act needed to be reviewed. She said that there needed to be more regulations when it came to accommodation across the country.
The Chairperson said that the Deputy Minister would be visiting Wits next week with NSFAS, and asked that the university engage with all stakeholders. He appealed to the VC to extend the registration period. He noted that this was affecting student’s mental health. He also expressed concern about the students who had not been given beds.
Mr Shongwe said that while the university wanted to assist the students, there were always constraints as to what they could do. The executive would reflect on what had been discussed about open communications to find a solution.
The Chairperson said that the Committee would send the questions that Members had not asked, and would set a date by which the university would have to respond.
The meeting was adjourned.
