Meeting Summary

Justice and Constitutional Development
The Committee met with the Auditor-General of South Africa (AGSA) to be briefed on the 2023/24 audit outcomes of the justice portfolio. A ministerial overview of the outcomes of the portfolio was also provided.
The Deputy Minister mentioned challenges such as rising crime and the impacts of COVID-19, stressing the need for a responsive justice system that ensures quick dispute resolution.
He reported on the Department of Justice and Constitutional Development's initiatives to enhance access to justice, including upgrading court facilities and implementing an online registration system. Despite these efforts, he acknowledged ongoing issues with service delivery and transparency within the legal sector and urged consideration of technological advancements, such as artificial intelligence, in the profession.
The Auditor-General's presentation outlined significant challenges for the Department in the 2023/24 financial year, including the late submission of the Annual Performance Plan, unmet targets, and persistent maintenance issues in magistrates' courts. Audit outcomes indicated regression in performance reporting and financial compliance, with unauthorised expenditure reaching R98 million. Major delays in the Integrated Justice System exacerbated case backlogs, particularly in the Western Cape and Gauteng. Recommendations focused on improving internal controls, increasing accountability, and enhancing oversight of financial reporting to avoid repeat audit findings.
The Committee expressed concerns regarding the reliability of performance metrics, budget constraints affecting service delivery, and the need for better integration within the justice system. It emphasised the importance of transparency and discussed strategies for leveraging technology in the legal field.
In the discussion, the Committee raised serious concerns about South Africa's crime statistics and the Department's significant case backlogs, urging collaboration with the Public Works Department for solutions. The Committee emphasised the ongoing issues with SITA and the impact of maintenance delays on gender-based violence, calling for a thorough investigation. The Committee criticised the lack of accountability in the State Attorney's office and urged better record-keeping and consequences for non-compliance with Auditor-General recommendations. It stressed the need to fill vacant positions and improve controls in the Master's Office. It highlighted the absence of backlog indicators in the Department's plans and called for proactive measures. The Committee requested updates on Magistrate appointments and maintenance issues, underlining the importance of accountability and collaboration to enhance the justice system's efficiency. The Committee raised concerns about the Auditor General's recommendations not being implemented and called for better stakeholder involvement and clarity on fund expenditures.
Meeting report
The Chairperson opened the meeting and stated that he would first call upon the Deputy Minister (DM) to provide a political overview and introduce the report. After that, the Auditor-General (AG) would present, followed by an opportunity for the Committee Members to interact with them.
Deputy Minister Overview
Mr Andries Nel, Deputy Minister of Justice and Constitutional Development, explained that this meeting coincides with the judiciary services committee meeting, which is scheduled from 7 to 16 October in Gauteng. Since the Minister could not attend, she asked him to present the political overview instead. The President, in his opening speech, provided clear guidance for the seventh administration. The Government of National Unity (GNU) is committed to working effectively on three key priorities: job creation and inclusive growth, reducing poverty and the cost of living, and building an ethical and developmental state.
He stressed that the Department of Justice and Constitutional Development (JCD) and the justice system have important roles in achieving these goals. He highlighted the need to avoid silo thinking and to ensure that growth and job creation involve everyone. He noted that a justice system that fails to deal with crime, fraud, and extortion is not effective. It should provide a reliable way to resolve disputes quickly.
Over the past five years, they have faced many challenges, including the ongoing effects of COVID-19, economic issues, cyber threats, and extreme weather. There has been a rise in crime, including organised crime and gang activities, which negatively affect their priorities. Internationally, he mentioned a rise in nationalism and right-wing politics in many countries, which threatens respect for multilateralism and the rule of law. He referred to the ongoing violence in Israel against the Palestinian people, which disregards international law. In 2023, the JCD and the Department of International Relations and Cooperation took this matter to the International Court of Justice (ICJ) to seek a ceasefire. He reflected on South Africa’s constitutional democracy, which aims to create a united, non-sexist, non-racial, democratic, and prosperous nation. As they celebrate the 30th anniversary of this democracy, it is important to remember issues like poverty, unemployment, racism, and inequality. To improve access to justice, the Department has upgraded 74 court facilities and ensured six courts are accessible for people with disabilities. Since December 2023, they have introduced an online registration system for deceased estates, allowing the public to access it from home. However, he acknowledged the need to improve service delivery. Through community engagement, the Department has conducted many awareness sessions to educate people about its services. This has led to fewer complaints and increased public confidence.
To promote fairness in the legal profession, the Department allocated 88% of its briefings to previously disadvantaged legal professionals, with 30% going to women. While progress has been made, there are still issues in the state attorney system regarding transparency and fairness. The Department is working on a legal sector charter with the Minister of Trade and Competition, which aims to improve practices within the legal sector. The fight against fraud and corruption is a top priority, and they are establishing special courts to address economic crimes. He urged the Committee to consider the impact of developments like artificial intelligence on the legal profession and to investigate these matters. The reports under discussion are crucial for transforming the judiciary system. He emphasised that access to justice is essential for everyone and that the judiciary has made advancements to assist this goal. The ICJ continues to use an online system in Gauteng and Pretoria, and improvements have been made since the pilot phase. The justice system relies on several departments, especially the State Information Technology Agency (SITA). He noted that modernising the court system is hindered by poor communication infrastructure, leading to inefficiencies. He highlighted the role of the South African Judicial Institute (SAJ Institute) in improving court effectiveness through training.
They have conducted 122 judicial education courses with over 3 000 participants. The journal published by the SAJ has been accredited, and he encouraged Committee Members to contribute articles. They have also started a Women Judges programme, which has successfully mentored participants who are now acting as judges. They plan to propose ways to make progress more transparent and to help prepare people for judicial roles. He concluded by mentioning that improved court efficiency contributes to their strategic goals, with most cases being resolved quickly. The Land Court Act was enacted in April 2024, allowing courts to expand their roles. For the 2023-2024 financial year, the Office of the Chief Justice (OCJ) allocated a budget of R2.578 billion, though cuts due to COVID-19 reduced this allocation. The OCJ has optimised its budget and achieved many of its targets, but budget cuts have impacted some priorities. He emphasised that budget cuts threaten the funding of the superior courts, an issue raised by the Chief Justice. While prioritising their budget, they need to mobilise resources. He expressed honour in presenting the annual report to the Committee and emphasised the importance of working together to enhance access to justice for all.
AGSA Briefing
Ms Tintswalo Masia, Deputy Business Leader, AGSA, said the Auditor-General of South Africa has a constitutional mandate and, as the supreme audit institution of South Africa, exists to strengthen the country's democracy by enabling oversight, accountability and governance in the public sector through auditing, thereby building public confidence. To be recognised by all our stakeholders as a relevant supreme audit institution that enhances public sector accountability. She handed over to her colleague to take Members through the detailed presentation.
Ms Aphendule Mantiyane, Senior Manager, AGSA, said the Department of Justice and Constitutional Development (DoJ&CD) faced significant challenges in the 2023/24 financial year. The Department failed to table its Annual Performance Plan (APP) by the 31 March 2023 deadline, a repeat finding from previous years. Overall, many key targets were not met, including those related to sexual offences courts, maintenance matters, and protection orders. However, the Department achieved 74 minor refurbishments of courts, surpassing the target of 70. Nevertheless, critical maintenance issues persist, with over 746 magistrates' courts needing attention, including the Pretoria Magistrate’s Court, which has remained unprepared since a fire in 2010.
In terms of audit outcomes, the Department experienced regression, particularly in the reliability of performance information and financial reporting. Audit concerns were related to material non-compliance, record-keeping, and the verification of performance achievements. Financially, the Department incurred unauthorised expenditure due to budget cuts, with contingent liabilities amounting to R98 million. Additionally, inefficient systems for collecting and verifying performance information, reliant on manual processes, contributed to unreliable reporting.
The administration programme showed limited progress, meeting the target of refurbishing 74 courts but failing to address significant maintenance issues. The Pretoria Magistrate’s Court remains in disrepair, highlighting the long-standing nature of these problems. Audit findings also pointed to the failure to table the APP on time and recurring issues with financial controls, including unauthorised expenditure and contingent liabilities. Court Services faced its own challenges, particularly in reducing case backlogs, which grew significantly in the Western Cape and Gauteng. The Integrated Justice System (IJS) development, which is vital for tracking cases across the criminal justice system, experienced delays, affecting the programme’s ability to meet targets related to sexual offence courts and the timely finalisation of maintenance matters. These issues further contributed to delays in service delivery and justice. The State Legal Services programme faced issues with staff turnover and delays in strategic litigation matters. Targets for civil legal aid access and strategic litigation were not met, reflecting capacity constraints and inefficiencies in collaborating with stakeholders. These delays and challenges hindered the programme’s ability to address social justice issues effectively. In National Prosecutions, progress was made but several targets were still unmet. The Specialised Commercial Crime Courts did not achieve the goal of establishing new courts in five provinces, and the conviction rate for cable theft cases did not improve as expected. The Special Investigating Unit (SIU) also faced challenges, failing to meet its target for issuing cases in the Special Tribunal. The Policy Development, Research, and Statistics programme showed some improvements, particularly in asset management compliance, though procurement, expenditure management, and consequence management remained problematic. The programme failed to meet several key targets, such as the review of the Small Claims Court system and the timely serving of domestic violence protection orders.
Financial performance saw an expenditure of R8.7 billion, slightly above the R8.1 billion budget. Unauthorised expenditure and contingent liabilities added to financial pressures. The financial management issues were compounded by entities such as the SIU and Public Protector of South Africa (PPSA), which regressed in their audit outcomes due to weak financial controls and a reliance on auditors to identify material misstatements. The year presented several challenges that impacted service delivery, most notably the failure to integrate departments fully into the Integrated Justice System. This lack of integration hindered effective case tracking and data sharing. Procurement delays also exacerbated the backlog of court cases, particularly in the Western Cape and Gauteng. However, the SIU's improvement in its audit outcome, despite revenue-related material adjustments, was a positive development, as was the continued clean audit outcomes for entities like Legal Aid South Africa (LASA), OCJ, and Justice Administered Fund (JAF), although these too faced challenges, such as delays in litigation and systemic investigations. In response to these issues, recommendations include improving internal controls, particularly in performance information reporting and procurement processes. There must be greater accountability and consequence management to ensure corrective action plans are effectively implemented. The Portfolio Committee should enhance monitoring of the IJS integration and backlog of cases to accelerate progress and improve service delivery. Finally, stronger oversight of financial reporting processes is essential to avoid repeat audit findings and material misstatements.

Discussion
Mr D Klopper (DA) thanked the Auditor-General (AG) for the work but highlighted the many worries the Department faces. He pointed out the ongoing crisis in South Africa, referencing 87 murders per day, which totals 30 000 a year; 150 rapes daily, equating to 50 000 a year; and 595 assaults, leading to over 200 000 incidents annually. He stressed that South Africa is a country at peace, yet these statistics should raise alarms for the Department.
He cited the report, noting 670 000 postponements, which equate to 770 000 delays. He expressed concern about how the Department plans to address these backlogs, particularly as new cases continue to emerge. He congratulated the Department for its progress in court but mentioned the remaining 400 cases that still require attention. He encouraged the Ministers to collaborate with the Department of Public Works and Infrastructure to find solutions for maintaining standards, as the backlog statistics are alarming. He noted that the figures reported do not reflect the true extent of violence, with research indicating that 40% of women in South Africa may experience rape in their lifetime. This unsettling statistic contributes to under-reporting of such crimes.
He discussed the culture shift approach by the AG, expressing concern that although money is being spent, many projects fail to be implemented. He questioned the focus of spending, emphasising that the effectiveness of projects should be a priority. Lastly, he addressed issues related to gender, age, and disability in appointments, highlighting repeated instances of unauthorised expenditure across Departments. He suggested implementing background checks to avoid hiring individuals involved in corruption. He called for a focus on employing competent individuals who genuinely care about serving South African citizens, rather than those who prioritise their own financial gain.
Ms H Denner (FF+) thanked the Auditor-General (AG) for the report, describing it as one of the excellent reports. She noted that this is a useful tool not only for development but also for the Committee to ensure accountability of the Department and its entities. She emphasised that next year, the Committee must not still be facing the same problems and findings regarding SITA, which has been a consistent issue across several departments. She acknowledged reports from the Office of the Chief Justice about deviations concerning SITA. She expressed understanding that departments and entities deviate from SITA due to challenges in completing their tasks. She highlighted that SITA is hindering government work, stating that the Committee cannot continue to hear year after year that SITA is problematic, slow with progress, and ineffective in implementing ICT within the Department. She expressed agreement with the Deputy Minister's call for focused efforts, noting that Public Works has also been a persistent issue. She stated that everyone, including the AG and the staff, recognises that Public Works and court facilities present ongoing problems that contribute to wider issues. Another significant concern raised was the impact of maintenance delays on gender-based violence (GBV). She argued that people cannot adequately care for their children if maintenance issues remain unresolved. She suggested a thorough investigation into maintenance to identify the root causes and determine potential solutions.
Regarding the Master's Office, she noted that the AG addressed related problems and called for more regular updates on the development of the turnaround strategy. She also requested the Department to provide a timeline for the Integrated Justice System (IJS). She concluded by stating that the AG mentioned case integration on certain matters, which warrants further feedback and a clear timeline.
Mr S Nomvalo (MKP) said he takes an unpopular position and will not join those giving credit where it is not due. He explained that the aim is to conduct oversight and be factual and scientific when discussing issues. He noted that the Deputy Minister praised the entities under the Department of Justice and Constitutional Development, claiming there are no serious problems affecting the people of South Africa. He highlighted a major issue with the backlog in the State Attorney’s office, saying the Department has not provided a clear plan to tackle this. Despite the backlog, the State Attorney is taking on work for which it lacks experience, especially regarding the Road Accident Fund (RAF).
He mentioned that many cases are being handled by people who do not know how to manage them, making the situation worse. He suggested that the Department should consider reorganising its Litigation Department to separate the merits and quantum areas. He stated that many cases could be identified early as not worth taking to court, which would help reduce unnecessary costs. He stressed that this change is important to prevent wasting money on ineffective legal actions.
Regarding the Auditor-General (AG)'s findings, he asked what steps had been taken to address problems with record-keeping. He questioned whether anyone has been held accountable for not keeping records for the required five years. He argued that without accountability, the same issues will likely continue. He also expressed concern about the AG's recommendations. He said that if entities do not follow these recommendations, there should be consequences. It is essential to ensure that those who fail to comply are held responsible, as this promotes a culture of accountability. Lastly, he noted the need for better access to justice, mentioning that people often face difficulties when trying to obtain necessary documents from Magistrate Centres. He called for a simpler system to make these processes easier and ensure that all citizens can access justice efficiently.
Ms N Motaung (ANC) said that the high number of backlog cases is long overdue for attention. She appealed to the Department to come up with a plan to address the seriousness of the backlog cases.
Secondly, regarding the issues in the Master's office, she suggested that a system should be developed to implement proper controls, as this office is crucial for service delivery, and we cannot afford to gamble with our people's needs. Lastly, she mentioned the filling of vacant posts and funding, stating that we should minimise this issue. She noted that there are many funded vacancies, especially in Senior Management positions, that are not being filled. She emphasised the importance of filling these vacancies to reduce the number of acting positions within the Department. She concluded by saying she would save her final point for the next meeting with the Department.
Adv B Mkhwebane (MKP) said that in the presentation, there were no backlog indicators in the Annual Performance Plan (APP). She expressed concern that if the Department did not create a plan, the same issues would arise next year. The Department is moving slowly with the Amendment Bill and the rationalisation of the Courts. She highlighted the need for the Department to be held accountable, especially regarding the funding of lower Courts, including the Magistrate Courts. She suggested that quarterly updates be required to address backlogs linked to over 600 postponements. She questioned whether any assessment had been made regarding minor cases, excluding serious crimes like rape, assault, and murder. She proposed that strategies should focus on resolving cases quickly rather than allowing them to pile up, as socio-economic issues often drive certain crimes. She asked whether to keep individuals in jail due to postponements or to find other solutions to prevent repeat offences.
She also raised concerns about payments to previously disadvantaged legal professionals, noting that this had not led to a proportional increase in experienced professionals. Concerns were raised about State Attorneys managing too many cases, with one attorney in Mpumalanga handling over 1 000 cases.
Regarding the Master's Office, she questioned how citizens are treated, citing potential collusion between lawyers and officials. She pointed out issues where vulnerable individuals are forced to seek legal help unnecessarily. She highlighted delays in finalising distribution accounts and the lack of response to public complaints, questioning the effectiveness of outreach efforts in addressing service delivery issues. She noted that the National Prosecuting Authority (NPA) was excluded from discussions about accountability, stating that issues of money laundering, corruption, and fraud should be straightforward for experienced prosecutors. Lastly, she emphasised the need for the Special Investigating Unit (SIU) to work closely with the Committee to improve the effectiveness of the justice system. Concerns about audit outcomes and the lack of clean audits were raised, questioning which sections of the Public Finance Management Act (PFMA) were being violated and what accountability measures were in place to address recurring issues.
The Chairperson said the legal sector code had been signed, highlighting the long effort since the 6th administration and emphasising the need to celebrate this achievement. On the topic of the appointment of Magistrates, he asked about the time-frames and whether guidelines were being amended, requesting a briefing for the Committee on this matter. The Auditor-General (AG) raised concerns about court maintenance in the Magistrate Courts, stating that issues such as energy failures and water shortages were significant problems. The Department responded that it was not solely responsible for these issues. The Auditor General also mentioned ongoing debates about performance targets, indicating that some entities set low targets to improve perceived performance, while others set them too high. He inquired whether there was a monitoring tool to help guide entities and departments in setting realistic targets and understanding the factors that influence these targets.
Regarding the case backlog, he asked about the status of the task team established by the Department to address this issue after COVID-19. They sought clarity on whether the task team still existed, to whom it reported, and what the Department's plans were to tackle the backlog if the task team was no longer active. He emphasised the importance of addressing these issues to improve the legal sector's overall efficiency.
Responses
Deputy Minister Nel addressed the questions of crime and organised crime, stating that there should be no disagreement on the need for a united effort. He noted the shared responsibility among various Departments and the importance of collaboration with the judiciary. He acknowledged the challenges of case backlogs and emphasised that the Department plays a significant role in supporting the justice system and must be held accountable. He raised concerns about the perception of competence and integrity within the Department, asserting that it employs skilled officials. He mentioned ongoing collaboration with the Department of Public Works to address systemic issues affecting the justice system.
Regarding the Integrated Justice System (IJS), he stated that effective implementation goes beyond technology and requires a solid foundational architecture. In discussing delays in the Road Accident Fund (RAF) cases, he identified jurisdictional challenges and the conduct of legal practitioners as significant obstacles. He confirmed that discussions with relevant stakeholders, including the Minister of Transport, are ongoing to address these issues. Lastly, he touched on State Capture and the National Prosecuting Authority (NPA), indicating a willingness to engage in further discussions on these complex matters.
Adv Doctor Mashabane, Director-General, Department of Justice and Constitutional Development (DoJ & CD), said the RAF is not being done by the State Attorney, but 100 and something attorneys of the RAF are working with the State Attorney, which has affected the workload of the State Attorney officials. We are going to relinquished at it and say three years down the line it was the best decision but it must not be linked with what the CEO and the Minister of Transport are doing around that. But in part it was to help the state entity that was dealing with the litigation. Secondly, the issues of case backlog and the number of files on attorneys have been addressed with the help of the audit committee, and we discovered that those files are SAPS-related to unlawful arrests, 30% remain, and are mainly on the Department of Health and the Home Affairs. With the resources we have from Treasury there are funds to fill vacant posts in some key State Attorney's offices as well. The AG spoke about the overspending of 1% which was caused by the living adjustments.
With the issue of the backlog, we have agreed on the adjustments that we need to do now in October. We will find a way to include it, but if we do not, we will find a way most definitely in the financial year plan. However, in this disclaimer, we are just one of the many players in the whole ecosystem. In the backlogs, we have a breakdown with the office of the Chief Justice, which aligns with the contributing factors. In the infrastructure part is that 50 something percent is made up of electricity and during that time load shedding has played a critical role. We bought generators. The Bill that the Parliament has passed does not allow us to procure generators. The Public Works Department must come in. There is a part that relates to SITA where the Department is also dependent on it. Then we have the SAPS, who contribute to the case backlog, where they do not finish the investigation of the cases and so on. The question of the backlog is not easy - there is so much administration contributing to it. The issue of 150 on the Magistrate Courts, clerks can sign letters of authority of up to 150 000. We are trying to bring amendments to the law through Judiciary Matters Amendment Bill. We would like the support to make adjustments in support of the senior official or court manager, so that 150 000 be authorised.
The Chairperson asked that 150 of the threshold should be considered.
Adv Mashabane replied by saying that is what we want to propose to adjust that 150. But the challenge is that the people in the rural areas will undervalue the estate to avoid going to town, and we have a number of commented servants. In the consequences management you will see that we have dismissed close to over 100 officials. The integrated justice system is dependent on SITA, our main objective is to coordinate with other departments and those departments are not willing to spend and that is why Treasury has reduced the budget.
Ms Masia said that the accountability ecosystem includes various stakeholders, with the role of the Auditor-General (AG) being to identify issues and understand their root causes. A significant concern is inadequate record-keeping within departments, affecting performance evaluations. The Director-General (DG) has acknowledged the challenges arising from insufficient systems. While the AG identifies these issues, accountability is shared across the system, including the State Information Technology Agency (SITA) and the Portfolio Committee. The Portfolio Committee should investigate root causes and influences affecting systems and data management within the justice sector. Manual processes exacerbate existing problems, leading to persistent backlogs. The team acknowledges the need for further action and has made initial progress. Their tools will help unpack insights related to the failure to implement recommendations, which include addressing backlogs and enhancing accountability measures. It is important to clarify that the focus is on departmental issues rather than external entities.
Regarding the Master's Office, the team will revisit the turnaround times for responses in the next cycle. Their previous evaluation addressed fraud indicators rather than broader performance metrics. When assessing performance against targets, they collaborate with the Department of Planning, Monitoring and Evaluation (DPME) to ensure that targets are realistic and informed by historical data. It is vital to analyse the context before concluding that targets are unrealistic.
Mr Kruger (unidentified) said that was raised in the SIU which is correct. They had the same non-compliance in the same year, which affected the current following non-compliance. The issue was in the legal panel that they set up, which already had a problem in the legal criteria functionality. The reason why I am facing the SIU problem is because they are also having a problem on revenue which was the cut off issue. It seems like the issue has been raised in relation to SITA and State Attorneys is having an effect on the client departments. In the current year, the PPSA said they would disclose their expenditure, but they had not complied, which is the consequence management we did not see against the individuals who perpetrated their regular expenditure. It is a compliance with the National Treasury framework, which is clear, so a cause analysis is conducted at the specific level of those who have not completed and apply the consequence management. We did not see that in the current year, they subsequently did do this process, but unfortunately, in the financial year, it resulted in the non-compliance of the audit report.
The Chairperson said that to the AG, there was a question about the availability to the AG where there is incomplete compliance and incompetence, non-compliance on the findings and recommendations. In instances where there is incompleteness there was a non-compliance.
Ms Mantiyane replied by saying there is a complete disregard in our recommendations and yes we are empowered by the additional powers to apply the irregularities that occurred during the audit. However, in the instances of the audit, there are no instances where we can directly say in this regard, yes, there are elements and pieces that we have seen the audit committee members. We have seen the DG directly addressing the officials and giving them strict instructions to follow certain recommendations of the AGSA. The problem is with the grassroots; either some of these recommendations are not implemented constantly, which results in them not being effective as they should. There should be serious consequences, it cannot be business as usual and people keep on doing the same thing when the system is taking accountability, accept that we should have given you warning. However as the AGSA we will continue to monitor these outcomes of the implementation of these actions. Should we find out that they are addressed as they should be, we will take the powers.
Adv Mkhwebane said she would follow up on the issue with the NPA. She expressed doubt about whether all relevant parties, such as the Public Protector, SA Human Rights Commission (SAHRC), SIU, and the Master's Office, had been adequately involved. She requested further explanation, emphasising the need to see how funds were spent before their next meeting. She also mentioned that the Director-General (DG) indicated MPs should resolve certain questions themselves. The Deputy Minister (DM) reminded everyone of the issues with SITA and DPW, particularly regarding capacity constraints. She noted that while they are facing significant challenges, Parliament continues to implement budget cuts. This puts a serious strain on the government, as rejecting the budget affects crime-fighting efforts due to a lack of resources. She highlighted concerns about compliance with recommendations from the Public Protector and HRM, which could have been beneficial.
The Chairperson asked if there were any responses.
Ms Masia replied by saying that she would clarify the work done regarding the NPA. She offered to compile their findings and share them in writing with the Members before the next session. She acknowledged the non-compliance of various institutions and stated they would consider recommendations for improvement.
The Chairperson thanked the Members for attending and noted that those absent were engaged in JSC interviews. The meeting was then adjourned.
