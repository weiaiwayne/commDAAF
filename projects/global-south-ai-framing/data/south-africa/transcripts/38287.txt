Meeting Summary
Video
In a virtual meeting, the Committee received briefings from the Department of Communications and Digital Technologies (DCDT), Broadband Infraco (BBI), Sentech, the Universal Service and Access Agency of South Africa (USAASA) and the Universal Service and Access Fund (USAf), on their revised annual performance plans for 2023/24.
The DCDT has made some changes to its annual performance plans. These included completing all the processes for the White Paper on Audio and Audio-Visual Content Services and Online Safety; completing the analogue switch-off in four provinces; implementing and monitoring the digital and future skills strategy, and coordinating its gender-responsive annual performance plans.
BBI had revised its targets for the SA Connect project. Initially, 50 000 households had to be connected, but it had been reduced to 10 000 households. It had to sign a service-level agreement with Sentech to ensure speedy delivery of the project, which had been extended to a three-year period. There were supposed to be 9 000 hotspots, but this had been reduced to 3 000 hotspots.
The key performance indicators for Sentech had been reduced from 14 to 11. The two key performance indicators for the satellite launch readiness plan had been removed and a third key performance indicator for the finalised state-owned company (SOC) rationalisation had been removed and placed under strategic projects to be monitored. The key performance indicator for the financial outcome had been amended from R410m for earnings before interest, tax depreciation and amortisation, to R376m. The key performance indicators for the analogue switch-off had also been amended to switch off all transmitters with frequencies above 694MHz.
USAASA’s target included a roadmap in response to the national strategy plan on gender-based violence and femicide. USAf's targets included 80% verification of the broadcasting digital migration project at qualifying households.
The Competition Commission had adopted a two-pronged strategy for dealing with the Sentech vs the South African Broadcasting Corporation (SABC) tariff dispute. The two-pronged strategy focused on the investigation of the complaint and a mediation process which had been unsuccessful. The SABC alleged that the tariffs charged by Sentech for terrestrial transmission or signal distribution services were excessive and in contravention of section 8(1)(a) of the Competition Act. There had been a two-step inquiry to determine if there was contravention in terms of section 8(1)(a). It had to be determined whether the price charged was higher than a competitive price and whether the difference between the price charged and the competitive price was unreasonable. The Competition Commission had conducted an excessive pricing assessment in the form of price-cost tests for the period 2018 to 2021, and was of the view that its annual tariff increases had been low and in line with inflation. In light of the above, the Commission was of the view that an excessive price case for terrestrial transmission against Sentech could not be sustained.
The Committee requested the details of the day-to-day operations of Sentech and the SABC. Concerns were raised about the financial statements provided, and the fact that the Competition Commission had had to disaggregate the figures themselves. The dispute matter had been handled with the Commission, and the way forward was now to ensure that the SABC was sustainable.
Concerns were raised about the USAASA not meeting targets, the debt that the SABC owed Sentech, BBI reducing targets, and the impact of load-shedding on the entities. The Committee asked about the impact of the severe floods on the broadcasting digital migration project, the core business of USAASA, and the lack of consequence management for collapsing entities. Members sought clarity on whether USAASA was disestablished or established, and on the dispute between BBI and Eskom.
The Government Communication and Information System also briefed the Committee on the introduction of the Council on the Marketing, Advertising and Communication (MAC) Charter and the written submissions received on the SABC SOC Ltd Bill [B32-2023]. The public hearings would take place on 22-24 February.

Minister's opening remarks
Mr Mondli Gungubele, Minister of Communications and Digital Technologies, said that the DCDT and its entities had been called upon to deal with the annual performance plans (APP) for 2023/24. The DCDT had taken a realignment approach that dealt with three factors insofar as the APPs were concerned:
The first factor was the fiscal revision from National Treasury, which Cabinet had adopted. This helped to wisely determine targets both on the scale and the timing thereof.
The second factor was that the APP had to be realigned with the Minister’s performance agreement.
The third factor was the disaster as it emanates from the Department of Planning, Monitoring and Evaluation (DPME).
Around May/July 2023, the DCDT had been driven by several things. The first one was the centrality of connectivity. Connectivity has become the most critical thing globally because of its ability to allow human beings to have access to online activities. This was the central part of the platform of the future and the capabilities that people required. People who make use of connectivity and online access should be skilled meaningfully. The DCDT had accepted that young people were mainly affected by economic exclusion. The digital economy was the economy for young people and women. This was where the focus lay. Small, medium and micro enterprises (SMMEs) were also key. It had become very evident that unless SMMEs participated in the digital economy, there was a risk of perpetuating market concentration. There was a risk of perpetuating inequalities to the extent that only those who were advantaged got access.
It was against this background that the global community emphasised pillars that would enable SMMEs in the pursuit of connectivity. The pillars consisted of a safe and secure connection, affordability, benefits such as data-freeness, advantages of online life, and training of employees. Businesses should be reliable in terms of the market being safe to interact with. Connectivity was primarily to access online for innovation, uploading, downloading, and giving access to the poorest people to access library resources from their gadgets.
All of these issues have lifted the issue of connectivity. There was a project called the Giga project, which was about connecting schools around the world. The DCDT was looking at working with that programme because it was a major programme that dealt with the issues of inequality. The Committee should consider a South Africa where all schools have connectivity. From a foundational point of view, over 1 600 had been connected in Mount Ayliff and an additional 500 in Kokstad. Some poor people felt the revival and the re-establishment of new hope for the future. He had connected with a poor family where children were very excited to use their gadgets. The children in that community had to travel to libraries at night. These people could now access the library for all kinds of books at home. A similar project would be implemented in places like Limpopo.
Some people in informal settlements had to travel 900 kilometres from the Eastern Cape or 300 kilometers from Polokwane and various other provinces to the cities. This cost money, and some people may not have access to funds due to unemployment, so connectivity was needed for people from poor communities to access the online world without having to use money to travel. These people could then be able to discuss employment online or sell their products. In Mount Ayliff, someone was selling pieces of chicken purely due to accessibility and the reduction of data costs. Connectivity across all societies, irrespective of history, was an opportune moment South Africa should never fail to take advantage of. These were the key objectives driving the DCDT to be able to commit to 8 000 Wi-Fi hotspots over three years. The budget had been revised so these numbers had changed. He said that the team would speak more about this.
A decision had been taken for the Universal Service and Access Agency of South Africa (USAASA) to continue operating normally until legislation was put in place for its disestablishment. The Universal Service and Access Fund (USAf) were not directed by the DCDT kit, but that of National Treasury (NT). There have been challenges and some delays. Overall, the DCDT had tried to turn around the speed and pace of how work was done. The performance to date and the projected performance towards the end of the year would demonstrate that.
He addressed the issue of the tariff dispute between Sentech and the South African Broadcasting Corporation (SABC). The rate at which the SABC still owed Sentech was higher than the rate at which it paid Sentech. This means that every given time, the net liability was higher than that which the SABC would have contributed. SABC was contributing no less than R20m a month, but the rate of the debt was actually at a scale higher. Sentech and SABC had engaged on this matter, with both having respective working committees. The matter had been taken to the Competition Commission due to non-satisfaction over the tariffs. The Competition Commission had determined by issuing inflationary measures, that there was nothing wrong with the tariffs. The SABC had decided to take the matter to the Competition Tribunal, with the results not yet being finalised. The SABC was trying to extricate itself from the challenges threatening its status as a going concern. The SABC would present and communicate its revenue strategy and turnaround plans to the DCDT. The amount of money that SABC owes Sentech had serious implications in terms of Sentech doing its job. The DCDT was aware of this and was engaging with National Treasury to see what could be done. The country could not do without an entity such as the SABC during an election year. He had spoken to the current management of the SABC and was relieved, as there were medium and long-term revenue intentions. The DCDT hoped that the SABC would be able to deliver.

Broadband Infraco (BBI) on its 2023/24 annual performance plan
Mr Gift Zowa, Acting Chief Executive Officer (CEO), BBI, said that in recent years BBI, had been significantly constrained by the inability to access network investment, which impacted the network performance and service availability, significantly impacting revenue generation and cash flow. The cash flow constraints inhibited its ability to meet financial obligations to the major fibre and servitude suppliers (Eskom and Transnet), including the transmission equipment supplier. Since the 2021-22 financial year, several customers have been lost due to the cancellation of the contracts and non-renewal of expired contracts by Eskom. The matter had been exacerbated by the refusal to repair fibre breaks and ultimately disconnect fibre links, which had had a catastrophic impact on the ability of BBI to provide customer solutions, and network availability, which had increased rebate payments to customers such as SITA, and sales had plummeted.
The Eskom disconnection has impacted three financial sustainability targets:
The target for new sales contracts signed has been revised from R100 million to R50 million.
The planned 15% year-on-year revenue growth was revised to 8%.
The R23m operating loss decline was revised to a R12m operating profit decline.
Two SA Connect targets have been revised:
50 000 households to be connected had been revised to 10 000.
Wi-Fi hotspots had been revised to 300, including the Broadband Access Fund.
Mr Gowa said the SA Connect key performance indicator (KPI) for SA Connect Phase 2 was originally established with specific targets, aligned with the project's scheduled delivery period spanning two years, starting from the 2023/2024 financial year. The DCDT had requested the company develop a commercialisation model to ensure SA Connect's sustainability, continuously generate revenue, and use the Telkom infrastructure during the first quarter of the financial year. BBI had also been requested to sign a service-level agreement with Sentech to ensure the speedy delivery of the project. Additionally, the project fund disbursement was initially planned to commence in April 2023. However, there have been changes in the project's delivery timeline, which has now been extended to cover a three-year period starting in the 2023/2024 financial year, and the project funding disbursement was now expected to commence in September 2023. Competition Commission on the outcomes of the Sentech vs SABC tariff dispute
Ms Doris Tshepe, Commissioner, Office of the Competition Commission, said that in dealing with this matter, the Commission had adopted a two-pronged strategy. The two-pronged strategy had focused on the investigation of the complaint and a mediation process, which had been unsuccessful.
The complainant was the SABC, and the respondent was Sentech. The SABC had alleged that the tariffs charged by Sentech for terrestrial transmission or signal distribution services were excessive and in contravention of section 8(1)(a) of the Competition Act. The Competition Act sets out several factors that need to be considered when assessing excessive pricing, such as prices for goods or services, the price-cost margin and more. The Competition Commission had referred to case law, where it was held that a two-step inquiry should be undertaken to determine if there was contravention in terms of section 8(1)(a). It should be determined whether the price charged was higher than a competitive price and whether the difference between the price charged and the competitive price was unreasonable.
The Competition Commission had conducted an excessive pricing assessment in the form of price-cost tests. The price-cost tests assessment focused on Sentech’s financial period from 2017 to 2021. The price-cost test covered the overall business and terrestrial transmission services provided by Sentech. The total revenues from its operations were used as prices. In assessing excessive pricing, one needs to consider the economic costs incurred. Economic costs include both operational costs (the cost of production) and capital costs (a return that could be earned on capital invested). Sentech was dominant in this terrestrial transmission services market.
The mark-up on economic costs -- capital plus operating costs -- under the two scenarios was negative, less than -9%. Low economic mark-ups, which were below the 20% benchmark in the Sasol matter, could not sustain an excessive pricing case.
An assessment of Sentech’s tariffs for radio terrestrial transmission services revealed that between the financial years of 2018 and 2021, Sentech’s prices had increased by low percentages of between 3.7% and 5.37%. An assessment of Sentech’s tariffs for TV terrestrial transmission services revealed that between the financial years of 2018 and 2021, its prices had increased by low percentages of between 3.37% and 4.85%. The Competition Commission was of the view that these annual tariff increases were low and in line with inflation. In light of the above, the Competition Commission was of the view that an excessive price case for terrestrial transmission against Sentech could not be sustained, given the above low tariff increases.

Mr Zowa said the dispute with Eskom had gone on for too long. The disconnection had happened more than a year and a half ago. BBI had done everything possible to find mechanisms and ways to resuscitate the network. It had made use of third parties, which had now become more expensive. In the process, it had lost several clients, especially wholesale clients. This led to the financial regression in revenue, sales, and profitability. An agreement had been reached and signed with Eskom to reconnect BBI in the next few days. He was in direct contact with the Group CEO of Eskom and was monitoring the situation. BBI had to pay a certain amount of money before the reconnection. The money had been paid the day before, and the reconnection would happen in the next few days. As soon as that was done, BBI would resuscitate the network and hopefully recover the lost clients. BBI would enter into an intergovernmental fiscal review arrangement (IGFRA) negotiation with Eskom to prevent this from happening in the future. This should assist with the perpetual sustainability of BBI and allow the focus to shift to the connectivity of the underserved and rural areas.
Sentech
Ms Mudau welcomed the comments made by Ms Mazzone that Sentech should have provided disaggregated information to the Competition Commission. There had been additional load-shedding initiatives at the department and entity levels. There was a programme to convert some of the sites into renewables. The net impact of that was that there would be operational cost savings. At this stage, it was capital-intensive. This was in line with quarterly reports, and the cost-saving targets for mitigating the impact of load-shedding would start reflecting when some renewable strategies were implemented.
Sentech had provided a revised APP, hence the impact of the SABC non-payment was not reflected. The revised APP had to do with Sentech’s earnings before interest, depreciation, tax and amortisation (EBITDA), which was the operating profit which included the element of the provision of debt, which had to do with accounting for the non-payment by the SABC. The financial impact was reflected mainly in the reserves, which were reducing at an accelerated rate. This was not included in the presentation due to the limitations. It was only the EBITDA that had been reduced by approximately 8%, which had a compounding effect.
Mr Leshope said that on 1 December 2023, the Regulator had published a notice of intention to conduct an inquiry into the impact of load-shedding across the sector. Sentech was responding to the issue and believed that the Regulator would guide the Minister with the next action steps and the way forward. This process would take time and would certainly fall outside the current financial year. In the next financial year, there would be processes, structures and guidelines for handling the issue of load-shedding across the sector.
The target for satellites was not being dropped. It was merely moving to the next financial year due to external factors causing a delay. It was spending around R300m just on satellites. Some technologies and satellites had to be provided for by parties outside of South Africa. If this had to be quantified across other sectors, billions of rands would flow outside the country without control. South Africa was reliant on external parties to provide these services. In times of political tensions, it could be a little exposed. The benefit was to strengthen self-reliance. Sentech needed to strengthen its technology capability to a point where it was self-reliant and could de-risk itself from external dependencies.
The floods had not been as severe in the Western and Eastern Cape. Some of the infrastructure was damaged, but there was a disaster management intervention plan to assist the site in the Western Cape. There was transport for diesel, where it was necessary before the services were affected.
There were operational and sustainability impacts for the current situation with the SABC. Signal distribution and broadcasting were inherently costly. In 2020, the Committee had been briefed on the cost drivers for tariffs. These included energy and satellites, which were inherently high for the provision of the service approach. Sentech was looking at a way forward and appreciated the comment by the Committee to do so. There would be assistance from the SABC for the way forward. Sentech had tried its best to show that its costs were not exorbitant and that the tariffs were not unfair, unreal or unreasonable. Sentech had, for a very long time, carried out the operations of the SABC, which was now becoming a serious challenge for cost containment.
USAASA/USAf
Ms Madikizela said that the presentation focused mainly on the revised APP for both USAASA and USAf. On 20 February, USAASA would brief the Committee on its performance for quarters one and two. The current APP achievement was 60% (USAASA) and 80% (USAf). From a performance point of view, the entity had improved. USAASA was the administrator. It took care of the employees, provenance and reputation of the entity. USAf took care of the actual mandate and the deliverables. Both USAASA and USAf had separate APPs which had been reflected throughout the presentation. USAASA dealt with the social and administrative aspects of the mandate, whereas USAf dealt with the core mandate such as the deliverables of the broadcasting mandate. There was the BDM project and the connectivity project for broadband. She thought it was important to make this separation. In light of the disestablishment, the Minister lifted the moratorium. USAASA had been given the ability to source the executives, which would bring about stability. The legislation was underway, and USAASA had been given three years' leeway. This was sufficient time to make the entity run, and to give it support to run effectively.
Insofar as load-shedding was concerned, there had been several engagements with service providers such as Sentech and BBI. Load-shedding had had an impact on projects from a social point of view. It also had an impact from an operational point of view because of the delays and deployment of projects, which were part of the core mandate. There had been discussions, but an in-depth analysis was needed, as well as the costs involved. The impact was directly on the installations because it could not be determined whether an installation was successful or not during load-shedding. She assured the Committee that the entity was on top of this particular matter.
SABC
Mr Khathutshelo Ramukumba, Chairperson of the SABC Board, said that their new Group CEO had started on 1 February, and would introduce herself when she came in with responses. There had been additional costs for backup power due to load-shedding. The backup power was there to ensure that the SABC remained on air on all the platforms, regardless of the load-shedding schedule. The bigger impact of load-shedding was on the revenue side of things, and not so much on the cost side of things. Load-shedding meant that the SABC lost viewership numbers, which directly impacted advertising revenue.
Notwithstanding the view of the Competition Commission on the tariff dispute, the SABC believed it had an additional responsibility to take this matter further to the Competition Tribunal. It was believed there were merits regarding the concerns about the methodology and approach the Competition Commission had adopted. These concerns were aligned with the lack of direct costs related to the provision of the transmission costs for the SABC. When applying the same data that the Competition Commission would have applied in calculating what the cost should be, there would have been different outcomes. The SABC was of the view that a prospect existed to reach out to the Competition Tribunal for the concerns that had been presented. It would present to the Competition Tribunal the methodology and approach the Competition Commission applied, and should be allowed to exhaust this process. The SABC agreed with the sentiments of the Committee and Sentech, and was grateful for the continued support from Sentech despite the challenges.
The resolution of the tariffs would not resolve the problems that the SABC faces. There could be some notable contributions to a better cost structure for the SABC. The SABC acknowledged that it must devise interventions that would enable it to trade itself out of the current hole that it was in. The SABC has been going through the process of applying itself to the development of the five-year strategy. The implementation should assist the SABC in its turnaround. The process had been concluded, and feedback was received from the DCDT. The comments would be incorporated and presented to the Committee at some point. Efforts need to be made internally, but intervention from government through the National Treasury was also needed. The SABC was doing everything in its power internally to improve the fortunes of the entity. He recalled that the Committee had been briefed on the short-term intervention. The SABC was forecasting that at least half of the loss of the R1.1b that it inherited would be settled by the end of the 2023/24 financial year. The SABC would continue with its internal efforts to turnaround the fortunes of the SABC.
Ms Nomsa Chabeli, Group CEO, SABC, said that Mr Ramukumba had covered the key issues. The SABC believed there was still an opportunity to go to the Competition Tribunal to get some clarity on certain issues that still needed to be addressed. The issue of load-shedding was very critical to the SABC. The most important one was the impact on the audience. There had been a reduction in the audiences, which subsequently impacted the SABC’s ability to make revenue from advertising. A document had been prepared and would be submitted accordingly.
Mr Lungile Binza, Group Executive: Technology, SABC, said that the corporation would continue to try and get access to the Competition Tribunal. The Competition Commission had not considered the SABC’s own analysis. The gross profit margin from Sentech on its operational centres was around 55%, and the net profit margin was around 10%. There was a lot of overhead or head office costs that the SABC was paying. The SABC objected to this, and direct access to the Competition Tribunal would help with this. When Sentech was decoupled from the SABC in 1996, there had been an understanding that Sentech would charge the SABC a cost-effective tariff. The Competition Commission had not stated what the tariff was -- it said only that the charge was fair. The SABC believed that the current tariffs were not fair. The SABC had been presenting to ICASA on this matter, not just for the SABC itself, but also for the industry affected by excessive pricing from Sentech. There was a unanimity on the fact that prices and the tariffs charged were excessive, hence the assumption that the Competition Tribunal might have a different view from the Competition Commission.
When there was stage six load-shedding, the SABC lost between 20-30% of its audience, and this compromised the entity because audiences were like currency for broadcasters. There were platforms such as the SABC Plus to provide repeats of programmes. However, this was sometimes not enough for the audiences who could not afford data for the repeat of programmes. There had been another request from ICASA to submit a report on the impact of load-shedding on the SABC. This report would be submitted.
Minister's response
Minister Gungubele said that regression was a problem insofar as the urgency for connectivity was concerned. However, by the end of the year, there would have been more connections than in any other year. From a technical point of view, it was an improvement but at a reduced rate. The DCDT hoped to do more by the end of the financial year.
The disestablishment of USAASA had some serious legislative implications. It would take some time and there had to be certainty that USAASA would continue to work normally. There should be a board, and employees should be hired so that it could be valuable. Confusion arising from the legislative situation was very costly.
Insofar as the SABC and Sentech’s tariff dispute was concerned, the proposal was to allow the SABC to approach the Competition Tribunal. The SABC accepted that it was indebted to Sentech. This matter had to be resolved. Insofar as the satellites were concerned, Sentech was trying to reduce the costs and to improve both its technological and data sovereignty.
Follow-up discussion
The Chairperson said that the presentations had focused on the revision of the APPs due to budget cuts, the alignment with the performance planning, and the release of funds in some instances. The Committee needed to understand what the impact was on the lives of the people on the ground. There had been mention made of the BDM projects and how households had changed in spaces such as Kokstad and Limpopo. For example, if it was said that there was a performance rate of 76%, what did this mean? Some of this information would be helpful for the Committee when oversight was done. The Committee was now aware of the Wi-Fi hotspots to be implemented to change the lives of the people on the ground. This information would be important not only for the Committee, but also for those people. For instance, how would it be implemented and how would it change their lives? It was important for the Committee to see the background of where things were, and how things were changing in a particular space. This helped to ensure that a change would happen in that space by the end of the financial year. He said that Ms Mazzone assumed that these were the annual reports, but this was just the revision of the APP. This background information would have been important for proper interpretation.
The decision of the SABC to approach the Competition Tribunal had to be respected. It would take some time for the Tribunal to reach an outcome that might favour the SABC. As a shareholder, it was important to know what the DCDT would do to help the situation. The judgment may not come now because it took a long time for the Competition Commission to come to a conclusion. There should be a sense of hope that the DCDT and the Ministry were in the process of intervening to resolve the dispute. The outcome may even come during the election time. It was not that it was not a priority, but it also depended on the matters that were before them. The problem was here now, and the way forward was to determine an intervention to resolve the matter, since both entities fell under this portfolio. Mr Binza had explained the situation from the beginning of the agreed costs, when the separation had taken place. There now seemed to be a disagreement because the previous agreement had not been kept over time as the costs changed. Intervention was needed without undermining the SABC case. The Competition Commission had been clear that it was reluctant to deal with the matter. Although it was now going to the Competition Tribunal, it was important to emphasise that there were other activities from the DCDT to try and resolve this matter.
The Committee was happy with USAASA's explanation about trying to build the capacity of the entity. The law enabled USAASA to proceed with capacitation while the disestablishment had not yet been enacted. There was a mandate to fulfill under the current legislation. There was uncertainty about what needed to happen, but the entity must also do what it was supposed to do now.
The BBI and Sentech merger had now also changed. It was initially based on amending the legislation. However, it had become clear that this was not going to happen. There was an issue with the acquisition of a shareholding of 74%, and this would need funding. Sentech was currently looking at different options. There had to be certainty given to this Committee and South Africans that BBI had to carry out the task that had been presented. If there was uncertainty, there might be a loss of ‘best skills’ for greener pastures. Someone needed to pay attention to what the implications were in the search for funding, and what would happen if it was not realisable. It must create the necessary stability by putting permanent people in to run the entity. The entities should therefore look at the risk of no funding or the prospects of the legislative amendments while awaiting a new term of office. Having this information would be helpful for South Africans to see what was happening in these areas.
The facts were also needed for this Committee -- the actual figures. The Minister might not need to come before the Committee for this information, but it was important to have the facts. The Committee Content Advisor would be assisting the Committee with interpreting this information. The Committee was happy with the presentation that had been made today.
He asked the Minister for a sense of direction for SITA, which was supposed to be presenting at 18h00 that evening.
Minister Gungubele said that it was common cause that the decision to have the board removed last year and install a new board had been challenged. There had been an order for the suspension of the interim board, and the suspension of the removal of the former board. He had received documentary evidence that there would be an application for an appeal. The documents had been filed at the relevant institution. The order spoke about the suspension of an interim board, but there was no interim board -- only a fully appointed board by Cabinet. It would have been useful for any court to pronounce on the board in terms of its current status. There was no interim board. Only three directors had filed court cases. If the order had been followed, there would have been no interim board because there were only three directors. The order, purely on facts, rendered itself unimplementable. In terms of law, when a body was instituted, the one that existed should be acknowledged. There needed to be a proper status of the institution before it was pronounced. The appeal had been filed and the convention therefore was that once that had been done, the order then stayed suspended until the appeal process had been resolved. The current board was duly in place until proper legal interventions.
The Chairperson asked if there were any questions of clarity.
Ms Bodlani said that what the media was reporting about SITA was very concerning. She acknowledged the Minister’s statements about the court ruling -- the objections, technical matters of board members, the interim board and the fully constituted board. Where did this leave the operations of SITA, especially since the departure of the managing director? Who was acting in that position? What plans were in place to fill that position? She recalled the strike that took place last year. She appreciated the fact that the Minister was not accepting the court ruling, which was his right. How was this affecting the day-to-day running of SITA? The elections were also coming up, and SITA was the backbone of government information and technology. People had seen how a small glitch in the system of SITA in the absence of leadership had crippled the country. How was the Minister going to assist in ensuring that nothing fell through the cracks during the court proceedings?
Minister Gungubele said that most of these issues would be responded to at the 18h00 meeting. Interventions had been made in December 2023. There was the departure of the managing director, who was youthful and excited. The issue of procurement had been a problem over the years and continued to be a chronic problem which could not be resolved within the powers of the Minister. Five experts in the sector had been appointed to analyse the state it was in, with other departments already applying to be exempted. This included various provinces, and included Premier Alan Winde. There was now this platform to see in what state each sector was. He said that the intervention had been taken when he was stressed, but it had proved to be the correct intervention. There was an acting managing director who had been appointed externally.
The Chairperson said that the Committee did not usually track a discussion if it was not on the actual agenda. There had now been feedback. Some stakeholders might be interested in this, and there was another meeting at 18h00. Most of the questions would be covered during that time. The Committee had now been briefed on the revised APPs and the appeal process for SITA.
