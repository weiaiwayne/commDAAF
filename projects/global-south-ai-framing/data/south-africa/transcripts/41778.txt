In its briefing to the Select Committee on the Department of Social Development (DSD) audit outcomes, Auditor General South Africa (AGSA) said that the South African Social Security Agency (SASSA) and the National Development Agency (NDA) had encountered compliance issues and that the Department had regressed in its performance. There were irregularities with the grant payment system and an inability to manage grant administration and payments in an effective, efficient and cost-effective way as it lacked efficient IT systems. This exposed the department to fraud and misappropriation of funds and the inability to produce credible financial and performance results.
The Committee made recommendations which included the improvement of awareness campaigns to communicate important information to the country. Open vacancies were to be filled to bridge the gaps caused by non-performance within the institutions. Consequence management had to be improved on by senior management to curb financial losses.
Meeting report
The Chairperson advised that the Committee would hear only from AGSA on the audit outcomes of the Social Development portfolio to allow for sufficient engagement by the Committee. A separate briefing on 29 October 2025 would be held on the Department of Social Development Annual Report to address concerns specific to the department.
Department of Social Development 2024/25 Audit Outcomes
The Auditor General South Africa (AGSA) team included Ms Michelle Magerman, Business Executive, and Ms Puleng Molapo, Senior Manager. They indicated compliance issues which had mainly resulted from lack of adequate reviews over the monitoring processes within the department. These inadequacies had resulted in the department to regress and obtain financial losses. Procurement processes lacked oversight, which aided in the unreasonable inflation of prices. The presentation also emphasized the financial irregularities within unresolved payments.
The ineligibility of some grant beneficiaries remains unresolved. SASSA lacks sophistication in its information technology department. It causes grant beneficiaries to be paid without the proper authentication processes being completed.
The NDA had paid out performance bonuses without formal board approval on record.
Discussion
Ms J Adriaanse (DA, North West) appreciated the report. She felt it was a true reflection of the status of SASSA which remained a concern to her. The major intervention steps needed had not yet been implemented. She asked AGSA if criminal cases had been opened as part of consequence management.
Mr P Sibande (ANC, Mpumalanga) felt the presentation lacked sufficient information as it was broad and most aspects had been generalised. He sought clarity on the non-compliance and senior management issues highlighted by AGSA. The report ought to specify which management team within SASSA or the NDA had not been compliant.
Mr Sibande acknowledged that some monies had been recouped however enquired about the R136 million which had not been accounted for. He asked for the status quo on the fraud cases in the Eastern Cape and if the alleged perpetrators had been arrested.
Mr Sibande had a concern about the procurement process depending on quotations as this causes price inflation. He asked AGSA to provide the Committee with recommendations on how to assist in terms of the Public Finance Management Act (PFMA).
Mr M Mokwele (ANC, Limpopo) confirmed the shift and reduction in the number of people unlawfully receiving a grant due to collaboration with Home Affairs and the South African Revenue Service (SARS). He enquired about the lack of internal audits which had caused the portfolio to regress. Departments must maintain clean audits.
Mr Mokwele asked for the qualifying criteria used to disburse grants to beneficiaries living abroad. The grant provided by the government must reduce poverty levels in the country. He agreed that all those found on the wrong side of the law within the department should face the rule of law. He encouraged the Committee to assist the Social Development portfolio when conducting their oversight visits.
Ms A Matshobeni (EFF, Eastern Cape) said that there ought to be repercussions for those who had received departmental funds illegally. She emphasised that awareness and the involvement of segments within municipalities was vital. She advised that citizens relied on social media to receive crucial information. For example, most people were not aware of the SASSA card change which resulted in many not being able to receive their grant timeously.
The Chairperson asked how beneficiaries received their grants without beneficiary files in place. What was the process followed by SASSA each month before disbursement? She asked why the overpaid monies to beneficiaries had been excluded from the financial records. She pointed to the lack of internal auditing within the department.
The Chairperson felt that the Department of Social Development had no control and monitoring over the number of grants issued by SASSA. The non-compliance by the portfolio was a broad concern amongst the Committee in its entirety.
Ms Fienies said that Gender Based Violence Command Centre (GBVCC) had not been operational for a few months and enquired how that affected its budget. Also, the NDA performance bonuses had not been paid in accordance with the board approval and asked for confirmation of the board approving bonuses.
The Chairperson said that the Public Audit Act gave the Auditor General (AG) the powers to take remedial action. She asked for clarity on the remedial process and its time frame. AGSA should also clarify how it was able receive accurate beneficiary information whereas SASSA was unable to.
Auditor General response
Ms Magerman replied to the last question posed by the Chairperson saying that AGSA conducts audits on all government entities and therefore has access to their respective databases. This includes the Department of Home Affairs, Persal, Government Employees Pension Fund (GEPF), for example. In this instance, the AG took data from SASSA and ran it through the databases. There was nothing stopping SASSA from acquiring such databases; all that was required was a memorandum of agreement with the various departments. However, even if SASSA accesses the data, it may still not be accurate as it will not receive it in real time. This is due to the unsophisticated system currently used by SASSA.
Ms Magerman made an example of how banks can access data from Home Affairs immediately because their systems had been upgraded. SASSA should have sophisticated systems in place in order to access live data from other entities. This is vital considering the amount of funds SASSA has and the number of beneficiaries it is distributing to. This is a concern. Another important factor was the introduction of artificial intelligence (AI) and cyber-attacks which require an all-round sophisticated system. This will aid in capturing, identifying and preventing fraud from occurring.
On a material irregularity (MI) for DSD, AGSA has issued a notification about the delays in disciplinary processes. There can be delays due to labour law requirements or untimely illness of the staff member. However if there are delays over 12 months, AGSA will zoom into these to see if the delays are valid or not. Remedial action will be effected by AGSA should the delays not be deemed necessary. Currently, DSD has a backlog.
For the NDA, Ms Magerman said that AGSA had not seen bonus approval from the NDA board. The Committee should raise this to ascertain approval for bonuses as this was a concern.
Ms Magerman confirmed that in the month that the GBV Command Centre had not been functioning, there had been a transition from their previous supplier. The supplier had been paid R1.2 million and did not render services. This was deemed a financial loss by AGSA.
Ms Magerman confirmed the implementation of consequence management was the responsibility of the accounting authority or Director General. Any transgressions should be investigated by them accordingly. She recommended that the Committee follow up on pending investigations and the efficiency of the consequence management processes.
In terms of the missing files, Ms Magerman confirmed that it identified 73 664 beneficiaries in payment from 2012-2025 without beneficiary files, the total grants paid to these beneficiaries in 2024/25 was R1,7 billion. AGSA has referred this to the Department and will review this audit finding in the coming year. She requested that the Committee assists in following up with the department to ensure completion of these beneficiary files.
Mr Puleng Molapo confirmed that 11 officials had been arrested in the Eastern Cape and that one case is currently with the SIU. Cases were reported in terms of the Prevention and Combating of Corrupt Activities Act (PRECCA) which stipulates only discrepancies with amounts above R100 000 are to be reported to the South African Police Service (SAPS).
The Committee adopted the 8 October 2025 meeting minutes.
Meeting adjourned.
