Meeting Summary
Video
The Multi-Party Women’s Caucus met with the Minister and Department of Employment and Labour (DEL) on the virtual platform to receive a briefing on Employment Equity Amendment Act and its Regulations that were brought into operation in January 2025.
In her opening remarks, Minister Meth noted that, despite more than two decades of democracy, women continue to face systemic marginalisation in the workplace and society at large. She reminded Members that South Africa’s labour framework has been progressively developed to transform constitutional rights into workplace realities. She highlighted findings from the 25th Annual Employment Equity Report, which show some progress in female participation in sectors such as ICT. However, she cautioned that women, particularly African women, remain underrepresented in senior and top management, while continuing to be concentrated in junior and semi-skilled roles. The leadership pipeline, she warned, remains narrow. Automation and digitalisation present a dual risk and opportunity.
The Minister raised concerns about resistance to transformation from certain employer groups, describing their actions as “organised resistance to justice.” She assured Members that the Department would intensify enforcement through compliance inspections, sanctions for non-compliance, and incentives such as preferential procurement, and stressed that incremental progress is insufficient. A radical acceleration of equality is required, positioning women—particularly black women—at the centre of the workplace rather than at its margins.
During the discussion, Members raised concerns about the lack of local employment in foreign-owned businesses such as China malls; pace of transformation in government institutions and state-owned entities for women’s advancement; difficulties faced by small businesses struggle with internet connectivity when submitting compliance documentation electronically; education road shows on new compliance requirements; how compliance would be enforced in areas with limited ICT infrastructure, and the consequences of non-compliance; and job advertisements limiting opportunities to candidates aged 35 and below.
The Multi-Party Women’s Caucus resolved to identify sectors that act as stumbling blocks to women’s employment and to engage directly with relevant ministers, including those responsible for basic education and early childhood development. Preparing for the future of work, including the rise of artificial intelligence, was identified as essential. Gender-based violence will remain a standing priority in all engagements. The Caucus will reconvene in October, at which Ministers, Directors-General, and employment equity heads will be invited to provide detailed briefings and respond to matters raised during the Women’s Parliament.
Meeting report
Minister’s opening remarks
Minister
Nomakhosazana
Meth
, Minister of Employment and Labour,
highlighted that, transformation in employment has remained uneven during more than two decades of democracy, with women continuing to face systemic marginalisation in the workplace and broader society.
Reference was made to the Employment Equity Amendment Act (Act 4 of 2022) and the Employment Equity Regulations (2025) as sharpened tools to advance constitutional commitments under Section 9 of the Bill of Rights. These measures are designed to eliminate structural barriers that hinder full participation of women, particularly African, Coloured, and Indian women, as well as women with disabilities, in the economy.
The labour instruments include:
- Labour Relations Act (1995) – protection of collective bargaining and dispute resolution.
- Basic Conditions of Employment Act (1997) – minimum labour standards.
- Employment Equity Act (1998) – affirmative action framework.
- National Minimum Wage Act – prevention of exploitation, with particular benefits to women workers.
She emphasised that these instruments collectively form the legal architecture intended to transform constitutional guarantees into workplace realities.
The 25th Annual Employment Equity Report indicates progress in certain sectors such as ICT, yet persistent underrepresentation of women, especially African women, in senior and top management positions. Women remain concentrated in junior and semi-skilled categories, reflecting a narrow leadership pipeline.
She noted the dual risk and opportunity presented by automation and digitalisation. Without deliberate intervention, women risk exclusion from emerging digital roles. However, with equitable access to digital skills, women could significantly contribute to global and national economic growth.
She pointed out the resistance from certain employer groups, often under the guise of meritocracy, was described as “organised resistance to justice.” The Department committed to intensified enforcement through:
- Compliance inspections and audits.
- Sanctions for non-compliance.
- Preferential procurement incentives for compliant businesses.
Strategic Interventions that the DEL proposed:
- Strengthening parliamentary oversight with regular sector-by-sector briefings.
- Ensuring employment equity plans are treated as strategic business imperatives at board level.
- Promoting mentorship, workplace-based training, and leadership pipelines for women.
- Addressing workplace barriers such as childcare support, flexible work arrangements, and gender-based discrimination.
- Enhancing data quality and transparency in monitoring.
- Employing both sanctions and incentives to accelerate transformation.
The Minister indicated that incremental progress is insufficient; radical acceleration of equality is required. Women, especially black women, must be positioned at the centre of workplaces rather than at the margins. This is a constitutional obligation, not a matter of charity.
She briefed Members on recent litigation outcomes, noting a successful defence against the National Employers’ Association, while
the Department
awaiting judgment in a separate matter.
Recent Employment Equity Act amendments and regulations
Ms Ntsoaki Mamashela, DEL Director: Employment Equity, presented on the Employment Equity Amendment Act, 2022 which came into effect from 1 January 2025 as well as its regulations
as part of South Africa’s broader legislative and policy framework on equality and gender equity. Grounded in the Constitution, particularly Section 9 on equality and Section 23 on fair labour practices, the framework seeks to address persistent historical inequalities in the labour market, especially along lines of race, gender, and disability.
She highlighted key legislative instruments such as the Labour Relations Act, the Basic Conditions of Employment Act, and the National Minimum Wage Act, which collectively safeguard fair labour practices. The Employment Equity Act of 1998 and its subsequent amendments remain central to driving transformation. The 2013 amendments introduced equal pay provisions, while the 2022 amendments, effective from January 2025, empower the Minister to set sector-specific numerical targets for 18 economic sectors and require an EE Compliance Certificate for access to state contracts.
The EE Regulations of April 2025 provide a five-year sectoral target framework to be met by 2030, with mechanisms for reporting, monitoring, and compliance. Importantly, sector targets differ from quotas by allowing flexibility and justifiable grounds for non-compliance, such as lack of recruitment opportunities or economic disruptions.
The 25th Commission for Employment Equity (CEE) Annual Report shows that despite some progress, significant gender imbalances persist across occupational levels, with women still underrepresented in top and senior management positions. The amendments aim to accelerate equitable representation of black people, women, and persons with disabilities, while reducing compliance burdens for small businesses.
Implementation efforts have included nationwide workshops, inspector training, and capacity building for trade unions to ensure effective monitoring. The current EE reporting period (September 2025–January 2026) is the first to align with the new sectoral targets and compliance certification.
Employment Equity Amendments & Regulations
Constitutional Basis: Rooted in Section 9 (Equality) and Section 23 (Fair Labour Practices) of the Constitution; seeks to redress historical discrimination and promote fair, inclusive workplaces.
Legislative Framework: Supported by the Labour Relations Act, Basic Conditions of Employment Act, National Minimum Wage Act, and centralised in the Employment Equity Act (1998) with amendments in 2013 and 2022.
2022 Amendments effective from January 2025
:
Minister empowered to set sector-specific numerical targets for 18 economic sectors.
EE Compliance Certificate now a prerequisite for state contracts.
Reduced compliance burden for small businesses (under 50 employees).
EE Regulations (April 2025)
: Establish five-year targets (to 2030) for designated groups (black people, women, persons with disabilities). Sectoral targets allow flexibility compared to quotas, with valid justifications for non-compliance.
Workforce Status
The 25th CEE Annual Report highlights continued underrepresentation of women at top and senior management levels despite incremental progress since 2001.
Implementation & Way Forward
:
- Nationwide training and capacity-building workshops for inspectors and trade unions.
- First EE reporting cycle under new rules opened September 2025.
- EE and gender equality framed as constitutional, social justice, and economic imperatives.
The presentation concluded that employment equity and gender equality are not only constitutional and human rights imperatives, but also moral, social, and economic priorities essential for social justice and inclusive development.
Discussion
Ms N Gcaleka-Mazibuko (ANC) raised her concern about the lack of local employment in foreign-owned businesses, particularly in China malls and restaurants.
She was concerned with the enforcement of employment equity in government institutions and state-owned entities, particularly women's representation.
She urged labour inspectorates to accelerate inspection and enforcement of labour laws.
Dr S Thembekwayo (EFF) raised her concern about small business owners who experience difficulties with internet connectivity when submitting compliance documentation electronically. Since the manual certificate option has been discontinued, these businesses are left without an alternative mechanism to meet compliance requirements. This raises questions if the Department is adequately prepared to support such groups.
She asked what measures has the Department put in place to assist businesses in areas with limited ICT access, given that manual certificates are no longer available.
Has the Department undertaken roadshows or consultative processes to share information about the new compliance procedures?
How does the Department enforce compliance in cases where businesses are unable to meet requirements due to connectivity challenges?
What are the consequences of non-compliance under the current system?
Ms B Kegakilwe (ANC) raised a concern about the enforcement of employment equity in government institutions and state-owned entities, particularly regarding women's representation. She thus wanted to know what the department does to ensure compliance and the promotion of women in public sector.
The Chairperson raised the concern about job advertisements favouring candidates aged 35 and below, impacting older unemployed individuals. She highlighted that the employment environment should be conducive for all age groups, particularly those above 35.
DEL response
Ms Ntsoaki Mamashela confirmed that all companies operating in South Africa, including those owned by foreign nationals, fall within its jurisdiction and must comply with labour legislation and sectoral targets published by the Minister. Inspectors have been capacitated since April 2025 to enforce compliance with recent amendments.
To support small businesses without internet access, departmental officials across all provinces have been trained to assist employers at labour centres. Officials are able to log into the system and generate electronic compliance certificates on behalf of employers, thereby removing barriers such as data costs and connectivity challenges. Manual certificates have been discontinued due to risks of fraud; all certificates are now system-generated, coded, and leave an auditable trail.
She noted that national road shows were conducted following the publication of the regulations, commencing on 20 May and concluding in Cape Town on 10 July. An additional online workshop was held on 22 July at the request of Cape Town stakeholders. Workshops and consultations continue to be provided, often on request, to employers, trade unions, HR practitioners, academics, and government departments.
Ms Mamashela said electronic compliance certificates became available on 1 September 2025. Certificates are valid for 12 months, after which employers are required to reapply.
Inspectors are mandated to enforce compliance across both the private and public sectors. Non-compliant companies or public entities, without justifiable reasons, will be referred to the Labour Court for penalties. Sanctions include fines of R1.5 million or 2% of turnover. In the case of government departments, turnover is defined as the allocated budget, and they will be required to account for spending public funds on penalties.
Sector-specific equity targets, including those for women and persons with disabilities, have been published for both the public and private sectors for the next five years. Government departments, state-owned entities, municipalities, and provincial administrations are included and will be assessed annually based on submitted reports.
Ms Mamashela said that age restrictions in job advertisements may constitute unfair discrimination under the Employment Equity Act. Departments and employers have been cautioned to avoid such practices.
Minister’s response
Minister Meth referred to the recent case involving seven Chinese nationals who were imprisoned, with judgment delivered two days prior. The matter serves as a reminder that all persons operating within South Africa are subject to the laws of the Republic and are expected to comply fully.
The Minister acknowledged that the Department of Employment and Labour faces significant financial constraints. Unlike other departments, it has not benefitted from budget increases, forcing a reduction in the working hours of part-time CEE commissioners. The Department has sought to bridge resource gaps and assists in turning around struggling businesses to preserve jobs.
The Department currently has approximately 2 000 inspectors responsible for over 10 000 business establishments, highlighting capacity shortfalls. To address this, the Department launched Project 20 000, which recruits graduate interns as researchers and inspectors for two-year periods. Over 2 000 interns have already been engaged, effectively doubling inspectorate capacity. The aim is to recruit 10 000 interns this year and a further 10 000 next year.
The Minister reported that inspections have revealed serious violations, particularly in sectors such as agriculture in the Western Cape, where women farmers face evictions, underpayment, harassment, and discrimination. Inspectors, often working with bargaining councils, have uncovered abuses such as employees being denied salaries, paid only commissions, underpaid relative to the national minimum wage, denied overtime, and denied lunch breaks. In such cases, employers are compelled to repay outstanding wages, UIF contributions, and other benefits. Persistent offenders face prosecution and referral to the Labour Court. The Department also collaborates with the Department of Home Affairs and the police in cases of severe non-compliance.
Concerns were raised about gender equity within government itself. Out of 45 national departments, only 10 are led by women, raising questions about government’s credibility in enforcing equity standards in the private sector while falling short internally.
The Minister expressed concern about discriminatory age restrictions in job advertisements. While maximum age limits are permitted in youth-targeted programmes (e.g. learnerships, graduate schemes, National Youth Service (NYS), blanket age restrictions in public or private recruitment processes are unlawful under the Employment Equity Act. Employers have been cautioned against such practices.
The Chairperson requested that both the Ministers of Employment and Labour and Monitoring and Evaluation provide written submissions, even in summary form, on areas of enforcement and identified difficulties. These should include broader issues of compliance and transformation.
The Chairperson made reference to the historic Women’s March of 9 August 1956, highlighting the sacrifices of women across race and class lines in advancing equality. The struggle for transformation was described as ongoing and difficult, with resistance often manifesting through legal challenges. Like the women of 1956, current efforts must unite women across political affiliations for the benefit of future generations.
The Chairperson stated that the Multi-Party Women’s Caucus committed to identifying key sectors that act as stumbling blocks for women’s employment. Targeted engagement will be undertaken with relevant ministers, including those responsible for basic education, early childhood development, and other critical portfolios. She noted the importance of preparing for the future world of work, including artificial intelligence.
Gender-Based Violence (GBV) will remain a standing priority within all engagements. Poverty and hunger continue to disproportionately affect women, and parliamentarians must act with urgency as public representatives.
The Minister said that Members of Parliament are themselves sometimes employers and must model fair treatment of employees, including domestic workers. The link between parenting, home environments, and broader social transformation was noted, with the reminder that “charity begins at home.”
She reaffirmed the principle that women’s unity can drive change in society and the economy.
The Minister indicated that the Multi-Party Women’s Caucus will reconvene in October for its final sitting of the year. Ministers and senior departmental officials, including Directors-General and employment equity heads, will be invited to provide detailed briefings and address matters raised during the Women’s Parliament.
The Committee adopted its 26 July 2025 minutes and the meeting was adjourned.
