Meeting Summary
The Portfolio Committee convened its first meeting of the Seventh Parliament to elect a Chairperson, and to consider presentations from the Department of Defence and Military Veterans, ARMSCOR, and the Castle Control Board on their 2024/25 annual performance plans.
Mr M Legoete (ANC) was nominated for Chairperson of the Committee by Ms T Sokanyile (ANC), who was seconded by Mr C Hattingh (DA). He accepted the nomination, and stressed the importance of defending the Constitution and the country’s borders.
At the outset, Members complained that they had not received all the documentation for the briefings. They were frustrated that they had received the presentations only earlier that day, asserting that they did not want to be accused of “rubber stamping” reports. They also felt that not enough time was afforded in the meeting to adequately address the issues raised in the presentations.
The Committee was concerned about the R50 billion budget shortfall of the Department of Defence (DOD), and insisted that the budget should reflect the importance of national defence and the security of the country. South African troops in the Democratic Republic of Congo (DRC) had to receive adequate support from the DOD, as recent fatalities indicated they were facing adversaries with sophisticated modern weaponry. It was proposed that the Department apply for additional funding from National Treasury, and also pursue other revenue streams. Members wanted to know more about the challenges involved in implementing the Defence Review.
The Committee felt that the Department of Military Veterans (DMV) was not fulfilling its mandate by failing to provide veterans with benefits such as education, housing, and pensions. There was particular concern over the inadequacy of the military veterans' database, which was essential for veterans to access these benefits. A Member wanted to know why veterans of uMkhonto we Sizwe did not receive the same health care benefits as veterans of the South African National Defence Force (SANDF).
Members praised the ARMSCOR presentation for its detail and the good work done by the entity. They said they would discuss the detailed report more fully in their subsequent virtual meeting.
Following the Castle Control Board (CCB) presentation, a Member wanted to know why they were responsible for funding a “symbol of colonialism.” The vision, mission, and values of the organisation were also not clear to Members. It was suggested that the Department of Arts and Culture should be responsible for funding the CCB, especially considering the budget shortfall of the DOD.

DOD contributions to national imperatives
Mr Cox said the implementation of the National Development Plan's (NDP's) “Vision 2030” -- through the five-year implementation plan outlined in the 2019-2024 medium term strategic framework (MTSF) -- would primarily focus on job creation, poverty reduction and the reduction of inequality. The Department of Defence (DOD) would contribute both directly and indirectly, as addressed in this plan, and endeavour to support the following MTSF and government pillars and priorities:
A strong and inclusive economy;
Economic transformation and job creation;
Capabilities of South Africans;
Education, skills and health;
Consolidating of social wage through reliable and basic services;
Spatial development, human settlements, and local government;
Social cohesion and safer communities;
A capable, ethical, and developmental state;
A better Africa and a better world.
National Growth Path (NGP)
The principal target of the policy was to create five million jobs by the year 2050. The DOD would endeavour to support the NGP through DOD internships, the National Youth Service (NYS) and the Defence industry job creation.
Industrial Policy Action Plan (IPAP).
The DOD would play a role and support the IPAP in the Public-Private Growth Initiative (PPGI) through the promulgated Defence industry strategy.
Ministerial priorities and direction
In the foreword of the 2024 APP, the Minister directed the DOD in terms of the following strategic priorities:
• Conclude the evaluation of the South African Defence Review 2015, and formulate an actionable plan for consultation with the National Executive by 31 October 2024.
• Enhance organisational renewal through a comprehensive business case report, enabling the establishment of a departmental organisational structure responsive to the mandate of defence.
• Ensure departmental governance, risk and compliance structures, enabling the effective and efficient utilisation of scarce resources.
• Ensure the digital transformation of current legacy systems, thereby enhancing the integration and modernisation of our Information and Communication Technology systems, enabling departmental and individual accountability, whilst ensuring value for money.
• Emphasise the operational utility of the Reserve Forces and ensure the continuous professionalisation of DOD personnel, which was paramount.
• Continue the institutionalisation of national imperatives that promote the equality, empowerment, and realisation of women, youth and people with disabilities.
• Maintain the main operating systems and prime mission equipment, support for national cyber resilience, and leverage defence capabilities to support the national developmental agenda. These were crucial aspects of the Department's commitment to socioeconomic upliftment and safeguarding South Africa's security and sovereignty.
Selected performance indicators
Key observations included that the number of landward sub-units deployed for border safeguarding remain at 15. This should be seen in the context of previous statements by the DOD, which stated that at least 22 sub-units were required to cover all the border areas effectively. However, it must also be noted that National Treasury had allocated additional funds for border safeguarding technology, such as drones, cameras and other relevant equipment, that must serve as a force multiplier to soldiers deployed on the borders. Funds of R225 million previously allocated for this purpose were not used by the DOD for technology acquisition; with only R85 million finally planned for spending on technology, the purchase of which was ultimately delayed. R65 million for Year 1 (2020/21) was re-allocated and utilised in the fight against the COVID-19 pandemic, while R75 million for Year 2 (2021/22) was utilised and spent on improvements to the condition of facilities in the borderline operational areas. The R85 million funding for Year 3 (2022/23) was to be utilised to acquire technology systems to be deployed in the borderline operational areas.
The lower target for 1.998 million Reserve Force man-days in 2024/25 had been driven largely by efforts to contain compensation of employees (CoE) expenditure, but in practice, it was highly unrealistic, given the high demand for deployed soldiers due to ongoing deployments in and outside South Africa. As such, the DOD would probably exceed this target, resulting in further overspending on CoE.
Overview of planned expenditure
The total allocation to the DOD for 2024/25 was R51.810 billion, which was lower than the adjusted appropriation of R52.468 billion for 2023/24. Compared to the adjusted budget of 2023/24, the DOD budget had decreased by 1.3% in nominal terms, and by 5.69% in real terms. The defence allocation for 2024/25 represented 2.19% of the country’s total expenditure of R2.369 trillion, compared to 2.28% in the 2022/23 main appropriation. However, as a percentage of gross domestic product (GDP) for 2024/25 (R7.452 trillion), defence expenditure stands at 0.695% (0.73% in 2023/24). The latter constituted a continued decline in defence expenditure as a percentage of GDP from around 1% of GDP when the Defence Review was drafted in 2015, resulting in the Review not being implementable.
Programme 1: Administration
This programme provides the Department strategic leadership, management and support services, and consists of 14 sub-programmes. The cost of the Ministry sub-programme had increased marginally from R125.4 million in 2023/24, to R135.6 million in 2024/25. However, the allocation for these years was much higher than previous expenditure, such as the R74.3 million spent on the Ministry in 2021/22 and R59.1 million spent in 2022/23. The allocation for the financial services sub-programme increased from R423.9 million in 2023/24, to R473.8 million in 2024/25. Finally, the allocation for office Accommodation decreased from R2.815 billion in 2023/24, to R2.642 billion in 2024/25. In terms of economic classifications, CoE expenditure for the programme increased from R1.964 billion in 2023/24, to R2.267 billion in 2024/25. With an overall decrease in the programme’s allocation, the increase in CoE then necessitates a decrease in funds available for goods and services, which decreased from R3.335 billion in 2023/24, to R3.196 billion in 2024/25.
Programme 2: Force employment
This programme provides and employs defence capabilities, including an operational capability, to successfully conduct all operations and joint, interdepartmental, interagency and multinational military exercises. It contains five sub-programmes that include the spheres of deployment of the South African National Defence Force (SANDF), be it for regional security, domestic deployments or special operations.
The overall allocation decreases significantly, as visible in Table 1, from R5.190 billion in 2023/24, to R3.988 billion in the current financial year. This reduction comes despite a number of significant SANDF deployments that only came about after the budget was approved, including the new SANDF deployment as part of a Southern African Development Community (SADC) mission to the Democratic Republic of Congo (DRC), as well as domestic SANDF deployments to assist in the elections and protect Eskom infrastructure. Key to note was that, as per the current budget, these deployments would be unfunded and therefore funded from within the DOD’s current budget. The most affected sub-programme was the regional security sub-programme, which allocation had decreased from R2.396 billion in 2023/24 to R842.6 million in 2024/25. This was likely due to initial planning for the SANDF mission in Mozambique coming to an end, but the SANDF’s deployment had been prolonged due to new insurgent activity in the region.
Programme 3: Landward defence
This programme provides prepared and supported landward defence capabilities for the defence and protection of South Africa, and was the largest programme in the DOD. It houses the SA Army and its various divisions and, since it was manpower-heavy, constituted the bulk of DOD expenditure in terms of CoE. The 11 sub-programmes of the programme represent the strategic direction of the SA Army and its various divisions, such as infantry, armour, artillery, air defence, engineering, intelligence and signal.
Most sub-programmes had received a marginal increase and remain aligned with the previous years’ expenditure. However, the strategic direction sub-programme’s allocation decreases from R590.8 million in 2023/24, to R546.1 million in 2024/25. Similarly, the infantry capability sub-programme’s allocation decreases from R6.248 billion in 2023/24, to R6.151 billion in 2024/25. The air defence sub-programme also shows a marginal decrease. In terms of economic classifications, CoE makes up the bulk of expenditure, increasing from R13.392 billion in 2023/24, to R14.255 billion in 2024/25. Spending on contractors was expected to be R147.4 million for the current year and although this was lower than 2023/24, it was still much higher than the preceding three years, which reached a peak of only R76.6 million in 2020/21.
Programme 4: Air Defence
This programme provides prepared and supported air defence capabilities for the defence and protection of South Africa. The programme’s sub-programmes constitute the various functions and capabilities of the SA Air Force, including its helicopter, transport, air combat and training capabilities. The strategic direction sub-programme’s allocation increases from R24.8 million in 2023/24 to R74.0 million in 2024/25, while the operational direction sub-programme’s allocation decreases from R154.7 million, to R56.4 million over the same period. The biggest change that contributes to the overall decrease in the programme’s allocation was due to the decrease in the transport and maritime capability sub-programme allocation from R1.708 billion in 2023/24, to R495.4 million in 2024/25. The high allocation in the previous year was due to an additional allocation to the SA Air Force for the repair of its medium-transport capability, which came about as a result of the intervention of the Sixth Parliament.
Programme 5: Maritime Defence
This programme provides prepared and supported maritime defence capabilities for the defence and protection of South Africa. It has six sub-programmes aligned with the functions of the SA Navy, namely its combat capability, logistics support capability, training and base support. All sub-programmes received a slight decrease in their allocation, except for the base support capability, which saw its allocation increase from R572 million in 2023/24 to R635.2 million in 2024/25.
Programme 6: Military health support
This programme provides prepared and supported health capabilities and services for the defence and protection of South Africa. The programme had seven sub-programmes related to its main functions, including military health support, area military health services, specialist services, product support, training and military health maintenance. The South African Military Health Service (SAMHS) maintains three military hospitals (Pretoria, Cape Town and Bloemfontein) and an array of sick bays at military bases around the country. It provides services to serving SANDF members, former (retired) South African Defence Force and SANDF Members, and military veterans verified by the Department of Military Veterans. The biggest change in the sub-programme allocation was for the Area Military Health Services sub-programme, which increased from R2.030 billion in 2023/24 to R2.206 billion in 2024/25.
Programme 7: Defence intelligence
This programme provides defence intelligence and counterintelligence capabilities, and defence diplomacy administrative support to the Department. The programme was the smallest of the DOD’s programmes and had only two sub-programmes -- operations, and intelligence support and services. The intelligence support services’ allocation increases from R734.3 million in 2023/24, to R815.7 million in 2024/25.
Programme 8: General support
This programme provides general support capabilities and services to the Department. This includes logistics services to the Department in terms of warehousing for ammunition, main equipment, and stores, as well as providing the defence works capability, the capital works programme, and the defence facility refurbishment programme. It further provides command and management information systems, related services to the Department, and military policing capability. The programme had five sub-programmes related to the aforementioned functions. Given the overall increase in the allocation to the programme, as visible in Table 1, two sub-programmes received substantial increases. The Joint Logistics Services’ allocation increases from R3.579 billion in 2023/24 to R3.928 billion in 2024/25. Similarly, the Technology Development allocation increases from R316.0 million to R411.3 million. In terms of economic classifications, a noteworthy shift in property payments was visible from R403.8 million in 2023/24 to R561.2 million in 2024/25.
Key cost drivers
The report noted that the key cost drivers for the Department in the 2024/25 financial year were the compensation of employees; border safeguarding; regional security; property payments; and the Special Defence Account (SDA).
DOD income for 2024/25
One of the Minister of Defence’s key priorities was to increase revenue generation for the DOD to boost the current allocation. However, the estimates of national expenditure (ENE) forecast only a marginal increase in Departmental receipts from R1.256 billion in 2023/24, to R1.319 billion in 2024/25. Department of Military Veterans' (DMV's) Annual Performance Plan 2024/25
Similar to the previous APP, the DMV’s 2024/25 APP states that the Department acknowledges the importance of the objective of building a fully functional Department over the MTEF to enable it to provide socioeconomic support services and benefits to military veterans and, where necessary, their dependants in an effective, efficient and sustainable manner. It lists the contributions to the NDP, the medium-term strategic framework outcomes, and the executive authority priorities as follows:
Priority 1: Capable, ethical, and developmental state;
Priority 2: Economic transformation and job creation;
Priority 3: Education, skills, and health;
Priority 4: Consolidating social wage through reliable and basic services;
Priority 5: Spatial development, human settlements and local government.
Overview of planned expenditure
The DMV’s budget had increased marginally from R846.3 million in 2023/24, to R863.8 million in 2024/25. However, in real terms, when adjusted for inflation, this represents a reduction of 2.5%. The biggest shift in in nominal terms was in Programme 3, with an additional R33.4 million allocated. Programme 1, in contrast, sees a reduction of R11.3 million (7.39%) when compared to the previous year.
In terms of economic classifications, the following should be noted:
• Compensation of employees remains stable and decreased marginally from R141.4 million in 2023/24, to R140.4 million in 2024/25.
• Expenditure on travel and subsistence was set to increase from R30.9 million in 2023/24, to R41.8 million in 2024/25.
In terms of transfers and subsidies, the following should be noted:
• Transfers to military veterans’ social benefits decreased from R195.0 million in 2023/24, to R171.9 million in 2024/25.
• R600 000 was allocated to be transferred to the World Veterans Federation in 2024/25, while no such payments were made in the previous year and only R31 000 in 2021/22.
Programme overviews
Programme 1: Administration
This programme provides the Department strategic leadership, management and support services and consists of six sub-programmes. Spending in the sub-programmes remains stable when comparing 2024/25 to the previous financial year. Of concern, however, was that the allocation for the financial Administration sub-programme decreased from R20.4 million in 2023/24, to R15.2 million in 2024/25. Given the Auditor-General’s (AG's) concerns around financial management at the DMV in 2023, this was of specific concern.
Programme 2: Socioeconomic Development
This programme develops and monitors the implementation of legislation, policy frameworks and service delivery cooperation agreements on compensation for injury in military service, counselling, health care, public transport, pension and housing benefits for military veterans eligible for such support. The programme had three sub-programmes related to database management, healthcare and wellbeing support, and socioeconomic support management. Planned spending on the three sub-programmes for 2024/25 remains similar to the previous financial year. Only the socioeconomic support management sub-programme sees its allocation decreasing from R235.4 million to R221.8 million.
Programme 3: Empowerment and Stakeholder Management
This programme manages and facilitates the implementation of military veteran empowerment and stakeholder management programmes. The programme had three sub-programmes related to the management of the DMV's provincial offices, skills development and education benefits, and heritage management of veterans. The only noteworthy change in allocation was for sub-programme 2 (empowerment, skills development and education), which increased from R150.4 million in 2023/24 to R177 million in 2024/25. This was related largely to the increase in the number of bursaries for veterans and their dependents. The number of bursaries provided to military veterans and their dependants was set to increase from 3 500 in 2023/24 to 4 800 in 2026/27. The medium-term allocation for Programme 3 was R882.1 million over three years.
Selected performance indicators for 2024/25
Given that the main focus of the Department is to render services and distribute benefits to military veterans, the selected performance indicators below have a specific reference to the DMV’s main tasks. Table 3 reflected the audited performance for 2021/22 and 2022/23, and then an estimated performance for 2023/24. In previous years, the DMV did not manage to achieve well against its performance indicators, and therefore the 2023/24 performance in Table 1 should be seen in this context. Specifically, it was noteworthy that the DMV had shown poor performance in the past regarding the provision of houses to military veterans. This had been as a result of the DMV being dependent on provincial housing departments for the constriction of houses, pointing to poor coordination between the DMV and the provincial departments. The military pension benefit was also delayed in its implementation due to the need to finalise relevant regulations. As such, it was implemented only late in 2023, and therefore the 2023/24 target of 4 000 veterans was unlikely to have been achieved.

Discussion
The Chairperson thanked the presenters for being precise and to the point -- that was what the Committee wanted. He would now allow the entities to present, unless the Members had any comments.
Mr Niehaus proposed that the Committee ask questions on clarity regarding the Departmental presentations before the entities presented, otherwise the bulk of information and the difference in presentations would not be helpful.
The Chairperson said this would not be a problem. Engaging the Department after their presentation was important before Members lost their concentration. The presentations made it clear they had a serious problem with budget allocations. If they were given R51 billion and their shortfall was R50 billion, their expected budget was R101 billion. This was half of the financial commitment they had expected as a department. They needed money to support military veterans, as per the Military Veterans Act. There was a problem with outstanding benefit payouts, which were not cashed back. There were not enough funds to fight the zama zamas.
Mr Niehaus thanked the Chairperson and the representatives from the departments for their presentation. These presentations were very intense, and it was very difficult to get all the detailed information as they were speeding through the presentations. He said they were in a crisis. The information provided by the Department and the R50 billion shortfall indicated that the Department of Defence was in a crisis.
He had heard reference to the Minister's performance agreements with the President. Among them were the internal safety and security of the country, as well as international support and provisional support for Africa. He referred to the deployment of SANDF troops in the DRC. He asked the Minister if the troops sent into the DRC were not “sitting ducks”. Evidently, the finances were not there to provide the necessary support. 22 members of the SANDF were seriously injured and two of them were killed in Sake in the DRC. They had to ask themselves if it was advisable for soldiers to be sent in, considering the budget shortfall and the well-equipped rebels they had to face. This was a very dangerous situation for them to be in, considering the Department was unable to provide the necessary logistical support.
Moving on to the presentation by the DMV, Mr Niehaus said he was surprised that the presentation gave the impression of a Department that was actually meeting its mandate. He said the Department did not meet its mandate and was the worst-performing department in government. There were huge internal problems in terms of capacity, and a lack of staff deployment on the basis of merit and ability. The Military Veterans Act 18 of 2011 provided for 11 benefits that needed to be extended to military veterans. The presentation indicated there was support for education, housing, health care, pensions, etc, but not one of these benefits was properly administered to military veterans. This was not general information, but information provided by the military veterans themselves. These veterans were deeply disturbed by the fact that they were not receiving these benefits. For instance, with regard to education and bursary payments, children and dependents of veterans were placed in a very unacceptable situation. Payments were not made to universities and other educational institutions. They also complained that they were treated in the most disdainful manner.
Mr Niehaus said the issue of pensions was not one where the department could say “they were happy to report, which was the phrase that was used in the presentation. There has been legislation since 2011 that provides pensions for military veterans. In 2024, the number of veterans who were receiving pensions was pitifully small. He spoke under correction, but said a figure was given of 280 beneficiaries out of thousands of military veterans. A budget of R331 million had been allocated, of which R250 million would be returned as a result of poor administration.
Lastly, he said the department must clarify what was going on with the Military Veterans database. This database was the gateway for military veterans to access benefits. The administration of that database had been an absolute disgrace. He said they had a Mr Mahlaule who chaired that Committee, and used it as a platform for political factions and infighting between the MK Military Veterans Association and the new military veterans association. ARMSCOR Corporate Plan 2024/25
ARMSCOR said it would seek to contribute to several government outcomes as per the NDP and the 2019-2024 MTSF. Contributions to the NDP included:
• Sharpening South Africa’s innovative edge by contributing to global scientific and technological advancement.
• Investing in research and development.
• Facilitating cooperation between the public service and technology institutions.
• Committing to procurement approaches that stimulate domestic industry and job creation.
• Procuring from and supporting small, medium, and micro enterprises (SMMEs), black-owned and black-managed enterprises, female-led enterprises, and youth and military veterans.
Budget analysis
The projected revenue for the ARMSCOR Group in 2024/25 (R1.825 billion) was only marginally higher than what was projected for 2023/24 (R1.811 billion). When planned expenditure was considered, it was expected to have a net shortfall of R88.8 million for 2024/25, in contrast to a projected surplus of R24.3 million in the previous financial year.
The main cost drivers for ARMSCOR in the 2024/25 financial year included the following:
• Direct personnel cost increases from R1.167 billion in 2023/24, to R1.240 billion in 2024/25.
• Depreciation and amortisation increases from R108.6 million in 2023/24, to R112 million in 2024/25.
• Cost of sales increases from R130.2 million in 2023/24, to R171.3 million in 2024/25.
• General expenses would increase marginally from R367.4 million in 2023/24, to R388.1 million in 2024/25.
ARMSCOR’S funding
ARMSCOR was largely funded from state finances through a transfer payment. The reduced defence allocation was pressuring ARMSCOR to supplement this income with other commercial projects. To lessen the pressure on the fiscus, these projects require expansion to increase commercial activities in order to increase ARMSCOR’S economic viability. For 2024/25, its planned funding comprised the following:
• Transfer payment: R1.169 billion (R1.250 billion in 2023/24);
• Sale of goods: R15.7 million (R13.9 million in 2023/24);
• Rendering services: R424.1 million (R367.4 million in 2023/24);
• Rental income: R74.1 million (R65.9 million in 2023/24);
• Other income: R25.7 million (R29 million in 2023/24);
• Interest received: R113.9 million (R82.4 million in 2023/24).
Additional financial information
The ARMSCOR Group consisted of various divisions, including ARMSCOR Corporate, the ARMSCOR Dockyard (which provides maintenance services to the SA Navy) and ARMSCOR Research and Development. All three divisions were expected to incur a deficit in 2024/25, as follows.
• ARMSCOR Corporate: Shortfall of R21.2 million (R54.3 million surplus was projected for 2023/24);
• Research and Development: Shortfall of R63.1 million (R27.3 million shortfall was projected for 2023/24);
• ARMSCOR Dockyard: Shortfall of R4.6 million (R2.7 million shortfall was projected for 2021/22);
• ARMSCOR Group: Shortfall of R88.8 million.

Goal 1: Defence materiel acquisition;
Goal 2: System support acquisition;
Goal 3: Schedule placement;

Goal 5: Defence technology research, test and evaluation; intellectual property (IP) management;

Castle Control Board 2024/25 Annual Performance Plan and Budget Vote
The Castle Control Board (CCB) proposed amending the Castle Management Act and the Defence Endowment Property and Account Act. As with the previous APPs, it was stated that the Board had identified that the current founding legislation was dated, and had submitted a review document to the Ministry of Defence and Military Veterans.
CCB contribution to national government planning
The CCB aimed to contribute to the following government planning priorities in the medium-term strategic framework:
•
Priority 1:
A capable, ethical, and developmental state.
The CCB would shore up its internal controls and governance policies, such as the ethics policy, the financial management policy, the supply chain management (SCM) policy, the gift policy, etc, to maintain good corporate governance.
•
Priority 2:
Economic transformation and job creation.
The CCB would strive to actively develop its spaces to extract maximum economic value. Improving the economic viability of the citadel would encourage the employment of youth, women, military veterans, and people living with disabilities.
•
Priority 3:
Education, skills, and health.
An environment would be created for students to do their internship and improve their skills.
•
Priority 4:
Consolidating the social wage through reliable and quality basic services.
The CCB strives to provide informative historical and architectural tours of the Castle of Good Hope. The CCB absorbs interns and casual workers into the CCB permanent structure when funds are available.
•
Priority 5:
Spatial integration, human settlements and local government.
The CCB, through tourism programmes, contributes to the local economy and ensures the
promotion of the Castle of Good Hope and the city as the tourist destination of choice. The Board was putting measures in place to ensure the accessibility of spaces for people with disabilities.
CCB performance plans for 2024/25
The 2024 CCB APP noted several indicators with targets largely aligning with those of the previous year. Of concern, however, was that the number of tourists visiting the Castle had not returned to the levels seen prior to the Covid-19 pandemic. With the CCB falling well short of attracting the desired number of tourists in recent years, its target to attract 130 000 tourists in 2024/25 seemed ambitious. Tourism in previous years may have been affected by the land occupation and erection of structures by homeless people on the Castle premises close to the entrance of the facility. This matter has since been resolved through the relevant legal means, and occupants have been removed and assisted with alternative housing.
CCB resource considerations
The Board provided a high-level overview of the CCB’s programme resource allocations for 2023/24. The projected expenditure for 2023/24 was R10.705 million, which was only a marginal increase on the R10.455 million projected expenditure for the previous year. The administration programme remained the largest programme, with expected expenditure of R8.875 million. This was the most significant spending programme, given that the CoE was the primary expenditure driver within this programme, with 27 staff members according to the 2024 APP.
Two major shifts in programme allocations could be noted. First, the allocation for the conservation management programme decreases from R1.147 million in 2023/24 to R942 000 in 2024/25. This is offset by an increase in the allocation to the tourism management programme, from R94 000 in 2023/24 to R156 000 in 2024/25. The latter could be seen as a positive, given the Castle’s slow recovery in attracting large tourist numbers. The administration cost also increases from R8.843 million in 2023/24, to R8.857 million in 2024/25.
