Title: ICASA Council Interviews Day 1
Date: 21 Nov 2024
Committee: 
URL: https://pmg.org.za/committee-meeting/39928/

Meeting Summary
Video
The Subcommittee interviewed 11 candidates for appointment as Independent Communications Authority of South Africa (ICASA) councillors. Committee members posed questions about the candidates’ knowledge of the many challenges facing ICASA and about their skills and competence for the role. The candidates were
Adv Luthando Simphiwe Mkumatela; Dr Mandisa Nompumelelo Khumalo; Ms Cassandra Cordelia Gabriel; Dr Tshifhiwa Joshua Maumela; Prof Mpho Primu ; Dr Charles Alistair Lewis; Mr Mathibela Selepe; Ms Mariza Jurgens; Mr Jean-Pierre Murray-Klein; Ms Karabo Mohale; Mr Melusi Mthethwa.
The candidates’ responses can be found on the accompanying video.
Meeting report
For each interview, the Chairperson explained the interview procedure to the candidate, introduced the interview panel and noted 45 minutes had been allocated for the interview. At the end of the interview, she thanked them and asked whether they had any questions or declarations for the Committee.
Candidate 1: Adv Luthando Simphiwe Mkumatela
The Chairperson asked the candidate to give a brief overview of who he was.
The Chairperson said that given the Advocate was a returning ICASA councillor, what was it that he planed to do differently in his second term.
Ms T Bodlani (DA) told the candidate that yesterday the Committee was briefed on the National Communications Satellite (SatCom) str4ategy. As an ICASA councillor what would his advice be about this? She understood that this could advance the country.
Ms Bodlani asked how the regulator of the future looked for the candidate. How did ICASA approach the future? What would be his contribution in mentoring new councillors? What portfolio did he have in mind for his second term? What did he think was the biggest impact during his current term?
Mr K Sithole (IFP) understood that the candidate was a councilor and asked what could be relevant to change ICASA. Was it still relevant for the state to allocate funding for ICASA?
Mr S Ngubane (MK) asked what made the candidate stand out among his colleagues during his tenure as an ICASA councillor. What made him unique and his term memorable? He seemed to have a lot of book knowledge; the challenge in the country was there was a lack of implementation of the knowledge. What of the candidate’s knowledge had manifested into something tangible? Other than having mentored people, was there a mentee of his who excelled and benefited from the knowledge gained from him? If given an opportunity to return to ICASA, what would he do differently to address the sociopolitical issues faced in the country? How would he use his knowledge to benefit ICASA?
Mr O Mathafa (ANC) said he would ask questions about the candidate’s experience as an ICASA councillor. On the ICASA funding model, what should be included or changed and from where should the funds stem? Based on his experience, was ICASA achieving its strategic objectives and what areas still required improvement? ICASA had some challenges, how would the candidate handle these? How would he keep himself up to date with technology?
Ms L Mathys (EFF) asked for clarity if he was appointed to ICASA in 2010. Was his first term 2022-2024? What happened in 2010? He was shortlisted and had to give a declaration on his businesses. The appointment had nothing to do with his declaration – was this an accurate statement? He had an impressive CV. In his term at ICASA, there were audit findings. She asked if the candidate was involved in the disciplinary matters raised. She noted the importance of continuation; how would he change a culture he was already part of for five years? She asked for clarity if the candidate was saying that he was not one of the officials implicated in the fruitless and wasteful expenditure.
Ms Mathys said the country had not seen a decline in data costs. Why did the ICASA Council not try to decrease the data costs in the country?
Mr Sithole said that there was a lack of regulating over-the-top (OTT) services. What was the candidate’s view on regulation of OTT?
The Chairperson said that she wanted to hear the candidate’s views on fair play. What was the candidate’s position on the fair play debate? Did he feel that ICASA should look at the convergence of the industry or should they rather look at consolidation? What was his view on market consolidation? Or should they introduce new players? What role could the regulator play in ensuring viable funding not just for the Post Office but also for SABC? Could ICASA play a role here? Looking only at regulation, what regulations needed to change?
Candidate 2: Dr Mandisa Nompumelelo Khumalo
The Chairperson asked how her experience led her to be considered for ICASA. What value could she bring?
Mr I Subrathie (ANC) asked questions about the SABC. ICASA regulated media broadcasts such as the SABC. Currently the SABC was faced with non-payment of TV licences. He asked if the candidate owned a TV and if her TV licence was up to date. What was her understanding of the challenges and how did the SABC seek to address such challenges? He noted the migration from television to online streaming services. The SABC Bill had intended to address the ongoing issues but the Seventh Administration had withdrawn the Bill. What were her reflections on this decision especially in the current political climate?
Mr Mathafa wanted to test the candidate’s knowledge of the ICASA mandate. He asked what the mandate entailed and how one would balance this between consumers and stakeholders. As a new applicant to the ICASA Council, she would have done research on its operations. What was her understanding of the resultant benefit of spectrum availability to the economy, connectivity and South African telecommunications users?
Ms Mathys asked how the candidate thought ICASA should have handled data costs and the spectrum sale. The response of service providers was that ICASA should have regulated this better. How could they improve on this? South Africa paid R26 more than Malawi for 1GB of data. If appointed, what would be the candidate’s first action in this regard? Data costs affected every person in the country.
Ms Mathys said that the Post Office was the only one licenced to deliver parcels under one kilogram. It was ICASA’s responsibility to issue licences. How would the candidate address this? There were companies operating illegally in the country. ICASA should have dealt with this and failed to do so. How would the candidate regulate this process?
Ms Bodlani asked what the candidate’s current work position was. How did she see herself transitioning from being a diplomat to entering ICASA? How did this set her apart from any other candidate? Having mentioned the SABC mismanagement and National Treasury needing to fund SABC, how would she manage the two? For what did she advocate?
Mr Sithole asked the candidate’s view on the funding of the SABC. What were her views on changing management of the SABC especially at a regulators’ level?
Mr Ngubane asked what government could have done to salvage the Post Office and how the ICASA regulator could have improved it.
The Chairperson said that the candidate had gained a true fan in the Post Bank as it wanted to continue paying the SASSA grants. What were the challenges of ICASA? How would she resolve such challenges if appointed?
In answer to the candidate’s questions, the Chairperson responded that the priority was to get the Post Office out of business rescue. If appointed, the candidate could assist the Committee in tightening the laws of protection. She informed her of the interview process and asked if the process was fair.
Candidate 3: Ms Cassandra Cordelia Gabriel
The Chairperson apologised for the technical issues they were currently facing in the meeting, which poorly affected the sound and there was a livestream break at 03:08:11.
The Chairperson asked the candidate what value she would contribute to ICASA? What were the challenges faced by ICASA currently and how would the candidate seek to address these challenges?
Ms Bodlani recalled the candidate previously worked at the Universal Service and Access Agency of South Africa (USAASA). Could she elaborate on the work she did there? What was her opinion on the Universal Service and Access Fund (USAF) being used to assist the SABC?
Ms Mathys would speak about the Post Office. There are many courier services which were not licensed in the country. What did the candidate think ICASA should do about this? She observed that large businesses did not regulate themselves. ICASA councillors needed to be equipped to stand on their own. She asked what qualities were needed to be an ICASA councillor. Since data costs were not regulated, ICASA could assist the country with lowering data costs. What qualities were needed for a competent councillor in ICASA?
The Chairperson said that the candidate’s passion and activism shone through the interview. If appointed to ICASA, she would be faced with advising the Competition Commission. For example, Vodacom may want to merge with other larger players, the costs were high and there was pressure on the candidate to advocate for consolidation. What would be her recommendation? If she had to advise the Minister on a policy directive on B-BBEE and Equity Equivalence, what would her view be on this?
Candidate 4: Dr Tshifhiwa Joshua Maumela
The Chairperson forewarned about the technical issues which greatly affected the sound.
The Chairperson asked for the candidate’s experience and expertise which would place him in good standing to be appointed to ICASA. She asked why the candidate would want to leave well-paying jobs to work at ICASA where he would be paid much less.
Ms Mathys said that as a councillor he would work with large businesses, how would he handle them especially when they attempted to bribe or influence him? It was his job to regulate. There was a failure to regulate the cost of data in the country. For what would the candidate advocate? South Africa paid R26 more than Malawi for 1GB of data. She asked to confirm that when necessary he would be able to stand up to large businesses which attempted to influence or intimidate him?
Ms Mathys asked if the candidate thought the country was behind in Artificial Intelligence (AI) and who would be best placed to develop the policy framework around this?
Mr Mathafa conversed with the candidate in Tshivenda asking him how he was feeling today. He said that he did not see a date of birth or a copy of his ID submitted. This was very important as they had to ensure that they covered all the demographics, including age. Was he comfortable responding to this question on livestream? What would be the candidate’s primary responsibilities at ICASA as a councillor? Access to affordable connectivity remained a challenge in rural areas and townships. What strategies would he employ to reverse this?
Mr Subrathie congratulated the candidate for being amongst the youngest selected for interview and for his other accomplishments. He advised him to be slower in his responses and not rely too heavily on his AI knowledge. South Africa needed to work towards consumer protection. What should the ICASA Council plans be to protect consumers against emerging technologies such as AI and cybersecurity? With AI and other technology, an effective digital strategy was required. What would be his plans to close the gap between communities? One of the apex priorities was employment and job creation. How did the candidate think AI would create more jobs?
Ms Bodlani congratulated him on his achievements. She wanted to speak about his commitment to ICASA. Based on his CV the candidate had very short terms in several companies, and she was concerned. What comfort could the candidate give the Committee about committing to ICASA? What could he say to the Committee about staying the course in his duties? How did the candidate translate the work he did for his PhD to the work required at ICASA? What would be his contribution to AI policy for the country? She asked what challenges he faced as he studied for his PhD with regards to the policy vacuum. Did he think ICASA could be more effective in collaboration with other regulators? If so, with which regulators should ICASA regulate?
Mr Sithole identified with the candidate in having grown up in the rural areas. What would he contribute to ICASA in bringing connectivity to the areas that were lacking it? On the licensing of small medium and micro enterprises (SMMEs) by ICASA, why were there small companies being enabled but not progressing? With the tools he had been exposed to, what would the candidate do differently?
The Chairperson said that sustainability and funding of the State-Owned Enterprises (SOEs) was very important to the Committee. Did the candidate see a role for the regulator in assisting the Committee with financial viability? She asked if the Universal Services Obligations were still relevant. If not, what would need to change? Had the spectrum auction reflected in the country as being beneficial? With ICASA being the regulator and licensing authority, was there anything to be done to assist with the financial sustainability?
Afternoon session
Candidate 5: Prof Mpho Primus
The Chairperson apologised for the technical issues.
The Chairperson asked the candidate to elaborate on who she was and why her skills would be valuable to ICASA?
Mr Subrathie said that South Africa was headed into a complex future with AI technology. Which players would play a critical role with the regulator to ensure that the country benefited from the digital economy appropriately? There was a challenge in balancing regulation with innovation. How would the candidate spearhead enforcing regulations to ensure the ethical use of AI?
Ms Bodlani congratulated the candidate on her achievements. She asked how the candidate would reconcile her previous position on the Advisory Board of the Pan African Information Communication Technology Association with the potential position at ICASA. As an AI expert, would her passion not be limited by being an ICASA councillor?
Mr Sithole asked the candidate on her view of funding the South African Post Office (SAPO) and management of the Post Bank.
Ms Mathys asked how the candidate saw her role of getting SOEs to be competitive. Was she familiar with the ICASA audit reports for the last five years? How would she improve this? If appointed, the candidate may engage with large wealthy stakeholders…... ICASA had dismally failed in reducing data costs in the country, how did the candidate see her role in contributing to the ICASA Council? What were the characteristics required of a councillor? She informed her of the courier companies operating illegally in the country. What was the candidate’s experience in working with companies set up to collaborate with government?
Mr Mathafa [inaudible due to sound and connectivity issues; livestream jammed at 05:57:00]
The Chairperson said, if appointed, the candidate would be part of a group of people who made important decisions for the country. She asked for the candidate’s view on competition versus regulation. Which would triumph the other? On transformation, what was the candidate’s view of the B-BBEE policy directive? The Chairperson noted there was a view in the country about ‘relaxing’ B-BBEE laws. Was the Universal Service Obligations framework effective? The TELCOs were not playing their part; what change could be made on this? How could they enhance universal access? What should the regulator of the future look like? There was 97% cell phone coverage but government institutions were operating without digital access to them.
She apologised to the candidate for the technical issues they faced.
.
Candidate 6: Dr Charles Alistair Lewis
The Chairperson asked the candidate, as a returning councillor, what positive impact had he made during his tenure and where could he have improved.
Ms Bodlani asked, having been part of the last spectrum sale, what was the most important lesson learnt? If the candidate were to write a report for incoming councillors, what advice would help them? Based on his experience, what skills had the candidate imparted to youth?
Mr Sithole said that the country had a challenge with emerging technologies. As a long-serving member of ICASA, what plan would the candidate have in place to protect consumers? What mechanism was in place for the funding of SAPO and management of Post Bank?
The Chairperson asked why ICASA was still lamenting about OTT services; why was there no movement? ICASA had the authority to regulate on Post Office matters. Why was the   regulator silent when the monopoly of the Post Office was eroded?
Mr Ngubane asked what the candidate’s view was on the withdrawal of the SABC Bill and current events at the SABC. Could he elaborate on unfinished business such as Transnet railways and Rain licensing? What more could he give to convince the panel to appoint him?
Mr Subrathie said that he was curious about the candidate’s thoughts on SABC however this had already been responded to. ICASA was governed by several pieces of legislation in delivering its mandate; some of the Acts had not been amended in over 15 years. If appointed, which areas would he recommend to keep abreast with technology? It had been said that government was too lenient on B-BBEE; what was the candidate’s view on this? As a senior member in the technology industry, what could the candidate do to fast track some of the plans in place? Seven years was too far along the timeline. In seven years’ time, AI might be redundant. He recalled conversing with a CEO who mentioned that one of the challenges faced was regulation.
Mr Mathafa asked if the candidate would say that the funding model was adequate and properly structured. If not, what innovative methods would he recommend to generate revenue? There was a narrative that the country was not able to grow in the sphere of technology due to the lack of political support. Did he agree with this narrative or not? Which areas required more legislative support from the Committee?
Ms Mathys informed the candidate that the EFF were the ones against the motion to increase the salary of ICASA councillors. During his ICASA Council tenure, what were some of its conversations on the Auditor-General’s findings on procurement costs, contract management, fruitless and wasteful expenditure? These findings were very damning for ICASA. Was he implicated in the fruitless and wasteful expenditure? She recalled the candidate saying that he had watched the downfall of the Post Office. She asked the candidate why ICASA had failed to reduce data costs in the country as South Africans pay R26 more for 1GB of data than Malawi. How was it that ICASA had not been able to regulate the cost of data? Was ICASA influenced by wealthy companies?
The Chairperson asked for the candidate’s view on the effectiveness of the ICASA structure. If not effective, what recommendations would he give on this?
Candidate 7: Mr Mathibela Selepe
(
some sound issues here
)
The Chairperson asked why the Committee should consider appointing the candidate. What were the biggest challenges affecting ICASA currently?
Ms Bodlani said that the Committee had not kept up with the times in updating policy which regulated the sector. How did the candidate see himself operating in a sector where the policy had not been updated but the technology was advanced? What would be the contribution of the candidate as a new councillor to ICASA? What did he wish to achieve?
Mr Sithole asked what strategy did the candidate consider for under-serviced communities.
Mr Ngubane mentioned the digital divide and asked what the candidate could do to assist in closing such a gap.
Mr Subrathie recalled hearing about 2G and 3G. He asked if South Africans benefitted from the saver spectrum. He recalled hearing that the candidate wanted to be regarded as a champion for universal access. South Africa’s mobile operators would be prohibited from activating any 2G and 3G devices by the end of December 2027. How should these deadlines be approached? And what transitional measures should be in place to ensure the smooth transition and to also ensure affordable access to the internet?
Mr Mathafa asked the candidate what his understanding was of the primary responsibilities of an ICASA councillor. What was the benefit to the country’s economy, with regards to the spectrum auction? What was the benefit to the country’s economy and the end users of communication services?
Ms Mathys acknowledged the mention of the material conditions of the people whom they were to serve. As a councillor, the candidate would interact with wealthy stakeholders who were influential and tended to bribe the councillors. Did the candidate have experience on this? And how would he maintain the integrity of his position?
The Chairperson said that she could sense the candidate’s passion for servicing under-serviced areas. She said that within three months, the telecommunications had made revenue of R75 000 000 000 off of the Auction Spectrum. Upon appointment, would he still support auctioning spectrum? There was a view of taking the spectrum not yet allocated and assigning it elsewhere, thereby creating a wireless open access network. Was this a better alternative to auctioning?
Candidate 8: Ms Mariza Jurgens
The Chairperson asked the candidate about her experience and expertise to make her suitable for ICASA. What did the candidate believe to be lacking in the regulatory environment in terms of accessing technology?
Ms Bodlani said that the sector was headed for a very complex future which the candidate would be part of. How could one use AI and cloud computing to benefit the digital economy and include everyone in the process? Taking into account the candidate’s interactions with ICASA, how would the candidate manage the limitations on the Funding Model with the current legislation?
Mr Sithole said that there was the challenge of dysfunction with SAPO. Upon appointment, how would the candidate handle the funding issues within SAPO? He said that the situation was rife in the South Western region of Gauteng, where there was lack of training on assistive technologies for persons with disabilities. In the Information and Communications Technologies (ICT) industry, was there enough being done to raise awareness in the neglected areas? What more could the Committee do?
Ms Mathys noted that the candidate was a member of the Consumer Advisory Panel of ICASA from 2018-2023. In that role did she think enough was being done in terms of regulation and policy? Where could improvements be made? The candidate had experience in engaging with the various stakeholders. What qualities were required of an ICASA councillor, especially when interacting with stakeholders who might attempt to bribe councillors?
Mr Subrathie asked a question about a Member of Parliament who had a visual impairment, and used assistive technological devices. He said that he was happy with the candidate’s depth of experience at ICASA. What were the candidate’s views on change management at the ICASA regulator leveL? Was ICASA ready for such a change? If not, what needed to change? Given the plethora of emerging technologies, what would be the most effective approach in the future? And was ICASA ready for this?
Candidate 9: Mr Jean-Pierre Murray-Kline
The Chairperson asked about the candidate’s skills and competencies which would place him in good standing for being part of ICASA. Given his vast experience and background in a different and more free environment, why would he think this was a natural progression in his career trajectory?
Ms Bodlani noted that the candidate’s highest qualification was a National Senior Certificate (NSC). What were his plans after earning his NSC? With his vast career experience, what had he done to specifically prepare himself for this role in ICASA? She asked what his view was on the current dire situation at SAPO.
Mr Sithole said that the candidate’s CV stated that he was a cyber defender; what input could he provide to protect consumers, given the challenges with emerging technologies?
Mr Ngubane asked if the candidate thought that ICASA could be more effective when collaborating with other regulators. If yes, which regulators should it collaborate with and in what capacity?
Mr Subrathie asked if the candidate was aware that this was a job with working hours: 08h00-17h00? How would the candidate manage his current career interests alongside his position at ICASA? Having assessed the website, what was the candidate’s diagnosis of the challenges faced by ICASA?
Mr Subrathie said that the SABC was very important to the public but its existence was currently under threat. Was he aware of the SABC challenges? Did he learn of the SABC Bill? Was he aware of the funding model challenge and did he have a solution to propose? He asked if the candidate had researched the challenges faced by ICASA.
Ms Mathys wondered if the candidate had read the ICASA Act. What skills would he contribute to the Council in line with the stipulated skills set? How would he ensure that his character reflected the mandate?
The Chairperson said that part of the work of a regulator was to stimulate competition and foster economic growth. Observing the regulatory environment, were there any regulations the candidate was aware of which hampered innovation and creativity in the sector?
Candidate 10: Mr Melusi Mthethwa
The Chairperson asked the candidate about his expertise and experience which would place him in good standing for the position at ICASA. She asked why the candidate considered departing from lucrative employment in the private sector to enter the public sector. Would he say that the problem which he had observed was a regulatory issue?
Mr Ngubane asked about the impact of the digital divide. What were the candidate’s views on ICASA conducting a socio-economic impact assessment system as a standard? What was the impact of not conducting this?
Mr Sithole said that the candidate had been in the technological scenario for a long time. What was his view on the funding of SAPO?
The Chairperson said that the candidate grew up in the sector; he had a very detailed CV. What could they have done differently, as a country and the SOEs?
Ms Bodlani said that in order to know where to go, they needed to know where they were coming from. What lessons had the candidate learnt which they could take with them to the future? She suggested that the candidate share his thoughts through writing a book. The sector was rapidly moving along. She recalled earlier discussion of licensing and service obligations. The service obligations were of an inferior quality. Upon appointment, what would he do about this?
Mr Subrathie said that the definition of insanity was: doing the same thing repeatedly but expecting different results. Would the Committee continue to do the same thing over and over again? In the candidate’s experience with ICASA; what was view on the appetite for change? He did not want to have this same discussion again in a decade’s time. Upon appointment, would the candidate be the instrument for change? Had the candidate evolved as a person to keep up with the pace?
Ms Mathys said that the candidate echoed many of her own thoughts. In the mining industry, it was seen that private stakeholders would rather pay penalties than adhere to regulations. The country was very lenient in terms of regulation and monitoring. What was a sound intervention that ICASA should monitor? There was a divide on this. His CV stated that the candidate was involved in frequency modelling. She listed a few radio stations whose frequency did not change despite being in different environments. Why was this not addressed by ICASA post apartheid? She asked for clarity if ICASA sold the frequency.
The Chairperson recalled the candidate asking a rhetorical question earlier: would a low-earth orbit (LEO) satellite  be the ‘magic pill’ to resolve the challenges in under-serviced areas? She was interested to hear the candidate’s thoughts on spectrum allocation. Cell C lacked funding and this caused its exclusion in the spectrum auction. The Committee had not yet resolved transformation which had been part of the intention of the spectrum auction. If appointed, how should ICASA conduct the upcoming spectrum release? Why did he think the WOAN was discontinued? If the spectrum was allocated to a broadband operator would this have made an impact?
Candidate 11: Ms Karabo Mohale
The Chairperson asked why the candidate thought she would make a valuable contribution to ICASA. Given the candidate’s experience, which area would best suit for her?
Ms Bodlani said that Parliament’s process in recommending ICASA councillors was quite cumbersome. Where did this leave the candidate if she applied elsewhere? The candidate’s nomination letter had been signed by the Vice-Chancellor of UNISA. She asked how the contents of the letter and vote of confidence from the Vice-Chancellor would impact her own work at ICASA.
Mr Sithole mentioned the challenge of emerging technologies. As a young professional, how would the candidate ensure protection of consumers?
Mr Ngubane asked questions on the licensing of high demand spectrum. There was an announcement for network operators to st
op activating 2G- and 3G- only devices by 31 December 2024
. How could ICASA better prepare for the upcoming retirement?
Ms Mathys said that the candidate had echoed many of her own thoughts. She asked for the candidate’s understanding of not reducing data costs in the country. If appointed, how would she manage such an issue? Perhaps the candidate could use her knowledge and experience of having served on many boards at such a young age. Had the candidate looked at the audit reports of ICASA over the past few years? She would have observed that ICASA was notorious for not paying service providers on time. How would she tackle this? Where did the candidate see the future of governmental departments leading AI research and policy development?
Mr Subrathie said that he believed in the mantra: leave no one behind. This applied to being considerate of the poor living in rural areas. SAPO was currently facing endangerment as was the SABC. Was the candidate aware of the challenges faced by these two entities? What would be her input about resuscitating these two entities?
The Chairperson said that the mission of ICASA was to regulate in the public interest. If appointed, what tools would be needed to regulate? Given her background in economics, what would be some of the policies on economics driving the regulation?
The meeting was adjourned.
