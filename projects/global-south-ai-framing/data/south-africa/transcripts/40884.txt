Report on the utilization of categories of leave in the public service
The Committee received a briefing from the Department of Public Service and Administration on the development and employment of young people in the public sector. The Public Service Commission also briefed the Committee on the management of leave benefits in the public service.
The Department reported that in 2025, youths accounted for approximately 27% of the employees in the public service, totalling over 347 000 individuals who fell in the under 35-year-old age group. The highest youth representation was in administrative, finance, supply chain and technical roles, with women constituting many youth employees in the public sector. Members were also informed of several systemic challenges, including the budget constraints that impacted the creation of posts and the low absorption of interns following their internships. Among the measures the Department would use to address this was to enforce youth hiring targets in departments' annual performance plans, and to scale up partnerships.
The Committee also heard that the Department was developing a policy proposal to introduce a voluntary early retirement scheme, which would enable older public servants to exit without incurring financial penalties. While appreciating efforts to remove unjustified barriers to entry into the public service, such as experience requirements, some Members were concerned about nepotism in placements, which they felt undermined the value of graduate internships. Concerns were also raised that interns were often assigned to menial tasks unrelated to their training, and were not provided with sufficient supervision or exit interviews to assess the programme's impact. The Committee called for formalised exit pathways and proper mentorship models for interns and graduates. Members also questioned the fairness and transparency of recruitment practices across departments, calling for more transparent recruitment processes and improved communication about opportunities.
In the Public Service Commission's (PSC) presentation on the review of public sector leave utilisation from 2020 to 2023, capped leave emerged as a big concern. The Committee was informed that by the end of 2023, capped leave, as a contingent liability, was valued at R16 billion. This figure continued to grow due to inflation adjustments, salary notch increases, and other factors. Most of this liability lay in the education and health sectors, where a significant portion of the workforce held substantial leave credits. The PSC warned that, beyond the financial risks, a human capital crisis was also looming, as many of these leave-holding employees were approaching retirement.
The Committee raised concerns about the high rates of sick and incapacity leave, particularly in the health and education sectors. Members called for better ways of verifying the legitimacy of medical certificates and investigating suspected patterns of abuse. The unequal distribution of study leave benefits, which appeared to favour senior management over lower occupational levels such as cleaners and clerical staff, was another concern for Members. The Committee called for transparent oversight of senior management's leave utilisation and for improved reporting on provincial disparities in access to leave benefits. There was also a call for improved data on the demographic breakdown of those accessing various forms of leave, as this information could be key to workforce planning and professional development.
Meeting report
Opening remarks
The Chairperson said that a capable and competent government remained a key strategic priority of the Government of National Unity (GNU) administration. Building a capable and competent government meant instilling a culture of adherence and of good governance in the public service. Further, building good governance in the public service was at the centre of the Department of Public Service and Administration (DPSA), through developing sound norms and standards for departments to ensure uniformity.
Human resource management practices remained a critical pillar of professionalising the public service. The Public Service Commission (PSC) conducted a study on leave management in the public service, which provided the Committee with a broader picture of the management of various leave benefits in public service. Moreover, June was considered Youth Month in South Africa, and an invitation was extended to the DPSA to provide a report on the impact of appointing youth through a graduate recruitment scheme in the public sector. Government had a problem of senior employees, and it must begin to prioritise youth employment in the public service.
Leave management in the public service
The PSC report examined how different types of leave were used in South Africa's Public Service. It highlighted concerns about leave management and offered recommendations to improve efficiency. The key findings addressed matters of capped leave, sick leave and study leave. The report also covered how hybrid work impacts leave utilisation. Regarding capped leave, the report reflected that the cost of capped leave keeps rising due to salary adjustments.
Education and health sectors had the most employees with capped leave, increasing future pay-out liabilities. The recommended measures to address these included an early pay-out strategy or enabling employees to convert capped leave into pension contributions. With regard to sick leave, use of sick leave had spiked in 2022, likely due to the return to full office-based work post-COVID-19.
Further, an average of six to eight sick days per year per employee was recorded. Temporary incapacity leave (TIL) averaged 44 days per employee per year, and it was stated that this could strain departmental resources. Recommendations made to address this were that there must be an introduction of stricter sick leave monitoring and encouraging employees to use wellness programmes.
On the matter of study leave, it was reported that only 1.4% of public service employees used study leave. Employees in the education and health sectors accounted for the majority of study leave usage, and more female employees than male employees took study leave. It was recommended that various departments should promote continuous learning while ensuring leave did not impact service delivery.
On hybrid work and leave utilisation, studies had revealed that remote work reduced sick leave usage, and that a full return to work led to increased sick leave absences. It was recommended that the government should explore hybrid work policies to balance employee well-being and operational efficiency.
Further recommendations that were made to government departments included improved monitoring and auditing of leave records. The report also showed that there was a need to strengthen employee health and wellness programmes to reduce sick leave. Encouragement of continuous professional development through study leave was recommended, as well as the exploration of more hybrid work arrangements for better productivity and mental health support.

Employment of youth in the public service
The DPSA's report outlined South Africa’s efforts to enhance youth employment in the public sector, emphasising policy frameworks, recruitment reforms, and structured development initiatives. As its key points, it noted that youth employment was a national priority, aligned with constitutional principles and transformation goals. The approach adopted aimed to remove barriers such as prior work experience requirements for entry-level positions.
Regarding matters of governance, the Minister for Public Service and Administration (MPSA) sets employment norms and standards, while the DPSA oversees policy implementation, governance, and compliance.
Regarding youth empowerment and development programmes, initiatives adopted include internships, learnerships, apprenticeships, and partnerships with universities and technical and vocational education and training (TVET) colleges. It was also reported that the National Youth Service provides work exposure and skills development.
On the improvement of recruitment and reform in section processes, entry-level job advertisements no longer required prior experience. Moreover, digital application systems improved accessibility, and integrity screening ensured ethical hiring.
Challenges faced by the Department included budget constraints and inconsistent programme delivery, which hindered progress. The proposed solutions included artificial intelligence (AI)-driven recruitment transparency, stronger partnerships with sector education and training authorities (SETAs), and targeted youth employment quotas. The document emphasised that youth employment was more than compliance, and encouraged structural reforms and early-career support that would foster long-term public sector transformation.

Discussion
Ms P Xaba-Ntshaba (ANC) asked about ways in which technology could be used in the Department of Employment and Labour to detect sick note certificates issued by the same medical practice granting various employees sick leave. This would require investigation of unethical behaviour, as most government departments were still using manual submissions for leave application. How did departments ensure that the medical certificates were captured on the records of employees? How did sick leave, especially when taken perpetually, affect the performance of employees?
She asked whether the PSC could investigate how those who abused the sick leave system performed in executing their duties, as well as how they had performed over the last four years. She said posts that did not require prior working experience attracted a lot of interest from young people, and asked how these posts could be accessed. She asked about the impact that the high leave incidence had on the work of health and education institutions; they also experienced shortages of staff. Although acknowledging the importance of study for staff members in these departments, she expressed concern about the number of staff taking study leave, stating that it would affect the work in the Department.
Ms L Potgieter (DA) expressed concern about the mental and physical well-being of staff who did not take leave in the Health and Education Departments. Considering that there were pay-outs for the leave days not taken, she asked whether substitutes could be used, to allow the staff members an opportunity to take leave. In the long-term, the staff members in these departments dealt with sick people and a lot of children every day, and this could impact their mental and physical health.
Regarding study leave, she said it seemed that the Department and the National School of Government (NSG) were unable to ascertain the value of the courses being taken and if the correct people had access to these opportunities. It had previously been mentioned that these opportunities were used as exit strategies, through obtaining the qualifications in order to get better employment elsewhere. She asked whether there had been investigations into the actual value of these study leaves, what people were studying, and the value that these options brought to the government. Have there been any engagements with the Department of Health and the Department of Education about the risk of losing experienced professionals?
On youth employment, she said there was an issue of the politicisation of youth employment in some provinces, and at the centre of it was the use of the expanded public works programme (EPWP), or temporary work opportunities, to create the idea that these were a form of empowerment measures. This was not really the case, as the way in which the EPWP was carried out showed that there was a gap between the national, provincial and local governments. The quotas reflected a struggle to balance equal opportunities where there were no entrance barriers. She asked if there were improved ways of advertising so that the public could get to know of the equal opportunities, and therefore apply.
Referring to government entities that were continuously in need of government bailouts, she asked the Department whether it would make sense to sever these entities from the public service and the Minister’s oversight in terms of norms, standards and remuneration frameworks.
Mr T Langa (IFP) asked the PSC for the specific causes that had contributed to the increase in sick leave utilisation in 2022. How had these been accounted for in the recommendations? Given the prevalence of capped leave in the education and health sectors, what targeted interventions were proposed to address these sector-specific challenges? He also asked about the risks associated with offering a once-off 2/3 pay-outs of capped leave, especially in the context of collective bargaining and fiscal sustainability.
What was the difference between the average number of study leave days between senior management service (SMS) members and non-senior management staff employees, and what was the impact this had on the equitable access to professional development opportunities? What trends were observed in the age and gender demographics of the employees who took study leave, and how could the data be used to inform future skills development planning in the public service?
Mr Langa asked about the mechanisms in place to detect and prevent the misuse of sick leave and their practical effectiveness, according to the report findings. To what extent had hybrid working arrangements and digitisation impacted the utilisation of sick leave and overall employee well-being after the Covid-19 pandemic?
Referring to the recommendations, he asked how the proposed collaboration between DPSA, the Government Pension Fund (GPF) and the Government Pensions Administration Agency (GPAA) could be structured to operationalise the idea of converting capped leave into pension value. What criteria would actuaries and economists use to conduct the proposed cost analysis for capped leave, and would the departments have input into this process? How would the capping of temporary incapacity leave be balanced with the need to accommodate severe and long-term illnesses without penalising employees? Lastly, he asked what standard framework or tool the departments would use to audit sick leave records annually, as well as how uniformity and compliance would be ensured across departments.
Ms W Tikana-Gxothiwe (ANC) asked about leave days, and why senior management from various Departments were not included in the statistics reported. She asked what the Department did when the leave days exceeded the required number of 36 days in a cycle. What was expected of the Department? She said that sometimes officials used medical practitioners to prolong sick leave to pursue other work opportunities. She asked how the Department verified the authenticity of sick leave applications, as sometimes employees could be on sick leave for more than six months or a year while on the payroll of various departments. She asked what became the status of leave that was capped until retirement -- were the employees paid out for this leave, or did the state withhold payment? If it was capped, she suggested that this might be a way of exploiting government by accumulating capped leave up to the retirement stage, if these capped leaves were paid for.
On the matter of interns, she said some interns were retained for a year, and sometimes for two. An unfortunate situation happens when there are vacant posts in the various departments in which the interns underwent training during their internship programme. This happens when the panels take people from elsewhere, rather than those who were capacitated, leaving the latter unemployed despite the availability of vacancies. She said nepotism by senior members needed to be investigated in the various departments and could be proven, as this was what was currently happening within the departments. This disadvantaged those who were part of the internship programmes. In most departments, the interns were neither rotated nor allocated mentors to guide and prepare them for the work ahead. She wanted to know about how interns were being monitored within the departments, how they were capacitated, mentored and tested, and if they had an exit interview to understand whether they had benefited from the programme. Some interns were used as private assistants without doing the work they had actually studied for.
Ms S Gcilishe (EFF) said that study leave was beneficial to the elite, because most public servants could not access it. The report reflected that the 1.4% of the employees who took and benefited from study leave were in top management. Although sick leave abuse was framed as a work problem, the report lacked the scrutiny of senior officials who also misuse leave while enforcing strict rules when it came to lower-level staff members. She wanted to know whether this was the reason why it was only 1.4% of workers who got study leave. Did cleaners and clerks get opportunities for upskilling for free? If over 20 000 national employees took study leave, how many of them were low-level workers?
She asked whether black women in rural areas were being denied study leave. Why was there a difference between Gauteng and other provinces, such as Limpopo and the Eastern Cape, when it came to study leave? The report reflected that Gauteng was currently in the lead when it came to study leave granted, leaving provinces like Eastern Cape and Limpopo with fewer opportunities.
She wanted to know whether sick leave applications by senior managers were being monitored, or if these rules applied only to poor people. Seeing that more than R10 billion was reported in sick leave costs, how much of this was accumulated by SMS members? Who was monitoring the SMS members' sick leave? She was suspicious that top management officials might be the ones greatly benefiting from the sick leave system. The report prioritised the cost saving over the welfare of workers, and ignored how understaffing and poor working conditions drove sick leave abuse. What was the strategy to ensure that there was no leave abuse? What measures were being taken to address understaffing and poor working conditions? The report made no reference to issues such as unpaid overtime, burnout and other issues contributing to the high number of sick leave applications.
Why did nurses and teachers have the most capped leaves? When it came to sick workers having to prove that they were ill, how did the government consider employees who may not have access to health services and could thus not obtain medical notes confirming that they were not well? She sought an explanation from the Department of Employment and Labour on how helpful the removal of the work experience requirements had been. Why have vacancies in this category decreased in the past three years?
Mr J Malinga (MKP) asked the PSC about permanent incapacity leave, and how the policy of 36 days in a three-year cycle for incapacity leave compared to the study leave policy. On the issue of learnerships, apprenticeships, student internship, national youth service, TVETs and universities, she asked if the DEL had future programmes for youth development.
PSC's response
Mr Vusumuzi Mavuso, Commissioner, Public Services Commission, said that not much focus was placed on what people chose to study. Monitoring of study leave by departments was of great importance. There were responsibilities to monitor and evaluate what had been happening within the different departments, to have oversight and be able to report and account. No discernible evidence had been found indicating that leave had been abused, and this was a systemic issue. The PSC did not investigate individual leave patterns or reasons for leave applications by every individual, as this had not been part of the studies conducted.
On the matter of capped leave, he said that the PSC could not prescribe to the DPSA, National Treasury and the Government Employees Pension Fund (GEPF) what they needed to do about implementing its recommendations. This was something within their own areas of competence and responsibility to decide what needed to be done. The responsibility of the PSC was to raise the matters, identify issues that required attention going forward, and indicate whether these could be responded to. It was not to recommend the formation of a particular structure between the DPSA, National Treasury and the GEPF.
The use of capped leave, when one had exhausted one's normal annual leave, or leave days had become an issue, was paid out at the retirement or death of the particular individual. The PSC had merely raised what their observations had indicated, and some of the recommendations that they had suggested. While they may not be definitive, they may have to be considered for what they entailed. This was because capped leave predated the seventh administration, so issues around capped leave were shaped by the fact that people had preferred to work in the public sector for a long period of time.
With regard to the education sector and the capped leave patterns, although teachers did not work during holidays, they had to do other administrative work. Although this may be a complex issue, further investigation could be done to determine the resolutions that needed to be made, but this would require engagement with the various departments, as well as the Department of Labour. Leave was often perceived as an entitlement, and there may be leave that an individual had a right to, but others were perceived as an entitlement.
Going forward, there may be a need to improve on the technological capabilities of examining sick leave and the notes that have been provided. This would determine whether a person had been diagnosed by a medical practitioner, to determine the genuineness of the sick leave application. The PSC had recommended the development of strict monitoring and a centralised leave system to manage leave of this nature.
With regard to study leave, the intention was to encourage everyone to apply for study leave. There was no situation where only SMS members could apply for study leave, nor should it be perceived to be elitist in its nature. Anyone could apply for study leave if they were able to prove that they were studying and that their studies required them to prepare for and write examinations. There were arrangements in place, and they were not restrictive -- they allowed anyone pursuing their studies to be afforded study leave.
Regarding wellness and mental health, Mr Mavuso stated that there must be a balance between the work an employee does and how it impacts their lives and mental health. Taking leave must be emphasised, especially when an employee is entitled to a certain number of days per year.
Dr Kholofelo Sedibe, Deputy Director-General (DDG): Leadership and Management Practices, PSC, said that engagements with various departments had been made on the recommendations, and some requests had gone to the DPSA, National Treasury and the NSG. There would be follow-up with the Department of Health and the Department of Education so that the PSC could assist them in understanding the recommendations and finding a way of implementing them.
With regard to study leave, the leave dispensation allowed the employer to grant employees study leave when those employees were studying in areas relevant to the mandate of the Department, or their responsibilities within the Department. This was approximately 22 days of study leave per annum. It was also indicated that the bulk of the number of leave days -- especially study leave and sick leave -- tended to fall within level 2 to level 12 employees. These were the categories which included employees such as cleaners, security guards and administrators. Although not covered in the study, many departments prioritise granting bursaries to their cleaners and security guards for them to study. Some of the employees register for programmes that allow them to obtain any qualification within their capacity to study.
The DPSA guidelines and frameworks supported employees who needed the aided programmes and those who needed to upgrade to diploma and degree qualifications. The intention of the presentation was to reflect that the majority of the beneficiaries of study leave were within a particular age group, and within this age group there were administrators and many employees who were not SMS members. What was highlighted was that the senior employees constituted a small number of employees who pursued their studies. This was important to mention as part of the principle of continuous professional development and lifelong learning, and was opposed to the notion that elderly public officials were reluctant to take up studying opportunities.
With regard to capped leave in the health and education sectors, some contributing factors originated during the pre-democratic years, when employees were prevented from carrying annual leave over from one year to the next. The current frameworks prohibit the transfer of annual leave indefinitely from one year to the next, as studies have revealed instances where employees abused the sick leave system in order to accumulate annual leave days. Capped leave was considered a contingent liability because the rate of pay-out was calculated at an employee’s current level of employment, even for days earned while they were at a lower level or different employment position. It was therefore recommended that employers should address such issues. Regarding the pay-out of capped leave, the huge financial implications of the formula of how pay-outs were made were not foreseeable, and the PSC was dealing with this issue at a systemic level to protect both the employees and the employer.
Ms Renel Singh, Director: Employment Management for the Public Service, DPSA, referred to the difference between study leave and normal training that takes place within a department. Regarding study leave, employees must develop a performance development plan, which entails training that takes place within a department, based on what was identified in the plan for a particular employee. On the other hand, study leave takes place when an employee decides to pursue a qualification, giving them an opportunity to apply for a bursary within the department, and the ability to receive time off through a study leave policy. This allows the employee time off to focus on the qualification of their choice.
The 1.4% of the public service that accessed training were employees that accessed actual qualifications to be pursued through study leave. National Treasury could do a cost-based analysis with actuaries to assess what the cost would be at the present time. The PSC had established that at the end of 2023, the cost was R16 billion, and at the end of 2025, it might be R2 or R3 billion more. Therefore, based on their cost-based analysis and the affordability of the state, National Treasury needed to determine whether it was feasible to enact some of the recommendations made in the report. This applied to all recommendations made in the report. The relevant institutions would be engaged to advise them on the recommendations made, based on evidence-based research. This was with respect to some of the things that had come up based on the different leave categories.
With regard to cost-saving and welfare, employees had a right to leave, specifically in relation to the legislative framework. The Labour Relations Act and the Basic Conditions of Employment Act, accompanied by collective agreements negotiated in the councils, determine the number of days, extent of leave and types of leave. This was backed up by section 3 of the Public Service Act, which empowers the Minister of Public Service and Administration to issue directives that operationalise these processes from resolution to directives in the public service. A directive was issued on leave in the public service that isolates specific types of leave, specifying annual capped leave, family responsibility leave, and other leaves that the public servants had at their disposal. The General Public Sector Bargaining Council (GPSBC) resolution that was issued in 2024 had been a culmination of a long engagement process between the council and the employer as the DPSA, to find a centralised policy on special leave. The special leave resolution had then been issued by the MPSA as a directive and determination based on negotiations with the council. Employees did have recourse when prevented from going on leave, as there were clear and specific policies, processes and frameworks in place for this HR practice.
Regarding capped leave, prior to 1 July 2000, any employee who had accrued leave was allowed to accumulate that leave. After 2000, there was a change in the policy imperative and reforms put in place whereby people who had capped leave were allowed to carry this over. However, no employee was allowed to accumulate leave after 1 July 2000. Annually, depending on one’s tenure of service, an employee got either 26 or 30 days of annual leave which had to be used in the applicable year for which it was located. Departments also leveraged an additional six months to finish these leave days, failure which would require proof that for operational reasons an employee was unable to go on leave despite attempts being made. Thereafter, a leave pay-out was made and constituted a separate cost for annual leave.
While the study had touched on annual leave, this was not the mandate of the report. Capped leave could be accessed only when annual leave was exhausted. Considering that many employees had been in the public service for a long period of time, they sat with an allotment of 30 days in a year. This usually meant that they had to exhaust these days before they could access their capped leave. What the report recommended was that departments must consider allowing officials to take their capped leave as part of the leave management process. This might result in potential operational costs, and a huge number of capped leave days might result in more time being spent away from work.
The other options require the relevant institutions to find plausible responses to recommendations made by the DPSA. These would address whether the implementation of this recommendation was possible, and if it was feasible in terms of a cost-based analysis. With regards to the TIL, an average employee in the public service usually consumed 21 days over a three-year cycle, which keeps within the 36 days requirement. However, when these days had been exhausted, an employee could apply for temporary capacity leave, which was subjected to the prescriptions according to policy relevant to these leaves.
On the matter of elitism, the report covered all levels of employment, and was specific with regard to categories of ages. It was not directed at SMS members only, and addressed all employees at all levels within the public service. The number of below SMS employees that accessed study leave was between 18 000 and 20 000 over the three-year period, while the number of SMS employees that accessed study leave was between 291 and 293. This was proportionate to the number of employees found within these employment categories.
With regard to the technology aspect, the report stressed that the DPSA needed to create a centralised management system that could be accessed by departments. It was of importance to have digitisation of the leave management process, as this would quell some of the questions raised, from a tracking and monitoring perspective, to make leave reports accessible.
Regarding HR practitioners and their ability to understand the leave policy and processes, there have been cases whereby they had failed to consider the pillar policies when handling leave applications. There was a need for a proper lead management course to be developed by the NSG in an e-learning format to educate HR practitioners and professionals. The PSC had also reported on the digitisation of HR processes, as well as hybrid working conditions. A webinar would be held by the PSC with respect to hybrid working arrangements, which would look at the extent to which these arrangements had led to improved productivity and influenced employee wellness. Engagements would be taking place, and consistency between departments would be aided by uniformity between these departments. Where gaps existed despite policies, this was taking place because departments were using a manual process to apply for leave, and this was a problematic process. The report had thus looked at how many departments had digitised this process. The PSC had a digital system in the form of an e-leave process, but many departments did not have this, which made tracking challenging. It was the prerogative of the Head of Department to implement these practices within the departments, which would ease the tracking of sick leave applications and the monitoring of related trends.
On the question of affordability of healthcare services, every department was required to have an employee health and wellness programme. This programme required the employer to involve institutions like the Government Employee Medical Scheme (GEMS) to come to departments and conduct screening centres where employees could access various healthcare services. For the benefit of employees, the PSC recommended that this should be expanded and that more sessions were made available during the year. This would require engagements with various departments based on recommendations articulated in the report.
DPSA's responses
Mr Willie Vukela, Acting Director-General, DPSA, said information was made available every Friday, directing potential employees as to where to furnish their curriculum vitae (CV). Regarding the external sourcing of employees, he said that government departments operated according to open competition, and there were no absorptions. Where positions were available, advertisements were made, and positions were made available to any person.
With regard to the lack of mentors and monitoring of performance, interns normally drew up personal development plans (PDP) in terms of their mentorship and inductions. It was a requirement that when existing interns left, interviews must be conducted and captured for future development of programmes. The presentation sought to establish the impact the decision to remove unjustifiable barriers had made on the intake of the youth in the public service. This made possible the absorption of the youth and graduates who did not have the experience to enter into the system. Departments had to submit their plans and performance to the Department of Performance Monitoring and Evaluation (DPME) and the DPSA in terms of their performance during the year's cycle.
Dr Anusha Naidoo, Acting DDG: Human Resources Management and Development, DPSA, on provincial politicisation of work opportunities, said that no law in the public service refers to the deployment of work opportunities to family members. Thus, all employees must be appointed on the basis of merit, integrity and ethics. The public service had been strict in investigating possibilities of nepotism, although public service members had been creative in their efforts to perpetuate nepotism. Where such conduct took place within departments, it did so outside the code of conduct of the public service.
On the issue of bailouts, the law allowed the MPSA and the DPSA to function insofar as provincial government was concerned. Anything beyond entities that do not report to the DPSA, such as the MPSA or the DPSA, was not mandated to comment on what could take place in those departments. The Portfolio Committees and oversight bodies could reach out to the departments and invite them to account.
In addition to the personal development plans (PDPs), interns also did quarterly reporting, and departments must analyse these feedback reports and report to their own governance structures. This was to account for why interns may not be used in the roles that they applied and were taken for. She said that National Treasury had austerity measures on the advertisements of posts. In the past, these measures limited the availability of these posts, but from 1 April, departments were free to advertise these positions again.
Closing remarks
The Chairperson thanked the PSC and the DPSA for the presentations, and commended the research conducted and the data presented. The theme reflected in the presentations addressed the management of sick leave and youth development, focusing not only on creating employment opportunities but also on creating development paths for the youth.
The meeting was adjourned.
