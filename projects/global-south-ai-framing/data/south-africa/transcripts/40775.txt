Title: Presidency briefing on socio-economic impact on National State Enterprises Bill
Date: 23 May 2025
Committee: 
URL: https://pmg.org.za/committee-meeting/40775/
============================================================

Meeting Summary
Video
The Committee met virtually to receive a presentation from the Presidency on a study of the socio-economic impact of a proposed central holding company to manage state state-owned enterprises (SOEs). The study into the National State Enterprises Bill was conducted in terms of government's Socio-Economic Impact Assessment System.
The Committee heard that the total running costs of the holding company would be approximately R195 million in the first year of operations, rising to R215 million in the third year. An amount of R615 million had been requested from the National Treasury. This would be seed capital for the holding company for the first three years. Thereafter, its operations would be funded by dividends from its subsidiaries.
Members expressed some concerns about the formation of a centralised SOE and about its ability to address many challenges facing existing SOEs related to governance and financial sustainability. They questioned whether subsidiary SOEs could generate sufficient profits to pay dividends, ensuring the sustainability of the holding company.
Some Members argued that centralising power in the Presidency might lead to a lack of accountability. They emphasised that the priority of SOEs should be their developmental mandate rather than commercial interests. They also suggested that the Presidency could be missing an opportunity to enhance regulations aimed at addressing governance challenges within SOEs.
Members requested a detailed presentation that included a range of assumptions and case studies. This presentation should address both the short-term and long-term quantitative effects, outlining positive and negative consequences.
Additionally, the presentation should specify who the stakeholders were and how they would be affected. Members felt that the presentation did not adequately identify the potential impacts on employment, local businesses, income distribution, and economic growth resulting from the establishment of the holding company.
The Committee also adopted an oversight report which included concerns about the discontinuation of a R2 billion subsidy to Denel from the Department of Defence. The report also noted concerns regarding confidence in the current management of Denel and security issues encountered during oversight visits.
Meeting report
Presidency briefing on socio-economic impact on National State Enterprises Bill
Ms Pulane Kule, Chief Policy Analyst, Presidency, made a presentation on the socio-economic impact of the National State Enterprises Bill. The presentation provided an overview of government’s Socio-Economic Impact Assessment System (SEIAS) as well as an independent analysis of the Bill by the Department of Planning, Monitoring and Evaluation (DPME).
She said the Bill sought to establish a rationale and foundation for adopting a centralised shareholder model for the ownership and management of strategic state-owned companies (SOCs) as subsidiaries of a holding company. This approach was intended to enhance their contribution to South Africa's development needs. The goal was to create a flexible organisation capable of competing as a SOC while remaining insulated from political interference and regulatory obstacles to growth.
Regarding the cost of implementation, the total running costs, inclusive of human resources, had been budgeted as approximately R195 million in the first year of operations, rising to R215 million in year three of the holding company's operations. The DPME had requested an amount of R615 million from National Treasury. This would be seed capital for the holding company for the first three years. Thereafter, its operations would be funded from dividends to be received from subsidiaries of the holding company.

Discussion
Ms L Dunjwa (ANC) said she found the presentation insightful. She proposed inviting the Financial and Fiscal Commission to assist in gathering more information as they proceeded with the process. She emphasised the importance of establishing a state holding company. She highlighted the need for professionalisation and the recruitment of personnel with the necessary skills. It was essential to get this right.
She asked how the holding company would balance commercial interests with its developmental responsibilities. She asked what measures would be implemented to ensure the holding company's self-sufficiency and whether state-owned enterprises (SOEs) would generate sufficient profits to sustain the holding company's funding after the three-year allocation of funds.
She asked what action would be taken if SOE subsidiaries failed to generate sufficient revenue to support the holding company's operations. To what extent was the state capitalising the holding company, and which aspects of the Bill would address the state's responsibilities regarding SOEs’ financial sustainability? Would there be sufficient resources to maintain the holding company’s commercial and developmental mandates?
The ANC would not shy away from prioritising the issue of professionalisation in the public sector. In the past, there had been weaknesses in this aspect. What role would the Department of Public Service and Administration have in appointing people to key positions?
Ms K Christie (DA) asked about the process for the appointment of directors based on integrity, merit and ability. Before the early 2000s, SOEs functioned well, and Eskom would get international awards for its ability to generate and transmit electricity affordably. If the decentralised approach was working well, why was it necessary to bring in a new system as proposed by the Bill? Cost analyses of the State Asset Management SOC
(
SAMSOC) indicated that this would be costly for SOEs while they drown in debt.
She questioned if this centralised approach would not result in corruption, malfeasance, political interference and mismanagement. She agreed about the professionalisation of critical positions in the public sector.
Sixty percent of OECD countries used this system, but she wanted to know why the other 40 percent did not. Did the Presidency believe that this expensive project was necessary, given that these SOEs had worked effectively before?
Inkosi N Buthelezi (IFP) hoped that financial bailouts by the state were not on the list of interventions for SOEs, and asked for clarity on their rehabilitation plans. He further questioned the objective of the state holding company. This company would either succeed or fail, but he did not hear much about the risks associated with establishing it.
Most of the SOEs were on the brink of collapse due to their high levels of debt, which had piled up over the years. He also asked if there were any initiatives to involve communities, and which communities had been heavily impacted by the failures of SOEs.
He encouraged the Presidency to respond in writing to questions if they did not have the answers now. Questions should not fall through the cracks and remain unanswered. Responses were critical to Parliament's oversight function.
Mr L Montana (MKP) expressed concerns that the presentation contained contradictory points. He noted that South Africa was a highly divided society. This was evident in how it approached problems, often viewing them through prejudicial, ethnic, and racial lenses. He observed that discussions about SOEs seemed to start in different historical contexts and emphasised the importance of reaching a common understanding.
SOEs were not a recent phenomenon. Before 1910, South Africa was not a unified country. When the English and Afrikaners decided to govern the country independently, excluding black Africans, under British imperial rule in 1910, they established SOEs, some of which delegated their responsibilities to the private sector, such as railway companies. Railways and telecommunications were essential for connecting South Africa.
He recalled that the South African railway system faced significant challenges in the 1980s, largely due to the state's fiscal challenges. Transnet and Prasa emerged from the economic restructuring that took place in the late 1980s, with Transnet being created in 1992 from the former South African Transport Services. He argued for a detailed presentation and engagement on this topic, asserting that attributing South Africa’s challenges solely to state capture in the past decade was flawed.
While he acknowledged the inefficiencies associated with SOEs, he highlighted the substantial economic services that they provided to the private sector and noted that private companies had benefited significantly from subsidies offered by SOEs. He mentioned that Eskom was once functioning well but encountered problems later. Examining the industry structure revealed that it was designed to subsidise the mining sector and the broader economy.
Most of the mining companies transporting goods to Richards Bay or Durban were heavily subsidised. Trains would often return empty, with unused slots and no penalties imposed for this inefficiency. He stressed the importance of revisiting the historical context of SOEs, arguing that selective historical analysis would not aid in formulating effective policy.
Mr Montana said he agreed with the presenter that there were critical success factors that the proposed Bill would not address. The overall effectiveness of the model depended on the assumptions and figures integrated into it. Different scenarios should be tested within the model to achieve optimal policy outcomes.
He underscored the necessity of having financially viable and sustainable SOEs in South Africa. After the introduction of competition and the establishment of the regulatory framework, Eskom began utilising its assets in new ways. Some assets not directly related to electricity production were not included in the tariff structure or Eskom’s financial calculations, which impacted their ability to manage debt.
He criticised the Finance Minister's approach to addressing the R20 billion debt gap within a R6.6 trillion budget over the Medium Term Expenditure Framework (MTEF), stating they were "chasing lizards, not crocodiles."
He argued that testing various assumptions and figures in the presented model could reveal that certain policies and legislation undermined the South African economy. He pointed to the significant capital investment Eskom made as it built power stations to provide affordable electricity. Despite this, Eskom had not fully recovered its initial investments. Mining companies that benefited from Eskom’s capital expenditures should have allowed Eskom to maintain a major shareholding in those companies linked to its power stations. This could reduce electricity costs significantly, and he criticised the government for failing to consider this perspective.
Mr Montana insisted that the proposed model could not yield diverse outcomes when different assumptions were applied. On a technical level, he emphasised their obligation to adhere to established policies. He rejected the argument that the primary challenge facing South African SOEs was debt. The situation should be assessed comprehensively, taking into account the financial positions of SOEs and the debt they could manage over time in the interest of national development. He noted that the costs of independent power producers (IPPs) had been subsidised at the expense of Eskom.
Part of having an SOE was ensuring that the national interest and security of supply were protected. If different assumptions were applied to this model, the critical success factors would come out differently. Although he agreed with the presentation, he cautioned against using one set of assumptions in this model.
Mr C Matiwane (EFF) expressed concerns about the processing of the Bill, questioning whose interests it served. He highlighted that the problem statement in the presentation identified poor governance as a central issue. However, the rationale behind the Bill and the proposed solutions to address this challenge did not align. He questioned the logic of addressing poor governance by centralising power in the Presidency.
Mr Matiwane asked how the state planned to tackle issues of accountability and lack of transparency by granting the President sole discretion to appoint and dismiss board members. He described this approach as reminiscent of the colonial era when governors held unchecked powers, noting the expansion of presidential authority since 2018.
He felt that the socio-economic impact analysis provided a picture of contending interests, which were the developmental and commercial objectives. How would the Bill respond to the socio-economic impacts if it focused more on the commercial objectives? He questioned the socio-economic costs of focusing on the commercial objectives.
A state enterprise was not for profit, but had a developmental mandate, and as a result, there would be state interventions. It was not purely profit-driven. He disagreed with claims that state bailouts meant the SOEs were failing. SOEs were established for developmental objectives, not profits.
Governance challenges in SOEs would not be addressed by the establishment of the holding company. There was a need to professionalise the public sector, which could be done without establishing this holding company. The rationale for establishing this holding company was missing, as well as whose interest it would serve. Was the plan to privatise SOEs?
The Chairperson appealed to Members to comment and pose questions based on the presentation. The nitty-gritty details would be discussed later during the clause-by-clause exercise and motion of desirability.
Mr D Bergman (DA) emphasised the importance of acknowledging the comments and concerns raised by Nedlac. There was an expectation for SOEs to be profitable, as they had been in the past. The core issue was whether this legislation sought to create an ideal situation or to address the gaps and weaknesses that had led to the decline of South African SOEs.
The Constitution was drafted during a time of hope for peace and reconciliation. While it presented an opportunity for renewal, it also failed to anticipate some of the weaknesses highlighted in the Zondo Commission's findings. No one envisioned that the South African Constitution would lack the authority needed to address the significant issue of funds being illicitly taken out of the country, or the challenge of unprofitable SOEs.
The Presidency was attempting to adopt a model similar to that of China, and fortunately, Members had visited there to study its effectiveness. However, South Africa's Constitution did not permit the same stringent measures against corruption offenders as in China. Therefore, it was crucial for South Africa to accurately benchmark itself. He illustrated that "small dogs" (smaller economies) often acted boldly towards "big dogs" (larger economies) because they did not realise their limitations. South Africa needed to fully comprehend its economic size, current trajectory, and future direction.
He expressed major concerns that the current legislation did not adequately address these issues and questioned whether they were merely substituting one set of problems for another. He wondered if the Presidency had considered maintaining the status quo after reviewing submissions on the Bill, and rather building regulations to protect SOEs. He highlighted that profitable SOEs, such as Eskom, were essential, especially given their considerable municipal debt and limited competition. This raised the question of whether the Presidency already possessed the necessary powers to implement measures that would ensure these enterprises were on the right path.
Mr M Gana (Rise Mzansi) stated that there were various interpretations of the objectives of SOEs, alluding to their mandates. He emphasised the need to clarify the concept of a developmental state.
During the presentation, the Presidency claimed that they had requested seed capital amounting to R650 million and hoped that within three years, the holding company would be funded by the dividends from its subsidiaries. This implied that the SOEs were expected to generate profits within three years. However, realistically, many of these SOEs had not turned a profit in years. Despite this, the Presidency confidently presented this claim to justify the need for seed capital. He urged the Presidency to provide more transparency about their projections that the holding company would no longer require funding from the national budget in three years.
Mr S Subrathie (ANC) said one of the greatest challenges facing SOEs was interoperability. The proposed holding company aimed to address interoperability issues, centralise skills, and provide focus and direction to tackle inefficiencies. He inquired about the measures that would be implemented if the subsidiary SOEs failed to generate sufficient profits to support the functions of the holding company.
Additionally, he questioned whether the principles outlined in the Bill and the national strategy would apply exclusively to the companies within the holding company or be extended to all SOEs to ensure strategic alignment. He noted that as the national strategy for the holding company was developed, it should include parameters for establishing subsidiary companies that focused on emerging economic sectors or other purposes determined by the SOC board.
He also asked what measures in the Bill would link the reporting of the entity to various social and development objectives to ensure that SOEs did not only report their commercial business, but also measured their social developmental impact beyond assessing them only based on profits.
He dismissed the claims that the Bill aimed to privatise SOEs. The Bill sought to strengthen them, address inefficiencies within them, and ensure well-managed boards of directors.
Ms M Chauke-Adonis (ANC) sought to understand the Presidency’s perspective based on its analysis of the risks associated with the implementation of the Bill.
She asked the Presidency to clarify how it planned to differentiate between the commercial and developmental mandates of the holding company. It was crucial to comprehend how the holding company would generate its investments for sustainability without relying on the SOEs, and how it would mitigate any risks that might arise. She inquired about the consequence and accountability measures that would be implemented to assess the social impact of the holding company and its subsidiaries, and asked where this was articulated.
Ms Chauke-Adonis also questioned what the matrix and measurement tools would look like beyond the initial three years. She believed that the legislation should incorporate measures that enabled Members to evaluate the effectiveness and successful implementation of the Bill.
Finally, she asked about the ultimate goal of ensuring that SOEs were protected. This question needed to be addressed comprehensively and assess the performance and relevance of various SOEs to ensure they were functioning optimally. The discussion should focus on how to ensure the viability, sustainability, and resilience of SOEs to prevent their collapse.
Mr S Gama (MKP) emphasised that the impact assessment was crucial for addressing various issues, particularly through a qualitative evaluation of potential impacts. However, he expressed concerns that the qualitative evaluation was insufficient. He noted that there was no quantitative evaluation, except for the R650 million requested to establish the holding company. It was essential to consider both short-term and long-term quantitative effects, including both positive and negative consequences. A more comprehensive analysis would have provided clarity on the scope of the assessment and the policy description.
Additionally, the presentation did not specify who the stakeholders were, how they would be impacted, or what their interests entailed. While he recognised that the assessment addressed relevant laws, regulations, and compliance requirements, it failed to identify the potentially significant impacts on employment, local businesses, income distributions, and economic growth resulting from the establishment of this holding company.
He also enquired how the subsidiary portfolio would be managed and what the positive outcomes of this Bill would be. Overall, he felt that the assessment did not adequately cover all critical aspects.
Responses
Ms Kole summarised the Members' comments and sentiments, noting that there was a consensus on the need to delve deeper into the history of SOEs. The aim was to highlight what had worked well, what had not, and the factors that had contributed to the current status quo. Members had also raised concerns about the professionalisation of SOEs, the associated risks for the sustainability of the funding model beyond three years, and the need to enhance the report to include quantitative evaluations.
She would request that comprehensive written responses be provided to all comments and questions raised by the Members. Additionally, some historical context on the SOEs and their evolution over the years would be included. For instance, Japan was currently investing in freight logistics through the use of underground tunnels, prompting questions about the future landscape given the global shifts in technology and artificial intelligence.
SOEs must play a role in addressing issues like poverty, unemployment, and spatial inequalities. They should also focus on building the necessary infrastructure to ensure the delivery of sustainable basic services.
Several of the articulated issues and details of the implementation plan would be addressed in the strategy that would be developed for the holding company.
Mr Montana said he appreciated the comments from the Presidency. He felt that the presentation’s role needed to be clarified, but hoped that the Department of Public Enterprises would come and present its policy directive itself, and unpack the issues that Members had outlined.
The Chairperson noted the comments. She indicated that the Department would appear before the Committee with an opportunity to engage further on all aspects of the Bill. They would be granted a week to submit the responses.
Ms Aadielah Arnold, parliamentary legal advisor, stated that once the public participation process was concluded, the Department would respond to all feedback and questions raised. Her role would focus on addressing the legal issues that had been identified. However, she would not address policy questions related to developmental versus commercial objectives, as those were within the Department’s purview.
Consideration of Committee Oversight Report
The Chairperson tabled the Committee's draft oversight report for Members to consider.
Ms Chauke-Adonis asked about the provision of a researcher and content advisor to assist the Committee.
The Chairperson said the content advisor for now was Mr Rodney Mnisi.
Ms Chauke-Adonis emphasised the importance of having a content advisor to guide Members on areas the Committee needed to consider and prioritise. At the start of sessions, the content advisor should present critical discussion points before moving into the main presentation.
The Chairperson acknowledged the comments and emphasised that this matter had been elevated. Mr Mnisi was well-versed in the subject of SOEs. He had been participating in the Committee for a year now. The issue of the researcher would be communicated once a response had been received from the House Chair.
Mr Mnisi took the Members through the oversight report.
Mr Gama raised several concerns regarding Denel that he intended to submit in writing. Two key issues should be highlighted in the report.
Firstly, Denel had previously received a R2 billion subsidy from the Department of Defence, which was discontinued in 2019. This subsidy appeared to be the root of many challenges faced by Denel. The Committee sought to understand why the subsidy was terminated, but current management was unable to provide an answer, as they were not part of the decision-making process regarding its discontinuation. This R2 billion subsidy represented guaranteed income for work Denel was to perform for the Department of Defence.
Secondly, there was a lack of confidence in the management's commitment to moving Denel forward. As a result, it seemed the company would continue to struggle for some time. That needed to be flagged for the Committee's ongoing oversight responsibilities.
Mr Bergman noted the report as read by Mr Mnisi and agreed with Mr Gama’s comments. He promised to make a written submission, but he did not have any substantive issues with the report.
Ms Dunjwa suggested differentiating their findings, observations and recommendations for each SOE in the report. She also agreed with Mr Gama’s comments. She suggested highlighting the security concerns encountered by Members during oversight visits. They had experienced a scary situation in Gauteng and felt that for the next oversight visit, they must be accompanied by local police officers. With those comments, she moved for the adoption of the report.
The Chairperson said the written submissions from Members would enrich the report.
The report was adopted with the proposed comments and amendments.
The meeting was adjourned.
