Meeting Summary

2024/25 Annual Reports
The Portfolio Committee on Small Business Development convened to be briefed by the Auditor-General of South Africa (AGSA) on the 2024/25 annual performance of the Department of Small Business Development and its entities, and to consider its fourth quarter programme. The meeting did not have quorum, meaning that no resolutions or decisions could be made. However, based on the guidance from the Content Advisor, the Committee agreed to hear the AGSA presentation.
The Department and the Small Enterprise Finance Agency (SEFA) had achieved a clean audit. However, both the Small Enterprise Development Agency (SEDA) and the Small Enterprise Development and Finance Agency (SEFDA) had received an unqualified audit outcome with findings. In terms of performance, the Department had achieved 88%, SEFA 67%, SEDA 60%, and SEDFA 72%.
Concern was expressed regarding the merger of SEDA, SEFA and the Cooperative Banks Development Agency into SEDFA. This had been intended to simplify support processes for small businesses, but the result had been leadership instability, with vacancies in key positions, poor integration of financial and non-financial support systems, and underperformance and audit qualifications in its first year. Members called for consequence management and further investigations into the merger process.
Members were also alarmed by the increases in irregular, fruitless and wasteful expenditure. They commented that the performance did not align with the budget expenditure, and were concerned that small businesses were not being assisted effectively and impactfully. They insisted that vacancies in senior management positions needed to be filled, as they created a lack of leadership. This was critical, as there were already concerns regarding weak political and administrative leadership. The Committee noted the need for strengthened oversight over the Department and entities.
Meeting report
Opening Remarks
The Chairperson welcomed Members of the Committee, officials from the Office of the Minister, and Auditor-General South Africa (AGSA) to the meeting, which was to receive a briefing on the 2024/25 annual performance of the Department of Small Business Development (DSBD).
She indicated that the Committee normally met on Wednesdays, which was why some Members were unable to attend due to other obligations. She asked if the meeting met quorum requirements.
Mr King Kunene, Committee Secretary, indicated that the meeting did not form a quorum. This meant that the meeting could continue to sit to receive the briefing, but no resolution or decision could be made.
The Chairperson was hopeful that more Members would be able to join throughout the course of the meeting, and asked if the Members were satisfied to continue the meeting and hear the presentation, reiterating that no decisions would be taken.
Ms C Mafagane (MKP) asked if the agenda could be adopted without a quorum.
Mr Kunene indicated that it would be okay to adopt the agenda -- it would be an indication that the Committee would hear the presentation. Any items on the agenda that required adoption or a resolution, however, would not occur. This included the consideration and adoption of the 2025 fourth term Committee programme.
Ms Mafagane cautioned the Committee to avoid talking for the sake of talking. If there was no quorum, the meeting was not legal. The meeting could not be recorded as having happened. It was also a disadvantage to other Members who were not present, as they would not hear AGSA’s presentation. She requested guidance on the matter.
The Chairperson said she had requested the opinion of Members on proceeding with the meeting, but no Member had made any comment at that time, and she had taken it as agreement to proceed. The meeting could continue to hear the presentation, but no resolutions or decisions could be taken without a quorum.
Ms B Mathulelwa (EFF) said that the Chairperson should know and guide what should happen. Without a quorum, committees would normally wait until a quorum was reached. The Committee could decide to listen to AGSA’s presentation for their own benefit without the meeting being recognised. Without a quorum, the meeting was not legal.
The Chairperson indicated that the Committee would not take any decisions or resolutions, as there was no quorum. She had not indicated that it would be a formal meeting.
Mr Sibusiso Gumede, Committee Content Advisor, said it was not a typical meeting that required a quorum. Nothing was being adopted. The Committee’s strategic plan document had highlighted a strategic objective to scale up interactions with Chapter 9 institutions. AGSA was a Chapter 9 institution, and had been invited to empower Members of the Committee. Meetings of this nature should not have to stop because a quorum has not been reached. There was no intention for anything to be adopted. The issue of a quorum could arise at the next day’s meeting, when items had to be adopted.
The Chairperson noted this.
Ms Mafagane thanked the Content Advisor for his guidance. If ASGA was present, the Committee should hear the presentation as it may be the only opportunity to hear it. She was happy for the Committee to proceed and to hear the presentation.
Ms Mathulelwa indicated that she was also happy for the meeting to proceed with the presentation.
The agenda was adopted, and the Chairperson indicated that the meeting would proceed with the briefing by AGSA.
AGSA presentation on the
Small Business Portfolio audit outcomes
Ms Nompakamo Matanzima, Business Unit Leader, AGSA, introduced the presentation, highlighting the importance of the Small Business Development portfolio in terms of promoting and developing entrepreneurship in South Africa, as well as stimulating economic growth and job creation.
AGSA said the DSBD and the Small Enterprise Finance Agency (SEFA) had received clean audit outcomes. The Small Enterprise Development Agency (SEDA) and the Small Enterprise Development and Finance Agency (SEDFA) had received unqualified opinions, with findings. The DSBD had maintained the clean audit opinion from the previous year.
In terms of performance against targets, the DSBD had achieved 88%, SEDA 60%, SEDFA 72%, and SEFA 67%. The non-achievement of key performance indicators would result in the portfolio not fulfilling its mandate and negatively impacting the stimulation of economic growth of the country and job stimulation and creation.
Irregular expenditure had increased from R180 000 in 2023/24, to R6.2 million in 2024/25. SEDA had been the top contributor (R4.2 million). There had also been an increase in fruitless and wasteful expenditure, from R1 100 in 2023/24 to R1.4 million in 2024/25. SEDFA was identified as the top contributor (R1.4 million).
AGSA indicated that the audit outcomes across DSBD and its entities reflected mixed performance and persistent internal control weaknesses. The gap between performance and budget utilisation highlighted an inefficiency in translating resources into service delivery impact. Recurring internal control deficiencies undermined operational effectiveness and compliance. Audit committees and internal audit should be held accountable for delays in the implementation of recommendations and audit action plans.
AGSA stated that to strengthen the system, the Committee should exercise its role by enforcing integrated reporting systems, filling key vacancies and compliance controls, and consequence management.

Discussion
Mr H Kruger (DA) thanked the AGSA for an informative presentation, and stated that it had been easy to understand. He expressed concern regarding the irregular expenditure, and was hopeful that the Department would provide feedback on this issue.
He suggested that the Content Advisor create a ‘score card’ for the Committee which it could discuss once a month. This would ease the Committee’s oversight over the Department, allowing it to become aware of and flag issues in the Department before the external audit occurred.
Ms Mathulelwa did not understand why the meeting was not being streamed, or on the parliamentary website. This issue had been raised previously. The streaming of the meeting would allow Members to go back and have a record of the meeting.
The Chairperson indicated that there was a link to the meeting.
Ms Mathulelwa had concerns about the merger of SEDA, SEFA and the Cooperative Banks Development Agency (CBDA) into SEDFA, saying that currently it had not produced any expected outcomes. The expectation was that it would be easier for entrepreneurs to engage with the Department via SEDFA. Instead, SEDFA has been a cause of instability, with key positions unfilled and weak integration of financial and non-financial support systems. Its underperformance was a demonstration of the collapse of the system at the level where small businesses were meant to be helped. She highlighted an example of small businesses that had applied for assistance several months ago and were still waiting. According to these businesses, the Department had indicated that they were waiting for the budget, but the budget had been allocated months ago and yet people were not being assisted. Despite being newly formed, SEDFA had already incurred material findings on financial statements, vacancies in key positions and weak internal controls. This reflected a poor merger process and poor political leadership.
There had been issues experienced with the Minister prior to this. The Minister had convened with the Committee only once during the current year. Despite being a new entity, which Members thought would have a positive impact, SEDFA had experienced several problems. She felt that the entity should have performed better. Irregular and fruitless and wasteful expenditure had increased. SEDFA had failures in financial discipline. This reflected a leadership crisis and an absence of accountability at political and administrative levels.
The Department achieved only 88% of its targets, while SEDFA continued to fail in achieving key indicators despite spending over 90% of its budget. This meant that money was being spent without outcomes being achieved. This was an inefficient use of public funds, and evidence of systemic mismanagement and weak oversight. Certain programmes in SEDFA had failed to disburse funds to small businesses and entrepreneurs. More than 100 micro, small and medium enterprises (MSMEs) had been waiting for assistance for almost a year. She felt that the Minister was good at releasing advertisements, but not at following through with disbursements. Some adverts for tenders had also been cancelled without informing the public and applicants. Consequence management was not being implemented.
Ms Mathulelwa asserted that the audit committees and internal audits had failed to drive accountability. The audits that were occurring were not legitimate, as those that were presented showed a positive result, but this did not match service delivery, and corruption continued. She recommended that ASGA’s findings be translated into disciplinary action and financial consequences for accounting officers who presided over maladministration. She urged that senior management vacancies be filled. There had to be a forensic audit into the SEDFA merger process to assess governance weaknesses, unfilled positions and transitional failures. There had to be a full review of the systems, and underperforming implementation had to stop. She suggested that there should be quarterly reports to the Committee on consequence management, detailing actions taken against officials responsible for irregular, fruitless and wasteful expenditure. AGSA has not acted on anything in terms of corruption found during the Committee’s oversight. A reprioritisation of budget should focus on direct township and rural enterprise support, rather than administrative overheads.
AGSA should provide tangible and helpful recommendations. Finding the same recurring issues in audits year after year without any corrective measures was useless. She rejected the celebration of compliance-driven audits while entrepreneurs continued to struggle without access to finance, infrastructure and markets. The Department had to transform from being a bureaucratic reporting entity into a developmental driver of inclusive economic growth. Until leadership accountability, data integrity and genuine service delivery were prioritised, the Department would remain a symbol of administrative mediocrity and failed transformation. She cautioned AGSA, and indicated that she was not pleased with the audit outcomes. She did not want an AG that used artificial intelligence (AI) to perform audits. AGSA should go and engage with entrepreneurs affected by corruption in the Department.
Ms Mafagane said that the Committee would do its best to hold the Department to account.
The Chairperson thanked AGSA for the report, stating that she was happy with the work done and the understanding the report had provided in order to effectively engage with the Department. The Committee would address the issues with the Department. She said the Department always seemed to have an answer to the Committee’s questions. She was hopeful that the Department could move towards a clean audit.
AGSA's response
Ms Matanzima thanked Members for their engagement, and said she had noted their comments.
Regarding the AGSA's recommendations to the Committee, she highlighted slide 21 of its presentation. It had acknowledged that there were persistent internal control weaknesses and non-achievement of targets. As part of the Committee’s oversight, it recommended that there be monitoring throughout the year, and that the Committee make use of the audit committee and internal audit. These were key governance structures to address the highlighted issues. She urged the Committee to engage with these structures.
AGSA had noted weaknesses in the DSBD's integrated reporting system. The vacancies were also an issue. She recommended that the Committee request regular updates in this regard. This would enable the Committee to exercise its oversight.
It was important to ensure that there was impactful service delivery within the Department and its entities. It was critical that key indicators be achieved. The Committee should monitor this throughout the year. The Committee should also work to ensure that the information provided was credible.
AGSA had conducted site visits as indicated in the presentation. Good work had been done within the Department and its entities.
The Chairperson thanked AGSA for their response and engagement.
The meeting was adjourned.
