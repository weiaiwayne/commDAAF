Date: 28 Mar 2025
Committee:

Meeting Summary
Video
WC Appropriation Bill 2025
The Standing Committee on Local Government, Environmental Affairs, and Development Planning met virtually to deliberate on Vote 9 in the 2025 Western Cape Appropriation Bill, focusing on environmental affairs and development planning.
Mr Anton Bredell, Western Cape Provincial Minister of Local Government, Environmental Affairs and Development Planning, emphasised the growing global and local environmental challenges, including wildfires, drought, and biodiversity loss. He highlighted the importance of ecosystem health and sustainable economic growth to ensure climate resilience.
The Department presented a R1.2 billion budget for compensation of employees, disaster prevention, air quality monitoring, and the green economy. It referred to operational and staffing challenges.
CapeNature representatives echoed the urgency of biodiversity preservation, linking it to tourism and economic growth. They stressed the importance of immediate post-disaster spending and intergovernmental collaboration to minimise wildfire impact.
Members raised several concerns, including delayed documentation, the Department’s mandate on social equity, slow licence issuing, and waste management in informal settlements. Questions were also asked about the underfunding of CapeNature, the environmental impact assessment system, law enforcement resources, and the need for Artificial Intelligence integration in planning. Concerns were also raised around Karoo water projects, landfill site licensing, and the slow rollout of socioeconomic development programmes.
In response, departmental officials detailed their multifaceted strategies for addressing waste management, environmental enforcement, water quality, and planning efficiency. They acknowledged resource constraints but highlighted interdepartmental cooperation and innovative measures, such as citizen science and AI-driven planning. They defended the non-appointment of an environmental commissioner, citing constitutional amendments in progress.
The Vote was ultimately supported by the majority. The African National Congress and Patriotic Alliance expressed minority views against it, citing the budget reduction and process issues.
Meeting report
Department of Environmental Affairs and Development Planning
Budget Vote
Mr Anton Bredell, Western Cape Provincial Minister of Local Government, Environmental Affairs and Development Planning, made opening remarks. He said that global attention was currently on political and trade alignment, but this did not mean that environmental issues were less pressing. He cited some global climate change incidents as examples. He noted that even the First World was grappling with these issues. Locally, there were challenges of wildfires, and the next drought was one dry winter away. The best strategy to minimise the impact of climate-related events was to ensure the health of ecosystems. The regulation of human activities was now more important than ever before and providing water security would be a challenge. Biodiversity was being threatened. Many plant and animal species in the Western Cape were categorised as critically endangered. To address and fund these challenges, it would be crucial to grow the economy in a manner that was in balance with the protection of biodiversity and ecological systems. This would ensure the support of sustainable economic growth while future-proofing the province through improved climate change and disaster resilience. He thanked the officials for coming up with a suitable strategy for this, within the confines of the available budget.
Mr Gerhard Gerber, Head of Department (HOD), Environmental Affairs and Development Planning, tabled the formal presentation to the Committee. He noted that they were at the beginning of a new five-year term and had played a role in informing the provincial strategy. The presentation covered the following areas:
Budget overview:
The total allocation was R1.2 billion, of which 82.7 percent was for compensation of employees. The transfer to CapeNature was R348.7 million, of which 60 percent was for operations and 40 percent was for salaries.
Key priorities:
Air quality monitoring:
An amount of R5.5 million was allocated for station repairs. Ten
stations were currently operational.
Disaster prevention:
An amount of 19.5 million was allocated for wildfire and flood
management by CapeNature.
Green economy:
A focus on energy and water resilience and township action plans.
Challenges included a high vacancy rate, with 61 staff aged 55 or older, as well as limited operational budgets.
Remarks by CapeNature
Ms Marguerite Loubser, Chairperson CapeNature Board, was invited to make some remarks. She reiterated the importance of biodiversity in building the economy, including through tourism. She said she was encouraged by the commitment, enthusiasm and resilience she had seen when the board interacted with the staff at CapeNature. She thanked the Minister and the HOD for their unwavering support.
Dr Ashley Naidoo, Chief Executive Officer (CEO), CapeNature, said their work was set out in a strategy map. It was centred around conserving the Western Cape’s biodiversity and dealing with floods and fires. He spoke of the budget that had to be spent immediately after floods to gain access to reserves to maintain fire breaks and later to clear the plants which contributed to fires. They were currently doing this through a special grant from the Department of Infrastructure, for which they were grateful. He commended the collaboration between the city, province, CapeNature and other stakeholders to minimise wildfires.
Discussion
Members discussed a concern raised by Mr N Constable (PA) that the documents for the meeting were distributed late and Members had not had enough time to prepare. The Chairperson said that the Minister of Finance had only tabled the budget the day before. There were views that the meeting should have been held later. There was no deliberate intent to circumvent processes, and the concerns raised would be noted in the records of the meeting and passed on to the relevant structures for consideration in the future. It was also suggested that Members air their views on the process through their representatives at the Programming Committee, which approved the timeframe.
Ms B Stoffel (ANC) asked about the Department’s mandate to promote social equity. What work was being done in the informal settlements, where poor living conditions directly impacted environmental health? She cited the example of a pre-directive that had to be issued in September 2024 to force the City of Cape Town to clear waste in Dunoon and said that many other areas faced similar cases. What specific measures was the Department taking to address these issues and how did this budget respond to them?
According to the Western Cape Constitution, the appointment of an environmental commissioner was mandatory. What was the rationale behind the refusal to appoint one, in disregard of that document?
Ms Stoffel said the Department should be firmer in how it dealt with waste management in municipalities, citing challenges in the management of landfill sites. She noted that the Department had received 184 environmental Impact assessments (EIAs) and 765 non-EIA applications. She asked what the R9.5 million allocated for the EIA system would be spent on. She also wished to know how many of the received applications were successful.
What were the reasons for the decrease in the Department's own receipts? What were the reasons for the decrease in the budget allocation for environmental policy planning and coordination?
She was concerned that CapeNature was taking too long to issue licences in the rural parts of the province.
She sought further clarity on the closure of landfill sites in cases of municipalities under financial recovery plans, which would mean the removal of waste from one area to another. Were there other options?
Mr N Masipa (DA) asked why there was no allocation for cost of compensation for CapeNature in the Appropriation Bill, unlike other programmes. He also wished to know if the department had begun deliberations on the Preservation and Development of Agricultural Land (PDAL) Bill, intended to safeguard agricultural land while supporting initiatives such as the Jobs4Growth strategy.
Mr G Marais (FF+) asked for details of the R32 million allocation under enforcement mechanisms. More boots were needed on the ground in terms of enforcement. He asked about the earmarked allocation of R19.5 million for disaster prevention measures, wildfire management and others. Was the Department comfortable that this was enough to cover unforeseen events such as floods during the winter season? How was the amount determined? Lastly, on water quality, he had not heard anything said about the rehabilitation of polluted rivers and estuaries.
The Chairperson asked about the money spent on air quality monitoring stations. Was there any intention to add to the current 10 stations in the province, working with the national department and South African Weather Services? He also asked how the Department envisaged the use of artificial intelligence (AI) being factored into functions like development planning and others, and what this would mean in terms of human resources.
Mr Constable asked about the rollout of the Regional-Socio Economic Projects (RSEP) Programme, noting that the HOD had said it would be rolled out to 14 municipalities. Were there plans to roll it out to more, and what was the timeframe? He also asked the Minister if he had had a conversation with the national water ministry on the water projects in the Karoo National Park and Beaufort West. If so, what was the progress of the process? How many unlicensed waste facilities were there in the province and how much assistance had been given to municipalities that still utilised these, including those that did not have the financial capacity to manage them?
Responses
The HOD, Mr Gerber, said that people could report environmental concerns or complaints to the Public Protector, the Human Rights Commission (HRC), the Public Service Commission or the Petitions Committee of Parliament, amongst others.
Regarding the environmental commissioner, a proposal had been tabled for the Western Cape Constitution to be amended, as it was drafted before the current governance structures and institutions were in place. The commissioner would not be able to determine remedial actions which were as binding as those of the Public Protector, but could only make recommendations. The HRC was the only other structure with such powers.
The Department’s mandate on social equity was indeed to ensure ecologically sustainable development while promoting responsible social and economic development as set out in the Constitution. There were several constitutional and legislative instruments that gave effect to this. There were oversight mechanisms and support for municipalities to ensure they fulfilled their responsibilities on such things as waste removal. Municipalities had their own integrated waste m management plans, as did the province.
Mr Gottlieb Arendse, Chief Director, Environmental Quality, said that the issue of waste management in informal settlements was indeed a challenge. There was a waste management forum to coordinate the activities of municipalities. Urbanisation had a huge impact on the ability of municipalities to render these services. Illegal dumping was also a challenge, which required the attention of law enforcement authorities. It was a multi-layered strategy that comprised a number of interventions.
Mr Saliem Haider, Director, Waste Management, said they were finalising a document on the collection services in the provinces, with a focus on informal settlements as well. Illegal dumping had become a national issue, and pressure was being put on municipalities to address this. Compliance notices and directives to the City of Cape Town were issued where necessary, as referred to in the question. They were also looking at infrastructure needs and requirements. If funding was accessed to ensure better support for municipalities, this would be done in the next two years.
Mr Ayub Mohamed, Chief Director: Environmental Governance, Policy Coordination and Enforcement, said they issued pre-directives to municipalities where necessary to ensure compliance with the duty of care. Where municipalities did not comply, prosecution would be the next step, and there were a number of these cases currently with the National Prosecuting Authority (NPA) for processing.
The province also had a Green Scorpions unit, like the national department. There were 16 officers in the unit, including investigators. Of the allocated R36 million, R18 million was set aside for environmental enforcement. There were vacancies, so the staff complement was not enough to deal with the transgressions, which had led to a prioritisation system being put in place. Some good work had been done despite this, and the unit had won some awards at the National Environmental Compliance and Enforcement Lekgotla (ECEL). There was also an active complaints line for issues related to the environment.
The CapeNature CEO, Dr Naidoo, said their budget was just over R420 million per year, from transfers as well as income generated from park visits. They spent about R243 million on the compensation of employees, which meant a 60/40 split between salaries and operations respectively.
The Minister said lessons were learnt from the 2017 drought. The conversation on water in the Karoo was an ongoing one. The national department understood the importance of the borehole projects, although the response on proceeding was negative at the moment. The hydrologist and engineering teams were focusing on the current system, and there were also skills shortages in that area. The Department had stopped the trucking of water in the area because it was expensive. The reservoirs had picked up and were currently at 50 percent, which was being closely monitored. The Garden Route Municipality had also stepped in to assist. Expansion of the Blossoms Project was being considered so that water could be put into the Klein Karoo Water Scheme and provided to the Beaufort West area.
The matter of the environmental commissioner would come up at the Premier's Committee as it was the custodian of the Constitution.
The budget needed to be balanced so that programmes continued, and the Treasury had done well in this area.
Dr Joy Leaner, Director: Air Quality Management, said that some years back, there were 12 air quality monitoring stations in the province.These were reduced to ten due to budget and human resource constraints, and at this time, there was no opportunity for any additions. Only three officials managed the stations, against the national guidelines for at least five officials for ten stations. The stations reported in real-time through a network managed by the South African Weather Service.
Mr Haider said 75 landfill sites were not licensed, with 14 having since lapsed. The relevant municipalities were informed of the process to follow in that regard. A service provider was appointed to conduct external audits at 21 facilities that had never been audited before. A report on the upgrading of infrastructure would assist communities to ensure better waste management, as well as opportunities in the sector. A report had been received from the service provider. It would inform the budget application for R3 billion that would be put through for the 2026/7 financial year to assist all municipalities in the Western Cape. Six municipalities had been assisted with infrastructure to ensure good water quality from boreholes.
Mr Gerber said they were working closely on the PDAL Bill with Agri Western Cape and the Department of Agriculture as key stakeholders for the Growth4Jobs strategy. Coordination and regulatory efficiencies were under discussion in that forum.
Ms Karen Shippey, Chief Director: Environmental Sustainability, said they worked together with local government and the Departments of Infrastructure, Agriculture, Economic Development and Tourism on the Water Resilience Committee of the province to ensure preparedness for the next drought. The programme looked across environmental infrastructure, including catchment quality, river stability, alien vegetation and erosion. They ensured that all of the parts of that water cycle worked. Parts of the system remained vulnerable, including Beaufort West as earlier mentioned, and these continued to be monitored.
Mr Gerber said he agreed that the Department could be better funded. They were engaging with all partners, especially the Treasury, to work on a strategy on blended, alternative international financing models. The partnering and prioritisation approach would be important here. This was especially because in the Provincial Strategic Plan, environmental resources, climate change and water were top priorities for the government.
Ms Fezeka Mabusela, Chief Director: Development Planning, said that they had been focusing a lot on meeting the legislative timeframes and the higher efficiency timeframes they had set for themselves. It was challenging for the teams, but they were trying to meet the legislative timeframes. The funding request was mainly for strengthening the teams, so they could look into such things as municipal outreach programmes and ensuring constant engagements and support in the areas of EIAs. This would ensure that proactive measures were in place so that the application process was smooth and fewer declined applications.
Ms Mare-Liez Oosthuizen, Director: Development Management, said that three applications for EIAs were refused, of the 184 applications received.
Mr Gerber said specific regulatory work and coordination was being done between the various authorities. There was also proactive work in environmental planning. The Environmental Management Framework was being turned into a standard that would manage conservation and sustainable production.
Mr Johannes Fritz, Director: Financial Management, said that the bulk of the Department’s revenue was from the equitable share. The revenue collected by the Department was only about 0.5 percent of the total budget. This came mainly from fines issued in terms of the National Environment Management Act (NEMA), which would at most times be subject to appeals. There would thus be delays in their settlement, and requests to pay in instalments. In terms of Programme 2, the funding was decreased by R5 million.
Ms Natalie Hayward, Executive Director: Conservation Operations at CapeNature, said they issued civil permits to facilitate access to biodiversity as far as they could. The Kannaland Water and Sanitation Forum played a bridging role between CapeNature, surrounding landowners and the community. CapeNature had issued recreational angling licences for access to waters in protected areas. There were private landowners in the area and she suspected access to waters in those areas might be an issue which was not within their powers to resolve.
Mr Gerber said an AI-driven approach would be important to development planning work in the future. Research and planning were being done to take this forward. They were also involved with the broader work on innovation in the province and nationally. AI would augment the work of the Department in terms of governance and other efficiencies, but humans would remain important to apply their minds and make decisions. It was an emergent area, with partners at international and local universities, and they looked forward to sharing the information with the Committee in the future.
Mr Haider said the landfill site in Zoar was not a closed facility, and the dumping of rubble by road constructors should never have happened. The municipality would need to exercise more control on the site. Waste diversion was undesirable and expensive, to be used as a last resort.
Mr Arendse said water quality was important for water security. It was not only about supply. The approach had been to look at monitoring both upstream and downstream from all settlements. There were multi-pronged approaches to the cleaning of rivers. The Department had partnered with the University of Cape Town (UCT) on these projects. A smart system had been linked to accelerate the cleaning of water at Stellenbosch, with the university there being a partner. He took Members through the water cleaning process in detail.
Ms Natasha Davis-Wolmarans, Department of Environmental Affairs and Development Planning, added that the Department had routine monitoring programmes, especially in the Berg and Breede catchments. They wished to expand these to other areas but could not because of funding and capacity constraints. As a result, they worked closely with the national Department of Water and Sanitation, as well as the Breede-Olifants Catchment Management Agency, so that resources were optimised. There was also a steering committee that dealt with water quality monitoring and pollution management, ecological infrastructure and water sensitive design and planning. The Water Resilience Committee was also an important structure that they worked with, to deal with wastewater works, amongst other elements. They also embarked on citizen science, working with the DWS and Stellenbosch University to take learners to the rivers to expose them to the system.
Dr Naidoo said the R19.5 million was not the amount they requested but was based on actual expenses over the past few years.
Ms Coral Birss, Executive Director: Biodiversity Capabilities, CapeNature, said that the amount allocated over the past five years was around R10 million, primarily for fire suppression on land managed by them. What they spent over the years had been increasing. This included standard costs like specialised training and provision of specialised personal protective equipment as well as costs like overtime and aerial firefighting capability.
Committee Report on Vote 9
The Chairperson put the report and the Vote before the Committee and asked that it be supported.
Ms Stoffel said she wished to declare. The ANC wished to note the decrease in the adjustment budget for Vote 9, and on that basis, their minority view was that the Vote was not supported.
Mr Constable said the Patriotic Alliance also did not support the Vote and asked that they be allowed to submit their concerns about the budget in writing.
The Chairperson advised that the concerns about the timeframe and the process be taken up with the Programming Committee.
The Committee Secretary noted the minority views of both the ANC and PA on the report, in line with Standing Rule 90. He then read out the amendments, to which the Committee agreed.
The Chairperson declared that the report carried the support of the majority of Members and the report was duly adopted.
The meeting was adjourned.
