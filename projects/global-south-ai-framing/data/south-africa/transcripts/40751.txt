Meeting Summary
Video
The Committee received briefings from the Construction Industry Development Board (CIDB) and the National Home Builders Registration Council (NHBRC) on contractor registration, enrolment protocols, and regulatory enforcement mechanisms in the housing and construction sectors. NHBRC emphasised inspection protocols, the enrolment process, and technical compliance in home building. CIDB outlined the legislative framework for grading contractors, the performance monitoring role of its registers, and upcoming amendments to improve inclusivity and oversight.
Committee members raised questions about compliance enforcement, skills development and upgrading building contractors. Of particular concern was the delay in NHBRC investigation report on the George building collapse.
Meeting report
Opening remarks
Deputy Minister Thandi Mahambehlala stated that the NHBRC’s role in the housing and construction ecosystem was both extensive and significant. The Council does not only approve housing enrolments but also undertakes corrective actions, such as instructing the demolition of non-compliant houses. She referenced a recent example in Richards Bay, KwaZulu-Natal, where NHBRC had ordered demolitions during a Portfolio Committee oversight visit. “This reflects the seriousness with which NHBRC enforces compliance,” she said.
The Deputy Minister noted that given the broad scope of the NHBRC mandate, it should be invited again to present in greater detail to the Portfolio Committee.
The Director-General added that the Department was fully aware of the capacity constraints, particularly in the material testing laboratories and the approval and certification process. He reported that the Department had already communicated expectations to develop a strategy to expand capacity, both geographically and operationally.
“This includes ensuring the establishment of laboratories in additional provinces and the development of a financially sustainable, targeted strategy that supports material testing
before
such materials are used in communities,” he said. The objective was not only to support the approval processes but also to strengthen the inspectorate nationally, by providing the necessary tools and standards to enforce compliance effectively.
National Home Builders Registration Council (NHBRC) briefing
Ms Noxolo Kiviet, NHBRC Board Chairperson, stated that the issues raised by the Director General were shared concerns. The Committee’s oversight keeps us on our toes—and that is how it should be. She reaffirmed the Council’s commitment to addressing the shortcomings raised and reminded Members that many of these weaknesses were being addressed through the new Housing Consumer Protection Act, which had been signed by the President in December 2024.
She was confident that by the end of the year, the supporting regulations would be finalised and the Act promulgated. These new regulations will strengthen the enforcement powers of the NHBRC, particularly on the imposition of penalties and improved technical oversight.
Ms Kiviet suggested that in the Committee’s next engagement, NHBRC should take Members through the provisions of the new Act in detail, to demonstrate how identified legislative gaps were addressed and how the Council’s regulatory mandate would be materially enhanced.
Acting NHBRC Acting CEO, Ms Tamlyn Bouwer, and her team presented. The NHBRC, established under the Housing Consumers Protection Measures Act of 1998, outlined its core mandate to protect housing consumers and regulate the homebuilding industry. It aims to provide warranty protection against structural defects and promote ethical standards in the sector. Builders are required to register with the NHBRC, with benefits including access to training, inspections, and marketing tools. Registered homebuilders are subject to mandatory home enrollment before construction can start.
The NHBRC detailed its inspection process, which includes a minimum of four site visits covering the substructure, superstructure, roof, and stormwater drainage. Homes that begin construction without enrolment are subject to disciplinary measures, and builders must submit extensive documentation for late enrolment, including technical assessments and financial guarantees. NHBRC engineers are responsible for reviewing geotechnical classifications and structural designs, ensuring alignment with the NHBRC Technical Requirements and SANS 10400 standards.
A five-year structural warranty is offered, which includes a 90-day defect liability period and a 12-month roof guarantee. The inspector’s final unit report is important as it triggers the post-construction warranty phase. Ongoing interactions with municipalities and provincial departments were crucial in ensuring that subsidy housing projects meet enrolment and compliance standards.
Construction Industry Development Board (CIDB) briefing
CIDB presented its role in developing, regulating, and transforming the construction industry in terms of the CIDB Act (Act 38 of 2000). The Board administers the national Register of Contractors and the Register of Projects to enhance transparency, performance tracking, and equitable procurement practices. CIDB outlined its strategic focus areas including green construction, industry digitisation, and support for marginalised contractors.
The Register of Contractors currently holds over 108 000 Grade 1 contractors and 21 000 in Grades 2–9, with 68% Black ownership and 36% women ownership reported as of Q4 2024. Contractor registration is based on financial capability, works capability, and track record. Grading criteria range from Grade 1 (contracts up to R500 000) to Grade 9 (unlimited tender value), with planned amendments including cumulative track records, skills assessments, and restrictions on large contractors bidding below their grade.
The Register of Projects collects data on public construction projects and facilitates performance evaluation across the infrastructure delivery chain. This data assists in monitoring if contractors are overstretched and supports CIDB’s oversight of public procurement performance.
CIDB also outlined its B.U.I.L.D Programme (Better Utilisation of Infrastructure for Learner Development) which integrates enterprise and skills development into public contracts. Around R450 million is allocated annually for structured training linked to occupational qualifications, with the target of 10 000 training opportunities per year. The CIDB B.U.I.L.D Fund is financed through a 0.2% levy on awarded contracts and is used to support emerging contractors, technical centres of excellence, and access to finance.
Finally, CIDB reported on efforts to scale up construction management systems training and research initiatives, including state of the sector seminars, postgraduate conferences, and BRICS collaboration on future skills.
Discussion and questions
Mr C Poole (DA) welcomed the presentations and asked CIDB:
a) Subcontractors were not required to register, yet delayed payments to them caused project disruptions and had community-level consequences. How could CIDB incorporate subcontractors into its regulatory framework to ensure protection against exploitation?
b) How would the proposed reforms to revise grading criteria, include skills assessments, and increase tender value limits avoid excluding smaller contractors who lacked capital but had proven records?
To NHBRC, he asked:
a) On the George building collapse, what systemic or leadership failures in NHBRC contributed to it, and what accountability measures would be implemented to restore public trust?
b) Considering late enrolments were a disciplinary offence, what new enforcement tools would ensure real-time monitoring, particularly on high-risk or multi-storey projects?
c) Could the Ministry provide data on non-compliant projects over the past five years and the enforcement actions taken, including their outcomes?
d) How many projects began without NHBRC approval over the past five years, and what geographic or systemic trends emerged?
CIDB response
CIDB Board Chairperson, Mr Khulile Nzo, began the response and then handed over to CIDB CEO, Mr Bongani Dladla. Mr Nzo noted the subcontractor issue was complex because these actors were not contracted directly by the state, and CIDB had limited jurisdiction over such private arrangements. Nonetheless, the Board recognised the prevalence of complaints and was exploring the use of practice guidelines to promote better industry norms.
Mr Dladla added that while CIDB could not legally enforce subcontractor protections due to their indirect relationship with the state, CIDB had issued guidelines to mitigate these challenges. The issue of delayed payments extended even to principal contractors, especially in dealings with government entities. He emphasised that poor payment practices affect CIDB’s grading system, as it distorted the financial profiles of contractors. To address this, CIDB was pushing for 30-day payment adherence through its regulatory interventions.
On reforms, Mr Dladla stated that any new grading and assessment requirements would be phased in through the BUILD Programme, ensuring contractors received developmental support. The anticipated regulatory updates would take effect in 2025/26, but training had already begun in 2023/24. Over 1 000 contractors were currently receiving support to bridge skills gaps.
He explained that the increased tender value limits, such as from R500 000 to R620 000, were linked to inflationary adjustments and created more opportunity rather than exclusion. However, CIDB remained mindful to balance risk management with inclusivity, especially since public funds were involved. He confirmed the Board was looking into structural reforms to improve outcomes for Grade 1 to Grade 3 contractors, who were often marginalised.
NHBRC response
Board Chairperson Ms Kiviet said the new Act would introduce stronger penalties for defaulting contractors and enforce stricter disciplinary action for internal failures. On the George collapse, she noted disciplinary proceedings were underway, but disclosure was limited due to process considerations. On the data requests, the statistical information on non-compliance and unauthorised builds would be submitted in writing to the Committee.
Acting CEO Ms Bouwer added that late enrolments triggered suspension and referral to an independent disciplinary committee. Sanctions included fines and technical compliance reviews. She noted that while the current Act limited their enforcement reach, the new legislation would expand powers. Late enrolments required submission of detailed technical documents and financial guarantees to assess project risks.

Although not encouraged, the Act allowed NHBRC to process such cases under strict conditions.
The Chairperson confirmed that NHBRC would provide the Committee with written responses on statistical data related to compliance and unauthorised builds.
CIDB response
Mr Nzo acknowledged the concern about gender equity in construction, stating that CIDB's regulatory role focused on monitoring industry transformation trends. Over the years, CIDB had amended its registration criteria to increase participation by historically disadvantaged groups. Previously 96% of contractors fell within Grades 1 and 2 but there had been measurable improvements particularly in the participation of women, youth and persons with disabilities. CIDB held and reported on disaggregated registration data and would provide statistics.
Mr Dladla elaborated that the CIDB BUILD Programme prioritised development support for women-owned enterprises. These companies were among the first to benefit from CIDB initiatives. He cited ongoing partnerships with private sector entities committed to supply chain transformation. One such company had agreed to designate project opportunities to women participating in the CIDB Empowerment and Recognition of Women in Construction (ERWIC) programme. Notably, women-owned companies had entered higher grades, including Grade 9. However, he conceded that participation by persons with disabilities remained low and required active engagement with the Department of Women, Youth and Persons with Disabilities.
On the challenges facing Grade 1 contractors, Mr Dladla outlined three strategic pillars: 1) Accredited contractor development programmes with infrastructure clients; 2) Mentorship initiatives to reduce the administrative burden on small contractors; and 3) The BUILD Programme’s updated construction management guidelines tailored for lower-grade contractors. He emphasised that the R500 000 tender ceiling for Grade 1s was a risk-mitigation measure. CIDB did not require them to prove capital or past turnover. With appropriate assessments, this threshold could be revised upward.
On fronting, Mr Dladla said CIDB’s Code of Conduct permitted disciplinary action, including deregistration. Detection, however, remained difficult and required site-level monitoring and cooperation from stakeholders. He urged project managers to report anomalies, such as the absence of registered contractors at site meetings.
Mr Dladla commented on transformation-related revisions to registration criteria. Earlier requirements that had disadvantaged small contractors had been relaxed. CIDB now placed greater emphasis on previously completed projects over the past five years.
On construction mafia activity, CIDB was working with the South African Police Service (SAPS) and National Treasury. A declaration signed at the 2024 National Summit on Crime-Free Construction Sites in Durban had formalised this collaboration. CIDB was assisting with policy and regulatory interventions. Statistics from reported cases and arrests since November 2024 could be shared with the Committee. CIDB was also partnering with the Private Security Industry Regulatory Authority (PSIRA) to address security firms acting as extortionists.

CIDB response
Mr Nzo responded to questions on contractor overload, transformation, and capacity-building. CIDB now registered both contractors and projects, allowing it to track when a single contractor was engaged across multiple sites. This helps us flag cases where contractors may be overstretched. Project registration also enabled CIDB to monitor transformation outcomes and link contractors to consultants and project locations. He provided a practical example: Before an appointment is made, especially in high-value contracts, the system allows clients to view submitted CVs and assess if the contractor has sufficient technical teams for the work proposed. This serves as a risk mitigation tool. This was working well with certain clients—such as SANRAL—who consistently register their projects and use CIDB systems to evaluate contractor capacity before awarding contracts.
Mr Nzo also drew Members’ attention to an important capacity-building challenge. He noted that CIDB offers free training to all registered contractors, with sessions tailored by grading bands (e.g. Grade 3–6 or Grade 6–7). However, uptake had been disappointingly low. “You would think contractors would take advantage of free training, but uptake has been very slow. It seems many are only interested in training when it is directly tied to an opportunity,” he said. As a result, CIDB was currently underspending on its training budget, despite having structured and accessible programmes in place.
Beyond contractor training, CIDB was also capacitating infrastructure clients, providing support to over 100 public-sector entities annually. This included guidance on procurement practices, the establishment of contractor development programmes, and the implementation of infrastructure delivery management systems. Additionally, CIDB worked with universities, TVET colleges, and regulators to deliver content on future skills, digital construction tools such as BIM, and technical standards. He concluded by noting that these interventions were part of CIDB’s broader role as a developmental regulator, aiming to balance risk management with inclusive growth and professionalisation of the sector.

NHBRC response
Ms Kiviet clarified that NHBRC was not the only entity involved in the investigation into the George building collapse. She emphasised that a multitude of departments and professional bodies were part of the process. These included the Department of Labour, which conducted its own investigation in line with labour regulations; as well as the Council for the Built Environment (CBE), Engineering Council of South Africa (ECSA) and other statutory bodies in overseeing professionals in the built environment. All these institutions—along with NHBRC—had submitted reports to SAPS and the SIU.
NHBRC itself had commissioned an independent investigation, and once that report was received, it was submitted to the Minister of Human Settlements, who is the entity’s reporting authority. “That is the sole person we are required to report to,” she said, “but we also had to act internally.” She emphasised that these parallel processes of reporting and internal action were carefully managed to avoid compromising any legal or disciplinary proceedings. “These are the prejudices that we are trying to avert,” she stated, “so that we do not jeopardise the case itself.”
Ms Kiviet reiterated that whenever discussions arise on the George incident, it is important to remember that it does not involve NHBRC alone. A house is not built by one entity. It involves multiple actors, from regulators to professionals to implementing agents so the accountability is multi-layered. She added that NHBRC remained committed to ensuring that those who failed in their duties were held to account, but stressed that this must be done in a manner that protected the integrity of ongoing investigations.
On concerns about substandard materials and the durability of approved products, she referenced NHBRC’s ongoing technical oversight work. Just the previous week, the team had visited the Eric Molobi centre to conduct material inspections. “We went to assess materials to either approve or disapprove them based on compliance,” she said. NHBRC has its own laboratory dedicated to material testing and quality control. This enables it to detect non-compliant materials early and ensure that only approved and durable products are used in residential construction. This is part of its proactive approach to safeguarding quality and consumer protection.
Ms Bouwer stated that under the current legislation, errant homebuilders could face suspension, deregistration, and fines of up to R25 000. However, the incoming Act would enable expanded prosecutorial powers and higher fines, thereby serving as a stronger deterrent. She added that NHBRC was improving material testing by using its own laboratory in Soshanguve and co-sourcing with labs in other provinces. However, testing was still random and not yet proactive.

Ms Bouwer confirmed that NHBRC had completed a technical assessment of Fleurhof and the report would be submitted via the Department. She acknowledged the concerns raised about reporting delays and committed to improving turnaround times.
Deputy Minister response
Deputy Minister Mahambehlala responded to suggestions that the NHBRC was deliberately withholding the George building collapse report, stating that such a characterisation was inaccurate. “It is not correct to say that NHBRC is refusing to give the Portfolio Committee the George report or any other report,” she said. She emphasised that the Committee itself holds the power to make the environment conducive for sensitive matters—particularly those that are sub judice—to be handled appropriately. Referring to Ms Dlamini’s earlier proposal, she supported the idea of a closed session where NHBRC could physically table the report and allow the matter to be put to rest.
Drawing from her experience as a former chairperson of a parliamentary committee, the Deputy Minister reminded Members that Parliament had well-established processes for accessing sensitive or classified reports, and expressed full confidence in the Committee’s ability to manage the matter effectively. “It cannot be that NHBRC is seen to be hiding information. If a closed meeting is what is needed, then a responsibility must be taken to convene one.
She clarified that the George report was not commissioned by NHBRC alone. Multiple entities—including the Department of Labour, SAPS, CBE, ECSA, and even the Western Cape provincial government—had conducted their own investigations and compiled reports on the matter. “It would therefore be prudent for the Portfolio Committee to invite all of these entities to present their respective findings. This would ensure the Committee has a comprehensive understanding of the incident,” she said. Parliament had the constitutional authority to summon any entity relevant to its oversight mandate and the Committee was fully empowered to request and receive every report related to the George collapse. Rightfully so, the Committee must conclude and rest this matter.
On the concerns about delays in receiving written replies, the Deputy Minister stated that she was not aware of any instances where the Department of Human Settlements had taken three months to respond to parliamentary questions. “When Members instruct the Department to respond in writing, protocol requires that those responses be submitted directly to the Chairperson of the Portfolio Committee—and that is done swiftly,” she said. She invited the Director-General to correct her if she was mistaken but reiterated her confidence that the Department responded timeously whenever formally directed to do so. She closed by reaffirming the Department's commitment to transparency, accountability, and cooperation with the Committee.
CIDB response
Mr Nzo responded to questions on contractor overload explaining that CIDB now registered both contractors and projects, allowing it to track contractors with multiple projects and flag overload. Project registration also enabled CIDB to monitor transformation outcomes and link contractors to consultants and project locations. He reported growth in Grade 9 contractors from fewer than 10 previously to more than 150, with many now empowered or black-owned. He acknowledged persistent Grade 1 saturation but highlighted a steady shift in upward mobility.
Black-owned Grade 9 contractors increased from 46% in 2021 to 59% in 2024, aided by CIDB’s BUILDd Programme and National Contractor Development Programme, which now supported upgrades up to Grade 8. He noted that 4 000 of 7 000 annual upgrade applications were successful among black- or women-owned businesses. CIDB had submitted procurement guidelines to improve equitable participation and addressed centralised procurement barriers.
He confirmed that CIDB was using AI and data analytics for fraud detection, cyber protection, and automating assessments. These tools allowed red-flagging of manipulated documents and financial anomalies. CIDB had a corporate cybersecurity strategy in place and planned to expand predictive analytics and auto-assessment systems as part of its broader digital roadmap. The aim was to increase system integrity, transparency, and regulatory efficiency.
Mr Mphithi responded to the Deputy Minister’s earlier remarks, stating that his concerns were grounded in specific delays, including the Fleurhof report, which had been requested the previous year, and lifestyle audit reports first requested in the previous term. He reiterated his willingness to submit a list of all delayed responses and emphasised that these delays affected the Committee’s ability to carry out effective oversight.
Deputy Minister response
Deputy Minister Mahambehlala replied that she believed the Department had submitted the lifestyle audit report to the Committee but would follow up on any outstanding items. She welcomed Mr Mphithi’s offer to submit the list of delayed responses and assured Members of the Department’s full accountability to the Committee. She noted again the importance of creating a conducive environment for entities like NHBRC to provide detailed reports—particularly in a closed session if necessary.
In conclusion, the Chairperson stated that he would engage the Minister on the Committee’s request to receive the George building report and supported proposals for a closed session to facilitate this. He reminded NHBRC to provide written responses to all unanswered questions urgently. The Committee would be scheduling oversight visits to include meetings with newly appointed board members, a visit to the Eric Molobi centre, and a visit to George to address the collapse matter. He concluded the meeting by thanking CIDB, NHBRC, the Department, and Members for their participation.
Meeting adjourned.
