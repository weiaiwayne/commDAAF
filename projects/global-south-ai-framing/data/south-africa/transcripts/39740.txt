Title: CSIR briefing on 10-year Foundational Digital Capabilities Research Programme
Date: 29 Oct 2024
Committee: 
URL: https://pmg.org.za/committee-meeting/39740/

Meeting Summary
The Council for Scientific and Industrial Research briefed the Committee on the importance of South Africa’s ICT development and digital capabilities and its response in the form of the 10-year Foundational Capabilities Research (FDCR) Programme. It discussed the digital twin for SA’s water system, self-sovereign identity (SSI), improving digital infrastructure, stakeholder engagement and collaboration, responding to cyber-crime and financial crime, capacity building and skills development.
Committee members asked about the budget of the project, performance metrics, self-sovereign identity, cyber security, infrastructure sharing, inclusion of marginalised communities, and the politics of AI chip allocation.
Meeting report
CSIR briefing with DSI introduction
Dr Mmboneni Muofhe, DSI DDG: Socio-economic Innovation Partnerships, discussed the context of the FDCR Platform situated in South Africa’s broader digital economy; the contribution of the development of foundational digital capabilities in addressing national challenges, such as a digital twin for SA’s water system, having a self-sovereign identity, enabling a digital economy, improving digital infrastructure, responding to financial and cyber-crime, as well as capacity and skills development. The size and breakdown of the ICT sector was compared to other sectors in the country. There is a widening ICT trade deficit as South Africa imports more ICT products than it exports. The FDCR project would ensure South Africa’s poor ICT infrastructure, inequitable access to internet connectivity, nascent digital economy, cyber-crime and the development of a digitally savvy multinational workforce are addressed
.
Mr Llanley Simpson, DSI Director: Socio-Economic Partnerships, stated that Africa has been caught in a fight between the USA and China. Africa is excluded from the mechanisms by which those protocols, systems, and standards are developed. This continent is not part of the decision-making process, especially when it comes to AI. Africa has yet to be allocated any of the new chips that come from the H100 chips, and the B200 chips have yet to come to Africa.
Mr Simpson said that the Department of Science and Innovation (DSI) funds ICT for ICT, meaning that it funds only the ICT sector. The DCDT (Department of Communications and Digital Technologies) is about funding the application sector in the communications through the digital economy master plan. There needs to be funding in every government department such as agriculture, health or minerals for their respective digitisation components. DSI is small so it cannot possibly fund all those application sectors for the economy.
Mr Simpson talked about how the crucial skills pipeline that connects to this digital economy. He noted that a level D in Maths is worth more than an A in Maths literacy. We need those Cs, Ds, and As in mathematics that recognise the importance of building the human resource pipeline
Mr Simpson highlighted inbreeding within the ICT sector. If AI systems are generated while the ICT systems are trained on AI-generated stuff, the product will be rubbish. AI trained on AI creates an inbreeding problem. The AI had to be trained on human development systems as opposed to AI development systems.
On AI bias, Mr Simpson gave the example of biased coding in facial recognition technology that does not detect black people the way it detects white people. Something happens during the development of these technologies which come out of the military-industrial complex and they are embedded in the value systems of war. He asked the question how this approach can be subverted to make it more developmental and human-centred. There are many people excluded by the technology itself and they need to be included in its development. These technologies led to new forms of social stratification, creating what he referred to as digital feudalism. This is where we will end up being peasants and have to pay for our right to exist in cyberspace. This is the social science research component of what is actually happening which needed emphasis as well.
The CSIR presentation followed (see document).
Discussion
Mr T Mjadu (MK) asked for insight into how the CSIR stakeholder workshops are designed to ensure the inclusion of marginalised communities. He asked how the CSIR perspectives are integrated throughout the development process.
Mr Mjadu asked what performance metrics will be employed to assess the success of the projects, such as the Digital Twin for the South African water system and the Self-Sovereign Identity (SSI). He wanted to know what strategies would be used to communicate these metrics to the public to ensure transparency and accountability.
Dr W Boshoff (FFP) was curious to learn that the ICT sector is larger than the agricultural industry and asked what is included in the ICT sector. He pointed out institutions such as banks have IT divisions and asked if the IT divisions are part of ICT. On the trade deficit, he asked if it was the hardware or software or both.
Dr Boshoff acknowledged that this is a lot of new information. The opportunity in this kind of thing is large-scale disruption of technology, often called evolution. He claimed that it provides space for communities to catch up. For example, when feudalism was replaced by the industrial society, some of society that did not thrive in the feudal system were able to catch up and bypass others. On the other hand, it could entrench existing disadvantages. He asked if we wanted the African, especially South African, disadvantages to be entrenched or do we wanted to gear up and take this to another level. He gave the example of how Finland was a logging and fishing society up until the 1950s, but now it is in the top five of every metric of most advanced societies. We have that opportunity, but one might wonder if we are ripe for that opportunity.
Mr V Pambo (EFF) welcomed the educational briefing. He was interested in the politics of the H100 and B200 chip allocation which does not include Africa and that Africa is caught between China and the USA where we go between the G7 and BRICS. He asked what the effects were of not being allocated the chips and what does it do to our development.
Mr Pambo found it fascinating that race was an issue in facial recognition technology, recognising the move towards including and tailoring products for the users. He acknowledged technological advancements recalling how video calls in telecommunications, were deemed impossible a while back when they were illustrated in cartoon programs, but nowadays we are dependent on them. Watching cartoons was subliminal messaging preparing the mind for this because technology can shock you. He made an example of older people having a fear of technology.
Mr Pambo stated that intra-trade in Africa is less than 12%. Even with technology, there is still a huge difference in exporting.
Mr V Nkosi (ANC) inquired about cyber security, pointing out that the majority of South Africans have moved their businesses online. High-profile data breaches and cyber-attacks have raised concerns about trusting online platforms. What can be done to prevent these from happening?
Mr Nkosi said that in 2020 it was announced that there would be 100% development ensuring everyone in rural areas would have access to the digital network. But according to Stats SA today only 60% have access. This poses a challenge to those left behind. He asked what could be done to fast-track rural areas accessing the network. He asked about the 10-year framework and how it would ensure that everybody has access to the network.
Mr T Ramongalo (DA) welcomed the presentation. He was concerned that South Africa has a lot of natural resources, yet we are importing so much from other countries instead of creating and exporting our own goods. It was important to leverage our foundational capabilities to foster our economic growth and contribute to the GDP. He asked if South Africa had the skills, scientific intelligence and ability to ensure that these foundational digital capabilities would be viable and that we would see results in economic growth. He asked if and how DSI had benchmarked with other countries to test its viability for success.
On South Africa’s greylisting, Mr Ramongalo asked what interventions have been done to address its non-compliance with the Financial Action Task Force (FATF) recommendations. Who is responsible for this and has there been progress on this?
Mr S Zondo (IFP) pointed out that budget cuts have affected every department and felt it was CSIR’s responsibility to ensure that they find ways to continue to meet their targets. Given that the country is facing 16% budget cuts, what plans and measures will help in mitigating this? He asked how the budget cuts would affect it and could this be managed.
Mr Zondo wondered if there were any plans for the network companies to work together.
Mr Zondo raised the gap between Higher Education and Basic Education in terms of science and technology and how differently it is defined in both. The question is what can be done to introduce technology at an early age? Also, what can be done to bridge that gap?
The Chairperson recognised the challenges and said something needed to be done about science and innovation in South Africa. She was concerned about the exportation of jobs and resources. Our mineral resources come back as finished goods that are sold to us at a high cost. She asked what mineral resources are needed for South Africa’s technology development and innovation. CSIR needed to specify what resources should and should not be exported. She requested the cost of the new programme.
CSIR response
Cost of the
FDCR programme
Mr Simpson pointed out that the 10-year plan took two years to develop. This involved the process of planning and creating what the country could have in 10 years’ time. This plan was broken down into deliverables per year for the next 10 years. These deliverables were divided into programs, projects and activities. In the first year, there would be 1077 projects in total in the five listed domains. Every cost is counted and flows through the budget, including master students research funding. Therefore on average it is R100 million a year, escalating at 5% a year, for about R1 billion in 10 years.
The 16% budget cut had affected this timeline because it was impossible to change the plan as it was detailed planning that went down to the activity level. The full logical framework for planning and resource allocation infrastructure would be to build capabilities at a university level to conduct research and produce the required students. The infrastructure needed at a university level is different from what is needed as a science council.
Mr Simpson explained that the budget does not include an infrastructure budget. This is the human resource research cost, and the infrastructure budget would be on top of that. Various items would be needed to address the infrastructure plan, which is not accustomed to the same detail yet but effective at looking at how to maintain the 10-year plan. South Africa has the basis to perform in the ICT industry, but we need children to have mathematical thinking skills. That thinking must be developed at the Basic Education level.
Mr Pambo remarked that the country is regressing, given the budget cuts. He compared South Africa to a vehicle without fuel. Other countries in the world are progressing and leaving us behind. Given our position now, it is likely that the country will be in a worse position in 10 years. He recognised that this is a result of the budget cuts and something must be done about it. The Committee needed to take this up with Treasury and the Minister.
Approach taken with stakeholders.
Dr Reevana Balmahoon, CSIR FDCR Domain Leader: Artificial Intelligence, Cybernetics and Robotics, replied that when the projects are designed, they have a certain use case in mind. When designing the projects, there is a certain proposition that is considered from an industry perspective. The process involved looking at what the stakeholders needed and designing the product based on that. FDCR looked at who from the industry would be applicable to the use case. FDCR has had multiple projects that resulted from both these domains, and others have been displayed and exhibited at multiple expos. Leads have come out of these exhibitions. The research group she leads has had one of its virtual reality products demonstrated, and individuals came out to create something larger. SMEs came through and helped with partnerships with larger companies. Among those companies is Sasol, who wanted to engage with FDCR on a project. This came from a link with an SME that came through from the ETCR program. They engaged with various stakeholders in an organic approach, which comes out when a project is designed and the product continues in terms of its development.
CSIR hosted an FDCR seminar last year, and some of their initiatives were exhibited. As a result, it had a few stakeholder conversations specifically related to AI and extended reality.
Performance metrics
Dr Lulama Wakaba, CSIR Executive Manager: Next-Gen Enterprises and Institutions, divided the performance metrics into two categories: (1) the work that the platform is doing to develop foundational capabilities and (2) the applications of these foundational capabilities. Within the development of the foundational capabilities, there are deliverables to ensure that certain objectives are met. However, the applications involve working with stakeholders, resulting in the application of rules. There are certain deliverables that help to improve the maturity level of the capability. Therefore, when it is applied to a certain industry, it will address a certain problem, and this problem will require its own deliverables. The question of metrics depends on how well CSIR is doing relative to the deliverables that were set and the maturing capability. How well it is doing relative to the deliverables that were set around the application area is deeply influenced by the people who are in the domain of the industry and is informed by their needs.
Mr Simpson added, for example, “ICT will work on solving the problem of boundary detection between two mediums. Where exactly is the boundary and how will this follow the model and solve that problem? It's an IT problem using mathematics. Now you want to take that issue, you now solve it, put it into different contexts. So, if you're going to space, how do you determine the age of galaxies? Where's the boundary of the galaxy? You put it in the health of the human body. Where's the boundary of the tumour? Within another medium, you can put it into a mine. Where's the boundary of the within an ore body”. He explained that solving one of these requires specific applications, but there is an underlying boundary issue: the technology that is developed in the ICT sector. Therefore, the DSI funds the ICT sector, and the other specific applications that use this tool are funded by other sectors/corporations.
Dr Wakaba added that when looking at performance metrics, it is important to note that it depends on which stage one is at.
On transparent communication on performance metrics, Dr Karen Calteaux, CSIR FDCR Co-Contract Manager, stated that there are multiple ways of communicating this information to the public. The best way would be when people feel the difference. This is where FDCR will communicate all the results of the projects and relevant conferences etc. There are reports about what the national repository could be called. FDCR works making the foundation and support layer. People need to build the applications and the products and services on top of that, and that is not necessarily the business that we are in. FDCR is trying to be the enabling layer that allows those to happen. This means that FDCR needs to reach a point where they see and feed into these services and products so that people can feel the difference.
Self-Sovereign Identity and the South African Water System
Mr Carel de Jager, CSIR FDCR Domain leader: Blockchain and Cybersecurity, explained that the SSI is basically a restoration of the entire system from the ground up. It is not analogue and is mostly digital. If a new technology like this is introduced, it means that many entry barriers of the old technology will be broken down, which may risk the introduction of new entry barriers. There is one data point that can be looked at to determine if it is good or bad. Smartphone penetration / adoption across the world is much higher than that of bank accounts. So the data will demonstrate that the use of technology such as phones to manage identity is good. That is basically what SSI is, and it is largely digital. It is a “complete redesign of the system from the ground up with new assumptions in a much better way”. Therefore “that identity cannot be lost, stolen, or defrauded”.
ICT sector larger than the agricultural sector
Dr Calteaux stated that a report from 2017 gave a breakdown of the aspects of the ICT sector, which used the OECD definition of what an ICT industry is to maintain comparability at an international level. The definition states that if an industry produces more than 50% of products, then it should be considered an ICT industry. However, the report stated that it is still a work in progress and that they are trying to define the sectors.
Trade deficit
Dr Wakaba replied that the trade deficit is caused by both hardware and software.
Chips
Mr Simpson stated that the decision on what to do was beyond his pay grade. The H100, which South Africa tried to import, has a cut-down chip which due to the USA restrictions, China was given access this downgrade version. USA then produced a new one, the B200.
Mr Simpson said that the program was established by the Centre for Artificial Intelligence Research (CAIR) which was set up in 2011, with its headquarters at UCT. This centre involves nine other universities around the country, which specialise in different areas of artificial intelligence. CAIR holds a conference, the South African Conference of Artificial Intelligence, every year around the country. UCT tried to get a chip, but they have yet to be responded to.
Mr Simpson mentioned that Huawei has produced its own artificial intelligence chip. They showed this chip at the Innovation Centre, but they would not explain the stuff in the chip. When he was in China for the AI capacity program, which South Africa supports, they showed us a lot of stuff the country has access to but not any of their cutting-edge stuff.
On the geopolitical situation, Mr Simpson mentioned how when the USA stopped developing its GPS, China developed its own different system. As time goes on, we will end with more Chinese vehicles in terms of our import system. Given that our trade is West-dominated, he wondered where we will go and where we will fit in this situation, what we will do as a country, and what further we build. Is that viable, or will Treasury allow it? He wondered about the relationship between economic policy and development.
He gave an example of Finland becoming a digital society, stating that “the Nokia people used to make boots for the forest industry. That's where they started, and then they went into the paper production. They had a little division trying to deal with communication in the forestry sector. That eventually became the Nokia cell phones that we know. Their adaptability and the mechanisms supported by their treasury and the structure of the finished system enabled them to move to a digital economy”. The integration of economic policy and how it makes changes and connections is important. It determines what happens next.
Skills gap in Higher and Basic Education
On the skills acquired through technology, Mr Simpson noted that it was about the changes in society, the change drivers, and the digital skills framework. He wondered: How do we collaborate with one another in systems to develop new things? What is cognitive load management? If we have information from all of these areas, how do we manage that process?
This kind of thinking is taught by teachers who teach critical integrated thinking. Social intelligence is important when it comes to understanding daily collaborative systems and abstract thinking. Integrating trans-disciplinary thinking into our daily activities is the kind of thinking that is needed. We must understand that it is not about the device itself but one’s ability to interact with it. That is the design mindset, designing it in a way with which people can interact.
Mr Simpson mentioned that some European teachers designed a computer system that helps children with learning and playing. This is the thinking innovation the FDCR needs from the Basic and Higher Education system.
On training / introducing technology at a young age, Mr Simpson stated that one must be careful when discussing
the production of people for work
. Germany went that route, and they now have a high level of specialisation, and they are finding problems with the education system now. It rather creates a well-rounded, critical individual who can adapt to different situations and is highly specialised for people in this world of work. There is a danger of reinventing and reproducing the National Party’s educational strategy. Producing people to work as opposed to producing individuals who are empowered to teach themselves and to engage in the digital world is a different educational approach. Social constructivism is developing knowledge as opposed to passing on the body of knowledge.
Minerals and exportation of jobs
Mr Simpson said South Africa has a whole lot of natural capital, which is predominantly underground. Under the capitalist system, these minerals are dug out, given how cheap they are now compared to 30- 50 years from now. He raised the issue of who owns this natural capital and to whom it belongs. South Africa sells to the EU which enforces the policy that what comes in does not leave again. This means that South Africa is giving away its natural capital. Whatever the case, this natural capital will always belong to South Africa; thus, these countries should pay rent.
Mr Simpson pointed out that when China placed bans on particular exports to the USA, they targeted specific minerals such as gallium and hafnium, which are important parts of the development of modern chips.
Mr Simpson stated that if we want to develop the chip industry, we should know what we need, how to place limits on these particular mineral resources we need and figure out the policy and how it connects.
Dr Mmboneni spoke about how South Africa should consider manufacturing its own devices. The country needs to see what it can do. Countries with no minerals have started to develop strategies on how to use minerals strategically. He mentioned initiatives such as the green economy, green energy and green hydrogen. He wondered how South Africa would position itself to take control. In the world of digitisation, ICT has become everything. He highlighted the importance of South Africa having the capability to own its own resources and not depend on others. For example, China has far more developed technologies than those that denied and sanctioned it.
Cyber security
Mr de Jager noted that there are a significant number of resources aimed at cybersecurity. There are early detection models, intelligence-based models, and reaction models. These models provide security before the cyber attacks take place and for how to react afterwards.
Greylisting
Mr de Jager stated that the responsibility to comply with FATF recommendations mainly lies with the financial sector institutions such as the Financial Intelligence Centre and the Reserve Bank. They are responsible for driving the attainment of the FATF recommendations. South Africa was non-compliant on some recommendations, including new technologies such as cryptocurrency. It is quite difficult to comply with this one as criminals saw South Africa as not having the capability to detect some of these crimes, such as money laundering and sanctions evasion. They then used South Africa to commit these crimes by using cryptocurrency.
Mr de Jager stated that DSI is working on harvesting intelligence on these transactions to detect when these crimes happen. USA has technologies to monitor every transaction happening around the world. This is because the information routes through Washington and over its Swift system which they control. Given that money is evolving and moving to new technologies, the USA maintains that strong form of intelligence. It is important for South Africa to have its own technologies so that it can play the same game and ensure that this intelligence is not kept by the strong countries. He pointed out that this kind of data requires highly specialised skills that take some time to develop.
Infrastructure sharing
Dr Hlabishi Kobo, FDCR Domain leader: Networking, Cloud Computing & Advanced Internet-of-Things, stated that there are two levels of infrastructure sharing, where operators share quite a few resources but only some things. If we use technology to have these operators share the infrastructure and other technologies, such as the cloud. Therefore, the capital will be minimised, enabling the ability to compete with others. This was part of the 10-year program. [Response inaudible 2:14:11].
Mr Zamokwakhe Dlamini, DSI Deputy Director: Foundational Digital Capabilities Research, stated that the department had invited the DCDT to be part of the FDCR.
Benchmarking countries
Dr Wakaba said South Africa has to look at the areas where it is lacking. One way to do this is to look at what the country is importing, as it shows the gaps. There are alternative ways the country can establish if it is investing in an appropriate level of research and development. The amount spent on R&D compared to the GDP is constantly monitored across multiple countries. South Africa is at 0.6%, China at 2.54%, and the USA about 3%. These numbers give us a sense that South Africa is investing less proportionately than developed countries.
Chairperson Shiviti concluded the meeting by highlighting that the role of National Treasury should be looked into. She asked for a detailed programme on FDCR projects and their budget. She wondered when the progress in this work would be seen. She expressed some concerns about the country’s backward global position and that it would be difficult to catch up with others as we are continuously chasing after them.
Chairperson Shiviti appreciated the opportunity to learn something new.
The meeting was adjourned.
