Title: Capacity of local government to fulfill local economic development mandate; Mechanisms to monitor registration of spaza shops
Date: 04 Jun 2025
Committee: 
URL: https://pmg.org.za/committee-meeting/40874/
============================================================

Meeting Summary
Video
The Committee met with the Department of Cooperative Governance and Traditional Affairs (COGTA) and the South African Local Government Association (SALGA) to be briefed on measures being implemented to enable municipalities to fulfil their economic development mandates and to monitor the registration of spaza shops.
COGTA said the programme to support spaza shops and address foodborne illnesses had seen Cabinet taking a resolution to establish a multi-disciplinary committee, chaired by the Minister of Health and the Minister of COGTA, which had drafted a set of standard by-laws around the registration to the spaza shops, and circulated them to the 257 municipalities so they could implement them.
Members acknowledged the existence of the by-laws, but were not impressed with the level of implementation, pointing out that if they were not implemented, they were meaningless. They referred to the large number of municipalities in crisis, which indicated that the interventions listed by the Department did not seem to be reaching the municipalities at large. They expressed scepticism over the District Development Model, which was now in the sustainability phase, and asked COGTA to explain the inconsistent adoption of plans, particularly in rural municipalities, and whether they actually transformed local economic conditions and were not just a desktop exercise. What was being done to ensure that these plans actually transformed local economic conditions?
The Chairperson said that given the widespread underperformance in so many municipalities, ranging from poor audit outcomes to persistent infrastructure backlogs, how would SALGA assess their own impact in support of struggling municipalities? Were they able to point this Committee to specific achievements in governance and service delivery? There appeared to be an ongoing overlap between SALGA and COGTA, particularly in respect of training, oversight and capacity building. Where did SALGA's mandate start and end, and how could they ensure that they avoided duplication of effort, especially when both bodies were using public funds? A Member commented that he was yet to be convinced of SALGA's value, having been a councillor in Limpopo and not experiencing any value from the entity while in that position.
SALGA indicated that a report from the Auditor General had highlighted specific areas of weakness, like financial and supply chain management, asset management, information communication technology (ICT) and record keeping. SALGA and COGTA did work together, but divided the work into specific areas. National Treasury was also working in partnership with them in this regard.
The Committee finalised and adopted its strategic plan, and agreed to hold a meeting with the Department of Communications and Digital Technologies virtually next week, despite their preference for in-person meetings.
Meeting report
The Chairperson welcomed everyone, and placed on record that neither the Minister nor Deputy Minister was present for this meeting due to a late notification for the Deputy Minister to attend a Cabinet meeting. Previously, when deferment was requested due to the Executive not being present, the House Chair would decline the request on the basis that the work of Parliament needed to continue, this being an important Committee regarding job creation and economic growth. The sister committee's work was seen as equally important, and they could not always be asked to alter the date.
The spaza shop presentation has been postponed to next week due to the lack of answers given to the questions raised. However, given the links to the spaza shop fund, this presentation would continue, as both the Department of Cooperative Governance and Traditional Affairs (COGTA) and the South African Local Government Association (SALGA) would be presenting, and local government was also critical to this discussion. Further, while the Executive should be held to account, the Chairperson was unable to question the Cabinet's powers. She trusted that everyone was in accordance, and requested that the meeting continue.
Mr M Modise (ANC, Gauteng) requested that the House Chairperson be invited to the meeting, albeit a virtual meeting. Given that it was Members who had to play an oversight role over the Department, it was necessary for the political leadership to be in attendance. Questions might be more political than administrative, where political heads were needed to respond. While political heads were not required to attend all meetings, given the nature of the meetings, political leadership should afford the committees the respect deserving of the role when required.
The Chairperson agreed with Mr Modise, and was seconded by Ms S Sithole (ANC, North West). She felt it important to get these two briefings today, as the spaza shop fund was cross-cutting with local government regarding licensing and other matters. Given that the meeting to be held next week was interrelated, she felt it important to go ahead. She apologised for deviating, and said a meeting would be convened at a convenient time.
COGTA on regulation of spaza shops
Mr Mbulelo Tshangana, Director-General (DG), COGTA, greeted everyone and confirmed the absence of the Minister and Deputy Minister, but gave an assurance that the entire top management team were all present and would be able to respond to any questions.
He highlighted that on the programme to support spaza shops and address foodborne illnesses, Cabinet had taken a resolution to establish a multi-disciplinary committee, chaired by Minister of Health, Dr Aaron Motsoledi, and the Minister of Cooperative Governance and Traditional Affairs, Mr Velinkosini Hlabisa. In that team, the core drivers of the work were COGTA, the Department of Small Business Development (DSBD) and the Department of Health (DoH). There was also the National Joint Operations and Intelligence Structure (NatJoints), which represented the security cluster responsible for managing the work streams that were assisting with the registration and enforcement of by-laws. COGTA had drafted a set of standard by-laws around the registration of the spaza shops and circulated them to the 257 municipalities so they could implement them.
Dr Kevin Naidoo, Deputy Director General: Policy, Governance and Administration, COGTA, commenced with the briefing on the capacity of local government to fulfil its mandate with respect to local economic development and the regulation of spaza shops.
He said the Department had tried different interventions to create a conducive local economic development environment, which included policy intervention, legislative intervention, creating a climate for good economic governance to financial management interventions, as well as the district development model (DDM) to encourage local economic development in municipalities. Requests from the Committee had been reviewed, and the presentation was structured according to the mandate.
The presentation provided a legislative and policy context, an economic governance and financial management outline, as well as municipal support to improve service delivery and turnaround strategies. Dr Naidoo outlined the ability of municipalities to maximise the grant funding opportunities offered by the Department, as well as the strategic interventions to support inclusive economic growth and job creation plans of the municipalities. The ability of municipalities to mainstream implementation of the DDM to support inclusive economic growth and job creation was also highlighted. He described the mechanisms put in place by the Department to support local government to monitor the registration of spaza shops.
Attachments to the COGTA presentation included the standard draft by-laws, the circular on their implementation and the ten-point plan of the government - see attached for full presentation
SALGA on capacity of local government to fulfill its mandate
The SALGA presentation was delivered by Cllr Merriam Mogoje and Mr Zamo Gwala, Portfolio Head: Economic Development and Investments. Its purpose was to brief the Committee on the capacity of local government to fulfil its mandate in respect of local economic development.
SALGA outlined their posture towards local economic development, its interventions and partnerships, and concluded with some remarks.
See attached for full presentation
The Chairperson commented that for the NCOP Committee to conduct a meaningful oversight role and to ensure accountability effectively, given the significant budget allocations, she had requested COGTA to present a comprehensive written breakdown of all the municipalities per province that were identified as distressed or at risk, and whether they had approved Municipal Systems Improvement Plans (MSIPs) and/or financial recovery plans. She had requested that the report specify the exact nature and scope of assistance that had been received per municipality, the status of implementation, the total cost of each intervention and what was covered under contingencies, and whether it was administrative, a financial buffer or due to unforeseen technical adjustments. The Committee had also requested that the Department submit an assessment of the return on the investments of these interventions, particularly when substantial grants or recovery funding had been deployed. This should reflect tangible service delivery improvements, economic development outcomes, including jobs created and the stabilisation or recovery in financial governance where applicable. This would give Members a broader view of what had transpired, and they would know which Municipalities to focus on.
Discussion
Mr Modise felt he needed to make a comment about the effectiveness of implementation, and said that without by-laws being put into effect, no amount of money put into municipalities would make a difference. His question was to what extent did COGTA and SALGA help the municipalities to play an effective role in implementing the by-laws. He gave examples of car wash activities, overcrowding in residential areas, as well as panel-beating releasing fumes in public areas, and emphasised that if no zoning was in place, the by-laws would remain ineffective without implementation.
Mr N Pienaar (DA, Limpopo) commented that the reality on the ground was very different to what COGTA had presented. Of the eight metros, there appeared to be only one clean audit report, indicating that the interventions listed by the Department did not seem to be reaching the municipalities at large. He asked how the gap could be closed, and whether COGTA thought that the DDM was going to make a difference. The slide indicating the municipalities in crisis had shown there were none in the Western Cape -- were there none in crisis, or why they were not mentioned? Many municipalities had signed up for agreements with Eskom related to their debt, and while some debts had been scrapped - how many municipalities had failed to uphold their end of the agreements, as it was jeopardising the country's energy security going forward? How many municipalities were keeping to the agreements?
Another issue was municipal grants received from national government, with billions of rands being returned to the Treasury. He questioned whether it was as a result of weak leadership, the inability of municipal officials, or bad planning. Did COGTA feel Parliament could further empower municipalities and hold them accountable for spending and building the most basic infrastructure? He commended the community work programme (CWP) but asked why the wage rate was not mandated by the government. He felt that the DDM had not produced any positive results, and asked what relevance it had in metros like Tshwane and Gauteng, who were fighting the model, and what role it played in Pretoria and Gauteng, and even Cape Town. With regard to SALGA, he was yet to be convinced of their value. Having been a councillor in Limpopo, he commented that he had experienced no value from SALGA while in that position.
Mr P Mabilo (ANC, Northern Cape) acknowledged the two different presentations, and felt they had been insightful. Of the 257 municipalities, only 120 had developed their local economic development strategies, and he asked if there was a specific time frame for all the other municipalities to have a well-defined local economic development strategy for implementation to ensure it was not just a tick box exercise.
How positioned and ready were the municipalities for the just energy transition (JET), which was a topical issue globally?
From the SALGA and COGTA perspective, how could one ensure meaningful input with regard to the audit action plans, as opposed to them just being a tick box exercise?
What was the turnaround time for strategic partnerships to support municipalities?
While the idea of the result management office (RMO) and its objectives and purpose was noble, was there a full-time person to ensure the RMO was functional and effective? Who could be held accountable to achieve its objectives?
The objectives of the CWP and expanded public works programme (EPWP) were clear -- to keep the wolf from the door -- but there were situations where people were employed for more than ten years with no clear skills plan developed. With regard to an exit programme, how far was SALGA with the skills development plan?
With regard to coastal economies becoming strategic, should the Department not be moving from opportunities to developing concrete plans to actualise the opportunities for coastal municipalities?
The Chairperson questioned COGTA about the DDM plan, which was now in the sustainability phase, and asked DG Naidoo to explain the inconsistent adoption of plans, particularly in rural municipalities, and whether they actually transformed local economic conditions and were not just a desktop exercise. What was being done to ensure that these plans actually transformed local economic conditions?
While several municipalities had received technical support and funding, what mechanisms were in place to track the return on investment of these interventions? Would COGTA commit to publishing a public performance report per municipality?
With regard to the 150 graduates that had been placed, she requested a list of municipalities where they were placed, including how they were selected, which disciplines they were assigned to, and the duration of their deployment. She also asked about the training of the 700 municipal officials, at which municipalities the training had occurred, the starting date of the training, as well the areas of focus or technical disciplines covered, and whether this was once off or part of a sustained capacity building strategy. Critically, she asked whether this could point to any measurable return on investment from these interventions.
Addressing SALGA, the Chairperson said that given the widespread underperformance in so many municipalities, ranging from poor audit outcomes to persistent infrastructure backlogs, how would they assess their own impact in support of struggling municipalities? Were they able to point this Committee to specific interventions in governance and service delivery? There appeared to be an ongoing overlap between SALGA and COGTA, particularly in respect of training, oversight and capacity building. Where did SALGA's mandate start and end, and how could they ensure that they avoided duplication of effort, especially when both bodies were using public funds?
SALGA had consistently raised the issue of unfunded mandates, but many of these mandates were for local government, which were constitutional requirements, such as water, sanitation and housing. She asked what successfully negotiated sustainable funding models with government had been put forward and what the plan was to prevent more municipalities from becoming financially unsustainable or defaulting on their core obligations.
The Chairperson asserted that thousands were spent on training by-law officers, but one did not see them executing their mandate. This was problematic for every municipality that required the support of by-law officers who did not execute their duties.
She questioned COGTA about the Municipal Public Accounts Committees (MPACs), asking where the training of these committees' members took place given their crucial role.
SALGA had spoken about the importance of tourism, but there were tourist attractions in Mpumalanga which were not being grown. Could this be addressed as a matter of importance, as it could speak to job creation?
Local economic development desks used to play a key role, and she asked if they could not be brought back, emphasising that support needed to go to rural municipalities.
The Chairperson indicated that should questions not be answered due to time constraints, they should be put in writing, and a response would be expected within seven days.
Responses
COGTA
Dr Naidoo began the response to questions by Mr Modise on the implementation of by-laws by municipalities, and indicated that they should not be restricted to by-laws per se, but when it came to implementation generally -- be it policy, circulars, or primary legislation that was in place -- it had been poor. The core mandate of COGTA was to support but not dictate by-law development, but to assist municipalities through capacity building, providing legal guidance, enhancing intergovernmental co-ordination, drafting and initiating policy, and to seeing how best support could be given to the various sectors within the ecosystem when it came to implementation.
Referring specifically to the spaza shop issues and noting the gravity of the matter, the Department of COGTA -- together with the DSBD, and with district and local municipalities, and the Department of Health (DoH) -- had undertaken a very coordinated and structured programme around the implementation of by-laws. If by-laws could be enforced in the manner in which they were designed during the volatile period, by-laws would be at a different level to what one would currently see.
Dr Naidoo said that with regard to the Western Cape, and the City of Cape Town in particular, where there were positive audit outcomes, more would be welcomed, but COGTA was also trying to see it being complemented by matters related to service delivery issues. Perhaps a twinning or pairing of municipalities would be a worthwhile consideration, to extend the circle of support and ensure more municipalities had positive audit outcomes. COGTA, together with SALGA and National Treasury, apart from providing dedicated hands-on support, had provided a web-enabled platform through Treasury dealing with the implementation of audit action plans, and COGTA was currently looking at how it could be upscaled together with impacts. Training would be demand-driven, with municipalities who approach COGTA through the impact forum to see how COGTA responds to training and capacity building exercises in relation to oversight. The list of graduates could be part of the list requested.
DDG Duma responded to the question about municipalities in the Western Cape not being listed among municipalities in crisis, and indicated that both the Western Cape and Mpumalanga were not listed as per the State of Local Government 2023 report, which the Department conducts annually, as those two provinces did not list municipalities classified as being distressed.
He said the spending on grants would be added to the written reply, but indicated that the main reason was poor planning on the part of the recipient municipalities as it related to supply chain management processes, but was also because municipalities' money was used for operational expenses allocated for infrastructure expenditure.
He said the DDM was a philosophy on how government should operate or work, and its intention was to reduce and ultimately eliminate fragmentation in all 52 DDM spaces. This also applied to metros, given that there was a lot of activity that was undertaken both by the private sector and government agencies or sector departments, and there was a need for collaboration and coordination to reduce fragmentation. Plans had been prepared in 50 of the 52 DDM spaces, and the Department had allocated funding to the provinces in this financial year to assess the adequacies of these DDM plans, which would be an ongoing exercise. One of the primary objectives was economic growth, so it was being monitored and tracked.
As COGTA interacted with the sponsors of the JET programme, they were focusing on Mpumulanga, where 17 municipalities had been identified. This was because this province had the most power stations where Eskom produces electricity from coal, and most municipalities that would be affected if they were to move away from coal were in Mpumalanga. The Department could not say the municipalities were ready, but programmes were being implemented to get municipalities ready, and there was funding available, particularly for renewable energy and water.
With regard to the results management office (RMO) and whether there was a dedicated person managing the RMO, Mr Duma said that while it was a temporary arrangement, and not meant to be a long term arrangement, there was a management structure and it was hoped that it would deliver.
Mr Vinny Rabothata, Chief Director: Development Planning, COGTA, answered questions relating to local economic development and the by-laws. He said Dr Naidoo had indicated that the Minister responsible for COGTA had already issued a standard draft by-law for township economies, and a circular had been issued to advise municipalities to implement them for township economies. A process had been started to monitor the by-laws, whose main objective was to facilitate economic development in rural and township spaces, so they would continue to be monitored.
He said there were about 120 municipalities that had up-to-date local economic development strategies. As regards time frames, for every electoral period, municipalities had to submit an integrated development plan (IDP) with a local economic development strategy to be implemented over a period of five years, and this would be reviewed as part of the overall review process. These IDPs needed to be updated in the current 2025/26 plans, and COGTA’s aim was to check there were updated plans to ensure they stimulated local economic development.
Mr Pankie Matomela,
Acting DDG: Community Work Programme, addressed the issue of CWP and EPWP workers, and explained that CWP workers work for two days a week, eight days a month, and the other group works five days a week and 20 days per month. These rates were determined through a ministerial determination that was issued by the Minister of Employment and Labour on an annual basis, and Unemployment Insurance Fund (UIF) and Compensation for Occupational Injuries and Diseases Act (COIDA) payments were made. With regard to an exit strategy, CWP work was determined for a set period where they were exposed to artisanal skills development which would enable them to be attractive to the working economy and smart partners. This would facilitate the assimilation of their skills into small and medium enterprises (SMEs) or cooperatives that could provide their services in their respective municipalities, working with some of the smart partners mobilised around JET issues and the digital economy.
Mr Tshangana acknowledged that Mr Modise's the comment on by-laws not being implemented was correct. The clear examples he had given had highlighted the disjuncture and nuisance to the neighbourhoods, and shown that they He indicated that this should be enforced.
With regard to the return on investment question being consistently asked, in the case of the public service and the municipalities in particular, the Department could respond through individual summaries and examples for the last three years and make an assessment of the return on investment. He gave an example of an Eastern Cape municipality, where an intervention was made by COGTA, SALGA and the municipality, and while he acknowledged that a good audit did not always determine good service delivery, it was a start.
SALGA
Mr Gwala said that while the issue of by-laws had been addressed at length, there was a working group that was focused on governance that aimed to assist municipalities with training and technical support on the issues of by-laws. However, their enforcement was the responsibility of the municipality.
Regarding the audit Action plans, SALGA found that interventions were not done timeously. It would be preferable to intervene earlier.
Much conceptualising was being done with regard to coastal municipalities, and some of them had taken advantage of opportunities, such as uMhlathuze in KwaZulu-Natal, Nelson Mandela Bay in the Eastern Cape, and Kynsna in the Western Cape, whether fishing or tourism. SALGA did try to assist others to take advantage, but in many instances, the infrastructure was not in place.
The list of supported Municipalities was quite long, and would be submitted at a later stage.
The issue of an overlap between SALGA and COGTA, to a certain extent, was inevitable. The example of Gauteng had been used, and while there may appear to be overlaps, this was where they agreed on different areas of focus for support. The report from the Auditor-General highlighted specific areas of weakness, like financial, supply chain management, asset management, information communication technology (ICT) and record keeping. The different departments did work together, but divided the work into specific areas. National Treasury was also working in partnership with them in this regard.
On the sustainability of funding for local government, SALGA had put together a study to look at alternate funding models, and the report had been presented to COGTA and Treasury, which were now looking at it. There was a lot of discussion currently taking place. SALGA has done its part to contribute to this discussion.
Mr Gwala said municipalities had the capacity to have a local economic desk, but given the different categories of municipalities, the numbers would vary. Should the municipality mentioned by the Chairperson not have a desk, it could be investigated and taken up. It had been mentioned that a limited budget would influence who was employed.
The Chairperson thanked those who had responded, and while most questions had been answered, should any Member feel that their questions were not fully answered, a written request could be made via the secretary and a response would be received within seven days of the meeting. The Chairperson felt better informed, and the COGTA and SALGA representatives were excused from the meeting.
Committee matters
Committee's strategic plan
The Chairperson moved on to the next agenda item -- the strategic plan -- which had been issued quite late. The issue of official tools of trade also needed discussion, which had hampered them from delivering the relevant documents timeously. She and Mr Modise would discuss this further.
She had sent out Whatsapps for input into the strategic plan, and a request was issued for feedback. No further feedback had been received, so it was assumed that everyone had received it.
Adoption of the strategic plan was proposed by Mr Pienaar and seconded by Mr H van den Berg (FF+, Northern Cape).
Meeting schedule
A reminder was issued to Members with regard to two meetings scheduled for next week. The first was the follow-up with the DSBD, and a request had been received to start the meeting at 18h00 and not 17h00, due to the Minister and the delegation travelling from Knysna.
The other meeting was with the SA Post Office business rescue practitioners, which was supposed to be a physical meeting. The Department had asked if the meeting could be virtual, given that the Department of Communications and Digital Technologies (DCDT) was hosting a G20 digital economy working group and task force on artificial intelligence (AI) from 09 to 12 June in Limpopo.
The Chairperson did not feel comfortable with virtual meetings, and felt that the Committee should sit around a table with presenters in a physical meeting, as the venue had been secured. She opened the floor to Members to indicate their feelings on this request.
Mr Pienaar felt that considering the seriousness of this meeting and that the DCDT did not show up to a previous meeting, the Committee should remain steadfast on the rejection of a virtual meeting.
Mr Mabilo felt that while the principle remained that in most instances, they should have in-person meetings, they had to understand that in certain instances, situations may arise where people were not able to attend in-person meetings, and flexibility was required. He did not support the rejection of the request, and suggested that while meetings should be physical humanly whenever possible, they should support the request and move forward.
Mr Modise pointed out that the DCDT's request had come in seven days in advance, and had shown a level of respect for the Committee, so he felt the request should be granted.
The Chairperson felt that the Department's non-attendance at the last meeting, and alerting the Committee only a day before, indicating that they would be sending junior officials to interact, had shown little respect, hence the request for the business rescue practitioners at an in-person meeting. However, the majority had spoken, and it was agreed that a virtual meeting would be hosted.
The meeting was adjourned.
