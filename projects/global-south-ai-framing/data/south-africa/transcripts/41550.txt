Meeting Summary
Video
The Portfolio Committee received two briefings from the Department of Correctional Services (DCS) concerning unnatural deaths in South African correctional centres. The first presentation included a macro-level analysis of national trends, while the second focused on specific incidents at the Mangaung, Goodwood and Oudtshoorn correctional centres. These briefings came amid rising concern about escalating deaths, alleged abuses, and serious governance failures in both public and privately managed correctional facilities.
The macro report outlined the Department’s analysis of confirmed unnatural deaths over the past three years. It identified a total of 152 unnatural deaths, with suicides (67) and homicides (55) as the leading causes, followed by deaths of unknown cause (22) and substance overdoses (eight). The report linked the upward trend to critical systemic challenges, including chronic overcrowding, as well as staffing shortages and non-functional security infrastructure in some centres. It also emphasised the Department’s commitment to constitutional standards, the Nelson Mandela Rules, and improving mental health and rehabilitation services.
The Committee was presented with details on consequence management, with 86 officials having been charged in connection with unnatural deaths. However, Members raised concern about inconsistent disciplinary outcomes, questioning why serious incidents often resulted in mere warnings or administrative sanctions rather than dismissals or criminal charges. They also noted with concern the Department's high vacancy rate in mental health services, including psychologists and social workers. They queried how meaningful support could be offered to vulnerable inmates under such conditions.
The micro report offered a case-by-case account of recent unnatural deaths and critical incidents. At Mangaung Correctional Centre, the March death of inmate Mpho Mkhumbeni was confirmed as resulting from blunt force trauma and exposure to pepper spray. The investigation had revealed severe misconduct by staff from the private contractor G4S, including a failure to notify the Department's temporary manager, non-compliance with operating procedures, and deliberate attempts to mislead investigators. The Committee was particularly disturbed by reports of nepotism, evidence tampering, and false affidavits submitted by G4S personnel. The Department was advised to support an ongoing South African Police Service murder investigation, and explore grounds for terminating the G4S contract.
At Goodwood Correctional Centre, the February death of inmate Quinton Fortuin had followed an incident in which he attacked a staff member, and was then fatally assaulted by multiple officials. The Committee was alarmed by the findings, which indicated excessive use of force after the inmate had been disarmed, the dragging of the inmate’s body, and attempts to alter official reports. The integrated security system was found to be non-functional at the time, and the Head of Centre was cited for failing to manage the situation appropriately.
The third case focused on Oudtshoorn Correctional Centre, where a mass assault had occurred during a joint search operation on 7 August. Inmates had attacked officials using padlocks and sharpened instruments, prompting the use of tear gas. The facility was operating at 132% of capacity. Early findings suggested serious flaws in the planning and execution of the search, as well as lapses in medical and security protocols. The Department was urged to improve oversight and its support for affected staff.
Throughout the meeting, Members expressed deep concern at what they described as a systemic failure in the management, oversight, and operational discipline across several correctional centres. Specific attention was given to the Department’s relationship with the Judicial Inspectorate for Correctional Services (JICS), which Members said had deteriorated despite prior directives to improve coordination. The Committee expressed frustration that its recommendations on strengthening inter-agency communication and data consistency had gone unheeded.
The G4S contract at Mangaung Correctional Centre emerged as a central focus of the meeting. Members strongly criticised the company's refusal to account to the Department and the Committee, citing late submission of reports, unauthorised searches, and defiance of standard oversight procedures. Other concerns raised included the underutilisation of legal tools, such as the Prevention of Organised Crime Act to combat gang activity in prisons, inadequate training of correctional staff in crisis response, and a lack of leadership visibility following incidents. The Committee emphasised the need for urgent restoration of broken security systems, standardisation of equipment accountability, and improved consequence management mechanisms.
The Committee concluded that these incidents reflected deep-rooted issues in correctional governance and accountability. It resolved to conduct further oversight, including facility visits and follow-up sessions, with a formal report on the DCS–JICS relationship expected in the first week of October.

Macro-level report on unnatural deaths in correctional centres
The DCS presented on unnatural deaths in its facilities, briefing the Portfolio Committee on confirmed incidents over the past three financial years (2022/23 to 2024/25) and the first quarter of 2025/26, along with its strategy to curb these deaths through a multifaceted operational plan.
The presentation defined unnatural deaths as fatalities resulting from causes such as homicides, suicides, accidents, or drug overdoses. It acknowledged the historical context of the apartheid era, where many prison deaths were falsely explained as suicides but were later exposed by the Truth and Reconciliation Commission (TRC) as torture and state killings. The Department emphasised its commitment to rectifying past wrongdoings and enhancing incarceration conditions.
The legislative framework governing DCS operations included the Constitution, Correctional Services Act, Criminal Procedure Act, and various DCS policies and strategies like security, psychological services, gang combating, death prevention and assault prevention. The Judicial Inspectorate for Correctional Services (JICS), an independent statutory body, played a critical oversight role, with DCS obligated to report all inmate deaths, the use of restraints, and the use of force to it.
A significant challenge highlighted was overcrowding, with 163 179 inmates against an approved bed space of 107 067 in Q1 2025/26, resulting in a 52% overcrowding level. This impacted various aspects, including physical and psychological wellbeing, security operations, rehabilitation programs, nutritional services, and information technology (IT )support.
Performance on unnatural deaths was measured by the "percentage of confirmed unnatural deaths in correctional facilities," verified by a medical practitioner or post-mortem/autopsy report. The presentation detailed the root causes of deaths in four categories: suicides, homicides, drinking of poisonous substances/medical overdose, and unknown causes. For the past three financial years and Q1 2025/26, a total of 152 unnatural deaths have been recorded. Suicides were the leading cause (67 deaths), followed by homicides (55), unknown causes (22), and substance overdose (8). Suicides were attributed to lack of supervision, overcrowding, staff shortages, delays in handling complaints, and mental health issues. The trend showed an increase in confirmed unnatural deaths -- from 42 in 2022/23, to 55 in 2024/25.
Regarding consequence management, a total of 86 officials had been charged for unnatural deaths of offenders during the past three financial years. Of these, 43 cases had been finalised, while 43 were still in process. The consequence management applied in the finalised cases included four dismissals, nine suspensions without salary, one final written warning, 19 acquittals, one resignation, three withdrawals, two verbal warnings, and four written warnings. Various management areas across regions had officials charged, with cases in process in areas like Waterval, Kimberley, Bizzah Makhate, West Coast and Goodwood.
In terms of enhanced security systems, the Department had invested in artificial intelligence (AI) technologies at the newly constructed Integrated Security Systems installation at the St Albans Management Area. This cutting-edge system was optimised to manage daily routines and emergency site activity, utilising the latest IT technology and communication protocols to integrate various sensor systems into a coherent security view and provide flexible, effective management tools for operators. The system includes an X-ray intelligent security inspection system that integrates functions such as intelligent recognition, face recognition, behaviour analysis, and variable speed, with built-in cameras monitoring both entrance and exit of the X-ray as well as the operator station. In addition, AI-driven automatic license plate recognition cameras cover the entrance and exit of each sally port with a vehicle drive-through, using an AI algorithm to define and read number plates. A Bowed Fence Monitoring System, an advanced fence-mounted perimeter intrusion detection system, was also in place, comprised of a multitude of sensors and closed-circuit television (CCTV) systems linked to control rooms.
The Department had also implemented a multifaceted approach by providing mental health care and support for inmates to reduce the risk of suicide and self-harm. This approach included routine health screenings to identify inmates who may be at risk of health issues or suicidal behaviour. It also involved implementing programs that offer counselling, peer support, and rehabilitation to provide inmates with coping mechanisms. Staff were trained to recognise signs of distress and mental health issues to enable timely interventions. Furthermore, the Department had embarked on additional interventions to support this multifaceted approach:
conducting a feasibility study for the installation of a prepaid programmable offender telephone system to promote offender support;
employing and maintaining advanced security systems for national roll-out;
revising the formal education policy to accommodate the use of laptops under controlled conditions;
revising the policy for the management of closed-monitored correctional centres (Super-Max and C-max);
training emergency security team (EST) officials in dealing with security incidents;
appointing additional psychologists and social workers; and
increasing oversight capacity, inspections, and security operations.
Lastly, the DCS concluded that overcrowding increases security risks, infringes on inmates’ privacy, limits their access to services, reduces offenders’ opportunities to participate in rehabilitation programs, and places immense pressure on custodial officials. Consequently, the Department provides needs-based social work, psychological and spiritual care services and programs to inmates to develop and promote their social functioning and mental wellbeing, with psychologists assisting offenders to adjust to the correctional facility and learn new coping skills.
The Nelson Mandela Rules, also known as the United Nations Standard Minimum Rules for the Treatment of Prisoners, state that inmates have the right to free healthcare. These rules emphasise that the quality of healthcare in correctional facilities should be the same as in the community, and the relationship between healthcare professionals and inmates should be governed by the same ethical and professional standards applicable to patients in the community.
Micro-level report on unnatural deaths in correctional centres
This presentation provided a report to the Portfolio Committee on specific unnatural death incidents at Mangaung, Goodwood, and Oudtshoorn Correctional Centres, including the findings and corrective measures implemented.
Mangaung Correctional Centre (MCC) – Death of offender Mpho Mkhumbeni
Following the escape of Thabo Bester in 2022, the National Commissioner appointed Mr Patrick Mashabathakga as the temporary manager and Head of Centre at the Mangaung Correctional Centre (MCC) on 29 March 2023. This appointment had initially restored operational stability and improved internal reporting. However, the authority of the temporary manager was reportedly undermined by certain G4S management officials. On 11 March 2025, a compliance callout drill was conducted without the knowledge of the temporary manager. This was followed by a series of searches during which offender Mpho Mkhumbeni was allegedly assaulted and tortured by officials in connection with R800 that was allegedly missing from an official’s bag. The following day, Mr Mkhumbeni collapsed in his cell and was transported in a wheelchair to the medical facility, where he was declared dead at 07h55. The post-mortem confirmed the cause of death as unnatural, resulting from blunt force trauma and complications related to pepper spray exposure.
The investigation found that the contractor, G4S, had failed to inform the temporary manager of the drill and searches, thereby breaching operational protocols. Officials implicated in the second search admitted to using force to elicit a confession from Mr Mkhumbeni. Statements from multiple offenders corroborated these claims. It was also found that senior G4S managers were in close proximity to the cell during the incident, and had failed to intervene. Several managers and officials later misled the Department by submitting false affidavits or withholding critical information. The post-mortem findings substantiated the allegations of assault and improper use of pepper spray. In addition, the contractor failed to report the incident and contraband findings in a timely manner. Efforts were made by senior G4S personnel to manipulate evidence and influence witness statements, constituting a serious attempt to defeat the ends of justice. Allegations that G4S employed mostly family members and relatives were also confirmed, reflecting a pattern of nepotistic recruitment practices.
The Department had been advised to support the ongoing SAPS murder investigation and consider opening a case of defeating the ends of justice against the contractor and implicated staff members. It was recommended that the certifications of Mr De Klerk, Ms La Grange, and Ms Malangabi be revoked, and that disciplinary proceedings be instituted against all remaining G4S officials involved. The Controller must issue notices of all contractual breaches, and the Department should seriously consider cancelling the contract with the service provider.
Goodwood Correctional Centre (GCC) – Death of inmate Quinton Fortuin
At Goodwood Correctional Centre (GCC), on 16 February 2025, inmate Quinton Fortuin attacked an official with a sharpened object during the morning count. After being restrained, the inmate was assaulted by multiple officials and later died from injuries sustained during the incident. The autopsy confirmed an unnatural cause of death. Investigations revealed that the force used was excessive and not aligned with Section 32 of the Correctional Services Act. After the inmate was disarmed and lying on the ground, officials continued to assault him, and he was later dragged by a blanket to the facility's hospital. The Head of Centre, Mr Shumane, failed to take control of the situation and to give proper instruction, effectively abdicating his responsibilities. The incident was further marred by dishonest conduct, including backdating the use-of-force reports and poor recordkeeping, particularly with regard to the allocation of tonfa batons. Critical components of the centre’s integrated security system, including CCTV and control room recording devices, were found to be non-functional or inadequate.
The Department recommended that eight officials involved in the assault, along with the Head of Centre, face disciplinary hearings in accordance with applicable resolutions. Furthermore, there was a need to ensure strict compliance with all provisions of Section 32, improve oversight of use-of-force equipment, and fully restore the facility’s security infrastructure.
Oudtshoorn Correctional Centre (OCC) – Mass assault incident
At Oudtshoorn Correctional Centre (OCC), which currently operates at 132% over capacity, a violent incident occurred on 7 August 2025, following a joint search operation the previous night involving the SAPS, the Emergency Security Team, and other units. During the unlocking process, several offenders from Cell 05 attacked officials with sharpened objects and a padlock. After the assault, offenders barricaded the cell, prompting officials to deploy teargas. The situation was brought under control, and several instigators were reclassified or transferred. The incident was reported to the JICS and SAPS, and the investigation team collected 33 statements from inmates and officials.
Although the investigation was ongoing, early findings indicated irregularities in the planning and execution of the search operation, in offender management practices, and in the provision of medical services. The management of the Centre was advised to strengthen security at vulnerable units, provide support to affected officials, and take corrective steps to prevent a recurrence.
These three incidents collectively reflected serious shortcomings in the management, supervision, and operation of correctional centres under both public and private administration. The unnatural deaths at Mangaung and Goodwood centres were the result of a systemic failure to uphold minimum detention standards, apply lawful use of force, and ensure accountability. The attempted concealment of misconduct at Mangaung, in particular, pointed to deliberate efforts to undermine the justice system. Urgent corrective measures, including disciplinary proceedings, criminal referrals, contract reviews and structural reforms, were necessary to restore institutional integrity and safeguard the rights and dignity of offenders in custody.

Ms James criticised the current programmes for lacking measurable outcomes, attributing this partly to the absence of an integrated information system capable of holding and analysing data on such interventions.
She concluded by returning to the stark images from the report, expressing disbelief and sorrow that these brutal realities persisted. She warned that unless real effort was made, such as removing contraband aids like cling wrap and toilet paper from cells -- simple but impactful measures -- these facilities would remain conducive environments for overdoses and suicides.
Her closing remark was a powerful charge to the Minister, reminding him that all of these failings and abuses had occurred under his leadership.
Mr M Sokopo (ANC) raised concerns about compliance and accountability within the DCS. He asked the Department to comment on its adherence to sections 15, 30, and 32 of the Correctional Services Act. He requested information on how much litigation relating to negligence or non-compliance the Department was currently facing.
He then focused on the recommendations from JICS regarding unnatural deaths over the past three years, questioning how many of those had been implemented and how long consequence management processes took when officials were implicated. Acknowledging the rising trend of suicides, he expressed concern about the Department’s admitted shortage of social workers and psychologists, suggesting that the DCS might need to consider alternative measures, such as training existing staff more intensively to manage these cases better.
Turning to specific incidents, he raised concerns about the Mangaung case, pointing out the contradiction where inmates were allowed to carry R180, but a death had occurred over R800, and questioned G4S’s compliance and accountability in that matter. On Goodwood, he stressed that the death of inmate Quinton Fortuin was clearly not self-defence, labelling it as murder and questioning the Department’s application of minimum force regulations.
Regarding Oudtshoorn, Mr Sokopo highlighted security issues, including break-ins by outsiders. He expressed alarm that the police had to be called in for a joint search operation, suggesting this indicated a serious security crisis that the correctional officials could not manage. He questioned who authorised police involvement, noting the police had outnumbered wardens during the operation.
He also raised concerns about attempts to obstruct justice in the cases of Mangaung and Goodwood, emphasising that those implicated in such actions should face criminal charges. While noting that investigations into Mangaung and Goodwood appeared complete, he acknowledged the Oudtshoorn investigation was still ongoing.
Finally, Mr Sokopo cautioned against comparing current correctional conditions to those during apartheid, calling such comparisons inaccurate and inappropriate given the extreme dehumanisation during that era. He closed by reiterating the need to pursue criminal charges against anyone involved in defeating the ends of justice, and indicated he would reserve further comments for later.
Mr C Niehaus (EFF) described both the macro and micro reports as deeply disturbing. He criticised the Department’s reference to apartheid-era prisons in the macro report, arguing that it seemed to suggest conditions had improved significantly since then, which he strongly contested. He stressed that South Africa’s prisons should be governed by constitutional human rights values, yet the reports reflected a failure to uphold these principles.
He expressed concern over society’s growing tolerance of unacceptable prison conditions, pointing out that a 30% increase in unnatural deaths showed the situation was worsening, not improving. Overcrowding was identified as a core issue, with more prisoners entering than leaving, which compounded the poor conditions. He questioned how the Department determined that reported suicides were indeed suicides, suggesting that many may have stemmed from neglect or mistreatment by correctional officials, rather than prisoners simply deciding to end their lives.
Highlighting the lack of psychological support, he noted the severe shortage of psychologists and social workers in prisons, leaving many inmates without the care they needed. He also criticised the reporting of “unknown causes of death,” calling it unacceptable in modern times that such deaths remained unexplained.
Mr Niehaus expressed frustration over delays in investigation reports, citing the case of Mpho Mkhumbeni, who died in March but whose report was available only in September, with little communication provided to the Committee in the interim. He noted gaps in communication between the Department and the JICS, with some statistics not aligning, which he described as a dereliction of duty.
Turning to the Mangaung prison case, Mr Niehaus condemned the continued undermining of the Department by G4S, and argued that the contract with G4S must end immediately, given their repeated failures. He also raised concerns about misinformation provided to the family of Mpho Mkhumbeni, emphasising the need for accountability for those responsible for false reports.
Regarding the Goodwood incident, he highlighted the unacceptable use of excessive force against an unarmed prisoner, and demanded to know what further measures were being taken to address such abuses.
In conclusion, Mr Niehaus lamented that South Africa’s prisons were failing to uphold the human rights culture enshrined in the Constitution. He rejected comparisons made to apartheid-era prisons, arguing that little had changed and that the current system continued to commit serious violations. He called on the Minister, the Deputy Minister and officials to take responsibility, warning that such reports would result in resignations or dismissals in other countries. While acknowledging the difficult conditions faced by prison officials, he stressed that these challenges did not justify the ongoing abuse and neglect of inmates. Finally, he emphasised that the high suicide rates reflected deeper problems related to prison conditions and treatment by officials, and he urged decisive action to address these systemic failures.
Mr M Gasa (MKP) noted the absence of any reference to societal stability in Dr Botha’s presentation on homicides and suicides within correctional centres. He expressed concern that every unnatural death of an inmate represented not just an operational failure, but a profound failure of the state in fulfilling its primary duty to protect citizens from harm, even after arrest. Such failures eroded the social contract between the state and victims or communities who reasonably believed that incarceration removed the threat posed by offenders. He asked the Minister to explain how this pattern of state violence -- amounting in some cases to extrajudicial killings -- could be interpreted as anything other than a complete erosion of public trust in the criminal justice system and a normalisation of violence within society.
Turning to overcrowding, Mr Gasa pointed to the Gauteng region’s reported influx of undocumented foreign nationals as a key contributor. He asked whether the Department had developed a specific protocol, in collaboration with the Department of Home Affairs (DHA), for the extradition, data processing, and potential deportation of incarcerated foreign nationals to alleviate pressure on the system. He queried whether the current approach simply resulted in a revolving door that both overburdens correctional infrastructure and fuels xenophobic tensions, instead of offering a clear, efficient, and humane resolution.
The Chairperson described the reports presented by the Department as deeply disturbing, and raised several points of clarity and concern, many of which aligned with those raised by other Members. She sought clarity on the average time it took to finalise consequence management cases involving implicated officials. Referring to Section 30 of the Correctional Services Act, she asked how frequently inmates identified as dangerous -- either to themselves, others, or infrastructure -- were assessed to determine the need for classification or reclassification. She also inquired about the proportion of homicides in correctional centres linked to gang-related violence, and questioned the effectiveness of the Department’s anti-gang strategies. She specifically asked whether these strategies were being actively implemented, and whether there had been progress in enhancing or amending them, as recommended in the Public Protector’s report.
Turning to public-private partnerships (PPPs), particularly at the Mangaung Correctional Centre operated by G4S, she asked whether the Department was able to enforce accountability in these facilities during inspections. She referred to the case of Mangaung, where the temporary manager appointed by the Department had reported ongoing non-compliance and resistance from G4S. She expressed concern that the Department may not have had sufficient oversight authority over such facilities, despite its contractual role.
Regarding the unnatural death at Goodwood Correctional Centre, the Chairperson agreed with earlier comments that the matter could no longer be considered self-defence. She expressed disappointment that key officials had been unavailable or unwilling to provide information when the incident was first reported. She recounted her own difficulty in reaching the Head of Centre and the Regional Commissioner for updates, eventually managing to speak with the National Commissioner. She noted that the Regional Commissioner did not appear to have visited the facility on the day of the incident, and requested clarification on when a site visit had occurred and what immediate measures were taken in response.
The Chairperson confirmed she was alerted on the day of the Oudtshoorn Correctional Centre incident, and had attempted to contact the Head of Centre, the Regional Commissioner, and the National Commissioner. She reiterated the urgency of receiving information when officials’ lives were at risk, and expressed disappointment at the delay in communication from senior officials. She asked whether intelligence received ahead of the incident, indicating a potential retaliation by inmates, had resulted in any action to strengthen security. If not, she wanted to understand why no preventive measures had been implemented, despite the Department having mobilised significant resources, including 90 SAPS officers, for earlier operations.
She emphasised that senior officials must show solidarity with frontline correctional staff by responding timeously to threats and incidents. She questioned why security was not reinforced if intelligence suggested an imminent risk, and criticised the apparent failure to prioritise officials' safety. She requested direct responses from the Head of Centre and the Regional Commissioner regarding their actions on the day of the incident.
On the Mangaung matter, she indicated alignment with other Members’ concerns, and confirmed that she had been aware of the incident shortly after it had occurred. She noted that both the Deputy Minister and National Commissioner had confirmed the matter was under investigation. While she refrained from commenting extensively on the G4S contract due to ongoing legal proceedings, she noted the Committee would return to this issue during its recommendation phase.
In conclusion, she reminded the Committee that part of the reason senior regional officials and Heads of Centres had been invited to the meeting was to provide clear accounts of events, and to address specific questions related to the incidents at Mangaung, Goodwood, and Oudtshoorn.
Responses
Minister Groenewald acknowledged Members’ repeated characterisation of the departmental reports as "disturbing," and agreed that the status quo could not continue. He emphasised the need to confront what he called the "inconvenient truth" about correctional services -- namely, that without adequate resources, the Department could not fulfil its legislated mandate. He drew comparisons to an artisan who could not perform without the necessary tools, arguing that the Department faced structural limitations, particularly with regard to staffing and operational capacity.
He cited the example of a state-of-the-art integrated security system installed at St. Albans Correctional Centre, which remained underutilised due to a lack of staff. Similarly, at Westville Correctional Centre, only four wardens were responsible for managing 650 inmates. He said such circumstances were untenable and unfair to officials. He attributed many of the Department’s systemic failures, including staff shortages, under-resourced mental health services, and inadequate rehabilitation programmes, to budgetary constraints. He warned that the situation would worsen, noting that the Department had received R29.2 billion for the current financial year, but was already projecting a R1 billion overspend. Despite these challenges, the Department was pursuing cost-saving and self-sufficiency initiatives, including operating bakeries, agricultural production, and manufacturing school desks. These efforts served both rehabilitation and financial sustainability goals.
The Minister highlighted the broader context of a national push for more effective crime prevention, noting increased pressure on police and the courts. He said Correctional Services sat at the end of the criminal justice pipeline, receiving increased inmate populations without commensurate increases in resources. He referred to South Africa’s obligations under international human rights instruments such as the United Nations’ Mandela Rules, and said that even developed countries struggled to meet all 200 minimum standards.
Responding to questions about the increase in unnatural deaths in custody, he clarified that while the number had risen from 42 to 55 over the past three financial years, the inmate population had also grown. He suggested that, proportionally, the rate may not have increased significantly, although he did not provide specific percentages. He deferred to the National Commissioner to provide further details and data.
On the issue of the G4S contract at Mangaung Correctional Centre, the Minister emphasised that while G4S was a subcontractor, the primary contract was with Mangaung Correctional Centre, and any legal action must be pursued within that contractual framework. He cautioned against cancelling the contract unilaterally, citing ongoing litigation and the financial risks of a court challenge with only 10 months remaining before the contract ends. Prematurely terminating the contract could result in millions of rands in legal costs, which the Department, and taxpayers, could ill afford.
The Minister addressed concerns about the Department's communication with the Committee. He committed to investigating why certain reports, particularly the one on prison polls, had not been shared despite being available. He acknowledged the Chairperson’s concerns, and proposed a dedicated meeting to discuss how preliminary investigation reports could be shared with Parliament while respecting procedural safeguards. He confirmed that the National Commissioner typically informed him of any inmate death within a day, followed shortly by a preliminary executive report. However, he cautioned against drawing conclusions from preliminary findings, as doing so could compromise investigations or lead to reputational harm if later disproved.
In closing, Minister Groenewald reiterated his agreement that the current situation was untenable, but emphasised that the Department required adequate tools and resources to meet expectations. He concluded by inviting Members to hold the Department accountable once those tools had been provided and it failed to produce results.
Deputy Minister Ntshalintshali provided a concise response to several outstanding issues raised by Members, acknowledging the seriousness of the Department’s current challenges and the loss of life within correctional facilities. She reaffirmed the Department's commitment to acting within the legal framework, particularly the Labour Relations Act, when dealing with internal disciplinary processes. While some Members had raised concerns about delays and a perceived lack of accountability, she stressed that the DCS followed the law “to the letter,” and that a comprehensive report on all disciplinary cases had previously been submitted to the Committee.
Responding to concerns around cases referred for prosecution but declined, the Deputy Minister said that this issue would be followed up on internally. She underscored that even in instances where prosecutions did not proceed, officials who had breached standard operating procedures (SOPs) must still be held accountable through internal disciplinary processes.
On operational matters, she echoed the Minister’s earlier sentiments, stating that while it was unacceptable for correctional centres to be overwhelmed by violence, drug abuse and suicide, the root causes were structural -- namely, chronic staff shortages, lack of supervision, overcrowding, delayed mental health services, and rising contraband. She particularly emphasised the security vulnerabilities, including non-functional or blind-spot areas in integrated security systems, noting that those responsible for failures would face internal or criminal consequences where appropriate.
On the issue of investigation timeframes, she deferred to the Department’s administration, but supported the Committee’s demand for improved turnaround and transparency. She acknowledged the recurring dysfunction within centres like Mangaung and the strained relationship with G4S, which she said was unacceptable and in need of resolution. She strongly asserted that G4S remained under the authority of the Department, saying: “They are not an independent board. They are part of us.” She affirmed that while a court process regarding termination of the G4S contract was ongoing, this did not exempt G4S from accountability in relation to other contractual breaches. The Department would act on those infractions separately, using clauses within the agreement to pursue appropriate redress.
The Deputy Minister also addressed Members’ frustrations over delays in communication with victims’ families and the lack of visibility from senior officials. She acknowledged that such delays were regrettable, but explained that internal protocols required the Department to first engage directly with families before releasing statements to the media. However, she acknowledged failings in how some incidents had been handled, especially allegations of report tampering and miscommunication, and said that the Ministry was “not proud” of those actions. She reiterated the Department’s condolences to the families of deceased inmates and injured officials.
In conclusion, she expressed support for the investigation recommendations, and reinforced the statement that consequence management would be implemented against any officials found to have violated protocols or mismanaged incidents.
National Commissioner Thobakgale responded to the Committee’s questions with specific reference to capacity challenges, operational procedures, and ongoing interventions.
Regarding unknown cases referred to in Slide 14, he explained that these were related to instances where, despite undergoing confirmation processes, the determination of the case remained unclear.
On staffing capacity, he said 71 psychologists and 37 community service psychologists were employed, with active recruitment ongoing. He detailed recent advertising of posts in April (410 posts, including psychologists, social workers, educators, and artisans), May (471 centre-based posts, such as unit managers, heads of centres, heads of community correction, security personnel, and case management committee chairs), and August (58 posts related to agriculture and farms, alongside 14 pharmacist posts). These efforts aimed to address capacity gaps, with appointment processes managed regionally. Monthly management meetings received progress reports from Human Resources (HR) on recruitment status, which were critical to improving conditions identified by the Minister.
On the contractual relationship with Bloemfontein Correctional Contracts (BCC) and G4S, Mr Thobakgale noted the existence of a supervisory committee comprising both the Department and contractors. This committee oversees the imposition of penalties for contract breaches, guided by legal processes and clearly stipulated penalty provisions within the contracts.
Concerning the composition of search teams and SAPS's involvement, the Commissioner emphasised adherence to standard operating procedures (SOPs) governing search planning and execution. He acknowledged irregularities identified in the preliminary Oudtshoorn report, including a disproportionate number of police officers relative to correctional officials, and the presence of inexperienced officers unfamiliar with correctional environments. He stressed that correctional officials should lead operations, given their expertise and understanding of the facilities, with the Regional Commissioner and area commissioner present to address further specifics.
On the POCA Act and gang management, the Commissioner outlined the assessment process upon admission, which includes identifying gang members through trained officers. Correctional facilities managed gang members by preventing the exposure of other offenders to harm, avoiding the mixing of rival gangs, and integrating rehabilitation and correctional programs aimed at addressing gang behaviour. He affirmed collaboration with the SAPS, particularly in the Western Cape, where ongoing discussions involved crime intelligence to manage gang-related issues more effectively.
Regarding the Integrated Security System (ISS), the Commissioner acknowledged that some facilities lacked the ISS due to budget constraints, necessitating prioritisation of resources. Following an incident at Goodwood, resources had been redirected from other projects to enhance the ISS there.
On security challenges at Kimberley, management and union engagements were underway to improve the security environment.
Addressing unmeasured deaths, especially suicides, Mr Thobakgale recognised the need for deeper investigation beyond mere classification. The Department reviews health records, disciplinary history, and complaint resolutions related to each case. Responsible officials, including unit managers, face charges where negligence is found. The DCS collaborates with the Department of Health to manage offenders with mental health issues. Segregated offenders receive hourly checks documented in registers, as per SOPs, to mitigate risks.
In conclusion, the Commissioner agreed that any death, natural or unnatural, was one too many. He reiterated his commitment to answering only the questions within his remit, referring other issues to respective Department representatives.
The Head of the Oudtshoorn Correctional Centre explained that security had been strengthened by deploying 18 additional officers on the morning of the incident, up from the usual four to five members. He emphasised that departmental protocol was followed by referring the matter to his superiors, especially given the many phone calls he received that day. However, he clarified that the centre had only eight security members in total, with some unavailable due to leave or transfers, and that there was no dedicated Emergency Support Team permanently stationed at Oudtshoorn. The investigation into the reinforcement of security was still ongoing, with conflicting statements from officials about the exact numbers involved.
The Area Commissioner added context by confirming she had authorised a joint search operation due to a series of security threats, including the presence of weapons and gang-related violence. This operation involved multiple police units, such as public order police, crime prevention, visible policing, and crime intelligence, all working under the Correctional Service’s mandate. Despite police presence in numbers, the correctional officials had maintained operational control. The Commissioner noted that inmate behaviour indicated clear resistance to searches, leading to changes in the unlocking procedure the following morning — specifically, unlocking cells one by one in smaller groups. Unfortunately, despite these measures, the incident still occurred during unlocking.
Turning to the Goodwood Correctional Centre, the Regional Commissioner apologised for any misunderstanding related to her availability at the time of the incident. She explained that she was attending to another serious matter elsewhere, but had arranged for the security manager to assist with the Oudtshoorn investigation. She had later visited Oudtshoorn for meetings with senior officials, where security threats and measures were discussed. She highlighted that governance around security included regular meetings, breach reporting, and preventative physical controls like access control and the use of canines and tower posts. Regarding the use of staff with criminology qualifications, the Commissioner acknowledged there were no dedicated criminologist posts, but noted efforts to assess and better deploy skilled personnel in the region.
The Head of Centre at Goodwood reported that the incident on 16 February was gang-related, involving a high-ranking member of the 26 gang who had stabbed another inmate to regain his rank. On the day of the attack, only four officers had been responsible for unlocking 121 remand detainees in the affected unit due to staff shortages. After the stabbing, other officers had intervened to control the situation, though there were allegations of excessive force during this process.
The Head of Centre expressed concern about the ongoing influence of gangs within prisons and the fear this instils in staff, warning that without intervention, gangs could effectively control prisons. He commended the National Commissioner’s office for responding swiftly by implementing safety measures following the incident.
He also apologised for the lack of stretchers used to transport the injured inmate, explaining that the stretchers were kept only in the hospital section, which was an oversight. He assured the Committee that stretchers had since been purchased for all units to ensure humane and dignified treatment during emergencies. Following the incident, the centre held debriefings with both offenders and staff to prevent retaliation and restore calm. The situation was reported to have stabilised, with ongoing monthly updates being submitted to the National Commissioner’s office.
National Commissioner Thobakgale concluded by addressing the question about the protocol with the Department of Home Affairs. He confirmed that such protocols were in place, and that all Regional Commissioners were ensuring that deportations were carried out when required. He also clarified that the offender at Allendale had taken his own life outside the facility. He acknowledged this was the offender who had escaped.
Chairperson’s concluding remarks
Concluding the session, the Chairperson began by reminding Members that although time constraints required the meeting to end, the issues under discussion were far from resolved. She indicated that the Committee would continue its oversight, particularly on the progress and outcome of investigations related to recent incidents in correctional facilities. She stressed that she remained especially concerned about the apparent discrepancies between written reports and verbal presentations by the Department. She stated firmly that accuracy and transparency were non-negotiable going forward.
She raised concern about late amendments to the presentations, noting that Members and their support staff prepared well in advance for these meetings. Presenting altered or revised versions shortly before a sitting undermined both preparation and the ability to engage meaningfully. She called on the Department to show better regard for the Committee by ensuring that documentation submitted ahead of meetings aligned with the presentations delivered.
On the issue of training, while the Department had recommended upskilling of Emergency Support Team (EST) officials, the Chairperson emphasised the need for training to be extended to all correctional officials, particularly those working directly with inmates. These were the individuals most often confronted with dangerous situations, and they must be adequately equipped to manage them. Training should therefore not be confined to specialised units alone.
The Chairperson flagged the non-functionality of the Integrated Security System (ISS) at Goodwood Correctional Centre as a major concern. Referring to incidents where cell doors were left open, she expressed alarm over the potential for escapes and violence. She drew parallels with past incidents at other facilities, such as those in Polokwane, where similar issues had occurred. The Department was instructed to submit a report on how long the security system at Goodwood had been down, whether it had been restored, and what interim measures had been implemented to safeguard inmates and staff during the period of failure.
She further addressed the issue of accountability regarding security equipment, particularly the lack of reporting on tonfa batons at Goodwood. The Chairperson questioned whether similar accountability issues existed at other facilities. She called for a standardised, national approach to the management and tracking of security equipment, insisting that security tools must be accounted for uniformly across all centres.
Moving to the matter of unnatural deaths, she expressed her deep concern at the increase in such incidents, reiterating that while overcrowding was often cited as a contributing factor, it could not continue to serve as a blanket explanation. She noted that overcrowding predated the current leadership, and called on both the Minister and the National Commissioner to implement concrete measures to manage the situation. She said the time had come for the Department to offer alternative strategies for addressing the underlying issues that contributed to violence, suicide, and poor inmate care. In particular, she was critical of the fact that some deaths were still being classified as having “unknown causes” despite the existence of autopsy procedures, stating clearly that this was unacceptable and had to be rectified.
The Chairperson then addressed the ongoing dysfunction between the Department and the JICS. She revealed that the JICS had written to her to request assistance in obtaining natural death reports from the Department, an appeal she had not responded to because she herself had outstanding requests from the Department. She reminded both entities that the Portfolio Committee was tasked with oversight, not mediation. The Committee had previously directed the DCS and JICS to sit together, resolve their reporting issues, and bring back proof in the form of minutes and attendance registers. The fact that this had not happened was viewed as evidence that such a meeting had never taken place. To address this, the Chairperson gave a directive that a full report on DCS-JICS engagements -- including resolutions and attendance -- must be submitted during the first week of October, when Parliament reconvenes.
Turning to the matter of G4S and the Mangaung Correctional Centre, the Chairperson was visibly frustrated and offered a scathing critique. She said she had deliberately chosen not to speak earlier on this matter because of the pattern of non-compliance and avoidance by G4S. She accused the company of operating as a law unto itself, disregarding instructions from the Department and Committee, and responding only when summoned or threatened. The late submission of a critical incident report, six months after the fact, was highlighted as unacceptable. She questioned G4S’s motivations, suggesting that legal tactics were being used to avoid accountability. The Chairperson gave her full support to the call for immediate termination of the G4S contract, echoing views expressed by Mr Niehaus. She also called for a status update on the legal process regarding the contract’s termination, and encouraged the Department to expedite efforts to take over the facility, warning against further delays. She also expressed doubts about the viability of private prisons generally, using Mangaung and Goodwood as examples of systems that had failed the state and the public.
With regard to search operations, both announced and unannounced, the Chairperson reiterated her support, but warned that all such operations must be properly authorised and documented. She raised concern over incidents in which officials were left vulnerable, stating clearly that searches must prioritise the safety of all involved. Criticising the National Commissioner for visiting affected officials only several days after an incident, she said leadership should be present at the onset, showing care and support to injured staff. Delegating this responsibility was not sufficient. The lives of officials mattered, and leadership must act as if that was truly the case.
The Chairperson confirmed that the Oudtshoorn Correctional Centre facility would be visited by the Portfolio Committee, either in full or via a delegation. She said reports of break-ins from outsiders, as well as the disproportionate deployment of SAPS personnel over DCS officials during searches, required urgent attention. She expressed concern that DCS officials were being rendered passive in their own facilities. She stressed the importance of joint operations in which contraband discoveries could lead to immediate consequences for offenders. Without this, she warned, inmates would not be deterred from smuggling or other illicit behaviour.
The Chairperson then noted that the first quarter report of the Department, which could not be addressed due to time constraints, would be deferred to the fourth quarter session and potentially clustered with the second quarter report. She apologised to the officials who had prepared for the session.
In closing, she thanked all Members and participants, but reiterated that the work would continue. She said the Committee may even hold its next meeting on-site at one of the affected facilities, and would expect a full breakdown of the situation at that time.
The meeting was adjourned.
