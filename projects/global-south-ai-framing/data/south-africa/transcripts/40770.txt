Meeting Summary
Video
The Joint Standing Committee on the Financial Management of Parliament convened to assess Parliament’s performance for the fourth quarter of the 2024/25 financial year.
Parliament senior management reported that of the ten assessed indicators across two programmes—Administration and Legislation/Oversight—80% were achieved. Under Programme 1 (Administration), efforts continued in digital transformation, organisational realignment, and wellness initiatives. Progress on the restoration of Parliament was hindered by permitting delays, but is expected to accelerate from May 2025. Programme 2 (Legislation and Oversight) saw advances in tools like the country outcome indicator dashboard and monitoring of state capture recommendations.
Financially, Parliament spent 71% of its quarterly budget, or 81% excluding rebuilding funds. Only 11% of the rebuilding funds were spent due to delays with SAHRA permits. Underspending resulted mainly from delays in recruitment and voluntary retirements. Parliament returned R22 million in unused direct charges to the National Revenue Fund and retained R82.26 million for shortfalls in 2025/26.
Members expressed concern over Parliament’s declining responsiveness to Members’ questions, particularly written ones. The National Assembly’s reply rate dropped to 28.69% in Q4, raising doubts about Executive accountability. In contrast, the NCOP maintained a 100% oral question response rate.
Members also sharply raised the reallocation of R124 million from the capital rebuilding fund to operational expenses. While Treasury authorised the transfer, Members raised concerns over compliance, transparency, and long-term consequences for capital projects. Members requested the disclosure of Treasury correspondence and further details on future contingencies.
Human capital management was also a key discussion point. Despite forming a Talent Acquisition Team, Parliament has filled only 54 vacancies. Members pointed to the critical gaps persisting in committees and Member support. Members asked about the appointment of programme managers in the STP’s office amid staff shortages. Members wanted a full breakdown of salaries, acting allowances, pay gaps, and criteria for internal appointments, including bursaries. Repeated calls were made for reports on the organogram.
On ICT infrastructure, Members asked about outdated email systems and expired software licenses.
Members asked about the value for money from international engagements. Members requested full disclosure of travel expenses and proof of value from diplomatic participation in forums such as the IPU, CPA and BRICS.
Several Members called for monthly reports, as stipulated by law, not just quarterly summaries. Members felt the quarterly report lacked detail.
The Co-Chairpersons acknowledged Members’ frustrations and emphasised the need for structured, decisive progress. Written responses were requested to detailed questions.

Parliament of RSA Q4 2024/25 Performance
Mr Xolile George, Secretary to Parliament (STP), took Members through the presentation, pointing out key highlights from Parliament’s performance in the fourth quarter of the 2024/2025 financial year, as mandated by Sections 52 and 54 of the Financial Management of Parliament and Provincial Legislatures Act (FMPPLA). The report outlined progress in achieving the Annual Performance Plan (APP) objectives and programme delivery, financial health, and institutional development.
Overall, Parliament assessed ten performance indicators in Q4, achieving 80% success. This includes:
Programme 1: Administration – four indicators
Programme 2: Legislation and Oversight – six indicators
It was reported that FMPPLA compliance was met in all reporting requirements for the quarter.
Programme 1: Administration
Focused on operational excellence and institutional support, key initiatives included:
Restoration Project: Progress affected by permit delays; construction to ramp up in May 2025.
Organisational Realignment & Culture: Advancing change management and structural efficiency.
Big Data Maturity: Continued digital and analytical capability development.
Member Satisfaction: Integral to administrative responsiveness.
Programme 2: Legislation and Oversight
This programme underpins Parliament’s core mandate:
Implemented systems such as the Country Outcome Indicator Dashboard and Impact Assessment Framework.
Advanced online petitions monitoring, committee planning, and oversight of State Capture recommendations.
Business of Parliament
National Assembly (NA):
Decline in oral questions (113 from 341); reduced response rate (76.65%).
Sharp drop in written question replies (305 of 1 063), lowering response rate to 28.69%.
National Council of Provinces (NCOP):
Oral question response rate maintained at 100%.
Slight decline in written question response rate (81.90%).
Committees & Oversight:
Oversight visits included major sectors: education, water, correctional services, and defence.
Municipal and governance reviews under Section 139 & 106 focused on accountability and service delivery.
International Engagement:
Strengthened diplomatic ties via multilateral platforms (IPU, UN, SADC).
Bilateral engagement with EU, Asian, and African states emphasised security and sustainable development.
Financial Performance
Overall Q4 expenditure: 71% of the R1.04 billion quarterly budget (81% excluding Rebuilding Fund).
FY2024/25 expenditure: 68% of R4.75 billion annual budget (90% excluding disaster funding).
Underspending Drivers:
Delays in hiring and implementing voluntary retirement.
Pending political endorsements delayed programme execution.
Rebuilding Fund: Only 11% spent due to SAHRA permit delays; construction now underway.
Unused direct charges (R22M) returned to the National Revenue Fund.
Retained earnings of R82.26M to address FY2025/26 shortfalls.
Human Capital
Recruitment Acceleration: The Talent Acquisition Team was established in October 2024.
Employee Wellness: Psychosocial and occupational health services delivered across departments.
Training and Culture: Focus on transformation, strategic planning, and “Return to Office” transition.
Conclusion
Eight out of ten performance indicators were met

The second policy matter is on the overall budget expenditure. In terms of the Joint Financial Management of Parliament Act, Section 19, the institution is allowed to spend up to 45% of the budget before it is actually approved. And that section is similar to section 17 of the PFMA, which also allows the government in general to spend a budget before it is processed in Parliament. but the outer timeline horizon is 31 July. So the threshold is 45%, but one cannot go beyond July without a budget for the country not being approved. So Parliament is equally enabled to spend. But what will happen is that that expenditure is not regarded as additional expenditure. It is part of what will be allowed. So you can spend 45% of what you had in the prior year, so any activities that took place between April, May and June, in the absence of a final budget, are accounted for and enabled by legislation.

On the issue of international engagements, the Speaker said that in Parliament, Members participate in multilateral institutions such as the Commonwealth Parliamentary Association, the Inter-Parliamentary Union, the SADC Parliamentary Forum, and the Pan African Parliament. At the beginning of the year, both Houses will indicate which Members will serve on these multilateral institutions. Therefore, there is an obligation beyond just paying for the membership of Parliament in that institution, but also an obligation to make sure that those participating Members are supported. Some of those Members within those institutions would either be elected to be members of the executive, where they might then need to play other roles beyond just attending general assemblies. This increases the costs. There is a committee in Parliament which is supposed to process the reports coming out of all of these meetings, which is the PGIR. After which, those reports must be tabled in both Houses, so that Members are updated on the issues that are dealt with in these institutions. There are also friendship committees. The PGIR needs to engage and advise on developing relationships with the partnering parliament and how this can be supported. For instance, some Members would ask the Speaker or the Chairperson about a friendship committee with particular countries, but the Speaker and the Chairperson do not make those decisions. This is for the PGIR, which is a multi-party structure of Parliament comprised of Members of Parliament and chaired by the House Chairperson of International Relations, who must then advise. Once there is agreement on the relationships, an MOU will be established and will also indicate which Members will form part of those friendship committees. That is the work that the PGIR does. There would also be instances where there are invitations directly to committee chairpersons for their committees to participate, either on a study visit, etc. Some of those get fully paid, and if so, this is disclosed as it is regarded as a benefit. In some cases, the travel and accommodation are paid for, then Parliament would pay a stipend, according to the rules in terms of how much is paid for the subsistence allowance. Sometimes there will be an invitation. For instance, as South Africa had taken over the IPU, the Speaker was invited, but the Speaker then requested the Deputy Speaker to attend the meeting in Brazil last year. This context is important.
Regarding value for money, the Speaker said this was something for Members to determine. The administration would not be in a position to answer those matters, whether there is value for money in being a member of BRICS or a member of PAP. There should be a reflection of parliamentary diplomacy because that was what these multilateral institutions offered Parliament.
On the issue about the money for the rebuild used for operations, there was indeed an engagement with the Minister of Finance just after we started to reflect on the state of finances of Parliament, firstly, relating to the transfers when Members do not return after the general elections, and the time it takes for Treasury to pay Parliament back. There were also consequences of the fire, such as the loss of offices of Members, loss of meeting facilities, and how this would be addressed as a consequence of the disaster. Beyond the rebuild, Parliament has to continue functioning. It was in those discussions where the Minister of Finance said what has come out as a result of this disaster will form part of the overall budget of the rebuilding, and given some of the challenges in terms of finances, this money could be utilised for operations.
The Speaker noted that some Members asked about disciplinary hearings, consequence management, issues that relate to members of staff, as well as the issues of contractual arrangements in the appointment of all staff and their benefits and salaries. However, it is important to be mindful that some of the decisions were taken before the seventh administration. This report can be given to the Committee related to all the various appointments that have been made by the institution.
Mr Van Rooyen appreciated the responses received. He asked a follow-up on some of the details. On the online petitions, he asked if there was a project rollout plan, and if so, would this ensure that those who are underserved are going to be accommodated in this plan. At the ultimate implementation stage, it should not be found that only the advantaged are entitled to access this important platform. As part of the rollout plan, he asked to be informed of how people in the underserviced areas would be accommodated on the online petition platform.
He appreciated the response on the cost of the budget speeches, but his question was asking about the cost for all three events and the costs incurred for that. He asked for a figure to show how much was wasted due to the lack of leadership demonstrated by the Minister of Finance.

Regarding the capex taken from the rebuild to be used in the operations, he asked for the correspondence with National Treasury to be shared for transparency. This will shed more light and also bring some logical conclusions to this particular aspect.
Ms Siwisa said the job titles of the people hired to temporarily work in the office of the STP did not matter. Her question was how these appointments have affected the unfunded vacancies.
Parliament cannot sit without any support staff doing the work. Is the answer that before the 54 vacancies were filled, there were only 15 people who were responsible for assisting? What she understood from the response was that so far, 69 total vacancies have been filled, but the report received spoke to 54 – does that mean we had 15?
She asked for confirmation that the talent acquisition team was an employee of Parliament. She asked this because mention was made of “being appointed”, and when it says appointed, it means there is a crisis. This means that these people were working somewhere, and they were brought in here. She asked for clarity on this. How were they appointed? For how long are they appointed? What are their expenses? He said this information could be received in a written reply. She needed to understand the role of talent acquisition and the programme managers, and how much was spent. The Committee cannot be told there are 69 vacancies, but for the past two financial years, the institution has been operating with 15. 15 plus 54 is 69, and it was said that it's not six programme managers but five. the core problem is, how has the appointment of these five or six project managers affected the unfunded vacancies?
Ms Siwisa asked how many unfunded vacancies and funded vacancies there were. As the Committee considers the budget, it needs to know. This information can be provided in writing. How did Parliament decide on talent acquisition? How did it decide to appoint these programme managers?
Mr Badenhorst said it would be good for the Committee Secretariat to keep a record of all the questions asked and which ones have been answered and unanswered, so that meetings are not prolonged by asking the same questions repeatedly.
with regards to monthly reports, he pointed out that Section 51 (1) of the FMPPLA says that within 15 days of the end of each month, the Accounting Officer must submit the financial statement to the Executive Authority and the National Treasury. Section 54 (1) says the Executive Authority must table monthly, quarterly and mid-year reports in Parliament within five working days of receiving those reports. Subsection 2 says Parliament must refer the reports to the oversight mechanism promptly – that is, this Committee. Legally, the Committee is entitled to monthly reports, and this might greatly assist the work of the Committee.
he noted his questions on the spending on catering, travel and protection fees for the accounting officer, EA and House Chairpersons were not answered. This is a simple question, and the Committee is entitled to an answer.
He was pleased to be getting answers on the acting allowances. This will allow for understanding around vacancies not filled while there were acting allowances, and looking at what is spent on acting allowances vs. vacancies.
He asked about how and when the CFO was suspended and asked for a report on that.
He repeated his question, which required a simple answer on the pay gap between the highest and lowest earners in Parliament. That is not a personal question about salaries. It is a general question on the pay gap between the highest and lowest earners. That was all he was asking for.
Mr Badenhorst said he was getting impatient. He noted that at the first meeting of the Committee, he had asked for the approved organogram, but he was still waiting for it. There was a lot of talk about it, and there was agreement that this would be sent to the Committee, but it was still not received. This was the third request he had made for the organogram.
He said he had never heard of a gym in Acacia Park, and other Members serving their third terms in Parliament have also never heard of such a gym.
He noted the Speaker referred to decisions having been taken in the past. With respect, he said that wrong decisions taken in the past remain wrong and if the Executive Authority made wrong decisions in the Sixth Parliament, then somebody must be accountable in the Seventh Parliament. Otherwise, there will be a flurry of wrong decisions just before the end of a term, because nobody is accountable for any wrong decisions after that. Perhaps a legal opinion on this can be received so that the topic is buried.
Ms Dlakude thought it was wrong for Members to cast aspersions against officials of Parliament who cannot maybe defend themselves. The Committee said that before anything is discussed in a meeting, a report must first be presented to Members for discussion based on those reports. She suggested that if the administration did not have answers immediately to some of the questions, they be allowed to respond in writing to the Committee. She urged that personal information shared should not be used for political expediency.
Chairperson Ndhlovu requested that the answers be shared in writing. The administration was requested to compile all information Members requested, especially on the HR matters such as the salaries, wage gaps, graduate payments, disciplinary hearings, and everything. This would perhaps enable a specific meeting on HR matters so that Members satisfy themselves. Thereafter, there will be a focus on the issues of the rebuild. This would allow meeting agendas to be focused on specific issues. The Chairpersons will look at the programme of the Committee with suitable dates to deal with those specific issues, including the resolutions. This would allow certain matters to be dealt with and put to bed.
Chairperson Lekganyane noted that the questions Mr Van Rooyen sent him would be forwarded to the STP. He requested that there be a standard on how things were done in the Committee with openness and transparency. He noted that Mr Badenhorst had issues he wanted to deal with, but these were not being ventilated. He requested that Mr Badenhorst share these issues to be placed on the agenda. Matters for clarification should be brought before the Committee so that there is mutual understanding, e.g. the matter of decisions taken in the previous Parliament. Issues Members feel strongly about should be given the attention they deserve. He assured Mr Badenhorst that he heard his issues. He asked what exactly Mr Badenhorst wanted to raise regarding the previous terms of Parliament, for this to be discussed, including the issue of benefits. When this is dealt with, the proper team to come and respond will be identified. The issues Members seek answers to must be responded to, and it is expected that they will not have to be raised in future meetings. The Committee must deal with things decisively and not repeat the same issues in meetings, otherwise the Committee will not realise its strategic objectives.
He thanked all participants. He hoped that the STP and senior management had noted the issues Members raised and needed answers to. The Committee will deal with the issues systematically to confine the matters to those on the meeting agenda. There must be openness and transparency in dealing with the work concerning the Committee.
The meeting was adjourned.
