Meeting Summary
The Portfolio Committee on Land Reform and Rural Development received a presentation by an independent researcher proposing a Youth Land Ownership Policy. The researcher drew attention to the under-representation of youth in land reform efforts, citing his own research conducted in the North West Province. He outlined the marginalisation of youth in current land reform policies and the broader economy, suggesting that a youth-centred policy could help reduce unemployment, inequality, and crime while fostering sustainable economic growth.
Committee Members disagreed among themselves on the willingness of young people to get into farming as a career. Most agreed with the idea in principle, but Members also wanted to know how the policy would be implemented on the ground.
The Committee also received a presentation on land redistribution and tenure reform by the Department of Agriculture, Land Reform and Rural Development. The presentation covered the Extension of Security of Tenure Act (ESTA), progress on labour tenancy claims, and mitigation strategies to address the gaps in land tenure security.
Committee Members sought clarity about the absence of labour tenant land claims in the Western Cape. They also asked about enforcement of land claim laws, the status of certain departmental policies, a permanent replacement for the Interim Protection of Informal Land Rights Act, and a correlation between the success of land reform and labour tenants. A Member stressed the need to halt unlawful evictions, and another argued that solving land issues required a whole-of-government approach.
Members raised concerns about tenant protections, the effectiveness of ESTA amendments, and the broader issues of land ownership, particularly among Black South Africans. In response, the Deputy Minister acknowledged the challenges outlined during the session and assured that the Department was working on strategies to increase youth participation in land reform and improve financial mechanisms for land ownership.
Meeting report
Opening comments
The Chairperson opened the meeting by citing section 25(6) of the Constitution: ‘
A person or community whose tenure of land is legally insecure as a result of past racially discriminatory laws or practices is entitled, to the extent provided by an Act of Parliament, either to tenure which is legally secure or to comparable redress.
’   and its relevance to land reform and tenure.
The apology of the  Minister, who was attending a Cabinet meeting, was noted. He said the farm dweller programme proposal would be presented in the fourth quarter of 2024. Cases related to land tenure had been referred to Legal Aid South Africa for investigation.
Presentation on Proposed
Youth Land Ownership Policy
Mr Simion Mashego, an independent researcher, presented a proposal for a Youth Land Ownership Policy, aimed at addressing the under-representation and exclusion of youth in land reform efforts. His presentation drew from his own research on youth and land reform politics in post-apartheid South Africa, with specific reference to the North West Province. The research covered critical areas, including land reform policies since 1994 and their impact on youth, ownership patterns and communal property associations as tools for reform, and the role of political will in enabling youth access to land​. The study involved young emerging farmers, government officials, and farm dwellers, all of whom confirmed that youth participation in the land reform space was minimal.
Mr Mashego identified several problems in the current land reform approach, including under-representation of youth, unresponsive policies, a too-general approach, and marginalisation of youth. While well-intentioned, the existing policies on restitution, redistribution, and tenure had not achieved their objectives concerning youth. The exclusion of the youth from targeted groups identified in these policies was a significant gap that needed to be filled by a Youth Land Ownership Policy​.
Mr Mashego said that youth apathy and unemployment were central to the discussion. Youth unemployment had risen, reaching 60.8% in Q2 of 2024. This marginalisation had driven many young people away from economic participation. A lack of belonging and disillusionment with the economy, including the agricultural sector, further alienated the youth​.
Mr Mashego argued that a Youth Land Ownership Policy was essential to addressing the growing youth crisis. Such a policy should aim to engage youth in land ownership, provide access and security of land rights for young people with diverse social and economic needs, and shift land reform from rhetoric to practical implementation​. The statistics in the agricultural sector illustrate a concerning trend: the number of commercial farmers declined from over 140,000 in 1994 to less than 20 000 today. Youth involvement in agriculture was limited by several barriers, including lack of land ownership, high input costs, lack of market access, and mechanisation challenges​.
Mr Mashego emphasised the importance of involving youth in the governance of land-holding entities such as Trusts and Communal Property Associations (CPAs). Traditional leadership structures also needed to integrate young people into decision-making processes to ensure intergenerational transfer of knowledge and leadership​. A major challenge for youth participation in land reform was the lack of access to financial support. Collateral requirements by financial institutions created barriers, as young people were often unable to meet these criteria. The proposed Youth Land Ownership Policy should include provisions for financial support specifically tailored to the needs of young landowners​.
The presentation outlined several solutions to improve youth participation in land reform: Enact a Youth Land Ownership Policy; Encourage financial institutions to consider traditional systems when dealing with rural populations; Ensure government visibility on platforms where youth are active; Establish a Youth Land Trust and Integrate youth into traditional leadership structures​.
The Policy could address the triple challenges of unemployment, inequality, and poverty. It would empower youth to become job creators, reduce crime rates, and foster innovation. Additionally, such a policy would ensure long-term economic sustainability by creating a generation of skilled, land-owning farmers​. Without a dedicated policy, the country risked political instability, a missed opportunity to leverage the skills and education of its youth, and a failure to achieve the objectives of land reform and transformation​.
Mr Mashego’s final recommendations included utilising artificial intelligence to engage youth in land-related activities, establishing a strong communication machinery on land matters to raise awareness among citizens, mentorship programs linking youth with experienced professionals, and focusing on not just agricultural land but diverse economic sectors where youth could be involved​.

Discussion
Dr W Boshoff (FF+) said that, according to his own research, successful school leavers expressed a strong preference for (inaudible) kinds of jobs. What were Mr Mashego’s views on this problem?
Mr A Mngxitama (MK) agreed that land reform policy should explicitly support young people. Lack of access to land was holding young people back.
Mr L Mahlatsi (UAT) said that there were young people who were willing and eager to work the land and he wondered whether perhaps Mr Boshoff’s research was out of date. He supported the proposed Policy. He observed that if the youth were not assisted through legal means, they would resort to revolution.
Ms S Lucas (ANC) asked how the proposed Policy would be implemented on the ground. She was aware of many young people who were involved in agriculture but working for other people.
(Three Members asked questions that were inaudible)
Response
Mr Mashego said that the state should support emerging farmers’ access to machinery, equipment and water, but also by providing markets. He agreed that it was not enough to just formulate policy, but land actually had to be distributed. He stressed the importance of integrating young people into decision-making structures of CPAs. This would provide them with a sense of belonging.

Mr Bonginkosi Zulu, Chief Director: Land Tenure, DALRRD, outlined the administration of communal land under traditional leadership, emphasising the intricate relationship between customary practices and the legal framework. Emphasis was put on the pivotal role played by traditional leaders play in land management. He noted that the tenure system operated according to Department of Cooperative Governance and Traditional Affairs (CoGTA) guidelines, which emphasised the involvement of local leadership in land allocation and conflict resolution.
The Extension of Security of Tenure Act (ESTA) served as the legal foundation to protect individuals living on agricultural land. However, significant challenges remained, such as evictions despite legislative protections. Farm dwellers often lacked awareness of their legal rights, leading to vulnerabilities in tenure security.
Mr Zulu discussed the recent amendments to ESTA in detail. These amendments were designed to enhance protections for individuals occupying agricultural land, particularly in cases of eviction. Processes must be transparent and fair, with the onus on the landowner to provide clear legal justification for removal. Panel mediation before eviction had been introduced as a conflict resolution strategy to avoid unnecessary legal battles.
Mr Zulu emphasised the importance of farm dwellers understanding their rights under ESTA. The law safeguarded their homes, jobs, and access to land while ensuring they were not evicted without due process. The Department had worked closely with Legal Aid South Africa to provide legal support for farm dwellers involved in disputes, reinforcing the state’s commitment to ensuring land tenure security.
Mr Zulu then discussed the Department’s progress on Labour Tenancy Claims and its allocation of budgetary resources to support land reform efforts. Significant attention has been given to finalising labour tenancy claims. These claims involved individuals who worked or had worked on farms in exchange for the right to live on and use a portion of the land. The process remained complex, often requiring the involvement of the Land Court to adjudicate disputes between farm dwellers and landowners. He noted that despite challenges, the Department has made considerable progress in processing claims, particularly during the 2023/24 fiscal year.
Mr Zulu reported that as part of its effort to address historical land injustices, the Department had acquired 5 907 hectares during the 2023/24 period for farm dwellers and labour tenants. These acquisitions were crucial to ensuring the long-term security of tenure for these vulnerable populations. He highlighted trends in budget allocation, illustrating the Department’s commitment to providing the necessary resources to support Communal Land Associations (CLAs) and labour tenants. CLAs have faced numerous challenges regarding management and governance. The Department aimed to implement mitigation strategies to address these issues and ensure that CLAs operated efficiently.
Mr Zulu presented a series of mitigation strategies to address the gaps in land tenure security. These strategies included more robust enforcement mechanisms to ensure farm dwellers and labour tenants were not unlawfully evicted, and enhanced monitoring and reporting systems to track tenure disputes and provide early intervention where necessary. He emphasised the need for sustained budgetary support to facilitate land reform initiatives. Without sufficient resources, the Department's ability to secure and redistribute land would be limited. Labour Tenancy Claims must be processed efficiently, with priority given to cases that have languished in the system for extended periods. The Department recognised the importance of working closely with traditional leaders in rural areas to ensure that land redistribution efforts aligned with local customs and governance structures. A balanced approach was needed to respect traditional governance while ensuring compliance with national legal frameworks.

Questions
Mr B Madikizela (DA) questioned why slide 34 listed no labour tenant land claims in the Western Cape, while R9bn was allocated to this province. He asked the Department if there was any difference in the occurrence of evictions before and after the enactment of ESTA. If the legislation was not stopping evictions, what should be done?
Ms Lucas also raised concerns about enforcement, asking how the Department could claim to be protecting farm dwellers when evictions continued to occur.
Mr N Matiase (EFF) did not get the sense that the Department had done enough to address findings arising from public hearings on labour tenants. He mentioned several specific cases in Mpumalanga and Limpopo provinces that needed attention.
Mr K Madlala (MK) asked whether the land claimant resolution had been presented to Cabinet and, if so, what its position was. When would the Department present land administration policy to Cabinet and Parliament, and did it have the capacity to implement the policy? When would a permanent replacement for the Interim Protection of Informal Land Rights Act (IPILRA) be tabled?
A Committee Member asked how much the State had paid in compensation to landowners when land was given to labour tenants. They observed that it would have taken as many as 50 years to resolve 20 000 claims at the current rate. This was a big problem. They suggested inviting Special Master for Labour Tenants Prof Richard Levin to address the Committee.
Dr Boshoff asked whether there was any correlation between the success of land reform and labour tenants. He expected there would be, given that labour tenants would invariably be experienced at working the land. He highlighted the relationship between land tenure success and the broader objectives of land reform, suggesting that failure to secure tenure could undermine the reform agenda.
Mr M Mrara (ANC) brought up the issue of land ownership and control, particularly among Black South Africans. He argued that many claims lodged do not reflect the realities on the ground, calling for an audit to ensure accuracy in claims. He also stressed the need for an effective policy to prevent further unjust evictions.
Responses
The Department confirmed that the land claimant resolution had been tabled to Cabinet and the Department was currently implementing the action plan. Admittedly, not much had been done to raise awareness of land claim rights. Informal land rights could not be formalised until IPILRA was replaced with permanent legislation, as it just protected informal rights. The Department pointed out that the rights given to labour tenant claimants by ESTA were similar to those given to farm dwellers. There was no objection to undertaking an audit but it should be done on the basis of the actual situation of people on the ground who had not lodged claims but still faced tenure security challenges, rather than the situation on paper.
The Department explained that the labour tenant system was not applied in the Western Cape, where the so-called “dop system” was used, according to which labourers were paid in kind rather than cash. The Department reiterated the importance of the ESTA amendments and the establishment of mediation panels to resolve disputes before they escalated to eviction. The Department was relying on the Statistics South Africa survey from 2011. The Department admitted that settlements had not increased significantly and said that negotiated settlements were a significant bottleneck because they involved the courts. It was too early to assess the direct impact of the ESTA amendments.
(Some of the Department’s responses to questions were inaudible)
Conclusion
A Committee Member observed that it was difficult to blame DALRRD entirely for the challenges it was faced with. The Committee should engage and reflect on how the matter could be elevated to the whole of government so that more resources and personnel could be dedicated to the complex issues at hand.
A Committee Member argued that the 7
th
administration needed to change its posture to deal with land issues.
A Committee Member said that the Department should update the statistics it was using. They wondered why landowners who frustrated the will of the state by illegally evicting labour tenants could not be simply arrested.
The Department acknowledged the Committee’s comments. It pointed out that its responsibility was only to administer the law, not enforce it. There was a need to engage the security cluster on this point.
(Some of the Department’s responses were inaudible)
The meeting was adjourned.
