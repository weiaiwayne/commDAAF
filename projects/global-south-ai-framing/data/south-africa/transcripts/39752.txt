Meeting Summary
The Portfolio Committee's meeting focused primarily on the proposed merger of SENTECH and Broadband Infraco (BBI) to form the State Digital Infrastructure Company (SDIC), a strategic move aimed at consolidating information communication technology (ICT) infrastructure in South Africa. The merger was intended to optimise resources, reduce redundancy and improve connectivity, particularly in underserved areas, by combining SENTECH’s wireless capabilities with BBI’s fibre infrastructure. Despite the merger's potential, the meeting highlighted financial and operational challenges, especially BBI's financial instability and reliance on leased infrastructure. Members raised concerns about the sustainability of the merger, questioning if it was the most effective solution and debating alternative approaches to leverage existing infrastructure efficiently.
A turnaround strategy for BBI, requiring R2.3 billion in funding, was presented, alongside discussions of potential government support and additional funding sources. Participants questioned the efficacy and feasibility of the merger, with some Members advocating a reassessment of the initiative, highlighting the need to ensure a robust and clear business case before proceeding. Concerns were also raised about the legal framework for the merger, and the importance of aligning with broader government goals for digital infrastructure, especially under budgetary constraints.
Discussions extended to the operational and financial viability of BBI and SENTECH, with calls for a detailed analysis to avoid further financial strain on either entity. There was also a strong sentiment to prioritise the turnaround of the South African Post Office, given its critical public role. Members questioned the wisdom of investing further in struggling entities, and suggested focusing on strategic consolidation under the SDIC for long-term sustainability.
The Film and Publication Board (FPB) also presented its progress in implementing the FPB Amendment Act, focusing on child protection in digital media and the regulatory challenges posed by new content platforms. Members stressed the importance of its role in safeguarding public values, and discussed the need for effective partnerships with international digital platforms to ensure compliance. The presentation highlighted FPB’s achievements and ongoing challenges, including resource limitations and the need to strengthen public outreach, especially concerning digital safety.
The meeting concluded with procedural matters, including the adoption of minutes from previous meetings and discussions on ICASA’s council composition, emphasising gender representation and diversity among the shortlisted candidates.

Briefing on the establishment of the State Digital Infrastructure Company
Mr Omega Shilembe, Deputy Director-General (DDG): ICT Enterprise and Public Entity Oversight, Department of Communications and Digital Technologies (DCDT), led the Committee through the presentation.
The presentation detailed the proposed merger of SENTECH and Broadband Infraco (BBI) to form the State Digital Infrastructure Company, a move rooted in multiple government policies like the Presidential Review Committee (PRC) recommendations, the National Development Plan (NDP), SA Connect, and the information communication technology (ICT) Policy White Paper. This rationalisation aimed to address the current fragmented ICT service landscape by consolidating resources and infrastructure across state-owned entities (SOEs). The ultimate goal was to streamline service delivery, eliminate redundancy, and establish a unified, efficient approach to providing broadband and ICT services to South African citizens.
The merged entity would combine SENTECH’s wireless broadband infrastructure with BBI’s fibre-optic network. SENTECH operates through wireless technology, whereas BBI’s strength lies in its optic fibre infrastructure. Together, they would serve as an open-access network focused on "last mile" broadband solutions for the public sector, especially schools, healthcare facilities, and other high-data-demand sectors. The merger would leverage SENTECH’s extensive terrestrial and satellite direct-to-home (DTH) platforms for radio and TV broadcasting, providing an efficient and widespread infrastructure for content distribution. This partnership would also fulfil South Africa's mandate for universal broadband access by consolidating SENTECH's and BBI’s infrastructures, optimising existing services, and expanding capabilities across the nation. The entity would also maintain connectivity with the global network via both the East and West African undersea cables, thus ensuring network redundancy and reliability.
Mr Shilembe said the benefit of rationalisation was that the complementary infrastructure of SENTECH and BBI allowed for a more cost-effective service delivery model by utilising SENTECH’s existing national support structures. For example, SENTECH’s regional presence would enable the combined entity to manage points of presence (PoP) efficiently across South Africa. This alignment was expected to improve connectivity and service access in underserved areas, creating a significant public benefit.
Referring to the drawbacks of rationalisation, he said a legal review by the Office of the State Law Advisor (OSLA) had recommended fast-tracking the merger through Chapter 5 of the Companies Act, which addresses mergers and acquisitions. However, the merger brings financial challenges, particularly for BBI. Due diligence conducted by SENTECH identified a series of financial concerns: BBI’s weak balance sheet, ongoing solvency and liquidity issues, and its inability to meet financial obligations due to persistent losses. The due diligence report also highlighted the ageing fibre network infrastructure, which had suffered from a lack of maintenance and upgrades. BBI’s cash flow was negative, and its revenue generation potential was compromised by declining margins and heavy reliance on leased infrastructure. As of early FY2024, BBI was technically insolvent and lacked sufficient assets to cover its long-term liabilities.
BBI’s turnaround strategy
To address these financial challenges, a turnaround strategy has been developed. BBI estimated a funding requirement of R2.3 billion over four years, with a projected internal rate of return (IRR) of 14.96% and a positive net present value (NPV). SA Connect, a government-backed project, had committed capital expenditures of R518 million and R818 million over two years to support BBI’s immediate funding needs. Additionally, R1.3 billion would be invested in network upgrades and technology enhancements to improve revenue potential and service reliability. Working capital of R120 million had been allocated for managed services, while R209 million was needed to settle liabilities with Transnet and Eskom.
The Department, SENTECH and BBI were exploring additional funding avenues to support the merger. Given the turnaround plan’s lengthy timeline, the Department was drafting terms of reference (TORs) to appoint a transactional advisor. This advisor would conduct a comprehensive situational analysis and propose the most viable legal frameworks, including options beyond the Companies Act. One possibility under consideration was creating a larger State Digital Infrastructure Company (SDIC) to unify digital infrastructure across various SOEs like Transnet, Eskom, the South African National Roads Agency Ltd (SANRAL), and municipal entities. In this scenario, both SENTECH and BBI would be folded into the SDIC to better manage state-owned digital assets and services.
The Department aimed to implement the merger gradually, with preliminary recommendations adopted as they become feasible to avoid delays. Immediate efforts were focused on securing the required R1.8 billion to bolster BBI’s financial position, with the intention to build a sustainable and resilient state-owned digital infrastructure that could support the country’s ICT goals well into the future. This roadmap would provide a structured path to establishing a comprehensive national digital infrastructure, improving public sector connectivity, and ensuring efficient and widespread broadband access across South Africa.

The next agenda item was an update from the Film and Publication Board on implementing the FPB Amendment Act, which had been amended in the last month of the previous term.
Update on implementing FPB Amendment
Act
Ms Zama Nkosi, Chairperson, Film and Publication Board (FPB) said that because they anticipated that time would be an issue, they had their 53 page presentation into 20 slides. Much of the content had been previously shared with the Committee.
Adv Makhosazana Lindhorst, Acting CEO, FPB, supported by her colleagues, led the Committee through the presentation.
The presentation covered the Film and Publication Board's (FPB) progress in implementing the Amendment Act of 2019, which was designed to address the shifting landscape of media content distribution, especially with the rise of online streaming and user-generated content. The FPB's mandate involved regulating films, games and publications to protect children from harmful content, support parental control through classification systems, and combat the distribution of child pornography and prohibited content. The amendments were motivated by technological advances, the need to redefine and prosecute child sexual abuse material, and alignment with other laws like the Sexual Offences Act.
The amendments aimed to create a regulatory framework that accommodates digital content and online distribution, requiring platforms to implement age ratings and provide legal clarity on prohibited content.
Key benefits included enhanced child protection, consumer guidance, and streamlined compliance processes for content distributors. FPB had expanded its capacity, increasing its workforce and investing in training on technologies like artificial intelligence (AI) and cybersecurity to handle the increased volume of online content.
Since the amendments, FPB has conducted extensive compliance inspections and issued numerous administrative notices for contraventions. The enforcement committee, supported by FPB, had been instrumental in prosecuting offenders, collaborating with the South African Police Service (SAPS) in cases involving unregistered distributors, and confiscating prohibited content. This enforcement had extended to addressing child protection concerns in high-profile cases, where the FPB had worked with law enforcement to convict perpetrators involved in child sexual abuse and distribution of related content.
The FPB had established partnerships with both local and international entities, joining organisations like INHOPE to combat child sexual abuse material (CSAM) on a global scale. Locally, it collaborates with SAPS, social media companies, and NGOs to foster digital safety awareness and training. The FPB has also strengthened its international footprint by joining forums such as the Global Online Safety Regulators Network, and partnering with the International Telecommunications Union (ITU) on cybersecurity initiatives.
The FPB faced several challenges, including insufficient funding for its expanded mandate, industry pushback, and fragmentation of laws on hate speech. To address these issues, it was enhancing its revenue model, increasing public awareness, and improving partnerships with industry players. It also seeks to overcome legislative gaps with the DCDT, and has prioritised internal talent management to bolster its operations.
In summary, the FPB was progressing in adapting to digital content regulation, enforcing protection for children, and building collaborative frameworks to ensure effective oversight. This presentation outlined FPB’s commitment to safeguarding public interests through regulatory clarity, enhanced enforcement, and partnerships, despite ongoing challenges in resources and legislative alignment.

Discussion
Ms Mathys inquired about any arrangements made with international platforms, such as Netflix and social media accounts like X and TikTok, to require them to obtain licences from various government entities to operate in the country. She asked if there had been engagement with the relevant licensing entity, as this could be an entry point to ensure compliance, by potentially threatening licence revocation or non-issuance. She asked for further information on what was happening in this regard, and how the Committee might facilitate the process. She emphasised that it should not be the case that if a concerning video was reported on TikTok, for example, there was no direct communication with someone high-ranking within the company. She stressed that the country should not be taken for granted and should not have to wait extended periods for responses. She pointed out that these companies should review their obligations under national law and should act, given that they were granted operational power under a specific act. She suggested that the process may need facilitation to ensure compliance, and prevent these companies from operating if they fail to do so. She also urged that they establish internal structures to ensure prompt action when concerns were raised, expressing her frustration at the perceived second-class treatment in their own country.
Mr Subrathie thanked the presenters for their professionalism, and emphasised that of all the entities he had interacted with, this one held a particular importance for him as a South African citizen, a parent, and someone who values the moral integrity of the country. He urged that the significance of this Department should not be underestimated, recognising that it operates in an increasingly challenging and evolving environment. He observed that the Chairperson prioritises other tangible departments, stressing the need to focus funding on preserving the moral fabric of future generations. He recalled a time when strict age restrictions were enforced by parents, and questioned where that vigilance had gone. He stressed that if this Department failed to act, future generations could suffer from the consequences.
He shared his personal investment in the Department's mission, inspired by a leader who once called for a moral revolution in South Africa, addressing issues such as violence in schools and teenage pregnancy. He questioned whether the Department had "dropped the ball" on these matters, suggesting it had not adequately communicated with the public about potential harms. As an active user of TikTok, he noted he had not seen any relevant messages on platforms like WhatsApp, TikTok, or community radio stations, despite these being low-cost ways to spread awareness. He criticised the Department constructively, saying it had not effectively used social media to reach people.
He challenged the Department to close gaps in communication, and asked why government did not act quickly when harmful international content impacted South African children. He wondered about the fine line between freedom of expression and protecting children, noting that such issues required careful regulation.
He also questioned the Department's independence, expressing concern that its integrity could be compromised if it depended on revenue from those it regulates. He praised the budget increase from R60 million to R85 million, but questioned the criteria for performance incentives given to 75 staff members, given his dissatisfaction with communication efforts. He inquired about the annual income of R3 million, potentially from interest, and asked if AI tools had been used to reduce manual content rating time.
He also wanted to know if the Department had measured the impact of child pornography on society over recent decades, aiming for outcomes that actively reduce immorality in young people. He called for specific success stories that demonstrate tangible impact, and mentioned that Members of the Committee should have clear channels for logging complaints.
Finally, he requested a clear definition of cyberbullying, commenting that the response to it had not been effectively communicated. He emphasised that both children and adults should know how to report such incidents, and suggested the need for more direct and accessible support channels for those affected.
Mr Vilakazi said that while the FPB had mentioned a self-classification system, he was unclear on how this self-classification was monitored and what enforcement measures were in place. He inquired whether they had the capacity and capability to take action, such as removing or banning content, if something inappropriate was found.
Referring to the financial slide, he recalled seeing an amount of R79 000, and questioned whether it was supposed to represent R79 million, as the missing zeros caused confusion about the budget size. He asked what constraints prevented the organisation from achieving 100% performance, despite the noted improvements, and inquired about the collection rates for fees, questioning who exactly was responsible for payments. He wondered if entities like Netflix fell into these categories, and sought clarification on the collection rate.
He expressed concern about manual monitoring, particularly for explicit content, describing it as abusive to staff members, and questioned why this process had not changed. He found it offensive that anyone would have to endure this daily, and urged the Department to consider alternative solutions.
He also raised the idea of implementing levies, suggesting that while the organisation required funding, it could risk compromising its independence by relying on the market. A levy could ensure consistent funding without relying on potential conflicts of interest. Finally, he asked for an update on the National Association of Broadcasters (NAB) court case, as he had been curious about its progress and wanted to know its current status.
Mr Mrara expressed his agreement with a previous Member, emphasising the importance of this Department addressing critical issues. He raised a key concern about the erosion of African values, which he felt should be a priority for the Department as a fully established regulatory body. He suggested that the DCDT's voice should be more prominent on these matters, as regulatory bodies were respected across government.
He highlighted that national sovereignty should be reflected in the Department’s policies, especially before engaging with foreign networks, noting that other countries were stringent on such matters. He cited Cuba as an example, where their education system did not expose children or parents to content that could be offensive, thanks to strong partnerships with both basic and higher education departments. He wondered if similar partnerships could be established within South Africa to support the Department's influence, and questioned if school governing bodies (SGBs) could assist in conveying its messages.
Raising the issue of African values, he said that even religious bodies often complied with national laws due to sovereignty. He appreciated the ongoing discussion about amending the law, emphasising that it was Parliament’s responsibility to reinforce societal values.
He also encouraged the Department's representatives to attend parents' meetings to better understand their concerns regarding media content and language, as many parents felt this was linked to the erosion of African values. He urged the Department to introspect on its responsibilities and deepen its commitment to addressing these issues.
He then turned to the presentation on finances, commenting that it seemed high-level and he had difficulty in understanding certain aspects, particularly the reference to the cost of employment (CoE). He inquired if the Department had a detailed chronogram to guide and measure expenditure patterns related to COE. He suggested that the Department employ field workers to engage in community public education, asking if such initiatives were part of their planning. He recommended that the DCDT consider requesting a budget specifically to hire field workers to support outreach efforts, emphasising that these were his primary concerns.
The Chairperson said she had many questions, but most had already been addressed by other Members. Observing that this was their first engagement and certainly not the last, she expressed confidence that she would have further opportunities to discuss additional issues with the Chairperson and CEO in the future. Expressing concern about the time, she handed over to them and asked them to respond to the issues raised.
FPB' s responses
Ms Mkosi expressed agreement with the sentiments on the critical importance of the Department. She said that whenever she was in the Department’s "emergency room," she preferred to speak last, after others had discussed connectivity, to emphasise the need to ensure that connectivity was pursued responsibly and safely. She noted that in the drive to connect every household and even young children, the essential aspects of safety and responsibility were often overlooked.
She reaffirmed the board's advocacy for these priorities and highlighted that the DCDT’s role in supporting law enforcement was one measure of success beyond reducing issues like bullying. She cited their achievements in securing convictions and assisting with content takedowns as examples of their positive impact.
Acknowledging the constructive feedback from Members, she said that while there were limitations due to resources, there was indeed more they could do to expand their reach. She agreed that if perpetrators used online platforms, these same platforms could be harnessed for positive impact. She accepted the challenge to explore beyond traditional methods, including field workers -- of whom there were currently too few -- and to better utilise digital platforms.
She also emphasised the importance of interstate collaboration, particularly with government departments and agencies, acknowledging that while some efforts had been made, there was still much more that could be achieved together.
Ms Beverley Nkumanda, Corporate Services Executive, thanked the Members for their valuable input and acknowledged their concerns. She noted that their TikTok presence was currently not educational, but said that they had reached out to the National Electronic Media Institute of SA (NEMISA), an entity that trains young people in digital skills, to help with social media efforts. She explained that they currently had only one digital coordinator managing all social platforms, so they had asked NEMISA's CEO for additional support. She promised improvements by November. She also mentioned plans to use platforms like LinkedIn, Facebook, and TikTok more effectively, recognising the importance of reaching the public where they are active.
Regarding content monitoring, she said staff members were manually analysing material, following standards that limit work on such sensitive content to four hours per day for wellness purposes. To support this, they planned to add staff to help rotate the analysis workload. She pointed out that material arrives from various provinces and through law enforcement channels, such as SAPS, which transfers a large amount of content to them. However, due to limited staff and budget, she emphasised the need for enhanced partnerships with law enforcement to secure funding and assistance for this work.
Addressing the performance incentives, she said that after initially achieving a 53% success rate when implementing the act without bonuses, they had improved to 93% in performance. She clarified that the recognition of staff through incentives was due to the alignment of their work with agency objectives, especially given past achievements that had not been acknowledged.
On the issue of CoE and staffing, she said that they had initially approached the Minister with a request for 70 additional positions, raising the CoE from R65 million to R80 million due to hiring new executives to guide the Department. However, she acknowledged that CoE now represented 77% of the grant, which was unsustainable, and that revenue generation and collaboration were crucial to address this.
The FPB currently had three field workers located in KZN, Limpopo and the Western Cape, which was insufficient. They had partnerships with the Department of Education, particularly focusing on life orientation teachers. to help convey important messages to parents, teachers, and students. She emphasised that these efforts alone were not enough, and digital outreach, including TikTok, was essential.
She also highlighted that while government resources were limited, they were building collaborations with platform distributors, who were responsible for protecting their users. By working together, they hoped to provide guidance on parental controls and help educate the community. She concluded by reiterating the need to balance resource limitations while ensuring community protection through these partnerships.
Ms Hulisani Bridget Ramugadi, Chief Financial Officer (CFO), said she would address two questions. First, she clarified that the "other income" of R3 million was the interest expected to be earned for the year. Regarding the collection rate, she explained that once distributors were registered, they were able to collect 100% of the fees listed. However, the primary challenge lies in getting distributors to register or comply with classification requirements for their materials. Once they were onboarded, the relationship with them was positive.
Adv Lindhorst provided an update on the NAB matter, explaining that they were currently awaiting a court date.
Regarding self-classification, she clarified that while distributors classify their own content, the Department monitored compliance by sampling and reviewing a selection of movies each quarter to ensure accurate age ratings. If a film was misclassified, they retrained the distributor and allowed reclassification. Continued non-compliance, however, would lead to enforcement action, though distributors had been compliant thus far. They were exploring additional measures, especially for larger platforms, and had initiated internal discussions on potentially geo-blocking social media platforms that distribute content in South Africa without compliance.
She commented that a major distributor, Apple, had been noncompliant for a significant time, impacting their revenue. After issuing a compliance notice, Apple responded through their lawyers, agreeing to comply. She mentioned that they were considering geo-blocking options to enforce compliance, as this had been a considerable challenge, and they may need support to implement such measures.
On the topic of preserving African values, she expressed agreement with the concerns raised. and highlighted their partnership with the Departments of Basic Education and Higher Education to engage with parents. She also emphasised the importance of community guidelines to ensure that social media standards align with South African values, thereby helping to preserve the country's moral fabric.
The Chairperson thanked the CEO, chairperson, and executives, describing the presentation as enlightening. She expressed appreciation for the work being done by the FPB, and echoed Members' sentiments that while significant progress had been made, more needed to be done. She acknowledged the importance of the FPB's role in addressing online harms, which was a major concern globally, and commended them for their efforts in this area.
She assured them they would review the issues raised, particularly those where support was needed. She emphasised the importance of keeping these issues at the forefront of their agenda as they moved into the Medium-Term Development Plan (MTDP) process. She noted that the Department had recently held a strategic planning session, and encouraged them to prioritise these concerns.
Sub-Committee report on appointment of ICASA Councillors
The Chairperson addressed the issue regarding ICASA, stating for the record that although Mr Ndhlela had left, she had informed the Secretariat of her understanding that a quorum had been formed at the start of a meeting and did not need to be reassessed for each new agenda item.
Before proceeding, she also addressed an issue raised by Ms Mathys, who had submitted an apology, and clarified that she might not have understood the context leading to the list shared on the group. She explained that during the sub-committee meeting, Members had discussed various issues, including the demographic composition of the current ICASA Council, the gender balance, and the representation of different national groups and age demographics. While there was a request to consider geographical spread, she had acknowledged the difficulty in doing so given that ICASA Members were professionals who were mobile. She said that the names recommended for shortlisting had been shared in the WhatsApp group after the Members agreed, solely for record-keeping purposes, and not for further discussion, as these issues had already been deliberated during the meeting.
She said that the current ICASA Council had five members, four of whom were female, along with the Chairperson. With ICASA intending to have nine members, they would aim for a gender split of at least 50%, which translated to a minimum of five female members. While this was not a legislated requirement, it aligned with Ms Mathys's concerns regarding gender consciousness. She reiterated that the goal was to prioritise women's representation, viewing 50% as a minimum standard rather than a maximum.
Finally, she emphasised that the list had been thoroughly reviewed by political parties over two days, and they had concluded that they had not been able to exclude women from the selection.
Mr Thembinkosi Ngoma, Committee Secretary, led the Committee through the presentation of the 17 ICASA shortlisted candidates.
Discussion
The Chairperson thanked Mr Ngoma for the report, but asked him to strike the second part of the presentation from the record. She noted that Ms Dlamini had objected, and while she had asked for an explanation, she was unsure if Ms Dlamini's response had been recorded, though she acknowledged she might have been mistaken due to fatigue. However, she expressed confidence that Ms Dlamini would be willing to restate her position if needed.
She then reiterated that they were not making appointments at this stage, but rather moving towards a shortlist from an initial pool of 270 candidates. She emphasised that all parties had reviewed the candidates based on skills, competence, and other relevant factors to narrow down the list to 17 names for interviews. She concluded by noting that the interviews would be the stage at which they would select the best candidates.
Mr Subrathie expressed his appreciation to the Chairperson for the way the latest stages of the process had been conducted, specifically as the Chairperson of the sub-Committee. He also appreciated the reflection on gender representation within the existing board, noting that he felt reassured that the board would maintain representation by at least four women, even if no additional females were appointed from the 17 shortlisted candidates.
He had raised the issue of ensuring the list represented the diversity of the Committee in a previous meeting. He was pleased with the information provided, confirming that this had been achieved. He also expressed satisfaction with the Committee’s decision to allow the two existing Members eligible for re-election to participate in the interview process, where they would distinguish themselves.
As all the key considerations had been addressed, he did not see a need for any further additions or deletions to the list, and moved to adopt the shortlisted candidates.
Ms Mathys expressed appreciation for the input and acknowledged the complexity of the process, noting it could not be taken lightly, and had involved multiple women advocating for representation. While recognising that there were already four women on the ICASA council, she pointed out that, as Members of Parliament, they were responsible for producing a list that reflected gender representation. She noted that about 150 women had applied, yet only four were shortlisted, which she found disappointing.
She observed that gender balancing was not something that happened naturally, but required deliberate effort, calling the current list "a violent list" due to the lack of female representation, despite the number of women applicants. She urged the Committee to reconsider and return to the drawing board to improve gender representation, emphasising that as Members of Parliament, they represented a country where women constituted 52% of the population.
She also clarified that the EFF had reservations about including two former ICASA members in the shortlist, as she believed these returning members had previously underperformed. She suggested that instead of reappointing board members who served on multiple government boards, they should seek individuals with fresh perspectives and no prior government board experience, to bring renewed hope to the Committee.
Finally, she commented that while they could address this in interviews, she did not support the idea of "rewarding underperformance" by reappointing former members, stating that this practice seemed emblematic of rewarding underperformance.
Mr Mrara said he understood the Chairperson had worked diligently to identify the 17 names on the shortlist. He expressed appreciation for her efforts, acknowledging that she had fulfilled the requirements. However, he noted that if certain requirements, such as representation for women or individuals with disabilities, had not been fully met, the Committee should be given a broad mandate to re-evaluate the list without needing to report back to the Portfolio Committee.
He emphasised the importance of maintaining confidentiality once the shortlist was finalised to avoid any potential leaks, which could harm the Committee's reputation. He cautioned that if the names were made public, intentionally or otherwise, it could create a negative perception of the Committee. To address this, he recommended considering confidentiality agreements for participants to protect the integrity of the process, given the high level of contestation and sensitivity involved.
In summary, he stressed the need for both a broad mandate to ensure all representation requirements were met, and confidentiality measures to uphold the integrity of the process.
The Chairperson proposed a compromise -- to meet Members halfway by revisiting the list submitted by all political parties, as recommended by the Members. Acknowledging that another Member had raised the issue of disability, she suggested revisiting the list to address representation for women and individuals with disabilities. She clarified that there would be no need to restart the entire process.
She outlined her understanding of the Committee's mandate: to return to the list provided by political parties, add more women, and identify candidates with disabilities for inclusion.
She proposed that the Committee adopt the report on shortlisted candidates with these additions, pending finalisation. She invited any objections, noting that Mr Subrathie moved for adoption and Mr Mrara seconded.
