WC Annual Reports 2024/25
The Standing Committee on the Premier and Constitutional Matters met to consider the 2024/25 Annual Report of the Department of the Premier (DotP). The meeting focused on the implementation of the Provincial Safety Plan, cross-departmental programmes, fiscal constraints, climate partnerships, and the alignment of provincial and national development plans.
Premier Winde reported a 4% reduction in the overall provincial murder rate and an 11% decline in LEAP-targeted areas, attributing this progress to the force-multiplier approach and the strong partnerships between the South African Police Service (SAPS) and LEAP officers. He highlighted the chronic under-resourcing of SAPS in the province and called for greater national support, noting that despite population growth in Cape Town over the past decade, the number of police officers had declined. The Premier emphasised that this situation was unsustainable and that residents, particularly in high-crime areas, continued to suffer the consequences.
He outlined the transversal social initiatives forming part of the Provincial Safety Plan, such as the Planet Youth programme, which addresses adolescent substance abuse, and the Khulisa Care programme, which targets child stunting in collaboration with Shoprite and the DG Murray Trust. He explained that these programmes were managed across various departments but coordinated through the Department of the Premier, which provides overarching policy oversight.
On climate commitments, Premier Winde confirmed that the Western Cape Government was an active member of the Under2 Coalition, a global network of regional governments committed to climate action. He clarified that all donations and gifts received in relation to this partnership were properly declared and recorded in the provincial gift register. He also emphasised the importance of aligning the Provincial Strategic Plan (PSP) with the National Development Plan (NDP) and the Medium Term Development Plan (MTDP), stating that ongoing engagements with national government were necessary to ensure consistency in planning frameworks. He further reiterated the need for equitable funding allocations to provinces, especially in education, health, and safety sectors, to address the financial pressures caused by slow national economic growth.
Director-General Dr Harry Malila elaborated on the Department’s transversal achievements, including its Top Employer certification for the third consecutive year, making the Western Cape one of only two governments in South Africa to achieve this status. He explained that the certification benchmarked human resource practices against international standards and reflected the collective work of approximately 90 000 employees across eleven departments. Dr Malila also discussed the Department’s Artificial Intelligence (AI) Policy, which provides ethical guardrails for the responsible use of AI technologies in provincial departments such as Health and Education. He emphasised that the policy balanced innovation with data protection and privacy compliance.
He reported that most departmental vacancies had been filled, with the exception of one senior post, and that appointments had improved gender representation and operational efficiency. On the question of the Under2 Coalition reimbursement, Dr Malila clarified that the coalition had reimbursed the Department for an economy-class ticket for the Premier’s attendance at the 2024 Climate Week in New York. The reimbursement had been paid into the Provincial Revenue Fund and not to the Premier personally and was properly declared and audited by the Auditor-General.
The Department provided an overview of the Top Employer certification process, which involved over 2 000 questions across several domains, including ethics, culture, leadership, and diversity. She reported that the Western Cape Government had achieved its highest scores in people strategy, organisational culture, ethics, and integrity. Another official presented the Governance Policy Framework for Artificial Intelligence, explaining that it guided departments in the responsible and ethical deployment of AI technologies within existing governance structures.
During the discussion, Members raised several key issues. Members questioned inconsistencies in the Annual Report's description of the Under2 Coalition reimbursement and whether it triggered any obligations under the Executive Ethics Act.
Members also raised questions regarding the alignment of the Provincial Strategic Plan (PSP) with the National Development Plan (NDP) and the Medium Term Development Plan (MTDP).
Further discussions centred on digital inclusion and broadband access. Members expressed concern about the maintenance of rural e-centres and the equitable expansion of broadband to underserved communities.
Members raised questions on occupational health and safety, referring to the eleven OHS incidents reported during the year.
Members raised concerns regarding the extension of contracts to Quantum Leap Consulting, a company previously implicated in corruption allegations.
The Chairperson concluded that the Committee’s engagement with the Department had been thorough and that the discussion had covered all relevant aspects of the Annual Report. No members of the public were present to make submissions under the public participation segment.
In his closing remarks, Premier Winde thanked Members for their engagement and confirmed that he had written to the Registrar: Members’ Interests earlier that morning to seek clarity regarding the reimbursement matter. The Chairperson commended the Department for its clean audit outcome and strong performance, noting that the Western Cape Government continued to demonstrate good governance and accountability. He emphasised that while challenges remained, the Committee’s role was to identify weaknesses and provide the Department with the opportunity to address them.
The Committee then proceeded to its internal deliberations on drafting the Committee's report on the Annual Report.

Mr Alan Winde, Premier of the Western Cape, acknowledged the Committee's appreciation for the Department's work and addressed matters relating to the Provincial Safety Plan, its cross-departmental implementation, climate partnerships, alignment of planning documents, and funding constraints affecting service delivery.
He confirmed that the Provincial Safety Plan was directly linked to the question raised regarding safety data and reported that the province had recorded a 4% reduction in the overall murder rate, while areas under the Law Enforcement Advancement Plan (LEAP) reflected an 11% reduction. He welcomed this progress as the most significant improvement to date, noting that it demonstrated the impact of the force-multiplier approach.
Premier Winde stated that he had recently visited Mitchells Plain, where he met with the LEAP Commander and the South African Police Service (SAPS) Station Commander to assess the station’s resourcing position. He observed effective collaboration between SAPS and LEAP officials, emphasising that this partnership had become essential given the severe under-resourcing of SAPS in the province. He expressed concern that, despite population growth in the City of Cape Town from four million to five million residents over the past decade, the number of SAPS officers had declined. He described this situation as unsustainable and noted that residents across the Cape Flats were bearing the brunt of violent crime. He recalled meeting a resident whose sixteen-year-old grandson had been shot twenty-seven times, with no subsequent arrests, calling the incident horrendous and emblematic of the urgent need for better policing and stronger “boots-on-the-ground” interventions.
He explained that the Provincial Safety Plan functioned as a cross-departmental initiative, coordinated through multiple provincial departments. While the Department of Police Oversight and Community Safety led the LEAP programme, other components were managed by departments such as Health, which oversaw violence prevention. He indicated that the Standing Committee on Health would engage further on related interventions.
Premier Winde elaborated on the social prevention initiatives forming part of the safety plan. He referred to the Planet Youth programme, aimed at addressing early substance abuse among adolescents. More than 50 000 learners from 123 schools had been interviewed to identify behavioural risks and inform targeted interventions. He also discussed the Khulisa Care programme, an initiative developed within the Policy Unit in the Office of the Premier to tackle child stunting caused by malnutrition. The project, implemented in partnership with Shoprite and the DG Murray Trust, aimed to establish a scalable model for child wellness that could be replicated nationally if successful. These initiatives, he explained, were managed across standing committees but coordinated within the Department of the Premier, which played a transversal policy and oversight role.
On climate declarations and international partnerships, Premier Winde responded to questions about the Under2 Coalition, explaining that it consisted of approximately 132 regional governments working collectively on climate action. He addressed concerns from the African National Congress (ANC) regarding the validity of the declaration, stating that the commitment had been properly executed, reported to Cabinet, publicised through the media, and reflected in the Department’s official documents. He clarified that all donations or gifts received were processed through formal government systems and recorded in the official gift register, adding that the Director-General could provide additional detail on the administrative procedures.
In response to questions on alignment between the National Medium Term Development Plan (MTDP) and the Provincial Strategic Plan (PSP), he explained that work on the PSP had begun towards the end of the previous administration, led by the policy team within the Department of the Premier. Following the national elections, the Department had worked with national government departments to ensure the two frameworks were aligned. He recalled that this process was first discussed at a Premier’s Forum held in Beaufort West, attended by Cabinet Members, municipal managers, and political leaders, where consensus was reached on broad areas of alignment. While the plans were largely synchronised, he noted that certain areas lacked concurrence due to the absence of concurrent powers. He indicated that at a recent Lekgotla, he had raised concerns about the national reporting framework, which failed to account for provincial responsibilities, and said that further engagement with national government was planned to correct these gaps.
Addressing resource constraints and funding considerations, Premier Winde supported the proposal to secure additional funding for LEAP through the forthcoming adjustment and annual budget processes. He cautioned, however, that slow national economic growth had created significant fiscal pressures, especially within education and health. He reiterated the need for equitable funding allocations to provinces and emphasised that the Western Cape would continue advocating for greater financial support in the social development and safety sectors. He underlined the importance of obtaining a fair share of national funding for education, health, and policing, noting that high crime levels and resource shortages required joint advocacy from all stakeholders.
He further highlighted the need for better management of correctional facilities, describing how illicit activities, including the use of cell phones linked to organised crime, undermined the criminal justice system. He urged collective lobbying for increased national investment in policing, justice, and safety infrastructure, asserting that such reforms were crucial to public confidence and security. He concluded by calling for a unified provincial stance to advocate for improved national funding and intergovernmental collaboration before handing over to the Director-General for additional remarks.
Dr Harry Malila, Director-General (DG), DotP, thanked the Committee and stated that most issues had been addressed. He elaborated on three main areas: the Top Employer certification, the artificial intelligence (AI) policy, and governance and staffing matters, before clarifying the donation linked to the Premier’s international trip and the alignment between the Provincial Strategic Plan (PSP) and the National Development Plan (NDP).
He explained that the Western Cape Government had received Top Employer certification for three consecutive years, one of only two governments in South Africa to have achieved this status, alongside Gauteng. The certification benchmarked the province’s human resource practices against international standards, comparing the provincial administration with major global institutions. The certification covered all eleven departments, including Education and Health, and involved a transversal team effort across the provincial government. Dr Malila said this recognition reflected the work of approximately 90 000 employees and provided valuable data-driven insights for institutional benchmarking and continuous improvement. The goal was to progress from country-level certification to regional (blue) and eventually global (gold) certification, reflecting the maturity of the Western Cape’s organisational systems.
On artificial intelligence, Dr Malila noted that Cabinet had approved the province’s AI policy, which formed part of its broader innovation, governance, and culture framework. The policy established guardrails to ensure responsible AI use, safeguarding the privacy and protection of citizens’ data across provincial departments such as Health and Education. He emphasised that institutionalising AI within government structures required a balance between innovation and ethical responsibility and stated that the province was committed to applying AI with accountability while maintaining compliance with data and privacy laws.
In relation to staffing, Dr Malila reported significant progress in filling vacancies across key programmes, despite fiscal constraints. Positions had been filled in Connected Government Buildings, Corporate Legal Advisory Services, Organisational Capacity Development, Recruitment and Selection, and Corporate Governance and Administration. Only one senior post—Head of Corporate Services—remained vacant, with all programme managers currently reporting directly to him as the Accounting Officer. He highlighted that the appointments had strengthened gender representation and enhanced operational efficiency within the Department.
Regarding the donation query, Dr Malila clarified that the Under2 Coalition had reimbursed the Department for an economy-class flight ticket purchased for the Premier’s attendance at New York Climate Week in September 2024. The reimbursement, received in April 2025, was paid directly to the provincial revenue fund as a donation to government, not to the Premier personally.
He stressed that the Western Cape Government had funded the entire trip upfront, covering all travel costs for the Premier and officials. The Under2 Coalition’s payment, made six months later, was properly declared in the Department’s Annual Report and audited by the Auditor-General of South Africa (AGSA). He confirmed that the Premier had declared the matter to the Western Cape Provincial Parliament (WCPP) prior to the reimbursement, and that the disclosure met all legal and procedural requirements.
Dr Malila also addressed the alignment of the PSP with the NDP. He explained that the Western Cape’s priorities were fully aligned with the NDP’s three pillars: economic growth, poverty reduction, and building a capable state. The province’s Growth for Jobs (G4J) Strategy mirrored the NDP’s economic growth focus; its investments in education, health, and social development supported poverty reduction; and its emphasis on innovation, governance, and safety advanced the NDP’s vision of a capable and ethical state. He concluded that the province had ensured full coherence between national and provincial policy frameworks, demonstrating practical implementation of the NDP’s objectives.
Ms Letitia Isaacs, Director: People Transversal Capacity Enablement, DotP, thanked the Committee and provided an overview of the Top Employer certification process undertaken by the Western Cape Government.
She explained that the process was extensive, involving the completion of more than 2 000 questions supported by documented evidence. The assessment was structured across several domains, including Steer, Unite, Shape, and Attract. Under
Steer
, the evaluation focused on the Department’s business strategy, people strategy, and leadership approach. The
Unite
category assessed the organisation’s purpose, values, ethics, integrity, and commitment to diversity, equity, and inclusion. Under
Shape
, the focus was on organisational culture, digital human resources systems, and ways of working, while
Attract
examined employer branding, talent acquisition, onboarding, employee development, engagement, well-being, and reward and recognition practices.
Ms Isaacs reported that the Western Cape Government achieved its highest scores in ethics and integrity, people strategy, organisational culture, performance, and well-being. The greatest improvements from the previous assessment were observed in employer branding, diversity, equity and inclusion, employee engagement, reward and recognition, and offboarding.
She added that the Department was awaiting the latest certification results, expected by the end of November. She expressed confidence that the provincial administration would maintain or exceed its previous performance in the upcoming evaluation.
Governance Policy Framework for Artificial Intelligence
Another official noted that the Department had submitted its Governance Policy Framework for Artificial Intelligence (AI) to Cabinet at the same time that the national draft policy framework for AI was being considered. She explained that the framework positioned the governance of data and AI in relation to the deployment and use of AI technologies.
She stated that the framework was not prescriptive but served as a guide for departments to apply within their specific sectoral contexts. It promoted the responsible and ethical use of AI within advisory, regulatory, and legal environments.
She added that the framework included six key standards, core principles, and governance structures, which together formed the foundation for the successful deployment of AI. She emphasised that the Department had not created new structures but had instead built on existing ones, adapting and strengthening them to accommodate AI deployment and the integration of emerging technologies.

Dr Malila stated that he was comfortable providing clarity on the matter, as he had made the relevant declaration in his capacity as the Accounting Officer for the Department. He affirmed that the disclosure had been done correctly, emphasising that the payment in question had not been made to the Premier in his personal capacity. He explained that declarations under the Executive Ethics Act typically related to gifts or donations received personally, whereas this case involved the reimbursement of an economy-class ticket cost for the Under 2 Coalition.
He clarified that the reimbursement appeared in the report because the donation had been approved during the 2024/25 financial year, even though the payment itself had only been received in the following financial year. He added that the Premier would therefore not have been aware of the reimbursement when he made his declaration in February.
Dr Malila stressed that the reimbursement constituted a donation to the state and not to an individual, noting that such reimbursements were common when officials or representatives attended sponsored conferences. He stated that all such donations were disclosed in the Annual Report, whether they related to a specific official or a unit within the Department, as part of the Department’s commitment to transparency.
He further explained that the Western Cape Government had initially covered all costs for the trip, including those of the Premier and departmental officials. The reimbursement had later been paid into the Provincial Revenue Fund, making the fund—not the Premier—the beneficiary. He added that the amount of R51 000 would be reflected under the revenue component in the 2025/26 financial statements.
Dr Malila reaffirmed that the disclosure had been made correctly and that the Auditor had reviewed and confirmed the accuracy of the approach. He noted that the relevant code of conduct dealt with personal benefits or gifts to Members of the Legislature, which was not applicable in this instance.
He suggested that the Legislature might consider obtaining clarity on how such situations should be handled in future, particularly when a Member of Parliament (MP) participated in a trip funded by a department that fell under their vote. He concluded that the Premier had received no personal benefit from the trip and that even if the reimbursement had not been made, the expenditure would still have been incurred and paid from state funds. The payment had simply constituted a reimbursement into the Provincial Revenue Fund.

He noted that the Committee could be furnished with a detailed report on the areas covered by the eBus.
Dr Malila added that the Western Cape Government currently had broadband connectivity at approximately 1 914 sites across the province, in addition to 1 600 public Wi-Fi hotspots where citizens could access digital services. He explained that many e-centres operated from shared facilities, often co-located with Thusong Centres, libraries, or municipal buildings. He said regional managers were assigned to oversee the operation of these centres, and that he, along with the Premier and other senior officials, periodically conducted site visits and spot checks to ensure the provision of quality services.
He acknowledged the concerns raised by Mr Johnson regarding the condition of some facilities and stated that the Department would engage with municipalities to address the physical state of certain centres where necessary. Overall, he maintained that the quality of e-centres across the province remained satisfactory, with only a few requiring attention.
Turning to cyber security, Dr Malila referred to page 51 of the Annual Report, noting that the Department had received additional funding from the Provincial Treasury to strengthen its cyber security capabilities. A comprehensive cybersecurity programme had been implemented to safeguard the government's digital infrastructure and monitor its online systems.
He reported that the Department had successfully blocked approximately 330 000 malicious emails and restricted unauthorised attempts to upload harmful content onto government websites. He said the Department conducted regular awareness exercises, including simulated phishing emails, to train staff on identifying cyber threats.
Dr Malila concluded that cybersecurity remained a global challenge, but the Department had made significant progress in building a mature and proactive system. He reiterated that the Department's ongoing focus was to expand digital access, particularly in rural areas, within the constraints of available resources, ensuring that all residents of the province could benefit from digital connectivity.
Mr Egsaan Samaai, Deputy Director-General: E-Innovation, thanked the Chairperson and responded to questions regarding site visits. He stated that, in addition to departmental visits, a manager and two support staff also visited the sites. He offered to provide the name of the manager responsible for the e-centres in the eastern region to enable Members to follow up on site visits.
He noted that towards the end of the previous financial year, significant investments had been made in technical infrastructure, particularly around February and March, and that this work was continuing. He invited Members to report any issues arising from these visits.
Regarding broadband access in rural and informal settlements, he informed the Committee that 350 additional sites were being rolled out under the Broadband 2.0 initiative. He added that while most of these sites were schools, there were several others included, and a full list could be provided to the Committee.
Mr Samaai further reported that to prevent the exclusion of communities without access to digital infrastructure, the Department was piloting a project called the Digital e-kiosks. The project aimed to place e-kiosks at frontline service delivery points so that residents could access e-services when visiting these facilities. For example, citizens could complete online school applications while visiting for other services. The pilot sites included libraries in Beaufort West, Caledon, George, Mossel Bay, and Cape Agulhas, as well as several museums such as the Andal Mission Museum, Oudtshoorn Museum, and Beaufort West Museum. He explained that if the pilot proved successful, it would be expanded to rural and low-income communities.
He concluded by stating that he would provide Members with the number of unique visits to digital services. On cybersecurity, he confirmed that the Western Cape Government had not experienced any serious cyber incidents on its network. However, one Department had been affected through a third-party service provider, and the Department had provided support to restore its systems.
Premier Winde added that the question raised by Ms Lekker about whether the initiatives targeted poor communities could be addressed through the Geographic Information System (GIS) map available on the Western Cape Government website. He explained that by searching for “Western Cape Government Wi-Fi,” Members could access an interactive map showing all Wi-Fi hotspots. For example, zooming into Beaufort West would reveal approximately 25 hotspots, with at least 35 in the Khayelitsha area. He encouraged Members to use the tool to identify Wi-Fi access points within specific wards.
The Chairperson asked whether the Department collected user information or profiled visitors to the e-centres. He stated that such data would be valuable in understanding user behaviour and identifying what attracted people to use the centres. This, he said, would assist in increasing public uptake and fostering a broader information technology mindset.
Dr Malila responded that the Department did collect data on e-centre usage, including the number of daily visitors and the type of services they sought. He noted that many users accessed the centres to conduct school research projects, among other activities. He offered to provide the Chairperson with this data in tabulated form, should it be required.
Mr Samaai added that every user visiting an e-centre was required to register. He explained that the Department was in the process of implementing an automated registration system, which had been piloted in the previous year. During the pilot, it was observed that many people in rural areas did not have mobile phones, leading e-centre managers to use their own contact details to assist users in registering. He said this was one of the key lessons learned from the pilot. He confirmed that the Department maintained daily records of visitors but emphasised that personal data could not be shared, although aggregated user statistics could be made available.
Premier Winde reflected on the early pilot phase of the e-centres, noting that data from the initial rollout revealed that most users searched for information related to employment opportunities, education programmes, and school research projects. He said it had been particularly interesting to observe that health-related searches ranked quite high, especially in rural areas.
He recalled an example from one of the pilot sites situated in a farming community near Wilderness, where many residents searched for health information. He explained that this trend highlighted how people were using online resources to avoid the cost of travelling long distances to clinics, which often required taxi fares they could not afford.
Premier Winde observed that this finding raised further questions about the reliability of online medical information, as search engines could not replace professional medical advice. He said that, although digital resources might have improved since then, the data remained valuable as it revealed what services and information communities most needed. He concluded that such insights were important for ensuring that e-centres fulfilled their intended purpose of supporting education and access to essential information, rather than being used merely for entertainment.
The Chairperson noted that the discussion had become wide-ranging but reminded Members that the purpose of the meeting was to consider the Department’s Annual Report. He referred Members to Part B of the report, covering pages 104 to 114, and asked whether there were any comments or questions. Seeing none, he indicated that Part C usually fell within the scope of the Standing Committee on Public Accounts and was therefore not a major discussion point for this Committee. However, for completeness, he again invited any comments. No questions were raised on Part C.
Turning to Part D, covering pages 148 to 193, the Chairperson again asked for questions, but none were raised. He proceeded to Parts E and F, which contained financial information typically dealt with by other committees, yet invited Members to raise any matters for completeness.
Ms Lekker referred to the section on health, safety, and environmental management, noting that the report listed eleven formal and informal occupational health and safety (OHS) incidents, none resulting in serious injury. She asked the Department to provide a breakdown of these incidents, including corrective actions taken to prevent recurrence, and to explain how the OHS Committee ensured compliance with the Occupational Health and Safety Act. She further enquired about the seventeen safety and security awareness sessions conducted during the year, requesting clarity on the evaluation tools used to assess their impact on employee safety behaviour. Lastly, she asked what budget had been allocated for training and compliance, and whether the Department considered this allocation sufficient given the level of risk exposure.
The Chairperson acknowledged that the Committee had already covered several sections but agreed to allow Ms Lekker’s questions in the interest of completeness. He reiterated that Members had been invited to comment on Parts C through F earlier, where no questions had been raised, but said he would permit any remaining questions before proceeding. He then recognised Mr Herron.
Mr Herron referred to Part E on page 205, raising concerns regarding the extension of contracts to Quantum Leap Consulting. He recalled that the Provincial Forensic Services had previously found that contracts awarded to this company had been tainted by corruption. He noted that while the Department had litigated the matter, the court had ordered the province to honour the contracts that were originally awarded in 2019 or 2020. However, he expressed surprise that the Department had extended those contracts given the company’s past involvement in the investigation and requested clarification on the Department’s current relationship with Quantum Leap Consulting.
Dr Malila said that the Department considered the safety of its employees as a non-negotiable priority. He stated that the matter fell under the leadership of the Chief Financial Officer (CFO) within Corporate Services, which had implemented several initiatives to strengthen safety and compliance. He added that health and safety were regularly discussed at Provincial Top Management meetings and in engagements with organised labour, underscoring the Department’s commitment to maintaining a secure working environment.
He explained that safety and security were monitored daily, and that staff training was facilitated through departmental newsletters and the e-learning platform. This included modules on safeguarding departmental assets, handling of equipment such as laptops, and adherence to general safety protocols. He said that the responsible team reported to the Departmental Executive, and any incidents were addressed immediately. He noted that a substantial number of employees had completed both initial and refresher safety training, which demonstrated the Department’s proactive approach to occupational health and safety.
On the matter of Quantum Leap Consulting, Dr Malila said that the issue had a long history and invited Mr Buter to provide a detailed explanation.
Mr Lucas Buter, Head of Legal Services, stated that there had been findings of fraud and dishonesty involving Quantum Leap Consulting, which had been reported to the South African Police Service. He said that Legal Services had advised the Director-General at the time that the Department was legally entitled, and indeed obligated, to cancel the existing contracts based on the findings of the provincial forensic investigation.
He explained that Quantum Leap had challenged the cancellation in court, and the court had ruled against the Department. The judgment found that the Department could not rely on alleged corruption linked to a previous contract as a justification for cancelling a subsequent contract. Mr Buter said that although the Department disagreed with aspects of the court’s reasoning, it was decided not to appeal the ruling, as this was deemed the more prudent course of action.
He clarified that, in law, the court’s decision meant that the service provider could continue to participate in tenders and that there was no legal impediment to extending existing contracts. He added that the criminal matter arising from the earlier investigation remained ongoing and had not yet been concluded.
The Chairperson noted that there were no further responses or follow-up questions from Members. He stated that this concluded the Committee’s engagement with the Department.
He explained that the next item on the agenda was the public participation component, where members of the public could make submissions or provide input. However, he confirmed that no written submissions or requests to speak had been received. After verifying that there were no members of the public present wishing to comment, he proceeded to close that section of the meeting.
He thanked the Department and the Premier for their interaction and constructive engagement. He remarked that it was the Committee’s responsibility to interrogate the contents of the Annual Report thoroughly, and he commended the Department for its performance and accountability.
He added that, in his view, the standards and operational efficiency observed within the Western Cape Government stood in stark contrast to what was often seen elsewhere in government, stating that the issues prioritised for resolution in the province were those that other regions could only aspire to achieve.
He commended the Department for its clean audit outcome and strong performance results, noting that such achievements reflected a high level of governance and service delivery. He concluded by affirming that while there was always room for improvement, it remained the Committee’s role to identify weaknesses and provide the Department with the opportunity to address them. He then invited the Premier to offer closing remarks.
Concluding Remarks from the Premier
Premier Winde thanked the Chairperson and Members of the Committee for their engagement and questions. He extended his appreciation to the departmental team for their presence, dedication, and hard work, which had resulted in strong performance outcomes. He stated that their efforts were recognised and highly valued.
In response to an earlier question regarding the declaration of a specific amount, he informed the Committee that at 09:25 that morning, he had written a letter to Adv P Burgers, Registrar: Members’ Interests, requesting a meeting to obtain clarity on the matter. He assured Members that the Registrar would report back to the Committee once the engagement had taken place.
The Chairperson then thanked the Premier for his remarks.
Committee matters
The Chairperson further explained that upon reconvening, the Committee would proceed with its internal deliberations. This session would allow Members to provide input for inclusion in the draft Committee Report on the Department’s Annual Report. He clarified that the purpose of this stage was not to adopt or finalise the report but to identify key points for consideration during the drafting process.
He invited Members to raise their hands to contribute specific items or observations they wished to see reflected in the draft, noting that they would have another opportunity to review and refine the final version before adoption.
Mr Herron raised concerns regarding the explanation provided on page 15 of the report concerning the coverage of travel costs for Premier Winde under the Coalition. He noted that the report initially indicated the reimbursement was for an economy-class ticket for the Premier, but this appeared to have been changed to reflect coverage for the Premier in his capacity as Chairperson of the Coalition. He expressed that the wording in the report was either inaccurate, or the explanations received were inconsistent, and he indicated that the report could not be adopted with unclear or inaccurate information.
Mr Booysen added to Mr Herron’s concerns, suggesting that the Committee should formally request the outcome of the meeting between the Premier, the Registrar, and the Director-General. He indicated that the outcome of this engagement would provide a way forward and could form the basis of a Committee recommendation or resolution.
The Chairperson noted that the substantive issue was clarification and indicated he would return to other Members for further input.
Ms Lekker stated that the information needed to be clarified and corrected, noting that the report and prior explanations were inconsistent. She emphasised the need for accurate information from the Department, without suggesting that any declarations were necessarily required from the Premier, but that proper information must be obtained. She further highlighted that the Committee should receive a report on the adjusted resolutions and medium-term targets discussed in quarterly briefings to the Budget Committee.
Ms Lekker also raised the issue of the 600 000 jobs target set for 2035, requesting a written report outlining progress to date towards this commitment. She further noted that a Committee report on the Annual Report and budget session should be compiled, pending the resolutions made by the Committee.
Ms D Stephens (PA) queried the Department’s monitoring of earmarked funds allocated to the Office of the Children’s Commissioner, specifically the R8.676 million intended for further digital systems development. She requested clarification on how the Department would ensure proper oversight of these funds.
The Chairperson suggested that this query be captured as part of the Committee’s resolutions for follow-up. Ms Stephens noted that, given the Commissioner is new to the role, she would require support in this regard.
Ms Lekker raised a final point concerning a draft safety plan previously discussed in a prior Committee meeting. She requested that the Department provide an indication of when the draft plan would be made available to the public to allow community members to provide informed input.
These points were noted for inclusion in the Committee’s resolutions and follow-up actions.
The Chairperson noted that the discussion concerned inputs for the draft committee report and clarified that the Committee was not yet formally adopting resolutions. He stated that the draft report should capture the lack of clarity regarding the Under2 Coalition issue on page 15 relating to the donation to the provincial government, emphasising that this point required correction and clarification under committee follow-up.
He suggested that the draft report should also include broader impact information beyond the targets listed in the annual report. Specifically, he proposed that the report reflect overall strategic outcomes, including progress on job creation, expansion of online learning, and developments in the e-centres. The Chairperson indicated that these points aligned with earlier inputs from Members and would ensure that the report reflects not only planned outcomes but actual strategic impact.
He noted that issues raised by Members Stephens and Lekker, namely the monitoring of earmarked funds for the Commission for Children and the online availability of the draft safety plan, would be addressed under committee resolutions when the agenda reached that item.
The Chairperson confirmed that these inputs had been captured for inclusion in the draft committee report and sought confirmation from Members that the points had been correctly recorded. Once confirmed, he stated that the Committee would move on to the next agenda item concerning formal committee resolutions.
A Member then asked whether a resolution should request feedback on the exercise regarding the Commission for Children, to which the Chairperson indicated that this would guide the wording for inclusion under committee resolutions, alongside the draft safety plan availability.
Mr Herron clarified that his proposed resolution did not concern obtaining feedback on the Premier's trip but rather focused on ensuring that the wording in the report accurately described the sponsorship received. He emphasised that the Committee's interest was in the accuracy of the report and, if the wording was incorrect, determining the correct version.
The Chairperson noted that the resolution should cover whether the report required clarification or correction, and whether any further action or implications arose for other processes. He sought agreement from Members on this formulation and confirmed that it summarised the Committee’s intended outcome without putting words into any Member’s mouth.
Regarding the Commission for Children, the Chairperson suggested a resolution to obtain clarity on how the Department would monitor the use of the funding provided to the Commission and whether the office was fully operational. Ms Stephens confirmed that her concern was specifically about monitoring the allocation of funding to the Commission.
The Chairperson then addressed the draft safety plan, noting that the Committee had previously agreed to obtain public submissions to assist the Department in finalising the plan. He highlighted that, based on his enquiries, the draft safety plan had not yet been made available online for public input. He proposed that the Committee include a resolution to follow up on the online availability of the draft safety plan and ensure that it is accessible for public contributions.
He sought confirmation from Members, including Ms Lekker, on the proposed wording for the resolutions and whether it accurately reflected the Committee’s intentions.
Members indicated agreement with the formulation.
Committee Resolutions and Adoption of Minutes
Ms Stephens indicated that she was satisfied with the proposed resolutions. She clarified, however, that while declarations were not necessarily a Committee issue, concern arose when differing explanations were provided. She emphasised that the Committee’s focus should remain on what was recorded in the report, which needed to be corrected and clarified, rather than on procedural matters.
The Chairperson noted that it was the Committee’s responsibility to ask questions and seek clarification where needed. He stressed the importance of ensuring that all Members were fully aware of how the rules operated and what was officially reported.
The Chairperson then confirmed that no further proposals for Committee resolutions had been raised. He proceeded to address the adoption of minutes, noting that the minutes of the previous meeting had been circulated. He invited a Member to propose the adoption of those minutes.
The Chairperson noted that the minutes of the previous meeting had been proposed by Mr Booysen and seconded by Ms Stephens and confirmed that the minutes were agreed to by the Committee.
Joint Oversight Request from Gauteng Provincial Legislature
The Chairperson then raised a request from the Gauteng Provincial Legislature for a joint oversight engagement with the Committee to compare practices. The Chairperson explained that the proposed date for the interaction was Monday, 10 November 2025, and that it would include briefings from the Department of the Premier on Western Cape practices, as well as a visit to the Cape Access Centre and the Centre for Innovation at Foord Street, accompanied by representatives from the Premier’s Office. He emphasised that while he had no objection personally, the Committee needed to confirm whether the date and engagement were suitable.
Mr Booysen indicated that the Committee should consider accepting the request, noting that opportunities to engage with counterparts from other provinces at this level were infrequent and should be accommodated where possible.
Discussion on Proposed Joint Oversight Date
Mr Johnson indicated that while he did not object to interacting with other provincial legislatures, he preferred a midweek date rather than Monday. He noted that Mondays were the only days constituency political aides living outside the Metro could travel back or be in their constituencies, and therefore he was not in favour of holding the engagement on a Monday.
The Chairperson suggested that, based on Mr Johnson’s input, the Committee should determine whether the Gauteng Provincial Legislature (GPL) could be flexible with the proposed date. If they were willing, the Committee could then use one of its standing committee days for the engagement as part of its official programme. He added that the purpose of the visit aligned well with the Committee’s work.
The Procedural Officer provided background, explaining that the GPL had initially requested 10 November, but formal approval was required through the Speaker’s office. She noted that the Africa Tech Festival was scheduled from 11 to 13 November and suggested that the delegation could meet either before or after the festival. She mentioned the possibility of using 14 November as an alternative, subject to approval from the Programming Authority, and encouraged members to consider attending the festival independently.
Mr Johnson raised that 14 November conflicted with an existing Conduct Committee meeting in Parliament.
Mr Herron sought clarification on whether the proposal involved the GPL delegation visiting the Committee in Cape Town.
Mr Bryant, speaking as an alternate Member, recommended requesting clarification from the GPL regarding the intended outcomes of the session and briefing, noting that while a trip to Cape Town was appealing, the Committee should ensure that the engagement would yield substantive benefits.
Chairperson’s Closing Remarks
The Chairperson noted that, based on the discussion, the Committee needed to engage further with the Gauteng Provincial Legislature to explore an alternative date to 10 November. He summarised that the Committee members were generally not in favour of the 10 November date, but a joint oversight engagement could potentially be arranged after the Africa Tech Festival, during the week.
He emphasised the need to accommodate the preferences of members from Johannesburg who had constraints regarding a Monday date. The Chairperson confirmed that the Committee would pursue a different interaction opportunity during the week. He then asked the Procedural Officer if there were any outstanding matters, and upon her response, concluded the meeting.
Meeting was adjourned.
