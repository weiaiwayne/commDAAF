Meeting Summary
Video
In a virtual meeting, the Portfolio Committee received a briefing from the Department of International Relations and Cooperation (DIRCO) on the conflict situation in Sudan and the Democratic Republic of the Congo (DRC), and South Africa’s role in finding a lasting solution to both conflicts.
The conflict in Sudan was one of the deadliest on the continent. Thousands of civilian lives had been lost, millions displaced, and properties seized and destroyed. The conflict had far-reaching implications, both nationally and in the region. The state had effectively collapsed. The situation was compounded by external interests, with regional and foreign actors allegedly supporting either side of the conflict to varying degrees. United Nations (UN) agencies and others had warned that Sudan was facing a humanitarian disaster. UN human rights bodies had also warned of a possible genocide and war crimes having been committed. Although there were various attempts at mediation, none had thus far yielded any positive results.
DIRCO provided details of the unrest in Sudan since 1956, leading to armed hostilities in April last year. The global response to the catastrophic situation had been very slow, and the cost of international humanitarian aid had risen as the need for support grew in the region. The African Union made many efforts towards developing a plan for inclusive and lasting peace, such as holding summits and conferences aimed at resolving the conflict and beginning a transitional path to democratic governance. South Africa had consistently condemned the unconstitutional change of governments. From the outset, it had been opposed to the series of military takeovers in Sudan and had always responded by calling for a transfer of power from the military to civilian rule. It still insists that only a negotiated settlement would bring an amicable end to the ongoing crisis. South Africa would continue to support ongoing mediation efforts aimed at bringing an end to the conflict and accompanying Sudan to a peaceful and democratic order.
Members asked about the interaction between DIRCO and the Department of Defence, especially ensuring that South African troops were not dying on foreign battlefields. The Committee asked for an update on the investigation into the funding sources of the warring parties. They asked about discussions with the leadership of Rwanda, how deliberate South Africa was about ending the war, and what humanitarian aid had been explored. A Member commented that it was pointless to have leaders attempting to intervene in the conflict when these leaders themselves were not fervent promoters of democracy in their own regions. African problems needed African solutions.
DIRCO said the DRC conflict was based on ethnic-regional violence and extremism attributed to competition for the country’s resources, involving militias from the region and limited state capacity to control its entire national territory, compounded by external foreign interests. South Africa’s mediated peace in the DRC led to the first democratic elections in 2006. There had been a general election in 2023 to continue the democratic path, and the bilateral agreement between South Africa and the DRC was strong and cordial. So far, 38 bilateral agreements have been signed covering various fields such as security and energy.
South Africa’s national interest in the DRC had to be protected and promoted, and that would require continued engagement. The role played by foreign actors in the eastern DRC had escalated the conflict situation. Punitive measures had to be imposed against governments, private companies, armed groups, and persons found to be involved. Military deployment on its own would not bring a lasting solution, and parallel diplomatic processes were required. Reference was made to the complicated Grand Inga Hydropower project, where South Africa engaged with the DRC to resolve the challenge of transmitting electricity to the Republic.
The Committee was worried that South Africa's efforts in the DRC were not achieving the desired results. Concerns were raised about how its national interests were being protected in the DRC. Questions were asked about the lack of humanitarian aid, and South Africa’s exit strategy. Members asked for details of the Republic's economic contributions and investments in the DRC, and whether South Africa was fighting for resources.

SA’s role in finding a lasting solution to the Sudan conflict
Ambassador Tebogo Seokolo, Acting Director-General: African Affairs, DIRCO, took the Committee through the presentation which consisted of an executive summary, the historical context, an overview of Sudan, the evolution of the conflict, mediation efforts, the International Criminal Court (ICC) and UNSC developments, South Africa’s position and role, and concluding remarks.
Executive summary
The conflict in Sudan marked its one-year anniversary in April 2024. This conflict was proving to be one of the deadliest on the continent. Thousands of civilian lives had been lost, millions displaced, and properties seized and destroyed. The conflict had far-reaching implications, both nationally and in the region. The state of Sudan had effectively collapsed. External interests were compounding the conflict, with regional and foreign actors allegedly supporting either side of the conflict to varying degrees. UN agencies and others had warned that Sudan was facing a humanitarian disaster. UN human rights bodies had also warned of possible genocide and war crimes having been committed. Although there were various mediation attempts, none had thus far yielded any positive results.
Historical context
Since independence in 1956, Sudan has suffered from numerous civil wars, including the independence of South Sudan in 2011 and the current war in Sudan. Overall, since 1952, the country has experienced 17 coups, with nine characterised as successful. The latest coup was that of former President Omar al-Bashir in April 2019.
The major political and socio-economic fault lines in Sudan had become the source of conflict and included the colonial legacy, ethnic and religious north-south divides, economic disparities and marginalisation, and desertification.
In June 1989, President al-Bashir overthrew a civilian government through a military coup. His almost 30 years of rule were marked by significant political repression, human rights abuses, and involvement in various conflicts. In February 2003, a ferocious conflict erupted in Darfur between the government-supported militia group, Janjaweed, and rebel groups representing the Christian/African population. Following the systematic murders perpetuated by Janjaweed, the ICC labelled the atrocities as genocide against Christian/African populations. It was also the first time the ICC issued a warrant against a sitting president.
In 2013, the Rapid Support Forces (RSF) was formed in Sudan, but it had been in existence for many years as it was formerly referred to as the Janjaweed. With former President al-Bashir’s blessing, the RSF had been given the status of a regular force and was formally employed as a border guard force, as a source of mercenaries for the Saudi coalition in the Yemeni war, as well as a hired security force to repress popular uprisings. The RSF also protected President al-Bashir from attempts on his life and coups.
In 2017, General Hemedti had taken over control of the lucrative Jebel Amer gold mines in North Darfur, and became one of Sudan’s wealthiest men. He took control of the mines from Musa Hilal, the former leader of Janjaweed, who was believed to have been behind the atrocities committed in Darfur since 2003. Mr Hilal was detained by President al-Bashir’s regime and released only in 2021. In 2019, General Hemedti started trading formally in gold.
Overview of Sudan
Ambassador Seokolo said Sudan was the third largest country in Africa. Its 853km coastline along the Red Sea provided access to one of the world's most important maritime routes and choke points. It lay at a strategic point for trade between Europe, the Middle East and Asia. Its location connected North Africa, Sub-Saharan Africa and the Middle East, and served as a land bridge facilitating trade and cultural exchanges between these regions.
The port of Sudan was a crucial gateway for landlocked African countries like Chad and South Sudan. Following the outbreak of the conflict, it was currently serving as a temporary seat of government. Sudan's control over a portion of the Nile gave it strategic leverage in regional water politics. Safe management of the Nile’s water was crucial for the stability of the region. Northern neighbour Egypt was 90% dependent on the river for its water supply, while Ethiopia to the east was looking to double the country’s electricity generation by constructing the Grand Renaissance Dam.
Sudan was well endowed with mineral and other resources, including oil with proven reserves of about 1.5 billion barrels of reserves. Before the conflict, it had produced 70 000 barrels a day. It also had rich deposits of gold, chromium, iron ore, manganese, copper, zinc and lead, uranium, gypsum, limestone, marble, and agricultural land.
Evolution of the conflict
In April 2019, former President al-Bashir’s 30-year repressive reign in Sudan was brought to an end following a revolution and subsequent coup. The Sovereignty Council of the Republic of Sudan (civilian-military partnership) was constituted as the government, with a civilian, Dr Abdalla Hamdok, appointed as Prime Minister under General al-Burhan and General Hemedti.
In 2021, General al-Burhan, supported by General Hemedti, orchestrated a coup against the government led by Dr Hamdok, and suspended the Constitution. In response, international institutions like the World Bank and the International Monetary Fund (IMF) suspended crucial debt relief to Sudan, while mass demonstrations in Khartoum demanded a return to civilian rule. Dr Hamdok was briefly reinstated as Prime Minister in November 2021 after conceding some powers, including the security sector, to General al-Burhan and General Hemedti. Following widespread protests in October 2022, Dr Hamdok was forced to resign, leaving the leadership of the country to the two generals. Following Dr Hamdok’s departure, major disagreements began to emerge as the parties began to negotiate the implementation of a transition plan. There were disagreements over the integration timeline, with General al-Burhan proposing two years and General Hemedti ten years.
On 15 April 2023, open-armed hostilities erupted between General al-Burhan and General Hemedti, with both accusing the other of firing first. To date, the RSF controlled about 50% of the national territory, or eight of the 18 states. Reports indicated that it controlled almost all of western Sudan and parts of central Sudan, including Khartoum. The Sudan armed forces generally controlled the north and east of the country, and parts of central Sudan. It appears that neither side could achieve a decisive victory any time soon.
The UN reports estimated that more than 16 500 people had been killed, 10 million were internally displaced, and two million had fled to neighbouring countries. Humanitarian access had been severely curtailed, making it extremely difficult to assist vulnerable communities. The UN Office for the Coordination of Humanitarian Affairs currently noted that about 25 million people, including 14 million children, were in dire need of humanitarian assistance. About 17.7 million people -- 37% of the population -- faced acute hunger, with three million children under five suffering from acute malnutrition. Internally displaced people in Sudan make up 13% of all internally displaced people globally. There were 20 million Sudanese children who were not attending school and were exposed to the risk of trafficking, sexual abuse, exploitation, family separation, abduction, and recruitment/use by armed groups. The global response to the catastrophic situation unfolding in Sudan had been very slow. The cost of international humanitarian aid had risen as the need for support grew in the region.
The conflict may create a fertile ground for extremist groups to operate, threatening regional security and potentially leading to an increase in terrorism or insurgency.
Mediation efforts
Ambassador Seokolo said there had been many attempts at mediation, but with no discernible outcome.
The AU efforts had included:
The AU High-Level Panel on Sudan aimed to end the fighting and put in place a process towards a lasting and inclusive peace.
In May, the AU Peace and Security Council (AUPSC) decided to appoint a committee of five led by Uganda’s President Yoweri Museveni. On 21 June, the AUPSC met in Addis Ababa to discuss Sudan's situation and called for an AU extraordinary summit to be held in Kampala to address the escalating war in Sudan.
Subsequently, from 10-11 July, the AU High-Level Mechanism on Sudan convened a meeting in Addis Ababa of Sudanese armed forces, aimed at laying the groundwork for an extraordinary summit and a comprehensive conference aimed at resolving the conflict and beginning a transitional path to democratic governance.
In addition, the council had mandated the AUPSC sub-committee on sanctions to identify all external actors supporting the warring factions militarily and financially, and to propose containment measures. The report was expected by September 2024.

ICC and UNSC developments
In July 2023, the ICC Prosecutor, Karim Khan, announced an investigation into allegations of war crimes and crimes against humanity in Darfur against the backdrop of the ongoing war. Subsequently, he had announced in June that his office was also investigating allegations of war crimes and crimes against humanity in el-Fasher, and was collecting information and evidence related to the matter, inviting the international community to support the investigation by providing any evidence in their possession. In March, the UNSC extended the mandate of the Sudan Sanctions Committee's Panel of Experts, tasking them to investigate the funding sources of the warring parties.
South Africa’s position and role
Ambassador Seokolo said South Africa had consistently condemned the unconstitutional change of governments
in accordance with the policy positions of the regional economic communities and the AUPSC. This was in line with the AU Constitutive Act; the protocol relating to the establishment of the Peace and Security Council of the African Union; the African Charter on Democracy, Elections and Governance; and the declaration on the framework for an Organisation of African Unity (OAU) response to unconstitutional changes of government, adopted by the 36th Ordinary Session of the OAU Assembly of Heads of State and Government, held in Lomé, Togo, in July 2000 (the Lomé Declaration).
From the outset, South Africa had been opposed to the series of military takeovers in Sudan and had always responded by calling for the transfer of power from the military to civilian rule. South Africa has always been a proponent of effective and consistent implementation of the AU roadmap for the crisis in Sudan. It was equally concerned by the ongoing bloodshed in Sudan and still insisted that only a negotiated settlement would bring an amicable end to the ongoing crisis. To date, President Ramaphosa has met General Hemedti, and twice with the Vice President of Sudan, Mr Malik Agar Nganyoufa. Plans were advanced for President Ramaphosa to engage with General al-Burhan.
South Africa would continue to support ongoing mediation efforts aimed at bringing an end to the conflict and accompanying Sudan to a peaceful and democratic order. It would also engage with all relevant parties involved in the conflict to highlight the need for an immediate end to the conflict and halt the humanitarian crisis facing Sudan. South Africa supports the investigations announced by the ICC prosecutor into allegations of war crimes and crimes against humanity in Darfur and al-Fasher.

There was a strong relationship economically, culturally and socially, so the discussion with Rwanda should be intensified.
DIRCO's response
Minister Lamola requested Ambassador Seokolo to respond to some of the questions, followed by Mr Dangor. He would respond to questions thereafter.
Ambassador Seokolo said that in March, the UNSC extended the mandate of the Sudan Sanctions Committee Panel of Experts and requested that they investigate the funding sources of the warring parties. The panel of experts had not yet released the report as to who those external actors were. The AUPSC, in its recent meeting, had mandated its sub-committee on sanctions to also identify those external actors that were supporting the warring parties both militarily and financially, and to propose containment measures. This report from the AU sub-committee was expected to be released in September. There would be a clearer picture as to who those external parties were when the reports were released by those duly mandated committees.
In the meeting between President Ramaphosa and General Hemedti, the general call had been to end the conflict. This message had been sent to President al-Burhan, his vice-chair and General Hemedti. It also included the concern about the loss of lives and the humanitarian crisis resulting in the displacement of internally displaced people. They had to ensure that an inclusive dialogue involving all the parties was initiated. This information was also released to the press. The same discussion had taken place with General Hemedti when he came to South Africa. Many missions had become impossible because it was impossible to operate in Khartoum. Most of the missions had left and been temporarily transferred to other missions. The head of the mission had been temporarily relocated to Ethiopia to conduct its licence with all the parties from Sudan, and to provide the necessary support from the mission in Addis Ababa. The lines were therefore open to engage with all of them, including former Prime Minister Dr Hamdok, who had visited South Africa and met with President Ramaphosa on several occasions regarding the civilian side.
Ambassador Seokolo said South Africa believed that through the AU and the implementation of programmes related to democracy and peace, the flagship programme of silencing the guns and strengthening the AU peace and security architecture, it could significantly contribute to creating conditions of peace on the continent. This required close collaboration between the AUPSC and the regional economic communities, which was SADC in this case. There was close collaboration between the SADC, the AUPSC and the UNSC. In fact, it was South Africa who had championed a much closer relationship between the AUPSC and the UNSC. DIRCO had also coordinated with all other relevant departments, such as the Department of Defence. When it came to implementing South Africa’s public policy, DIRCO took the lead but recognised that it had to coordinate with other departments with their own specific mandates and responsibilities.
Mr Dangor said there had been several questions about the history and the root causes. At some point, there had to be a focus thematically on the interplay between justice, peace and a just resolution to any conflict. The lesson from Sudan over the last few decades was that if the root causes and the issue of impunity were not dealt with, there would be a situation called ‘play, rewind, repeat,’ with the same actors involved in the atrocities being involved in peace talks and becoming part of some sort of interim government, and the conflict would then begin again. If the root causes and impunity issues were not dealt with, it led to a cycle of violence, which led forever to mini-wars on the continent. DIRCO was quite cognisant of this, and that was why the presentation was so detailed in the history and some key points. These were the key issues for discussion, and needed to be looked at. Dialogue and peace were important, but so were the issues of justice and impunity. This could not be ignored any longer.
The meeting between President Ramaphosa and General Hemedti had been a request for South Africa to play a role and engage in peace talks. South Africa had participated in the talks on the Saudi initiative, but there had been concerns about this initiative without actors such as South Africa playing a role to be successful. This was why these meetings were taking place, and the meeting with General Hemedti was facilitated by civil society engagements in South Africa. He said that South Africa would play a role, but the role was cognisant of building peace and justice, but not ignoring the issues linked to justice and impunity. This Committee would deal with a lot of sensitive issues. On a very public and open platform like this, some of the conflict issues could not be shared. He suggested that some briefings take place in person.
The Chairperson noted the suggestion.
Minister Lamola thanked the Committee for the input. Ambassador Seokolo already covered a number of issues on the engagement of various role players, and the issues of the DRC that would be dealt with in detail after the presentation. There have been continuous engagements with all the countries affected by the situation in the DRC, including Rwanda, Uganda, and the DRC itself. There was a Luanda process which President Museveni, had led and all the efforts and engagements were in line with Sudan and within its auspices. There were also the AU programmes, such as the "Silencing of the guns" that South Africa had undertaken, and was part of the member states involved in peace initiatives and peacekeeping missions on the continent. South Africa was also guided by the UN.
There were a number of outstanding reports regarding who the sources of the funds were in Sudan. These reports would play an important role in shaping the way forward. All things had been done in line with international and humanitarian law, and were not informed by the assumptions of ‘friendships.’ South Africa had been guided by the law, the organs of the AU, the SADC, and the UN resolutions regarding this conflict. South Africa had been deliberate about ending the war.
When President Ramaphosa was the chairperson of the AU, the main focus had been on silencing the guns, and that had a number of focus and component points. In terms of silencing the guns, there was economic emancipation in various parts of the continent that focused on youth and women's employment. This has now led to the flagship programme of the AU. There was also the African Continental Free Trade Agreement, which would go a long way in beneficiating the minerals on the African continent and ensuring there was intra-African trade amongst the role players. This would ignite the industrial policies of various individual countries on the African continent, because it had trade products that it could produce. It was a revolution that, with more emphasis, collaboration and coordination, would build an economy in the African continent that could lead to stability. For economic growth and integration, peace and stability were needed. This was a balancing act that had to be navigated. If the majority of the people, particularly the youth and women in the African continent participated in the mainstream of the economies of their own countries, there would no longer be a number of available people to be involved in conflict. This was a task that had to be focused on, to enable the continent to see peace. This was the role that South Africa would continue to play within the auspices of the AU.
There were international foreign missions, but DIRCO had taken the lead and accountability for the methods. There had been budget cuts across many departments, including DIRCO. The Department of Defence would be in a better position to respond to the questions that had been asked in this meeting. DIRCO had been working with National Treasury to find a workable solution to enable DIRCO to continue functioning.
South Africa’s role in finding a lasting solution to the conflict situation in the DRC
Ambassador Seokolo took the Committee through the presentation that covered the introduction/historical context, the profile of the DRC, bilateral relations, the security situation in the eastern DRC, mediation processes, the latest political situation, and the conclusion.
Introduction
He said the DRC was very important because of its geostrategic location in the heart of Africa.
It was endowed with vast natural and mineral resources for its development and regional and continental economic integration. However, since its independence in 1960, it has never experienced durable peace and stability, particularly in the eastern part.
The South African and DRC diplomatic relations were formalised in 1992 and managed by the Bi-National Commission (BNC). South Africa provided conflict resolution in 1997/98 (President Mandela’s SAS Outeniqua) and the Inter-Congolese Dialogue in Sun City in 2002 (President Mbeki), as well as electoral capacity (2006, 2011). South Africa’s commercial footprint includes mining, services, infrastructure, and real estate. The current military deployment in the DRC needed to be supported through a diplomatic track.
Profile of the DRC
The DRC was the second largest country in Africa, with a population of over 105 million. The Congo River had the largest hydropower potential in the world, with a potential of some 100 000 MW. It had a large share of the world’s green economy minerals, like coltan, cobalt, tin and copper.
Historical context
The DRC gained its independence in May 1960 when the nationalist movement, the Mouvement National Congolais led by Patrice Lumumba, won the elections. In the same year, a secessionist movement was launched in the eastern Katanga province, supported by external powers, to carve off the rich copper belt from the Congo state. Conflict resulting from secession escalated until Lumumba was assassinated. In 1965, President Sese Seko seized power and ruled Congo/Zaire until he was removed in 1997 by President Kabila. In April 1994, Rwanda's Hutu extremist government orchestrated a genocide of some 800 000 Tutsi and moderate Hutu. In January 2001, President Kabila was assassinated, and his 29-year-old son, Joseph Kabila, took office.
South Africa’s mediated peace in the DRC had led to the first democratic elections.
The latest political situation
General elections were held in December 2023 to elect the President and Members of Parliament, as a way of reinforcing the DRC’s democratic path that had started in 2006 when the first democratic elections were held. President Felix Tshisekedi had won the presidential elections by 73% and was inaugurated in January 2024. President Ramaphosa attended the inauguration ceremony. A new Cabinet was appointed in May, and Ms Judith Siminwa Tuluka was appointed as the first female Prime Minister of the DRC. Mr Vital Kamerhe, former Chief of Staff of President Tshisekedi, was appointed as the Speaker of Parliament.
Political/diplomatic and bilateral relations
The bilateral relations between South Africa and the DRC were strong and cordial. The last session (12th) of the BNC was held in Kinshasa on 6 July 2023. South Africa was expected to host the next session of the BNC. The two countries had signed 38 bilateral agreements and memoranda of understanding (MoAs) covering various fields, including security, energy, infrastructure development, capacity and institutional building, trade, agriculture, health and transport.
Economic
South Africa was the DRC’s third-largest export destination and 69th-largest source of imports from the rest of the world. In 2023, South Africa’s imports from the DRC consisted of iron products and steel. South Africa has invested in several sectors in the DRC, including financial services, mining, and the construction sector. Challenges experienced by some South African companies were a lack of clarity on the sub-contracting rules, an unreliable tax regime, delays or non-repayment of value-added tax (VAT), traffic congestion at border posts, non-repatriation of profits and non-tariff barriers. South Africa has been using different bilateral instruments such as investment and trade initiatives and technical missions, to address these challenges. In November 2011, South Africa and the DRC signed a memorandum of understanding on the development of the Grand Inga Hydropower Project.

Security situation in the eastern DRC
SA’s involvements in the eastern DRC was informed by its foreign policy objective to contribute to peace and stability in the region, and its commitment to the SADC- Regional Indicative Strategic Development Plan (RISDP) Foundation for Peace and Security, the AU’s flagship priority to "Silence the Guns," and the implementation of Aspiration 4 of Agenda 2063 (a peaceful and secure Africa). The eastern DRC had been faced with unending conflict since independence in June 1960.
The conflict was based on ethnic-regional violence and extremism attributed to competition for the country’s resources involving militias from the region, and limited state capacity to control its entire national territory, compounded by external foreign interests. The eastern DRC continued to be the base for about 120 armed groups, including the M23, which was in North Kivu. There was a clear correlation between the location of mineral resources and the presence of armed groups.
The reported involvement of Rwanda in support of the M23, as per the UN Group of Experts report, had strained the relations between Rwanda and DRC. Burundi had deployed its soldiers on a bilateral arrangement to also pursue the rebel group (Red Tabara) that had vowed to unseat the government of President Ndayishimiye, now hiding in the DRC territories. Uganda had also deployed troops on a bilateral arrangement in DRC to pursue the Allied Defence Forces that had been attacking Uganda and killing civilians.

Mediation processes
The Nairobi Process was launched on 8 April 2022, led by Kenya’s former President Uhuru Kenyatta under the auspices of the East African Community. The Nairobi Process had focused on intra-state talks between the Congolese government and several rebel groups aiming to end hostilities within the DRC. The Luanda Process, led by Angola’s President João Lourenço on behalf of the AU, aimed to address the political dimensions between the DRC and Rwanda. Angolan-led mediation efforts had secured an agreement to meet between President Tshisekedi and his Rwandan counterpart, President Paul Kagame, on the condition that Rwanda withdrew its troops from eastern Congo and that M23 fighters were resettled into government-approved military garrisons. The Quadripartite Summit of the Eastern African Community (QSEAC), the International Conference on the Great Lakes Region (ICGLR), the Economic Community of Central African States and the SADC took place on 27 June 2023 in Luanda, Angola. The QSEAC had endorsed the need for a coordinated approach to all political and diplomatic initiatives and efforts in pursuit of peace and security in the eastern DRC, under the auspices of the AU. The Peace and Security Cooperation Framework Agreement for the DRC and the region was signed on 24 February 2014 by 11 African countries, including South Africa, the UN, the SADC and ICGLR. It focused on, among others, the neutralisation of negative forces, repatriation, resettlement, and reintegration of disarmed combatants, as well as a review of the dialogue and political processes. The low political will and trust, the persistent conflict, accusations and counter-accusations against and amongst the member states had polarised the parties, and were hampering the implementation of the objectives of the framework.
In August 2023, President Ramaphosa appointed former Minister Jeff Radebe as his Special Envoy to the Great Lakes Region. South Africa has a history of positively contributing to the pursuit of peace and security in the Great Lakes Region and the DRC. The appointment of the Special Envoy, although expanded to cover the whole region, should be understood from the context of South Africa’s immediate interest in bringing about peace in the DRC, thereby contributing to a stable and safer Great Lakes Region. The role of the Special Envoy was critical in ensuring that the President of South Africa received real-time information regarding developments in the Great Lakes Region, and was sufficiently advised on the next course of action to pursue durable peace.
Conclusion
South Africa’s national interests in the DRC must be protected and promoted, and that would require continued engagement. The role played by foreign actors in the eastern DRC had escalated the conflict situation. Punitive measures had to be imposed against governments, private companies, armed groups, and persons found to be involved. Military deployment would not bring lasting solutions, other than to pacify the situation. Initiated diplomatic processes must be supported. South Africa must share its expertise and contribute towards capacity building of state institutions.

Discussion
Mr Montwedi said that he did not agree with the suggestion in the concluding remarks that military deployment would not bring last solutions to the problems of Africa. This started in 1960 in the DRC, and it was more than 60 years later. A statement had been made that there had been engagements between the DRC and the President of Rwanda to approach all groups for a ceasefire, and it had not assisted. The Peace and Security Cooperation Framework agreement was signed by 11 African countries in 2013. This had been aimed at assisting the situation in the DRC. This had not helped, so that was why he did not agree with the view that military deployment should be intensified. It was like fighting fire with fire. He agreed that South Africa’s national interest should be protected in the DRC. How was it going to protect South Africa’s national interest in the DRC if it was not going to fight? There must be a solution to wiping out some of these things.
He said the Minister of the Department of Trade, Industry and Competition (DTIC) was going to be humiliated when he tried to engage with the UN on the decision it had taken on reviewing the African Growth and Opportunity Act (AGOA) in relation to South Africa. He said that agriculture was going to be seriously impacted. There had been a number of agricultural opportunities. There had been deliberate efforts to bring peace and security to the DRC, and South Africa should be able to assist by exploring other markets.
In 2011, there was the Grand Inga Hydropower project -- what was the status of this project? There were 11 countries doing business in the DRC, and 38 bilateral agreements had been signed. Why were there not a number of South African players in that space?
The conflict in Sudan was only a year old, and a report on the involvement of external actors would be available only in September. The conflict in DRC has been going on for much longer. Was there a report on the foreign players in that space, and could it be shared with the Committee? Why had the AU not led by producing its own independent report?
He said a statement had been made about the smuggling of minerals. A report last year indicated that there were a number of landing strips where people came to collect minerals and then just left. Why were these landing strips not being closed down so that no one would be able to leave that country with the minerals? What was being done about dealing with the actions of Rwanda?
Mr Douglas asked the Minister to clarify South Africa’s strategic objectives and its involvement in the DRC that had been outlined in the DIRCO's 2024 report. How did these objectives serve the national interest? He specifically referred to diamonds and mineral conflicts. How was South Africa participating? Were security services provided for South African companies there, or were they only bringing about peace and security for the people of the DRC? Was South Africa fighting for resources? How was South Africa coordinating with the AU and the UN peacekeeping missions? What was expected in terms of increased financial spending? There was a major concern about the human rights of people. There was a lack of humanitarian aid. Humanitarian aid was needed because people were fleeing those conflict zones and coming to South Africa. It was creating a huge influx of people. What measures were in place to ensure the protection of rights and the safety of civilians in the DRC? How was South Africa contributing to these efforts?
What were the details of the economic contributions and investments that were made by South Africa in the DRC? He specifically referred to the infrastructure development projects and the anticipated impact on the local economy. South African companies were there, and were allowed to provide services and goods. However, it was usually just large white-owned monopoly capital companies from South Africa that were exporting, with their colonial mindset. What was South Africa’s exit strategy for its involvement in the DRC? Under what conditions would a mission be considered successful and completed? On what level would South Africa exit? One of the main challenges facing South African forces on the ground was poor equipment and bad maintenance. There were many problems with the payment of soldiers and problems with their families back home. Concerns had been raised about the current strategy and international relations. A greater amount of oversight and accountability was needed for this Committee. It needed to have oversight of the actual documentation around what was happening on the ground in the DRC.
Ms Kumbaca commented on the regional and foreign actors who had intensified the conflict situation. South Africa had to be intentional in its efforts to identify these parties through the AU and the UN. Punitive measures should be imposed, despite relations with any of those bodies that were part and parcel of the conflict situations. These actions undermined the efforts to ensure peace, making them a futile exercise.
DIRCO's response
Minister Lamola thanked the Members for the questions. He said that Ambassador Seokolo and Mr Dangor would answer some questions, and thereafter he would respond.
Ambassador Seokolo said that reference had been made to the issue of a military solution versus peace. The submission was that the two tracks had to run parallel. In that context, many initiatives and structures had been established around the Great Lakes region that were diplomatic in nature. The military intervention sought to create space for diplomacy. He agreed that all efforts did not initially yield results. However, this time around, there was a push for both the diplomatic and military paths to go together and be complementary. 38 bilateral agreements were signed. Many companies in South Africa were involved in the DRC. There also had to be common standards to facilitate trade and cooperation in specific areas. It did not directly relate to business, but it created an enabling environment for the two countries to exchange knowledge and expertise that would result in increased trade and investment.
The Grand Inga Hydropower project was a complicated project, and South Africa was engaging with the DRC to bring into effect the planning issues around transmission. There were issues as to how to transmit electricity from the DRC to South Africa, having to go through regions that were in a conflict situation. The discussions continued, and the Department of Mineral Resources and Energy led the negotiations. The latest updated report on their engagements would be requested. It was an expensive project, and would require a lot of external funding from South African development finance institutions, the Development Bank of South Africa (DBSA) and other institutions outside the African continent. However, it was preferred that African financial institutions should be the ones leading in providing the finance for such an infrastructure project.
South Africa’s approach to the DRC was informed by the nomadic frameworks at the AU and UN levels to arrive at a peaceful, prosperous and developed African continent. It was not motivated purely by getting resources from the DRC. This was not what drove South Africa. What drove it was an Africa that was united and peaceful to unleash its development. The efforts in the DRC were coordinated to a regional structure, which was why the presence of the soldiers was in the eastern part of the DRC. It was not a bilateral initiative -- the project involved the deployment of SADC, and the FIB by the UN. It was due to the United Nations that South Africa was not there alone. It was purely guided by the rules of engagement, policies and procedures set out by the organisations.
The eastern DRC, just like Sudan, was experiencing major humanitarian issues. The expert report by the UN on the international sponsors of the conflict in the DRC was detailed and available. There were important engagements that were going on that were not about exiting, but about finding a sustainable solution to the security challenges in the country. The conflict had been recorded, and this time around there was a desire and a need to ensure that whatever solution was found, that this did not happen again. The Department of Defence would be in a better position to comment on the operational challenges and the soldiers in the context of the SADC mission.
Mr Dangor said that he wanted to amplify a bit on the response to the question posed by Mr Douglas with an anecdote. In 2019, South Africa’s involvement was to ensure that there was no more violence after the elections. There had been engagements with many stakeholders around the world. The key question that was repeated was ‘if it was engaging in building peace, what was your stake?’ The human rights of all the people in the DRC was a major concern. He said that the reference to a ‘stake’ emanated from people with a specific stake in extracting resources.
South Africa had many national interests, including economic and security interests, and the values that emerged from the Constitution. The engagement in the DRC was not purely for economic purposes. It was about building peace and stability. What arises out of the stability that results in peace, was that it lays the basis for sustained economic cooperation that was mutually beneficial for South Africa and the people of the DRC, as well as other African countries such as those in the African Continental Free Trade Agreement. The DRC was one of the largest countries on the continent and had the largest resource basis. There was an old saying that ‘if the DRC situation was not resolved, the entire African content would not be resolved’.
Minister Lamola said that he had found the questions very constructive. The national interest document and the Constitution informed South Africa's approach, with a human rights outlook, to find long-lasting peace in the DRC and many other conflict zones. There were also bilateral economic relations between the two countries. There was always a challenge of which one came first. For economic relations to thrive, there had to be peace and stability. However, for peace and stability to thrive, there had to be good economic opportunities which created an enabling environment for the locals and for the economy of that country. This required a balancing approach, and discussions were underway through the peacekeeping missions and the bilateral relations.
It was not only private companies that were involved in the DRC. Some state-owned companies were involved in huge infrastructure projects aimed at facilitating the public good. There were other projects as well, such as road infrastructure, health infrastructure and the involvement of developmental finance institutions like the DBSA. It was a developmental approach, and the development had to be a deliberate part of the bilateral agreements. Private companies had a role to play in bilateral trade and through the various available platforms. Everyone could see the opportunities, and were welcome to participate. The number of companies was based on the commercial interests they had identified.
The missions would be considered successfully completed only when there was peace and stability in the DRC. Against this background, South Africa would continue to pursue political engagements and solutions. As Ambassador Seokolo had said, the two programmes were running parallel. The military interventions helped with stability, peacekeeping, and the protection of civilians. The political intervention brought the whole divided factions to a dialogue to find long-lasting solutions. There were no military interventions that could provide long-lasting peace -- they must always be accompanied by engagement and dialogue, and that line was always open, hence the participation in the Luanda process. This was to ensure that political dialogue leads to long-lasting peace under the auspices of the AU.
The Grand Inga Hydropower project was important for national interest, bilateral trade, and the development of the DRC. There was the production of electricity, with issues of transmission and how to finance it. It was a huge project. International financial institutions should also assist. The Department of Mineral Resources and Energy should be encouraged, because it was leading the negotiations to ensure that this would become a success, and that the agreement would become a live reality for both the people of the DRC and South Africa.
The operationalisation of the bilateral agreements would further enhance the operationalisation of the African Continental Free Trade Agreement. This was an important flagship programme for long-lasting trade and bilateral relations between African states on the continent. The DRC was home to a lot of natural resources, and it feeds into the discussion of the different departments when engaging on strategic minerals. This would help position it strategically for a beneficial position of industrialisation for local manufacturing on the African continent. There were developments like electric vehicles and artificial intelligence. The African continent needed to define a clear agenda in terms of its minerals. The agenda should include what to do with them, the benefits and where they fit into the flagship programme of the African Continental Free Trade Agreement. There were some big trade areas in the world, and they should be utilised to ignite the economies of countries.
Chairperson's closing remarks
The Chairperson thanked the Minister and the team for coming to the Committee to account on behalf of DIRCO as far as the situation in some parts of the African continent was concerned. He hoped that in future there would be a discussion in the context of South Africa's relationship with other countries, particularly those in the West. There had been a request that the two reports by the AU and UN on the external actors be shared. He agreed with this -- it would be useful for the Committee to receive those reports.
He thanked Ambassador Seokolo for the comprehensive briefing, and the Members for the manner in which they had engaged with the presentation. A lot of effort, investment, labour and intellect had gone into these presentations. One thing that the Committee would always emphasise was investment in both the African continent and internationally, based on trade agreements. There had to be aggregation in so far as those investments were helping to align with the preamble of the Constitution, which says that one had to deal with the injustice of the past. The Committee was more interested in disaggregating those investments as far as the economic injustices of the past were concerned. One could not give a quantification of how those trade agreements were helping to build a non-sexist, non-racial, and prosperous society, which were the three things the Committee was interested in. If the aggregation was done, it would help to indicate to the people of South Africa how the trade agreements were having a practical effect on their lives. This included their impact on unemployment, inequality, and poverty. This had not been achieved in the last administration.
The meeting was adjourned.
