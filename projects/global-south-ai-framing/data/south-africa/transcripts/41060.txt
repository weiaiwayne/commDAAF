Meeting Summary
Video
2025/26 Annual Performance Plans
The Committee met with the Department of Home Affairs (DHA), Independent Electoral Commission (IEC), and Government Printing Works (GPW) to discuss revised strategic and performance plans, and ICT-related challenges.
The Independent Electoral Commission (IEC) presented its 2025–2030 Strategic Plan and 2025/26 Annual Performance Plan (APP). It spoke to strengthening election integrity, addressing low voter turnout, and tackling misinformation and political interference. The IEC acknowledged challenges such as system downtime, registration app issues, and reduced outreach budgets.
Members raised concerns about voter education and youth engagement, cybersecurity, misinformation (including AI use), and result transparency, funding irregularities and the IEC’s capacity to investigate violations and accessibility for remote voters and the need for inclusive digital voting systems. The IEC was asked to submit a report on low voter registration and turnout strategies ahead of the next meeting on 11 July.
Government Printing Works (GPW) presented its 2025–2030 Strategic Plan and 2025/26 Annual Performance Plan (APP). It outlined its digital transformation strategy, focusing on becoming a digital trust centre. It emphasised the need to redefine its role in a digital era and improve customer experience and operational resilience.
The Department of Home Affairs (DHA detailed serious issues with the State Information Technology Agency (SITA), citing frequent system outages, infrastructure delays, and cybersecurity gaps and misalignment between SITA’s capabilities and DHA’s digital transformation goals. It noted the termination of an underperforming software service provider and delays in kiosk deployment. It called for more autonomy in selecting service providers to avoid inefficiencies and delays.
GPW and DHA will return for a dedicated session to allow for detailed deliberation on their presentations. Members were encouraged to submit written questions to support deeper engagement.
Meeting report
Chairperson's opening remarks
The Chairperson opened the meeting by welcoming the Committee and the delegations of the Department of Home Affairs (DHA), the Independent Electoral Commission (IEC) and the Government Printing Works (GPW).
He started by reflecting on recent developments, including a symposium on the Party Political Funding Act and upcoming engagements with the IEC and GPW. He expressed appreciation for the efforts of the IEC leadership and team in hosting stakeholders, and preparing reports on both provincial and national elections. He highlighted the need for continued discussions on policy documents, particularly around electronic voting. He emphasised the importance of addressing information communication technology (ICT) challenges and system downtimes at the DHA. The Committee anticipated further briefings and updates from various parties to ensure preparedness for upcoming elections and effective legislative review.
IEC's 2025-2030 strategic plan and 2025/26 annual performance plan
Mr Sy Mamabolo, Chief Electoral Officer, IEC, said the Commission's long-term goals included strengthened institutional effectiveness, free and fair elections, well-informed public and stakeholders, and increased transparency within the system
Potential plans and strategies had been developed to counter the risks that follow elections, such a cyberattacks, inadequate funding, political violence and declining voter population. These were all being handled by enhanced monitoring and security measures, while seeking additional resources.
The Electoral Commission’s strategic programmes aimed to strengthen institutional effectiveness, deliver free and fair elections, and enhance transparency in party funding. These objectives would be achieved through comprehensive management, ongoing civic and voter education, stakeholder engagement, transparent communication, and the continuous fostering of environments supportive of democratic participation. The Commission also ensured compliance with party funding legislation and built cooperative relationships with political entities via sustainable systems and processes.

Responses
Mr Mamabolo acknowledged that while the IEC had flaws, it was still able to fulfil its constitutional and legislative responsibilities effectively. The challenges would be addressed in detail during the upcoming presentation of the 2024 elections report. It was essential to understand that electoral processes were shaped by the broader context in which they occurred. For example, the last election cycle was marked by unprecedented litigation, including late-stage legal challenges to legislative amendments passed by Parliament, some of which had emerged as late as December 2023. These developments, including court rulings that impacted requirements such as candidate signatures, had forced the IEC to make urgent and significant adjustments to its systems at a critical time. Furthermore, these systems were targeted by Distributed Denial of Service (DDoS) attacks between 30 May and 2 June, which attempted to block public access to the platforms. Fortunately, these threats were effectively mitigated.
With the Chairperson’s permission, he invited colleagues to respond to the issues raised.
Legislative changes
Mr Masego Sheburi, Deputy Chief Electoral Officer, IEC, addressed the issue regarding whether any legislative changes were expected before the 2026 local government elections. Initially, the IEC had aimed to avoid amendments to ensure stability, but after reviewing recent elections and challenges with by-elections, it had become clear that some changes were necessary to support free and fair elections. As requested by the Portfolio Committee, the IEC would submit proposed amendments alongside the 2024 election report. A key focus would be Section 15 of the Electoral Act, which deals with objections to the voters’ roll, crucial for protecting citizens' political rights during elections.
Electronic voting
Mr Sheburi said they recognised that technology was not neutral, and any system proposed must promote broad and equal access to voting. It should not deepen existing divides, whether between rich and poor, urban and rural, or literate and illiterate citizens. To ensure this, the outreach programme would actively engage communities across all social contexts and gather input from areas alike to ensure that the technology chosen was appropriate, inclusive, and responsive to the diverse needs of the South African population.
The role of SAPS
While presiding officers manage order within the voting station, that law enforcement remained the responsibility of SAPS, even on election day. SAPS officers inside a station usually acted at the request of the presiding officer, but they were also empowered to act independently if a crime occurred within the station’s boundaries, though the presiding officer’s authority was limited to the immediate area around the voting station. They may intervene if party activities obstruct voter access, such as setting up gazebos or tents, but they have no jurisdiction over criminal activity occurring outside the voting station perimeter. Further engagement with SAPS would be continued in order to clarify and reinforce roles and responsibilities for future elections.
Transparency
He addressed concerns regarding transparency in the electoral process, particularly around results collation, as he disputed that the process was secretive. The process started with vote counting at the station, in the presence of party agents who may sign and photograph the result slips. Duplicates were then displayed publicly at the station, while another copy was securely transported to a local site for capture. Once in the system, parties could access these slips through an application programming interface (API) or designated computers, compare them with their own records, and raise any discrepancies using an issue tracker. Differences may occur due to human error at the station, but all versions of a result slip, original and corrected, were kept in the system for full traceability.
The other issue was transparency involving the recruitment of electoral staff. Mr Sheburi said the Commission was working with the national Party Liaison Committee, developing strict recruitment criteria to ensure impartiality and prevent political interference. Before elections or registration weekends, the names of key voting station staff were shared with local party liaison committees. This was where valid objections were raised and supported -- they remove the individuals in question and blacklist their identity document (ID) numbers to prevent future involvement in electoral administration.
Voter registration
Mr Sheburi said the reduction in the registration target was due to a net decline in the voters’ roll during non-election years. On average, 30 000 to 40 000 voters were lost monthly due to mortality, resulting in a naturally shrinking poll without major registration events. Therefore, the Commission aimed to reverse the decline before growing the election roll, hence disregarding this trend, while setting unrealistic targets was poor planning.
Another key issue was declining voter motivation, especially among the youth. Research showed that most young people understand how to register and face no administrative barriers. However, they did not see the need to register since they did not plan to vote, because there was a loss of confidence in political institutions, including parties and government. The response involved adopting a two-pronged approach -- first, to engage directly with citizens to understand their concerns and renew trust in the democratic process, and second, to support these efforts through targeted outreach and registration drives.
Election representative replacement
Mr Sheburi explained the importance of noting that Schedule 1A of the Electoral Act remained unchanged, and the law governing replacements had not been amended. Moreover, they had no legal role in the replacement process, beyond handing over the list of elected representatives to the Chief Justice. However, the Commission had initiated a working session with Parliament and the provincial legislatures to review and possibly refine the administrative aspects of this process.
Special votes continued to grow, with around 13% of votes in the last election cast this way. While the Commission had successfully managed the overall process, there were certain operational challenges. Special vote lists were not printed in alphabetical order, making voter verification difficult at stations. These matters would be addressed while engaging with parties honestly. Though special votes help voters needing assistance, some parties had abused the system by applying on behalf of individuals who had never requested it. Measures were being considered to tighten controls, balancing electoral integrity with access for vulnerable voters.
Voter turnout
Mr Mawethu Mosery, Deputy Chief Electoral Officer: Outreach and Communications, IEC, said that research on voter turnout consisted of two main long-term studies. The first examined voter participation by tracking voters’ intentions to vote or abstain, and the reasons behind withdrawal. Over 20 years, this research has shown a steady decline in turnout for both national and local elections. Key reasons for this decline included dissatisfaction with government performance, socio-economic challenges affecting citizens’ lifestyles, and decreasing trust in state institutions. Despite many of these issues being beyond the Commission’s direct control, promotion for voter participation continued.
A focused study from May 2023 had explored why young voters planned not to participate in the 2024 elections. It revealed two main concerns -- socio-economic dissatisfaction, and uncertainty about the importance of voting and good citizenship. The voter education campaign for 2024 had addressed these issues. Thus, improving voter turnout involved multiple factors meaning it required a joint effort from government bodies, civil society and citizens, to encourage and promote voter participation.
Voter education
Regarding voter education aimed at the youth, there was ongoing work with basic education, but there was also a recommendation to extend efforts to higher education. He confirmed that there was engagement with higher education institutions, including technical and vocational education and training (TVET) colleges and universities, through active campus programs which were done at the start of each year. These programs focus on registering new students as voters based on their campus residence. Starting in the second half of this year, “champions” who would promote voter registration and participation on campuses through monthly programs would be introduced. Various youth-focused materials would be produced, including a cartoon booklet titled
30 Years of Democracy
, which was currently being reviewed for distribution in high schools and tertiary institutions.
Mr Mosery said the voter education strategy had shifted significantly after 2019 and during the COVID-19 pandemic, moving towards digital platforms. Research showed young people are primarily engaged on digital devices, hence the use of these platforms to deliver educational content. This shift explained the increased budget allocation, as digital materials were designed for reuse over multiple election cycles rather than being election-specific. In addition, from August to October 2023, the IEC had focused on educating voters about changes for the 2024 election, such as legislative updates and new provisions like Section 24A, which required extra funding which was allocated accordingly. Therefore, while budget figures may appear to have declined, it was an actual reflection of the shift in approach and the evolving electoral environment.
Stakeholder engagement
Mr Mosery said there was close engagement with other electoral management bodies to exchange knowledge, provide technical support, and promote transparent and professional election practices. At the regional level, they participated in the Electoral Commission Forum of the Southern African Development Community (SADC), while internationally the IEC was an active members of the Association of World Election Bodies (AWEB), where they had previously served as world chairperson, providing leadership and guidance. There was also collaboration with the Institute for Democracy and Electoral Assistance (IDEA), which conducts extensive research on best election management practices. Beyond these partnerships, the IEC responds to requests from other countries for technical assistance. In addition, many electoral bodies visit South Africa to observe and learn from its election management processes, which strengthens the country's global stakeholder network and helps foster best practices across democracies.
Misinformation
Mr Mosery said there was a developed framework and active collaboration with tech companies through formal agreements. They also worked closely with civil society and other state entities to track and monitor false information. It was important for Committee Members to recognise that much misinformation originates from political contestants who undermine the credibility of the country’s sole election management body. This politicisation complicates efforts to manage disinformation effectively. Moreover, the largely unregulated environment allows for widespread sharing of misleading views about the electoral process.
Regarding election observers, he concluded by mentioning the IEC accrediting them to ensure they had full access to the electoral process, promoting transparency. This topic could be explored further in a future discussion.
International funding
Mr George Mahlangu, Chief Executive: Political Party Funding, IEC, said current law requires political parties and donors to declare all donations to the Commission, whether intentional or not, as it was the legal framework in place. In response to a related question about public disclosure, he said the IEC publishes declared international donations quarterly. There was also an annual political funding report submitted to Parliament, detailing all activities and compliance for the year under review.
Non-declaration consequences
The law included a schedule of offences that specified penalties for violations. If a political party breaches any provision, these penalties apply. In the 2021/22 financial year, over 480 political parties, both represented and unrepresented, were fined. These fines were imposed through the electoral court for parties that violate the law.
Rationale
Regarding the rationale behind the 80% figure, Mr Mahlangu said the CFO had emphasised that it referred to 80% of complaints that met the legal requirements. Posts or claims on social media did not qualify as formal complaints. A valid complaint must be formally submitted to the Commission and supported by prima facie evidence, as required by law. If someone believed a political party had violated funding rules, such as failing to declare a donation, they must present this evidence to the CEO. Only such formal submissions were counted, and the 80% referred specifically to those that met these criteria.
Unrepresented registered parties
As with international funding, the law placed the responsibility on political parties to report donations to the Commission. If they failed to do so, and no complaint was filed, the Commission might not be aware of the undeclared funding. To address this, Mr Mahlangu said there was an appointed panel of investigators. When a formal complaint was submitted to the CEO with sufficient evidence, one of the firms from this panel was assigned to investigate the matter on the IEC’s behalf.
Expenditure
Ms Dawn Mbatha, Chief Financial Officer (CFO), IEC, said that although 2022/23 was a non-election year, the R1.4 billion expenditure was necessary due to the ongoing operational and preparatory demands of the Commission. Preparing for a general election was an 18-month, project-based process, meaning much of the planning, procurement and system development takes place in the years leading up to an election. During that year, the Commission had conducted 120 by-elections and hosted over 50 000 civic and democracy education events, which required significant resources. Investments were also made in maintaining and enhancing ICT systems critical to election infrastructure. She said the Commission operates more than 250 offices nationwide, across national, provincial, and local levels, which carry fixed operational costs such as salaries, rent and utilities, which remain constant regardless of the electoral calendar. The timing of National Treasury’s approval of retained surpluses, between the end of July and September, also affects when procurement and planning activities could occur. Thus, the Commission remained highly active outside election periods, with financial outlays necessary to ensure readiness and continuity.
Addressing the question about what informs the expenditure in the subsequent years, she emphasised the 18-month election planning cycle that guides the operations. While 2022/23 saw R1.4 billion in spending, the following year included two national voter registration weekends, which were major logistical events requiring early and extensive preparation. The subsequent period had also led directly into the national and provincial elections, meaning the organisation began ramping up operationally and financially to ensure readiness.
She commented that it was important not to view the IEC's budget as a uniform annual expenditure but more on a project basis, aligned with key electoral milestones and events. Expenditure was influenced not only by planned activities, but also by the timing of National Treasury’s approval of retained surpluses, which determines when previously saved funds could be committed. Therefore, spending patterns reflect the electoral calendar and operational needs, rather than fixed annual targets.
Cost containment
Ms Mbatha indicated the importance of noting that the Commission operates under the guidance of National Treasury, which provides clear frameworks on expenditure control. In line with this, internal policies were in place along with a dedicated compliance unit that monitors adherence to these cost-containment measures. However, their most effective strategy lay in early procurement, which depends on the availability of resources and the timely approval of retained surpluses. Early engagement allowed the attraction of a larger pool of bidders, fostering healthy competition and enabling the IEC to negotiate better prices. The approach not only applied to logistics but also spanned across all major projects. By initiating procurement well in advance, inflated costs often associated with last-minute purchasing, particularly in the lead-up to elections when suppliers may take advantage of urgency, were avoided. Starting early ensures better planning, improved value for money, and ultimately, more efficient use of public funds, so it was a central part of how the IEC contains costs across the organisation.
Head office
Ms Mbatha emphasised that their current occupation was under a valid lease, which had been extended through a process that fully complied with the relevant legislative and regulatory requirements. The most recent extension had been done in consultation with National Treasury, and approval was obtained in line with applicable regulations. Therefore, the lease arrangement remained lawful and legitimate. Recognising the need for a more permanent solution, the Commission had initiated a process to explore the viability of acquiring a permanent head office through a partnership with the Government Technical Advisory Centre (GTAC), in consultation with National Treasury, given the funding implications. While the intention to purchase was clear, accumulating the necessary funds required detailed discussions. Given the complexity of this procurement, a tender had been open for three months and was expected to close by the end of July, so the process had started and had been handled in accordance with all legal and financial protocols.
AI usage
Ms Mbatha acknowledged that artificial intelligence (AI) was recognised as a strategic risk, but emphasised the need to understand that the risk environment is dynamic and evolves, depending on the stage of the electoral cycle. Therefore, maintaining a strategic risk register that was reviewed and updated quarterly ensures that emerging threats, including those related to technology, are not only identified but actively monitored and mitigated. She also recognised AI as a potential risk for misinformation, manipulation, or cybersecurity threats. The likelihood and impact of this risk increased as the election drew nearer, which was reflected in the risk’s elevated rating during their reviews. The IEC did not take a reactive approach to risk. Instead, they engage in ongoing analysis and evaluation to ensure the institution remains resilient and prepared for any evolving threats, including those presented by emerging technologies.
Mr Moepya addressed the matter regarding the IEC member who was currently involved in ongoing court proceedings. He assured the Members that the Commission had taken the necessary steps to manage the issue responsibly and transparently. There had been a formal engagement with the Speaker of the National Assembly, providing updates on the actions taken by the Commission, the status of the legal process, and the extent to which the affected member was participating in Commission decisions. It was therefore being handled in line with due process, and the IEC remained committed to upholding their integrity throughout.
He responded to the issue of the global trends influencing the country. In the South African context, proactive steps had been taken to understand and respond to these pressures through targeted research conducted both before and during elections. These studies assisted in the assessment of voter sentiment, perceptions of electoral democracy, and the public's engagement with political processes. He suggested that the Committee and Parliament reflect on the findings, particularly how they manifested in last year's elections, as there were plans to conduct and publish similar research ahead of the upcoming local government elections. It would also inform the preparations for the IEC and the political parties, because experience showed that these findings were often accurate and useful. Public satisfaction with electoral democracy and political parties requires collective engagement from all stakeholders. The goal was shared responsibility for strengthening democracy, not assigning blame.
Mr Moepya said the IEC did not take the concerns raised by Members or other electoral stakeholders lightly. The development of the strategic plan was a consultative process involving input from political parties, instead of an isolated effort. There was constant collaboration with the Political Liaison Committee, which serves as a platform for regular engagement with all represented parties, ensuring that the perspectives and priorities of all stakeholders were integrated into their planning and operations.
However, he noted issues such as staff recruitment, election transparency, and system development had been discussed extensively within the party liaison committee (PLC), but had also been raised and discussed as new concerns in this meeting, which was an important concern. There had been assumptions that the discussions in the PLC had been communicated back effectively to party members, so they would not be taken for granted. When necessary, he would strongly support convening a dedicated session with Members of Parliament, and particularly this Committee, to unpack these matters directly. Such engagement would be both useful and constructive for enhancing mutual understanding and trust in the electoral process.
He concluded by reaffirming the clarity already provided by Mr Sheburi regarding the current legislative approach. While amendments had not been pursued currently, by-elections were taking place regularly, as nine were being overseen this week. Deficiencies in the law were not the cause of any of the issues in these by-elections, but rather stemmed from misunderstandings or the complexity of interpreting certain legal provisions and practices. There would therefore be an IEC meeting on Friday to consider senior counsel’s opinion on how best to address these recurring concerns, and engagement with Parliament so that proposed legislative interventions could be done promptly if necessary. The aim was to ensure clarity, compliance, and the smooth functioning of the electoral process without unnecessary delays.
Commissioners’ responses
Commissioner Ms Dhaya Pillay said the IEC followed a multi-layered approach in all its work to ensure objectivity, transparency and accountability. Research from bodies like the Human Sciences Research Council (HSRC) was commissioned not to justify decisions, but to inform strategies with credible evidence, avoiding reliance on personal opinions. This layered system extended to electoral operations, where both digital and manual processes, such as dual registration systems and audits, ensured integrity at every step.
She acknowledges that low voter turnout is a growing global concern. The recent example of Italy annulling an election due to extremely low participation highlighted the urgency of addressing voter engagement. The IEC's efforts aimed to maintain both the technical strength and public trust needed to support a healthy democracy.
Commissioner Ms Janet Love supported the Chairperson’s earlier point. She proposed that several matters discussed within the PLC should be more widely understood, not only by this Portfolio Committee, but also by the broader public. Topics surrounding the recruitment and review of voting station staff were not just operational, they were crucial to transparency and public trust, making it worthwhile to explore these issues further when the IEC meets with the Portfolio Committee to discuss the 2024 elections report.
She also responded to the issue of party funding, at the request of the IEC chairperson. While admitting a detailed discussion was better suited for another time, she confirmed the intention to submit the party funding report to the Committee. It would raise important questions around a possible review of the current legislation, along with the need for deeper cooperation with other institutions.
The Chairperson noted the proposals and fully supported the approach outlined. There was an understanding that the Committee would compile follow-up questions on any outstanding matters that had not been addressed, and that the IEC would respond to those questions promptly. These issues would be prioritised in the agenda for the meeting on 11 July to discuss the 2024 national and provincial elections.
Regarding the matter involving the Commissioner currently subject to court proceedings, he acknowledged the importance of transparency and accountability, especially given the parliamentary process that Commissioners undergo, which includes vetting by the Committee, the Chief Justice, and ultimately the President. Therefore, the preparation and submission of a detailed report outlining the steps taken by the Commission in handling this matter, without infringing on the sub judice rule, would be done. It would include the nature and scope of the engagements with the Speaker of the National Assembly, the President, and other relevant stakeholders. It would clarify the Commissioner’s status within the Commission. He expressed the belief that it would assist as a helpful reference for the Committee in respect of future matters of a similar nature.
Mr Mogale’s concern over low voter registration and turnout was serious and longstanding, and the responses from the IEC had not adequately addressed the urgency of the issue. He therefore requested the IEC to submit a detailed report outlining what actions were being taken to increase voter registration, including specific strategies, measurable targets, and any identified barriers. The report should be submitted ahead of the 11 July meeting, so the Committee could engage meaningfully with the IEC on this critical matter.
The Chairperson accepted the proposal, and stated that the matter would be discussed in the next session as outlined, and would include a specific update on this item. This would ensure that all Members had full clarity on the issues raised, particularly as they related to the upcoming budget vote. There was mutual agreement on addressing this matter comprehensively in the next meeting, in order to determine the most effective way forward.
Mr Moepya was pleased that the agreement on returning to this matter in the next session was mutual, and assured Mr Mogale that the concerns he raised had also been recognised within the PLC. He said the IEC's strategies and plans were grounded in solid data, not speculation, hence the full commitment to address them in detail at the next meeting, as requested.
However, he noted that Commissioner Mashinini had been inadvertently overlooked during the earlier discussion, and would appreciate it if he could be given the opportunity to contribute, as his input would further enrich the Commission’s response.
The Chairperson stated that Commissioner Mashinini had not indicated that he wanted to speak, so he was not skipped.
He expressed his gratitude on behalf of the Minister and the Department for the constructive discussions on the IEC’s revised strategic plan and APP. There had been recognition of important issues raised by Members, and the Committee supported the commitment to continue addressing outstanding concerns in the next session, as the collaborative energy in the discussion would strengthen the electoral processes and democratic institutions.
