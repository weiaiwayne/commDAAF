Meeting Summary
The Department of Tourism (DOT) and Department of Science, Technology and Innovation (DSTI) presented to a joint meeting of the this committee and the Portfolio Committee on Tourism. The National Astro-Tourism Strategy implementation plan is based on three primary pillars: indigenous Celestial Narratives and Human Capacity Development, Infrastructure Development, and Inclusive Tourism Growth and Partnerships.
Committee members asked about the potential impact of the Astro-Tourism project on local communities in the Northern Cape. Public participation was a point of concern, with one member questioning how governance and public engagement mechanisms were being implemented for the Astro-Tourism project. They suggested that committee members could play a greater role in spreading information and engaging the public, as the low number of public submissions indicated a lack of widespread involvement. Transparency and accountability in private sector-led projects were questioned, with a request for clarification on measures to prevent corruption and ensure the selection of suitable providers.
The sustainability of Astro-Tourism, particularly in supporting local economies, was raised as an issue. One member pointed to the struggles of small and medium-sized enterprises (SMMEs) in rural areas and stressed the importance of investing in infrastructure—roads, electricity, and connectivity—to make these regions more attractive to tourists and ensure the locals benefit from the project. Concerns were raised about the role of local municipalities in developing infrastructure, as well as efforts to improve transport, accommodation, and connectivity in remote areas.
The importance of community involvement in the project was emphasised, especially in towns like Carnarvon, which have not seen the same level of tourist innovation as Sutherland. Members questioned whether local communities were benefiting from job creation and skills development or if workers were being brought in from outside the region. The lack of education and skilled labour was flagged, and the need for simple skills training was stressed to ensure local participation and reduce unemployment.
Further concerns were raised about the safety and environmental impact of increased tourism, particularly in areas like Sutherland and Carnarvon. The risk of crime, particularly at scientific projects like MeerKAT, was mentioned, as well as the environmental implications of higher tourism volumes in sensitive areas. Discussions focused on financial transparency, with some members expressing concerns about potential mismanagement of funds, as well as the involvement of politically connected individuals in the projects.
Ultimately, the meeting highlighted a mixture of hope for the potential benefits of Astro-Tourism and concerns about its ability to deliver on those promises. The discussion centred on the importance of improving infrastructure, ensuring local community involvement, promoting public engagement, and safeguarding indigenous knowledge while addressing governance and transparency issues. The Committee emphasized the need for proper channels to handle allegations related to the project and its implementation.
Meeting report
Opening Remarks
Chairperson Shiviti welcomed the Minister and Deputy Minister of Science, Technology and Innovation and the Deputy Minister of Tourism. She stated that the focus of the meeting was on the dual mandate an collaborative effort of both departments for the National Astro-Tourism Strategy and Implementation Plan. She added that this would be the subject of the briefing to be provided by both departments. Last week, the Portfolio Committee on Science, Technology, and Innovation had visited the South African Astronomical Observatory as part of the same initiative, and next week, the Portfolio Committee would be visiting the Northern Cape to further this work.
Chairperson Shiviti said that the goal of the initiative was to position South Africa as a leading destination for Astro-Tourism, leveraging the region’s unique geographical advantages, such as its clear and dark night skies, as well as its rich astronomical heritage. The Astronomy Strategy would incorporate elements of local culture, providing tourists with the opportunity to appreciate the stars while engaging with indigenous narratives, particularly those of the KhoiSan people.
Chairperson Shiviti clarified that while the Astro-Tourism Strategy sought to highlight the region's aesthetic appeal, there was a strong emphasis on incorporating indigenous knowledge, physical culture, and the development of human capacity. Their primary concern was how to involve the local community in the initiative, ensuring they both understood and could actively participate in these initiatives, enabling them to communicate their knowledge to visitors. Ideally, the aim is to foster a community that not only will comprehend the principles of astronomy but can explain them to those who visit their area.
Chairperson Shiviti proposed that in the future it would be beneficial to establish astronomy schools in local communities, thus promoting greater participation from both residents and the general public. This would ensure that South Africans could engage with tourists in a meaningful way, particularly on the science of astronomy. The broader aim was to integrate science into everyday life and to ensure that technological and scientific innovations in South Africa became a point of interest for tourists. By doing so, South Africa would be able to showcase its innovations and promote them as an integral part of the tourism experience.
Chairperson Shiviti recalled a personal experience from her childhood when she had been envious of how other countries showcased their technological advancements. South Africa should offer a similar experience, particularly in the field of Astro-Tourism.
Minister’s Comments
Dr Blade Nzimande acknowledged everyone including Ms Pontsho Maruping, Managing Director, South African Radio Astronomy Observatory (SARAO), who is overseeing the Square Kilometre Array (SKA) project.
Minister Nzimande noted that this meeting was of significant importance for several reasons. Firstly, it involved two portfolio committees, which presented an excellent opportunity for collaboration, particularly when there were common interests at stake. It was a pleasure for the executive to report to both committees simultaneously, allowing them to gain valuable insights and advice on the work being undertaken. He recalled that in September 2024, his colleague had presented the National Astro-Tourism Strategy to the Cabinet, which was approved. The programme had been launched in Carnarvon, Northern Cape.
Minister Nzimande provided background on South Africa’s advancements in astronomy, mentioning that it had established itself as one of the leading nations in the field. He highlighted the hosting of the Global Conference on Astronomy in Cape Town in August 2024, which marked the first time the event had been held on the African continent. The conference, which attracted astronomers from around the world, served as an opportunity to engage young learners, many of whom were fortunate enough to interact with astronauts in space. He underscored the importance of stimulating curiosity among the youth and fostering an interest in science and technology.
Minister Nzimande noted the history of astronomy in South Africa, beginning with the establishment of the Royal Observatory over 200 years ago. Major milestones included the development of the Southern African Large Telescope (SALT) and the MeerKAT Telescope in Carnarvon, a key component of the SKA project. He acknowledged the support of international partners, including Germany, Italy, and China, in making these advancements possible. He noted South Africa’s growing involvement in gamma-ray astronomy through the High-Energy Stereoscopic Telescope based in Namibia.
Minister Nzimande emphasised that the country’s investment in high-calibre scientific instruments had led to a significant increase in the number of South African astronomers, from 60 to approximately 300 in the past 30 years. He defined Astro-Tourism, explaining that it was a niche market that combined astronomical experiences with tourism, offering opportunities to visit sites with dark skies, radio-quiet zones, and visible celestial objects. Minister Nzimande highlighted the historical and cultural significance of astronomy in South Africa, noting that indigenous communities had long engaged in astronomical observations for practical purposes, such as predicting agricultural cycles. He asserted that the study of astronomy tapped into valuable indigenous knowledge and traditions, which could be leveraged to enhance Astro-Tourism.
Minister Nzimande outlined the strategic importance of the Astro-Tourism strategy, which sought to build on South Africa’s competitive advantages in both astronomy and tourism. The strategy aimed to develop the country’s astronomy tourism sector by enhancing infrastructure and optimising the functioning of various aspects of both the astronomy and tourism industries. These two sectors were mutually reinforcing, as visitors interested in astronomy would be exposed to South Africa’s other tourist attractions, such as wildlife. Visitors to South Africa could potentially become investors in science, technology, and innovation.
Minister Nzimande discussed the broader goals of the strategy, including the ambition to position South Africa as a world-class Astro-Tourism destination, leveraging the country’s significant scientific infrastructure, including the SKA. One of the strategy’s objectives was to raise awareness and educate local communities about the importance of astronomy, dark skies, and radio-quiet zones. Some individuals who questioned the relevance of astronomy in the context of more immediate social issues, such as housing and education. He explained that astronomy had practical benefits, such as contributing to the development of WiFi and GPS technologies, which had become integral to modern life.
Minister Nzimande spoke to the investments being made in local communities, particularly in the Northern Cape, where the SKA project is located. These investments included providing bursaries to students, with a particular focus on those interested in pursuing careers in science and astronomy, as well as enhancing laboratory facilities in local schools. He invited the committees to provide their feedback and ideas on how these investments could be further developed to benefit South Africa’s economic growth and social development, particularly in disadvantaged communities.
Minister Nzimande reiterated the close collaboration between the Department of Science and Innovation and the Department of Tourism in implementing the Astro-Tourism strategy. A steering committee had been established, comprising officials from both departments, as well as representatives from the Northern Cape Department of Economic, Development and Tourism. One of the key projects co-funded under this partnership—the construction of the SKA Science Tourism Visitor Centre in Carnarvon. This centre would serve as a hub for educating schoolchildren and visitors about the SKA project and the broader field of astronomy. He encouraged the committees to remain engaged and informed about the progress of the initiative.

Problem Identification
Mr Takalani Nemaungani, DSTI Chief Director of Astronomy, indicated that the key problems identified include widespread light pollution, which prevents half of the global population from seeing the stars. In South Africa, high unemployment, low education levels, and a shortage of skilled labour, particularly in rural areas, compound the issue. There is a lack of awareness and understanding of astronomy and tourism in local communities, as well as disjointed narratives around indigenous astronomy. The absence of coordination between local, district, and national policies and a lack of integration in astro-tourism content further hinder progress.
Strategic Focus
Mr Nemaungani stated that the goals are to foster transformation and inclusivity within the Astro-Tourism sector, ensuring that its economic benefits are accessible to a wider population. Additionally, there is a focus on boosting destination competitiveness by making optimal use of both new and existing infrastructure. Lastly, strengthening South Africa’s position in the Astro-Tourism industry will be achieved through intentional collaboration and strategic destination development efforts.
Competitive Advantage
Mr Nemaungani emphasised that South Africa benefits from several geographic and scientific advantages in the Astro-Tourism sector. The African sky is renowned for being one of the clearest and darkest, due to favourable weather conditions and minimal light pollution. The southern hemisphere's positioning allows for a view of the densest and most fascinating parts of the Milky Way, as it points toward the centre of the galaxy. Additionally, South Africa is home to four of the world's best-preserved meteorite impact craters, offering unique multi-science opportunities. The country has a rich history of indigenous celestial narratives, adding cultural depth to its astronomical offerings. Furthermore, South Africa boasts the Southern African Large Telescope (SALT) in Sutherland, the largest single optical telescope in the southern hemisphere, and the Square Kilometre Array (SKA) project in Carnarvon, which aims to create the world's largest radio telescope.
Pillar One: indigenous Celestial Narratives and Human Capacity Development
Dr Shamilla Chettiar, DOT Deputy Director General: Destination Development, stated that the objective of Pillar One is to enhance transformation and inclusivity of the economic benefits of the Astro-Tourism sector.
Pillar Two: Infrastructure Development
The objective is to enhance destination competitiveness through optimal use of new and existing infrastructure, equipment and resources.
Pillar Three: Inclusive Tourism Growth and Partnerships
The objective is to strengthen South Africa’s Astro-Tourism competitiveness through deliberate collaboration efforts and destination development.
Socio-Economic Impact
Dr Chettiar said that the Socio-Economic Impact Assessment has been completed, and the report is included in the Communications Plan. The National Astro-Tourism Strategy underwent consultation with tourism and astronomy stakeholders across various sectors in the country. This process included sessions with national departments and engagement with affected stakeholders. The feedback received during these consultations was categorized and analysed to align with the strategic document. Following this, the strategic document was updated to incorporate relevant inputs, ensuring it aligned with the mandates of the affected departments. Overall, stakeholders supported the strategy, expressing that the process is both exciting and essential for local economic development.
A Critical Success Story, the case study of Sutherland
Dr Chettiar presented Sutherland in the Northern Cape, home to the Southern African Large Telescope (SALT), which has become a key Astro-Tourism destination due to its ideal location for astronomical observation. Since SALT’s construction, the town has seen high demand for tours during peak months, often surpassing the observatory’s capacity. To meet this demand, local operators use amateur telescopes and attractions. The tourism industry has embraced the astronomy theme, with establishments named after celestial bodies, such as Skitterland Guesthouse, Jupiter Restaurant, and Southern Cross, enhancing the town’s Astro-Tourism appeal.
Way Forward
Dr Chettiar stated that the next steps involve continuing to promote the National Astro-Tourism Strategy across various government levels and among key stakeholders in the public, private, tourism, and astronomy sectors. The strategy will be gazetted for public notice and submitted to Parliament for formal approval. Ongoing projects outlined in the implementation plan will receive continued support and monitoring. The Astro-Tourism governance body will be strengthened with regular updates and engagements. Efforts will be made to secure funding from both Treasury allocations and private-public funding instruments to support the strategy's implementation. Additionally, a planning workshop for Astro-Tourism will take place in Sutherland in 2025 to further advance the sector.

DSTI response on Astro-Tourism Strategy
Ms Gugulethu Zwane, DSTI Acting Director-General, said that DSTI would respond to the astronomy questions while tourism-related questions would be addressed by DOT.
Mr Takalani Nemaungani, DSTI Chief Director of Astronomy, noted that some of the questions during last week’s visit would be addressed in the upcoming visit to the Northern Cape sites. He introduced Dr Rosalind Skelton, Managing Director of the South African Astronomical Observatory (SAAO), who would assist in answering.
Mr Nemaungani replied that the tenders for the construction of the fence, WiFi and the visitor centre had come in higher than expected due to what was referred to as the "Karoo Premium," which made construction in remote areas like the Northern Cape more expensive. As a result, additional funding had been secured, including R1.2 billion from Treasury, and the tender adjudication process was underway. They were working on resolving delays caused by these additional costs, but anticipated beginning construction within the year.
On the potential negative impact of increased tourism on light and radio pollution, Mr Nemaungani, assured the Committee that the Astronomy Geographic Advantage Act already provided a legal framework for managing such interference. The Astronomy Management Authority within the Department handled permit applications to ensure that projects, such as renewable energy or mining, did not negatively affect the telescopes. The visitor centre in Carnarvon was designed to help channel visitors through the town, limiting direct visits to the site and mitigating interference. Special transport, such as a minibus, would be used to take visitors to the site without affecting the telescopes. Efforts were being made to provide WiFi to some communities in the region.
On skills development, Mr Nemaungani highlighted the establishment of an artisanal technical training centre in Carnarvon to address skill shortages, particularly in fields like mechanics and electrical work, which were required for the telescopes. While Sutherland had seen improvements in Astro-Tourism due to its longer history, Carnarvon was just beginning to develop this sector. He assured Members that DSTI was working to replicate the success of Sutherland in Carnarvon.
On protecting indigenous knowledge on astronomy, Mr Nemaungani affirmed that DSTI had a unit dedicated to Indigenous Knowledge Systems and worked with indigenous communities to protect and preserve their knowledge.
On the University of the Witwatersrand's involvement in the Digital Dome Project, he explained that the university had received funding from Anglo-American to upgrade its planetarium. DSTI was engaging with Sol Plaatje University in the Northern Cape to incorporate astronomy and astro-tourism into its curriculum, with plans to introduce astronomy as an accredited discipline.
Mr Nemaungani assured the Committee that more comprehensive responses would be provided during the site visits and through future engagements.
Ms Pontsho Maruping, SARAO Managing Director, provided an update on the fence construction. DSTI had been finalising contract negotiations with the preferred tenderer. She hoped the contract will be signed within the next week, with construction scheduled to begin in March. The completion of the fence is expected to take approximately 10 months.
On the provision of WiFi, Ms Maruping explained that WiFi was available in towns, including public libraries and schools. However, the fibre infrastructure used for research could not be used for commercial purposes due to regulatory restrictions. As a result, DSTI was unable to provide WiFi along the fibre route. Nonetheless, commercial services were being utilised to supply WiFi in the four towns surrounding the telescope.
Ms Maruping stated that DSTI was in the final stages of the approval process for the visitor centre. The designs were complete and the approved bidder was now in place. She hopes that the contract could be awarded by mid-June, with construction expected to begin in September, subject to the necessary tender approvals.
Mr Nemaungani explained that Northern Cape was chosen due to its geographical advantage which was crucial for the location of astronomical facilities like the SKA. The legislation passed by Parliament granted the department authority only in that region, enabling the regulation of interference with the telescope. However, the Astro-Tourism strategy was a national one and not limited to the Northern Cape.
Mr Nemaungani noted that although the initiatives were being piloted particularly in Carnarvon, there were plans to expand to other communities. In response to concerns about stakeholder engagement, he assured the Committee that several workshops were planned for the Northern Cape, after which DSTI would move to other provinces. He acknowledged that only 13 submissions had been received thus far and recognised the need for outreach to spread awareness of Astro-Tourism in other regions.
Department of Tourism response on Astro-Tourism Strategy
Mr Vele said that Dr Jacobs would address the potential of the market, after which Dr Chettiar would discuss skills development, public-private partnerships (PPPs) and other relevant issues emerging in the discussion.
Dr Laeticia Jacobs, DOT
Deputy Director for Tourism Integration
, noted the Carnarvon absence of a souvenir shop and where the community had questions on the direct benefits of the astronomy instruments. Although this is a pilot project, the intention is to replicate lessons learned from Sutherland in Carnarvon and beyond. She stressed the delicate balance between protecting the ecosystem, the radio telescopes, and ensuring local community involvement in tourism.
Dr Jacobs explained that Carnarvon still had a long way to go in terms of buy-in, but efforts were being made through the Global Sustainable Tourism Council Sustainable Tourism Training Program (GSTC STTP) which had been appointed by DSTI to conduct research. This research involved surveys and consultations with various stakeholders, from public sector representatives to communities in areas like Carnarvon and Sutherland to understand how to offset potential challenges. Despite the focus on the Northern Cape, the strategy was national in scope.
Dr Jacobs discussed the importance of improving basic services, including road development, as part of the tourism infrastructure. While the focus was on ensuring road safety rather than tarring, the ambience of the Karoo was an essential aspect for tourists visiting the region. She emphasised the need for local municipalities to collaborate on road development and other essential services to complement tourism offerings, such as accommodation and restaurants.
On governance, Dr Jacobs clarified that the governance body consisted of the two leading departments, the Northern Cape province and other partners involved in the strategy. This initiative was part of a 10-year plan with a review after five years to learn from the experience. She mentioned a successful example from the Indian side of the Himalayas, where rural women, without astronomy backgrounds, were able to provide homestays and teach astronomy. Similarly, local astro-guides in the Northern Cape, with only basic qualifications, were trained in both astronomy and tourism communication skills, ensuring they could offer an enriching experience to visitors.
Dr Jacobs noted that the Northern Cape Department of Economic Tourism and Development (DEDaT) in partnership with SARAO, are funding stakeholder engagement efforts in the region to increase their starting number of six engagements.
Dr Chettiar highlighted that South Africa was the first country to implement a national Astro-Tourism strategy, setting a global precedent for other countries. Both public and private sector projects were integrated into the strategy, with input gathered through an extensive public consultation process. The limited 13 public submissions received during the consultation phase were viewed as a sign of success, indicating that the public consultation had already addressed most concerns. The few comments that were received focused on wanting to be involved in the strategy’s implementation and minor adjustments to its details.
Dr Chettiar emphasised that Astro-Tourism is a niche market, not expected to become mass tourism. She pointed to the example of the Cradle of Humankind, where 70% of visitors were school groups, illustrating that educational tourism would be an essential part of the Astro-Tourism strategy. The strategy includes several projects that will be monitored for their progress, with key milestones outlined for the coming years in the Medium Term Expenditure Framework (MTEF). These projects are already funded, and the monitoring process will track visitor numbers, experiences and feedback.
On the long-term sustainability of Astro-Tourism, Dr Chettiar clarified that Astro-Tourism alone would not drive economic growth, therefore, complementary sectors like agriculture, biodiversity, and scientific investments were being prioritised. These sectors, in combination with Astro-Tourism, would provide long-term sustainability for the region. The strategy is not limited to the Northern Cape but has a national scope. For example, the Naval Hill Project in Bloemfontein is part of the broader Astro-Tourism strategy.
Dr Chettiar concluded by discussing the importance of working with provincial and local governments under the District Development Model to enhance tourism infrastructure, including roads, access, and signage. WiFi was identified as a critical element for tourism development, and government would work collaboratively with both public and private sector partners to improve connectivity. The strategy is set to run for 10 years, with a review after the first three years. While the first three years of the strategy would focus on implementing already funded projects, the government will be exploring funding models for future projects.
Director General Vele highlighted the importance of market development and the role of the private sector in bringing tourists to the region. While the government has not yet fully developed the incentives for attracting tourists, the local communities should still benefit from the wider tourism supply chain. Local residents can capitalise on opportunities such as converting their homes into bed-and-breakfast accommodation, which would allow them to directly profit from the influx of visitors.
Mr Vele acknowledged that attracting investment to the area can be challenging without basic infrastructure and development already in place, which would require government funding. However, government was committed to ensuring that these investments would eventually come to fruition. He emphasised the importance of continued communication and efforts to stimulate interest in the area.
In response to concerns about safety, Mr Vele noted that they would explore best practices for safety measures to ensure the protection of both tourists and locals. Where possible, PPPs would be considered, although the framework for these partnerships is still under review by National Treasury.
Mr Vele expressed optimism about the initiative and believed that, with strong leadership from the host countries and the collaborative efforts of all involved, the Astro-Tourism strategy will successfully take off.
