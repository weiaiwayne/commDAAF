Strategic Plan
The Portfolio Committee met to consider the Department of Home Affairs (DHA) and the Border Management Authority (BMA) 's strategic and annual performance plans and address their challenges.
The Chairperson opened the meeting by highlighting persistent service delivery failures at DHA offices, serious network downtimes, delays in finalising the White Paper on Citizenship, and a lack of responsiveness to the public's needs. The Council for Scientific and Industrial Research (CSIR) presented a diagnostic report on the DHA’s information communication technology (ICT) and service delivery performance, and the Department provided an update on the rollout of its self-service kiosks.
Members welcomed the DHA's strategic focus on digital transformation, but expressed frustration with the slow pace of real improvements. They raised concerns about DHA’s chronic underfunding, weak ICT infrastructure, the slow Smart identity document (ID) rollout, legal costs from ongoing litigation, poor rural service access, limited border scanning technology, and failure to collect fines from transport companies. Particular concern was shown for the underfunding and operational limitations of the BMA, including its restricted 10-kilometre enforcement range at the country's borders.
The DHA and BMA leadership acknowledged the challenges, citing major budget and capacity shortfalls, but outlined measures underway to improve digital systems, border security, civic registration services, and customer service access. Plans included digitising over 340 million civic records, expanding mobile offices, strengthening cybersecurity through a Security Information and Event Management system, and enhancing its contact centre support.
The Chairperson stressed that although the plans were impressive on paper, tangible service improvements, better infrastructure, faster policy implementation, and stronger accountability would be expected during the second parliamentary term. The Committee committed to maintaining strong oversight, closely monitoring the public hearings—especially on the Marriage Bill—and the Department’s efforts to meet its critical targets.
The Committee also considered its second term programme for 2025, including the upcoming public hearings, oversight visits, and a planned study tour to Brazil to learn about its border management and digital identity systems.
Meeting report
Chairperson’s opening remarks
The Chairperson commenced the meeting by extending a warm welcome to all attendees, including Members of Parliament, the Deputy Minister, the Director-General (DG), officials from the Department of Home Affairs (DHA), representatives from the Border Management Authority (BMA), the Independent Electoral Commission (IEC), and other stakeholders.
He said the primary purpose of the meeting was to discuss the annual performance plans (APPs) of institutions under the Committee’s oversight—the DHA, the BMA, and the IEC. The discussions would also include civic services, immigration services, budget allocations, programme implementation, and other arising matters.
The Chairperson acknowledged the Deputy Minister's leadership role in guiding discussions on critical departmental issues. He appreciated the Deputy Minister’s consistent presence and involvement in matters requiring executive attention, interpreting this as a demonstration of confidence and familiarity with Departmental challenges.
He then raised specific concerns, beginning with Barberton's recent service delivery disruptions. He expressed disappointment regarding the two-to-three-week service suspension due to alleged contract termination and related security concerns. He asked the Deputy Minister to address this issue comprehensively, highlighting that the Deputy Minister and the DG had previously been alerted. He further confirmed that he had conducted his follow-up with Departmental officials, who had provided insight into the ongoing challenges. He stressed the importance of officials being accessible and responsive to the public and public representatives.
The Chairperson proceeded to address the issue of infrastructure capacity at DHA offices. Referring to the recent unannounced oversight visit to the Umtata office, he emphasised persistent network downtimes and related issues with service kiosks. He cited incidents, including one where a Member of Parliament had encountered long queues and unassisted residents during a visit to KwaZulu-Natal (KZN). He stressed the urgency of addressing these recurring service frustrations despite the Department’s ongoing digital transformation agenda. Immediate, tangible improvements were necessary to meet the needs of South African residents and other legally present individuals.
The Chairperson proposed a slight modification to the agenda, prioritising the presentation from the DHA and then the BMA. Thereafter, the Committee would provide commentary on both. A break was scheduled at 13:00, after which the meeting would reconvene at 14:00 for the Council for Scientific and Industrial Research (CSIR) presentation on kiosks. The Committee would address item three concerning correspondence with the Minister involving cases raised by Phil Craig, Bulelwa Toyana, and Steve Dion Prince.
The Chairperson elaborated on including the Bulelwa Toyana matter, which was identified during an unannounced visit to Umtata. Ms Toyana had reported years of unassisted attempts to receive services from DHA, raising her concerns publicly during a hearing. The matter had been escalated for a response and resolution. He recalled a similar case in 2021/22, involving a woman in Vuwani who had spent nearly 20 years without an identity document (ID), which was resolved within a month following Committee intervention. That resolution involved collaboration between the Chairperson, the Committee Whip and the Deputy Minister. He emphasised that such cases highlighted systemic issues and the Committee’s duty to ensure responsive service delivery. He requested updates on these matters to form part of a broader discussion on institutional accountability. He specifically mentioned the case of Steve Dion Prince, who had encountered difficulties registering a spouse from another country, an issue that aligned with broader concerns around immigration procedures and citizenship registration.
Mr Eddie Mathonsi, Committee Secretary, said an apology had been submitted on behalf of the Minister of Home Affairs, who was travelling to Lesotho with the President. The Chairperson acknowledged the noted apologies and invited the Deputy Minister to introduce the two scheduled presentations.

Director-General’s Overview
Strategic and Operational Overview
Mr Tommy Makhode, DG, DHA, presented a major transformation agenda focused on digitalisation and decentralised service delivery under the theme:
“Home Affairs @ Home.”
Central to this was the rollout of a digital ID system allowing citizens to apply for smart IDs and e-passports online, complete payments digitally, and receive documents via home delivery. This system would also enable biometric verification and introduce secure, verifiable credentials.
A second key innovation was the electronic travel authorisation (ETA) system, which would automate visa applications using artificial intelligence (AI) to streamline processing, combat fraud, and promote tourism and skilled immigration. These developments were closely tied to the broader government priorities of economic growth, job creation, and service accessibility.
Progress had been made in several areas: digitisation of over 340 million civic records was underway, and birth registration was being automated in public and private health facilities. The Department also aimed to eliminate permit backlogs and roll out smart ID cards to all eligible citizens, including naturalised citizens and permanent residents.
The DHA invested in mobile offices, kiosks, and public-private partnerships to expand its service reach and redesigned physical offices for higher efficiency. In addition, the Department supports employment through digitisation projects, focusing on youth, and integrates goals around gender, disability, and district-level service access.
Challenges remained, as the Department faces serious funding shortfalls for its digitisation efforts, legal costs, and infrastructure maintenance. With no additional funding for goods and services, pressures would need to be absorbed through reprioritisation within the current budget, placing a strain on supplier payments and project implementation.
Programme highlights -- APP targets 2025-2028
Programme 1:
It focuses on internal operations, information communication technology (ICT) stabilisation, Protection of Personal Information Act (POPIA) compliance, governance, and audit performance. The goal is to support the Department’s digital transition while maintaining good financial controls and a clean audit outcome.
Programme 2:
Leads the digitisation of civic services, with targets around smart ID and e-passport rollout, automating birth registration, and increasing access to enabling documents. It also manages civic record digitisation and enhancements in front-line service delivery.
Programme 3:
Oversees the implementation of the ETA system, addresses the permitting backlog, and manages the Trusted Tour Operator scheme aimed at increasing tourism from key international markets. It supports secure migration and skills attraction.
Programme 4:
This includes the BMA and the Electoral Commission. Priorities include developing infrastructure at ports of entry and ensuring readiness for the 2026 local government elections.
Budget and expenditure estimates: 2025/26- 2027/28
Mr Gordon Hollamby, Chief Financial Officer (CFO), DHA, presented the budget section, indicating baseline increases of R321.2 million in 2025/26, R1.2 billion in 2026/27, and R369.5 million in 2027/28. These increases primarily supported the BMA’s infrastructure and staffing needs. An additional R885 million in 2026/27 was allocated to the Electoral Commission for the upcoming municipal elections.
Despite these increases, the Department highlighted serious budget constraints. No additional funding had been provided for core operational needs under goods and services. High-priority areas like digitisation, infrastructure maintenance, legal services, and security had to be funded through internal shifts and reprioritisation. Contractual obligations, especially those with forex exposure, posed a risk to service continuity, and delays in supplier payments were expected near the financial year-end.
Recommendations
The Department requested that the Portfolio Committee support the DHA's 2025 to 2030 strategic plan and its 2025/26 annual performance plan. These approvals were crucial to sustain momentum in digitisation, secure borders, expand access to identity services, and build a more responsive and efficient DHA.

Discussion
The Chairperson raised several key concerns. He began by expressing disappointment at the Department’s projected 2030 timeline for finalising the White Paper on Citizenship, Migration, and Refugee Protection. He reminded the Department of previous commitments made during ministerial oral question sessions, and emphasised that this delay contradicted the urgency the Committee had continually stressed. He urged the Department to produce a demonstrable plan to conclude the White Paper within the current administration, as the issues it aimed to address were pressing and of high public concern.
He then addressed budget constraints and highlighted the ongoing infrastructure challenges, particularly in rural offices such as Umtata, where poor conditions undermined staff efficiency and public service. He emphasised the need for the Department to align its infrastructure and capacity planning with its strategic objectives.
The Chairperson also raised the issue of the Department’s rising legal costs, which he said had resulted from losing numerous court cases. He asked the CFO to present figures on these expenses and criticised how such costs reduced funding available for critical initiatives like digital transformation. He also questioned the Department’s reliance on rental offices. He requested a clear plan to transition toward ownership and modernisation of facilities, citing incomplete buildings and the underutilisation of available public works offices. He expressed concern about ongoing delays in paying service providers, especially small businesses, within the mandated 30-day window. Referring to a personal visit to a Home Affairs office where basic equipment was non-functional, he stressed how delayed payments and resource shortages compromised operations.
He requested detailed cost implications on deportations, especially in light of the Department’s target to deport 10 000 individuals in the first quarter. He then addressed the recent closure of a DHA office in Barberton due to unresolved security contract issues, calling it unacceptable, and urged the Department to manage such contracts more proactively. He also reminded the CFO of the Auditor-General’s (AG's) findings about inadequate implementation of corrective actions, especially around supply chain management (SCM) and internal auditing. He demanded a report on progress in these areas.
He concluded by highlighting the importance of adequately funding the BMA. Despite its critical mandate, it faced resource constraints, and he criticised National Treasury’s lack of response to its business case. He warned that without proper funding and support, the BMA’s ability to secure the country’s borders and modernise its operations would be compromised.
Mr T Mogale (EFF) began by aligning with the Chairperson on concerns about the chronic underfunding of the BMA, an issue he emphasised had been raised repeatedly without effective resolution. He referred to a previously proposed meeting with National Treasury to address the funding situation, and questioned whether any progress had been made on that front. He also criticised the existing policy that limits the BMA’s operational responsibility to a 10-kilometre radius from the border, arguing that this policy severely hampered the agency’s effectiveness. He proposed that the BMA should be given authority over the entire border stretch to ensure proper enforcement and to prevent unauthorised entries, especially given South Africa’s long and porous borders.
He raised concerns about the current practice where skilled personnel from the BMA head office were frequently dispatched to border posts to inspect consignments due to a lack of adequately trained staff on the ground. He requested specific information on what measures were being taken to build these skills locally at the points of entry. He welcomed the implementation of body cameras for BMA personnel as a positive move toward transparency and accountability, but asked whether all officers had been equipped, or only a few.
He then addressed the core issue of resource allocation, arguing that it was counterproductive to spend over R100 million annually on deportations when the BMA remained underfunded and ill-equipped. He proposed that instead of using these large sums on deportations, which often resulted in deported individuals re-entering the country undetected, the funds should be redirected toward capacitating the BMA. A strong, well-funded BMA would enhance border security and reduce illegal entry, thus addressing the root problem rather than its symptoms.
Turning to the DHA presentation, Mr Mogale addressed the digital ID project and overall digitisation strategy. He welcomed the modernisation, but echoed the AG's concerns about the Department’s ability to secure digital systems and protect citizens’ information. He questioned the Department’s preparedness to counter risks such as hacking and data breaches, which could compromise national security and individual privacy.
He also raised the AG's findings regarding the Department’s failure to effectively collect fines from companies that illegally bring people into the country, such as bus companies and airlines. He asked why this revenue was not being collected and what steps the Department took to correct this.
On the issue of staff shortages, he shared a firsthand account from a visit to the Port Elizabeth (Gqeberha) office, where only five staff members were responsible for administrative duties and field operations across the entire metropolitan area. He challenged the Department on how it planned to meet its stated goal of conducting 2 000 operations with such limited human resources, asking for a detailed explanation of how the Department addressed vacancy challenges within the immigration services.
Lastly, he addressed the AG's report of R205 million irregular expenditure tied to the digitisation project. He asked what had led to the irregularity, what actions had been taken to recover or account for the funds, and what measures were in place to prevent similar occurrences. He concluded by urging transparency and accountability, especially given the tight fiscal environment and the critical nature of the Department’s work.
Rev K Meshoe (ACDP) expressed strong support for the proposal to expand the mandate of the BMA to manage and secure the entire border of South Africa. He acknowledged the current limitations in staffing and resources, but emphasised that increased funding was essential for national security. He pointed out that criminals were entering the country illegally and were not properly processed because their fingerprints were not taken at entry, making law enforcement efforts ineffective. He stressed that expecting the BMA to function effectively was unrealistic when its responsibilities and capacity were limited. He said the situation bordered on being laughable and undermined the country’s efforts to fight crime.
He criticised the cycle of deporting undocumented individuals, only for them to re-enter the country through unmonitored border areas. He said that some deportees even mocked the system, promising to return. He argued that criminals exploited these gaps in border security and that the government must take this issue seriously if it genuinely seeks to reduce crime. He reiterated that a properly funded and capacitated BMA was crucial in achieving effective border security.
Continuing on the theme of national urgency, he questioned the long-term target dates set by the DHA. He referred specifically to the White Paper scheduled for 2030 and replacing green barcoded IDs with smart IDs by 2029, asking why these timelines could not be accelerated. He urged the Department to show greater urgency, noting that banks were already raising concerns about fraud linked to outdated ID systems. Delaying the implementation of smart IDs sent a message to financial institutions and citizens that there was no rush to address the problem.
Rev Meshoe also expressed frustration over the ineffective implementation of the Protection of Personal Information Act. Despite assurances, citizens continued receiving unsolicited communications from marketing companies, raising the question of how these companies obtained personal data. He challenged the Department to explain how POPIA was being enforced, and questioned the point of having such legislation if it remained toothless.
On digital literacy within the DHA, he pointed to the plan to train 2,500 officials by 2030, asking how many staff would be trained annually and whether there was a genuine sense of urgency to build this critical capacity. He emphasised that such training should not be delayed in an era of digital transformation.
Finally, he raised the issue of illegally transporting goods, particularly cash, across borders. He cited longstanding concerns about people smuggling large sums of money in backpacks and carry-on luggage, often avoiding taxes and banking systems. He called on the BMA to develop strategies to detect and prevent such activities, highlighting that many informal businesses did not bank their income and instead smuggled cash out of the country. He said that banking personnel were increasingly questioning the lack of visibility of these business operators in the formal financial system.
Following Rev Meshoe’s remarks, the Chairperson added a related query directed at Acting BMA Commissioner Thupana. He acknowledged the challenges posed by limited resources, but emphasised the need to understand how the BMA ensured the integrity of goods transported through South Africa’s borders, especially by trucks. He specifically asked whether the BMA had any instruments or technological systems in place to inspect the contents of these trucks as they pass through the border. He wanted clarity on the actual process -- whether border officials relied on manual inspections by opening trucks entirely, or if there were proper scanning devices or other detection instruments used to verify that the trucks were carrying legitimate goods. He highlighted a particular interest in knowing whether such systems were used across all border posts, especially those operating 24 hours a day. This was to get a sense of how the BMA currently manages the flow of goods and what capacity it has to detect and deter illegal activity at ports of entry.
Mr A Roos (DA) congratulated the BMA for their recent significant increase in apprehensions at South Africa’s borders, noting a rise of over 200%. Despite operating under challenging budgetary constraints, he emphasised that this was a remarkable achievement and testament to their efforts. He said the current financial shortfall facing the BMA was not simply a one-year issue, but the cumulative result of decades of underinvestment across various line departments historically responsible for border security. He stressed that national budgeting should reflect the country’s priorities and questioned the current status of the BMA’s funding request to the National Treasury. Specifically, he asked whether Treasury had set a deadline for a decision. He requested a breakdown of the proposed R4.5 billion budget, indicating what was critically needed versus what could be phased in gradually. He wanted clarity on the business case submitted, seeking insight into what operational capabilities would be unlocked at different funding thresholds.
He then turned to the coordination between agencies operating at the borders, particularly the BMA, the South African Police Service (SAPS), and the South African National Defence Force (SANDF). He recalled how border patrol responsibilities had shifted from the SAPS to the SANDF over the years. He noted that when the task reverted to the SANDF, the number of operational units had been drastically reduced. While acknowledging the BMA’s effectiveness within the 10-kilometre jurisdiction from the border, he expressed interest in the SANDF’s use of drone surveillance. He questioned whether there was any integration or information-sharing between the SANDF’s and BMA’s drone programmes. Improved collaboration in this area, he suggested, could enhance border surveillance and interception efforts.
Mr Roos addressed the recurring and costly challenge of deporting undocumented foreign nationals who often re-enter the country illegally. He requested an update on diplomatic negotiations with source countries regarding their willingness to accept deported nationals and contribute to the associated costs. He argued that such countries had a vested interest in curbing the cyclical re-immigration of their citizens, and therefore, more robust cooperation should be sought.
Shifting focus to the DHA, he referred to five commitments made by the Minister at the start of his term, specifically lauding the progress on reducing the visa backlog, accelerating the implementation of Operation Vulindlela, reinstating the Immigration Advisory Board, and enhancing site inspections within the Republic. However, he flagged concerns about the system modernisation initiative, which he acknowledged was not entirely under the control of DHA. Notably, he pointed to the high costs imposed by the State Information Technology Agency (SITA), which reportedly exceeded market rates and were straining the Department’s budget for critical infrastructure and cybersecurity upgrades. He sought a detailed explanation of these additional costs. He asked what potential savings could be realised if the DHA could source these services independently from the open market.
He welcomed the introduction of smart IDs for naturalised citizens and permanent residents. He asked for a status update on that project and whether implementation was on track to meet performance targets. He praised the new initiative, which allowed online ID ordering with home delivery, likening it to the secure systems already used for banking cards. However, he urged the Department to consider expanding this service to overseas citizens, referring to the example of South Africans in Wellington, New Zealand, who reportedly face year-long waits just to secure an appointment to apply for documentation. He asked whether there were plans to roll out similar online application and delivery services abroad, and what strategies were being explored to improve consular services.
Mr Roos referred to the Deputy Minister’s new responsibility over early birth registrations and urged attention to a particularly pressing issue -- the documentation of abandoned and orphaned children. He raised concerns about children who, due to bureaucratic failures in the late registration of birth (LRB) process affecting their parents, were now themselves at risk of becoming stateless. He described a scenario in which intergenerational documentation failure was growing. He highlighted the additional problem of abandoned or orphaned children who could not be legally fostered until they were properly registered. He requested that the Deputy Minister prioritise these cases and report back on strategies to streamline documentation for vulnerable children.
Lastly, he underscored the need for a culture shift within the DHA. He echoed the Minister’s call for a new culture centred on dignity and respectful treatment of South Africans. While oversight visits showed progress, he expressed concern that not all DHA staff had embraced this ethos. He directed a final question to the Director-General, asking what specific programmes or systems were being implemented to embed this cultural shift across the Department. He emphasised the importance of complaint mechanisms and accountability to ensure that, where service delivery falls short, corrective action is taken against those responsible.
Mr J Ngubane (MKP) opened his remarks by acknowledging and commending the entities that had presented their strategic and budget plans, recognising the visible good work being done. However, he quickly shifted to express concern that there may be more hidden issues than those regularly reported. He raised alarm about what he described as the arrogance displayed by certain foreign nationals, specifically Nigerians, Somalians and Pakistanis, towards South African citizens and even law enforcement officers. He attributed this behaviour to weaknesses and loopholes in South Africa’s immigration laws, particularly under the DHA.
He warned that strategic and budget plans must be more than routine presentations -- they should be geared toward effectively addressing the nation’s real challenges, which persist despite the repetition of well-articulated plans. He criticised what he termed a “religious” annual presentation of plans that yield little practical progress, citing persistent issues such as porous borders, ineffective deportation processes, sham marriages, fraudulent deaths for insurance scams, and widespread undocumented migrants, as evidence of ongoing failure.
Mr Ngubane raised serious concerns about the digitisation of DHA operations, noting that while digital transformation was important, it brought risks. He emphasised the need to integrate security, efficiency, and corruption prevention into digital systems from the outset. He stressed that introducing new technology without proper safeguards could lead to new forms of fraud. To this end, he advocated advanced technological safeguards such as barcodes, biometric features, secure portable document formats (PDFs), and robust access controls. He also lamented the discontinuation of a once-useful online system that allowed citizens to check their marital status, or even verify if they had been falsely declared dead. He questioned why such a helpful system had been taken offline without explanation, and suggested it could have spared many from fraudulent or bureaucratic complications.
Addressing internal departmental challenges, he highlighted under-capacitation as a chronic issue within the DHA. He requested data on the number of vacant posts and called for clear targets and timelines to fill them. He claimed that many departmental failures stemmed from this lack of human resources, resulting in officials claiming they could not fulfil their duties due to insufficient staffing. He cited the real-life example of a 35-year-old man in the Eastern Cape, who had never had an ID or birth certificate, despite years of trying, to illustrate how the absence of a functional case tracking system, such as a customer relationship management (CRM) tool, hampered service delivery. Such a system would help to track applications and ensure accountability for unresolved cases.
Mr Ngubane then focused on economic issues, linking the DHA's performance to national economic growth. He questioned the effectiveness of the Department’s plans if they failed to positively impact the economy. He also stressed the importance of aligning resource use with intended outcomes, criticising what he viewed as inefficient allocation of departmental resources. He called for transparency on the potential risks associated with plan implementation, recommending proactive risk identification and mitigation.
Regarding the BMA, he expressed cautious optimism about technological innovations, particularly using drones for border surveillance. However, he challenged the celebrated statistic of a 200% increase in performance, calling for evidence to support such claims. He requested baseline data and a clear explanation of what actions had led to the reported success, warning against politicising departmental achievements. In his view, all parties in Parliament should share and celebrate victories collectively, rather than be framed as wins for specific political affiliations.
Ms M Modise-Mpya (ANC) acknowledged and appreciated the budget allocated to the BMA. She noted that concerns had long been raised regarding the BMA’s underfunding since its establishment. While the current budget may not fully meet their operational requirements, the increase from approximately R200 million to over R1 billion marked a significant improvement. She viewed this development as a positive step in the right direction. She hoped the BMA would use the funds effectively to address corruption, which often compromised institutional integrity, especially in security sectors.
She expressed optimism that the BMA’s current plans, particularly those involving workshops to instil ethical and professional conduct among its members, would yield tangible and positive outcomes. The Department and its entities now largely rely on the BMA to safeguard South Africa’s borders. While the SANDF and the SAPS were still involved, the recent effectiveness demonstrated by the BMA had been commendable and encouraging.

She proposed that the DHA consider conducting public engagement sessions or
imbizos
in various provinces. This would help the Department understand people's everyday challenges and allow them to communicate proposed changes more effectively.

Legal costs and consequence management
The Deputy Minister explained that rising legal costs were primarily linked to the Department’s strong approach to consequence management. As corrupt officials were pursued through internal disciplinary processes and courts, many resisted termination by hiring private legal counsel, often using wealth accumulated improperly. To succeed in these cases, the Department had to secure expert legal representation, which increased legal expenses. In addition, external individuals, particularly in the permitting environment, were increasingly challenging the Department in court, adding to the legal cost burden.
Multi-channel service strategy
The Department had adopted a multi-channel strategy to ensure accessibility for all citizens. This included service delivery through malls, banks, mobile units and hospitals. Persistent network challenges hampered the department’s efficiency, largely due to over-reliance on SITA. Overview of the systems downtime diagnostic assessment
Dr Calteaux presented the findings of a two-year system downtime diagnostic Assessment conducted by the CSIR for the DHA, covering the period from April 2023 to March 2025. The study had addressed systemic weaknesses across five key domains: networks, applications, cybersecurity, people and governance, and enterprise architecture.
Major network limitations included outdated and inconsistent architecture, reliance on copper-based Diginet links (limited to 2 Mbps), and an absence of proactive monitoring tools. Pilots on alternative high-speed networks, like the Wynberg office shift to a 100 Mbps link, have demonstrated marked improvements in latency, packet loss, and performance. Bandwidth modelling had confirmed the need for a minimum 10 Mbps connection per site, especially given rising biometric data demands. A network monitoring dashboard had been introduced to provide a live view of operational bandwidth per site and improve planning, but poor link quality, over-reliance on mobile or microwave connections, and fragmented routing, continued to impair performance.
On the applications front, the CSIR had found a highly complex, fragmented ecosystem of over 38 systems across multiple operating platforms, with no real-time system-wide visibility. Applications were being monitored in isolation, making it difficult to identify failure points. A live application monitoring dashboard piloted during the 2024 elections had helped to mitigate critical errors and improve processing for high-demand services like temporary IDs and external verifications for example, the Gauteng Department of Education (GDE) and the Companies and Intellectual Property Commission (CIPC).
Key recommendations included full deployment and operational integration of the monitoring solution, decoupling verification services from mainframes, and modernising infrastructure to introduce High Availability (HA) between data centres. The cybersecurity assessment had revealed major gaps in posture and architecture. However, improvements were underway, such as monthly patching, antivirus renewal, Hypertext Transfer Protocol Secure (HTTPS) migration, and network detection and response (NDR) implementation. However, the CSIR advised further investment in security operations centre (SOC) capabilities, broader endpoint protection, regular penetration testing, and improved coordination of incident response and disaster recovery planning.
From a governance and skills perspective, the CSIR found that fragmented responsibilities, inadequate third-party management, and a shortage of critical ICT skills compromised system resilience. Enterprise architecture (EA) frameworks, like The Open Group Architecture Framework (TOGAF) and Information Technology Infrastructure Library (ITIL), were in place but underutilised. The EA team lacked sufficient visibility across the DHA’s full business model. The study concluded that the root causes of downtime stemmed from a combination of scalability bottlenecks, especially in the National Population Register (NPR) hosted on constrained SITA mainframes, interdependent and siloed data centres, suboptimal security and application integration, and poor data quality across platforms.
The CSIR’s overarching recommendation was for:
the full re-architecture of the DHA’s IT infrastructure, moving toward modular, scalable systems with redundancy;
adopting a unified security architecture with SIEM and SOC integration; and
establishing a single source of truth for identity data.
The Portfolio Committee was advised to support a comprehensive transformation strategy incorporating these diagnostics into the DHA’s broader digital overhaul.

Overview of systems downtime status
Mr Du Preez presented a comprehensive update on progress to reduce system downtime, particularly in implementing the CSIR’s recommendations. Overview of the Self-Service Kiosk report
Background and introduction
Mr Mavuso said the DHA had conducted a geographic accessibility study in 2019/20 to assess how population changes affected service demand. The study evaluated travel distances to service points, identified service gaps, and recommended solutions for better access. The DHA had adopted a multi-channel strategy combining walk-in offices, mobile units, and digital innovations like web services, mobile apps, and self-service kiosks. The broader goal was to automate business processes, move towards a paperless environment, and offer faster, alternative service delivery options.

Following the study, the Department had developed an access model aligned with the District Development Model (DDM) to improve equitable access. Key measures included maximum travel distances and population thresholds for service points. Many citizens still travelled long distances for services, highlighting the need for better coverage. Office space standards (large, medium, and small offices) were set to ensure purpose-built infrastructure, and templates were developed with the DPWI to guide future acquisitions.
Self-service kiosks
The DHA appointed a service provider to design and supply kiosks equipped with the live capture system. These kiosks allowed citizens to independently apply for Smart ID cards and passports and reprint vital certificates by capturing fingerprints and photos, uploading documents, making payments, and collecting Smart IDs. Although the project had faced delays, the DHA was taking action to complete integration and start rolling out kiosks nationwide in the 2025/26 financial year.
Deployment Strategy
The kiosks would be placed in underserved areas, such as malls, to provide citizens with greater flexibility by offering services beyond traditional office hours, including weekends. This strategy aimed to reduce queues and make services more accessible to the public.
Recommendations
The Department recommends that the Portfolio Committee note the progress of the kiosk project, which supports the DHA’s broader modernisation and service delivery goals.

Referring to his earlier presentation, he pointed out that the IT budget for goods and services, particularly information services for modernisation, was R767 million for the current financial year, increasing to R840 million in the next year.
The Chairperson asked Mr Makhode when the CIO position would be advertised, and if it would be available on the site.
The DG responded that the CIO position had already been advertised, and a panel had met to consider the candidates who had applied at one point. The panel believed another round of advertisements was necessary due to the calibre of work required for the position. The panel, which included the Deputy Minister and other Ministers, had considered headhunting for the role.
The Chairperson said that he was deliberately asking the question to the DG because the Department had been struggling with ICT-related issues. The Committee had decided to focus on ICT, informed by the diagnosis report. He emphasised the importance of assessing the unit's capacity as they moved forward and requested that the Department begin advertising for the necessary positions. He expressed concern about the prolonged struggle over seven years and noted that it was an issue that would be dealt with after receiving a comprehensive report.
The Chairperson then asked Dr Malinga and the CSIR team to respond to the issues raised, especially about the strike plan and the progress on the Government Printing Works (GPW). He assured Mr Makhode that they would follow up on all the recommendations within the specified timeframes to resolve the matter. He also linked the appointment of the CIO to the ongoing presentation, highlighting its relevance to the issues at hand. Finally, the Chairperson noted the need to explore both short-term and long-term strategies regarding the network and requested Dr. Malinga to guide the team in responding to the concerns raised by the members.
Dr Malinga noted that the department had already addressed most of the questions and comments. There was little left to add except to provide clarification on a couple of specific matters.
Regarding the mobile trucks or offices, he said that although the CSIR had not specifically worked on them, it was understood that their connectivity would be similar to rural areas, meaning they would face similar constraints.
On the issue of network administration, he clarified that the CSIR did not imply that there was no administration happening. The Department was already addressing it, but additional resources were necessary to strengthen the system's administration. He also acknowledged that the study they conducted had focused on areas where things were not going well, and he wanted to emphasise that many positive efforts by the DHA were not highlighted in the report. He appreciated the hard work of the teams within the Department to ensure that the country received the necessary services.
He concluded by expressing the CSIR’s commitment to continue working with the Department on these matters and thanked the DHA and the Portfolio Committee for the opportunity to present.
The Chairperson thanked Dr. Malinga and the CSIR team for their hard work and contributions to the process. He added that from time to time, Mr Makhode would request Dr Malinga to invite the team to engage with the Committee on issues related to the recommendations, which would contribute to the ongoing work of the CSIR team.
Deputy Minister Nzuza first thanked the CSIR team for their continuous work alongside the Department. He acknowledged the importance of the work that had been done, emphasising that the Department was no longer uncertain regarding the issues at hand. The CSIR team had provided them with clear and specific issues that needed to be addressed, which was a significant step forward.
He then addressed the question raised regarding SITA, confirming that, as per Mr Du Preez’s earlier statement, regulations had already been issued. These new regulations would enable the DHA to move away from relying on SITA, which had been a major dependency in the past. This change would allow the Department full control over its systems and operations, eliminating the need to depend on external stakeholders. It would provide a clearer understanding of what needed to be done within the Department.
In closing, Deputy Minister Nzuza remarked that the only remaining step was implementing the recommendations provided. He assured the Portfolio Committee that the Department was fully committed to executing the recommendations. He reaffirmed that the Department's primary focus was to build a digital Home Affairs by improving the current infrastructure and connections as the top priority moving forward.
The Chairperson wrapped up the responses by expressing gratitude to Deputy Minister Nzuza, the CSIR team led by Dr Malinga, the Director-General, and all the team members who had been involved in the discussions and had stayed with the Committee since the morning. He thanked them for their efforts in engaging with the Department’s work and assisting in assessing its performance.
He said that, as per the previous summary, the Committee would reconvene with the Department before adopting all the reports to present them to the House during the budget vote. He acknowledged that various issues had been raised with the Department and incorporated into the budget and strategic plan.
The Chairperson also expressed his appreciation to Dr Malinga and his team for their continuous support of the DHA, noting that their efforts would ultimately improve service delivery for South Africans and visitors to the country.
He informed the Deputy Minister and their respective teams that they were now excused. The remaining two items on the agenda will be dealt with by members of the Committee. He invited Mr Mathonsi and the content advisor to address the final two items.
Committee matters
Second term programme
Mr Mathonsi, the Committee Secretary, started by outlining the programme and giving more details on the upcoming public hearings and study tour. He informed the Committee that the programme had been sent to Members the previous week, starting with this week’s meeting, being the last one for the current period. He noted that Members would be on leave from 29 April to 2 May, and on 6 May the Committee would receive presentations on the strategic plan and budget of the Independent Electoral Commission (IEC) and the Government Printing Works.
If time allowed, the Committee would also receive a brief presentation on submissions regarding the one-stop border post. A few submissions have already been received. The content advisor would summarise them for the Members before public hearings on the Immigration Bill are conducted on Tuesday, 9 May.
The BMA would brief the Committee on the festive season report, and it would deliberate on the Immigration Amendment Bill. On the same day, the content advisor would table the draft Committee strategy and annual business plan and consider the oversight visit reports for the EDM port of entry and Government Printing Works, which had been conducted in December.
Although the order had not been finalised, on 13 May, the Committee would consider the budget vote report, the recent report received, and the strategic plan. It aimed to adopt the report by 13 May, as Parliament would be engaged in budget vote debates from 13 to 23 May, with the budget vote debate scheduled for 22 May.
The Committee would also deliberate on the counter-corruption and security services report, addressing corruption and fraud within the Department. It would also look at the progress made in amending sections of the Refugee Act, which had been declared unconstitutional.
Public hearings on the one-stop border post were scheduled for 27 May, to be held virtually with stakeholders. On 30 May, the IEC would brief the Committee on the national and provincial election report. Public hearings for the North West and the Free State were proposed to take place from 12 to 15 May, with the Committee considering splitting into two groups to conduct them simultaneously in these provinces.
Mr Mathonsi also mentioned the study tour to Brazil, for which the Committee had written to the embassy and received a response. The embassy’s recess would begin on 17 July, so the proposed dates for the study tour were around 19 July. The Committee had also notified the Minister of International Relations and the House Chairperson about the tour.
In conclusion, Mr Mathonsi noted the need to rectify a mistake in the agenda regarding the IEC presenting the election report twice. He reiterated the importance of continuing the Committee’s work in the coming months.
The Chairperson began by presenting the report on the programme for public hearings and the trip to Brazil. He asked for Members’ comments, but first made several key observations:
Strategic plan and reports
The Chairperson emphasised that the team had noted all the reports presented, and the Committee would work with the content advisor and research team to consolidate the views expressed by the Members on the DHA and BMA's strategic plans. This would include recommendations on the budget allocations. A special meeting would be arranged before adopting these views on 13 May.
Agenda adjustments
The Chairperson proposed shifting the item on the border post update to a later meeting and incorporating the Department’s feedback, in line with the Committee’s deliberations. This would allow the Committee to focus on agreed-upon issues with the BMA and the DHA.
IEC report and electoral Issues
Regarding the IEC report, the Chairperson acknowledged that while a court case was ongoing between the IEC and other parties, the Committee still needed to process the report on the national and provincial elections. This report was crucial due to its wide-ranging implications, and the Committee needed to allocate sufficient time to discuss it, particularly in the context of preparations for future elections. The IEC had indicated it would be ready to present the report by June.
Public hearings observations
The Chairperson raised concerns about the participation levels in recent public hearings, particularly in the Northern Cape and Mpumalanga. Despite a good turnout from public representatives, there was an issue with Committee Members’ attendance during long weekends. This absence was noted as a challenge and a potential limitation for effective public hearings, with the Chairperson suggesting that long weekends should not interfere with Committee work.
Brazil study tour
The Chairperson recalled that the Committee had previously agreed to a study tour to Brazil to explore electronic voting systems, which had been deferred due to logistical issues. He stressed that by the end of May, the Committee needed a detailed report on the logistics, research and proposed activities for the Brazil trip. This would ensure full clarity and preparation ahead of the tour, particularly regarding how Brazil had implemented electronic voting. The Committee would also seek input from the IEC on the subject.
Legal costs and budget concerns
Finally, the Chairperson referred to a previous meeting with the Standing Committee for Refugee Affairs (SCRA) regarding the legal costs and budget implications associated with the ongoing legal battles in which the Department was involved. The committee will focus on this area, and the SCRA will be invited to present further details.
In conclusion, the Chairperson invited Members to comment on the programme, noting that the Committee would navigate urgent matters necessary to maintain effective oversight and accountability.
Discussion
Mr Mogale raised three issues. Firstly, he said he did not see any mention of a meeting with the BMA regarding the issues that had not been responded to in the previous physical meeting. He emphasised that the Committee had requested the BMA to address specific issues, which had yet to be presented. He inquired when the BMA would return to the Committee to respond to these outstanding issues.
Secondly, he opposed splitting the Committee for the upcoming public hearings in Limpopo, Northern Cape, and another province. He argued that splitting up the Committee would leave only a few Members to facilitate the proceedings, depriving the hearings of broader deliberations and public input. He supported the idea of the Committee travelling together as a full team, as they had done in the past, to ensure all Members could be part of the discussions.
Lastly, regarding the IEC meeting where the upcoming election plans would be presented, he proposed that this meeting be scheduled as a physical meeting. He urged the Chairperson to ensure that the necessary approvals were obtained for this meeting to be held in person, rather than remotely, to facilitate better engagement.
Mr Ngubane raised two points. Firstly, he inquired about the reasoning behind splitting the Committee into two teams for the upcoming public hearings in different provinces, as mentioned in the court’s findings. He asked if the purpose was to expedite the process of the public hearings and finish early, seeking clarification on the intention behind this decision.
Secondly, he noted a discrepancy in the presentation materials, specifically with the CSIR presentation. He pointed out that some of the previous presentations differed from those currently being presented. He requested that the latest version of the presentations be provided to the Committee so that everyone had the same documents as those presented previously.
The Chairperson addressed several matters in response to previous discussions. He confirmed that the IEC had agreed to have a physical meeting on a single item related to the upcoming elections. This meeting would provide ample time for members to investigate the issues in detail. He emphasised that the IEC must also address matters related to the upcoming local government elections. Even if no specific legislation was proposed, the IEC should highlight possible issues, particularly given the challenges observed in recent by-elections contested by political parties.
He said the date for the IEC meeting should be carefully checked to ensure that no key matters in the House conflicted with it. The goal was to allow enough time to address the IEC’s concerns properly during the meeting.
Regarding the upcoming meeting, the Chairperson indicated that the issues raised in the previous meeting would be included. He also referred to a prior interaction with the BMA and DHA, acknowledging the need to address the issues raised in the previous meeting before adopting the final reports. He called for further input from the Committee Members to ensure all the relevant issues were addressed.
Committee Secretary Mathonsi interrupted, recalling that on 18 March. However, they were not present at the meeting, the team had sent a presentation in response to the issues raised in the previous meeting. He clarified that it had been a physical meeting, and they had sent the presentation to all Members, even though they had not attended it themselves.
The Chairperson responded, clarifying that he agreed with Mr Mogale’s point about the issues regarding public hearings. He acknowledged the concerns raised by Members about the challenges of splitting the Committee for public hearings. He emphasised that this had been discussed in previous meetings, and the decision would be based on the committee's collective experience. He suggested that tomorrow’s meeting should include a session with the legal team to ensure the Committee was fully informed of the legal parameters for 2025, particularly about public hearings.
The Chairperson also recalled past issues with legislation coming late to the National Council of Provinces (NCOP) before elections, and highlighted the importance of legal clarity. The Committee should maintain a unified approach, ensuring that public hearings were conducted effectively and the work was done collectively. The decision about splitting teams for public hearings would be further discussed with the legal team, and a final decision would be made, informing Members of the outcome.
Finally, the Chairperson mentioned that the Committee would maintain the current schedule structure and manage time carefully, acknowledging the limited meeting days. He thanked the members for their patience during the extended session and said that further discussions would take place.
Mr Mogale interrupted the Chairperson, requesting clarity on an agenda item. He pointed out that there was a scheduled item for the Minister to provide feedback on issues raised by Members, specifically regarding Phil Craig and certain matters that were identified during the oversight visit to the Eastern Cape.
The Chairperson acknowledged that Mr Mogale was correct, as other key issues had overshadowed the matter. He apologised for not addressing the issue earlier due to the length of the meeting. He confirmed that the Minister’s report on Phil Craig’s membership status and related issues would be included in the next session. He said that reports on the oversight visit to the Eastern Cape, including the issues raised regarding public hearings in Port Elizabeth (Gqeberha) and Mthatha, were ready. He promised that these matters would be prioritised at the next meeting.
He also proposed receiving a report earlier to allow Members to review the information beforehand. He thanked the members and staff for their patience and contributions, acknowledging the hard work put into enhancing the committee’s efforts to conduct oversight. He appreciated the collective efforts, which helped shape the Committee’s strategic plans.
Lastly, he emphasised the importance of addressing pressing issues at DHA offices, such as collapsed networks and delays in issuing IDs, and noted the team’s efforts to present solutions.
The meeting was adjourned.
