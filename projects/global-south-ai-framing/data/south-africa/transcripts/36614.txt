Title: DOD ICT Systems requirements, upgrade needs and planned expenditure for technologies along the border; with Deputy Minister
Date: 22 Mar 2023
Committee: 
URL: https://pmg.org.za/committee-meeting/36614/

Meeting Summary
Video
Annual Report of the Activities of the Portfolio Committee (Not made available)
The Portfolio Committee on Defence and Military Veterans met on a virtual platform to receive a briefing from the Department of Defence on ICT Systems requirements, upgrade needs and planned expenditure on the ring-fenced funding for technologies to safeguard the borders.
The Deputy Minister of Defence and Military Veterans stated that the Department of Defence and Military Veterans had been operating with old ICT systems which had to be upgraded but had not had the necessary budget to do so and the ICT system had now become a serious risk for the Department. During the COVID period, money had been diverted from ICT maintenance and upgrade programmes but now the Department had developed a plan to upgrade its technology to address shortcomings. The Department had a strong infrastructure but also some aged bespoke applications, some of which were more than 40 years old and the management of the interfaces was becoming difficult. The technology refresh would include the hardware and software of the switching platforms at the Primary and Secondary Data Centres to optimise the availability of the two data centres. The centres also required a renewal and upgrade of the computing infrastructure. Access portals were being used to interface with other government departments which allowed the Department to ensure that there was no contamination of its own databases. The Department presented a detailed and specific list of changes that were to take place in the ICT environment, but it was acknowledged that there were challenges. The national telecommunication backbone infrastructure programme had enabled the rollout of microwave systems to link military bases across the country and that was proving to be a success.
Members asked the Department to explain how systems would be interfaced with those government departments, such as National Treasury, that had to interface with the Department. With regards to CMIS, who determined all the ICT needs indicated in the briefing? To what extent was the ICT upgrade plan funded in the current financial year as well as the medium-term expenditure framework budget? What were the timelines for the upgrade?
The Committee was briefed by the Department on its planned expenditure of the ring-fenced funding made available by National Treasury specifically for integrated border technologies. The allocations were not confined to the border safeguarding environment, particularly in respect of vehicles as well as technology sensors. The allocation was also for medium airlift aircraft for the Air Force and the refit of Navy vessels and submarines and some money was allocated for the procurement of ambulances and deployable medical equipment. The Army would receive funding in respect of border safeguarding. The DOD had taken a global view and intended to give a comprehensive presentation to the Portfolio Committee, although the emphasis would be on border management and technology. It was the view of the DOD that territorial integrity included the defence of the Republic in the landward (within land borders), air (within airspace borders), maritime (within sea borders) and cyber domains. The presentation contained specific, budgeted figures for the Border Safeguarding Database supported by Artificial Intelligence for Pro-Active Deployments and the Central “War Room” capability for a shared common operating picture. Safeguarding the nation included implementing strategic security and regional development through the involvement of DOD in peace and security capability but also in being able to withstand a total onslaught of the national cyber resilience environment or digital defence digital protection, the protection of South Africa's intangibles. Specific details of amounts allocated, tasks, timeframe, start and finish dates (which had yet to be indicated) and the division responsible for each task were provided.
Members asked if the plan to spend R41 billion in 25 years was feasible in light of the current allocation of R3.3 billion by National Treasury. Would that not exacerbate the problem of the perceived implosion of the capabilities of the Defence Force? Over the timeline, would it lead to an implosion or further deterioration of DOD capabilities or would it turn the tide towards a much more positive scenario? Had the R75 million to R85 million that had been reallocated in the current financial year been re-allocated? Members asked for clarity on the funding for the C130H medium airlift aircraft and whether the cost of chartered aircraft had been included in the budget. Could more funds be allocated to the maintenance of aircraft so that it was not necessary to charter aircraft? Why was there such a long waiting period for an aircraft to be serviced by Denel? The Navy had presented plans for the midlife upgrade of one submarine and one frigate; what about the other two submarines and the three frigates? Had the Department looked at the possibilities of using UAVs (unmanned aerial vehicles) to do long-term reconnaissance and patrols?
It was agreed that the Department would submit responses to the questions in writing.
Meeting report
Opening Remarks
Mr T Mmutle (ANC) informed Members that the Chairperson was currently travelling to Parliament and would join the meeting as soon as he landed in Cape Town. The Chairperson had charged him with the responsibility of chairing the Portfolio Committee meeting until he arrived. He wanted concurrence from Members to do so, noting that he was only standing in for the Chairperson.
The meeting concurred.
The Acting Chairperson welcomed the Deputy Minister of Defence and Military Veterans, Thabang Makwetla, and the Department of Defence (DOD), led by Lt Gen Ramantswana, Chief of Staff.
Remarks by Deputy Minister
Deputy Minister Thabang Makwetla informed the Committee that the Department of Defence had, over time, been operating with ICT systems which required or demanded to be upgraded. That was one area of the Department's work where it had not had the budget to continuously acquire and operate ICTs to make business operations more efficient within the Department. There was a whole host of managerial practices within that Department that were held hostage by the archaic ICTs that it relied on. The Department would share its plans to address that challenge. Members of the Oversight Committee would know the ongoing risk of working that way, as they were aware of the need for cyber security. It was a serious risk in the modern world and the ICT systems that DOD had needed to be properly interference-proof and should guarantee the continuous functioning of the Department. Concerning the presentation on border technologies, Members of the Committee would remember that, towards the end of the previous year, there had been a discussion on capital projects for border security management and there had been unhappiness when it came to the budgets that were dedicated for projects to acquire the appropriate technologies for border security but were utilised for other purposes during the COVID period. Money had to be diverted from ICT maintenance and upgrade programmes. Now the Department had all the details in respect of total technologies for domain awareness and also the technologies for detecting movement and activities on the border. Over and above that, the Department would create a system for oversight of other systems and equipment, such as armoured personnel carrier vehicles that were to be procured to fortify border management.
Presentation on ICT Systems requirements and upgrade needs in the DOD and plans to address shortcomings
Lt Gen Ramantswana, Chief of Staff, DOD, led the team in the absence of the Secretary for Defence. He indicated that Brig Gnl T Ntoni, Command and Management Information System (CMIS), would be making the presentation.
Members had questions regarding the copies of the presentation that they had received as they were different from the one being flighted. After an extended discussion about the various versions, the presenter agreed to inform Members of the changes that had been made before commencing the presentation.
Brig Gnl Ntoni briefed the Committee on the dangers of operating an outdated system. The presentation consisted of a series of detailed slides on ICT infrastructure capability showing the requirements and the current status of the ICT system. He explained that the defining characteristics of ICT in the Department of Defence were that it had a strong infrastructure as well as aged applications. DOD was using bespoke applications, some of them more than 40 years old. The infrastructure had been developed in a different era and environment and while management of the interfaces was becoming difficult, one of the critical matters was the risk attached to using old technology.
He stated that the relocation of the primary data centre to an alternative site, as opposed to both centres being located at the same site, was critical as the Department faced a high risk should a significant event occur at the site, such as an earthquake. The hardware and software of the switching platforms at the Primary and Secondary Data Centres had to be upgraded to optimise the availability of the two data centres. The centres also required a renewal and an upgrade of the old computing infrastructure. It was noted that DOD had to rely on the Government Communication and Information System (GCIS) which managed the procurement processes. That was an added challenge, but DOD was determined to move with speed. The national telecommunication backbone infrastructure programme enabled the successful rollout of microwave systems that linked military bases across the country, including several bases in the Free State and Gauteng. It was also necessary for the Department of Defence to meet national priorities in terms of defence in the Southern African Development Community area.

Discussion
Mr S Marais (DA) regretted not having the presentation earlier so that Members could properly prepare themselves. However, it seemed essentially a more comprehensive presentation than the previous one.
Gnl Ntoni knew the subject very well and that made a great impression on him as at least someone knew what he was talking about. With regards to CMIS, and the whole process, who determined all the ICT needs? He understood that there was obviously a specific DOD budget, the role of DOD administration, and the operational integration system in SANDF and sometimes those were different. He added that the Committee had picked up complaints from some of the Officers Commanding, even on the borders, about system integration. And then there had to be an integration of the human resource systems, such as the DPSA in terms of civilian employees, and a different system for the SA Defence Act employees. All of those systems had to, somehow, be integrated. There were also the procurement requirements, accounting and National Treasury requirements, PFMA requirements and the requirements of the Auditor-General of South Africa.  Could the General explain how all those systems operated within that framework to get full integration? He acknowledged that sometimes there had to be independence of the Defence Force, which was unique.
Mr Marais agreed that ICT was crucial; technology was crucial. If DOD did not change and adapt and keep up with technology, it was falling behind and both adversaries and the people that DOD worked with had the best technology in the world, so it was necessary to invest in that and he was fully in support of that. However, the Committee needed to know how much of the presentation had been fully funded in the budget of the current year, and the following years over the medium-term expenditure framework, and how much did DOD still need to negotiate in future budgets. Members needed to know exactly where the Department stood. And then in terms of timelines as well, because the renewals, the upgrades, ICENET, etc. were all critically important, to what extent were they provided for in the current budgets?
Brig Gnl Ntoni responded to the first question about how the DOD arrived at those requirements. He confirmed that the Department of Defence had a Government Information Technology Officer (GITO) who attended the government GTalk Council and facilitated the necessary cooperation between the various ICT units of departments within the government. The DOD had access to all the government initiatives in that way and they were aligned with the DOD administration which was quite crucial.
He agreed with Mr Marais that how DOD reconciled administration and operations was an important question. They were reconciled through the Plenary Defence Staff Council as well as the Military Council and the rest of the Secretariat Council. In addition, the ICT CMIS (Command and Management Information System) Council met monthly with services and technicians. From those governance structures, ICT and the execution of ICT over the next life cycle were managed, with very vibrant participation of both the administrative side as well as the operational side of the Department of Defence. It was also true that there had to be interoperation between the DoD HR systems and those of the government. Currently, the Department of Defence was not using PERSAL, which the rest of the government was using and therefore there was a need to integrate government departments through the initiative of the demilitarised zone, as well as the secure gateways. However, there had to be cooperation between the various government departments. Although the DOD had not modernised the legacy systems, it was able to update the necessary information, frame it and structure it through a DMC (access portal), but the other departments, such as the Department of Public Service and Administration (DPSA) had to be able to access certain DOD data. The same applied to National Treasury. DOD was able to access the database of the National Treasury but did not directly connect the systems precisely because it wanted, as much as possible, to minimize the vulnerability of cyber-attacks on DOD infrastructure. DOD would take the information and transfer it after it had been cleaned up. Brig Gnl Ntoni stated that through those kinds of initiatives, especially the modernisation of existing data systems, he believed that there would be a way forward.
Brig Gnl Ntoni was quite encouraged that the Members of the Committee acknowledged the necessity to invest in ICT. He addressed the question of how it was going to be funded. It was quite true that a number of the initiatives that Mr Marais had referred to were funded within the current budget of the Department of Defence. He highlighted them for the Oversight Committee. The first crucial one that was currently not funded was the Integrated Defence Enterprise System (IDES) initiative. That was the entire organisation of the legacy systems of the Department of Defence, starting from its infrastructure to its applications and business intelligence. He had addressed that in the first slide. DOD had been able to stabilise by hosting computing platforms and the Department was intervening in terms of enhancing business continuity. Over the MTEF, the Department of Defence would need R900 million to continue with the necessary acquisition of the skill and resources as well as to develop the necessary specifications and reverse engineering and engineering for that capability going forward. That R900 million would also provide the necessary software for the enterprise resource planning systems that would replace the old systems in the Department of Defence. The Members of the Committee would recall that they had also talked about the NTBI that the Department of Defence had, despite its limited budget, been able to move with speed in terms of rolling out the microwave networks that would give higher capacity to all the transactions that the Department of Defence needed to do, as well as the security management thereof. For that programme to be able to move forward over the MTEF, DOD would need a further injection of R700 million. The Department had established network links through the microwave roll-out, establishing 79 links. There were still approximately 200 more links that needed to be made to cover the entire network, at which point the network of the Department of Defence would be cyber resilient, as was required by the government. He added that further investments were required for the ICT Academy but calculations were still being done as to how much that was going to cost going forward.
The intention of the DOD was to optimise the current existing capabilities that were distributed across the services, and then add further systems to be able to pull it up to establish the necessary capability. The figures were still being calculated. He summarised: the R900 million was for the IDES programme and the R700 million was for the continuation and completion of the backbone infrastructure project.
The Acting Chairperson commended the developments and the planning.  Most importantly, there was still much that needed to be invested as indicated and there was still much that had to be done in terms of technology to ensure that the Department in its totality was at the level where it would be able to say with confidence that it had reached a level where it had closed the gap. The Auditor-General always said some of the problems could easily be addressed if technological systems were in place as opposed to running things in the current manner.  If energies and resources were invested to address the challenges in those areas, there would be a better outcome and DOD would be more efficient and effective going forward.
Mr Marais was pleased to see that DoD was in a better position financially than he initially thought. He requested that the items in the presentation be incorporated into the quarterly presentations so that the Committee could be kept abreast of developments.
The Acting Chairperson stated that the Committee simply had to put it in the programme.
Gnl Ramantswana agreed to update the Portfolio Committee quarterly on progress concerning the roll-out of the ICT initiatives.
The Deputy Minister was not available for concluding remarks as he had earlier indicated his need to leave early to travel to Parliament.
Presentation by the DOD on the planned expenditure of the ring-fenced funding for integrated border technologies
Major General Ngcobo, Deputy Chief of Staff, stated that in the earmarked MTEF budget from the National Treasury, there were some implications and obligations imposed on the Department of Defence and the National Defence Force. The allocations were not confined to the border safeguarding environment, particularly in respect of vehicles as well as technology sensors. The allocation was also for medium airlift aircraft for the air force and the refit of Navy vessels and submarines. Some money was allocated for the procurement of ambulances and deployable medical equipment as well as for the SA army in respect of border safeguarding. So the DOD had taken a global view and intended to give a comprehensive presentation to the Portfolio Committee, although the emphasis would be on border management and technology. It was the view of the DOD that territorial integrity included the defence of the Republic in the landward (within land borders), air (within airspace borders), maritime (within sea borders) and cyber domains. The presentation contained specific, budgeted figures for the Border Safeguarding Database supported by Artificial Intelligence for Pro-Active Deployments and the Central “War Room” capability for a shared common operating picture.
Safeguarding the nation included implementing strategic security and regional development through the involvement of DOD in peace and security capability but also in being able to withstand a total onslaught of the national cyber resilience environment or digital defence digital protection, the protection of South Africa's intangibles.
The General provided specific details of amounts allocated, tasks, timeframe, start and finish dates (which had yet to be indicated) and the resource person or division responsible for each task.
(See Presentation)
Discussion
Mr Marais found the presentation to be thorough but it raised more questions than answers. It was clear that the requirements were more expensive than National Treasury realised and probably more than the economy could afford at the moment. The required amount of R41 billion as opposed to the R3.3 billion allocated by National Treasury was significant. He asked if DOD could afford R41 billion within 25 years as indicated, in other words, over a couple of medium terms expenditure frameworks. Would that not exacerbate the problem of the perceived implosion of the capabilities of the Defence Force? The answer to that was important because there was a saying, “Pennywise but pound foolish”. It did not help to say that it could be done but then they could not get the money when the time came as there would be an implosion of the capabilities of the Defence Force, which could not be allowed. To what extent was that realistic? Over the timeline, would it lead to an implosion or further deterioration of DOD capabilities or would it turn the tide towards a much more positive scenario?
Mr Marais recalled that the allocation from the National Treasury indicated specifically that it was “to procure equipment and technology to aid safeguarding of borders”. The focus was specifically on technology, and in the past, money had been allocated for technology, but not used for technology. These funds had to be correctly used. Some R75 million to R85 million had been reallocated in terms of the current financial year and that was probably why National Treasury had not allocated additional money for the 2023/24 financial year; National Treasury had provided an extra R500 million for 2024/25 and R200 million extra for 2025/26. How had that R75 million been reallocated? Where was that done? He did not see that in the calculations.
Concerning the requirements set out in slide five, R1.56 billion for 2023/24 and then R1 billion and R745 000 over the medium term, he asked if all those funds were in Account Two or were some of them in Account One. The Committee knew that funds in Account One fell away at the end of the financial year if not spent. How much of that R1.56 billion was in Account Two? That would determine what had to be done first. On the requirements given in slides nine and ten, were those new APCs (armoured personnel carriers)? There were six warehouses all over the country containing APCs and several had been deployed to different parts of the country recently. How many of those in long-term storage could be utilised on the border rather than buying new APCs? Could DOD upgrade or maintain the current ones by utilising defence industry players like the military veterans of OTT Technologies to do the maintenance and upgrades?  To what extent had that been incorporated, or even considered, rather than buying new ones as that would be a lot less expensive?
Mr Marais noted that on slide five, the C130 was indicated at a cost of R1 billion for the 2023/24 financial year. Was that in the 02 funds? Was that why on slide 14, only R455 million was earmarked for the 2023/24 financial year? Could the General confirm that the R455 million plus minus R545 million was in Account 02 for the next financial year? Would that be added to that R500 million DOD would get in the next financial year?  He knew that the Minister of Defence had indicated in a parliamentary question that the DOD would make use of the offer of the Americans for the C130H and that DOD was busy with the process. Did the R900 million additional requirement take into account the cost of chartering aircraft at the moment? The Ilyushins chartered in December 2022, January and February 2023, had cost between R300 and R500 million. If DOD could allocate some of that money towards the maintenance program of the C130s, or the acquisition of the two C130H aircraft, then DOD could pay off the aircraft within one or two years, if there was no need to charter. But if DOD did not have at least three or four C130s, then there was no option but to charter at the additional cost. Somewhere along the line, all those funds should be put into one pot.
He noted that the presenter referred to aviation inflation. He had not heard the term “aviation inflation” for a long time and he liked to hear that because his view was that, for a long time, aviation inflation had been between 12 and 20% per year. So, if DOD did not have an annual increase in allocation of between 12% and 20%, then it was falling behind, which meant having to compromise and a process of reallocations or reprioritisation would need to take place.
Concerning the Denel situation, the Committee was going to Denel Aeronautics the following week. Hopefully, the visit would touch on the capability of Denel to also support the maintenance of the C 130s. Mr Marais questioned why the waiting bay periods were so long. It seemed to be at least six months. Why could the aircraft not go for major services sooner? If there was proper coordination between the Air Force, Armscor and Denel, surely that period could be shortened? That had to be the objective. He would raise it with Denel.
Mr Marais noted that the Navy had presented plans for the upgrade of one submarine and one frigate between December 22 and March 25. What about the other two submarines? What about the other three frigates? They were all in dire need of midlife upgrades because they were critically important in terms of maritime security. Lastly, he asked about the long-term capabilities and requirements. Had DOD looked at 35 Squadron capabilities and the need for capabilities and maybe one option was to look at UAVs (unmanned aerial vehicles) to do long-term reconnaissance and patrols. That had not been touched on and that was a critical part of maritime security.
The Chairperson arrived at the meeting. He indicated that he had received a call from Gnl Ramantswana indicating that the DOD had lost its connection owing to load shedding in the DOD building from which the Generals were speaking. They were attempting to reconnect.
Mr Marais suggested that the questions be sent to DOD for a response in writing.
Mr Mmutle informed the Chairperson that it had been decided that DOD would report quarterly on the implementation of the procurement of resources as indicated in the presentation. He wanted DOD to prioritise requirements because obviously, the allocated resources would not cover its requirements, but it needed to make a commitment as to what it would do with the available resources. That would assist with oversight and checking on the implementation and expenditure of priorities as opposed to the holistic approach that the DOD had taken.
The Chairperson suggested that the Committee would want the DOD to report on the ring-fenced funds separately; maybe together with the other programmes in the Department, but Members needed to keep track of the ring-fenced expenditure. He understood that DOD was in the process of developing a business plan that would look at how the money would be expended on the identified projects. When the Department was ready, the officials would have to make a presentation on the business plan so that the Committee could monitor the progress. Members did not want to be told in June that DOD still had not committed the funds - or, worse than that, to learn at the end of the financial year the money was not spent. Money had to be spent in the year allocated and the only time the Committee could accept a rollover was when the money had already been committed and, for genuine reasons, it could not be spent in the financial year. If the Committee did not keep track, Treasury would take the money back or allocate it as if it were new money. The Department had to move quickly on the matter.
The Chairperson added that the Committee had also asked Armscor to synchronise the requirements arising from the special allocation because it had been ring-fenced for certain purposes and could not be reallocated to anything other than what it had been budgeted for.
Mr Marais added that it was important to know whether it was folio one or folio two funds. In that way, the Committee would know whether the money could be rolled over or not. Folio one was the funding that DOD would lose if not spent in the financial year but folio two funds could be rolled over. The Committee could then be alerted to correct expenditure patterns.
The Committee Secretary stated that the DoD was back online but he was concerned about the timeframe as there was a sitting in the House that afternoon.
The Chairperson welcomed the DoD back on the platform but indicated that questions from the Members would be sent to DOD for a response in writing. The Secretary would also inform them about the future engagement in respect of the funding.
Lt Gen Ramantswana agreed to present the responses in writing.
The Chairperson thanked the Generals for their engagement with the Committee.
Annual Report of the Activities of the Portfolio Committee
Dr Wilhelm Janse van Rensburg, Committee Researcher, informed the Committee that he had been provided with a template according to which the Committee had to indicate activities during the year. The draft had been distributed to Members. It was an annual report of the activities of the Committee for the year January to December 2022. He took the Committee through the draft report.
Proposed by Mr Marais and seconded by Mr Mmutle, the report was adopted by the Committee without amendment or objections.
Consideration of Minutes
The Minutes of 15 March 2023 were presented and there being no amendments, the Committee adopted the minutes without objections.
Closing Remarks
It was agreed to give the DOD two weeks to respond to the questions in writing.
The meeting was adjourned.
