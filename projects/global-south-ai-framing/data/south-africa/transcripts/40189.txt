Meeting Summary
The Portfolio Committee on Tourism was briefed by the Department of Tourism and South African Tourism on preparations for South Africa's hosting of the G20 Summit in 2025 and other strategic international tourism initiatives. The Committee also reviewed its first term programme and planned oversight visits for the year.
The Department of Tourism outlined South Africa’s G20 Tourism Work Programme, emphasising the economic and strategic importance of hosting the Summit. The theme of South Africa’s G20 Presidency was Solidarity, Equality, and Sustainable Development. It aligned with four key tourism priorities: AI-driven innovation for travel startups and small, medium and micro enterprises (SMMEs), tourism financing and investment, air connectivity improvements, and building resilience for sustainable tourism growth. South Africa would host three G20 Tourism Working Group meetings and a G20 Tourism Ministers’ Meeting in September 2025 in Mpumalanga. The Department assured the Committee that SMMEs and black-owned businesses would have opportunities to benefit from the economic activities linked to the Summit.
South African Tourism presented its global tourism promotion strategy, which focuses on leveraging international trade platforms, expanding air access, and supporting SMMEs. It included participation in Meetings Africa, ITB Berlin, Arabian Travel Market, and WTM London to strengthen South Africa’s global tourism profile. The Route Development Strategy, approved in December 2024, aimed to improve air connectivity and attract more international tourists. South African Tourism also highlighted its commitment to enhancing the participation of small businesses through market access initiatives and affordable exhibition opportunities.
Committee Members raised concerns about ensuring tangible economic benefits for SMMEs and black-owned enterprises, as well as the effectiveness of implementation and monitoring mechanisms for the G20 tourism agenda. The Committee sought clarity on South African Tourism’s decision to keep Africa’s Travel Indaba exclusive to Durban and questioned whether other provinces would have opportunities to host similar events. Challenges in air connectivity and the impact of aviation policies on tourism growth were also discussed.
The Committee welcomed the progress on G20 preparations but emphasised the need for clear implementation plans, interdepartmental collaboration, and measurable outcomes. The Minister committed to providing further updates on budget allocations, procurement plans for SMMEs, and the anticipated economic impact of the G20 tourism initiatives.
Following the briefings, the Committee reviewed its 2025 work programme, which included a joint meeting on astro-tourism with the Portfolio Committee on Science and Technology (18 February) and participation in Meetings Africa (24–26 February) in Sandton, where engagements with South African Tourism, the Department of Tourism, and the newly appointed board of the Tourism Transformation Council were scheduled. Additional briefings with Airports Company South Africa, the Airlines Association of Southern Africa, and the Tourism Grading Council were planned, along with joint oversight visits to key tourism sites in collaboration with other portfolio committees.

The Chairperson welcomed attendees to the first Portfolio Committee meeting of 2025. She accepted apologies from Mr SR Moodley (MKP), who was unable to attend due to a doctor's appointment, and the Director-General (DG) of the Department of Tourism, Mr Victor Vele, who was unavailable due to official departmental business. Minister’s introduction
Ms Patricia de Lille, Minister of Tourism, reported that the coordination of activities for the G20 was well underway and being managed through established structures at a national level. The Department of International Relations and Cooperation (DIRCO) was leading this process, with an inter-ministerial committee overseeing key aspects of planning. The inter-ministerial committee met every two weeks and reported directly to Cabinet on both operational and substantive matters related to the G20.
She highlighted that South Africa’s G20 Presidency provided a valuable opportunity to showcase the country as a premier global travel destination. She emphasised that the Department, alongside SAT and the Tourism Business Council of South Africa (TBCSA), was actively working to leverage the G20 Summit to encourage delegates to extend their stay and return for future visits.
She observed that South Africa was the first African country to host the G20, making this a historic milestone. Given the significance of this event, the government was working closely with all stakeholders to ensure smooth execution. The Department, SAT, TBCSA, and the Mpumalanga Provincial Government were meeting weekly to coordinate their efforts, particularly as Mpumalanga would serve as the host province for the G20 Ministerial Meeting.
She reiterated the importance of collaboration between various levels of government, the private sector, and other stakeholders to ensure that South Africa maximised the economic and tourism benefits of hosting the G20 Summit.
Department of Tourism briefing on pr
eparations for the G20 Tourism Work Programme
Ms Aneme Malan, Deputy Director-General (DDG): Tourism Research, Policy and International Relations, Department of Tourism, outlined South Africa’s preparations for its Presidency of G20 in 2025, focusing on how tourism would play a pivotal role in economic growth, sustainable development, and international cooperation.
Overview of South Africa’s 2025 G20 Presidency
South Africa was the first African country to host the G20. The theme of its Presidency would be “Solidarity, Equality, and Sustainable Development.” In addition, South Africa would aim to advance the African Union’s Agenda 2063 and support the United Nations (UN) Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs).
The G20
Tourism
Working Group
The Working Group served as a forum for growth and sustainable tourism development by promoting dialogue, exchange of knowledge and best practices. Its objectives were to:
- Position tourism as a driver of economic, social, and environmental change;
- Support economic recovery and transition to a green economy; and
- Develop tourism policies that enhance sustainable investment and economic development.
The Department of Tourism would host three G20 Tourism Working Group Meetings and a G20 Tourism Ministers’ Meeting in September 2025 in Mpumalanga, alongside a Tourism Financing and Investment Summit.
South Africa’s Four Tourism Priorities for the G20
- People-Centred Artificial Intelligence (AI) and Innovation in Tourism: Developing an action plan to enhance travel startups and small, medium and micro enterprises (SMMEs) through AI and digital innovation;
- Tourism Financing and Investment: Hosting a summit on partnerships for sustainable tourism investment to ensure funding for tourism development;
- Air Connectivity for Seamless Travel: Developing a G20 Tourism Report on air connectivity to improve international travel links; and
- Enhanced Resilience for Sustainable Tourism: Creating an action plan for building inclusive and resilient tourism sectors.
Promotion of South African Tourism During the G20
- All nine provinces would be showcased on the G20 website to highlight South Africa’s diverse tourism offerings;
- Tourism promotions would be conducted at all major G20 meetings to attract investment and increase visitor numbers; and
- Special tourism packages would be created for G20 delegates and visitors.
In conclusion, the Department of Tourism would use South Africa's G20 Presidency as an opportunity to position the country as a global tourism leader. The tourism workstream aligned with national priorities, supported SMME development, and promoted economic resilience. The government’s approach ensured that the benefits of the G20 would extend beyond the Summit and contribute to long-term economic growth.

Strategic Platforms
Ms Nombulelo Guliwe, Chief Executive Officer (CEO), SAT, outlined SAT's strategy for leveraging international business and leisure platforms to position South Africa as a premier global destination.
Strategic Vision and Priorities
Three “visa enablers” would be used to boost international tourism:
- Trusted Tour Operator Scheme (TTOS) (for India and China)
- Electronic Travel Authorisation (ETA)
- Digital Nomad Visa
In December 2024, Cabinet approved the Route Development Strategy to increase international air arrivals to South Africa. SAT was establishing a Special Purpose Vehicle to expand international air travel to South Africa. The key focus markets for the next financial year would be India, China, Africa, and Southeast Asia.
Business and Leisure Tourism Platforms
SAT’s business and leisure tourism strategy revolved around major international events such as:
- Meetings Africa (February 2025)
- ITB Berlin (March 2025)
- Arabian Travel Market (April–May 2025)
- Africa’s Travel Indaba (May 2025)
- IMEX Frankfurt (May 2025)
- IMEX America (October 2025)
- WTM London (November 2025)
- IBTM World (November 2025)
SME Integration and Economic Impact
Emphasis was placed on supporting SMMEs by ensuring their participation in trade fairs and international business networking. Efforts included affordable exhibition spaces, matchmaking programmes, and increased funding for SMMEs to access international markets.
In conclusion, SAT was focused on expanding South Africa’s global tourism footprint through strategic participation in international trade and business events. Their efforts aimed to maximise economic impact, drive inclusivity, and strengthen South Africa's competitive edge in global tourism markets.

Responses by the Minister
Minister de Lille explained that the Department of Tourism and SAT were finalising their Medium Term Development Plan (MTDP) for the next five years and ensuring its alignment with the Seventh Administration’s three key priorities. The MTDP was informed by multiple strategic frameworks, including the Tourism White Paper, the National Tourism Master Plan, the National Tourism Sector Strategy (NTSS), and the National Development Plan (NDP). She explained that a Cabinet leKgotla had been held several weeks before, at which the Seventh Administration's three key priorities were determined, namely 1) Driving inclusive growth and job creation, 2) Reducing poverty and tackling the high cost of living, and 3) Building a capable, ethical, and developmental state. This was followed by the State of the Nation Address (SONA), in which President Cyril Ramaphosa formally announced the government’s commitments. Following these developments, all government departments, including Tourism, were in the process of finalising their five-year MTDPs and corresponding budgets. She cautioned against expecting significant budget increases. Most departments, including hers, were likely to receive only a Consumer Price Index (CPI) or inflation-adjusted budget increase. She added that the official budget would only be announced by the Minister of Finance on 19 February 2025.
She reported that she, the Deputy Minister, and the DG had a meeting scheduled for later in the week to map out the Department’s strategic direction for the next five years. Once a draft plan was ready, she would present the Annual Performance Plan (APP) and the National Tourism Transformation Plan (NTTP) to Parliament, in line with legislative requirements. Many aspects remained work in progress, but the Department was actively engaged in designing the next phase of tourism sector development rather than merely replicating previous administration policies.
She explained that Cabinet had mandated that all 132 business events linked to the G20 Summit must be spread across all nine provinces. The full schedule of events was available on the G20 Summit website. The Department of Finance had already hosted its G20 event on 9–10 December 2024, during which all Reserve Bank governors from G20 member states had convened in South Africa. Additionally, on 20–21 February 2025, the Minister of International Relations and Cooperation would host the G20 Foreign Affairs Ministers’ Meeting. The full G20 event calendar outlined when and where each ministerial meeting will take place.
For the tourism sector, Mpumalanga was the official host province for the G20 Tourism Ministers’ Meeting. In preparation, she had personally convened a meeting with Mpumalanga Premier Mr M Ndlovu, the Head of Department (HOD), and the MEC for Tourism. Together, they had established an operational committee to oversee planning and logistics for the event. All official gifts for the Tourism Ministers’ Meeting delegates would be sourced exclusively from Mpumalanga-based businesses. The province had already showcased examples of high-quality crafts and products inspired by Ndebele culture, demonstrating the region’s rich artistic heritage. Furthermore, the provincial government would engage local cultural groups to provide a traditional welcome at the airport for visiting delegates.
Regarding accommodation and event venues, she explained that DIRCO had set minimum requirements for hotel and conference venues that all host provinces must comply with. The Mpumalanga government had identified three hotel options that met these standards, and a site inspection would take place in two weeks to ensure full compliance with health, safety, and security regulations. She offered to share the full list of DIRCO’s venue requirements with the Committee.
She assured the Committee that the G20 would not be an isolated, elite event, but rather a nationwide initiative aimed at broad community engagement. President Ramaphosa was leading an outreach programme to raise awareness about the G20 and its benefits for ordinary South Africans. This initiative involved every Minister and MEC hosting outreach activities in their designated provinces, focusing on informing local communities about the opportunities that arose from the G20 and the long-term value it provided.
She acknowledged that Members of Parliament had a crucial responsibility in engaging with communities and raising awareness about the benefits of the G20 Presidency. She encouraged Members to inform their constituencies about how the G20 could foster economic growth, tourism development, and job creation. She reiterated President Ramaphosa’s call that “we must leave no one behind.”
She committed to ensuring that G20 tourism-related events would specifically focus on empowering SMMEs and black-owned businesses in Mpumalanga. Every Minister, in whichever province they are hosting their ministerial meetings, must follow this principle, ensuring that economic opportunities are equitably distributed across communities.
She elaborated on inter-ministerial coordination, stating that the Department of Tourism was working closely with DIRCO, SAT, and the TBCSA to align their efforts. These partners met weekly to discuss progress and ensure all planning phases were executed efficiently.
She reaffirmed her willingness to return to the Portfolio Committee to provide a full operational report on G20 preparations, including detailed timelines, budget allocations, and economic impact assessments. South Africa’s G20 Presidency represented a historic opportunity for the country and the African continent, and every effort was being made to maximise its long-term benefits for the tourism sector and the broader economy.
She reported that five African regional organisations, including the AU and the Economic Community of West African States (ECOWAS), were actively involved in the planning and discussions for the G20 tourism workstream. She encouraged members to visit the G20 website for a complete list of invited regional bodies.
On the G20 tourism hackathon, she explained that the initiative had been conceptualised to encourage youth participation in tourism innovation. The Department was currently in the process of selecting two or three South African universities to assist in designing the hackathon framework. This concept was still under development, and final decisions had not yet been made regarding the competition format, assessment criteria, or the nature of the projects participants would be tasked with. However, the overall objective was to engage young minds in proposing creative solutions for tourism development. She noted the suggestion to extend the hackathon to high school students and said she would raise it with the G20 Tourism Working Group to determine its feasibility. A competition element would likely be included, with prizes awarded to the university that developed the best tourism-related solution.
Regarding the upcoming Tourism Investment Summit, she said that the Department had been actively engaging all provinces to identify viable tourism investment projects. So far, 123 project proposals have been received, most of which focused on infrastructure development, destination marketing, hotels, and guesthouses. To be approved, projects had to undergo bankability assessments, feasibility studies, and environmental impact evaluations. Of the 123 proposals, only 17 currently met the necessary standards for investment readiness to be considered viable. To ensure a rigorous selection process, the Department had partnered with Infrastructure South Africa, which was assisting in evaluating and refining the projects to ensure they met international investment requirements. Only fully bankable projects would be included in the investment book for the Summit. The deadline for finalising this investment portfolio was set for May 2025, and the Department was aiming to officially launch the roadmap to the investment conference at the Africa Travel Indaba. Additionally, the Department would develop investment guidelines to provide clear direction on project preparation and financing models.
She explained that the Department had recently reviewed a draft budget for all aspects of the G20 tourism meetings, including event logistics, security, transport, and hospitality. Approximately 225 delegates, including tourism ministers and support teams, were expected to attend the G20 Tourism Ministerial Meeting. However, when including all associated personnel, the total number of attendees would be closer to 400. National Treasury had issued protocols allowing the Department to seek sponsorships and partnerships from the private sector. If the Department estimated that R20 million was required but could only reprioritise R10 million from its 2025/2026 budget, it was permitted to seek external sponsorships to cover the shortfall. She assured the Committee that Treasury and DIRCO had provided a structured framework for securing sponsorships, to ensure transparency and compliance with financial regulations. However, this process was still under development and formed part of the broader operational plan.
She asked SAT to provide a detailed update on the readiness and deployment of the projection dome. She agreed that the Dome was a subject of concern and she acknowledged the need for accountability in ensuring it was fully operational for major tourism events, including G20 meetings. She committed to receiving clarity from SAT on whether the Dome would be ready for the G20 Tourism Ministers’ Meeting in Mpumalanga, and if not, what the delays were and when it would be fully functional.
On the question of air connectivity and route development, she noted that Cabinet had approved the Route Development Strategy on 4 December 2024. She offered to share the full document with the Portfolio Committee. Key features of the newly approved strategy included the introduction of a Special Purpose Vehicle (SPV) to oversee route development for the entire country. Currently, route development is fragmented, with provinces pursuing their own separate initiatives. The SPV would centralise and coordinate these efforts to enhance South Africa’s overall air connectivity. The Department had already allocated R6.5 million as an initial investment into the SPV, and discussions with the private sector to secure additional funding and strategic partnerships were ongoing.
She responded directly to Ms Mashabela’s allegations about wrongdoing at SAT, emphasising the importance of evidence-based accountability. She said that if there was any wrongdoing, even by herself as Minister, it was a legal obligation to report it to law enforcement. She invited the Committee and any stakeholders with evidence to bring it forward. She acknowledged that there was ongoing backlash from companies that had previously benefited from exclusive tenders from SAT, but she stressed that in compliance with supply chain management policies, all tenders had to go through competitive bidding processes, which SAT had strictly followed. She recalled that in December 2024, she had followed up on procurement issues after SAT had appointed a forensic investigator to scrutinise irregularities. At that time, the SAT Board had refused to approve a particular tender, which had led to its cancellation. Following this, a forensic investigation was launched to examine the circumstances surrounding the tender process. The companies affected by the tender cancellation were the same ones now publicly attacking SAT and the Ministry. These companies had taken their grievances to the media in an attempt to portray her and the SAT CEO as obstructing procurement.
She warned the media against being manipulated by bidders with vested interests. She urged MPs to request concrete evidence from these service providers instead of relying on allegations. She assured the Portfolio Committee that the Department remained fully transparent and was willing to provide documentation for oversight purposes. She had already instructed the SAT Board to take decisive action based on the forensic report. She pledged to share it with the Committee once finalised. Finally, she explained that when a tender was cancelled, alternative arrangements were made to ensure continuity in services, and she reassured the Committee that preparations for Meetings Africa and the Durban Travel Indaba were proceeding as planned.

Responses by the DT
Ms Malan responded to several outstanding questions, beginning with Ms Ligaraba's question on regional organisations invited to the G20 discussions. Besides the SADC, four other regional economic communities had been invited to participate: the East African Community (EAC), the Common Market for Eastern and Southern Africa (COMESA), ECOWAS, and the Economic Community of Central African States (ECCAS). South Africa’s engagement with these organisations aligned with its commitments to the AU’s Agenda 2063 and the SADC Tourism Programme, ensuring that regional integration remained a priority in all G20 engagements.
In response to Ms Linde’s suggestion to expand the Hackathon Initiative to include high schools, she reiterated that the Minister had committed to taking this proposal back to the working Group. The working group was currently in the process of identifying two or three universities that could lead the initiative.
Regarding provincial participation and logistics, she explained that the G20 Summit operational planning was guided by an inter-ministerial committee (IMC) that reported directly to Cabinet. Each ministerial session was assigned to a specific province, with Mpumalanga selected as the host for the Tourism Ministerial Meeting. She reassured the Committee that this decision had been based on logistical assessments and the ability of the province to meet the hospitality and security requirements set out by DIRCO and National Treasury. The G20 Logistics Working Group, chaired at the DG level, was tasked with ensuring compliance with minimum accommodation and conference venue requirements. Mpumalanga’s contribution to the G20 preparations included providing cultural gifts for delegates, coordinating airport welcome ceremonies with local cultural groups, identifying black-owned businesses for service provision, with a focus on women-owned enterprises, and proposing accommodation options that adhered to DIRCO’s prescribed hospitality standards.
She confirmed that the Sustainability Village concept, which had been successfully implemented at Meetings Africa and Africa’s Travel Indaba, was being incorporated into the G20 Summit planning. This initiative would allow delegates to purchase goods from SMMEs using vouchers, ensuring direct financial benefit to small businesses. A free grading programme had also been introduced as part of the preparations, which would allow eligible tourism establishments to receive formal grading at no cost.
On the question of interdepartmental coordination, she affirmed that the Department was actively working with various government bodies. For example, progress had been made with the Department of Home Affairs on visa facilitation, including the launch of the Trusted Tour Operators Scheme on 12 February 2025, which would streamline visa approvals for reputable tour operators. Collaboration with the Department of Transport was also central to the Route Development Strategy.
Lastly, she assured the Committee that the Tourism Master Plan and the White Paper were in the implementation phase, with clear action points, timelines, and accountability mechanisms. She welcomed the Committee's continued oversight in monitoring progress on these initiatives.
Responses by SAT
Ms Guliwe assured the Committee that SAT had followed a fair, transparent, and competitive bidding process when selecting the host city for Africa’s Travel Indaba and Meetings Africa. There was no permanent home for the Travel Indaba, as it was awarded through a five-year contract, after which the opportunity to host was opened to all provinces through a competitive process. This ensures that different regions benefit from hosting major industry events.
Regarding the support of SMMEs, she explained that while SAT took SMMEs to strategic platforms, the Tourism Incentive Programme, which provided financial support for these businesses, was administered by the Department of Tourism. SAT worked closely with the Department and received direct support from the DG to ensure that SMMEs received financial assistance. When SAT returned to the Portfolio Committee to present its performance report, it would be able to show a clear link between the Department’s financial support and SAT’s efforts to integrate SMMEs into global markets.
On the issue of grading facilities, raised by Ms Mchunu, she confirmed that quality assurance is a key mandate for SAT, as it directly impacts visitor experience and confidence in South African tourism products. In addition to the free grading initiative under the Route to G20 Programme, SAT was also running a Basic Quality Verification Programme, which assisted establishments by identifying the improvements needed for them to qualify for grading. Just last week, Deputy Minister Maggie Sotyu led a session to encourage establishments to take advantage of the free grading opportunity.
On market readiness of SMMEs, she stressed that SMME participation at global trade shows must be meaningful and productive. SAT did not merely fund SMMEs to attend events but also actively prepared them through a three-year incubation programme, which trained businesses on how to effectively engage with industry players in the market. It included pre-departure training sessions to ensure that participants understood market expectations before travelling to international platforms.
Addressing concerns regarding SAT’s financial performance and issues raised by the Auditor-General, she assured the Committee that closing outstanding financial and governance issues remains a priority. As the financial year was in its final quarter, significant work had already been undertaken to address audit findings and strengthen SAT’s governance frameworks. She committed to providing a full update when SAT returned later in the month to account for Quarter 3 performance.
Turning to Ms Mashabela's concerns about marketing tools, particularly the Dome, she confirmed that SAT's G20 marketing work stream was evaluating all available promotional tools, including the Dome. She noted that leveraging technology to enhance South Africa's tourism visibility was a priority, and any resource that could positively showcase the country during the G20 events would be utilised.
Finally, she assured the Committee that for each strategic platform SAT participated in, a comprehensive, detailed implementation plan was developed in advance. These plans guided execution and allowed SAT to measure the return on investment from participation in global trade shows and business forums.
The Chairperson sought clarification on the figures presented on slides 9 and 10 of the SAT presentation, specifically regarding the quoted figures for exhibitors’ average expenditure and return on investment for ITB Berlin and IMEX Frankfurt in 2024. Should these figures be interpreted as the overall benefit to Berlin and Frankfurt as the host cities, or to Germany as the host country?
Ms Guliwe explained that the economic benefit accrued to both Germany as the host nation and the participating exhibitors from various countries. She drew a comparison to Meetings Africa, which was hosted in South Africa. In 2023, the event generated R420 million in economic activity and created 770 jobs. Trade shows offered dual benefits, one for the individual businesses marketing their services and another for the host country, as exhibitors and visitors contributed to the local economy.
Ms Mashabela asked for a detailed report from SAT on the list of service providers, outstanding payments to suppliers, and the reasons for delayed payments.
Minister de Lille assured her that the requested information would be included in the Quarter 3 performance report, which would be presented to the Portfolio Committee on 25 February 2025.
Ms Maggie Sotyu, Deputy Minister of Tourism, added that requests such as that made by Ms Mashabela should be formally processed through the Chairperson, who would then submit them to the Minister for an official response.
Committee business: 2025 Committee Programme
The Committee Secretary highlighted some upcoming engagements, including a joint meeting with the Portfolio Committee on Science, Technology, and Innovation on 18 February regarding the astro-tourism Strategy. The week of 24–26 February was scheduled for Meetings Africa in Sandton, and arrangements had been made for discussions with SAT and the Department of Tourism to review their third-quarter performance report. A meeting with the newly appointed board of the Tourism Transformation Council was also planned.
Due to regular parliamentary plenaries at 10am on Tuesdays, fewer meeting dates were available, and certain matters had to be removed from the agenda or postponed. Outstanding items included a briefing by the Department of Tourism on its plans to empower SMMEs and promote rural development and job creation, which would now be incorporated into the Department’s Strategic Plan presentation, at the Minister’s request.
Briefings were also required from the Airports Company South Africa and the Airlines Association of Southern Africa to discuss global trends and challenges in air transport and their impact on tourism. The Tourism Grading Council of South Africa and the South African National Convention Bureau had yet to meet with the Committee, and it was suggested that these briefings be scheduled during Parliament’s two-week oversight period, with one week dedicated to oversight visits and the other to meetings.
On the Strategic Planning Workshop, he noted that Parliament had not yet finalised its strategic plan for the financial year, which could delay the Committee’s own strategic planning. The workshop had originally been planned for the beginning of the term but had already been postponed, and there was concern that it might not take place as expected.
Ms Linde asked whether Members were required to attend all three days of Meetings Africa.
Ms Mchunu supported the Committee’s planned engagements but suggested adding joint meetings with additional portfolio committees as the country prepared for G20 tourism events. She recommended engaging the Departments of Police, Home Affairs, Forestry, Fisheries and Environment, and Transport, given their relevance to tourism infrastructure and logistics.
Ms Ismail suggested coordinating oversight visits with other portfolio committees. She cited Kruger National Park as an example of a tourism site that fell under the Department of Forestry, Fisheries, and Environment, noting that a joint oversight visit would allow for better collaboration rather than working in silos.
The Chairperson agreed, adding that the Department of Sport, Arts, and Culture, as well as the Department of Small Business Development, should also be included. The Committee should prioritise joint meetings throughout the year, with the goal of concluding them before December, despite international visitors usually arriving in September.
Ms Mchunu suggested a joint meeting with the Standing Committee on Appropriations to address budgetary concerns regarding tourism funding, noting that the tourism sector frequently raised complaints about financial constraints.
The Chairperson confirmed that additional meeting details would be shared by the Committee Secretary, particularly regarding the 24–26 February sessions. She asked whether the Committee’s application for oversight visits had been submitted.
The Committee Secretary confirmed that the application had been submitted and that members would be updated as matters progressed.
Mr Maeco proposed that the Committee schedule meetings during oversight visits to maximise efficiency.
The Chairperson agreed.
The meeting was adjourned.
