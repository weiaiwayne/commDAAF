Western Cape Government Departments Annual Report 2024/25
The Standing Committee on Public Accounts (SCOPA) meeting was convened to review the Western Cape Department of Health and Wellness’s 2024–2025 Annual Report.
The Department confirmed that it had achieved its seventh consecutive clean audit. It credited the result to a strong culture of accountability, collaboration with the Auditor-General, and a proactive approach to addressing weaknesses. The department explained its multi-layered assurance framework, which includes managerial oversight, internal control units, and external audits, to detect and prevent corruption.
Members commended the department for maintaining service delivery despite financial challenges but expressed concern over the ongoing budget crisis. It was noted that limited funding had negatively affected staffing levels, infrastructure maintenance, and capital projects. Members questioned the long-term sustainability of current financial strategies and urged the department to find ways to secure stable funding. Concerns were also raised about fraud and corruption, including a past case involving payment for undelivered goods. Members asked for updates on investigations, accountability measures, and details of recent fraud cases, stressing the importance of stronger internal controls and supply chain management systems.
Further questions focused on the department’s internal audits and risk management processes. Members asked whether there had been improvements in compliance and oversight despite reported fraud cases. They emphasised the need for transparency in investigating irregularities and recovering public funds. Additional concerns were raised about the potential for organised crime or procurement-related syndicates to exploit weaknesses in the system. The discussion also covered the department’s openness in reporting operational risks such as staff shortages, patient waiting times, and medicine availability. Members asked how internal committees coordinated risk management and how global supply disruptions affecting essential medicines were being addressed.
The meeting concluded with questions about infrastructure and financial management. Members highlighted hygiene and safety issues at certain hospitals, particularly around linen storage and infection control, and asked whether these facilities were included in upgrade plans. Financial concerns included delays in supplier payments, material losses such as vehicle damage, and redundant or expired stock. Members requested clarity on the causes of late payments, disputed invoices, and contract extensions. They also asked for information on how funds transferred to external foundations were monitored and how much of the department’s procurement spending went to women-owned businesses. Overall, the discussion focused on ensuring accountability, financial discipline, and effective management within the Department of Health and Wellness.
Meeting report
The Chairperson opened the meeting by welcoming everyone and formally commencing the session. He introduced himself as the Chairperson of the Standing Committee on Public Accounts (SCOPA) and acknowledged Mr Dustin Davids, the committee’s Procedural Officer, seated beside him.
The Chairperson invited members present to introduce themselves, explaining that this was done for procedural and record purposes. Members of SCOPA proceeded to introduce themselves. The Chairperson then welcomed members who were attending the meeting online before handing over to the Minister, Ms Mireille Wenger and Dr Keith Cloete, Head of Department (HOD), for their introductions and opening remarks. In response, the Minister greeted the Chairperson, the members of SCOPA, and representatives from the Department of Health and Wellness, expressing appreciation for the opportunity to appear before the committee. The HOD joined in acknowledging the committee and its members, marking the formal start of the engagement.
Minister and HOD Remarks
The Minister expressed her appreciation for the opportunity to appear before the SCOPA, stating that she and her team were pleased to be present for the engagement. She reported that the Department of Health and Wellness had once again achieved a clean audit for the 2024–2025 financial year, its seventh consecutive one, and highlighted this as a significant accomplishment for a department of such size and complexity.
She acknowledged that maintaining this level of financial compliance and accountability required a considerable collective effort and extended her gratitude to the departmental team for their dedication and hard work. Concluding her remarks, the Minister said she looked forward to engaging with the committee and responding to any questions they might have about the department’s financial performance and management.
Dr Cloete began his remarks by extending his appreciation to the members of SCOPA for their ongoing oversight and for the constructive relationship maintained between the committee and the Department of Health and Wellness. He stated that the department took great pride in achieving its seventh consecutive clean audit and viewed this accomplishment as a shared success between the department and SCOPA, given the committee’s role in promoting accountability and sound financial governance. He reflected on the department’s regular engagements with the Auditor-General’s office, specifically mentioning Liesl and Simon, and described these interactions as collaborative and transparent.
Dr Cloete shared that one of the key lessons reinforced during these sessions was the importance of ownership and accountability. He explained that when the Auditor-General identified a challenge or deficiency, the department’s approach was not to deflect or deny it but to take full responsibility, understand the scope of the issue, and ensure it was addressed. He said that this process demonstrated the department’s commitment to accountability, transparency, and continuous improvement, qualities that underpin its strong audit record.
Dr Cloete concluded by commending the professionalism and maturity of the department’s internal oversight structures, including the Audit Committee chaired by Dr Lawrence. He noted that the department’s engagements with the Audit Committee were open and strategic, allowing for honest reflection and planning. As the accounting officer, he expressed pride in the culture of accountability within the department and reaffirmed his respect for SCOPA’s oversight role. He assured the committee that the department welcomed scrutiny and saw it as an opportunity to strengthen governance and public trust.

Mr L Van Wyk (DA) began his remarks by commending the Department of Health and Wellness for the quality of its annual report and its overall performance despite facing significant operational and financial constraints. He acknowledged that the department had demonstrated strong commitment and efficiency in carrying out its responsibilities, but cautioned that the broader fiscal environment presented serious and ongoing risks. Referring to Part C of the report, which addresses governance and risk management, he highlighted that while many risks had been identified and managed effectively, the most pressing and potentially destabilising one remained the fiscal crisis confronting the department.
Mr Van Wyk described the situation as “a train that is coming,” noting that budgetary limitations were increasingly affecting the department’s ability to maintain service delivery. He observed that while the department had managed to stretch its budget on goods and services, this had come at the expense of critical staffing levels. He expressed concern about the long-term sustainability of this approach, warning that the continued underfunding of key posts could undermine both operational capacity and service quality.
He further emphasised the challenges associated with capital replacement, facility refurbishment, and maintenance, pointing out that the level of available funding fell far short of what was required to maintain infrastructure adequately.
Mr Van Wyk noted that the department had continued to “step up to the challenges,” but cautioned that the current financial strategies were akin to applying “a small piece of band aid” to a much larger wound. He urged the department and the committee to consider how to navigate the years ahead in an environment of fiscal austerity, particularly in securing sustainable funding to preserve and strengthen the province’s health system.

She further requested that the department commit to providing an update on the investigation, including clarification on who bears accountability when such irregularities occur, if it is only the final official who authorises the payment or all individuals involved in the supply and verification chain.
Turning to page 122 of the report, Ms Lekker requested detailed information on all fraud-related cases opened since 1 April 2024, including those that had been closed. She asked for a breakdown of the number of cases, the financial amounts involved, and the specific outcomes or disciplinary actions taken. Ms Lekker’s questions reflected a concern for transparency and accountability in the department’s financial management and a call for stronger internal controls to safeguard public resources against fraud and corruption.
Mr B Petrus (PA) remarked that the number and nature of corruption and fraud cases reflected in the Department of Health and Wellness’s Annual Report appeared to be more serious compared to those observed in other departments. He expressed concern that the scale and gravity of these cases suggested systemic weaknesses that required closer scrutiny and stronger internal safeguards.
Referring to page 125 of the report, Mr Petrus shifted focus to the department’s compliance and internal control assessments. He asked if there had been any positive findings or improvements noted during internal audits and compliance reviews. His question sought to understand whether, despite the identified cases of fraud and corruption, there were areas within the department’s governance and oversight systems that demonstrated progress, effectiveness, or resilience.
Dr Cloete began his response by addressing the concerns raised by Mr Van Wyk about the fiscal crisis, acknowledging that the department’s financial challenges represented one of its most significant risks. He explained that the department was continuously forced to make difficult trade-offs within its limited budget, balancing the allocation between staffing and goods and services. When more funding was directed toward personnel, the result was often a backlog in procurement and maintenance, and conversely, prioritising goods and services tended to constrain the filling of critical posts. He admitted that while the department was managing to maintain stability, the long-term sustainability of this balancing act was uncertain, particularly given the growing backlog in maintenance, which led to higher costs over time. However, he expressed cautious optimism that the upcoming budget adjustment and the 2025 Medium-Term Expenditure Budget (MTEB) process might bring some fiscal relief, with indications of potential baseline stabilisation that could improve financial planning going forward.
Turning to Ms Lekker’s questions about the long-standing issue involving the fraudulent procurement of mattresses, Dr Cloete confirmed that the matter had been raised in that morning’s engagement. He requested that the specific case details be formally submitted to the department so that officials could investigate it thoroughly and provide a detailed written response. He noted that, in general, the department had made significant improvements to its supply chain management systems to prevent such incidents from recurring.
He shared that the Western Cape’s electronic procurement system had become a model for other provinces, as it included built-in monitoring features that flagged irregularities and enabled real-time tracking of all transactions. He revealed that the National Health Council had recently resolved that other provinces should visit the Western Cape to observe and learn from this system’s functionality.
Dr Cloete elaborated on the department’s approach to fraud cases and investigations. He reported that there were currently six active cases opened since April, with detailed records maintained for each. He explained that all suspected cases were referred to the Provincial Forensic Services for investigation and, where necessary, to the South African Police Service (SAPS). The department maintained a policy of zero tolerance for corruption, ensuring that disciplinary processes were expedited, and any employee found guilty faced dismissal.
Responding to Mr Petrus’s query about compliance, Dr Cloete noted that Mr Anthony Small, who oversaw compliance assessments within the department, would elaborate further on the internal assessment tools and assurance processes that had helped strengthen governance and risk management.
Mr Simon Kaye, Chief Financial Officer, DHW, explained that the Department operates within a structured framework known as combined assurance, which integrates multiple layers of oversight to ensure accountability and prevent corruption. He noted that the first line of assurance is managerial oversight, governed by the accounting officer system. This system clearly outlines delegated responsibilities across various stages of the procurement process, from ordering to receiving, verifying, and approving payments. He emphasised that for corruption to occur, collusion between multiple individuals.
duals would typically be required, which the system’s separation of duties makes highly unlikely. Mr Kaye elaborated that the second level of assurance involves the Department’s Internal Control Units, known as DICUs, comprising around 40 officials deployed throughout the system. These units conduct continuous checks and audits at different levels of operation to test compliance and detect irregularities. He highlighted the Department’s extensive use of digitised supply chain systems that provide transparency and traceability in procurement. Through electronic systems, the Department can monitor who purchased what items, the quantities, and the prices paid. The system integrates with the Provincial Treasury’s central database, ensuring that only compliant suppliers with valid statutory documentation can bid on tenders. This digital infrastructure, he noted, has become a cornerstone of transparency and fraud prevention within the Department.
Moving to the third line of assurance, Mr Kaye cited the external oversight provided by bodies such as the Auditor-General, the Department of the Premier’s internal audit function, and the Provincial Forensic Services (PFS). He gave an example from Beaufort West, where whistleblower reports and internal expenditure reviews revealed misinterpretations of supply chain regulations, leading to irregular expenditure. The issue was promptly investigated, and corrective measures, including staff training, were implemented to prevent recurrence. The Auditor-General later audited the Department’s response and confirmed that it was handled appropriately, demonstrating that the internal control systems functioned as intended.
Mr Kaye concluded that while perfection is unattainable, the Department’s
culture of compliance
and proactive management of risks continues to result in consistently low levels of irregular expenditure, reflecting an effective and transparent control environment.
Mr Anthony Small, Director: Financial Accounting, DHW, explained that the Department’s procurement processes are largely decentralised, meaning that each hospital or facility is responsible for managing its own procurement activities. To ensure consistency, compliance, and accountability across the system, the Department has a dedicated team that conducts regular on-site assessments. These assessments serve two purposes: first, to identify any gaps or weaknesses in procurement practices, and second, to provide direct support and capacity building to institutions where shortcomings are detected. He described this team as highly skilled and effective, noting that their work has significantly strengthened the Department’s internal control environment. In many instances, the Provincial Forensic Services (PFS) refers cases back to this internal group because they are capable of conducting thorough preliminary investigations and resolving most issues before they escalate.
Mr Small explained that this proactive approach means the team often completes the bulk of the investigative work that would otherwise fall to PFS, reflecting the Department’s internal capacity to handle procurement-related risks and irregularities efficiently.
He concluded by emphasising that the Department maintains a strict recruitment standard for positions within this space, ensuring that only individuals with the requisite knowledge and integrity are appointed. All members of the team, he said, operate with a shared commitment to accountability and transparency. The Department enforces a zero-tolerance stance on misconduct or corruption, ensuring that any misgivings are dealt with decisively to maintain the integrity of the financial management system.

She cited challenges within the Metro, particularly bureaucratic and time-consuming staff appointment processes. While acknowledging that these disclosures demonstrated commendable openness, she questioned whether publishing such detailed internal challenges might expose the department to unnecessary reputational risk.
Dr Mbombo then turned to the department’s risk management framework, noting that the current policy and strategy for the 2024–2025 financial year were being revised. She sought clarity on the relationship between the Departmental Risk Management Committee (DRMC) and the Technical Subcommittee (TechSCOM), questioning if their roles overlapped and potentially created duplication of effort. In closing, Dr Mbombo addressed the department’s response to broader health risks, specifically mentioning the impact of the global polio crisis and pharmaceutical supply chain disruptions. Dr Mbombo concluded by encouraging the department to integrate its twelve major risks into the upcoming ERM strategy and to present that plan to the committee once completed, emphasising that SCOPA’s role was not merely oversight but to support the department in achieving systemic resilience and effective risk management.

The Chairperson referred to the department’s identified risks on page 120 of the Annual Report, particularly those about service fragmentation and the availability of medicines and vaccines. He acknowledged that financial constraints played a major role in these challenges but asked the department to elaborate on how such risks were being mitigated in practical terms.
He questioned how the department prioritised patient needs when resources were limited, especially in cases involving chronic disease management, where consistent access to medication and continuity of care were critical.

During these inspections, the Auditor-General’s teams had monitored issues such as patient waiting times, queue management, and infrastructure conditions. They had reportedly found that facilities were instructed to check patient flow every two hours to ensure movement in the queues and improve service efficiency.
The Chairperson requested the department to expand on how these findings were being addressed operationally and if similar measures were being institutionalised across all facilities to enhance service delivery despite ongoing fiscal limitations.
Dr Cloete began by responding to the concerns raised by Mr Booysen about the potential infiltration of syndicates into the department’s supply chain. He confirmed that this very issue had been discussed the previous week during a national meeting convened by the Minister of Health, where all provincial departments were asked to assess the likelihood of syndicate activity within their procurement systems. He explained that such discussions were prompted by revelations from the Special Investigating Unit (SIU), which had uncovered large-scale procurement syndicates operating elsewhere in the country.
Dr Cloete described how these criminal networks typically functioned through collusion between multiple individuals across the procurement chain, including clerks responsible for receiving goods, authorising payments, and falsifying records. This collusion allowed fictitious payments to be made into private accounts without goods being delivered. However, he assured the committee that such a modus operandi would be “virtually impossible” to replicate in the Western Cape, given the province’s stringent internal controls, digital tracking systems, and multiple layers of oversight.
While he acknowledged that isolated attempts at fraud did occur, he emphasised that they were detected quickly and addressed through established forensic and disciplinary procedures. He added that the National Minister had encouraged other provinces to adopt the Western Cape’s model for supply chain management as a national standard.
Turning to Dr Mbombo’s comments on the department’s risk management framework, Dr Cloete expressed appreciation for her deep understanding of the department’s internal mechanisms. He confirmed that senior management had recently held a workshop with the Internal Audit Committee to review the Enterprise Risk Management (ERM) system, which was being updated to reflect lessons learned from recent challenges. He explained that the department’s risk management approach was designed to be agile, ensuring that significant strategic risks were escalated to senior management, while more operational risks were managed at appropriate levels within the organisation. This structure allowed for rapid response and adaptability, as illustrated by the department’s swift mitigation efforts when PEPFAR funding delays were reported earlier in the year.
Addressing the Chairperson’s query about service fragmentation and the availability of medicines and vaccines, Dr Cloete explained that service fragmentation remained a long-standing challenge, particularly in the overlap between municipal and provincial responsibilities for primary health care in the Metro. He said the department was actively working to consolidate these services under provincial management to improve coordination and reduce inefficiencies. About medicine and vaccine availability, he provided examples of how global market disruptions, such as the discontinuation of insulin pens due to pharmaceutical companies’ shift to newer products like Ozempic, had affected local supply chains. In response, the department had implemented strong contingency plans, including provincial procurement contracts and specialist task teams that met immediately when shortages occurred to ensure continuity of care.
In closing, Dr Cloete addressed Ms Lekker’s concern about hygiene practices and infection control. He gave his assurance that the province maintained a highly effective infection prevention and control system, supported by an efficient laundry service that ensured regular collection and delivery of hospital linen. He noted that while temporary accumulation of laundry might occur at facilities awaiting collection, it was part of a well-monitored process, and the Western Cape’s laundry and infection control systems were often cited as best-practice examples by other provinces.
Dr Mbombo referred to page 125 of the Annual Report and revisited the issue of primary health care (PHC) facilities within the Cape Town Metropole. She recalled that the Head of Department had previously acknowledged the fragmentation of services between municipal and provincial facilities as a longstanding risk that undermined the efficiency of healthcare delivery. She asked for an update on the rationalisation process that had been initiated to address this challenge, noting that the department had earlier indicated that the integration would be implemented in phases across sub-districts.
Dr Mbombo sought clarity on how far the process had progressed and if there were concrete timelines or measurable outcomes from the initial phases. She further pointed out that service fragmentation in the Metro was not only a structural challenge but a governance and performance risk that the department had previously highlighted in its performance review. She urged the department to provide specific details on how the rationalisation was improving coordination between municipal and provincial health facilities and if this restructuring was reflected in improved patient care and operational efficiency.
In her final point, Dr Mbombo turned to the department’s ongoing response to the polio crisis, which she linked to broader issues of climate change and environmental health. She acknowledged the department’s leadership in preparedness and readiness, noting that it remained the only health department in the country to have taken substantial steps in this area. Drawing on her academic background, she referenced the One Health approach, which integrates human, animal, and environmental health and expressed concern that it was not prominently featured in the department’s risk and governance reporting. She asked how far the department had progressed in implementing this integrated approach and how One Health considerations were being incorporated into planning and response frameworks, particularly given the increasing impact of climate change on disease patterns and public health.
Dr Cloete responded to Dr Mbombo’s questions by expressing appreciation for her insightful engagement, noting that the issues she raised were central to the department’s ongoing reform and coordination efforts.
He began by addressing the matter of the city transfer and the rationalisation of primary health care (PHC) services within the Cape Town Metropole. He explained that the process had reached an advanced stage, with Minister Mireille Wenger and her counterpart in the City of Cape Town, who serves as the Mayoral Committee (MAKO) Member for Health, currently working together to identify all municipal health services that should be consolidated under a single provincial structure.
Dr Cloete added that discussions had been escalated to the National Treasury to finalise the financial arrangements required to effect this transfer as a unified process rather than in fragmented phases. He acknowledged that while the department had previously undertaken phased integrations, such as the transfer of the ten city clinics and the later Atlantis III facilities, those piecemeal approaches had proven administratively and financially cumbersome. He stated that the department’s preferred strategy, now under active consideration in the upcoming budget process, was to finalise the rationalisation in one comprehensive transaction to achieve a coherent, integrated PHC system across the Metro.
Turning to the One Health approach, Dr Cloete explained that it remained a high priority and an area of personal commitment for him. He noted that he co-chaired a multi-sectoral structure with the Heads of Environmental Affairs and Agriculture, and that the One Health framework had been formally recognised as the fifth outcome under the Provincial Strategic Plan for an “Educated, Healthy, and Caring Society.” He shared that significant progress had been made in this collaborative initiative, highlighting four key focus areas: preparedness for outbreak response, developed jointly with the Department of Agriculture; a surveillance system for air and water quality; monitoring and prevention of pesticide poisoning; and tracking antimicrobial resistance (AMR), which examines antibiotic resistance across both human and animal health systems.
Dr Cloete concluded by assuring Dr Mbombo that these efforts were well advanced and gaining momentum. He noted with enthusiasm that the partnerships among the Departments of Health, Agriculture, and Environmental Affairs, together with municipal environmental health teams, had created a dynamic and integrated working relationship, reflecting real progress in the province’s practical implementation of the One Health agenda.

Dr Cloete responded to several questions raised by members, addressing the issues sequentially. He. In response to Dr Mbombo’s question about the Health Foundation, Dr Cloete explained that the foundation had originally received seed funding from the Department of Health over three years to establish itself. After that period, the foundation became financially independent, generating its own income and operating without direct transfers from the department. He clarified that while the foundation no longer formed part of the departmental budget, the department still maintained governance oversight through an annual governance meeting and representation on the foundation’s board.

He noted that the department was working toward developing a more formal mechanism to reflect non-voted funds, that is, external donations or contributions that benefit the health system but are not part of the department’s allocated budget. This, he explained, would eventually provide full transparency on all supplementary resources flowing into the health sector, including those from entities like the Health Foundation and the Children’s Hospital Trust.
Mr Kaye began by addressing the questions raised by Ms Lekker about government vehicle damages and losses. He explained that these typically occur in situations where departmental vehicles are damaged but the driver is not found liable, such as in cases of hijackings, which remain a real and traumatic risk. He added that a new strategy was being implemented to make government vehicles less identifiable to reduce such incidents. Where a driver is found liable, an affordability assessment is conducted, and the matter proceeds through legal channels under the State Attorney for recovery.
On redundant stock, Mr Kaye clarified that this referred mainly to expired medical inventory. He noted that with a large stockholding, some level of expiry is inevitable, though the department’s stock control is considered strong, spending approximately R2 billion on medicines annually, with around R2 million in losses, a very small proportion by comparison.
Responding to the issue of late or non-payments of suppliers, Mr Kaye explained that the department processed over 202,000 invoices valued at R13.8 billion across more than 550 cost centres, and despite strong controls, some delays were unavoidable. He acknowledged that small suppliers are often affected most but emphasised that delayed invoices are always paid subsequently, and the Auditor General verifies this.
He added that a national systems upgrade to the government’s BAS payment platform at the end of the financial year temporarily disrupted payments across departments, requiring manual intervention to process outstanding invoices.
On contract expansions and variations (pages 193–196), Mr Kaye stated that there are legitimate reasons for such extensions, such as limited responses from the market or cost-effectiveness when current contracts remain more affordable than new bids. He assured members that all extensions undergo a formal adjudication process chaired by Ms Isaacs and supported by commodity managers, ensuring accountability and value for money. Health services, he stressed, cannot be interrupted due to procurement lapses, particularly for essential contracts like cleaning or medical supply services.
About procurement linked to women-led businesses, Mr Kaye confirmed that provincial treasury maintains a comprehensive database connecting departmental procurement data to supplier information systems, which allows the province to verify and report progress on women-owned business participation. Finally, on the Health Foundation, he explained that there had been a commercial transaction involving the Desmond and Leah Tutu Foundation and the foundation’s Knowledge Translation Unit (KTU), resulting in a temporary transfer of funds. This led the department to adjust its governance arrangements, stepping back from direct ex officio involvement to maintain transparency. He noted that this arrangement was later unwound after year-end, with the KTU operating independently again, while the department maintained its collaborative relationship with the Health Foundation in a governance-compliant manner.
Mr Booysen raised a concern that had been triggered during the discussion, referring to instances in the third-quarter system where there appeared to be irregular expenditure linked to items procured from a company whose contract had already expired. He questioned how such a situation could occur within the department’s procurement and oversight framework, given the described systems of control and contract management. He sought clarification on this, which reflected a lapse in monitoring contract validity, a delay in renewal processes, or a procedural oversight allowing transactions to proceed after a contract’s official end date. His question implied concern that even with strong systems in place, expired contracts might still be used to procure goods or services, potentially leading to irregular expenditure and raising questions about accountability and adherence to supply chain management regulations.
Ms Esme Isaacs, Chief Director of Supply Chain Management, DHW, responded to Mr Booysen’s question by explaining the procedural and technical context behind such instances. She clarified that the department operates under a fully electronic purchasing system (EPS), which eliminates manual quotation processes. This system is open to all registered suppliers who have submitted the required compliance documentation, such as valid tax certificates and registration details, allowing them to participate transparently in procurement opportunities. She noted that the Western Cape Department of Health adheres strictly to directives issued by the provincial treasury to ensure uniformity across all provincial departments. In accordance with these directives, all procurement transactions valued between R10,000 and R1 million must be conducted through the EPS. This framework reduces opportunities for manipulation because all bids and quotations are captured electronically, ensuring a verifiable record of the process and limiting manual interference.
Addressing the specific issue of irregular expenditure involving expired contracts, Ms Isaacs explained that, due to the electronic system’s control measures, it is very difficult for such irregularities to occur undetected. However, she acknowledged that there are exceptional cases, particularly in medicine procurement, where a non-compliant supplier may be awarded a contract out of necessity. For example, when an essential medicine is only available from a single international manufacturer that does not meet all local compliance criteria, the department may proceed with the procurement to avoid disrupting patient care. In such cases, the expenditure is transparently recorded as irregular, and the department follows a formal regularisation process in line with treasury regulations to justify and document the decision.
Ms Isaacs concluded by emphasising that while no system is completely immune to risk, the electronic procurement system provides a high level of transparency and accountability, greatly reducing opportunities for manipulation or unauthorised spending.
Dr Mbombo began by addressing the under-expenditure on health facilities management on page 217, noting that this had become a recurring issue because the Department of Health functions as the client department to the Department of Infrastructure.
She expressed specific interest in understanding the current situation of NHI-funded infrastructure projects, especially those in George, her constituency, such as the Pacaltsdorp Clinic. She explained that residents in the area were questioning delays and attributing responsibility to her, prompting her to seek clarity from the department on the progress and implications of these projects. She asked if the Energy Fund referenced in the report had contributed to the delays, and if it was linked to the Western Cape Energy Grant Management Programme or the broader Energy Council initiative, mentioning a pilot site in Hessequa as a possible example.
Turning to staffing concerns on page 218, Dr Mbombo focused on the loss of 989 positions in the department, calling it an emotional issue because it directly affects service delivery and morale. She sought a detailed explanation of what was meant by “abnormal increases in staff resignations,” asking what factors had contributed to this unusual trend. She noted that the Western Cape had historically not struggled with high staff turnover, making the recent spike particularly concerning.
Lastly, on page 219, Dr Mbombo raised the issue of community health workers (CHWs) and the non-profit institutions (NPIs) that support them. She asked for an update on the current situation of CHWs in the province, acknowledging the long-standing national debate around their employment, recognition, and integration into the formal health system. She highlighted that while the Western Cape had developed its own provincial policy to manage CHWs, recent developments, particularly the loss of NGO funding, were threatening their sustainability. Many NGOs that had previously supported CHWs were now reducing staff, which, she warned, would leave major gaps in preventive and promotive health services.
Dr Mbombo stressed that CHWs were the “Aunt Saras” of the system, the trusted, grassroots workers essential to maintaining community-level care and that their uncertain future posed a serious risk to the broader public health network.
Ms Lekker referred to page 203 of the report, seeking clarity on the statement that an investigation was being conducted across the health sector in South Africa. She asked for an update on the progress and current status of that investigation, specifically wanting to know if the Western Cape Department of Health was included or implicated in the inquiry. Her question aimed to establish if the department had been subject to any form of scrutiny or review under this national investigation, and if so, what findings or developments had emerged from it.
The Chairperson referred to page 254 of the report, raising a question about the medical-legal claims listed there. He sought an update on the 114 remaining cases that were described as having strong merits.
The Chairperson requested clarity on the current status of these cases and if any progress had been made in resolving or finalising them. He then opened the floor to other members, asking if there were any additional questions from the committee.
Dr Mbombo referred to page 290 of the report, focusing on the Violence Prevention Unit (VPU) and its partnership with the Council for Scientific and Industrial Research (CSIR). She requested an update on the current progress of this collaboration, particularly about the integration of logistics systems and the use of artificial intelligence (AI) tools mentioned in the report. She noted that the department had previously indicated that funds had to be shifted and that shortfalls were affecting certain transfers and payments. Drawing a link to her earlier reference on page 219 about the community health workers, whom she referred to as the “Aunt Saras” of the health system, she explained that high levels of crime reported in the media highlight the need for a strong, coordinated prevention response from the health sector.
Dr Mbombo commended the department for establishing the VPU, noting that the Western Cape had been the first province in the country to embed a violence prevention programme within its health system, adapting the model from the Cardiff approach in the United Kingdom. However, she expressed concern about recent delays and the seeming lack of public communication about the VPU’s work. Given the ongoing reports of violent crime, she asked for clarity on where the programme currently stands, how effectively it is operating in partnership with the CSIR, and if its visibility and impact have diminished in recent months.
Mr Petrus referred to page 261 of the report, raising a question about the medical legal claims and settlements reflected in that section. He requested detailed information on the nature of those claims, including the types of cases involved, their current status, and the amounts settled. Mr Petrus sought clarity on the settlements were made through court judgments or out-of-court agreements, and if the department had identified any trends or recurring causes behind the medical legal cases being lodged. His inquiry aimed to understand how the department is managing these claims financially and institutionally, and what measures are being taken to reduce future liability.
The Minister responded in part to the question about the Pacaltsdorp Clinic, explaining that it is one of nine projects funded under the NHI indirect grant within the Health Facility Revitalisation component. She confirmed that all nine projects have experienced significant delays. These are national infrastructure development programmes overseen by the National Department of Health, which were originally scheduled for completion in 2019 but remain unfinished. The minister noted that the delays have been largely due to complications involving the Independent Development Trust (IDT), which served as the implementing agent. The IDT’s well-documented administrative and operational challenges have severely affected progress on the projects. She stated that the department has repeatedly raised the issue with the National Department of Health, including through direct correspondence from her to the national minister, in an effort to find solutions. She acknowledged the pressing need for these facilities and expressed frustration that they remain stalled despite their importance to local communities. The minister concluded by saying that while the department had recently received an update on the matter, progress remains slow, and they continue to engage the national department to ensure tangible movement on these delayed projects.
Dr Laura du Toit, Chief Director: Infrastructure and Technical Management, DHW, provided further clarification on the Pacaltsdorp Clinic matter following the minister’s remarks. She confirmed that after the minister’s correspondence with the national minister, the National Department of Health began restructuring its responsibilities regarding this specific project. Two weeks prior, officials from the National Department had visited Cape Town to meet with the provincial team. During that meeting, they confirmed that the Independent Development Trust (IDT) was in the process of having its implementing agent agreement cancelled, due to the ongoing problems that have hindered project delivery. In response, the Western Cape Department of Health proposed that the national department make use of the province’s newly awarded framework agreement for infrastructure projects.
Dr du Toit explained that this framework, awarded in the previous financial year, allows other government entities to “piggyback” on it for project implementation. The department has already received two such requests from the Western Cape Department of Education and the City of Cape Town, and currently works with seven major contractors in the construction sector under this agreement.
She stated that the province had formally offered the national department the option to use this framework to fast-track the Pacaltsdorp project. If accepted, the national department would need to send an official request, after which the provincial supply chain team would prepare a draft agreement. Dr du Toit added that the provincial department suggested that the same consultants who had originally designed the clinic be reappointed through the management contract, to ensure continuity and avoid further delays. She concluded by expressing optimism that this approach would finally provide a viable way forward for completing the long-stalled project.
Dr du Toit further explained the reasons behind the underspending of R46 million in the Provincial Equitable Share (PES) infrastructure allocation and the Energy Funds. She clarified that under the Energy Funds, the department operates through the G4J Energy Programme, which is managed collaboratively with the Provincial Treasury and the Department of the Premier. Each year, the departments jointly assess the available funding and realign allocations based on project cash flows and procurement progress. In this financial year, delays arose in the procurement and contracting process for the installation of photovoltaic (PV) solar systems at hospitals. As a result, the department ran out of time to complete all planned installations within the financial year. To mitigate this, the Department of Infrastructure was requested to use its own management contractor to implement some of the projects, which enabled progress at several facilities such as Mossel Bay Hospital, Red Cross Children’s Hospital, and Hermanus Hospital.
However, Dr du Toit acknowledged that the full scope of planned interventions could not be completed, and similar adjustments would be required in the current year through discussions with the Treasury and the Department of the Premier.
About the PES infrastructure allocation, Dr du Toit explained that part of the budget is ring-fenced for the new Tygerberg Hospital project under a public-private partnership (PPP). The department, in agreement with the Provincial Treasury, channels these funds into the capital asset fund to serve as the capital contribution toward the project’s debt financing and construction phase. She emphasised that this earmarked funding ensures the department can meet its financial obligations for the redevelopment of Tygerberg Hospital, which remains one of the province’s major long-term infrastructure priorities.
Dr Cloete addressed several outstanding questions from members, beginning with the matter of staff losses. He explained that the net loss of 989 employees was primarily due to an unusual surge in resignations between August and September, when many staff members chose to resign to access their pension savings under the new two-part retirement system. This wave of resignations occurred just as strict appointment rules from the Department of Public Service and Administration (DPSA) took effect, making it difficult to replace staff immediately. However, by the last quarter of the financial year, the department had begun to recover, with appointments once again exceeding resignations.
He then turned to the issue of community health workers (CHWs), noting significant national developments following a High Court ruling that declared CHWs in the other eight provinces who were paid directly through PERSAL as permanent employees. This ruling now compels those provinces to integrate CHWs into the public service. The Western Cape, however, remains outside the scope of that ruling, as CHWs are contracted through non-profit organisations (NPOs).
Dr Cloete said the department must now engage in a policy discussion about the future of CHWs in the province, including remuneration, fair labour practices, and their integration into the broader health system. He added that while the Western Cape will consider these issues as part of its upcoming budget process, the absence of a DPSA job evaluation and official job description complicates direct employment.
In response to Ms Lekker’s earlier query about the investigation mentioned on page 203, Dr Cloete confirmed that it referred to an investigation by the Special Investigating Unit (SIU). He shared that he had personally written to the SIU thanking them for their findings, which commended the Western Cape Department of Health for its strong systems of control and minimal irregularities compared to other provinces.
Addressing Dr Mbombo’s question about the Violence Prevention Unit (VPU), Dr Cloete explained that the partnership with the Council for Scientific and Industrial Research (CSIR) aims to enhance data capabilities for mapping and analysing patterns of violence. He highlighted that the VPU’s work is rooted in social prevention, focusing on parenting support, early childhood development, and youth interventions rather than law enforcement. The department has launched the
Planet Youth
initiative, surveying over 49,000 Grade 8 and 9 learners across 121 schools to understand risk behaviours and inform community-based prevention strategies.
Lastly, Dr Cloete responded to Mr Petrus’s question on medical legal claims, noting that the department’s dedicated medical legal unit, comprising representatives from the department, legal services, and the State Attorney, reviews cases early to determine if settlements are appropriate. By settling valid claims before they reach the courts, the department avoids costly litigation and expedites compensation for affected individuals. He added that the department had recently recovered R9 million from a claimant’s trust following their death, demonstrating the effectiveness of the structured oversight system in ensuring funds are properly managed and returned when appropriate.
Dr Mbombo raised a question about page 236 of the report, focusing on the Road Accident Fund (RAF) claims. She recalled that in previous years, the RAF had owed the provincial Department of Health millions of rands for services rendered, particularly for the treatment of road accident victims. Given that the RAF had recently come under national scrutiny by SCOPA for financial and administrative challenges, she asked if the fund still owed the department money, the current value of that debt, and what mechanisms were in place to recover the outstanding amounts.
Ms Lekker raised a question about page 241 of the report, focusing on expenditure under goods and services, particularly the portion spent on legal fees. She requested clarity on what these legal fees covered, specifically asking how much of the total was about staff disciplinary matters. In addition, she sought details on how many of those cases involved the department as a defendant and how many were referred to the Commission for Conciliation, Mediation and Arbitration (CCMA), the Labour Court, or the Public Health and Social Development Sectoral Bargaining Council.
The Chairperson posed two questions for clarification, referring first to page 201 of the report under Programme 2 on district health services. He asked for an explanation of the term “IDLE clinic status obtained rate,” noting that he was unfamiliar with what this measure indicated. His question sought to understand if it referred to the operational efficiency of clinics, compliance with infrastructure standards, or another form of departmental assessment about service delivery at the district level.
The Chairperson’s second query referred to page 277, which mentioned “payments made as an act of grace.” He asked for an explanation of what such payments entailed, seeking clarity on the circumstances under which the department authorises them. His question implied an interest in these payments that were made in cases where individuals or entities suffered loss or harm due to departmental actions or omissions, but where no legal liability had been established. He requested a clearer understanding of how these discretionary payments are justified, approved, and recorded within the department’s financial reporting framework.
Dr Cloete responded to several questions raised by members, beginning with an update for Dr Mbombo about the Road Accident Fund (RAF). He explained that following the RAF’s recent announcement of having funds available for disbursement, the national CFO Forum would coordinate efforts with provincial health departments to recover outstanding monies. He noted that the Western Cape Department of Health had already begun to see some recoveries locally and was actively pursuing the retrieval of as much funding as possible owed by the RAF for services rendered to road accident victims.
Addressing Ms Lekker’s question on legal fees, Dr Cloete clarified that these costs fall into three main categories: medical-legal cases, labour-related matters (including those referred to the CCMA and Labour Court), and supply chain management disputes, which have become increasingly litigious in recent years. He committed to providing a detailed breakdown of these costs to both the SCOPA committee and the Portfolio Committee for consistency.
Turning to the chairperson’s questions, Dr Cloete explained that the “ideal clinic” is a national assessment tool used to evaluate facilities against a set of norms and standards established by the Office of Health Standards Compliance. It measures the readiness and quality of services at each facility. The Western Cape achieved 80% compliance, slightly below its 83% target, largely because 11 facilities were undergoing renovations and could not be audited.
Finally, he clarified that “payments made as an act of grace” refer to discretionary payments allowed under the PFMA, where the accounting officer can authorise up to R100,000 in certain cases, such as where harm or loss occurred without legal liability. Any payment above that threshold requires approval from the legislature.
Ms Lekker noted the explanation provided about “acts of grace,” acknowledging that these were primarily humanitarian or compassionate payments, often covering cases such as assistance for staff members dealing with bereavement or family deaths in other provinces. She then indicated that, since detailed information on these payments had already been requested earlier in the day, she would await the formal written report before commenting further.
She proceeded to raise a new question, referring to page 245 of the report under the section on “debts written off.” Ms Lekker asked for clarity on these debts related specifically to supplier payments and requested details on the reasons for their write-off. She sought to understand if the debts were considered irrecoverable due to supplier liquidation, administrative errors, legal disputes, or prolonged non-payment, and further asked for the names of the suppliers involved. Her question reflected a concern with financial accountability and transparency in how the department manages its supplier obligations and the circumstances that lead to such debts being written off.
Dr Cloete responded to Ms Lekker’s question by acknowledging that while the amount in question was relatively small, the department could provide a detailed breakdown of the debts written off. He explained that, in most cases, these situations arise when there has been an overpayment or an over-delivery of goods or services, after which the supplier either becomes insolvent, ceases operations, or cannot be located. In such circumstances, after reasonable efforts to recover the funds have been exhausted, the department proceeds to write off the debt in accordance with the provisions of the Public Finance Management Act (PFMA). He assured the committee that these instances are rare and managed within the allowable limits of the PFMA, reiterating that the department would supply the committee with a full account of the specific cases and amounts involved.
The Chairperson concluded the meeting by confirming that there were no further questions from members and announced that the session would be adjourned shortly. He invited the Minister of Health to deliver her closing remarks, followed by Dr Mbombo, whom he humorously acknowledged for asking “the most intelligent questions,” to extend a formal vote of thanks to the department. The chairperson expressed appreciation to all members for their active participation and thoughtful engagement throughout the session, noting that the committee had thoroughly examined the department’s financial information and audit outcomes for the year. He emphasised that the depth of questioning and discussion demonstrated the committee’s commitment to accountability and effective oversight, before formally handing over to the Minister to close the proceedings.
Minister and HOD's Closing Remarks
The Minister closed the meeting by expressing her appreciation to the Standing Committee on Public Accounts (SCOPA) for its constructive oversight and continued engagement.
Dr Cloete extended his gratitude to the department’s assurance partners, including the Auditor General’s office, for their year-round work in supporting the department’s commitment to sound governance and accountability. He noted that the department valued SCOPA’s oversight not as criticism, but as an essential part of maintaining transparency, compliance, and institutional integrity. He reiterated that the department operates on a principle of accountability that the Auditor General had specifically commended them for: when an error is identified, they take ownership of it, act to resolve it, and invite SCOPA to verify that corrective measures have been implemented.
Dr Cloete emphasised that this approach fosters both credibility and trust between the department and oversight bodies. Finally, he expressed heartfelt thanks to all departmental officials, acknowledging the unseen and tireless work done by staff at every level. He commended their professionalism, ethical integrity, and shared commitment to serving the public. He thanked the Auditor General’s team for its long-standing partnership, noting that the mutual understanding and open communication developed over time have been key to sustaining the department’s strong governance and accountability record.
Dr Mbombo concluded the session by extending her gratitude to the Department of Health, the Minister, and the auditors for their cooperation and detailed responses throughout the engagement. She thanked her fellow committee members for their insightful contributions and constructive participation. She emphasised that the questions and scrutiny posed during the session were not intended to be adversarial, but rather to strengthen accountability and improve governance.
Dr Mbombo highlighted that, as members of the legislature, their role as an oversight arm of the state is to ensure that government departments remain transparent and responsive to the public they serve. She closed by reaffirming the committee’s commitment to collaboration, accountability, and the continuous improvement of service delivery across the province.
Committee Resolutions
Mr Davids concluded the meeting by summarising the resolutions and follow-up actions agreed upon during the session. He noted that Mr Van Wyk’s concern about the department’s fiscal crisis, specifically the tension between maintaining goods and services and ensuring adequate staffing, would be reflected in the committee’s report for future reference.
He further stated that the matter of the mattresses, raised by Ms Lekker, would be referred back to the department for a detailed written response. He requested that Ms Lekker provide supporting details, namely that 200 mattresses were procured at a cost of R211,000, to assist the department in preparing a comprehensive report.
Ms Lekker added that the committee should determine if a forensic investigation was conducted into the matter and if it was reported to the Auditor General. Mr Davids noted that, following Dr Mbombo’s request, the department’s Enterprise Risk Management (ERM) plan would be scheduled for presentation to the committee in the coming year. He recorded that Mr Pieterse’s inquiry about contract extensions and expansions (page 193), as well as the issues about goods and services (page 241), debts written off (page 245), and payments made as acts of grace, would all be included in the department’s forthcoming written submissions. Members acknowledged that these follow-ups would be critical to maintaining accountability and strengthening oversight.
The meeting was adjourned.
