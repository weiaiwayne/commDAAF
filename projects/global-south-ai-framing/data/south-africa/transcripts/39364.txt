Meeting Summary
The Standing Committee on Public Accounts (SCOPA) convened to be briefed by the Office of the Accountant-General (OAG) and the Office of the Chief Procurement Officer (OCPO) on their mandate, initiatives to enforce consequences using the Public Finance Management Act (PFMA), supply chain management (SCM) processes, deviations and expansions, and how these entities could support SCOPA in its oversight role.
The Committee was extremely frustrated with the lack of progress over the past 30 years and was concerned that the situation was deteriorating. The Committee expressed its commitment to addressing the challenges being experienced and indicated that it would not be a ‘tick box’ Committee that produced no results.
The Committee was concerned and frustrated by the large number of non-compliant municipalities. The Committee viewed non-compliance as a major stumbling block for democracy and service delivery.
The Committee was concerned about payments of invoices within 30-days. This was often not complied with resulting in late- or non-payments. This negatively affected the ability of suppliers to supply services and goods.
The Committee was frustrated by the delayed implementation of the Integrated Financial Management System (IFMS). This system had been in the works for several years, and yet had not been implemented. The IFMS would also strengthen National Treasury’s ability to follow the money, understand when compliance was being neglected, and allow adequate time for intervention.
The Committee was concerned that the legislation did not sufficiently empower National Treasury and the OAG. The legislation should enable service delivery and not deter it. The Committee indicated that it was open to amending the PFMA and Municipal Financial Management Act (MFMA) if National Treasury presented the amendments and the impact of these amendments were presented. The Committee felt that amendments to the legislation would strengthen National Treasury’s authority to intervene timeously and prevent the negative repercussions of transgressions within entities or departments. National Treasury had to be firm in its response to non-compliance and financial mismanagement and should have the ability to intervene.
In terms of procurement, the Committee was concerned that government departments and entities often paid above-normal prices for commodities and services. This issue had to be addressed.
In terms of SCM processes, the Committee felt that the issue of 30-day payments did not account for SCM processes resulting in late- and non-payments. The Committee also noted that in some instances where conditional grants were provided to municipalities, municipalities were unable to use some of the money and had to pay it back due to extensive red tape and SCM processes which created challenges in terms of service delivery.
The Committee expressed its intent to engage further with the OAG, OCPO and National Treasury on these issues and how National Treasury and SCOPA could address these issues.

Office of the Accountant-General presentation
Mr Shabeer Khan, Accountant-General, OAG, presented the briefing of the Office of the Accountant-General (OAG).
The OAG presentation provided a comprehensive summary of the OAG’s mandate, including strategic intent of the OAG, strategic focus and structure, the legislative framework of the OAG, and the products and services offered by the OAG (slides 3-10)
The OAG provided information regarding National Treasury Instruction No. 4 of 2022/23. Instruction No.4 indicated that all national and provincial departments and public entities listed in schedules 2 and 3 of the Public Finance Management Act (PFMA) where required from the 2022/23 financial year to report on specific compliance matters in a dedicated section of their annual reports. The compliance report was introduced as a mechanism of accountability. Compliance reporting enabled transparency, accountability, and good governance within public sector entities.
The OAG also highlighted initiatives to enforce consequences using the PFMA. This included internal control reviews. The Specialised Audit Services (SAS) unit has conducted a number of performance audits and cyber security reviews, and a performance audit was conducted on the qualifications of CFOs, including forensic investigations and law enforcement support.

Mr Khan noted the concerns of Members. The questions asked were very relevant, and the OAG also reflected on those questions. At a National Treasury level there were reflections on the state of local government and how to better support local government.
He said that the PFMA provided power to National Treasury, the Minister of Finance and the OAG. As the law stands, OAG provided norms and standards, and developed and issued instructions and regulations through accounting guides. OAG was considering how best to use the current instruments to ensure that when money is being utilised, it is utilised in the correct manner, accounted for, and in compliance with the PFMA. It was also important to ensure that there was value for money. The remedies provided through law were the only instruments OAG could use to deal with those challenges. Non-compliance was the core issue flagged by the AG in the General Report. Only 13% of municipalities had clean audits in terms of financial statements, performance reports and compliance. If 13% were compliant, this meant that 87% were not compliant with the PFMA. When considering the general history, majority of the non-compliance was centralised around supply chain management (SCM). Reforms had been introduced around public procurement. This included new measures and instruments to better regulate and ensure strong consequence management.
He referred to the remedies that were currently available. He highlighted the accountability ecosystem. There were many players in the accountability ecosystem including Treasury. Every accounting officer, by law, had to ensure that, in terms of Sections 38 and 51 of the PFMA, that there were the necessary controls and mismanagement systems to ensure that the entities were able to comply and that there was effective use of money. When there was non-compliance, the law forces the particular accounting officer to investigate the non-compliance. Once the accounting officer has investigated and confirmed the breech of the law, the law asks the accounting officer if the non-compliance resulted from fraud or corruption and if there was any criminal conduct attached to the non-compliance. If the answer was yes, the law indicated that the accounting officer should open up a case and institute formal disciplinary action. Following this, the money should be recovered.
From an accounting perspective, the only way this could be cured was to condone the non-compliance or recover the money lost in the system. Only National Treasury could condone an irregularity. The irregularity had to demonstrate that there has been disciplinary action, recovery of money and where there was criminal conduct it has been reported to the South African Police Services (SAPS). Within the law, these were the available instruments to remedy the issue of non-compliance. Currently, the PFMA and the Municipal Finance Management Act (MFMA) are being reviewed. Some of the Zondo Report's recommendations have requested the strengthening of the legal framework around consequence management. This would improve governance around audit committees, and internal audit functions.
In terms of National Treasury’s interventions in cases of non-compliance, the law gave National Treasury an instrument to investigate instances of financial mismanagement. Treasury and OAG were considering ways to strengthen this so that National Treasury could have stronger powers to enforce compliance and subpoena any documents around the areas of financial mismanagement.
In local government, the grant framework was being reviewed. Officials from the inter-government relations team were reviewing this framework. Section 139 of the MFMA gave Treasury and the Department of Cooperative Governance and Traditional Affairs (COGTA) the ability to put a municipality under administration. This process would allow funds to be stopped, appoint an administrator, and ensure a turnaround plan. The law gave National Treasury the power to put together a financial recovery plan within a certain time frame to ensure that a plan was in place in terms of recovery.
Mr Khan responded to the concerns regarding the General Report and the issue of non-compliant municipalities. The General Report that had been presented was not a good reflection. OAG was using the instruments it had around audits to see how OAG could intervene. OAG was reviewing the qualifications of Chief Financial Officers (CFOs). What stopped Treasury from applying an intervention that followed the money allocated and approving expenditure, until the institution was in a good place?
She highlighted the issue of 30-day payments. It could not be that the remedy was what had been explained by the Accountant-General. This was a very serious issue. She suggested that National Treasury accompany the Committee on its oversight, so that Treasury could see the impact of non-payments and late payments. National Treasury should have the authority to be able to take over certain functions if institutions do not effectively execute these functions. There were functions that should be taken over whether the department in question wanted to or did not want to.
She highlighted the issue of the construction mafias. This had started out at 30% as an unintended consequence of the law, which indicated that 30% of whatever project had to be given to small businesses in the particular community. This was a very good initiative. However, Treasury has no actual involvement in this, rather the allocation of 30% of the work to small businesses was left to the main business or service provider. In the area of waste collection, the situation arose where the big contractors did not pay the small businesses resulting in small businesses not collecting the waste – and Treasury did not get involved. In terms of construction, the service provider often took on the major roles of construction and left the 30% that should be allocated to small businesses to deal with the finishing touches such as painting, tiling, etc. When small businesses did not do a good job – Treasury did not get involved. At what point was the shortfall of this 30% rule corrected? This has resulted in people using the 30% for criminal operations, for example, companies were brought in and paid and then did not finish the job, or the money was given to the big company, who did not pay small businesses or sub-contractors. This is a result of National Treasury and government excluding itself from the actual work being done on the ground. It was a good thing that small businesses had the opportunity to showcase their talents and skills and have an opportunity to grow, but the 30% should be properly accounted for to ensure that the rightful people were receiving the 30%.
Ms Lindy Bodewig, Chief Director: Technical Accounting Support, OAG, responded to the question on the National Treasury and OAG’s previous interventions. National Treasury has stepped in at a local government level, and interventions have been made in municipalities where Treasury and COGTA have taken over. There were instances where National Treasury had taken over a whole province, for example, the North West and Limpopo. There were instances also where National Treasury had intervened in a department. There were also instances where payments to municipalities were stopped due to poor performance. One of the challenges was to strengthen Treasury’s intervention powers. Treasury’s interventions powers had been used in the past. The intervention power envisaged in the Constitution was more difficult. Treasury was considering different mechanisms and ways to amend the MFMA and PFMA to give Treasury more power to intervene and prevent wrongdoings before it was too late. She noted suggestions for additional interventions, such as taking over some of the functions of CFOs. Treasury would consider this suggestion.
National Treasury had a great deal of engagement with accountant-generals in Africa. In many African countries, the accountant-general’s office was different from the South African case where the OAG was just responsible for providing guidance, standards and monitoring. In other African countries, the accountant-general’s office was the ‘super-CFO’ and made payments. This meant that departments and entities all had their budget and captured payments, but the accountant-general’s office released the payments. This was definitely something that National Treasury had to consider, in that if some CFOs did not deserve the responsibility, the OAG could take it over and the CFO would no longer have the capability of releasing payments. This was useful to consider. There have been several dedicated local government programmes that have taken technical experts and placed them in municipalities to assist with SCM and finance functions. There was strict reporting and monitoring by Treasury. Treasury has taken a step back to consider how effective these programmes were. An expert was placed in a municipality. While the person was there, they were responsible for improving and putting systems in place to assist the municipality and generate information, but what happened when the experts left the municipality. While there should be skills transfer, the question was how effective this was.
In terms of the grant frameworks and the conditional grants, Treasury was re-looking at these to understand how effective these frameworks were or if a different model or approach could be utilised. She suggested that further engagement on this matter was necessary.
There was a fine line between teaching someone their job and doing someone’s job. This has always been an issue that which National Treasury has struggled with. At this point, it seemed as if National Treasury had to do the jobs of CFOs and accounting officers, but then where was the accountability? Treasury needed to do better at teaching officials and enabling bodies like SCOPA to hold entities and officials accountable. Often, Treasury was asked to do things that meant Treasury was practically doing the jobs of certain officials. She indicated that further engagement with SCOPA would be useful in assisting Treasury in terms of sharing progress and enabling SCOPA to have an input on what Treasury and OAG were thinking of doing or changing in the future.
She responded to the question regarding the lifestyle audits. While this was not within the mandate of the OAG, it could be handled by the OCPO. This was a very important mechanism.
One of the aspects that OAG has never been able to touch on was the Ministerial Handbook. This could be another area where Treasury could build additional control and mechanisms around the ministerial roles and the consequences that could be applied in particular instances.
She said that the IFMS could allow Treasury to have a better role in terms of an early warning system. She agreed that Treasury and the OAG should play a more active role throughout the year. Treasury was looking at using the new transversal systems and thinking about using other mechanisms to enable Treasury to be more informed about challenges early on and look into interventions that could be applied early on. Treasury was looking into systems where Treasury could view the management findings, the audit plans of entities and how to monitor them, paying particular attention to entities that have disclaimers and continuous negative outcomes. Many discussions had taken place with IT companies, looking at how artificial intelligence (AI) could be used to generate intelligence and gather insights into what was happening in institutions. For example, Government Printing Works (GPW) lost data and was unable to prepare financial statements for three years because the data had been lost, and consecutive system meltdowns occurred. This did not seem like an accident. However, GPW spent a significant amount of money to reconstruct financial statements, get invoices and recapture, where AI could have been used to help generate this data. Treasury was considering these types of solutions to monitor departments.
In terms of the IFMS, Treasury and OAG have reached a point where they have agreed to disagree with the AG. Too much time was spent arguing without any movement. It did not service the country to continuously fight over IFMS not being implemented. There were stumbling blocks to implementation, but it was important to focus on moving ahead and implementing solutions to be able to implement the IFMS. There was alignment between the Minister, DG, and the Accountant-General in terms of the way forward, as well as absolute dedication to moving forward and getting the right skills to drive the programme. IFMS was an important aspect for accountability.
Regarding the CFO function, one of the exercises Treasury and the OAG considered was the performance of CFOs. CFOs were not supported in terms of their function. She felt it was important to question whether CFOs had the correct people supporting them. Accounting officers and CFOs were overwhelmed because they had to deal with audit outcomes, new budgets, and strategic plans. The focus should be on whether CFOs and accounting officers have sufficient capacity and how this capacity could be increased. As much as the focus should be on service delivery, but sometimes accountants were forgotten about and the capacity of administrative functions within departments to ensure that reports could be generated. CFOs and accounting officers should be supported throughout, ensuring that they have capacity to do their jobs. The report that OAG was busy with considered this issue.
Mr Khan noted the comments regarding the intervention powers of Treasury. This came back to the mandate of Treasury. National Treasury was in the process of reviewing the MFMA and the PFMA. This was an issue of how much capacity building Treasury did and at what point did Treasury take over. Did the law give Treasury the power to take over.
He responded to the questions regarding RAF. This matter was a legal matter involving the AG and the Accounting Standard Board. OAG provided technical support and guidance on which standards were applicable and which were not. OAG provided reports to the 6
th
Administration on the work OAG did.
Ms Bodewig elaborated on the issue of RAF. RAF has spent a lot of money arguing this matter and going to court. RAF was of the view that they had found a loophole in their accounting framework to reduce their liabilities. Instead of focusing on fixing the control environment, RAF reduced their liability to make performance look better. RAF reduced their liability to the point that they indicated their liabilities would only be recorded when they agreed to pay a claim. The claims that had not been paid or accepted would not be included in the entity’s liabilities. There had been a huge question mark regarding when liability should be recognised. This matter has been debated at great length with RAF, to the point that RAF has decided to change their accounting policy. OAG disagreed with RAF and had written to them to indicate this. RAF was adamant that their new accounting framework was the most appropriate way to reflect their liabilities. It could be appropriate to recognise liability when there is a car accident or someone makes a claim. RAF’s approach has not served accountability. There needed to be an agreement on how to move forward, rather than continuing to fight over the matter.
Mr Khan responded to the question regarding specialised audit services and the support provided to law enforcement agencies. The law gave Treasury the power to investigate any financial mismanagement. A report was drawn up indicating if there was any criminal conduct. These reports were shared with law enforcement agencies. National Treasury and law enforcement agencies then worked together to take these matters forward. National Treasury was a member of the National Priority Crimes Operational Committee (NPCOC) along with the Hawks, SAPS, etc, so there was close collaboration.
He said that the Public Procurement
Bill
addressed issues around political interference. The idea of blacklisting CFOs and accounting officers required a broader discussion with DPSA, as the responsible authority on recruitment matters.
He referred to the potential amendments. National Treasury and OAG were in the process of discussing and reviewing possible amendments. It would be useful to engage with SCOPA on this matter. The amendments to the PFMA and MFMA would be in existence for the next 20 years and it had to be ensured that the legislation was fit for purpose and served the public service.
Ms Ramoipone responded to the comments regarding the Western Cape payments. For the 2023/24 financial year, Western Cape was actually third; the top-performing province was Limpopo and the worst was Gauteng. In the first quarter, the analysis that had been conducted did not reflect Cape Town in the first position. Cape Town had previously held the first position and did quite well in terms of controls. Other institutions had good systems for tracking invoices, and OAG had engaged with these institutions to try and customise these systems for government use until the IFMS was implemented. There were issues with the payment of invoices; some issues were systematic, others were fraudulent, and some were people not knowing how to complete the processes. Accounting officers had indicated to OAG that the process of verifying bank details sometimes took long and the payment could not be processed.
The major issue experienced at National Treasury was the retention of documentation, even when dealing with the condoning of irregular expenditure. There had been a meeting with law enforcement agencies, who had raised this as an issue that was starting to become a transversal issue. It seems that no documents could be used to refer to certain issues. Even the auditors experienced this.
Ms Bodewig responded to the issue of the 30% of business that should be allocated to small businesses in communities. In the past Treasury had intervened in this matter. She was uncertain but thought that Treasury’s intervention had been ignored. OAG could report on this at a later stage. There have been a lot of investigations done which could better explain what Treasury had done.
Ms Mendoe Ntswahlana, Chief Procurement Officer, said that Treasury did not have a policy or framework in place for the 30%. This began in 2017 and was spearheaded by DPWI. This directive did not come from a National Treasury policy. She would not be able to say that this policy had emanated from National Treasury, Treasury and the OCPO was involved in terms of saying how best the matter could be dealt with and assisting institutions in implementing this.
The Chairperson thanked OAG for the presentation and their engagement.
The Chairperson said that OAG and SCOPA should work together to assist the Committee in its work. A general expectation was that a multi-disciplinary approach should be adopted. Some of what the reports asked for would require the cooperation between OAG, OCPO, AGSA, SIU and so on. Between these institutions, the Committee would arrive at a method of reporting that assisted the Committee in grasping an integrated picture. He recognised that different powers were allocated to different ministries. There were three spheres of government, and there were political problems in all three spheres, but these three spheres did not always engage with each other. It was politically ridiculous for the RAF to hold onto the sentiment that there was no minister for RAF to report to. It was ridiculous for RAF to take the matter to court when Cabinet was obliged to resolve issues between ministers and the agencies under them – this was unacceptable, but it continued to happen. This was not a Treasury problem; it was a political management problem. Everyone felt that they could do what they wanted and disobey the rules, with no consequences. There needed to be a conversation about the amendments to the PFMA and MFMA that Treasury was considering. When considering the lessons of the past 30 years, it had to be ensured that implementing the legislation helped move forward and reduce bureaucracy. It was also important that there were officials who would implement legislation properly and ensure that accounting standards and government standards were met. There had to be a way to portion responsibility to officials. There would be an ongoing discussion with various institutions to begin to tackle the issues being experienced systematically.
The Chairperson excused the officials from OAG. He indicated that the Accountant-General would remain in the meeting to answer any questions that may arise. The Chairperson requested that Members and presenters be mindful of time.
OCPO presentation
Ms Mendoe Ntswahlana, Chief Procurement Officer, presented OCPO’s briefing to the Committee.
The OCPO provided insight into the mandate and functions of the OCPO, as well as the functional structure.
The OCPO provided an overview of the general supply chain management (SCM) process. Indicating various elements that should be included at each stage of the process and the various steps. A model for the strategic sourcing process was provided, as well as a depiction of the procurement cycle.
OCPO highlighted the matter of procurement through deviations, whereby the accounting officer/authority may procure goods or services by other means provided that the reasons for deviation were recorded and approved by the accounting officer/authority. This included procurement through limited bidding. The presentation detailed the different types of limited bidding as well as the different types of contract variations.
Common reasons for deviations and expansions included sole supplier and/or singe source, closed bid process, business continuity, failed tender processes, value for money, and exceeding the 15% or 20% threshold for expansions, amongst others.
Root causes of deviations and expansions included poor planning, inadequate market research and analysis, poor contract management, a lack of understanding of the procurement legislation, and the use of emergency provision to enter into long term contracts, amongst others.

Discussion
The Chairperson thanked the OCPO for the presentation. He appreciated the offer to assist with very specific insights because an oversight period was coming up. It would be helpful to inform the Committee’s inquiries with information provided by the OCPO. He invited Members to share any questions.
Mr M Blose (EFF) said that the core interest was that the South African people received the services they needed. Treasury gave municipalities conditional grants which were an additional benefit to assist municipalities. However, the municipalities usually indicated that the time frame for them to spend this money was not in line with SCM processes. For example, in eThenkwini, after the disasters, the municipality was given R1.2 billion to alleviate the impact of the disasters. National Treasury wanted some of this money back and the municipality had applied for a rollover, and fortunately, this had been approved. Part of the municipality’s argument was that National Treasury gave them a grant and they had been given 12 months to spend it; within these 12 months, the municipality had to follow SCM processes and if they did not, they would be in trouble for non-compliance. How much were the SCM processes a deterrent in ensuring the municipalities deliver on services vis-à-vis them being a guard in terms of mismanagement of funds? Too much red tape did not enable accounting officers to speedily provide services. In terms of the 30-day payment to service providers, there needed to be an understanding of the SCM processes and whether this period was long enough for SCM processes to take place and deliver services timeously. He wanted to avoid the position where laws were being enforced that were limiting service delivery due to a need to comply with legislation. This had to be addressed if the legislation was a deterrent to service delivery. Legislation should be an enabler of service delivery, not a deterrent.
Mr Subrathie said that he had heard that there was too much value added tax (VAT) in the prices the state paid for commodities compared to the price when buying commodities ordinarily. How and what could be done to address this?
He asked if there was a conversation regarding the availability of cash flow and products and services. Often what was found at a local government level, was that this was a challenge. For example, an electricity department was unable to pay for parts because it did not have the capital, and by the time they were able to pay for the parts, problems had already arisen.
He said that when larger contracts and significant payments were made up front, the incentive for contractors to complete the deal or job or deliver the services was reduced simply because a substantial cash-flow benefit had already been received. Often, service providers lose interest in completing the job or providing the service because they have already received a larger percentage of the payment. This was the key cause of the failure of many big projects.
Ms Neale-May said that the legislation was very clear. She had noticed that there was a challenge in local government when municipalities received top-ups on their grant funding. Could National Treasury not consider procurement a year before? This has not been allowed, unless there was awareness that the money had been allocated. Sometimes, municipalities had their own capital budgets, municipalities could share their list of contracts and National Treasury could include these in the next budget and allow the procurement process to occur in the year before. For example, the fire engines which came from overseas, where the amount was budgeted for but the procurement process took so long. Getting rid of red tape while simultaneously monitoring procurement may require a register with National Treasury and the municipality to indicate the next year’s projects and the capital expenditure and to be able to get authorisation to do the procurement and not issue it until the new financial year. This would alleviate the time pressures of the procurement process.
If there were any issues in the procurement process National Treasury and the OCPO could address this as early as possible, so that as the financial year starts the municipality could hit the road running and begin projects rather than waiting to complete the procurement process. The insecurity of grant funding was a big challenge to local government. The procurement aspect of getting this funding was difficult, especially in emergencies. National Treasury and the OCPO could provide checks and balances earlier on in the process to ensure that money is not being sent back. All the challenges she has experienced in local government have been related to procurement and SCM. This was where officials could be deployed to assist municipalities and metros. Leeway was important. Monitoring was important. Being able to spend the money given to municipalities correctly was important. This would prevent emergency deviations outside of disaster situations. Regarding disaster situations, National Treasury and the OCPO should step in and guide municipalities on how the procurement processes should be conducted for the best outcome.
Mr Skosana said that SCM was the area where most institutions had issues and where corruption was taking place. Evaluation of SCM was where the problem was. In some institutions, during the evaluation process, officials removed documents from some tenders so that the same businesses or suppliers could be used. This allowed officials to utilise the same businesses and state that the other applicants did not comply due to documents that were missing, and then they were able to appoint the business of their choice. To what extent was the OCPO able to deal with these issues?
He referred to the issues of deviations and expansions. This was another area where corrupt officials made easy money. For example, corrupt officials could appoint someone to a R20 million tender knowing that the scope of the tender and cost would be expanded, knowing that the tender could not be pitched at R50 million. How could OCPO monitor such cases? If there were tenders of a certain amount and deviations, the accounting officer should report to the OAG or OCPO. How could this issue be addressed?
Mr Kubheka said panels had become a standard practice in many government entities. In terms of SCM, one of the slides highlighted transparency, equity, and fairness. Were these panels a strategic intervention on behalf of the procurement management? Was this the best way to do this from a fairness perspective? In this space, only 12-15 companies were doing the majority of contract work. When selected for the tender, these companies were chosen exactly as Mr Skosana had described.
The Chairperson referred to a statement made by the Finance Minister, which stated that government paid up to one and a half times the value of goods and services. This meant that government did not always receive value for money. What was the reason behind this trend?
Ms Ntswahlana said that when OCPO presented on deviations and expansions, it was mentioned that Treasury did the approvals. However, due to the reasons raised by Members, the accounting officers found that Treasury was encroaching on their mandate. In this instance, Treasury had been evaluating the applications and assessing applications and would approve/disapprove applications, but it was felt that National Treasury was delaying service delivery and as a result, the approvals and mandate were sent back. It was important to find a balance in these situations in terms of the mandate of Treasury and the mandate of accounting officers, and how these areas affected each other. When the law was being applied, it became a deterrent. When the law was ignored, it was non-compliance.
Disasters were not supposed to be deterred by the law. The MFMA and PFMA provided for emergency cases. The accounting officer had authority to apply its mandate. In the case of disasters, the option to wait for the law was not always available.
In terms of procurement in advance, municipalities had procurement plans that were meant to be put together that should depict the municipality’s priority over the Medium-Term Expenditure Framework (MTEF) period. This should be preceded by strategic sourcing to understand what commodities would be a challenge to access or procure when the time came to procure. The procurement plan had to be monitored – this was a part of OCPO’s responsibilities. The institution should be guided by its procurement plan. Regarding the procurement payment and the price difference or the state paying one and a half times – this was not true for all commodities. When it came to transversal contracts, this was where there was a high scale. When putting together a contract or putting out a bid, strategic sourcing should be done to determine which was the best option to get value for money. For example, institutions could not pay R50 for a loaf of bread if it could be bought for R20 at Pick n Pay. In terms of evaluation, some officials did remove documentation to allow their friends to get contracts at a higher price. The institution did have the right not to award tenders to an entity that was overpriced.
Ms Sizi Qolohle, Chief Director: Governance, Monitoring and Compliance, OCPO, said government has not dealt with this issue effectively. Institutions should consider how much funds are available and what option is the best value for money. Strategic sourcing was a key driver in reducing the total cost of ownership. Treasury has been engaging with institutions to convey that this process would likely reduce some costs. When procurement was done, it should have been approached from a strategic sourcing perspective. Treasury had conducted workshops on this approach and was satisfied with the turnout. OCPO was of the view that this approach would help procurement and budget challenges. It would also minimise risk.
Under the panel arrangement, suppliers would have been appointed to supply various goods and services for a set time period under agreed terms and conditions, at a good price. Slide five was a part of the planning. In procurement, it was critical to have robust planning methodologies. To assess the needs and specifications, this could be done by shopping around and seeing what other companies could provide the particular service. This had been achieved to some extent, but work still had to be done. Planning went hand in hand with the budget. OCPO was trying to assist institutions in understanding and conducting market research and indicating that market research would assist in providing specifications, specifications, in turn, would be guided by the assessments that were done. This meant that these matters would already be completed when the bidding process occurred. For example, an entity or department could not plan to build a dam if there was no budget. However, as part of the research, this could be done. The budget could also be realigned. In some cases, the budget was taken away despite the procurement plan being in place. Engagements were taking place to encourage robust planning methodologies to assist institutions in getting what they wanted.
The Chairperson said that the panel question asked how long these panels should be retained before they are refreshed. Was there a guideline for the appointment of the panel?
Ms Qolohle said that it depended on the institution. This had not yet been regulated. This was regulated by the institution.
Mr Kubheka said that it was not fair for a panel to sit for 24-36 months when there were other people capable of doing the same job.
Mr Maduna expressed that he had a problem with panels. There were few regulations in terms of how these panels should work. There were even panels of contractors to maintain buildings. There could not be such a panel because the scope of what had to be maintained was unknown. In terms of contractors, he was of the view that it was difficult to award a contract when there were contractors in the panel. Was this allowed?
He highlighted the issue of risk management in awarding projects. Could more than one project be awarded to the same contractor in one sitting? Risk management had to be done. What would happen if, during risk management, the contractor indicated that they could undertake multiple contracts or projects but were unable to deliver on this?
He highlighted the issue of quotes. For example, quotations would be requested if people were in a panel and a service was required. Was it allowed to get less than three quotes at that time, if the panel was already formed?
Mr Blose requested that the OCPO respond to these questions in writing to allow the Members time to have lunch before the House sitting.
