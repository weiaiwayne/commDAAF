Meeting Summary

Health
The Portfolio Committee on Health convened a virtual meeting for a briefing on the Department of Health 2022/23 Annual Report with both Minister and Deputy Minister present.
Committee Members asked many questions. Among notable concerns, was the financial risk to the Health Department budget as the contingent liability for medico-legal claims stood at R77 billion; underspending of the conditional grant by R2.3 billion; audit findings of the provincial heath departments especially Northern Cape; irregular; fruitless and wasteful expenditure; record keeping systems; and accruals. Gauteng province alone had underspent R1.1 billion on its conditional grant.
Members stated it was totally unacceptable that NDoH has underspent whilst the infrastructure of its healthcare facilities was in a dilapidated state, maternal care in rural hospitals was under-capacitated that resulted in infant mortality and disability such as cerebral palsy, patients not adequately fed and inadequate medicine supplies.
The Committee urged NDoH to take consequence management against those who were responsible for irregular expenditure and to capacitate its healthcare facilities with adequate medicine supplies and sufficient staff. Reference was made to racial representation of the South African Medical Research Council (SAMRC). Members enquired about the backlogs of blood alcohol tests in Pretoria and Johannesburg, implementation of the national digital health strategy, Health Ombudsman Bill, Northern Cape governance instability, Youth Zones (Adolescent and Youth Friendly Services), project management for the infrastructure build challenges, funding for the Office of Health Standards Compliance, “drop off” zone at healthcare facilities for unwanted newborn babies, destroyed COVID-19 vaccines, mental health programme, Ideal Clinic standards, recovery plans for HIV and TB and Digital Vibes irregular expenditure consequence management.
Meeting report
The Chairperson acknowledged the Minister and Deputy Minister Sibongiseni Dhlomo.
Minister’s opening remarks
Minister Joe Phaahla reminded everyone that it was only about a year ago that restrictions on gatherings and wearing of masks were lifted in June 2022. Further, the WHO only lifted the status of COVID-19 as constituting a public health emergency of international concern on 8 May 2023.
He remarked that NDoH had performed well under the heavy cloud of the pandemic.
The NDoH mission is to ensure that South Africans can live a healthy life and its key mandate is the promotion of health. He corrected the misconstrued idea that NDoH is dealing only with curing illnesses or is a department of hospitals. In essence, it is a department which must promote health, prevent disease and galvanise the country in the prevention of diseases both non-communicable and communicable as well as injuries. NDoH is also responsible for early detection of illness and then initiating treatment and the most appropriate level of care at the best possible quality with the available resources.
NDoH focused on recovering the provision of comprehensive health services at the Primary Health level including at community level through its community health workers right up to the most advanced specialised services as it moved its resources back from dealing with COVID.
NDoH begins to refocus on the fight against HIV/AIDS, TB and sexually transmitted infections. It has made progress as South Africa has the biggest antiretroviral programme in the world. It currently has about 5.8 million people who are on ARVs. The number of people known to have tested positive in the region is 7 million so there is a gap of just over 1.2 million. NDoH is making plans to reconnect them with and encourage them to start treatment.
NDoH is working on TB recovery which is going to remain the challenge. However, the silver lining is that with TB, the current health service can treat it up to cure. The country is still under 80% in terms of success of cure rate. The Deputy President launched the new National Strategic Plan for HIV/ AIDS just before the end of 2022/23.
South Africa was not only dealing with the burden of infectious diseases but also faced episodes of acute infections like cholera early this year. He was glad to inform the Committee that the country has not had a new case. It had got to the bottom of a recent case and established it was actually a patient who had presented himself in May with cholera.
NDoH is doing everything in the prevention of cancer and provides support for oncology treatment in the provinces.
NDoH recognises the health of mother and children which is the key to every country. NDoH has made some improvements in maternal mortality ratio, infant mortality but Minister did concede that it could do better.
With the restrictions on alcohol lifted after COVID-19, casualty units are flooded with injuries caused by motor vehicle accidents and interpersonal violence. This is a community matter which we need to work with all in society.
NDoH has had a reduction in budget allocations that are not only stunted but even reduced. This is impacting on its services in future. However, he was happy to inform the Committee that as far as accounting for the resources allocated to NDoH, this is improving. In the past financial year, NDoH had the challenge in as far after many years of unqualified audit from the general, NDoH had the challenge in the last financial year in 2022 in recouping funds from the private sector who are administering vaccines and providing vaccinations. The recouping of funds resulted in the qualified audit. NDoH had now returned to an unqualified finding. Further, many provincial health departments are also getting unqualified audits.
The Minister noted the health strike which had disrupted a lot of services but he was happy to report that subsequent to that, over the last six months, there has been very good stability and NDoH is able to focus again on the improvement of the provision of services to our people.
Department of Health 2022/23 Annual Report
Dr Sandile Buthelezi, NDoH Director-General, noted that overall NDoH achieved 31 out of its 52 targets which is a 59.6% rate. He outlined the performance of its programmes. Areas that under-performed included the TB programme, the slow uptake of COVID vaccinations across all ages groups, as well as quality and safety of care.
Mr Phaswa Mamogale, NDoH Chief Financial Officer, stated that 97.4% of the 2022/23 budget was spent. Underspending was in compensation of employees, goods and services amounting to R49 million. NDoH reported that it had spent 95.9% of its conditional grant with R2.4 billion underspent. The Gauteng province incurred a R1.78 billion underspending.
NDoH provided a summary of the audit outcomes of provincial health departments from 2016/17 to 2022/23. Western Cape provincial health department continued to receive clean audits.

Ministe's response
Health Minister Phaahla reassured the Committee that NDoH medico-legal claims is a permanent item at its National Health Council meetings and it was working together on this. He confirmed that 75% of NDoH budget is being spent on claims. NDoH has no control over some elements of the actual submissions of claims to the courts but where it does have control is reducing the opportunity for claims. NDoH case management system is a comprehensive system of prevention. In the event that there is a claim, NDoH should focus on how to adequately utilise various interventions such as reliable record systems.
He fully agreed that medical negligence must be dealt with but he pointed out that there is also collusion. NDoH was informed by its own staff in health facilities that staff stole the physical records and provide them to the litigants and to the legal representatives. When the case comes, hospital managers cannot find those records but they surface in court where the lawyers have a copy. He noted such an incident in a provincial health department. Such incidents highlight the need for digitalisation. Some collusion cases can even be traced to the Office of the State Attorney.
Minister Phaahla noted that the national department does not have absolute authority over its provincial counterparts because healthcare is a service which the Constitution has designated to both national and provincial governments. There are certain operational aspects that take place at the provincial level and the national department does not always get cooperation. However, NDoH is treating the matter very seriously and is doing everything to ensure that the claims will be reduced, if not totally eliminated. NDoH also noted some court rulings were appealed. Since a large chunk of the medical claims are for future medical costs, there are court rulings that if our public health providers can prove that we have the necessary ability to look after these patients where there has been a ruling of medical negligence, then the court will accept that. However, there are lawyers who are approaching the court to appeal this judgement.
On youth employment, NDoH is working on this but is facing financial pressure so it struggled to meet the 30% target. NDoH is not a frontline service; however, frontline services such as hospitals have accomplished this target or have been closer to this youth employment target.
On gender disparity, NDoH performance on this 50% target was not far off at 46%. With more measures in place such as retention strategies, training and empowerment, this target should be achievable. At the middle management level, there is quite a huge cohort of female managers.
The youth zone is a comprehensive service that deals with young people who are not coming forward for testing as well as family planning and teenage pregnancy. NDoH is making sure that each and every district has a number of youth zones especially at primary health care facilities. The numbers of youth zones in those areas would be provided to the Committee in writing.
NDoH had dedicated a lot of resources to deal with cancer treatment. A certain portion of the conditional grant is given to provinces to ensure that they capacitate oncological services where it was lacking. NDoH has also put a lot of work into cancer awareness programmes. It has partnered with some of the NGOs such as Pink Tribe to spread the message about early detection, screening, cancer advocacy and awareness promotion. NDoH provides services including screening and testing for breast cancer, cervical cancer and prostate cancer. The Minister agreed that NDoH could always do more. He confirmed that there is a long waiting list for those in need of these services.
The Minister clarified that R 3.5 billion referred to the funds that NDoH had already committed to vaccines. Yesterday, at the National Health Council meeting, it approved various changes in terms of the children's vaccine programmes. A rubella vaccine was added into the system.
The Minister confirmed that there all ongoing discussions with the CFO on the budget of the Office of Health Standards Compliance. The option has been explored if the Office would be able to get a bit more additional financial resources from its savings for the current financial year. Due to the constrained fiscus, Treasury has instructed NDoH to put on hold the Ombud Bill which would separate the Ombuds Office from the Office of Health Standards Compliance. There had been a meeting that government should be decreasing entities instead of increasing them.
The Minister assured the Committee that there have been various interventions through the CFO Forum and the National Health Council’s technical committee led by the DG where experiences are shared. NDoH has also identified common areas where provinces do struggle.
The Minister agreed that NDoH needs to upgrade its capacity on caesarean sections. The NDoH top clinical specialist team has been trying to ensure that every district does have at the minimum an obstetrician who is supervising all maternity services in that district and providing leadership and training. NDoH expects that regional hospitals should have qualified obstetricians.
The challenge, however, is to attract and retain obstetricians in rural areas. NDoH strategy to that is that it should at least have the minimum at district level. Also, it is not only the obstetrician that is in shortage in rural areas but also anaesthetist, paediatrician and advanced midwives. So that is the area where NDoH is trying to improve its capacity, provide leadership and expertise to reduce the burden of birth injuries including cerebral paralysis and other bad injuries.
The Minister replied that he would discuss with the Department the “drop off” areas for unwanted newly born infants. He affirmed his view that there should be a safe pathway for women who have just given birth but do not want their newborn, regardless of social or psychological reasons. Having a safe pathway would ensure that baby's safety.
NDoH has made it very clear to its health facilities that any pregnant visitor under 16 must be regarded as a case of statutory rape because that is where the law stands. In the case where it involves a minor teenage boy, it is an issue which the law must deal with.
On transformation in SAMRC, it is a matter one would have to have a look at. Females are adequately represented but the fair representation of races should be discussed further.
The Minister assured the Committee that mental health is one of the priorities on the NDoH agenda. It has been included in its Annual Performance Plan (APP). There are mental health services at its health facilities from the primary level to the specialised level. He acknowledged that there is backlog as a result of the mental health explosion caused by COVID-19.
The Minister acknowledged that the infrastructure challenge in the Eastern Cape is huge. NDoH is working on it and it has just opened a brand new hospital in OR Tambo district. A number of teaching hospitals there also need NDoH attention. NDoH was disappointed that more than R300 million in infrastructural grants had not been spent in 2022/23 despite the poor infrastructure. A huge number of health facilities in Gauteng also needed attention. NDoH has been discussing with Treasury various funding models to fund the infrastructure programme. It included a blended financing model which aimed to work with private partners and not rely only on the fiscus resource.
The Minister denied that CMS struggled to get a hold of him because they had convened a afternoon meeting for more than three hours last week Thursday.
The Minister commented on Ms Clarke's questions which have been asked on every other platform including on the NHI funding sources. He reiterated that the fund is already in the system. South Africa can provide NHI services to its people with its current GDP spending on health expenditure. It has been agreed that the equitable share to frontline services will not be changed. NDoH is also in discussion with Treasury on the way forward on the wage bill as a result of the wage agreement earlier this year. It may have to make adjustments such as the R35 billion tax credits in the private sector which could be reallocated to the public health system.
The Minister explained that in the current year, 2023/24, it would look at the State Liability Amendment Bill with the Department of Justice.
The Minister said NDoH is working very closely with the provincial health departments of the Eastern Cape, Gauteng and KZN since those three provinces have the most medico-legal claims.
The Minister replied that there is no provision for the national department to bail out provincial health departments. When there is accrual due to medico-legal claims, the national and provincial department have to discuss and work out a way to close the gap.
The Minister apologised for the spelling errors in the presentation.
The Minister agreed that underspending on key budgets such as infrastructure is unacceptable, but it needs to play ahead of time and improve the department's infrastructure team capacity.
On Digital Vibes, NDoH did take steps in 2021 which resulted in the suspension and charge of some officials. Consequence management measures included demotion, suspension without pay for a couple of months and so on. On the recovery of the funds, the Special Investigating Unit (SIU) is pursuing that as NDoH is not the expert in that area.
On target non-performance, the Minister attributed the decrease in performance to Programme 1 on equity targets. Medico-legal claims also constituted many areas of performance measurement. Also, the poor performance of its provincial counterparts may also affect non-performance of the national department.
The Minister noted that the current load shedding it did not affect the Central Chronic Medicines Dispensing and Distribution (CCMDD). Medicines such as insulin and vaccines are not part of the CCMDD. Those which need cold chain are being managed strictly in facilities. All NDoH facilities with cold chain have backup generators. NDoH has not lost vaccines and other medicines which need cold chain.
NDoH has a new recovery plan post-COVID which was driving the recent improvement. Although the programme has not been implemented to the level which it anticipated, the Minister assured the Committee that this has been attended to.
On infrastructure project management, the Minister stated that NDoH is improving its capacity at national but is also working with the provinces in terms of a contract management project to ensure that there is value for money.
NDoH does have a report on the hospitals that have been assessed by the Office of Health Standards Compliance. It will make the report available to the Committee.
On Ideal Clinics, NDoH does have interventions through our primary healthcare division to look at those requirements and standards which must be met for infrastructure, human resources, IT equipment. NDoH is also supporting its provincial counterparts on that. The Minister hopes that the Committee will be able to see progress in its quarterly report.
The Census on population is providing good lessons for NDoH. Even though NDoH is not at the zero baseline, it is now able to receive an update on where the people are, where the big numbers of the population are and also other information. This information can be used for future planning in the next financial year.
As a result of a National Health Council decision, NDoH is putting together a document which looks at all aspects of the lessons from COVID on the use of modern technology. The utilization of health technology is saving costs for human resources and for resource optimisation of health professionals. For instance, in Limpopo, the rural health strategy outlines that patients should be able to acquire modern X-rays, CT scans, mammograms and so on. In rural hospitals, with the use of technology, tests can be conducted in the absence of a radiologist or a specialist in that area.
The Minister highlighted the importance of consent in sharing medical history within health technology. NDoH is building on the products of artificial intelligence and to what extent the health sector could benefit from the Fourth Industrial Revolution.
The Minister noted the concern about irregular expenditure at its entities and guaranteed that NDoH will be doing a follow up. The Northern Cape Health MEC reported to national department yesterday on the governance instability in the Northern Cape Health Department. The provincial department had not had an HoD for a long time. The newly appointed HoD had been charged and possibly will be on full suspension very soon.
Department response
Director General, Dr Sandile Buthelezi, reported that NDoH had procured 62 million vaccines in total and then it received 8.4 million vaccines from donations mainly from the US government which then made just over 70 million vaccines that South Africa had. Of these vaccines, NDoH currently has destroyed 2.9 million doses of Pfizer and 16 million doses of J&J. There are others that will be destroyed in the next five months. The total cost of the vaccines that would be destroyed combined with those that were donated and those that the country procured came up to approximately R 3.6 billion. Excluding those that were donated, then it is about R2.2 billion. NDoH had to destroy them because NDoH would be incurring fruitless expenditure to store them whilst they are no longer going to be used.
On the issue of ICT, NDoH has taken quite a lot of steps to address the weaknesses and governance of ICT which includes the whole issue of cybersecurity. Currently, SITA is working on improving NDoH cyber security. NDoH has also appointed the ICT steering Committee that is overseeing all the ICT issues that have been raised.
Mr Phaswa Mamogale reported to the Committee that there was a discussion in NDoH around funding around R10 million during the current financial year for the Office of the Ombuds to be able to deal with the backlogs of investigations so that those processes would be finalised. That process would happen at the adjustment process.
On the under-spending of fund, he reported that the most underspending came from earmarked funding which NDoH was unable to shift those funding to address other commitments that include that R712 million for vaccines, R396 million for Limpopo academic hospital and R85 million under the capitation under the NHI. NDoH will implement stop and reallocation to address the underspending in the provinces so that we are able to attend to the provinces that are having a budget pressure while others have underspending.
On the quality of financial statements, he said that it was necessary to correct the previous financial statement errors and the quality of financial statements has been noted. Together with the national department and the provinces, as a sector, it is strengthening the internal review of its financial reports which includes the audits that should be done by the internal audit. There has been additional internal audit staff appointed solely to improve and strengthen finances and other aspects.
He highlighted that the national CFO forum will continue to discuss and resolve some of the sector related issues which includes the medical litigation which is the contingency liabilities which NDoH had in the Eastern Cape as well as some of the issues outstanding Limpopo.
NDoH continues to have the 10 by 10 engagement which include the nine provincial health departments and one national department and the nine provincial AGs and one national Auditor-General. That engagement process helps clears some of those inconsistencies that were made clear during the audit processes.
On unauthorised, irregular and fruitless expenditures, there were 14 cases that have been investigated by the internal audit. Some have been finalised. Currently, NDoH is within the process of doing the disciplinary processes so the matters have been referred to Labour Relations to be able to assist with the disciplinary processes. As soon that that process concludes, NDoH can then apply for the condonement of expenditures to the National Treasury.
On R359 million said as irregular expenditure, he confirmed that it was true that the amount is waiting to be condoned. Some of those have been investigated and consequence management are implemented. He highlighted to the Committee that NDoH did not incur any irregular expenditure. The R19.1 million as said was incurred in 2020/2021.
For fruitless and wasteful expenditure for R1.5 million, it's also the case from previous financial years. There was only R49 000 for the year under review which was related to SARS penalties to NDoH for late payments. Upon the investigation, NDoH also realised that NDoH had made the payment in time but then it was not transmitted in time. Hence, that fruitless expenditure would be reversed.
On payments made not within 30 days, 7 653 invoices have been paid within 30 days while NDoH also had 2786 which were not paid within 30 days. In terms of the value, the invoices which were paid in 30 days accounted for over 95% of the total payment value. The delays were mainly due to the invoices that it had from CCMD and also legal services for supporting the provinces. Those were the invoices coming from different call centres and hence there might have been delays. For those payments which were not made within 30 days, he highlighted the increasing accruals which are a direct impact to the sector in terms of compliance. As a national Department of Health, it managed to reduce its accrual by 39.9% or rather reduced from R750.9 million to R450.7 million in the previous financial year. NDoH is aware that there is pressure from provinces in budget reduction as it continued to put pressure on the critical items within the sector which includes medicine, accounts, launching services, food and so on. Also, the accrual has increased by 7.3% which had increased from R16.8 billion to around R80.1 billion. So NDoH urged the Committee to understand that the health budget reduction will continue to put pressure to the sector make especially the provinces to provide the services.
NDoH has had engagement with the National Treasury on the funding for the health sector. It also highlights that the budget cuts would negatively affect service delivery.
Dr Nicolas Crisp said that NDoH has a digital development environment in preparation for NHI. But even without NHI, it is able to link the many different digital activities that are going on in the public sector, across the provinces and in the private sector. He clarified the difference between ICT which is what happens in NDoH for managing its own connectivity and the activities within NDoH itself and the digital branch. In the NHI branch, the digital development of the digital system will link everybody and that includes the various databases, major elements of the components, etc. He described this digitilisation path a very long road but NDoH has made significant inroads in the recent past and has an active support plan for the next five years.
Ms Milani Wolmarans emphasized that digitalisation requires huge investment in the IT infrastructure that includes the equipment, the broadband connectivity, continuous electricity supply, etc. it also requires investment in the development of different solutions which we call the health information exchange. With different systems and solutions communicating with each other, then a uniform health information system for South Africa would be developed. Research has shown that 70% of the effort should go into the people that use the systems because ultimately the efficiency and the effectiveness of a solution is around the people using the system which includes not only the healthcare workers but also our public who can build trust in the digital solutions.
South Africa has the Digital Health Strategy 2019-2024 which provides the broad strategic areas for intervention within the digital health context. It must be noted that the digital health strategy was approved in 2019 and in 2020 a lot of the resources had to be redirected to develop digital solutions to support the COVID-19 interventions in the country. Therefore, there was a delay in some of the areas in the digital health strategy. At the moment, NDoH is in the process of reviewing the digital strategy so that it can remain relevant as the digital landscape is evolving and it actually evolves on a daily basis. NDoH has worked on gradually integrating the digital healthcare solution towards the achieving of one uniform information system for South Africa which will then contribute to the quality of healthcare. Digital health solutions in the way that has been evolved could be seen as a transition into an intervention to provide our services. Similar to providing medicines etc, it can improve how we render our services and also the quality of our services. The most important one is the health patient registration system. That system collects patients’ medical history information from the point of service at any health facilities. In the context of the NHI, NDoH would also need to look at what cost the service was being provided. The health patient registration system has previously been reported and has been installed in 3227 public health establishments. 3138 of them are primary healthcare facilities. 89 of them are hospitals. The system was designed to be intuitive so that patients can use smartphone to access the system. NDoH also provides training from facility to facility to ensure the use and the implementation of the system.
NDoH has developed the master health facility platform where it currently has 55,000 facilities. Each one of them is being given a unique identifier. It has also done coding and extensive work on the enterprise architecture for the digital solution which is being used with the electronic vaccination data system. NDoH has developed a very extensive electronic vaccination data system within three weeks and it has been going live within three weeks. She also highlighted that it was the first time in the history of South Africa where a system was designed by government, owned by the government and funded by government which was extensively used in both the public and private sector. NDoH is now ready to move into medical record which is a portable record. In other words, if a person has received services in one facility using all these linkages, it would be possible for a clinician in another facility to see what services a person has received. However, it requires huge investment and NDoH has developed a five-year plan and the estimated plan on the cost for the development is going to be approximately R1.5 billion. Because of the huge cost, NDoH will therefore be using a modular approach. The first module of this chronic health record will be live and starting to roll out in the first quarter of the next financial year. One of the critical things that's required in terms of quality service delivery is broadband connectivity and NDoH has developed this situation to work with mobile service providers. But this is funding dependent for NDoH to implement that solution.
On telemedicine, NDoH also uses SMS mobile solutions where they recently deployed youth chatbot where they use AI to do that. NDoH is using AI and specifically with machine learning in the national Department of Health such as the big data platform which allows NDoH to take different information systems and links that into one record for the patient. NDoH participated in an ethics and governance workshop on artificial intelligence in health with the WHO. NDoH is in the process of including a chapter on the use of artificial intelligence and governance in the digital health governance framework. Especially if there is resource constraint, AI could greatly help improve efficiency and productivity.
NDoH has a data centre that complies with utmost security standards and security controls for the digital health space. It does regular penetration testing. It has a partnership with the Council for Science and Industrial Research where they are working together with the cybersecurity specialists. One of the critical areas that NDoH needed to include further in its development is when there is a duplicate site which is not on the same premise as the site where data is being hosted. That area will be looked at that in the next financial year.
Ms Jeanette Hunter informed the Committee that in terms of infrastructure, many of the clinics under NDoH are small because they were built many years ago. Also, there is the issue of human resources which prompted the question of if clinics have dedicated supervisors that can lead the process. Even though NDoH didn't make its target, it did significantly increase the numbers that it had the year before. The initiative of blended funding certainly will eradicate that hurdle of dilapidated infrastructure.
17% of primary healthcare facilities do not have operational managers which was about 578 facilities across the country.
Ms Gambu said that the equity targets were determined by the Department of Planning, Monitoring and Evaluation (DPME). In addition, NDoH of Public Service and Administration had a directive in 2009 which states that the number of young people involved in a leadership or internship programme should be at least 5% of the establishment in NDoH. Unfortunately, in the past two years, NDoH performance against the target has been largely influenced by fiscal constraints. NDoH is going to engage both the DPME and DPSA to highlight the challenge of adhering to those targets in light of the budget reduction.
Dr Pillay said that there are a number of challenges in terms of service delivery for forensic chemistry labs. NDoH has moved the forensic chemistry labs to the NHLS so that they could increase the equipment as well as improve the skills and number of the staff so that they can deal with these backlogs. The huge backlogs that are here in Gauteng are largely because the huge number of tests that they have to do which were much higher than Durban or Cape Town. Consequently, what the NHLS has done is that they are increasing the amount of equipment here in the labs in Gauteng and moving to new premises at CSIR where they will be able to have additional staff. That would reduce the backlog.
Mr Ramphelane Morewane, NDoH Acting Deputy Director-General for HIV/AIDS, TB and MCWH (Maternal Child and Women's Health) highlighted the four key principles of the NDoH TB recovery plan. It is about finding people; treating them; retaining them in care and advocating for prevention. This is undergirded by five cardinal pillars which are based on communication and advocacy; finding and linking people to care; treat and retain; prevent and prepare; and monitoring and assessment. NDoH realised that that this is linked to the number of patients that are also defaulting or disengaged from HIV/AIDS treatment.
On the youth zones in the Northern Cape, there are about 6.7 facilities to one youth zone amongst the 161 primary healthcare facilities which he believed was really a great fit. NDoH does hope that the number will increase.
Deputy Minister Dhlomo said that the foresight of NDoH was that nine years ago, NDoH had thought that they should also be trying and starting cancer prevention, especially for young girls. The process has gone very well. To date, the country now has about up to 19 years of age when those young girls were actually vaccinated at an early age. The WHO commended the work of the NDoH and encouraged it to strengthen the programme.
The Chairperson adjourned the meeting.
