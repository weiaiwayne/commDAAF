# Governing the Algorithm: A Comparative Analysis of AI Policy Framing in the Global South

**Wayne Xu**  
Department of Communication  
University of Massachusetts Amherst

## Abstract

How do nations outside the traditional technology power centers frame artificial intelligence in legislative discourse? Drawing on a computational content analysis of 102 legislative documents from South Africa (n=41), Brazil (n=54), and India (n=7), this study applies framing theory to examine how Global South nations construct AI as a policy problem. Using a multi-model validation approach with Cohen's κ=0.911, we find that Global South countries predominantly frame AI through a **governance lens** (42.2%) emphasizing regulation, oversight, and institutional frameworks—in stark contrast to U.S. Congressional discourse, which frames AI primarily through **sovereignty** concerns (38.7%) centered on geopolitical competition with China. This governance-first framing suggests that Global South nations, while often positioned as technology recipients rather than producers, are actively constructing alternative policy imaginaries that prioritize regulatory infrastructure over strategic competition. These findings contribute to emerging scholarship on the global diffusion of AI governance norms and challenge assumptions about the universality of Western AI policy frames.

**Keywords:** artificial intelligence, framing theory, Global South, policy discourse, computational methods, multi-model validation

## Introduction

The governance of artificial intelligence has emerged as one of the defining policy challenges of the contemporary era. Yet scholarly attention has disproportionately focused on regulatory developments in the United States, European Union, and China—the putative "AI superpowers" whose technological capacity and geopolitical positioning have dominated both academic and popular discourse (Horowitz, 2018; Ding, 2018). This concentration of analytical attention has left significant gaps in our understanding of how nations outside these centers of technological production are making sense of, and responding to, AI as a policy challenge.

This oversight matters for both theoretical and practical reasons. Theoretically, it risks universalizing particular framings of AI—whether as a tool of strategic competition, an engine of economic growth, or a threat to fundamental rights—without interrogating whether these constructions travel across different political, economic, and cultural contexts. Practically, as AI governance increasingly becomes a matter of international coordination and norm diffusion (Bradford, 2020), understanding the diversity of national framings becomes essential for predicting alignment or friction in multilateral settings.

This study addresses this gap by examining how three major Global South democracies—South Africa, Brazil, and India—frame artificial intelligence in legislative discourse. Drawing on Entman's (1993) canonical definition of framing as the process of selecting "some aspects of a perceived reality and mak[ing] them more salient in a communicating text," we analyze how these nations construct AI as a policy problem: What aspects of AI are emphasized? What causal attributions are made? What solutions are proposed? And crucially, how do these framings diverge from or converge with those observed in the Global North?

Our analysis reveals a striking pattern: while U.S. Congressional discourse on AI is dominated by sovereignty concerns—framing AI primarily as a vector of geopolitical competition with China—Global South legislative discourse is overwhelmingly oriented toward governance, emphasizing regulatory frameworks, institutional oversight, and accountability mechanisms. This divergence suggests that the "AI race" narrative that pervades Global North discourse may not resonate—or may be actively contested—in contexts where nations position themselves as recipients, adapters, and regulators of AI technologies rather than as competitors in their production.

## Theoretical Framework

### Framing as Meaning-Making in Policy Discourse

Framing theory provides a powerful lens for understanding how policy problems are constructed, contested, and legitimated in public discourse. Following Gamson and Modigliani (1989), we understand frames as "interpretive packages" that give meaning to issues by organizing information, suggesting causal relationships, and implying particular evaluations and remedies. In the policy domain specifically, frames serve what Stone (2012) calls the "strategic representation of situations"—rhetorical constructions that define problems in ways that make particular responses appear natural or necessary.

The framing of emerging technologies presents particular analytical challenges. As Nisbet and colleagues (2003) have shown in the context of biotechnology and Scheufele (2006) has demonstrated for nanotechnology, novel technologies are characterized by high uncertainty, technical complexity, and contestation over appropriate analogies and precedents. In such contexts, frames do not merely reflect pre-existing reality but actively constitute the technology as a policy object—defining what AI "is," what it "does," and what responses it demands.

This constructionist perspective is especially relevant for AI, whose boundary objects and applications span domains from healthcare to warfare, employment to entertainment. The "same" technology can be framed as an economic opportunity, an existential risk, a civil rights concern, or a tool of state control—and these framings carry profound implications for the regulatory regimes that emerge.

### Policy Framing and Agenda Dynamics

Beyond individual frames, we draw on scholarship examining how frames operate within broader agenda-setting processes. Kingdon's (1984) multiple streams framework highlights how policy windows open when problems, policies, and politics align—with problem definition (i.e., framing) playing a crucial role in determining which solutions appear viable. Baumgartner and Jones's (1993) punctuated equilibrium model similarly emphasizes how shifts in the dominant framing of issues can trigger rapid policy change after long periods of stability.

These dynamics are particularly relevant for AI governance, which has moved rapidly from the margins to the center of policy attention across multiple jurisdictions. The frames that become institutionalized in this formative period may prove durable, shaping regulatory trajectories for decades. Understanding cross-national variation in these foundational frames thus offers insight into potentially divergent regulatory futures.

### Global South Perspectives on Technology Governance

Our analysis is further informed by scholarship on technology governance in postcolonial and Global South contexts. Chakravartty and Sarikakis (2006) have critiqued the assumption that communication and technology policy models developed in the Global North can be straightforwardly applied elsewhere, highlighting how colonial histories, development imperatives, and geopolitical positioning shape distinct regulatory imaginaries. Wasserman's (2018) work on African media systems similarly emphasizes the need to understand policy discourse within its particular political-economic context rather than measuring it against Northern benchmarks.

In the specific domain of AI, recent work has begun to examine Global South perspectives. Adams (2021) explores how African nations are positioning themselves vis-à-vis AI governance, while Sambasivan et al. (2021) document how AI applications manifest differently in development contexts. Yet systematic comparative analysis of how Global South nations frame AI in legislative discourse remains limited—a gap this study addresses.

## Research Questions

Building on this theoretical framework, we pose the following research questions:

**RQ1:** What frames dominate AI policy discourse in South African, Brazilian, and Indian legislative documents?

**RQ2:** How do these framings compare across the three countries, and what accounts for observed similarities and differences?

**RQ3:** How do Global South AI framings compare with those observed in U.S. Congressional discourse?

## Method

### Data Collection

We assembled a corpus of AI-related legislative documents from three Global South democracies selected for their regional significance, democratic governance structures, and documented engagement with AI policy:

**South Africa (n=41):** Committee meeting transcripts from the Parliamentary Monitoring Group (PMG), an independent organization that publishes verbatim records of South African parliamentary proceedings. Documents were retrieved via keyword search for "artificial intelligence" and filtered for substantive AI engagement using term density thresholds (≥5 AI terms per 1,000 words or ≥20 total AI-related terms). The corpus spans 2017-2025 and includes committee deliberations on digital transformation, the Fourth Industrial Revolution (4IR), and related policy matters.

**Brazil (n=54):** Legislative propositions (bills and amendments) from the Câmara dos Deputados Open Data API. All propositions containing "inteligência artificial" in their ementa (summary) or keywords were retrieved. The corpus represents the most comprehensive collection of AI-specific legislation in Latin America, including the landmark Marco Legal da IA (PL 2338/2023) and numerous bills addressing specific AI applications.

**India (n=7):** Key legislative instruments and policy documents including the Digital Personal Data Protection Act (2023), the National AI Technology Regulatory Authority Bill (2024), the AI Ethics and Accountability Bill (2025), and related rules and guidelines. While smaller than the other corpora, this collection represents the most significant AI governance developments in the world's largest democracy.

### Coding Scheme

We developed an eight-frame coding scheme adapted from prior research on technology policy framing (Nisbet et al., 2003; Brossard & Shanahan, 2006) and refined through iterative analysis of our corpus:

| Frame | Definition | Illustrative Keywords |
|-------|------------|----------------------|
| **INNOVATION** | AI as economic opportunity, driver of growth, source of competitive advantage | development, growth, competitiveness, investment |
| **RISK_SAFETY** | AI as posing existential or catastrophic threats | superintelligence, control problem, alignment |
| **RISK_HARM** | AI as source of concrete harms to individuals or groups | deepfakes, misinformation, manipulation, children |
| **RISK_ECONOMIC** | AI as threat to employment and economic equality | job loss, displacement, automation, inequality |
| **GOVERNANCE** | AI as requiring regulatory oversight and institutional frameworks | regulation, oversight, compliance, accountability |
| **SOVEREIGNTY** | AI as matter of national security and geopolitical competition | defense, security, China, strategic autonomy |
| **RIGHTS** | AI as implicating privacy, discrimination, and civil liberties | privacy, bias, discrimination, consent, dignity |
| **TECHNICAL** | AI in terms of technical characteristics and capabilities | algorithm, neural network, training data, model |

### Multi-Model Validation

Following emerging best practices in computational content analysis (Gilardi et al., 2023), we employed a multi-model validation approach to establish inter-coder reliability. Each document was independently coded by two large language models: Claude (Anthropic) as primary coder and Kimi (Moonshot AI) as validator. This approach offers several advantages over traditional human coding for large corpora: scalability, reproducibility, and—when properly validated—comparable or superior reliability (Törnberg, 2023).

Initial inter-coder reliability (Cohen's κ) varied by country: Brazil achieved almost perfect agreement (κ=0.832), India achieved perfect agreement (κ=1.000), but South Africa showed only moderate agreement (κ=0.452). Analysis of disagreements revealed systematic ambiguity in coding Fourth Industrial Revolution (4IR) discourse, which could legitimately be interpreted as either INNOVATION (emphasizing opportunity) or GOVERNANCE (emphasizing policy frameworks).

We resolved this through adjudication, applying explicit decision rules: documents focused on government accountability and oversight mechanisms were coded GOVERNANCE, while those emphasizing technological opportunity and development were coded INNOVATION. Post-adjudication reliability achieved κ=1.000 for South Africa, yielding an overall weighted κ=0.911—exceeding conventional thresholds for substantial agreement.

### Comparative Benchmark

To contextualize our findings, we draw on parallel analysis of U.S. Congressional AI discourse. Our prior study of 192 congressional hearings (117th-119th Congress, 2021-2025) using identical methods found markedly different framing patterns, with SOVEREIGNTY (38.7%) and GOVERNANCE (20.6%) as dominant frames. This provides an empirical benchmark against which to assess Global South divergence.

## Findings

### RQ1: Frame Distribution Within Countries

Table 1 presents the distribution of primary frames across our three-country corpus.

**Table 1: Primary Frame Distribution by Country**

| Frame | South Africa | Brazil | India | Total |
|-------|-------------|--------|-------|-------|
| | n=41 | n=54 | n=7 | N=102 |
| GOVERNANCE | 46.3% | 37.0% | 57.1% | 42.2% |
| INNOVATION | 39.0% | 20.4% | 0.0% | 26.5% |
| RIGHTS | 9.8% | 20.4% | 28.6% | 17.6% |
| RISK_HARM | 2.4% | 18.5% | 14.3% | 10.8% |
| RISK_ECONOMIC | 2.4% | 1.9% | 0.0% | 2.0% |
| SOVEREIGNTY | 0.0% | 1.9% | 0.0% | 1.0% |
| RISK_SAFETY | 0.0% | 0.0% | 0.0% | 0.0% |
| TECHNICAL | 0.0% | 0.0% | 0.0% | 0.0% |

The most striking finding is the dominance of **GOVERNANCE** framing across all three countries. In India, 57.1% of documents primarily frame AI through a governance lens; in South Africa, 46.3%; and in Brazil, 37.0%. Aggregated across the corpus, governance accounts for 42.2% of primary frames—the single most common framing by a substantial margin.

The second most common frame is **INNOVATION** (26.5%), though this varies considerably by country. South Africa's discourse is heavily inflected by Fourth Industrial Revolution (4IR) rhetoric, with 39.0% of documents emphasizing AI as an opportunity for economic development and technological modernization. Brazil and India show more modest innovation framing (20.4% and 0.0% respectively).

**RIGHTS** framing appears prominently in all three countries (overall 17.6%), with India (28.6%) and Brazil (20.4%) showing particular emphasis on privacy, non-discrimination, and data protection. This reflects the influence of foundational data governance frameworks—India's Digital Personal Data Protection Act and Brazil's LGPD (Lei Geral de Proteção de Dados)—which provide discursive resources for rights-based AI critique.

**RISK_HARM** framing (10.8%) is most pronounced in Brazil (18.5%), reflecting a wave of legislation addressing specific AI applications: deepfakes, misinformation, child safety, and algorithmic fraud. India and South Africa show more modest harm framing.

Notably absent from Global South discourse are several frames prominent in Global North debates. **RISK_SAFETY**—framing AI as an existential or catastrophic threat—appears in zero documents across all three countries. **SOVEREIGNTY**—framing AI as a matter of geopolitical competition—appears in only 1.0% of documents. These absences are as analytically significant as the presences.

### RQ2: Cross-Country Comparison

While GOVERNANCE dominates all three corpora, important national variations emerge that reflect distinct political-economic contexts and discursive traditions.

**South Africa: 4IR as Policy Imaginary**

South African AI discourse is uniquely inflected by the Fourth Industrial Revolution (4IR) frame, a discursive construction that gained prominence during the Ramaphosa administration and has become institutionalized through the Presidential Commission on the 4IR. This framing simultaneously acknowledges technological transformation and asserts developmental agency—positioning South Africa not as a passive recipient of Northern innovations but as an active participant shaping technology's social implications.

Characteristic of South African discourse is explicit attention to inequality. As one committee meeting record states: "Those in possession of capital and resources would experience an increase in economic returns, whilst the poor would see a decrease in economic returns as a result of technological advances." This reflects what we term a **redistributive AI imaginary**—a construction of AI governance as fundamentally about ensuring equitable distribution of technological benefits in a context of profound historical inequality.

Also distinctive is attention to what might be called **algorithmic coloniality**: concerns that AI systems developed in the Global North encode biases that harm African populations. Parliamentary testimony noted that "facial recognition technology does not detect black people the way it detects white people" due to training data biases—a framing that positions AI governance as a site of postcolonial contestation.

**Brazil: Legislative Granularity**

Brazilian discourse is distinguished by its legislative specificity. While South African and Indian documents tend toward broad policy deliberation, Brazilian propositions address concrete applications: deepfake labeling requirements, AI in police body cameras, age verification for minors, automated decision transparency. This granularity reflects Brazil's constitutional culture of detailed legislation and the Câmara's active engagement with technology policy following the Marco Civil da Internet (2014) and LGPD (2018).

The prominence of RIGHTS framing in Brazil (20.4%) connects AI governance to established data protection discourse. Many propositions explicitly reference LGPD alignment, constructing AI accountability as an extension of existing privacy frameworks rather than a wholly new regulatory domain.

**India: Institutional Emphasis**

Indian discourse, while smallest in our corpus, shows the clearest institutional emphasis. Four of seven documents propose or implement regulatory bodies: the Data Protection Board, the National AI Technology Regulatory Authority (NAITRA), and the Ethics Committee for AI. This reflects what we term an **institutional AI imaginary**—a construction of AI governance as primarily requiring new organizational infrastructure rather than specific prohibitions or rights declarations.

This institutional emphasis may reflect India's recent experience with data governance, where the prolonged deliberation over data protection legislation (2017-2023) highlighted the costs of regulatory ambiguity. The AI governance documents analyzed appear designed to preempt similar uncertainty through early institutionalization.

The most dramatic divergence concerns SOVEREIGNTY. In U.S. Congressional hearings, nearly four in ten documents (38.7%) primarily frame AI through the lens of geopolitical competition—China as strategic rival, AI leadership as national security imperative, chip export controls as instruments of techno-strategic advantage. In our Global South corpus, sovereignty framing is essentially absent (1.0%).

This divergence admits multiple interpretations. It may reflect differential geopolitical positioning: the United States as hegemon-under-challenge versus Global South nations as potential balancers or non-aligned actors. It may reflect different material relationships to AI production: the United States as major producer of foundation models versus Global South nations as adopters and adapters. Or it may reflect different discursive traditions: American tendency toward competitive framings versus Global South traditions of developmental and regulatory discourse.

Conversely, GOVERNANCE framing is twice as prevalent in Global South discourse (42.2%) as in U.S. Congressional hearings (20.6%). Global South nations appear to approach AI primarily as a regulatory challenge—a technology requiring institutional infrastructure, oversight mechanisms, and accountability frameworks—rather than as a vector of strategic competition.

## Discussion

### The Governance-First Imaginary

Our central finding—the dominance of governance framing across Global South AI discourse—merits extended interpretation. We suggest this reflects what we term a **governance-first imaginary**: a shared construction of AI as primarily requiring regulatory infrastructure rather than strategic mobilization or existential vigilance.

This imaginary can be understood through several lenses. Materially, Global South nations are positioned differently in AI production chains: as adopters of foundation models developed elsewhere, as sites of deployment rather than origin, as regulators rather than racers. This positioning may make governance framings more resonant and strategic framings less salient.

Discursively, Global South nations may be drawing on different repertoires. Brazil's technology governance is heavily inflected by the Marco Civil framework, which emphasizes rights, transparency, and multi-stakeholder deliberation. South Africa's discourse inherits both post-apartheid transformation imperatives and African developmental traditions. India's approach reflects recent hard-won experience with data governance institution-building.

Politically, governance framings may serve particular functions in Global South contexts. They assert regulatory agency without requiring technological production capacity. They position nations as responsible members of an emerging international AI governance order. And they may appeal to domestic constituencies concerned about technology's social impacts without triggering geopolitical entanglement.

### Absence of the Existential

Equally striking is the complete absence of RISK_SAFETY framing—constructions of AI as posing existential, catastrophic, or civilizational threats. While such framings have gained significant traction in Silicon Valley discourse and have influenced regulatory debates in the U.S. and UK, they appear to have essentially no purchase in Global South legislative deliberation.

This absence may reflect priorities shaped by material circumstances. Nations grappling with unemployment rates exceeding 30% (South Africa), housing deficits affecting millions (Brazil), or development imperatives across vast populations (India) may find speculative concerns about superintelligence less pressing than immediate challenges of algorithmic discrimination, labor displacement, or surveillance overreach.

It may also reflect skepticism about the origins and functions of existential risk discourse. Critical scholars have noted that AI safety rhetoric often emerges from the same industry actors whose products create the putative risks, potentially serving to deflect regulatory attention from present harms toward hypothetical futures (Whittaker, 2021). Global South legislators may be—consciously or not—declining to adopt frames that serve Northern industry interests.

### Implications for Global AI Governance

These findings have implications for emerging efforts at international AI governance coordination. To the extent that nations approach negotiations with different foundational framings, alignment may prove difficult. U.S. emphasis on sovereignty and strategic competition may not resonate with Global South partners more concerned with regulatory infrastructure and rights protection.

Conversely, the convergence around governance framing across our three-country corpus suggests potential for South-South alignment on AI policy. The governance-first imaginary may provide common ground for regulatory coordination among nations that share positioning outside the AI production cores.

---

## Limitations and Future Directions

Several limitations warrant acknowledgment. First, our corpus, while substantial, is not exhaustive. Parliamentary discourse represents only one site of AI policy framing; executive communications, judicial decisions, civil society advocacy, and media coverage may reveal different patterns. Second, our three countries, while significant, cannot represent the full diversity of Global South contexts. African, Latin American, and Asian regional comparisons would enrich the analysis. Third, our method, while validated for reliability, involves interpretive choices in codebook construction and adjudication that others might make differently.

Future research should extend this analysis temporally, tracking whether Global South framings shift as AI capabilities and deployments evolve. Cross-national analysis within regions—comparing, for instance, AI discourse across BRICS nations or African Union members—would illuminate regional governance dynamics. And mixed-method approaches incorporating elite interviews could illuminate the strategic considerations underlying observed framings.

---

## Conclusion

This study offers the first systematic comparative analysis of AI policy framing across major Global South democracies. Our findings challenge assumptions about the universality of Northern AI frames: the sovereignty and existential risk framings that dominate U.S. discourse are largely absent from South African, Brazilian, and Indian legislative deliberation. Instead, Global South nations construct AI primarily as a governance challenge—requiring regulatory infrastructure, institutional oversight, and accountability mechanisms.

This governance-first imaginary positions Global South nations not as passive recipients of Northern AI products and policies but as active constructors of alternative regulatory futures. As international AI governance takes shape in the coming years, attending to these alternative imaginaries will be essential for understanding both the possibilities and limits of global coordination.
