    After Hamas attacked Israel 4 months ago, some claim that 
these terrorists were using crypto as a primary funding 
mechanism for their operations. Through hearings and 
confidential briefings, this committee has heard from the 
intelligence community, blockchain experts, and law 
enforcement, and we have a much better understanding of the 
scope and scale of crypto's role in terror finance.
    While the reactionary claims prove to be greatly 
exaggerated in the case of Hamas and Gaza; it is still 
important for us to identify and close any potential gaps that 
can be exploited by criminals and terrorists. Just yesterday, 
here in this room, Under Secretary Nelson testified that bad 
actors still prefer to use traditional finance rather than 
digital assets. He also confirmed prior reports that successful 
international collaboration with Israeli law enforcement had 
limited Hamas' use of digital assets ahead of the attack 
against Israel.
    Today, this committee seeks to understand the tools that 
law enforcement has to go after bad actors using crypto, as 
well as learn what, if any, gaps exist in those law enforcement 
authorities.
    Just last week, the Financial Times reported that Iran used 
two of the United Kingdom's largest banks to evade sanctions, 
which just goes to show, again, that terrorists will use any 
means necessary--cash or crypto--to accomplish their means.
    The fact remains that the transparency of a public 
blockchain provides law enforcement with critical data about 
illicit actors and their networks so that they can be gone 
after. Much of this success can be attributed to the 
partnerships between law enforcement and blockchain analytic 
firms. As bad actors become more proficient, regulators and law 
enforcement officials must continue increasing their own 
capabilities and expertise.
    Let us be clear: Much of the illicit financing through 
digital assets does not take place here in the United States. 
It is done offshore in obviously noncompliant, rogue 
jurisdictions. The borderless nature of blockchain technology 
necessitates international cooperation.
    Today, we will examine what can be done to reinforce the 
set of tools that Financial Crimes Enforcement Network 
(FinCEN), Office of Foreign Assets Control (OFAC), and the 
Department of Justice possess. Members on both sides of the 
aisle are interested in finding solutions. This hearing, and 
the legislation noticed, build off last year's efforts in both 
our stablecoin and market structure legislation.
    We must ensure that there is a functional regulatory 
framework in place, so the digital asset companies operate 
within the United States and subject themselves to our laws. 
Last summer, this committee passed bipartisan legislation that 
would do just that.
    Today, we are fortunate to have an incredible lineup of 
witnesses to examine our current capabilities to hold bad 
actors accountable. I want to thank each of our panelists for 
being with us today. We appreciate your willingness to provide 
your expertise and your testimony.
    I look forward to continuing our work with my good ranking 
member, Mr. Lynch, the members of this subcommittee, so that we 
can use our time in search of solutions to combat illicit use 
of digital assets.
    With that, I will yield back and yield 5 minutes to the 
ranking member for his opening statement.

traditional financing methods to fund illicit activities, the 
concern that the crypto sector is vulnerable to terrorist 
financing and money laundering is certainly a valid one.
    In its recent risk assessment on illicit financing, 
Treasury reports that terrorist organizations and their 
material facilitators have markedly improved their 
experimentation with virtual assets. Criminal and malicious 
State actors have also expanded beyond the use of fiat currency 
and are now exploiting virtual assets to facilitate ransomware 
attacks, human and drug trafficking, and sanctions evasion.
    According to cybersecurity industry reports, global 
ransomware payments exceeded a record $1.1 billion in 2023, 
with criminals primarily demanding the delivery of extortion 
payments to digital wallet addresses. Similarly, the 
international Financial Action Task Force reports that Hamas, 
al-Qaida, and ISIS have all solicited funding through bitcoin 
and privacy coins and other digital assets.
    They have also learned to leverage anonymity-enhancing 
services such as cryptocurrency tumblers and convertible 
currency mixers to obscure the origin and transfer of funds. 
Ironically, a primary reason that terrorist organizations do 
not rely on digital assets on a greater scale is actually their 
extreme volatility and value.
    The illicit financing risks relating to digital assets have 
also been exacerbated by the rapid evolution of artificial 
intelligence. According to the Combating Terrorism Center at 
West Point, deep learning models could enable terrorists to 
propagate their fundraising activities with maximum efficiency 
and impact.
    Last week, Secretary Yellen testified here in Congress that 
Treasury has already identified critical gaps in its authority 
to deter illicit financial activities. Importantly, the 
Department recently issued a detailed action plan to address 
the specific illicit financing risks associated with digital 
assets.
    Several priority recommendations, including the regulation 
of payment stablecoins and enhanced agency enforcement 
capabilities, will require bipartisan cooperation, 
congressional funding, and statutory authorization.
    FinCEN, the Financial Crimes Enforcement Network, has also 
taken important actions in this area. The agency recently 
proposed its rule to increase transparency in convertible 
virtual currency mixing and combat the use of anonymity-
enhancing technologies by malicious actors, whether that is 
Hamas, drug cocktails, or North Korea.
    In the interest of national security, I look forward to 
working with my colleagues on a bipartisan basis to further 
ensure that we are promoting the responsible innovation of 
cryptocurrency and other digital assets while mitigating their 
susceptibility to illicit financing activities.

 STATEMENT OF CAROLINE HILL, SENIOR DIRECTOR OF GLOBAL POLICY 
               AND REGULATORY STRATEGY AT CIRCLE

    Ms. Hill. Chairman Hill, Ranking Member Lynch, and members 
of the subcommittee, thank you for inviting me to speak today.
    I began my career investigating illicit networks for the 
U.N. and at OFAC, where I recommended sanctions actions against 
individuals and companies that were assisting the Iranians', 
North Koreans', and Syrians' attempts to fund their malign 
activities and access hard currency.
    I experienced firsthand the challenges of tracking illicit 
finance networks in the traditional financial space, 
particularly when involving foreign jurisdictions, as most 
cases did. I also saw the importance of a compliant private 
sector in being a partner to detect, freeze, and prevent these 
illicit flows.
    I then worked as a senior advisor to the U.S. president of 
the Financial Action Task Force, FATF, and oversaw anti-money 
laundering/combating the financing of terrorism (AML/CFT) 
policy for the Treasury, focused on Africa and Latin America. 
In these roles, I would frequently hear from developing 
countries that U.S. financial institutions would cite AML 
regulations as the biggest challenge to facilitating banking 
services. A lack of banking services negatively affected cross-
border payments, remittances, foreign direct investment, and 
other economic activity.
    At the same time, I saw--could see that digital assets, 
with proper regulatory clarity, could overcome many of these 
challenges with the benefit to increased AML transparency.
    Digital assets brings nearly instant 24/7 settlement to the 
everyday consumer and serve as an important enhancement to 
today's traditional payment networks, which are typically 
restrictive, antiquated, and fragmented.
    The United States should be at the forefront of this 
technology in order to have a modern domestic payment system 
that is efficient, supports U.S. foreign economic power, and 
safeguards the integrity of our financial system.
    Circle is the sole issuer of the payment stablecoin USDC, 
and our track record of sanctions and Bank Secrecy Act 
compliance has exported stability, rule of law, and respect for 
U.S. AML/CFT standards to the broader digital assets market.
    Circle has established the same financial crimes compliance 
controls as expected with other financial institutions, and we 
have been able to leverage novel technologies due to the 
transparent nature of transaction on blockchains. This 
contrasts with transactions at traditional financial 
institutions, which are siloed to that specific bank.
    Anyone can view transactions on the blockchains in real 
time, even when there is no U.S. nexus, and Circle has worked 
domestically and with foreign governments to identify, trace, 
and deter illicit financial flows using USDC. As a result of 
this increased transparency, we have also had the United 
Nations and the U.S. Government use or facilitate the use of 
USDC in Ukraine and Venezuela, respectively, to facilitate 
efficient and transparent payments across borders.
    The technology behind smart contracts also allows for the 
freezing of assets without delay on chain beyond the ability of 
traditional financial institutions with fiat. Anyone with an 
internet connection can see, for instance, the exact amount of 
USDC that has been frozen by Circle and sanctioned addresses. 
As such, payment stablecoin issuers should, without fail, be 
able to point to on-chain data to demonstrate compliance with 
sanctions laws.
    Unfortunately, this sector is not without rebuke. Some 
digital asset companies have a public track record of 
proclaiming themselves above AML/CFT regulation even if issuing 
a U.S. dollar-backed token.
    So why is it so important to get regulation of this sector 
right? The power of the U.S. Government's economic tools 
depends on the jurisdiction that Treasury has over U.S. 
financial institutions, and by proxy, the U.S. dollar in 
international payment services. As a result, many strategic 
competitors have been motivated to find alternative payment 
systems so they can be out of reach of U.S. sanctions and law 
enforcement.
    The United States should have a whole-of-government effort 
approach to study these efforts and potential challenges to 
U.S. dollar dominance. The U.S. Government should also 
strengthen the role of the U.S. dollar in the future by 
supporting innovation that would make the dollar the base 
currency of digital assets.
    Regulated U.S. dollar-denominated stablecoins import 
compliance and the rule of law to the digital asset space, so 
it is important to ensure that all U.S. dollar-backed 
stablecoins have compliant Bank Secrecy Act (BSA) programs and 
adhere to OFAC sanctions. U.S. touch point should also be 
responsible when they are facilitating the existence of these 
noncompliant tokens.
    Furthermore, continued domestic enforcement of existing 
regulations is critical. Recent actions by the U.S. Government 
have demonstrated that digital assets companies are not out of 
reach. The U.S. Government should also continue to press other 
countries to adopt similar AML/CFT frameworks to ensure 
regulatory arbitrage is minimized.
    Finally, Congress should consider increasing funding for 
U.S. law enforcement agencies to identify, track, and analyze 
financial crime networks. The U.S. should also be the leader in 
training other countries in the technical know-how that has 
allowed U.S. law enforcement to track and seize digital assets.
    Thank you again for the opportunity to testify today, and I 
welcome your questions.

 STATEMENT OF MICHAEL MOSIER, CO-FOUNDER AND PARTNER, ARKTOUROS

    Mr. Mosier. Thank you.
    Thank you for allowing me to speak on topics to which I 
have dedicated most of my career: countering exploitation and 
supporting the democratization of opportunity.
    When I was associate director at OFAC, we not only created 
sanctions programs to defend national security, we created 
pathways for humanitarian aid and censorship-resistant 
technology, like virtual private network (VPNs), to support 
human rights activists under authoritarian regimes like Iran. 
When I was acting director of FinCEN, we launched a privacy 
tech program and created a senior digital identity role to 
focus on preventing victims, not just avenging after.
    I recently coauthored a paper on Genuine DeFi as Critical 
Infrastructure, proposing a framework to improve our ability to 
prevent victims, leveraging public and private sectors, and 
drawing upon government experience securing the integrity of 
financial infrastructure. The paper calls for greater 
involvement of Treasury through the Office of Cybersecurity and 
Critical Infrastructure Protection, or OCCIP. OCCIP works 
across law enforcement and financial infrastructure on threat 
detection and mitigation. Their touch point is an industry 
cybersecurity center called Financial Services Information 
Sharing and Analysis Center (FS-ISAC) governed by its roughly 
4,600 members, and importantly, membership is not limited by a 
financial institution definition, but rather, your relevance to 
financial infrastructure and security.
    Much of this is already being built in the crypto sector; 
notably, the security alliance and security alliance (SEAL) 
initiative and a crypto Information Sharing and Analysis Center 
(ISAC) information sharing center modeled on FS-ISAC, but 
OCCIP's public sector intelligence coordination would 
meaningfully advance this work for everyone, including the 
government.
    Treating every gear of code in a technological network as a 
financial institution, collecting KYC on every data packet that 
is mathematically validated, overestimates the safety of 
financial institutions and the effectiveness of KYC.
    Major banks doing KYC have paid roughly $385 billion in 
penalties for illegal activities since 2000. KYC did not stop 
violent cartels from laundering nearly $900 million through 
Hongkong and Shanghai Banking Corporation (HSBC).
    I can tell you, as a former Federal prosecutor who obtained 
the first BSA plea to hold a bank president responsible for 
soliciting Venezuelan black market peso dealers and walking 
them through KYC, the traditional finance system faces serious 
challenges.
    Overreliance on static, easily duped KYC has created a 
voracious market for deepfake, stolen, and exploited 
identities. This is failing the people we should be protecting 
and the public servants working to address illicit finance. 
Opaque international banking, from which the Bank Secrecy Act 
takes its name, prevents investigators from tracing global 
hops, making KYC limited help.
    The original anonymity-enhanced technology is cross-border 
fiat banking with nested accounts and shell companies. As every 
Treasury risk assessment has shown, nothing else can compete.
    This is less applicable in the on-chain world of traceable 
public ledgers where activity-based risk monitoring is globally 
accessible and harder to spoof than a fake ID. Law enforcement 
has followed Lazarus funds to cash-out points and ultimately 
recovered over $30 million.
    To be effective, we have to recognize that blockchain 
networks are fundamentally cyber infrastructure, and we are 
better to capture the next Lazarus as an early cyber threat 
well before it makes its way all the way to a national 
emergency on an OFAC list or shows up in KYC.
    In addition to activating OCCIP, a concrete action that you 
could take to fundamentally impact illicit finance is to 
resource existing Treasury authorities before adding more 
unfunded mandates. Throwing more shovels at people who already 
have 15 in each hand is not making them more effective; it is 
setting them up for failure. Four years after the expansion of 
authorities in the AML Act of 2020, FinCEN still has not 
received the full funding to implement.
    Here are key items that we mapped out at FinCEN but have 
not been resourced by Congress to succeed: FinCEN's global 
Financial Intelligence Unit (FIU) liaisons to leverage 
operational work directly with foreign FIUs in country.
    FinCEN's whistleblower office. People will stop sending 
tips if they do not see cases. Domestic liaisons were to be put 
in regions across the country and an innovation officer to 
drive both internal and external innovation. All unfunded, all 
unfilled.
    Last, we separately had staffed a chief digital currency 
advisor who has not been backfilled since the last departure. 
If crypto is such a concern, fund the position and give the 
U.S. financial intelligence unit a chance to succeed.
    My father grew up in a small mining and steel town and 
always said, ``all people want is opportunity.'' In a world of 
authoritarians who despise the unpredictability of distributed 
opportunity, our democracy was founded and thrives on enabling 
opportunity.
    That democratization and the natural noncoerced alignment 
of people around principles is far more resilient for national 
security than a deputized and weaponized failing personal 
information collection apparatus, which is the Eastern Europe 
my father's ancestors were leaving.
    Thank you.

 STATEMENT OF GRANT RABENN, DIRECTOR, FINANCIAL CRIMES LEGAL, 
                            COINBASE

    Mr. Rabenn. Chairman Hill, Ranking Member Lynch, and 
members of the subcommittee, thank you for this opportunity to 
testify today.
    My name is Grant Rabenn, and I serve as the Director of 
Financial Crimes Legal at Coinbase, America's largest digital 
asset exchange. I lead the team of lawyers at Coinbase 
responsible for anti-money laundering, sanctions, and law 
enforcement matters. We work collaboratively with law 
enforcement to keep our platform and the broader crypto 
ecosystem safe.
    I have spent most of my professional life prosecuting 
financial crimes and stopping the bad guys from using 
technology to hurt Americans. I joined Coinbase after a decade 
as a Federal prosecutor in which I focused on the intersection 
of money laundering and cybercrime.
    While assigned to lead a suspicious activity report joint 
Federal task force, I first saw reports coming in about 
bitcoin-related transactions in 2013. I initiated some of the 
Federal Government's earliest criminal investigations into 
crypto-related money laundering, including partnering with 
FinCEN on one of its first crypto enforcement actions. I also 
organized and taught at some of the Department of Justice's 
first national trainings on crypto crime for prosecutors and 
Federal agents.
    I am most proud of my work as a leader of the takedown of 
the world's largest dark web marketplace, AlphaBay, which was 
described as the most successful cybercrime prosecution in the 
history of the internet and for which I was given the Attorney 
General's Award, the FBI Director's Award, and the Director of 
National Intelligence's Meritorious Service Award.
    This case revealed the power of blockchain analytics in 
uncovering cyber criminals and involved the Federal 
Government's first ever seizures of Monero and Zcash. I came to 
Coinbase to continue this work.
    Coinbase is a company laser focused on being the most 
secure, trusted, and compliant platform in crypto. Our mission 
is to protect our customers' crypto and the country. Just like 
me, hundreds of former national security and compliance 
professionals have joined the fight for the security of crypto 
in America.
    My career has shown me the breadth of tools Congress has 
given to law enforcement and how they can be applied to new 
technology and how to make crypto safer. As described in detail 
in my written testimony, there are four main points that I 
would like to leave you with today.
    First, there is a national security imperative to get this 
right. Keeping crypto here in America ensures compliance, 
enhances law enforcement, and secures the financial system. The 
reverse is true. If the pipes and plumbing of a technology that 
moves value flow elsewhere, the U.S. Government loses its 
ability to shape it.
    Second, Coinbase has invested heavily in its anti-money 
laundering program and global intelligence teams that partner 
with global law enforcement on a daily basis. We are constantly 
working to leverage new technology to establish a program that 
rivals any traditional financial institution. We have also led 
industry-wide anti-money laundering compliance initiatives. We 
take our obligation to protect Americans seriously.
    Third, sanctions evasion, terrorist financing, and criminal 
activity is concentrated with offshore platforms. Law 
enforcement should use all of the extensive legal tools at its 
disposal to police those offshore actors today. If additional 
funding is needed so law enforcement can prioritize crypto, 
Congress should provide it immediately.
    Fourth, Coinbase is supportive of legislation providing 
targeted additional tools that will make crypto a safer place, 
but it is important that Congress be thoughtful about its 
approach.
    As this committee knows, Coinbase is held to robust anti-
money laundering and Know Your Customer standards like any 
American financial institution. Our work is further bolstered 
by a characteristic unique to crypto, the public ledger of 
transactions within the blockchain, which let us trace illicit 
funds and track those trying to send or receive to a bad actor 
or a sanctioned party. In this way, crypto is compliance on 
steroids. It allows for a new dimension of compliance: public 
ledgers that enable visibility into what users do on and off 
our platform.
    In traditional finance, one bank has no visibility into 
what their customers do at another bank. However, in crypto, 
public ledgers provide immediate information about what 
customers are doing across the entire technology, which creates 
better investigative tools that can then be used by law 
enforcement and compliance departments.
    Case in point, OFAC has sanctioned around 560 crypto 
addresses in total, but Coinbase has identified more than 8 
million through our proactive investigations. By extrapolating 
out from these ground-truth OFAC addresses, we are able to stop 
the flow of funds to bad actors in real time.
    While onshore-regulated exchanges invest heavily in 
compliance to stop bad activity, criminals continue to seek out 
offshore platforms without those same robust anti-money 
laundering programs and controls. Those offshore entities often 
play jurisdictional Whac-A-Mole, attempting to avoid tough 
anti-money laundering roles and expecting that regulators will 
not care. The U.S. Government should use all of its existing 
tools to go after these platforms. Many recent enforcement 
actions in the crypto anti-money laundering space are good 
news. Accountability should happen.
    Additionally, regulating the broader crypto market through
