                         WEST VIRGINIA

    The Chairman. The Committee will come to order.
    Today, we are going to be discussing the opportunities and 
challenges associated with advanced nuclear energy 
commercialization. But before we do, I would be remiss if I did 
not acknowledge that our time with our nuclear fellow, Dr. 
Sarah Stevenson, is coming to an end. Sarah, wave your hand. 
That's Sarah. Sarah has been an integral part of our team here

because she has been a tremendous contributor to what we have 
been doing and how far we are advancing, and basically 
educating people on what nuclear has done, what it will do, and 
what it can do, and that's what we are relying on. So she is 
going to be missed.
    Our conventional nuclear fleet of light water reactors has 
provided reliable power for over 60 years and makes up about 95 
gigawatts of generation capacity and around 20 percent of our 
annual energy production. Nuclear power is available 24/7, 
emissions-free, making it incredibly important to our energy 
security, while also helping us meet our climate goals. DOE 
estimates that 200 gigawatts of new nuclear plants will be 
needed by 2050--more than double our current nuclear capacity. 
We must have this reliable power for our critical 
infrastructure, our defense, our homes, and most importantly, 
our economy.
    There is a growing sense that the next generation of 
nuclear power will have a smaller footprint, using different 
technologies than we have relied on in the past. These advanced 
nuclear technologies have the potential to play a significant 
role in securing our energy security and independence. Advanced 
reactors have a range of end-uses. For example, advanced 
reactors under 50 megawatts, often called micro-reactors, have 
the potential to provide off-grid energy for remote towns, 
villages, and defense applications. And small modular reactors, 
which can provide several hundred megawatts of power, have the 
potential to provide traditional baseload power generation as 
well as more flexibility for peaking and also high-temperature 
process heat for industrial applications.
    In the last few years, Congress has provided significant 
investments through public-private partnerships to support the 
demonstration of first-of-a-kind reactors, such as the 
Department of Defense's Project Pele. I have supported Project 
Pele as a member of the Armed Services and Appropriations 
Committees. This transportable micro-reactor will provide five 
megawatts of safe and secure power for three years without 
needing to be refueled, circumventing the risks associated with 
transporting additional diesel generators and large volumes of 
diesel fuel for our forces. The Pele reactor is also small 
enough to be transported anywhere in the world by C-17 
aircrafts. And I am proud to say its inaugural flight will be 
carried out by the West Virginia National Guard C-17 fleet. 
This didn't happen--you know, things happen like that.
    In addition to supporting Pele, Congress has supported 
commercial technology demonstrations through the Department of 
Energy's Advanced Reactor Demonstration Program. The Bipartisan 
Infrastructure Law provided $2.4 billion to support competitive 
DOE awards for Advanced Reactor Demonstration Projects at 
commercial scale. The TerraPower project in Wyoming and the X-
energy project in Texas have won grants under this program. 
TerraPower's reactor is being built at a retiring coal facility 
and will be used to follow daily electric load changes, and X-
energy has partnered with Dow to provide heat and power for one 
of their manufacturing plants. I look forward to hearing more 
on the progress of some of these demonstration projects from 
our expert panel of witnesses today.
    Building upon these demonstration project programs, the 
Inflation Reduction Act provided ten years of certainty to 
commercialize advanced reactors through a 30 percent investment 
tax credit or $25 per megawatt-hour production tax credit for 
new nuclear plants, along with making tens of billions of 
dollars in Title 17 loan guarantees available for nuclear 
projects. And we ensured a ten-percent bonus credit for 
advanced nuclear in coal communities, like my home State of 
West Virginia, and these fit perfectly there, and in Ranking 
Member Barrasso's home State of Wyoming, which he will have one 
of the first ones installed there, and in other communities 
that have sacrificed for generations to power our country but 
now face the threat of being sidelined during the energy 
transition, which we are not going to let happen.
    The IRA incentives were not just about building power 
plants, but also securing our nuclear supply chain. Congress 
provided $700 million for high-assay low-enriched uranium 
(HALEU), which is the fuel that most advanced nuclear 
technologies need, including Pele, TerraPower, and X-energy. 
But despite all of the federal private-sector support, we are 
witnessing struggles and hesitancy in getting advanced nuclear 
projects off the ground. There are large design, cost, and 
regulatory uncertainties associated with a first-of-a-kind 
nuclear technology, which is why we have now created numerous 
federal programs to help reduce these risks. But someone will 
need to go first. And unfortunately, many of the utilities that 
I have spoken with won't get in the game until others have done 
it first. We also must continue to ensure that taxpayer dollars 
are being spent judiciously and we are managing risk and 
learning from instances like the NuScale project with UAMPS, 
which fell far short of all of our expectations.
    I am grateful to have our witnesses here today to discuss 
their plans and challenges as they attempt to build the first 
generation of advanced reactors in our country. And we are in a 
competitive race with other people around the world as we sit 
here today. Looking forward into the future at several of the 
incentives Congress has created for new nuclear sunset after 
2032, I am interested to hear what our witnesses think is 
needed to create sustained development for the coming decades. 
That means how will we basically be able to maintain cost 
competitiveness with our nuclear fleet? It is clear to me that 
our country is at a critical juncture and our actions today 
will impact our nuclear landscape for years to come.
    The U.S. has long been a leader in civil nuclear energy, 
which has allowed us to share our expertise and gold standards 
for safety and non-proliferation around the world. Over time, 
Russia and China have made a concerted effort to supplant our 
nuclear leadership. We must push back, and they cannot succeed. 
To regain our civil nuclear leadership, we need to demonstrate 
domestically that we can develop and deploy the next generation 
of nuclear energy to attract our international partners. We 
also need to export these technologies to our allies and 
partners to help reduce their energy dependence on foreign 
adversaries. That is why Senator Risch and I introduced the 
International Nuclear Energy Act and the Civil Nuclear Export 
Act to provide strategic guidance and the financing mechanisms 
required for the U.S. to retake its leadership position on the 
global stage.
    But our adversaries are not just pushing to dominate the 
export market for our nuclear technology. Putin has a 
stranglehold on the nuclear supply chain. Certainly, Russia is 
the only commercial supplier of HALEU, the fuel used for our 
DOD micro-reactor project, DOE Advanced Reactor Demonstration 
Projects, and many other advanced nuclear technologies. That is 
why I have led the bipartisan Nuclear Fuel Security Act of 
2023, along with my Ranking Member here, Senator Barrasso, and 
Senator Risch, which authorizes a DOE program to onshore U.S. 
uranium conversion and enrichment capacity for both traditional 
and advanced reactors. We must reestablish a domestic nuclear 
supply chain, and our bill will do just that. I have also 
worked with Senator Barrasso and other members of this 
Committee to introduce legislation that would limit and 
eventually ban uranium fuel imports from Russia. Today, I am 
calling on my Congressional colleagues to include these 
critical nuclear fuel bills and associated funding in the 
defense and appropriation packages that we are currently 
negotiating.
    I look forward to hearing our witnesses' views of what else 
we need to do to achieve our shared vision of American advanced 
nuclear commercialization, and most importantly, global 
leadership. We have a distinguished panel representing the 
government and industry perspectives that are essential to 
discuss this public-private partnership. So before I introduce 
the panel, I am going to turn to Senator Barrasso for his 
opening remarks.

    Over the past several years, much has changed. Public 
opinion polls show increasingly broad support for nuclear 
energy, and nuclear energy now has bipartisan and bicameral 
support in Congress. Through both legislative and technical 
efforts, the U.S. is on the cusp of achievements that could 
usher in a new era of nuclear energy and reestablish U.S. 
global leadership. And so, it is no longer a matter of whether 
we need advanced nuclear technologies, it is a matter of how 
much nuclear power do we need? How can we enable first movers 
and how quickly can we develop and deploy these new reactors? 
That's important for several reasons: the stability of our 
power grid, bolstering the national economy, enhancing national 
security, and meeting the nation's clean energy goals.
    While my written testimony addresses these topics in 
detail, I would like to focus my time here on actions the U.S. 
can take to advance nuclear reactor commercialization. First, 
we must address the challenges associated with first-of-a-kind 
cost and schedule uncertainties that discourage first movers. I 
will note that if our electricity markets properly valued firm, 
dispatchable, non-carbon-emitting attributes of nuclear energy, 
this would be far less of a challenge that we are talking about 
today. One option though is to encourage and incentivize first-
mover projects that repurpose existing infrastructure. A great 
example of this, as was mentioned, is TerraPower's Natrium 
project in Wyoming, which will be built at a site where the 
coal plants are scheduled to retire. These sites often have a 
capable and supportive workforce and an existing 
infrastructure, like transmission and distribution lines that 
can be repurposed. In addition, the Federal Government's energy 
needs represent an opportunity for first movers. The range of 
government and defense installations, national laboratories, 
and remote power needs in rural communities create an ideal 
environment for deploying nuclear technologies.
    Another consideration is to lengthen the time of power-
purchase agreements. There are opportunities for federal 
entities, such as military installations and national 
laboratories, to leverage power-purchase agreements. This would 
enable meeting federal clean energy goals with nuclear energy 
through better alignment with the long lifetimes of nuclear 
reactors. The Department of Energy is currently limited to ten 
years for power-purchase agreements, while the Department of 
Defense has more flexible authority. A more uniform approach 
could increase the attractiveness and deployment of nuclear 
energy at DOE sites around this country.
    As discussed in the March hearing and was referred, to 
enable deployment, the U.S. must quickly develop new domestic 
mining, conversion, and enrichment capabilities. Doing so will 
ensure the availability of a domestic supply of fuel, provide 
certainty to the existing plants, and help ensure our domestic 
energy security. We also need to address the back end of the 
fuel cycle. The Nuclear Waste Policy Act reflects dated 
national priorities and concerns. Various attempts have been 
made to amend the Nuclear Waste Policy Act to better reflect 
the nuclear waste management realities, policies, and needs of 
today, but none have yet succeeded. A new waste policy 
framework is needed.
    And finally, while not the jurisdiction of this Committee, 
to enable timely demonstrations and support large-scale nuclear 
energy deployment, we need an effective and efficient licensing 
process, one that recognizes and values the benefits of nuclear 
energy for public health and safety and climate mitigation and 
encourages innovation. This challenge is particularly acute for 
advanced reactors, which may raise unique regulatory questions 
and be smaller in size, resulting in a much higher proportional 
impact from regulatory and cost challenges. Such reforms can be 
done without compromising safety or transparency. We have 
written a report documenting some of our recommendations.
    I want to conclude by saying that despite the numerous 
challenges, I am optimistic about nuclear energy in our nation 
and around the world. We are no longer debating the need for 
nuclear energy. Instead, we have moved into discussions about 
how much nuclear energy we require and how we can address and 
overcome challenges to deployment, particularly first movers. 
Thank you for this Committee's attention to this topic and for 
the opportunity to participate today. I look forward to the 
questions.

 STATEMENT OF DR. JEFF WAKSMAN, PROGRAM MANAGER, OFFICE OF THE 
      SECRETARY OF DEFENSE, STRATEGIC CAPABILITIES OFFICE

    Dr. Waksman. Thank you, Chairman Manchin, Ranking Member 
Barrasso, and distinguished Members of the Committee, for the 
opportunity to testify before you today on the Department of 
Defense's efforts associated with advanced reactor 
commercialization. It is an honor and privilege for me to 
appear before you today. My name is Dr. Jeff Waksman. I am the 
Program Manager for Project Pele, a prototype transportable 
micro nuclear reactor, within the Strategic Capabilities Office 
of the Office of the Secretary of Defense. Project Pele is a 
one to five megawatt nuclear reactor prototype designed to fit 
within four 20-foot shipping containers, capable of being 
transported by truck, rail, boat, and plane, able to be set up 
and moved within several days. Using TRISO fuel, Pele is 
designed to be passively safe in all failure modes.
    The DOD, through SCO, initiated Project Pele in the fall of 
2018. I have been the program manager of this project since its 
inception. Modern warfare is critically dependent on large 
quantities of electricity to power almost everything, from 
missile defense radars, to command and control systems, to 
uncrewed weapons systems and platforms. There are significant 
mission impacts to the Department without access to reliable, 
resilient 24/7 power. The strategic need for electrical power 
at Defense installations is critical, yet today that 
electricity is largely dependent upon the delivery of huge 
volumes of fossil fuels to power generators.
    Moving fossil fuels was a significant challenge even in 
Iraq and Afghanistan, leading General Mattis to declare a 
desperate need to ``unleash us from the tether of fuel.'' When 
the Department plans for future competition and conflict with 
near-peer adversaries, the challenge to move fossil fuel across 
thousands of miles of ocean is immense. Consistent with the 
2022 National Defense Strategy, innovative clean energy 
technologies such as nuclear power are true potential game 
changers. Additionally, transportable nuclear micro-reactors 
provide the capability to support remote outposts or to respond 
quickly when natural disasters destroy existing infrastructure. 
SCO undertook a design competition for Project Pele, with three 
companies initially selected in early 2020 to develop 
preliminary designs. We eventually down-selected to a single 
prime contractor, BWXT, which is currently working to finalize 
the last details of the design while manufacturing fuel and 
purchasing long-lead hardware. SCO is working with the 
Department of Energy to gain approval of the Pele engineering 
design by mid-2024. We have already begun fabricating fuel, 
making moderator blocks, and forging the containment vessel. 
Our team is on pace to have the Pele reactor assembled by mid-
2025.
    As part of this Administration's Strategy for Re-
Establishing U.S. leadership in Nuclear Energy, the United 
States is focused on the development of markets for U.S. 
nuclear technology exports to provide reliable, always-on, 
emissions-free electricity and power. With bipartisan support, 
this Administration is working to scale up U.S. technology 
deployment, including small modular reactors and micro-
reactors. Work is also ongoing to secure and sustain the 
nuclear fuel supply chain, enhance energy security and 
resilience, strengthen nuclear safety, security, safeguards, 
nonproliferation, and to compete globally. Given current 
emerging threats, and the military's need for reliable power 
without long logistics tails, we may be willing to pay a little 
extra per kilowatt-hour to make that happen. And by 
demonstrating the Pele reactor, we can also collect the data 
and perform the safety analyses to help inform the regulatory 
and supply chain pathways to commercial reactor variants as 
well.
    In closing, I would like to thank Congress for its 
continued strong bipartisan support of Project Pele. And in 
light of Chairman Manchin's announced retirement at the end of 
the 118th Congress, I would like to personally thank him for 
his steadfast support of Project Pele. I can state definitively 
that this program would not have proceeded as fast as it has 
without his support. Thank you to the entire Committee for 
calling this hearing and for the opportunity to testify. I look 
forward to your questions. Thank you.

produce heat, power, and steam at 25 major manufacturing sites 
worldwide. That's enough energy for more than seven million 
homes, which is more than the number of homes in Michigan and

    I want to express our appreciation to the Committee as well 
as the Senate and House for the overwhelming bipartisan support 
that nuclear energy enjoys today. This is far different than 
the views toward nuclear energy that existed when I first 
became a junior Senate staffer in 1987. Unlike then, when large 
nuclear units were purchased solely by electric utilities, 
today there is a diverse group of advanced reactor developers 
who are working on remote-sited micro-reactors, molten salt 
research reactors, data centers, steel mills, chemical 
complexes, mining operations, and offshore mounted platforms. 
Companies such as Dow, Microsoft, Google, and Nucor are 
considering how nuclear energy can meet their need for clean 
power and industrial heat. Possessing large balance sheets and 
significant project management capabilities, these companies 
are not deterred by the complexity and patience that comes from 
the deployment of nuclear.
    But this begs a question: Where are the electric utilities? 
While some, including Ontario Power Generation in Canada and 
Tennessee Valley Authority have leaned further forward, many 
U.S. nuclear utilities have not yet committed to new nuclear 
generation. Rather, many of these utilities indicate that they 
want to buy the second, third, or fourth unit of a given 
design. Why is that? The answer is that financial markets and 
public utility commissions have viewed the path that it took to 
get Southern Company's Vogtle Units 3 and 4 online, and have 
made it clear that they don't want to repeat that example. 
Despite government-backed loan guarantees and other federal tax 
incentives intended to jump-start the process, utility 
executives who are compensated on meeting quarterly financial 
targets and managing PUC prudency reviews have a variety of 
incentives for not moving forward. Utility CEOs fear that if 
they commit to building a first-of-a-kind plan, it could have 
financial and timing risk on the back end of the project.
    One way to address this concern is to have a financial 
backstop program for first-of-a-kind nuclear deployment 
projects to share the risk between the utility and the Federal 
Government for potential delays and unexpected cost increases 
and not force a single utility to bear undue risk for being the 
first to deploy a new design. Alternatively, you could provide 
financial incentives for utilities that come together in a 
consortium to build a series of nuclear plants and spread that 
cost among a larger pool. This risk-sharing model was utilized 
with mixed success in New England in the 1970s through the 
1990s. Others, including former DOE Secretary Ernie Moniz, have 
been working on versions of a risk-sharing concept that could 
spur utilities to move forward. Yet, even if these efforts 
succeeded, Congress could take additional steps to further 
enable successful advanced reactor deployment. Frankly, our 
national capabilities to build large infrastructure projects 
are rusty. We need additional investment in university 
engineering programs focused on project management and civil 
construction, given that the non-nuclear portion of these 
plants represent two-thirds of the cost and materials needed to 
build the units. Congress should revisit the way it funds 
university-based programs supporting nuclear power to ensure 
that it fully covers the full scope of needed engineering 
disciplines.
    Since the invasion of Ukraine, our country has reviewed how 
we have sourced many critical components, and nuclear is no 
exception. Fabricating and manufacturing nuclear-grade 
components can't be done at the snap of your fingers. If we are 
to achieve the economies of scale needed to efficiently and 
cost-effectively manufacture small modular reactors here in the 
U.S., there is further work needed. For new nuclear suppliers, 
there are rigorous and costly training and quality assurance 
requirements that are appropriately imposed to meet NRC 
expectations. Congress could facilitate this supply by 
providing incentives to encourage existing non-nuclear 
manufacturers to enter the field. This could include training 
funded by the Departments of Energy and Commerce to educate 
non-nuclear suppliers about the potential opportunities. It 
could also include tax incentives for companies that seek to 
meet these qualification standards or who expend training 
resources to prepare their workers in these highly specialized 
nuclear trades.
    The events in Ukraine have also made it clear that our 
nation has become overly dependent on Russian-supplied low-
enriched uranium (LEU) and high-assay low-enriched uranium 
(HALEU). While recent Congressional efforts are helpful, 
additional legislation, including creating a federally owned 
inventory of LEU and HALEU, is vital to help spur additional 
domestic enrichment investment. Toward this end, the
