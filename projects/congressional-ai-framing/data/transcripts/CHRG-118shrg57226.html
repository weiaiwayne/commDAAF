        OPENING STATEMENT OF HON. CHRISTOPHER A. COONS, 
           A U.S. SENATOR FROM THE STATE OF DELAWARE

    Chair Coons This hearing of the IP Subcommittee of the 
Senate Judiciary Committee will come to order. Thank you to all 
of our witnesses on the first and second panel for 
participating today. I apologize for being a few moments late. 
We are in the middle of a two-vote series, so all of us will, 
at some point, get up and go vote and come back. My hope is 
we'll be able to continue this hearing, regardless.
    I'd like to particularly thank my Ranking Member and 
friend, Senator Thom Tillis, and his staff for continuing to 
work so well together and putting this hearing together on a 
consensus basis. Today we are examining the Patent Eligibility 
and Restoration Act, or PERA. This is our second drive through 
this thicket, and I'm looking forward to a return not just of 
many of the witnesses from our previous hearing but to a 
reconsideration of many of the core issues.
    PERA would reform U.S. patent eligibility law. More than 70 
years ago, Congress passed the Patent Act, and that law defined 
patent-eligible subject matter very broadly. The legislative 
history suggests that Congress intended to allow anything under 
the sun, as made by man, to be patentable. Other provisions in 
the Patent Act, requiring a new invention be novel, nonobvious, 
have a written description, and so forth served as the fine 
filters to patentability, and eligibility was a course filter.
    That all changed roughly a decade ago, when the Supreme 
Court announced a new two-step test for determining patent 
eligibility. Patent examiners and judges have to determine 
whether an invention is directed to a patent-ineligible concept 
like a law of nature or an abstract idea, and if so, the 
examiner or judge then has to consider whether the patent 
claims contain an inventive concept. The result: Fewer 
inventions are patent eligible in the United States, 
particularly in rapidly advancing fields like medical 
diagnostics, software, artificial intelligence; however, those 
same inventions are still eligible for patent protection 
abroad, which means if innovators and investors don't have 
confidence in our ability to protect inventions here in the 
United States, they may or will take their skills and 
investments elsewhere.
    This new test doesn't just put the U.S. at a competitive 
disadvantage. It is also quite difficult for judges to apply 
with any degree of certainty. Here's how judges on the Federal 
Circuit, the country's specialized patent court, have described 
the State of U.S. eligibility law: incoherent, fraught, 
unclear, inconsistent, and confusing. Things are so bad, the 
Chief Judge of the Federal Circuit has made an unprecedented 
plea for guidance to the Supreme Court; multiple Solicitors 
General of the United States have asked the Court to clarify 
this law, this area of jurisprudence; and the Patent and 
Trademark Office has asserted that the legal uncertainty from 
the Supreme Court's decisions has caused the agency unique 
challenges.
    In 2019, Senator Tillis and I held a marathon three days of 
hearings and heard from forty-five witnesses on the issue of 
patentability. Almost every witness agreed that eligibility law 
was a mess and needed further clarity and guidance. The Supreme 
Court has consistently refused to step in, denying every one of 
the roughly hundred cert petitions raising this question over 
the past decade. With the Supreme Court unwilling, unlikely to 
act, legislation is the best and may be the only path forward, 
which is why I was happy to once again join Senator Tillis in 
introducing PERA.
    PERA will return eligibility to important inventions like 
biotechnology, medical diagnostics, 5G, AI, blockchain. It will 
also make clear what is not patent eligible: mere ideas; 
discoveries of what already exists in nature, like unmodified 
human genes; and social or cultural content--things that 
everyone agrees is beyond the scope of our patent system. The 
bill would accomplish this in three ways. First, PERA would 
abolish judicially created exceptions to patent eligibility, 
including those set forth in the Supreme Court's two-step test. 
Second, it would clarify that eligibility determinations are to 
be made by considering every claim element of the patent as a 
whole and without regard to the novelty, nonobviousness, and 
disclosure requirements. And, third, it would set forth a 
specific but extensive list of excluded subject matter while 
maintaining the existing statutory categories of eligible 
matter.
    I look forward to continuing this work with Senator Tillis 
and with the wide range of stakeholder groups represented by 
witnesses here today, as we move forward with PERA. With 
Senator Tillis's cooperation, we've assembled two panels with 
diverse views and perspectives, pro and con. And I encourage 
our witnesses to be direct about what they like about the bill 
or to be specific about what they'd like to see changed in the 
bill and why. I'll introduce this first panel of witnesses in a 
moment, but first I'll turn it over to Senator Tillis of North 
Carolina.

 STATEMENT OF HON. ANDREI IANCU, PARTNER, SULLIVAN & CROMWELL 
                  LLP, LOS ANGELES, CALIFORNIA

    Mr. Iancu. Thank you, Mr. Chairman. Chairman Coons, Ranking 
Member Tillis, Senator Hirono, Members of the Subcommittee, 
good afternoon, and thank you for the opportunity to discuss 
PERA. I want to start by particularly thanking the Subcommittee 
for undertaking the critically important task of modernizing 
the statute which in essence first appeared in the Patent Act 
of 1793, written when Madison and Jefferson were drafting 
patent laws. A lot has changed in the world of technology since 
1793, and courts and the USPTO will be forgiven for struggling 
to apply an 18th-century statute to 21st-century technology. 
Our founding fathers were brilliant, but even they did not 
anticipate DNA processing or the Internet.
    The current State of Section 101 law is the result of many 
court decisions over the decades, trying to determine whether 
these modern technologies fit into categories defined in 1793 
or whether they are subject to certain exceptions courts have 
imposed since then. These court decisions also have resulted in 
certain de facto rules, such that diagnostic techniques, for 
example, are generally not eligible for patent in the United 
States--that Congress has never considered, debated, or passed 
into law. If the United States is not to recognize patents for 
certain categories of inventions that would otherwise be part 
of the categories outlined by Congress, then it is up to 
Congress to make that rule. In other words, Congress defined 
the categories of invention that are eligible for patent. If 
there are to be exceptions to those categories, they must 
likewise come from Congress.
    One of the biggest errors in the current jurisprudence is 
that court decisions have recently imported into Section 101 
analysis concepts from other parts of the patent code, such as 
questions of novelty, which should be dealt with in Section 
102; questions of obviousness, which should be addressed in 
Section 103; and questions of specificity and disclosure, which 
should be addressed in a Section 112 analysis. All this has 
even sown confusion among the expert ranks of the hardworking 
patent examiners at the USPTO.
    To address this issue, the patent office promulgated 
guidelines in 2019 that synthesized the relevant caselaw and 
provided examiners and applicants a significantly improved 
framework for analyzing eligibility under 101. This has 
dramatically improved the analysis at the USPTO. For example, a 
study by the office's chief economist has shown that 
uncertainty of Section 101 decisions for the relevant 
technologies decreased by 44 percent in just that first year 
following publication of the guidelines. It's working really 
well.
    Courts, however, are independent and not bound by 
administrative guidelines. So, they persist. They persist in 
their approach, which has caused profound uncertainty in the 
lower courts and among inventors, investors, and patent law 
practitioners alike. As a result, congressional action is 
needed to determine affirmatively which categories of 
inventions should be deemed as statutorily patentable or 
unpatentable. Here is the most important point. Congress needs 
to create clear rules. Whatever those rules may end up being 
after the legislative debate, at least the public deserves to 
know what the rules of the road are.
    Fortunately, PERA provides a legislative vehicle for the 
United States to correct the state of the law. PERA outlines a 
correct three-step framework for analysis. First, PERA 
maintains the broad categories of what qualifies as an 
invention in the U.S.--in other words, what is in the patent 
system. PERA then expressly outlines certain categories that 
are not considered to be eligible for patent--in other words, 
what is out of the patent system--and further indicates that 
courts are not to create any other exceptions that are not in 
the statute.
    Third, PERA clarifies that excluded matter does not 
automatically destroy eligibility. Specifically, an invention 
that combines excluded matter with eligible matter into a 
useful, practical application--then that combination may still 
be eligible. In the end, once the categories of included and 
excluded matter, after debate and adjustment, as appropriate, 
are settled on and passed into law, PERA will bring immediate 
certainty to Section 101.
    I commend the Subcommittee for holding this hearing to 
bring much-needed reform to Section 101 and to set our Nation 
on a path of innovation and success for the 21st century and 
beyond. Thank you again for the opportunity to testify, and I 
look forward to your questions.

submission for the record.]

STATEMENT OF RICHARD BLAYLOCK, PARTNER, PILLSBURY WINTHROP SHAW 
               PITTMAN LLP, SAN DIEGO, CALIFORNIA

    Mr. Blaylock. Chair Coons, Ranking Member Tillis, and other 
Members of the Subcommittee, thank you for the opportunity to 
address the topic of patent subject-matter eligibility. I'm 
appearing on behalf of Invitae Corporation, for whom I've been 
corporate and intellectual property counsel since its formation 
in 2010.
    Invitae and the clinical diagnostic community it serves is 
concerned that the Patent Eligibility Restoration Act, or PERA, 
as introduced, would stifle innovation and harm patient care in 
the fields of diagnostic genetic testing and precision 
medicine. Invitae is a leading medical genetics company and 
delivers clinical, actionable genetic information for both 
children and adults. Invitae has served over four million 
patients, and its success in providing critical genetic 
information has been fostered by the current patent eligibility 
jurisprudence in the United States.
    We have often heard the suggestion that the publication of 
the human genome has rendered moot the need for concern about 
patents related to human genes. The argument rests on the idea 
that the risk of patenting human genes has passed, because the 
sequences of human genes are no longer new. This represents a 
fundamental misunderstanding of the risks to science and 
patient care caused by permitting natural phenomena to be 
patented. Under current law, natural phenomena are not patent-
eligible subject matter, nor are applications of conventional 
technology to natural phenomena.
    No person has the standard human genome. All of us have 
variants in our genetic sequences. Some of these variants are 
clinically significant. A well-known example is the collection 
of variants in the sequences of the BRCA1 and BRCA2 genes that, 
when present, indicate a substantially elevated lifetime risk 
of breast cancer, pancreatic cancer, prostate cancer, or other 
types of cancer. Variants such as these are natural biomarkers, 
and this information--including its association with disease 
risk--is an unpatentable natural phenomena.
    Similarly, the application of conventional sequencing 
technology to detect the presence or absence of such biomarkers 
is unpatentable. By contrast, nothing in the current law 
impedes the patenting of new diagnostic techniques such as new 
tools for sequencing genes. While some important biomarkers 
have been discovered, the human genome is vast, and far more 
important biomarkers remain to be discovered. Our increasing 
sophistication in understanding these biomarkers and their 
clinical relevance is essential to the practice of personalized 
medicine.
    The absence of patents on natural phenomena has not 
impaired innovation in the genetic diagnostics industry. Quite 
the opposite is true. In the last decade, the genetic testing 
industry has thrived. The cost of genetic testings and genetic 
sequencing has fallen dramatically. There are no barriers to 
innovation in detecting new, clinically relevant biomarkers. 
New biomarkers are being recognized on a routine basis.
    PERA would permit the privatization of natural phenomena in 
the form of knowledge about human health and disease risks such 
as that embodied by genetic biomarkers. PERA would slam the 
door shut on innovation and its clinical application in 
medicine. A new patent thicket covering newly discovered 
biomarkers would appear and impede patient access to such basic 
information. Because so many are engaged in identification of 
new biomarkers, a diverse group of new patentees would stand in 
the way of a clinician's effort to conduct an analysis of a 
patient's genetic information, because state-of-the-art 
clinical testing would require permission from all of them, 
none of whom would be required to grant such permission.
    That was the reigning set of conditions before the Mayo, 
Myriad, and Alice decisions, and PERA would take us back: back 
to a time when the toll charged for access to biomarkers in 
just the two BRCA genes was $4,400. Today, a test yielding far 
more information costs only a fraction of that amount. The 
patent system should afford the opportunity to own the cure but 
not the disease. Variants such as these natural biomarkers and 
this information, including association with disease risk--I'm 
sorry. The patent system does not now and should--I'm sorry. 
The patent system does now and should permit the protection of 
inventions but not mere discoveries of information, and it does 
and should reward innovation in the form of new therapies and 
new tools for detecting biomarkers, but not the biomarkers 
themselves.
    We urge the Subcommittee to recognize natural phenomena 
such as biomarkers as precompetitive information available to 
all. This can be accomplished by revising PERA to include the 
requirement that natural phenomena, natural materials, and 
abstract ideas be treated as prior art to all patent 
applications. This would be the most efficient way to 
effectively codify the essence of current Supreme Court 
jurisprudence on patent subject-matter eligibility. Invitae 
stands ready to offer any assistance the Subcommittee may 
request in order to approach patent law reform while preserving 
the vitality of the genetic testing industry, innovation, and, 
importantly, patient access to the promise of precision 
medicine. Thank you.
    [The prepared statement of Mr. Blaylock appears as a 
submission for the record.]
    Chair Coons. Thank you. Ms. Brinckerhoff.

            STATEMENT OF COURTENAY C. BRINCKERHOFF, 
          PARTNER, FOLEY & LARDNER LLP, WASHINGTON, DC

    Ms. Brinckerhoff Chairman Coons, Ranking Member Tillis, 
thank you for the opportunity to speak on this important issue. 
I am providing comments from the perspective of a practitioner 
with many years' experience helping clients in various life 
sciences industries obtain patents on their inventions, but I 
think I'm the first one who has to include a disclaimer that my 
testimony is personal to me and should not be attributed to any 
of my clients or Foley & Lardner, its partners, or its clients.
    I want to touch on three issues: International 
inconsistency, the impact on microbial technologies, and 
ongoing uncertainty. Since Mayo, Myriad, and Alice, there are 
inventions that cannot be patented in the U.S. that can still 
be patented in other countries, including Europe, Japan, and 
China. These include most isolated natural products, such as 
vaccine antigens; therapeutic peptides; chemicals isolated from 
plants; and bacteria that are useful in commercial and 
industrial applications.
    With regard to diagnostic methods, although some countries 
have limited exceptions on public policy grounds, most still 
grant patents on methods of detecting new diagnostic markers 
such as the claims invalidated by the Federal Circuit decision 
in Sequenom. Because you have to disclose your invention to 
obtain a patent, this means that inventors have to disclose 
their inventions to the whole world but cannot protect them to 
the same extent in the U.S. As I understand it, PERA would 
bring the U.S. eligibility back in line with patents that can 
be granted in other countries.
    The current imbalance and the quid pro quo of disclosure 
for patent rights is particularly acute for technologies 
related to microorganisms such as bacteria useful in brewing, 
baking, production of dairy products; oil and plastic degrading 
bacteria; carbon dioxide fixing bacteria useful in 
environmental remediation, just to name a few examples. In 
order to satisfy the written description requirement for a 
patent, the inventor has to deposit a sample of the bacteria 
with a facility such as the ATCC in Gaithersburg, Maryland and 
then has to make samples available to the public when the 
patent grants. Even if the patent only covers a specific method 
of using the bacteria, samples have to be made available to the 
public. Prior to Myriad, a patent could be obtained on the 
isolated bacteria, but post Myriad, the possible scope of 
protection is more limited.
    This imbalance, requiring a physical specimen but not 
granting an equivalent scope of protection, disincentivizes 
patenting in this area and makes trade secret protection more 
attractive, especially when the bacteria can be used in-house. 
I think it's important that PERA restore eligibility in this 
important space, to encourage continued investment in 
innovation.
    Turning to the continued expansion in the judicial 
exceptions, in my written testimony, I compare and contrast two 
Federal Circuit decisions: the 2021 decision in Yu v. Apple 
that invalidated claims on a digital camera under the abstract 
idea exception, even though the claims require structural 
components such as lenses and sensors--but a year earlier, in 
CardioNet, the Federal Circuit reversed a district court 
abstract-idea ruling on claims to a device for detecting and 
reporting atrial fibrillation, where the claimed components 
were detectors, logic, and something called event generators. 
Every useful invention has a purpose that could be described as 
an abstract idea by analogy to Yu, but most can be claimed so 
the subject matter as a whole could be found eligible by 
analogy to CardioNet.
    But how can stakeholders predict which approach will be 
taken? The Patent Office has interpreted Myriad as permitting 
patenting of natural products that have been modified in a 
conventional way, such as the cDNA claims upheld by the Supreme 
Court. But some district courts feel compelled to apply the 
Mayo-Alice framework to all claims and search for a separate 
inventive concept. If that approach becomes the law of the 
land, innovators may not even be able to patent specific 
formulations of drugs that have been derived from natural 
products or any life sciences inventions that do not also 
involve a new scientific technique.
    When the judicial exceptions are applied in such an 
unpredictable manner, the validity of a patent and the value of 
associated technologies are also unpredictable. 101 issues are 
considered in potential deals, and unpredictability can be a 
drag on valuation and impact investment decisions. We know now 
that the Supreme Court is not going to fix these issues, so 
it's imperative that legislative action like PERA be taken, and 
I thank you for your efforts in this regard.
    [The prepared statement of Ms. Brinckerhoff appears as a 
submission for the record.]
    Chair Coons. Thank you, Ms. Brinckerhoff. Mr. Johnson.

effort and where it will be rewarded.
    PERA accomplishes this, as you've mentioned, by prohibiting

determinations. This is the most important area where people

that is eligible for patenting doesn't mean that it's

    Professor Mossoff. Thank you. Ranking Member Tillis and

reform of Section 101 and why Congress should pass the Patent

created the Alice-Mayo inquiry under Section 101 of the Patent 
Act. This new legal test converted patent eligibility doctrine 
into something never before seen in the U.S. patent system. It 
turned what the Supreme Court once rightly called a threshold

caused a revolution in U.S. patent law in ways never before 
seen by either inventors or lawyers.
