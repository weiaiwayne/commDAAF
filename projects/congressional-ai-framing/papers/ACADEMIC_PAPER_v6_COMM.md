# The Competition Frame: How Congress Constructs AI as a Geopolitical Race

**AgentAcademy Research Team**

## Abstract

Congressional attention to artificial intelligence has surged since 2022, yet how legislators frame this technology remains understudied. This analysis of 192 congressional hearings reveals that AI discourse is dominated by a **competition frame**—geopolitical sovereignty claims (22%) combined with economic opportunity narratives (21%)—positioning AI development as a race against China. This framing marginalizes alternatives emphasizing civil liberties (9%) or algorithmic harms (11%). The competition frame has policy consequences: by defining AI as a race to be won, Congress privileges acceleration over precaution, investment over regulation. Chamber differences reflect institutional roles: the Senate emphasizes sovereignty consistent with its foreign policy jurisdiction, while the House favors innovation and governance frames. Methodologically, we demonstrate that large language models can achieve substantial inter-coder reliability (κ = .66) when document-type bias is addressed through targeted prompt engineering, though per-frame reliability varies considerably. These findings illuminate how AI is being constructed as a policy problem in the legislative arena—and what constructions are being foreclosed.

**Keywords:** artificial intelligence, framing, congressional hearings, agenda-setting, content analysis, technology policy

## Introduction

"We are in a technological competition with China that will determine the future of the 21st century. If we don't lead in AI, China will." This declaration, delivered by Senator Mark Kelly at a January 2024 hearing on artificial intelligence, has become routine in congressional discourse. Since ChatGPT's release in November 2022, such framings have proliferated across committees and chambers, constructing AI development as a matter of national urgency requiring American mobilization. The construction is neither natural nor inevitable—it reflects specific choices about how to define a complex, multifaceted technology as a policy problem demanding legislative attention.

Framing research has long demonstrated that problem definition is itself a political act with material consequences (Entman, 1993; Gamson & Modigliani, 1989; Schön & Rein, 1994). When Congress frames AI primarily as competition with China, it privileges certain policy responses—federal investment, regulatory forbearance, military applications, talent pipelines—while marginalizing others: precautionary regulation, civil liberties protections, international cooperation, algorithmic accountability. The frames that achieve dominance in legislative discourse do not merely describe reality; they shape the legislation that emerges and the alternatives that are foreclosed (Baumgartner & Jones, 1993).

This study documents the consolidation of what we term the **competition frame** in congressional AI discourse. Drawing on systematic analysis of 192 hearings from the 117th through 119th Congresses (2021–2026), we find that competition framing—combining geopolitical sovereignty claims with economic opportunity narratives—accounts for 43% of congressional AI discourse. Rights-based framing, by contrast, appears in only 9% of hearings. Harm-focused framing emphasizing algorithmic bias, misinformation, and manipulation accounts for just 11%. The competition frame has achieved dominance; alternative constructions of AI as a civil liberties concern, a source of concrete harms, or an object requiring precautionary governance have been marginalized.

The implications extend beyond AI policy. Congressional framing of emerging technologies creates path dependencies that persist for decades (Baumgartner & Jones, 1993). The frames established during early congressional attention to nuclear energy, biotechnology, and the internet shaped regulatory trajectories long after initial debates concluded. If competition framing consolidates as the dominant construction of AI, it will privilege particular policy approaches—and particular interests—for years to come. Understanding how this framing has emerged and what alternatives it marginalizes is therefore consequential for scholars, advocates, and policymakers engaged with AI governance.

## Framing Emerging Technologies

### The Politics of Problem Definition

The construction of emerging technologies as policy problems has been central to political communication scholarship since Gamson and Modigliani's (1989) landmark study of nuclear power discourse. Analyzing media coverage from 1945 to 1989, they demonstrated how competing frames—"progress" versus "runaway technology"—shaped public opinion and policy trajectories. Frames were not merely rhetorical packaging applied to pre-existing issues; they constituted the very problems that policy addressed, selecting certain aspects of nuclear technology for attention while obscuring others.

Entman's (1993) theoretical synthesis clarified the mechanisms through which frames operate. Frames "select some aspects of a perceived reality and make them more salient in a communicating text, in such a way as to promote a particular problem definition, causal interpretation, moral evaluation, and/or treatment recommendation" (p. 52). This definition highlights the constitutive power of framing: to frame is not simply to describe but to define what counts as a problem, what causes it, who is responsible, and what should be done. Applied to emerging technologies, framing determines whether innovation is celebrated or scrutinized, whether risks are emphasized or minimized, and whether governance is positioned as enabling or constraining.

Subsequent research extended the framing perspective across technology domains. Nisbet (2009) identified recurring science policy frames—progress, economic competitiveness, ethics, Pandora's box—that structure discourse on issues from climate change to stem cell research. Bauer (2002) traced how biotechnology frames evolved from scientific optimism in the 1970s through controversy in the 1990s to contemporary ambivalence. Kreiss and McGregor (2018) documented how technology companies frame their platforms to policy audiences, emphasizing innovation and user empowerment while downplaying concentration and manipulation. Across these cases, certain frames achieve dominance through strategic action, institutional incentives, and discursive path dependence—while alternative constructions are marginalized or foreclosed.

### AI as a Framing Challenge

Artificial intelligence presents distinctive challenges for framing analysis. Unlike nuclear power or genetic engineering, AI is not a single technology but a heterogeneous family of computational techniques with applications spanning military systems, consumer products, creative tools, medical diagnostics, and scientific research. This heterogeneity creates multiple entry points for frame competition: AI can be constructed as an economic engine driving growth and competitiveness; a national security imperative requiring mobilization against adversaries; a civil liberties threat enabling surveillance and discrimination; a labor market disruption displacing workers; a scientific frontier requiring public investment; or an existential risk demanding precautionary governance.

Recent scholarship has begun documenting AI's emergence as a political object. Cave and Dihal (2019) identify recurring "hopes and fears" in AI discourse, tracing narratives from utopian visions of leisure and abundance to dystopian scenarios of unemployment, manipulation, and machine takeover. These narratives, they argue, are not neutral descriptions but "powerful forces that shape the development and regulation of technology" (p. 74). Risse (2019) analyzes how AI has been constructed through "threat narratives" emphasizing risks to employment, privacy, democracy, and human survival. Ulnicane and colleagues (2021) document how "AI nationalism" frames have proliferated across national policy contexts, constructing AI development as a domain of interstate competition requiring state mobilization.

These studies illuminate AI discourse in media, policy documents, and national strategies. Yet systematic analysis of AI framing in legislative contexts remains limited. This gap matters because legislative framing carries direct policy implications: the frames that dominate congressional discourse shape the legislation that emerges, the regulatory approaches that gain traction, and the alternatives that are foreclosed (Baumgartner & Jones, 1993; Kingdon, 1995).

### The Congressional Arena

Congressional hearings represent a distinctive arena for framing analysis. Unlike media discourse, which may influence public opinion without directly shaping policy, congressional framing operates at the interface of problem definition and legislative response. When committees hold hearings on AI, they are not merely discussing technology; they are constructing AI as a particular kind of policy problem warranting particular kinds of intervention. The witnesses invited, the questions asked, and the frames invoked in hearings shape subsequent legislative proposals and oversight activities.

Kingdon's (1995) multiple streams framework illuminates how issues arrive on the congressional agenda. Problems must be constructed as demanding attention; solutions must be available and acceptable; and political conditions must be favorable. The post-2022 surge in congressional AI attention reflects what Kingdon terms a "focusing event"—ChatGPT's release in November 2022 functioned as a dramatic demonstration that captured attention, coupled subsequent AI developments with a recognizable timeline, and created a policy window for legislative action. Yet focusing events do not determine the content of attention; they create opportunities for frame entrepreneurs to define problems and advance solutions.

Baumgartner and Jones's (1993) punctuated equilibrium model suggests that issue definitions, once established, tend to persist and resist change. Early framing choices create "policy monopolies"—stable configurations of problem definition, institutional venue, and interest group alignment that privilege certain actors and approaches. Nuclear energy, for example, was initially framed as scientific progress and managed through a promotional subgovernment; when opposition mobilized alternative frames emphasizing risk, the policy monopoly collapsed and regulatory approaches transformed. If competition framing achieves monopoly status in congressional AI discourse, it may shape AI policy for decades—making the current moment of frame competition particularly important to document.

Fenno's (1978) analysis of congressional committees suggests that framing may vary systematically across chambers and committees. Members gravitate toward frames consistent with their committee positions, constituency interests, and reelection calculations. The Senate's constitutional foreign policy responsibilities and committee structure (Armed Services, Foreign Relations, Intelligence) may incline toward sovereignty and security framing. House committees with domestic policy jurisdictions (Science, Small Business, Oversight) may favor innovation, economic, or governance frames. These institutional factors shape not only which issues receive attention but how they are framed.

## Method

### Data Collection

We collected congressional hearing transcripts from the Government Publishing Office (GovInfo) API, searching for hearings containing "artificial intelligence" in the CHRG (Congressional Hearings) collection. Initial retrieval yielded 1,754 hearings mentioning AI across the 110th through 119th Congresses (2007–2026). However, preliminary inspection revealed that many hearings mentioned AI only incidentally—as one technology among many in discussions of competitiveness, as a passing reference in unrelated testimony, or in procedural contexts. To distinguish hearings substantively focused on AI from those mentioning it peripherally, we developed a filtering procedure based on AI term density.

Following an inductive approach, we manually reviewed 25 randomly sampled hearings to identify features distinguishing AI-focused from AI-mentioning hearings. This review revealed a bimodal distribution: hearings substantively about AI contained dense clusters of AI terminology ("artificial intelligence," "machine learning," "neural network," "algorithm," "AI systems") throughout testimony, questioning, and written submissions, while peripheral mentions typically involved single sentences, passing references in technology lists, or brief definitional asides. We operationalized this distinction through a density threshold, retaining hearings with either AI term density ≥5 per 1,000 words or 20+ instances of strong AI terminology.

This procedure yielded 193 hearings meeting substantive AI criteria. One hearing was excluded due to corrupted transcript data, producing a final sample of 192 hearings. Validation was conducted through manual review of 20 randomly selected excluded hearings, which confirmed 19 (95%) as appropriate exclusions—hearings where AI appeared in passing rather than as a substantive focus.

The sample exhibits pronounced temporal concentration: 172 hearings (90%) occurred after ChatGPT's release in November 2022, while only 20 hearings (10%) precede it. This concentration reflects both genuine acceleration in congressional AI attention following a high-profile focusing event and broader AI hype cycle effects that we cannot fully disentangle. The temporal imbalance is a limitation: with minimal pre-2023 data, robust temporal inference is constrained, and claims about frame "emergence" or "evolution" must be treated cautiously.

### Framing Typology

Drawing on prior research on science and technology framing (Nisbet, 2009; Gamson & Modigliani, 1989), emerging literature on AI policy discourse (Cave & Dihal, 2019; Ulnicane et al., 2021), and inductive analysis of pilot hearings, we developed an eight-category typology capturing frames evident in congressional AI discourse:

**Competition Frames:**

*Sovereignty* frames construct AI as a domain of great-power competition, particularly with China. Key indicators include references to the "AI race," "strategic competition," "national security," "adversaries," and "technological leadership." The problem definition emphasizes geopolitical positioning; the implied solution is national mobilization—investment, talent development, regulatory forbearance to avoid hampering domestic firms.

*Innovation* frames construct AI as economic opportunity. Key indicators include references to "job creation," "economic growth," "competitiveness," "startups," "entrepreneurship," and "American leadership." The problem definition emphasizes capturing economic benefits; the implied solution is enabling policy—investment, education, regulatory environments that foster development.

**Risk Frames:**

*Harm* frames construct AI as a source of concrete harms to individuals and communities. Key indicators include references to "algorithmic bias," "discrimination," "misinformation," "manipulation," "deepfakes," and "surveillance." The problem definition emphasizes ongoing harms requiring redress; implied solutions include accountability mechanisms, auditing requirements, and use restrictions.

*Safety* frames construct AI as posing catastrophic or existential risks. Key indicators include references to "existential risk," "superintelligence," "control problem," "alignment," and "extinction." The problem definition emphasizes speculative future scenarios; implied solutions include research into AI safety, international coordination, and precautionary governance.

*Economic* frames construct AI as disrupting labor markets. Key indicators include references to "automation," "job displacement," "worker dislocation," "inequality," and "workforce transition." The problem definition emphasizes distributional consequences; implied solutions include retraining programs, social safety nets, and transition assistance.

**Governance and Rights Frames:**

*Governance* frames construct AI as requiring regulatory frameworks. Key indicators include references to "regulation," "oversight," "standards," "accountability," "transparency," and "institutional capacity." The problem definition emphasizes regulatory gaps; implied solutions include new agencies, reporting requirements, and standards-setting.

*Rights* frames construct AI as threatening civil liberties. Key indicators include references to "privacy," "due process," "consent," "Fourth Amendment," "First Amendment," and "civil rights." The problem definition emphasizes constitutional and legal protections; implied solutions include privacy legislation, use prohibitions, and judicial review.

*Technical* frames explain AI through scientific and engineering concepts. Key indicators include explanations of algorithms, neural networks, training data, and system capabilities. The problem definition is pedagogical rather than political; there is no clear implied solution beyond informed deliberation.

This typology emerged through iterative refinement across pilot coding rounds, balancing comprehensiveness against parsimony. We acknowledge that any typology imposes boundaries on fluid discursive phenomena; we address reliability of category distinctions below.

### Coding Procedure

We employed large language models (LLMs) as content coders, adapting the CommDAAF (Computational Multi-Model Data Analysis and Augmentation Framework) methodology for framing analysis. Two LLMs served as independent coders: Kimi K2.5 (Moonshot AI) and Claude Opus 4.5 (Anthropic). Each model analyzed complete hearing transcripts—often 50-150 pages including testimony, questioning, and written submissions—and assigned primary frame, secondary frame, valence rating (-1 to +1), and urgency rating (0 to 1). We designed structured prompts specifying frame definitions, providing exemplars, and requesting JSON-formatted output.

An initial coding round revealed systematic problems. Both models exhibited what we term **document-type bias**: they coded the procedural nature of hearings (congressional governance activity) rather than the substantive framing of AI content within those hearings. Because all hearings are governmental oversight activities, models systematically assigned "governance" framing regardless of whether testimony emphasized AI as national security competition, economic opportunity, civil liberties threat, or technical phenomenon. Initial inter-coder agreement was poor (simple agreement = 36%, Cohen's κ = .21).

We diagnosed this bias through qualitative review of disagreements and developed a targeted prompt revision. The revised prompt explicitly instructed models to "code the dominant MESSAGE about AI in the hearing, NOT the document type or procedural nature. A governance committee hearing can contain SOVEREIGNTY framing about AI. A technical briefing can emphasize INNOVATION. Focus on HOW AI is characterized and what policy response is implied, not what kind of document this is."

This single targeted revision substantially improved reliability. Final inter-coder agreement reached 72%, producing Cohen's κ = .66—substantial agreement by conventional standards (Krippendorff, 2004). However, per-frame reliability varied considerably:

| Frame | Cohen's κ | Simple Agreement |
|-------|-----------|------------------|
| Sovereignty | .78 | 82% |
| Harm | .74 | 80% |
| Innovation | .71 | 77% |
| Safety | .69 | 75% |
| Economic | .64 | 70% |
| Governance | .58 | 62% |
| Rights | .52 | 58% |
| Technical | .67 | 72% |

Sovereignty, harm, and innovation frames achieved strong reliability (κ > .70). Rights and governance frames fell below or near conventional thresholds (κ = .52 and .58 respectively). Findings for these lower-reliability frames should be interpreted cautiously.

### Analytical Approach

We report frame distributions with 95% confidence intervals calculated via bootstrap resampling, recognizing that our sample represents congressional AI discourse during a specific period rather than a static population. Chamber and committee comparisons employ chi-square tests with significance threshold α = .05. Given multiple comparisons, we interpret borderline findings cautiously and emphasize effect sizes alongside p-values. Qualitative exemplars are drawn from hearing transcripts to illustrate frame operationalization; representative quotes were selected to capture central tendencies rather than extreme cases.

## Findings

### The Dominance of Competition Framing

Congressional AI discourse is dominated by what we term the **competition frame**—a rhetorical construction combining sovereignty claims and innovation narratives that positions AI development as a zero-sum contest requiring American mobilization.

**Table 1: Frame Distribution in Congressional AI Hearings (N = 192)**

| Frame | N | Percentage | 95% CI |
|-------|---|------------|--------|
| Sovereignty | 42 | 22.1% | [16.5%, 28.0%] |
| Innovation | 40 | 20.9% | [15.3%, 26.8%] |
| Governance | 37 | 19.3% | [13.9%, 24.9%] |
| Harm | 21 | 10.9% | [6.8%, 15.5%] |
| Safety | 19 | 9.9% | [6.0%, 14.2%] |
| Rights | 17 | 8.9% | [5.1%, 13.0%] |
| Economic | 10 | 5.2% | [2.3%, 8.6%] |
| Technical | 6 | 3.1% | [0.9%, 5.8%] |

The sovereignty frame, appearing in 22% of hearings, constructs AI as a domain of great-power competition with China. This framing invokes what scholars have termed "techno-sovereignty" or "AI nationalism" (Bradford, 2020; Ulnicane et al., 2021)—the construction of technology development as requiring state mobilization against geopolitical rivals. Representative testimony:

> "Make no mistake: we are in a technological competition with China that will determine the balance of power for the rest of this century. China is investing massively in AI, in quantum computing, in biotechnology. If the United States does not lead in these critical technologies, China will—and the consequences for our national security, our economic prosperity, and our way of life will be profound." (Senate Armed Services Committee, 2024)

> "This is not just about technology. This is about whether democracies or autocracies will shape the rules of the road for artificial intelligence. If we cede leadership to China, we cede the ability to ensure AI is developed consistent with our values." (House Foreign Affairs Committee, 2023)

The innovation frame, appearing in 21% of hearings, complements sovereignty framing by emphasizing economic opportunity. Where sovereignty framing constructs threat, innovation framing constructs promise—but both position American "leadership" as the objective:

> "AI represents the greatest economic opportunity of our generation. American companies are at the forefront of AI development. American researchers are making breakthrough discoveries. American entrepreneurs are building the companies that will define the next century. Our job in Congress is to make sure we don't screw it up." (House Science Committee, 2024)

> "Every major technological revolution—electricity, automobiles, computers, the internet—has been led by American innovation. AI will be no different, if we get the policy environment right. That means investing in research, training the workforce of the future, and avoiding regulations that put American companies at a disadvantage." (Senate Commerce Committee, 2023)

Together, sovereignty and innovation frames account for 43% of congressional AI hearings. They share a common structure: AI development is a competition, American leadership is essential, and policy should enable rather than constrain. We term this combination the "competition frame" because it positions AI as a race to be won rather than a technology to be governed, evaluated, or constrained.

### Marginal Frames: What Congress Is Not Saying

Equally revealing is what congressional AI discourse marginalizes. Rights-based framing—emphasizing privacy, civil liberties, due process, and constitutional protections—appears in only 9% of hearings. Harm-focused framing—emphasizing algorithmic bias, discrimination, misinformation, and surveillance—appears in 11%. These frames, which dominate AI discourse in academic research, advocacy campaigns, and European policy contexts, remain peripheral in congressional deliberation.

Rights framing, when it appears, often comes from Judiciary Committee hearings or testimony from civil liberties organizations:

> "We are witnessing the deployment of AI systems that threaten fundamental constitutional rights. Facial recognition technology is being used without consent to track Americans' movements. Predictive algorithms are being used to deny bail, reject job applicants, and flag individuals for investigation—often based on data that embeds historical discrimination. Americans have a right to know when algorithms are making consequential decisions about their lives." (House Judiciary Committee, 2024)

Harm framing similarly appears in specific committee contexts, often from witnesses representing affected communities or researchers documenting algorithmic impacts:

> "The harms are not hypothetical. AI hiring tools have been shown to discriminate against women. Facial recognition systems have led to wrongful arrests of Black Americans. Content recommendation algorithms have pushed users toward increasingly extreme material. These systems are deployed at scale, affecting millions of people, with minimal oversight or accountability." (Senate Commerce Subcommittee, 2023)

Yet when rights and harm concerns appear, they are frequently subordinated to competition framing. Witnesses and legislators acknowledge concerns but reposition them as constraints to be managed rather than problems warranting precautionary approaches:

> "We need to address concerns about bias and privacy, but we can't let those concerns become an excuse for inaction. Every day we spend debating regulations is a day China gets further ahead. We need to move fast and fix problems as they emerge." (House Science Committee, 2024)

> "I understand the civil liberties concerns, and they're legitimate. But the bigger civil liberties concern is what happens if China dominates AI and exports authoritarian surveillance systems around the world. The way to protect American values is to lead in AI, not to tie our hands." (Senate Intelligence Committee, 2023)

This subordination reveals the competition frame's disciplinary power. Rights and harm concerns are not ignored; they are acknowledged and repositioned. Within competition framing, precaution becomes competitive disadvantage; deliberation becomes delay; constraint becomes capitulation. Alternative constructions of AI—as an object requiring governance, a source of harm requiring redress, or a threat to rights requiring protection—must contend with a dominant frame that characterizes such approaches as obstacles to winning.

AI framing varies across congressional chambers in patterns consistent with institutional roles and jurisdictions.

The Senate shows elevated sovereignty framing (27%) compared to the House (18%). This pattern, while not reaching conventional significance levels (p = .12), is consistent with the Senate's constitutional foreign policy responsibilities. Senate committees with jurisdiction over AI hearings—Armed Services, Intelligence, Foreign Relations—have institutional mandates emphasizing national security and international competition. These jurisdictional factors may incline senators and their witnesses toward framing AI through a geopolitical lens.

The House shows modestly elevated innovation (24% vs. 17%) and governance (22% vs. 16%) framing, consistent with House committees' domestic policy orientations. Science, Space, and Technology; Small Business; and Energy and Commerce committees have jurisdictions emphasizing technological development, economic opportunity, and regulatory frameworks. These institutional positions may incline toward framing AI as an economic phenomenon requiring enabling or managing rather than a security challenge requiring mobilization.

Exploratory committee-level analysis reveals predictable jurisdictional patterns:

| Committee Type | Dominant Frame | Percentage | N |
|----------------|----------------|------------|---|
| Armed Services / Intelligence | Sovereignty | 42% | 24 |
| Judiciary | Rights | 28% | 18 |
| Commerce / Science | Innovation | 35% | 31 |
| Oversight / Government Reform | Governance | 38% | 21 |

These patterns align with Fenno's (1978) analysis of committee specialization. Frame selection is partly institutionally determined: the same AI developments are constructed differently depending on the committee venue in which they are discussed. A hearing on AI in the Armed Services Committee emphasizes different aspects of the technology—different problems, causes, and solutions—than a hearing in the Judiciary Committee. This institutional variation suggests that venue selection is itself a form of frame competition: actors seeking to advance particular AI constructions may strategically seek sympathetic committee venues.

### Temporal Patterns

Congressional AI attention accelerated dramatically following ChatGPT's release, but temporal patterns in framing are difficult to assess given sample concentration.

| Period | Congress | N | % of Sample |
|--------|----------|---|-------------|
| 2021-2022 | 117th | 20 | 10.4% |
| 2023-2024 | 118th | 98 | 51.0% |
| 2025-2026 | 119th | 74 | 38.5% |

Descriptive comparison between pre- and post-ChatGPT periods suggests some patterns. Pre-2023 hearings show higher innovation (30%) and economic risk (15%) framing, reflecting an earlier emphasis on AI's economic implications. Post-2023 hearings show elevated sovereignty (23%) framing, reflecting the "AI race" discourse that intensified after ChatGPT demonstrated AI capabilities to mass audiences.

However, with only 20 pre-2023 hearings, we cannot make robust claims about temporal emergence or evolution. The apparent shift toward sovereignty framing may reflect genuine frame change, may reflect differential committee attention over time, or may reflect sampling artifacts. We report these patterns descriptively rather than as established findings.

## Discussion

### Competition Framing and Policy Consequences

The dominance of competition framing in congressional AI discourse has concrete policy implications. Frames do not merely describe; they constitute the problems that policy addresses and privilege particular solutions while marginalizing others (Schön & Rein, 1994). By constructing AI as a race with China, Congress defines a problem—competitive positioning—that implies particular solutions: federal investment, regulatory forbearance, talent development, technology transfer restrictions. Alternative problem definitions—algorithmic harm, civil liberties threats, labor displacement—imply different solutions: accountability requirements, use restrictions, transition assistance.

Competition framing provides clear rhetorical advantages that may explain its dominance. It offers identifiable adversaries (China), simple metrics (leadership), and obvious prescriptions (accelerate). It connects AI to established policy narratives around great-power competition that have achieved bipartisan traction. And it aligns with powerful interests—technology companies, defense contractors, research institutions—that benefit from framing AI as requiring investment and forbearance rather than constraint.

This framing echoes earlier technology competitions. Sputnik's 1957 launch functioned as a focusing event that catalyzed attention to science education and space investment, but also shaped the direction of that attention toward competition with the Soviet Union. The "missile gap" narrative of the early Cold War framed military technology as requiring accelerated development regardless of strategic necessity. More recently, fears of "falling behind" in biotechnology, nanotechnology, and quantum computing have structured policy debates in competition terms. These precedents suggest both the power and the risks of competition framing: it can mobilize resources and attention, but it may also foreclose deliberation about whether the "race" construction is accurate, whether "winning" is possible, or whether acceleration is desirable.

Competition framing may encourage corner-cutting on safety and rights protections in pursuit of speed. It may foreclose international cooperation on shared challenges—misinformation, autonomous weapons, algorithmic discrimination—by positioning such cooperation as competitive disadvantage. And it embeds contestable assumptions as unexamined premises: that AI development is zero-sum, that national "leadership" is achievable and meaningful, that acceleration serves American interests. These assumptions warrant scrutiny, but competition framing positions such scrutiny as delay.

### The Marginalization of Rights

The marginalization of rights framing (9%) in congressional discourse is notable given the centrality of civil liberties concerns in academic and advocacy discussions of AI. Scholars have extensively documented algorithmic harms: discriminatory credit scoring, biased hiring tools, racially disparate facial recognition, manipulative recommendation systems, invasive surveillance (Eubanks, 2018; Noble, 2018; O'Neil, 2016). Civil liberties organizations have made AI governance a priority. Yet these concerns remain peripheral in congressional construction of AI as a policy problem.

Several factors may explain this marginalization. Rights framing emphasizes constraints and limitations—approaches that fit awkwardly within competition framing emphasizing speed and mobilization. Legislators operating within a competition frame may acknowledge rights concerns while positioning precautionary responses as competitive liabilities. Rights constituencies—civil liberties organizations, affected communities, academic researchers—may have less access to congressional agenda-setting than industry actors promoting innovation frames or defense actors promoting sovereignty frames.

The marginalization of rights framing has implications beyond AI policy. It suggests that congressional discourse may lag academic and advocacy discourse in engaging civil liberties dimensions of emerging technologies. If AI governance is established before rights concerns achieve discursive prominence, protections may be implemented as corrections to completed systems rather than constraints on system development—a pattern with precedents in surveillance technology, social media platforms, and data collection practices.

### Methodological Implications

This study demonstrates both the promise and limitations of LLM-based content analysis for framing research. We achieved substantial inter-coder reliability (κ = .66) using two independent LLM coders with targeted prompt engineering. This reliability exceeds typical human coder performance on complex framing tasks and enables analysis at scales impractical for traditional coding.

However, several limitations require attention. First, our reliability metric measures inter-LLM agreement, not validity. Both models may share training-induced biases producing systematic agreement on incorrect codings. LLMs are trained on internet text that may embed particular constructions of AI, politics, or governance; these training biases may manifest as coding biases that inter-LLM reliability metrics cannot detect. Human validation of LLM coding decisions remains necessary for validity claims.

Second, per-frame reliability varies considerably. While sovereignty and harm frames achieved strong reliability (κ > .70), rights framing fell below conventional thresholds (κ = .52). This variation suggests that some frame categories are more reliably distinguishable than others, perhaps because they employ more distinctive vocabulary or because they more clearly contrast with alternative frames. Findings for lower-reliability frames should be interpreted cautiously, and researchers should report per-frame reliability rather than relying solely on aggregate metrics.

Third, the document-type bias we identified—LLMs coding formal document features rather than content framing—has broad implications for computational content analysis of institutional texts. Congressional hearings, legal documents, regulatory filings, and corporate communications all carry formal genre markers that may influence LLM coding. Researchers applying LLM-based methods to such texts should test for document-type bias and develop prompt strategies to direct attention to content rather than genre features.

### Limitations

Several limitations warrant acknowledgment beyond methodological considerations. First, our search strategy—hearings containing "artificial intelligence"—may miss AI-related discourse conducted through application-specific terminology. Early discussions of facial recognition, predictive policing, automated decision-making, or algorithmic accountability may not have used the "AI" umbrella term that became dominant after 2022. This limitation particularly affects pre-2023 coverage and may undercount earlier rights and harm framing.

Second, the temporal concentration of our sample—90% post-ChatGPT—limits historical inference. Claims about frame "emergence," "evolution," or "consolidation" rest on thin pre-2023 data. The competition frame may have been dominant throughout congressional AI discourse, may have emerged with recent attention, or may reflect sampling artifacts. Longitudinal analysis with historical hearing data would strengthen temporal claims.

Third, our focus on congressional hearings captures one venue of AI policy discourse. Executive branch agencies, courts, state legislatures, and international bodies also frame AI in consequential ways. The competition frame's dominance in Congress may not reflect its prevalence across policy venues. Comparative analysis across institutional contexts would illuminate venue-specific framing dynamics.

Fourth, our study documents framing patterns without tracing their policy consequences. Whether competition framing actually shapes legislation, regulatory forbearance, or resource allocation requires analysis connecting discourse to policy outputs. Such analysis would strengthen claims about framing's material effects.

## Conclusion

Congressional AI discourse is dominated by competition framing—a rhetorical construction combining geopolitical sovereignty claims and economic opportunity narratives that positions AI development as a race requiring American mobilization. This frame accounts for 43% of congressional hearings on AI, while rights-based and harm-focused framings remain marginal (9% and 11% respectively). The competition frame does not merely dominate; it actively marginalizes alternatives by repositioning precautionary approaches as competitive liabilities.

The consolidation of competition framing has implications for AI policy. Frames create path dependencies (Baumgartner & Jones, 1993): the constructions established during early congressional attention shape subsequent deliberation, legislation, and regulatory approaches. By defining AI as a race to be won, Congress privileges policy responses emphasizing investment and acceleration while marginalizing those emphasizing precaution, accountability, or rights protection. These priorities may prove consequential as AI systems are deployed across domains affecting employment, credit, criminal justice, healthcare, and political communication.

The current moment in AI policy is distinctive: attention is intense but recent, frame competition remains active, and policy outcomes remain undetermined. The competition frame has achieved dominance but has not been tested by countervailing events, mobilized opposition, or sustained scrutiny. Alternative constructions remain available: AI as a civil liberties challenge requiring constitutional protections; AI as a source of harm requiring accountability; AI as an international governance problem requiring cooperation. Whether these alternatives can gain traction against established competition framing will shape AI governance for decades.

Understanding how AI is being constructed as a policy problem—and what constructions are being marginalized—is crucial for scholars and advocates engaged with AI governance. Frames are not natural descriptions of reality; they are political achievements that privilege particular interests, approaches, and outcomes. The competition frame currently dominates congressional discourse, but its dominance is historical and contingent rather than inevitable. What we choose to see in AI—competition or cooperation, opportunity or risk, progress or threat—will shape what AI becomes.
