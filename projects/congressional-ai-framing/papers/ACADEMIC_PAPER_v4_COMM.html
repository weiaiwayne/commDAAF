The Competition Frame: How Congress Constructs Artificial Intelligence as a Geopolitical Race

Wayne Xu
Department of Communication, University of Massachusetts Amherst

Abstract

The arrival of artificial intelligence on the U.S. congressional agenda has prompted a familiar question in political communication: how do legislative actors construct novel technologies as policy problems? Drawing on 192 congressional hearings (2021–2026), this study examines the framing of AI in legislative discourse. We find that Congress has overwhelmingly constructed AI through a competition frame—a combination of geopolitical sovereignty claims (22%) and economic opportunity narratives (21%)—that positions AI development as a race against China requiring American mobilization. This competition frame marginalizes alternative constructions emphasizing civil liberties (9%), algorithmic harm (10%), or precautionary governance (19%). The dominance of competition framing has implications for AI policy: by defining the problem as “winning the AI race,” Congress forecloses deliberation on approaches emphasizing international cooperation, precautionary regulation, or rights-based governance. Methodologically, we employ a multi-model coding approach using large language models as content analysts, achieving substantial inter-coder reliability (κ = .66) while identifying critical considerations for computational framing analysis.

In January 2024, Senator Mark Kelly opened a hearing on artificial intelligence with a declaration that has become routine in congressional AI discourse: “We are in a competition with China to lead the world in AI. This is not a competition we can afford to lose.” Such framings have proliferated across congressional chambers since ChatGPT’s release in November 2022, constructing AI development as a matter of national urgency. Yet this construction of AI as a geopolitical race is neither natural nor inevitable—it reflects specific choices about how to define a complex technology as a policy problem.

How legislative actors frame emerging technologies shapes the trajectory of public policy. Framing research in political communication has long demonstrated that the definition of policy problems is itself a political act (Entman, 1993; Gamson & Modigliani, 1989). When Congress frames AI primarily as a competition with China, it privileges certain policy responses (investment, acceleration, military applications) while marginalizing others (precautionary regulation, civil liberties protections, international cooperation). Understanding how this framing emerged and consolidated is therefore crucial for scholars of technology policy, political communication, and AI governance.

This study presents a systematic analysis of AI framing in congressional discourse, drawing on 192 hearings from the 117th through 119th Congresses. We argue that congressional AI discourse is dominated by what we term a competition frame—a rhetorical construction combining geopolitical sovereignty claims with economic opportunity narratives. This frame positions AI development as a zero-sum contest requiring American mobilization, echoing what scholars have termed “AI nationalism” or “techno-sovereignty” (Bradford, 2020; Cave & Dihal, 2019). The competition frame now accounts for over 40% of congressional AI discourse, while rights-based (9%) and precautionary (10%) framings remain marginal.

Our findings contribute to ongoing conversations about technology framing in political communication. Scholars have examined how emerging technologies from nuclear power (Gamson & Modigliani, 1989) to genetic engineering (Nisbet, 2009) become objects of political contestation through competing frames. AI represents a particularly consequential case: its rapid development, broad applications, and potential societal impacts make frame competition over its meaning a high-stakes arena of political communication. By documenting the consolidation of competition framing in congressional discourse, we illuminate how AI is being constructed as a policy problem—and what constructions are being crowded out.

Framing Emerging Technologies: From Atomic Energy to Artificial Intelligence

The construction of emerging technologies as policy problems has been a central concern in political communication research. Gamson and Modigliani’s (1989) landmark study of nuclear power discourse demonstrated how competing frames—“progress” versus “runaway technology”—shaped public opinion and policy debates. Their work established that frames are not merely rhetorical packaging but constitutive elements of political reality, selecting and emphasizing particular aspects of complex issues while obscuring others.

Subsequent research has extended this framing perspective to domains from climate change (Nisbet, 2009) to biotechnology (Bauer, 2002) to social media platforms (Kreiss & McGregor, 2018). Across these cases, scholars have documented recurring dynamics: certain frames achieve dominance through strategic action by political actors, create “path dependencies” that shape subsequent discourse, and enable or constrain particular policy responses. The framing of technologies is thus not merely descriptive but performative—it constitutes the very problems that policy seeks to address.

Artificial intelligence presents distinctive challenges for framing analysis. Unlike nuclear power or genetic engineering, AI is not a single technology but a family of techniques with applications spanning military systems, consumer products, creative tools, and scientific research. This heterogeneity creates multiple entry points for frame competition: AI can be constructed as an economic engine, a security threat, a civil liberties concern, a labor market disruption, or a scientific frontier. The question of which construction achieves dominance is thus empirically open—and consequential for policy.

Recent scholarship has begun documenting AI’s emergence as a political issue. Cave and Dihal (2019) identify recurring “hopes and fears” in AI discourse, from economic prosperity to existential risk. Risse (2019) traces how AI has been constructed through “threat narratives” in policy contexts. Ulnicane and colleagues (2021) document how “AI nationalism” frames have proliferated across national policy discourse. Yet systematic analysis of AI framing in legislative contexts remains limited, leaving open questions about how representative bodies construct AI as a policy problem.

The Congressional Arena: Agenda-Setting and Issue Definition

Congressional hearings represent a distinctive arena for framing analysis. Unlike media discourse or public opinion, congressional framing carries direct implications for policy: the frames that dominate legislative discourse shape the legislation that emerges (Baumgartner & Jones, 1993). Understanding congressional framing is thus crucial for anticipating the trajectory of AI policy.

Kingdon’s (1995) multiple streams framework illuminates how issues arrive on the congressional agenda. Problems must be constructed as demanding attention; solutions must be available; and political conditions must be favorable. The post-2022 surge in congressional AI attention reflects a “focusing event”—ChatGPT’s release—that created a policy window for AI issues. Yet the content of that attention—how AI is framed within congressional discourse—remains to be documented.

Baumgartner and Jones’s (1993) punctuated equilibrium model suggests that issue definitions, once established, tend to persist and resist change. Early framing choices create “policy monopolies” that privilege certain actors and approaches. If congressional AI discourse has consolidated around competition framing, this pattern may shape AI policy for decades—making the current moment particularly important to document.

We therefore pose the following research questions:

RQ1: What frames dominate congressional discourse on artificial intelligence?

RQ2: How does AI framing vary across congressional chambers and committee jurisdictions?

RQ3: What frames remain marginal in congressional AI discourse, and what does their marginalization imply for AI policy?

Method

Sampling Congressional AI Discourse

We collected congressional hearing transcripts from the Government Publishing Office (GovInfo) API, searching for hearings containing “artificial intelligence” in the CHRG (Congressional Hearings) collection. Initial retrieval yielded 1,754 hearings mentioning AI across the 110th through 119th Congresses (2007–2026). However, many hearings mentioned AI only incidentally—as one technology among many, or in passing reference. To distinguish hearings substantively focused on AI from those mentioning it peripherally, we developed a filtering procedure based on AI term density.

Following an inductive approach, we manually reviewed 25 randomly sampled hearings to identify features distinguishing AI-focused from AI-mentioning hearings. This review revealed a bimodal distribution: hearings substantively about AI contained dense clusters of AI terminology throughout testimony and questioning, while peripheral mentions typically involved single sentences or brief passages. We operationalized this distinction through a density threshold, retaining hearings with either AI term density ≥5 per 1,000 words or 20+ instances of specific AI terminology (e.g., “machine learning,” “neural network,” “algorithmic”).

This procedure yielded 193 hearings meeting substantive AI criteria. One hearing was excluded due to corrupted transcript data, producing a final sample of 192 hearings. The sample is concentrated in the 118th and 119th Congresses (2023–2026), reflecting both genuine policy attention following ChatGPT’s release and broader AI hype cycle effects. This temporal concentration is a limitation we address below.

Framing Typology

Drawing on prior research on science and technology framing (Nisbet, 2009; Gamson & Modigliani, 1989), we developed an eight-category typology capturing frames evident in congressional AI discourse:

Competition Frames: - Sovereignty: AI as geopolitical competition, particularly with China; emphasizes national security, military applications, and strategic positioning - Innovation: AI as economic opportunity; emphasizes job creation, competitiveness, entrepreneurship, and American technological leadership

Risk Frames: - Harm: AI causing concrete harms to individuals and communities; emphasizes algorithmic bias, misinformation, manipulation, and surveillance - Safety: AI posing catastrophic or existential risks; emphasizes advanced AI systems, control problems, and speculative future scenarios - Economic: AI disrupting labor markets; emphasizes automation, job displacement, and economic inequality

Governance and Rights Frames: - Governance: AI requiring regulatory frameworks; emphasizes standards, oversight, accountability, and institutional capacity - Rights: AI threatening civil liberties; emphasizes privacy, due process, consent, and constitutional protections - Technical: AI explained through scientific concepts; emphasizes mechanisms, capabilities, and limitations

This typology emerged through iterative refinement across pilot coding rounds, balancing comprehensiveness against parsimony. We acknowledge that any typology imposes boundaries on fluid discursive phenomena; we address reliability of category distinctions below.

Coding Procedure: Large Language Models as Content Analysts

We employed large language models (LLMs) as content coders, adapting the CommDAAF (Computational Multi-Model Data Analysis and Augmentation Framework) methodology for framing analysis. This approach responds to calls for methodological innovation in computational content analysis while attending to validity concerns that accompany automated coding (Grimmer & Stewart, 2013; Nelson et al., 2021).

Two LLMs served as independent coders: Kimi K2.5 (Moonshot AI) and Claude Opus 4.5 (Anthropic). Each model analyzed full hearing transcripts and assigned primary and secondary frames along with valence and urgency ratings. We designed a structured prompt specifying frame definitions, providing exemplars, and requesting JSON-formatted output. Full prompts are reproduced in the appendix.

An initial coding round revealed a systematic problem: both models exhibited what we term “document-type bias,” coding the procedural nature of hearings (congressional governance activity) rather than the substantive framing of AI content. Initial inter-coder agreement was poor (κ = .21). We diagnosed this bias through qualitative review of disagreements and developed a targeted prompt revision explicitly instructing models to “code the dominant MESSAGE about AI in the hearing, NOT the document type or procedural nature.”

This revision substantially improved reliability. Final inter-coder agreement reached 72%, producing Cohen’s κ = .66—substantial agreement by conventional standards (Krippendorff, 2004). However, per-frame reliability varied considerably: sovereignty and harm frames achieved strong reliability (κ > .70), while rights framing fell below conventional thresholds (κ = .52). We interpret rights findings with appropriate caution.

Analytical Approach

We report frame distributions with 95% confidence intervals, recognizing that our sample represents congressional AI discourse during a specific period rather than a static population. Chamber and committee comparisons employ chi-square tests with significance threshold α = .05. Given multiple comparisons across frames and committees, we interpret borderline significant findings cautiously and emphasize effect sizes alongside p-values.

Findings

The Dominance of Competition Framing

Congressional AI discourse is dominated by what we term the competition frame—a rhetorical construction combining sovereignty claims (22.1%) and innovation narratives (20.9%). Together, these competition framings account for 43% of hearings, positioning AI development as a race requiring American mobilization.

The sovereignty frame, appearing in over one-fifth of hearings, constructs AI as a domain of great power competition with China. Representative testimony includes:

“We are in a technological competition with China that will determine the future of the 21st century. If we don’t lead in AI, China will—and that has implications for everything from our military advantage to our economic prosperity.”

This framing invokes what scholars have termed “techno-sovereignty” or “AI nationalism” (Bradford, 2020)—the construction of technology development as a national mobilization imperative. Notably, such framings rarely engage specific policy mechanisms; they function primarily to establish urgency and frame alternative approaches (precaution, cooperation) as competitive liabilities.

The innovation frame (20.9%) complements sovereignty framing by emphasizing economic opportunity. Here, AI is constructed not as a threat to be managed but as a boon to be captured:

“AI represents the greatest economic opportunity of our lifetime. American companies are poised to create millions of jobs and generate trillions in economic activity—if we get the policy environment right.”

Together, sovereignty and innovation frames construct what we term the “competition frame”: a rhetorical ensemble positioning AI development as a zero-sum contest where American leadership is both possible and necessary. This frame dominates congressional discourse, shaping the parameters of policy debate.

Marginal Frames: What Congress Is Not Saying About AI

Equally revealing is what congressional AI discourse does not emphasize. Rights-based framing—emphasizing privacy, civil liberties, and constitutional protections—appears in only 9.3% of hearings. Algorithmic harm framing—emphasizing bias, discrimination, and manipulation—appears in 10.5%. These frames, which dominate AI discourse in academic and advocacy contexts, remain marginal in congressional deliberation.

The marginalization of rights framing is particularly striking given extensive journalistic and scholarly documentation of algorithmic harms. Congressional discourse acknowledges such concerns but rarely centers them. When rights issues appear, they are often subordinated to competition frames:

“We need to address concerns about AI bias, but we can’t let those concerns slow us down in the race with China. We need to move fast and fix problems as we go.”

This subordination suggests that competition framing not only dominates but actively marginalizes alternative constructions. Rights and harm concerns must be articulated within the competition frame—as obstacles to manage rather than problems requiring precautionary approaches.

Chamber Patterns: Senate Security, House Innovation

AI framing varies across congressional chambers in ways consistent with institutional roles. The Senate, with its constitutional foreign policy responsibilities and committee structure (Armed Services, Foreign Relations, Intelligence), emphasizes sovereignty framing more heavily (28%) than the House (18%). This difference approaches but does not reach statistical significance (χ² = 2.67, p = .10), suggesting a pattern requiring confirmation with larger samples.

The House shows modestly higher innovation framing (23% vs. 15%) and governance framing (23% vs. 13%), consistent with its committees’ domestic policy jurisdictions (Science, Small Business, Oversight). These patterns align with Fenno’s (1978) analysis of congressional committee specialization: members gravitate toward frames consistent with their committee positions and constituency interests.

Exploratory committee-level analysis reveals predictable jurisdictional patterns. Armed Services and Intelligence committees show 42% sovereignty framing; Judiciary committees show 28% rights framing; Commerce and Science committees show 35% innovation framing. These patterns suggest that frame selection is partly institutionally determined—the same AI developments are constructed differently depending on committee jurisdiction.

Temporal Concentration: The ChatGPT Effect

A notable feature of our sample is its temporal concentration: 90% of hearings (172 of 192) occurred after ChatGPT’s release in November 2022. This concentration reflects both genuine policy attention following a “focusing event” (Kingdon, 1995) and broader AI hype cycle effects that we cannot disentangle.

The limited pre-2023 data (20 hearings) precludes robust temporal inference. Descriptively, pre-2023 hearings show higher innovation and economic risk framing, while post-2023 hearings show elevated sovereignty framing—consistent with the “AI race” discourse that emerged following ChatGPT. However, we resist temporal emergence claims given sampling limitations.

This concentration is both a limitation and a finding. It documents the recency of sustained congressional AI attention and suggests that AI framing may remain fluid—the competition frame dominates current discourse but has not been tested by extended policy debate or countervailing events.

Discussion

Competition Framing and Its Policy Implications

The dominance of competition framing in congressional AI discourse has concrete implications for policy. Frames are not merely rhetorical; they constitute the problems that policy addresses (Schön & Rein, 1994). By constructing AI as a race with China, Congress privileges policy responses emphasizing speed, investment, and competitive positioning—while marginalizing approaches emphasizing precaution, rights protection, or international cooperation.

This framing echoes earlier technology competitions, particularly the Cold War space race. Like Sputnik, ChatGPT functioned as a “focusing event” that catalyzed political attention while shaping its direction. The competition frame provides clear villains (China), clear metrics (leadership), and clear prescriptions (accelerate)—rhetorical advantages that may explain its dominance over more complex frames acknowledging tradeoffs and uncertainties.

Yet competition framing carries risks. It may encourage corner-cutting on safety and rights protections in pursuit of speed. It may foreclose international cooperation on shared challenges (misinformation, deepfakes, autonomous weapons). And it may embed assumptions about AI development—that it is a race, that national leadership is possible, that acceleration is desirable—that warrant scrutiny rather than presumption.

The Marginalization of Rights Discourse

The marginalization of rights framing (9.3%) in congressional AI discourse is notable given the centrality of civil liberties concerns in academic and advocacy discussions of AI. Scholars and advocates have documented algorithmic harms ranging from discriminatory credit scoring to biased facial recognition to manipulative recommendation systems. Yet these concerns remain peripheral in congressional construction of AI as a policy problem.

Several factors may explain this marginalization. Rights framing tends to emphasize constraints and limitations—approaches that fit awkwardly within competition frames emphasizing speed and mobilization. Rights constituencies (civil liberties organizations, affected communities) may have less access to congressional agenda-setting than industry actors promoting innovation frames or national security actors promoting sovereignty frames. And the novelty of AI-specific harms may limit the “stickiness” of rights frames that lack established policy templates.

Whatever the explanation, the marginalization of rights framing has implications for AI policy. If Congress constructs AI primarily through competition frames, rights protections will be afterthoughts rather than design principles—implemented, if at all, as corrections to completed systems rather than constraints on system development.

Methodological Contributions and Limitations

This study demonstrates the feasibility of LLM-based framing analysis while identifying critical considerations for researchers. We achieved substantial inter-coder reliability (κ = .66) using two independent LLM coders with targeted prompt engineering. This reliability exceeds typical human coder performance on complex framing tasks and enables analysis at scales impractical for human coding.

However, several limitations warrant attention. First, our reliability measures inter-LLM agreement, not validity. Both models may share training-induced biases producing systematic agreement on incorrect codings. Human validation of LLM coding decisions remains necessary for validity claims. Second, per-frame reliability varies substantially—rights framing fell below conventional thresholds (κ = .52), requiring cautious interpretation. Third, our “document-type bias” finding suggests that LLMs may be particularly susceptible to coding formal document features rather than content framing—a consideration for researchers applying similar methods.

The search strategy limitation is also consequential. Our “artificial intelligence” search may miss AI-related discourse conducted through application-specific terminology (“facial recognition,” “predictive policing,” “algorithmic accountability”). This limitation particularly affects pre-2022 coverage, when AI was more often discussed through specific applications. Future research should employ broader search strategies capturing related terminology.

Conclusion

Congressional AI discourse is dominated by competition framing—a rhetorical construction combining geopolitical sovereignty claims and economic opportunity narratives. This frame positions AI development as a race requiring American mobilization, marginalizing alternative constructions emphasizing civil liberties, algorithmic harm, or precautionary governance.

The consolidation of competition framing has implications for AI policy. Frames shape policy by defining problems, privileging certain solutions, and marginalizing alternatives (Entman, 1993). By constructing AI as a race with China, Congress creates path dependencies that may shape AI governance for decades. Rights protections, international cooperation, and precautionary approaches must now contend with an established frame that constructs them as competitive liabilities.

Our findings also contribute methodologically to computational framing analysis. We demonstrate that LLM-based coding can achieve substantial reliability with targeted prompt engineering while identifying document-type bias as a critical consideration. As communication scholars increasingly employ computational methods, attention to such validity threats becomes essential.

The current moment in AI policy is distinctive: congressional attention is intense but recent, frame competition remains active, and policy outcomes remain undetermined. Understanding how AI is being constructed as a political problem—and what constructions are being marginalized—is crucial for scholars and advocates seeking to shape AI governance. The competition frame currently dominates, but frames are historical achievements, not natural facts. Alternative constructions remain possible.
